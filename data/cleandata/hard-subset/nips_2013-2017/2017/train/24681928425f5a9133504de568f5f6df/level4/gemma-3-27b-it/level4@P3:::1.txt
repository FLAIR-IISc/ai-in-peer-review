This paper introduces a computational framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The authors evaluate this approach in the context of a “Battleship”-like game, aiming to predict and generate questions humans pose when attempting to learn an ambiguous game state. They represent questions using a compositional, program-like language and employ a log-linear model with features capturing informativeness (expected information gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance to the game board.

The idea of modeling questions as programs builds upon a long line of work in both linguistics and AI, relating to formalizing question meaning and program synthesis [6, 24]. In active learning, the paper addresses the limitations of traditional approaches focused on simple label requests [21], by proposing a more expressive framework for information seeking. The work also connects to recent progress in visual question generation [16, 20], but distinguishes itself by focusing on goal-directed question asking in a novel, ambiguous scenario.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its novel combination of ideas. The authors successfully integrate concepts from program synthesis, Bayesian inference, and cognitive science to create a generative model of question asking. The experimental results demonstrate that the model can predict human question frequencies and generate plausible, novel questions, suggesting that the features chosen – particularly complexity – are important for capturing human-like behavior. The ablation studies, comparing the full model to variants with removed features, provide valuable insight into the relative importance of informativeness, complexity, and answer type.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The task, while well-defined, is relatively constrained and may not generalize well to more complex, real-world scenarios. The reliance on a predefined grammar, while enabling program-based representation, introduces a degree of artificiality. Although the grammar is demonstrated to cover the human questions, the effort required to adapt it to new domains is unclear. The paper lacks a more thorough comparison to simpler baseline models, beyond the feature ablation. For instance, a baseline that randomly samples questions from the grammar, weighted by their length, would provide a useful point of comparison. While the authors acknowledge limitations regarding natural language processing, future work could explore incorporating techniques for grounding the program representation in actual human language. Finally, the descriptions of some features, such as "relevance", could benefit from further elaboration.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to modeling question asking as program generation.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict and generate human-like questions in a defined task.
*   Insightful ablation studies highlighting the importance of question complexity.
*   Clear connection to relevant work in active learning, cognitive science, and linguistics.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The task is relatively constrained and may not generalize well.
*   The reliance on a predefined grammar introduces artificiality.
*   Limited comparison to simpler baseline models.
*   Potential scalability concerns regarding adapting the grammar to new domains.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the understanding of human question asking. While the task is constrained, the proposed framework is novel and the results are promising. The paper is of reasonable quality, well-written, and addresses a significant challenge in AI. However, the limited generalizability and the lack of thorough comparison with simpler baselines raise concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The novelty of the approach and the insightful analysis of feature importance make this paper worthy of inclusion in NIPS, despite the limitations. I encourage the authors to address the concerns regarding generalizability and baseline comparisons in future work.
