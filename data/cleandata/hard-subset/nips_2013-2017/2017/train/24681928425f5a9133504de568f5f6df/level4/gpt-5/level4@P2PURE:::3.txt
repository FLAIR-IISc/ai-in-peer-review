The paper introduces a cognitive model that aims to predict which questions are asked in particular contexts (belief states). In this framework, the unnormalized log probability of a question is a weighted sum of four factors: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (roughly question length), answer type, and “relevance” (whether the question pertains to the current belief state versus world-independent logical facts). The model is evaluated in a Battleship-style task, where the goal is to locate multiple ships (clusters of grid cells) on a grid. The full model, incorporating all features, achieves better held-out log-likelihood on human data than ablated/lesioned variants. However, in terms of correlation with human judgments, a model that omits EIG performs about as well as the full model.

Overall, the paper appears technically sound.

I view the work as more successful as an AI contribution than as a cognitive science contribution. The paper claims that “We find that our model predicts what question people will ask” and that “from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity.” I am not convinced the results reveal much about how people trade off these factors. In the Battleship domain, people seem either slightly influenced by EIG (if we credit the log-likelihood results, which have a 19% bootstrap-estimated chance of being incorrect) or not influenced by EIG at all (if we trust the correlation results, which are themselves noisy due to sparse human data). These findings do not, in my view, support strong conclusions about people’s general balancing of these factors.

The paper is well written and clear, and I expect I could reproduce the results.

Some relevant prior work is missing. One apparent contribution is to “model questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer.” This aligns with the notion of a “goal” in Hawkins (2015, “Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.”), which itself builds on the question-under-discussion (QUD) framework introduced by Roberts (1996, “Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics.”). The Hawkins questioner model also uses expected information gain and trades it off against question complexity, though in a simpler domain.

I think this family of models—using a compositional prior over questions and selecting based on EIG and other factors—makes strong sense for both cognitive and AI applications and will likely see wider use. I see the paper’s main contribution as demonstrating that this approach can generate interesting, compositional questions in a non-trivial domain.
