This paper models question asking in a Battleship-like game by treating questions as programs in an artificial language with compositional, Montagovian-style semantics. A log-linear model is trained over a small set of simple features of the questions—expected information gain (informativeness), answer type (e.g., Boolean or numerical), and whether the question refers to features of the board at all—to reproduce the questions users ask.

Strengths:
- The high-level motivation, connecting question asking to active learning, is reasonable.
- The formalization is logically precise and the setting is unambiguous, which makes the approach easy to analyze.

Weaknesses:
- The core ideas are rather obvious, and the modeling choices are straightforward.
- The task is very simple, highly constrained, and logically unambiguous, limiting the scope of the contribution.
- Given the narrow, controlled setting and the simplicity of the features, I do not believe the work will be of interest to the NIPS community.

Quality: The approach appears technically sound at a basic level: the representation of questions as compositional programs is standard in formal semantics, and the use of a log-linear model over hand-designed features is routine. However, the feature set is very limited (informativeness, coarse answer type distinctions, and a relevance indicator), and the task itself is highly constrained. As a result, the technical contribution feels minimal.

Clarity: The core idea is clear, and the use of a formal artificial language removes ambiguity. The paper’s presentation of the modeling framework is straightforward to follow.

Originality: The overall framing—questions as programs with compositional semantics—is not new, and in this context the particular instantiation is obvious. The modeling components (log-linear modeling over simple features) are also very standard. The constrained game setting further reduces the sense of novelty.

Significance: Because the task is simple and tightly controlled, and the techniques are conventional, the potential impact is limited. I do not see this work engaging the broader NIPS audience or advancing the state of the art in a meaningful way.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Reasonable motivation via active learning.
- Clear, unambiguous formalization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Ideas are obvious and the modeling is straightforward.
- Task is overly simple, constrained, and logically unambiguous.
- Limited novelty and limited relevance to the NIPS community.

Overall, while the paper is clearly written and the general motivation is sensible, the simplicity of the setting and the obviousness of the approach make the contribution too limited for NIPS.
