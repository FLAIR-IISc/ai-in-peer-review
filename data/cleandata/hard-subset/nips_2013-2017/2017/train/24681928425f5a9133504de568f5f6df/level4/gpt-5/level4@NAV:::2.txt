The authors investigate human question-asking with K-ary answers. They hand-specify a PCFG for a “battleship” setting, where colored ships are hidden in a partially observable grid (some cells are revealed as containing a segment of a ship of a particular color or as empty). The agent’s task is to pose a question that elicits a single-word answer and maximizes information about the board state. The PCFG is used as a prior over questions, which are represented as lambda-calculus expressions. Question quality is modeled as a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (either length or negative log probability under the PCFG with a uniform distribution over rewrite rules), and “answer type” (e.g., whether the response is boolean or a color). Human data were taken from prior work and manually mapped into the grammar. The authors compare ablated versions of the model using leave-one-out cross-validation and find that all components contribute non-trivially, with complexity clearly dominant. They also introduce a set of novel questions.

Question-asking is central to linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors offer a fresh framing that integrates contemporary linguistic theory with cognitive modeling to analyze how people ask questions, with the potential to inspire active learning systems that pose more sophisticated queries. The paper is exceptionally clear and well written. The model is evaluated appropriately from both quantitative and qualitative angles.

From a computational cognitive science standpoint, this work advances self-directed learning. Earlier research showed that informativeness alone poorly predicts human question-asking. This follow-up clarifies part of the discrepancy: informativeness plays a secondary role to complexity and may also be affected by the size of the question space available to the model. As such, the paper meaningfully contributes to cognitive science, which could suffice to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

That said, there are significant limitations that may curb its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. Even if one could automate the mapping from natural-language questions to logical forms, it is unclear how well the approach scales or transfers to domains of wider interest. The battleship environment is small with limited features, and the method already appears computationally taxing. Still, the core idea—active learning with richer question types—is compelling; the authors may need to present a concrete, broadly relevant application where their technique is likely to scale and provide new insights.

Minor comments:
1) While perhaps not definitive, you could conduct nested model comparisons using the log-likelihoods of the ablated models to obtain statistical significance tests.
2) Looking ahead to sequential question-asking, there are related models in formal semantics and pragmatics, especially within the Questions Under Discussion tradition. Some pointers:
Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131-139.
