- Introduces a cognitive model treating questions as executable programs over possible world states.  
- Core probabilistic framework scores questions by informativeness (EIG), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance to current belief.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship dataset; full model outperformed variants in held-out log-likelihood and achieved Spearman’s ρ = 0.64 with human frequencies.  
- Complexity-agnostic variant performed significantly worse, suggesting humans prefer concise questions beyond pure informativeness.  
- Grammar-based compositional representation enables novel, contextually relevant question generation, advancing beyond template or data-hungry neural approaches.  
- Thorough evaluation using leave-one-out cross-validation and detailed supplementary materials for reproducibility.  
- Well-written paper with clear explanations of grammar, features, and optimization.  
- Extends prior work by introducing compositional grammar for open-ended question generation and demonstrating applicability to a complex cognitive task.  
- Offers a scalable method for human-like question generation with implications for active learning and human-AI interaction.  
- Overstates cognitive claims: evidence is domain-specific (Battleship) and indirect, limiting general conclusions about human cognitive mechanisms.  
- Negligible correlation between EIG and human frequency weakens the “balancing informativeness and complexity” interpretation.  
- Misses key citations (Hawkins et al. 2015; Roberts 1996), which contextualize the approach and features.  
- Domain-specific grammar (e.g., ship-size predicates) limits immediate applicability and requires significant effort to adapt to new domains.  
- Sparse human data reduces confidence in cognitive interpretations and generality of results.  
- Recommendation: Accept — solid technical contribution and novel compositional question generation, but revise to temper cognitive claims and add missing citations.
