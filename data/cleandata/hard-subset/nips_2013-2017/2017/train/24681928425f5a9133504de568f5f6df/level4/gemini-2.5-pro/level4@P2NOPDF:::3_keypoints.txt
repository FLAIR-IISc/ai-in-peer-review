- Proposes a cognitive model that scores questions as a weighted sum of four features: informativeness (EIG), complexity (question length), answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluates the model in the Battleship domain where the task is to locate hidden ships on a grid.  
- The full model achieves higher held-out log-likelihood than lesioned versions, showing improved fit to human data.  
- Evidence for the role of informativeness (EIG) is mixed: a model without EIG performs nearly as well by correlation, and bootstrap analysis gives a 19% chance the EIG advantage is due to chance.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, technically sound, and reproducible from the provided methodology.  
- Main contribution is a successful proof-of-concept that a compositional prior over questions can model question generation and may be useful for AI and cognitive modeling.  
- Cognitive science claims are not fully supported given noisy human data and ambiguous evidence about how people balance informativeness and complexity.  
- Paper should better situate and differentiate itself from prior work (e.g., Hawkins et al. 2015; Roberts 1996) that also frame questions/goals trading off information gain and complexity.
