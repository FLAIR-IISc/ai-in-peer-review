- Models human question asking as lambda-calculus–style program generation evaluated on a Battleship-like world.
- Uses a hand-built PCFG to define a compositional space of question programs.
- Scores programs with a log-linear model using features: expected information gain (EIG), complexity, answer type, plus a relevance filter.
- Parameters learned by importance-sampled gradient ascent; inference requires large importance samples and approximations.
- Evaluated on 605 human questions with leave-one-context-out cross-validation for frequency prediction and novel question generation.
- Lesion studies show all feature groups matter, with complexity especially critical to matching human behavior.
- Connects active learning, compositional semantics, and questions-as-partitions perspectives; aligns with program induction and semantic parsing.
- Novel framing: open-ended question asking as probabilistic program synthesis bridging cognitive modeling and active learning.
- Clear, well-organized presentation with careful definitions of grammar, features, and inference.
- Produces both quantitative fits to human question distributions and qualitatively sensible novel questions.
- Key cognitive insight: complexity moderates informativeness, explaining limits of EIG-alone models.
- Main weakness: heavy domain specificity and hand-engineered grammar plus hand-coded natural language-to-program mapping.
- Computational burden: inference is expensive even in the small domain and some contexts were excluded for tractability.
- Lacks an end-to-end natural language interface, limiting practical impact beyond the controlled domain.
- Unclear generalization: transferability of grammar and feature importance to richer domains is not demonstrated.
- Quality: technically sound and transparent about limitations, though some statistical comparisons could be strengthened.
- Clarity: very clearly written with interpretable figures and decomposition.
- Originality: fresh synthesis representing question asking as probabilistic program generation.
- Significance: strong for cognitive science and self-directed learning; broader ML/NIPS impact depends on demonstrated scalability or wider application.
- Pros for acceptance: elegant unified account, strong evidence for complexity’s role, ability to generate novel questions, clear writing and ablations.
- Cons for acceptance: reliance on hand-crafted grammar, computational intensity, lack of end-to-end NL interface, limited statistical significance assessment.
- Suggested improvements: provide principled statistical comparisons of lesioned models (nested or bootstrap tests).
- Suggested improvements: outline concrete path to broader domains or add a second-domain demonstration to show transferability.
- Suggested improvements: discuss integrating a semantic parser and expected performance/noise trade-offs.
- Suggested improvements: connect to formal semantics/pragmatics for multi-turn question selection (Questions Under Discussion literature).
- Minor comments: consider nested model comparisons or bootstrap tests and cite QUD/pragmatics work (Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
- Overall: thoughtful, well-executed computational cognitive science contribution; needs stronger evidence of scalability/generalization for broader NIPS impact.
