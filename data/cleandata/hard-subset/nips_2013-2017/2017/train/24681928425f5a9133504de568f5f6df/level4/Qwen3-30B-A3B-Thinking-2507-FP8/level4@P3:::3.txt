

This paper proposes a computational model that frames questions as programs executable on possible world states, with the goal of predicting and generating human-like questions in an information-search game. The authors formalize questions as programs in a simple programming language, using a probabilistic model that balances expected information gain (EIG), question complexity, answer type, and relevance to the current context. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like game, where players attempt to identify hidden ship configurations through question asking.

The paper makes a significant contribution by formalizing question asking as program generation, building on prior work in computational linguistics (e.g., Lake et al. 2015 on program induction) and cognitive science (Hawkins et al. 2015 on question asking as information-seeking behavior). However, the authors overlook crucial connections to Roberts' (1996) foundational work on question-under-discussion (QUD) and Hawkins' (2015) use of EIG for question selection in a simpler domain. While the paper correctly identifies program-based question representation as novel for modeling human question asking, it fails to properly contextualize this within existing linguistic and cognitive frameworks.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The probabilistic model is well-constructed with clear features (EIG, complexity, answer type, relevance), and the evaluation methodology is sound, employing cross-validation and bootstrapping to compare model variants. The authors convincingly show that the full model outperforms variants that omit key components (particularly complexity), suggesting that humans balance information gain with question simplicity. The model's ability to generate novel human-like questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") is particularly compelling.

The paper excels in clarity. The formalism is well-explained, the grammar for question generation is carefully designed, and the experimental setup is transparent. The writing is precise, and the supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors make a strong case for the model's predictive power, with a 0.64 Spearman correlation between predicted and actual question frequencies across contexts.

The originality is somewhat limited by the domain-specific application to the Battleship game. While the programmatic approach to questions is novel for this context, the core idea of balancing question informativeness with complexity has been explored in Hawkins et al. (2015) in a simpler setting. The paper's claim that it "provides the first predictive model to date of open-ended human question asking" is overstated given this prior work. However, the model's ability to generate creative, contextually relevant questions beyond the training set represents a meaningful advance over previous neural approaches that produce stereotyped questions.

The significance of the work lies primarily in its methodological contribution to modeling question asking as program synthesis, which could inform more human-like AI dialogue systems. The paper successfully demonstrates this approach in a non-trivial domain, showing that compositionality and context-sensitivity are essential for predicting human question patterns. While the cognitive science implications are somewhat limited (the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how humans balance these factors in general), the approach could be valuable for future work in computational cognitive science and AI.

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of questions as executable programs
- Rigorous evaluation of model variants with appropriate statistical validation
- Successful generation of novel, contextually relevant questions
- Well-structured, clearly written, and reproducible methodology

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient engagement with prior work on question semantics (Roberts 1996, Hawkins 2015)
- Overstated claims about being the "first predictive model" of human question asking
- Limited cognitive science insights about how humans balance question features
- Domain-specific focus (Battleship) without clear path to broader applicability

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the computational modeling of question asking, particularly in demonstrating how program synthesis can generate creative, context-sensitive questions. While the cognitive science claims are somewhat overstated, the methodological approach is sound and could be broadly applicable to other domains. The missing connections to Roberts' QUD framework and Hawkins' EIG work represent a significant opportunity for improvement in the final version.

Pro-acceptance arguments: The model provides a principled, generalizable approach to question generation that successfully predicts and creates human-like questions in a non-trivial domain. The technical execution is strong, the methodology is sound, and the approach represents a meaningful advance over existing neural question generation methods.

Con-acceptance arguments: The paper fails to properly contextualize its work within existing linguistic and cognitive science frameworks, and the cognitive science claims are not fully supported by the results. The contribution is more methodological than conceptual.

Overall, this paper makes a solid technical contribution to the modeling of question asking, with clear potential applications in AI dialogue systems. With minor revisions to properly contextualize the work within prior literature, it would be a strong fit for NIPS. The technical quality is high, the method is sound, and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the missing connections to prior work on question semantics.
