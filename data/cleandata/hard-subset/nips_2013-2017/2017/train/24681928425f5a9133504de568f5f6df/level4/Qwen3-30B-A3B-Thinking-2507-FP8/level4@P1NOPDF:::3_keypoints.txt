- Introduces a cognitive model predicting question-asking as a weighted sum of four components: informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance to belief state.
- Evaluated on Battleship; the full model achieves higher held-out log-likelihood than lesioned variants.
- Correlation with human judgments is similar between the full model and a version without EIG, suggesting EIG may not be critical in this domain.
- Bootstrap analysis indicates a 19% chance the observed EIG advantage is not statistically significant, creating ambiguity about EIG’s role.
- Technical approach is sound, the paper is well-written, and results are reproducible.
- The cognitive-science claims (predicting questions and revealing trade-offs) are overreaching given the ambiguous evidence.
- Sparse human data and conflicting metrics (log-likelihood vs correlation) prevent strong conclusions about how people balance informativeness and complexity.
- The paper overlooks prior work—Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996) use closely related frameworks (EIG, complexity, QUD)—and should acknowledge this lineage.
- Main contribution is stronger for AI: demonstrating a compositional prior that generates meaningful questions in a non-trivial domain.
- Would benefit from clearer alignment with cognitive theory and more explicit discussion of limitations relative to existing frameworks.
