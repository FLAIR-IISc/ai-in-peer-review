The authors investigate human question asking in scenarios with K-ary answers. They define a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) for a “battleship” domain, where hidden colored shapes are located within a partially observable grid (some tiles reveal portions of ships of specific colors, or confirm emptiness). The agent’s task is to formulate questions with single-word answers that maximize information gain about the board’s state. This PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are represented as statements in lambda calculus. Question quality is evaluated using a linear function incorporating informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (measured by question length or negative log probability under the PCFG, using a uniform distribution over rewrite rules), and answer type (e.g., true/false or color identification). Human data, sourced from a prior publication, was manually encoded into their grammar. The authors assess lesioned versions of the model via leave-one-out cross-validation, revealing that each factor contributes significantly, although complexity proves most influential. They also present a set of novel questions generated by the model.

Question asking is a crucial area within linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors offer an innovative perspective, unifying modern linguistic and cognitive theory to examine human questioning behavior. This approach has the potential to stimulate new research in active learning, inspiring models capable of formulating more sophisticated queries. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, and the model is evaluated thoroughly through both quantitative and qualitative analyses.

From the perspective of a computational cognitive scientist, this work advances the field of self-directed learning. Previous research indicated that question informativeness alone is a poor predictor of human question choice. This paper partially explains why – informativeness is secondary to complexity, and may be limited by the range of questions available to the model. This contribution to the cognitive science literature is substantial and may, in itself, justify publication in NIPS.

However, the approach’s limitations raise concerns about its broader appeal to the NIPS audience. While automating the translation of human questions into semantic logic may be feasible, it’s unclear how this method could scale to domains of greater interest to the wider research community. The battleship domain is small, with a limited number of features, and the method is already computationally demanding. Despite this, the core ideas – active learning utilizing richer, more nuanced questions – are compelling. However, the authors may need to clearly articulate a realistic application scenario relevant to the broader NIPS community, demonstrating where their technique is likely to yield novel insights.

Minor comments:

1) It might be informative to perform a nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods (LLs) of the lesioned models, allowing for tests of statistical significance.
2) For future work involving sequential question asking, several models exist within the linguistics literature, specifically within formal semantics and pragmatics. The “Questions under Discussion” framework offers relevant insights. I’ve included a few references for your consideration:

Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131-139.
