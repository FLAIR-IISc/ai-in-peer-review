

The authors investigate human question-asking in K-ary answer domains, defining a PCFG (Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar) for a "battleship" domain where hidden colored shapes occupy a partially observable grid (some tiles reveal ship segments or emptiness). The agent's task is to pose single-word questions maximizing information gain about the board state. Questions are formalized as lambda calculus statements, with "goodness" defined as a linear combination of: (1) informativeness (expected information gain), (2) complexity (question length or negative log probability under the PCFG with uniform rewrite rules), and (3) answer type (e.g., binary vs. color). Human question data, sourced from prior work and hand-coded into the grammar, was used for model evaluation. Ablated model variants were compared via leave-one-out cross-validation, revealing all factors contribute non-negligibly—though complexity proved most significant. The paper also introduces novel question examples.

Question-asking bridges linguistics, cognitive science, and hypothesis testing. The authors offer a novel integration of modern linguistic and cognitive theory to model human question-asking, potentially advancing active learning by enabling models to pose more sophisticated questions. The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and rigorously evaluated through both quantitative and qualitative analyses.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this work significantly advances self-directed learning. Previous studies found question informativeness poorly predicted human behavior, but this paper clarifies why: complexity dominates (and may partly stem from the combinatorial explosion of possible questions). This resolves a key gap in cognitive science literature, justifying publication in NIPS on theoretical grounds alone.

However, the approach faces critical scalability limitations that may restrict broader NIPS appeal. While automating human question text into semantic logic is feasible in constrained domains, the method struggles with computational complexity even in the small battleship domain (limited feature space). More importantly, the authors provide no clear pathway for extension to real-world applications of interest to the ML community. The work’s novelty hinges on "richer questions," but without a compelling scenario demonstrating where this technique yields novel insights beyond toy domains, its impact remains niche. The authors should explicitly identify such a scenario—e.g., in medical diagnosis or robotics—where their framework’s advantages over simpler active learning approaches become evident.

Minor comments:  
1) For statistical rigor, nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods of ablated variants could yield significance tests for factor contributions.  
2) For sequential question-asking, the authors might engage with formal semantics/pragmatics literature on "Questions under Discussion" (e.g., Ginzburg, 1995; Rojas-Esponda, 2013), which offers complementary frameworks for modeling question structure and context.
