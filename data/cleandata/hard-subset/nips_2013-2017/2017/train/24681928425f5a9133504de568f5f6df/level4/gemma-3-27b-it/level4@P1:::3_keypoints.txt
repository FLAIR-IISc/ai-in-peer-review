- Paper presents a cognitive model that predicts question selection by scoring questions as a weighted sum of informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Model is applied to a Battleship-style grid task to infer ship locations.  
- Full model outperforms ablated variants on held-out human data by log-likelihood.  
- Correlation with human judgments shows a model without expected information gain performs comparably to the full model.  
- Technical implementation appears sound.  
- Cognitive science contributions are weak: evidence for claims that the model predicts human questions and reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity is limited.  
- Log-likelihood results suggest only marginal influence of EIG and may be spurious per bootstrap estimates.  
- Correlation results are noisy and sparse, limiting strong conclusions about human behavior.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and results appear reproducible.  
- Related work section is incomplete; misses prior work on question meaning and goals (e.g., Hawkins 2015, Roberts 1996).  
- The compositional program-based question model is a promising approach for AI and cognitive modeling.  
- Main contribution: demonstrating feasibility of generating interesting, compositional questions in a non-trivial domain.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel technically sound approach, generates human-like questions, shows informativeness and complexity matter, clear writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak empirical support for cognitive claims about EIG, incomplete related work coverage, limited domain and unclear generalizability.
