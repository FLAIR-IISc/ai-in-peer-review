The paper presents a cognitive model designed to predict question selection based on context and belief states. The model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of several factors: informativeness (measured as expected information gain, or EIG), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance – whether the question pertains to the current belief state or world-independent facts. This model is applied to the Battleship domain, where the goal is to locate hidden ships within a grid. The full model, incorporating all features, outperformed ablated versions with fewer features in predicting held-out human data, as measured by log-likelihood. However, when evaluating correlation with human judgments, the model excluding EIG performed comparably to the full model.

The paper appears technically sound.

I believe this work is more compelling as an AI project than as a contribution to cognitive science. The authors claim their model "predicts what question people will ask" and that their results "show how people balance informativeness and complexity.” However, I am not convinced that the findings significantly advance our understanding of this balance. In the Battleship domain, the evidence suggests people are either weakly influenced by EIG (based on the log-likelihood results, which have a 19% chance of being spurious according to bootstrap estimates) or not influenced at all (based on the correlation results, which are themselves noisy due to limited human data). These results do not seem sufficient to draw firm conclusions about how people generally weigh these factors. 

The paper is well-written and clear, and I am confident I could reproduce the reported results.

A significant omission is the lack of discussion regarding relevant prior work. The paper highlights modeling questions "as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer.” This concept is directly analogous to the “goal” representation used in Hawkins (2015, "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.") and builds upon the notion of a question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts (1996, "Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics."). Furthermore, Hawkins’ questioner model also incorporates expected information gain and balances it against question complexity, though applied to a simpler domain.

I find this type of model – one with a compositional prior over questions that selects based on EIG and other factors – intuitively appealing and potentially valuable for both cognitive and AI modeling. I anticipate seeing it employed more frequently in the future. I view the primary contribution of this paper as demonstrating the feasibility of generating interesting, compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this approach.
