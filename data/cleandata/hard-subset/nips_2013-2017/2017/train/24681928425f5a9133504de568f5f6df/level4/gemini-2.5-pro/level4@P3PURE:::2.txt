This paper examines human question-asking in a setting with K-ary answers. The authors manually define a Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) for a "battleship" domain, which consists of hidden, colored shapes on a partially observable grid. The agent's task is to ask a question with a single-word answer that provides the most information about the board's state.

The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are formalized as statements in lambda calculus. The quality of a question is modeled as a linear function of its informativeness (expected information gain), its complexity (its length or negative log-probability under the PCFG), and its "answer type" (e.g., true/false vs. color). Human data from a previously published study was hand-coded into this grammar. Using leave-one-out cross-validation, the authors compared ablated versions of their model and found that all factors made a non-negligible contribution, with complexity being the most important. Finally, the authors present a series of novel questions generated by the model.

**Main Review**

Question-asking is an important area of research in both linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors offer an innovative perspective that unifies modern linguistic and cognitive theory to examine how people ask questions. This work has the potential to foster novel research in the active learning literature by enabling models to ask more sophisticated, structured questions. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, and the model is appropriately evaluated from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this paper significantly advances the field of self-directed learning. It builds upon previous work that found informativeness to be a poor predictor of human question-asking. This paper helps resolve why this is the case, demonstrating that informativeness is secondary to question complexity and is constrained by the size of the space of possible questions. This is a notable contribution to the cognitive science literature, which in itself may be sufficient to justify publication at NIPS.

However, the approach has serious limitations that may restrict its interest to the broader NIPS audience. It is unclear how the method could scale or be extended to domains of interest to the wider machine learning community. The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar is a major bottleneck, and while it might be possible to automate the parsing of natural language questions into a semantic logic, the path to doing so is not discussed. The battleship domain is small, with only a few features, yet the method is already described as being computationally strained.

That being said, the core ideas—particularly active learning with richer, more structured questions—are compelling. The paper's impact would be significantly greater if the authors provided a clear scenario or application of interest to the broader community where their technique is likely to be applicable and provide novel insights.

**Minor Comments**

1.  It might be informative to perform a nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods from the ablated models. This would provide formal tests of statistical significance for each model component.
2.  For future work on sequential question-asking, the authors might consider other models from the linguistics literature on formal semantics and pragmatics. Much of this work comes from the "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) framework. I have listed a few references below as pointers to this literature.

    *   Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy, 18*(5), 459-527.
    *   Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17*, 131-139.
