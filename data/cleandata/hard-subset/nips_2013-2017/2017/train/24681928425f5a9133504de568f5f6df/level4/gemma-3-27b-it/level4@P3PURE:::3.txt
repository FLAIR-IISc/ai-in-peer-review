The paper presents a cognitive model designed to predict question selection based on context (belief states). The model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of informativeness (measured as expected information gain, or EIG), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance – whether the question pertains to the current belief state or world-independent facts. This model is applied to the Battleship domain, where the objective is to locate hidden ships within a grid. The complete model, incorporating all described features, outperformed ablated versions with fewer features in predicting held-out human question data, as measured by log-likelihood. However, the model without EIG exhibited comparable correlation with human judgments to the full model.

The paper appears technically sound. 

While a valuable AI project, its contribution to cognitive science is less clear. The authors claim the model “predicts what question people will ask” and that the results “show how people balance informativeness and complexity.” However, I find the evidence insufficient to support strong conclusions about this balance. In the Battleship domain, the results suggest that human question selection is either weakly influenced by EIG (based on log-likelihood results, though with a 19% bootstrap-estimated chance of being spurious) or not influenced at all (according to the noisy correlation results, potentially due to sparse human data). These findings do not strongly inform our understanding of how people generally balance these factors. 

The paper is well-written and clear, and I believe the results could be reproduced.

A significant omission is relevant prior work. The paper positions “modeling questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer” as a novel contribution. However, this approach mirrors the concept of a “goal” in Hawkins (2015, “Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.”) and builds upon the notion of the question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts (1996, “Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics.”). Hawkins’ questioner model also leverages expected information gain and trades it off against question complexity, albeit within a simpler domain.

I believe this type of model – featuring a compositional prior over questions and selection based on EIG and other factors – holds considerable promise for both cognitive and AI applications. I anticipate its increased use in future research. The primary contribution of this paper, in my view, is demonstrating the feasibility of generating meaningful compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this approach.
