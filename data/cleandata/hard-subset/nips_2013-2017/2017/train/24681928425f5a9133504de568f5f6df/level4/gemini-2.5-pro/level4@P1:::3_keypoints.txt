- Introduces a cognitive/AI model that represents questions as programs executed on a belief state to produce answers.  
- Defines a log-linear probability distribution over questions with an energy as a weighted sum of features.  
- Features include informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity (program length), answer type (Boolean, Number), and task relevance.  
- Evaluated in a "Battleship"-like grid game to predict human-generated questions.  
- Primary result: the full model outperforms lesioned versions on held-out human data by log-likelihood.  
- However, an information-agnostic model matches the full model on correlation with human question frequencies (Spearman ρ 0.65 vs. 0.64).  
- Evidence for EIG guiding human question-asking is mixed and not statistically robust (full-model advantage in only 81% of bootstrap samples).  
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible with sufficient model/grammar/experiment detail.  
- Main AI contribution: demonstrates feasibility of generating compositional, context-sensitive questions via programmatic representations.  
- Cognitive-science claims (people balance informativeness and complexity) are overreaching given noisy/sparse data and weak empirical support.  
- originality concern: omits relevant prior work (e.g., Hawkins et al. 2015 and QUD literature) that also trade off information gain and complexity.  
- Strengths: novel framework, successful generation of complex human-like questions, valuable for active learning/AI.  
- Weaknesses: overstated cognitive claims and incomplete citation of prior formal models of question asking.
