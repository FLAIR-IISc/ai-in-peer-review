- Proposes a cognitive model that predicts which questions people ask by scoring questions as a weighted sum of informativeness (EIG), complexity (length), answer type, and relevance.

- Evaluated in the Battleship domain; the full model outperforms ablated variants on held-out human data using log-likelihood.

- When evaluated by correlation with human judgments, a model without EIG performs as well as the full model, making the role of EIG ambiguous.

- Methodology is technically sound, clearly described, and appears reproducible; main contribution is a compositional prior over questions integrated with pragmatic factors.

- The work is a strong AI/engineering proof of concept for structured probabilistic question generation in a complex domain.

- Claims about modeling human cognition and how people balance informativeness and complexity are not fully supported by the noisy and conflicting single-task evidence.

- The EIG effect is weak/conflicting: log-likelihood shows a marginal effect (19% chance of being spurious per bootstrap) and correlation shows no effect.

- The paper overlooks prior related work (e.g., Hawkins et al. 2015 and the QUD framework from Roberts 1996) and should situate its contribution relative to these foundations.
