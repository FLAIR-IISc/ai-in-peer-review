This paper investigates human question-asking where answers are K-ary. The authors hand-specify a PCFG for a “battleship” domain with hidden colored shapes on a partially observable grid (some tiles are revealed as parts of a ship of a specific color or as empty). The agent’s task is to ask a single-word-answer question that maximizes information about the board state. The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are represented as lambda-calculus expressions. Question quality is modeled as a linear function of (i) informativeness, measured by expected information gain; (ii) complexity, operationalized either by length or by the negative log probability of being generated by the PCFG under a uniform distribution over rewrite rules; and (iii) answer type (e.g., whether the answer is true/false or a color). Human data were taken from a previously published study and hand-coded into the grammar. Using leave-one-out cross-validation, the authors compare lesioned versions of the model and find that all factors make non-negligible contributions, with complexity clearly the most important. They also present a set of novel questions.

Question-asking is central to linguistics and hypothesis testing. This paper offers an innovative perspective unifying modern linguistic and cognitive theory to explain how people ask questions, with the potential to inspire active learning models that ask more sophisticated questions. The paper is very clear and well written, and the model is evaluated appropriately from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives.

From the perspective of a computational cognitive scientist, the work advances the study of self-directed learning. Prior work found that question informativeness was a poor predictor of human question-asking; this follow-up helps explain why—informativeness is secondary to complexity, and the effect may partly reflect the size of the model’s question space. Accordingly, the paper contributes meaningfully to the cognitive science literature, which may by itself justify publication at NIPS.

That said, there are serious limitations that could reduce its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While it may be possible to automate the mapping from human question text to logical semantics, it remains unclear how the approach scales or extends to domains of broader interest. The battleship domain is small with limited features, and the method already appears computationally strained. The ideas—especially active learning with richer questions—are compelling, but the authors may need to articulate a concrete, larger-scale scenario of interest to the broader community where their technique is applicable and yields novel insights.

Minor comments:
1) It may not be especially revealing, but you could perform nested model comparisons using the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models to obtain tests of statistical significance.
2) For sequential question-asking, there are relevant models in formal semantics and pragmatics, particularly within the Questions Under Discussion tradition. Pointers:
- Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459–527.
- Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of SemDial (DialDam), 17, 131–139.
