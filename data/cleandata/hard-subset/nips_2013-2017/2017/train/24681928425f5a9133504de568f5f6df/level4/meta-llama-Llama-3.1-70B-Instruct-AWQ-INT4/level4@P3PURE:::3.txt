The paper presents a cognitive model that aims to predict when certain questions are asked in specific contexts (belief states). Under this model, the unnormalized log probability of a question is defined as the weighted sum of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (roughly, question length), answer type, and "relevance" (whether the question refers to the belief state or just world-independent logical facts). This model is applied to the Battleship domain, where the task is to determine the location of ships (clusters of grid elements) within a grid. The full model with all the mentioned features outperformed lesioned versions with fewer features in predicting held-out human data, as measured by log-likelihood. However, in terms of correlation with human judgments, the model that did not account for expected information gain (EIG) performed similarly to the full model.

The paper appears to be generally technically sound. However, I believe it succeeds more as an AI project than as a cognitive science project. The paper claims that "We find that our model predicts what question people will ask" and "from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity." Nevertheless, I am not convinced that these results provide significant insights into how people balance these factors. In the Battleship domain, it seems that people are either slightly influenced by EIG (if we rely on the log-likelihood results, which have a 19% chance of being incorrect according to bootstrap estimates) or not at all (if we rely on the correlation results, which are also noisy due to sparse human data). I do not think these results allow us to draw general conclusions about how people balance these factors.

The paper is well-written and clear, making it possible for me to reproduce the results. However, it lacks some prior work. One of the apparent innovations of the paper is the idea of "modeling questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer." This concept is similar to the notion of a "goal" used in Hawkins (2015) ("Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.") and is based on the concept of a question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts (1996) ("Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics."). The questioner model by Hawkins also utilizes expected information gain and trades it off with question complexity, but it is applied to a much simpler domain.

I believe that this type of model, which has a compositional prior over questions and chooses based on EIG and other factors, makes sense as a component for cognitive and AI models, and I expect it to be used more in the future. In my view, the main contribution of this paper is demonstrating that it is possible to generate interesting compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this approach.
