The authors examine human question asking where answers are K-ary. They hand-specify a PCFG for a “battleship” domain with hidden colored shapes on a partially observable grid (some tiles are revealed as containing part of a ship of a specific color or being empty). The agent’s task is to ask a question whose one-word answer provides as much information as possible about the state of the board. The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are represented as lambda-calculus expressions. Question quality is defined as a linear function of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (either length or the negative log-probability of being generated by the PCFG with uniform rewrite-rule probabilities), and answer type (e.g., whether the answer is boolean or a color). Human data from a previously published paper were hand-coded into the grammar. The authors compare lesioned model variants via leave-one-out cross-validation and find that all factors make non-negligible contributions, with complexity clearly the most important. Finally, they present a series of novel questions.

Question asking is an important domain within linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors provide an innovative perspective that unifies modern linguistic and cognitive theory to examine how people ask questions, with the potential to spur new research in active learning using models that ask more sophisticated questions. The paper is extremely clear and well written. The model is appropriately evaluated from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives.

From the perspective of a computational cognitive scientist, this paper advances the field of self-directed learning. Previous work found that question informativeness was a poor predictor of human question asking. This follow-up helps explain why: informativeness is secondary to complexity and, in part, prior null results may reflect the size of the possible question space available to the model. Thus, the paper contributes to the cognitive science literature, which may be sufficient to justify its publication at NIPS.

However, there are serious limitations that may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. Even if it were possible to automate the mapping from human question text to formal semantic representations, it is unclear how the approach would scale or extend to domains of broader interest. The battleship domain is small, with only a few features, and the method is already computationally strained. That said, the core ideas—active learning with richer questions—are interesting, but the authors should provide a clear, broadly relevant scenario where their technique is applicable and offers novel insights.

Minor comments:
1) Although it may not be especially revealing, you could conduct nested model comparisons using the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models to obtain tests of statistical significance.
2) For sequential question asking, consider related models in formal semantics and pragmatics, especially the Questions under Discussion literature. Pointers:
Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459–527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131–139.
