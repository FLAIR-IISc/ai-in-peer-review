- Models questions as executable programs (lambda-calculus) evaluated on possible world states.  
- Key innovations: compositional question representation, a log-linear quality model balancing EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance, and emergence of human-like questions.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship-like human QA dataset; model outperforms ablated variants and generates novel, contextually relevant questions.  
- Originality/significance: bridges cognitive science and ML, resolving prior paradox by showing complexity matters alongside informativeness.  
- Technical quality: rigorous evaluation (leave-one-out CV), clear EIG computation, intuitive examples, and thorough ablation studies.  
- Clarity: well-written with precise definitions, careful grammar design, and cognitively plausible feature engineering.  
- Generalizability weakness: relies on manually engineered grammar for a narrow domain, limiting scalability to richer tasks.  
- Scalability/compute concern: extending to larger domains would require significant knowledge engineering and is computationally strained.  
- Methodological gap: natural language → lambda-calculus mapping is hand-coded; lacks an end-to-end or semantic-parsing solution.  
- Methodological gap: paper does not address sequential/multi-turn question-asking scenarios.  
- Recommendation: accept—important theoretical contribution—but authors should discuss extension pathways to larger domains.  
- Recommendation: explicitly acknowledge and propose solutions for the NL-to-program translation bottleneck.  
- Minor suggestions: add statistical tests (e.g., bootstrap) for log-likelihood differences to strengthen comparisons.  
- Minor suggestions: cite formal semantics work on question-asking (e.g., Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).  
- Summary: high-quality, innovative paper; accept pending minor revisions addressing generalizability and translation limitations.
