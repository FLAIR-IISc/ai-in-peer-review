This paper introduces a computational framework for modeling human question asking, representing questions as programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The authors posit that people construct questions by favoring concise programs that maximize information gain in a given context. They evaluate their model on a dataset of questions posed by humans playing a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating that it can predict human question choices and generate novel, plausible questions. The model incorporates features for informativeness (measured by Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance to the game state. Experiments show the full model, utilizing all features, outperforms variants lacking individual feature sets.

This work builds upon a rich history of research in active learning [21], which traditionally focuses on simple label requests, and extends it to the more nuanced domain of natural language questioning. It also relates to efforts in AI dialog systems [25, 1], though those systems typically rely on pre-defined question sets. The approach shares conceptual similarities with recent work on visual question generation [16, 22] but aims for goal-directed, context-sensitive questioning rather than open-ended generation. The central idea of modeling questions as programs resonates with earlier work on question meaning as partitions over possible worlds [6, 18, 9] and formalizing question answering as program execution [24, 12]. 

The paper is technically sound, with a clearly defined model and a well-executed experimental evaluation. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their dataset and the approximations made in the optimization process. The experimental setup, utilizing leave-one-out cross-validation, is reasonable for evaluating predictive performance. The inclusion of ablation studies, comparing the full model to variants with reduced feature sets, strengthens the analysis. The demonstration of novel question generation is a particularly noteworthy result.

However, the paper’s claim to provide insights into the cognitive mechanisms underlying human question asking is less convincing. While the model can *predict* human questions, the results regarding the relative importance of informativeness (EIG) and complexity are mixed. The log-likelihood results suggest some influence of EIG, but the correlation analysis shows comparable performance without it. This ambiguity limits the strength of the conclusions regarding how humans “balance” these factors, as the authors state. The sparse nature of the human data likely contributes to the noise in these results. Further, the model’s reliance on a pre-defined grammar and domain-specific knowledge raises questions about the generalizability of its findings to more open-ended questioning scenarios.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results. The inclusion of a supplementary materials section is helpful. However, the related work section could be strengthened by acknowledging relevant prior work that also explicitly models questions as programs with a trade-off between informativeness and complexity, such as Hawkins [2015](“Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.”) and Roberts [1996](“Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics.”). These works similarly frame question asking in terms of goal-oriented information seeking and utilize related concepts like expected information gain.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of modeling questions as executable programs within a probabilistic framework is a compelling and potentially impactful contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The model is well-defined, and the experimental evaluation is rigorous.
*   **Positive Results:** The model demonstrates promising predictive accuracy and the ability to generate novel questions.
*   **Potential Impact:** This work could contribute to the development of more intelligent and human-like AI systems capable of active learning through natural language interaction.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Cognitive Insight:** The conclusions regarding the cognitive mechanisms underlying human question asking are not strongly supported by the results.
*   **Domain Specificity:** The reliance on a specific game domain may limit the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The related work section could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding prior work on similar program-based approaches to question asking.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some limitations regarding its cognitive science claims and missing related work, this is a strong paper that presents a technically sound and novel approach to modeling question asking. The ability to generate plausible questions is particularly impressive. I believe the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and warrants acceptance. I recommend a score of **7/10**. A minor revision addressing the related work and tempering the claims regarding cognitive insights would further strengthen the paper.
