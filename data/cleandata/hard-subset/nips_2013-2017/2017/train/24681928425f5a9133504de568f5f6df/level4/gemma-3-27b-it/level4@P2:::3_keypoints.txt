- Introduces a computational model framing human questions as executable programs producing answers.  
- Model learns a probability distribution over compositional program space, favoring informative and concise questions.  
- Evaluated on human questions from a spatial reasoning (Battleship) game dataset.  
- Model predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions.  
- Demonstrates that both expected information gain (EIG) and question complexity influence question generation.  
- Technically sound, well-motivated, and builds on active learning literature.  
- Clear presentation, reproducible methodology, and provided code.  
- Inclusion of lesion/model variants clarifies contributions of informativeness, complexity, and answer type.  
- Cognitive-science claim—that people trade off informativeness and complexity—is under-supported by ambiguous results.  
- Log-likelihood analysis shows some EIG influence but with notable uncertainty (≈19% error chance via bootstrapping).  
- Correlation analysis shows little improvement from including EIG, weakening the cognitive claim.  
- Suggests need for larger datasets or more ecologically valid tasks to strengthen cognitive conclusions.  
- Omits related work linking questions to goals or computations (e.g., Hawkins 2015; QUD/Roberts 1996).  
- Acknowledging these prior works would improve contextualization of the core idea.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, technical rigor, empirical validation, and potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak cognitive interpretability and incomplete related-work coverage.  
- Overall: strong, valuable contribution; recommend acceptance with fixes to related work and tempered cognitive claims.
