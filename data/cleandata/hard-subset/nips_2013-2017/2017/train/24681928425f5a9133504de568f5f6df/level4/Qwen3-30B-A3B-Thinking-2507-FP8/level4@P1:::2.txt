

The authors present a computational model of human question asking, framing questions as formal programs that execute on possible world states to produce answers. This approach extends active learning by modeling questions as compositional programs rather than simple label requests, addressing a key limitation in prior work where most active learning algorithms focus on simple label queries. The paper makes a significant contribution by formalizing question generation as program synthesis within a probabilistic framework, drawing on linguistic compositionality and cognitive science principles. The model is evaluated on a dataset of human questions from a Battleship-style game where players infer hidden ship configurations through single-word-answer questions.

The paper's main strengths are its clear theoretical foundation, rigorous evaluation, and insightful analysis of human question generation. The authors effectively demonstrate that human question selection balances informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain) and complexity (measured via program length and grammar probability), resolving a key puzzle from prior work where informativeness alone poorly predicted human behavior. The model's quantitative evaluation through leave-one-out cross-validation shows that all key features contribute meaningfully, with complexity emerging as particularly important. The qualitative generation of novel, human-like questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") demonstrates the model's ability to produce creative, contextually appropriate questions beyond the training data.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making complex concepts accessible. The formalization of questions as lambda-calculus programs provides a principled framework for understanding question compositionality, while the grammar-based approach elegantly captures the open-ended nature of human questioning. The evaluation design is strong, using both quantitative prediction of human question frequencies and qualitative generation of novel questions. The authors thoughtfully address limitations, including the domain-specific nature of their grammar and the need for semantic rather than natural language processing.

However, the paper's narrow focus on the Battleship domain raises concerns about broader applicability. While the authors note that some grammar elements (logical connectives, set operations) are domain-general, the current implementation requires substantial domain knowledge engineering. This limits the approach's potential for immediate adoption in more complex, real-world settings that NIPS attendees might care about. The computational complexity of the model (requiring 150,000 samples per iteration) also suggests scalability challenges for larger domains.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to computational cognitive science by providing the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking, advancing our understanding of how people balance informational value and cognitive effort when formulating questions. This cognitive science contribution alone would justify publication in NIPS, but the broader machine learning community may question the approach's generalizability to more complex active learning scenarios. The authors could strengthen their case by more explicitly articulating concrete future applications where their method would provide unique advantages over existing approaches.

Minor suggestions: The authors could strengthen their statistical claims by performing nested model comparisons on the log-likelihood differences rather than relying on bootstrapping alone. Additionally, while the paper references relevant work in formal semantics (e.g., Groenendijk & Stokhof), it would benefit from engaging more deeply with the Questions under Discussion literature (e.g., Ginzburg, 1995; Rojas-Esponda, 2013), which addresses how question contexts shape question generation.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions to computational cognitive science. While the domain-specific nature of the current implementation may limit its immediate appeal to the broader NIPS audience, the core ideas about modeling question generation through program synthesis represent an important step toward more human-like question-asking systems. The paper's clarity, methodological rigor, and novel perspective on question asking make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that future work should address scalability to more complex domains.
