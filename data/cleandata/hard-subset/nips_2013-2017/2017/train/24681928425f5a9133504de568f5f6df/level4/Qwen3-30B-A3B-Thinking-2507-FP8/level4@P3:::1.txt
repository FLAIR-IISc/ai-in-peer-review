

This paper presents a computational model of human question asking in an active learning context, framed as program generation. The authors model questions as formal programs that, when executed on possible world states, output answers. They develop a probabilistic generative model over a compositional space of questions, using a grammar-based approach to capture question structure, and evaluate their model on a dataset of human questions generated during a Battleship-like game. The model incorporates features for question informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (via grammar probability), answer type, and relevance to the game domain.

The paper makes a clear connection to related work in cognitive science and computational linguistics. It acknowledges the limitations of existing active learning approaches that only query for labels or use canned questions, and contrasts with recent work on visual question generation that requires large datasets and lacks goal-directed flexibility. The authors position their work as a bridge between program synthesis (e.g., Lake et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2015) and human question asking, providing a formal framework that captures compositionality and computability of questions. This relates to foundational work in cognitive science on the language of thought (Fodor, 1975) and recent advances in probabilistic program induction (Lake et al., 2015).

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The authors provide a clear, principled framework for modeling question asking as program generation. The probabilistic model is well-motivated and the features (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance) are grounded in cognitive considerations. The experimental design using cross-validation on a carefully collected dataset is appropriate.

2. **Clarity of Contribution:** The paper clearly articulates the problem, the proposed solution, and how it differs from previous work. The grammar-based representation of questions is well-explained, and the use of a controlled game environment provides a clean testbed for evaluating question generation.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The model successfully predicts human question frequencies across contexts (Spearman's ρ = 0.64) and generates novel, meaningful questions not present in the training data. The ablation studies (lesioning key features) provide convincing evidence for the importance of both informativeness and complexity in human question selection.

4. **Novelty:** While the idea of modeling questions as programs has been explored in other contexts (e.g., for question answering), the paper's focus on open-ended, goal-directed question asking in a domain with limited prior knowledge is novel. The grammar-based approach to capturing compositionality in question generation is particularly noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Scope and Significance:** The paper's application to a highly constrained Battleship game with a fixed set of ship parameters significantly limits the broader impact of the work. While the game is a good testbed, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate how this approach would scale to more complex, real-world domains where question asking is essential (e.g., scientific discovery, medical diagnosis). The significance claim that this work "represents a step toward more capable active learning machines" is overstated without evidence of application to more challenging domains.

2. **Lack of Comparative Analysis:** The paper compares model variants but does not compare its approach to alternative methods for question generation. For instance, how would a neural network-based approach (e.g., sequence-to-sequence models) perform on this dataset? How does the grammar-based approach compare to recent work on program synthesis (e.g., in the context of visual reasoning)?

3. **Overly Simplistic Model of Informativeness:** The Expected Information Gain calculation assumes a uniform prior over possible board configurations, which may not reflect how humans actually form beliefs about the game state. The paper doesn't address how the model would handle more complex belief structures or incorporate human cognitive biases that influence question selection.

4. **Implementation Details:** The optimization procedure relies on importance sampling with a large number of samples (150,000) and gradient ascent for 100,000 iterations. While this is technically sound, the computational cost of this approach is not discussed, and it's unclear how this would scale to more complex domains with larger hypothesis spaces.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling human question asking as program generation.
- It provides a clear, compositional representation of questions that captures important aspects of human linguistic behavior.
- The empirical results demonstrate that the model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, meaningful questions.
- The ablation studies provide strong evidence for the importance of multiple factors (informativeness, complexity, etc.) in question selection.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The application domain (Battleship game) is too constrained to demonstrate broad significance for the NIPS community.
- The model's reliance on domain-specific grammar rules limits its generalizability to other question-asking contexts.
- The paper does not adequately address how this approach would scale to more complex, real-world question-asking scenarios.
- The significance claim overstates the contribution without demonstrating broader applicability or impact.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes an interesting contribution to the modeling of human question asking as a computational process, with a principled approach that connects to cognitive science and program synthesis literature. The technical execution is sound, and the empirical results are convincing within the constrained domain of the Battleship game.

However, the paper's limited scope and lack of demonstration of broader applicability significantly weaken its significance for the NIPS community. The work represents a valuable step in understanding question asking within a controlled setting, but it does not yet provide a compelling path toward more human-like question asking in real-world machine learning systems. To strengthen the paper, the authors should either:
1) Expand the evaluation to a more complex domain where question asking is essential (e.g., scientific discovery, medical diagnosis), or
2) More thoroughly discuss the limitations of the current approach and provide a clearer roadmap for extending it to more challenging scenarios.

Given the current state of the paper, I would lean toward a borderline acceptance (weak accept), as the technical contribution is sound and the approach is novel within the specific context, but the significance for the broader NIPS community is limited by the narrow application domain. The paper would be stronger with more concrete discussion of how this work could be extended to more complex settings where question asking has real-world impact.
