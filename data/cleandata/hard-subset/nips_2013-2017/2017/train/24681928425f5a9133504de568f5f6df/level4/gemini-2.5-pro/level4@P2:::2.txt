This paper presents a computational model of human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The authors propose that meaningful, goal-directed questions can be represented as programs in a compositional language based on lambda calculus. The study is situated in a "Battleship"-like game where participants ask natural language questions to resolve ambiguity about a hidden board state.

The core of the model is a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) that defines a vast space of possible questions (programs). A log-linear model is then used to define a probability distribution over this space, favoring questions that are both informative and simple. The "goodness" of a question is a learned weighted combination of several features: its informativeness (modeled as Expected Information Gain, EIG), its complexity (negative log probability under a uniform PCFG, penalizing longer programs), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, number, color), and its relevance to the game.

The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions that were manually translated into the formal programming language. Through leave-one-out cross-validation and model lesion studies, the authors demonstrate that their full model provides the best fit to the human data. A key finding is that question complexity is the most critical predictive factor, even more so than informativeness, thus offering a compelling explanation for prior findings where EIG alone was a poor predictor of human queries. The paper also qualitatively demonstrates the model's generative capacity by showcasing novel, human-like questions it produces for specific game contexts.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength is its novel and ambitious conceptualization of question asking as program synthesis. This work forges a strong connection between active learning, computational cognitive science, and formal linguistics. It pushes the active learning paradigm beyond simple label queries towards a much richer and more human-like form of information seeking. This perspective has the potential to inspire new research directions in creating more sophisticated and flexible learning agents.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound and rigorously evaluated. The authors provide a detailed quantitative analysis, systematically comparing their full model against several lesioned versions to demonstrate the contribution of each component (informativeness, complexity, and answer type). The finding that complexity plays a dominant role is a significant scientific contribution, clarifying a puzzle in the cognitive science literature on self-directed learning. The qualitative evaluation, showcasing the model's ability to generate creative and contextually relevant new questions, further strengthens the paper.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the complex model, its theoretical motivations, and the experimental results, making the work accessible and compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability and Generalizability:** The most significant limitation of the current work is its scalability and generalizability. The framework relies heavily on a hand-crafted grammar tailored specifically to the "Battleship" domain. Furthermore, the human questions from the dataset had to be manually translated into the formal language, a process that would be a major bottleneck in any practical application. The authors note that the method is already computationally strained in this relatively small, well-defined domain. It remains unclear how this approach could be extended to more complex, open-ended domains of interest to the broader NIPS community without substantial, domain-specific engineering for each new task.
*   **Significance for the Broader NIPS Audience:** While the paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to computational cognitive science, its immediate impact on the core machine learning community is less apparent. The reliance on a bespoke semantic representation and grammar distances it from current end-to-end learning paradigms. The paper would be significantly stronger if it could outline a more concrete roadmap for how these foundational ideas might be integrated with modern ML techniques (e.g., neural semantic parsing) or applied to a practical AI problem where rich, compositional queries are essential.

**Conclusion:**

This is a well-executed and thought-provoking paper that presents an innovative formal model of question asking. It makes a solid contribution to the cognitive science of learning and offers a fresh perspective for the active learning community. However, its practical applicability is limited by its reliance on a hand-crafted grammar and its demonstrated use in only a constrained, toy domain. While the challenges of scaling are significant, the ideas are interesting enough to be of value to the NIPS community.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions:**

1.  To strengthen the quantitative comparison, you could perform nested model comparisons (e.g., using likelihood ratio tests) between the full and lesioned models. This would allow you to report formal statistical significance for the contribution of each feature set (informativeness, complexity, etc.).
2.  For future work, especially on sequential question asking, you might find the "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) framework from formal semantics and pragmatics to be highly relevant. This literature provides sophisticated models of how dialogue context constrains and guides question asking. The following are good starting points:
    *   Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy, 18*(5), 459-527.
    *   Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17*, 131-139.
