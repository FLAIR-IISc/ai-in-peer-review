Summary and relation to prior work
The paper models human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a small, typed, lambda-calculus/LISP-inspired language, with a hand-crafted probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) over the Battleship domain (colored, contiguous shapes hidden on a 6×6 grid). Executing a program on a hypothesized world state yields an answer of a specific type (Boolean, number, color, or location). A log-linear model assigns probabilities to questions using features for expected information gain (EIG), complexity (−log probability under the PCFG with uniform rule choices), answer type, and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit by maximizing the likelihood of human questions via importance sampling over the grammar and leave-one-context-out cross-validation. The model predicts the distribution of questions humans ask in a given partially revealed board, and can sample novel, human-like questions.

This work bridges several threads: active learning (e.g., label queries vs. richer queries), semantic/pragmatic accounts of questions as partitions over worlds, and program-based models in cognitive science and semantic parsing. Compared to contemporary dialog and question-generation systems, it provides a goal-directed, compositional account with a precise utility model. Relative to prior cognitive work showing that EIG alone poorly predicts human questions, this paper explains why: complexity costs and answer-type preferences are crucial.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized exposition with a principled formalization of questions as executable, typed programs. The grammar captures compositionality and supports productivity beyond a fixed template inventory.
- Sound probabilistic framework: the use of a log-linear model with interpretable features (informativeness, complexity, type, relevance) is appropriate and aligns with cognitive theories of resource-rationality.
- Empirical evaluation on a challenging, open-ended dataset of human questions in context. Leave-one-context-out experiments and model lesioning show that all feature families matter, with complexity being especially important.
- Ability to generate novel, context-sensitive questions not present in training, illustrating the benefits of compositionality and utility-aware sampling.
- Useful analysis connecting model energies with human frequencies (average Spearman 0.64), and bootstrap comparisons of variants.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Domain and scalability: The grammar and semantics are hand-engineered for a small, well-structured domain. Inference is computationally heavy (large importance samples; removal of some contexts and rules due to tractability). It is unclear how the approach scales to richer environments (vision, language, or scientific discovery) without substantial engineering and additional approximation.
- Language interface: The model operates on meaning representations; mapping from natural language to programs is assumed rather than learned. This limits immediate applicability to real-world dialog or interactive learning.
- Evaluation breadth: Quantitative evaluation is confined to held-out contexts within the same domain and to variants of the proposed model. Comparisons to alternative families (e.g., retrieval/template baselines, seq2seq conditioned on context, or neural semantic parsers trained to predict human questions) would clarify the benefit of the program-based approach.
- Approximation and stability: Likelihood estimates and normalizing constants rely on importance sampling over a vast space; variance and sensitivity analyses are limited. Dropping contexts and rules (e.g., draw) introduces dataset mismatch and potential bias.
- Measure of fit: Correlations are moderate and constrained by sparsity (many once-asked questions). More distributional metrics (e.g., KL divergence or calibration of predicted ranks) and significance tests for lesion comparisons would strengthen claims.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with reasonable modeling choices and careful feature design. The Bayesian ideal observer and EIG computation are standard and well motivated. However, some practical limitations (approximate normalization, restricted contexts) and the hand-engineered grammar suggest this is a substantial but domain-bound demonstration.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well structured. The formalism, grammar, features, and optimization are described in sufficient detail to understand and (in principle) reproduce, given the grammar and code.

Originality
Modeling question generation as program synthesis within a utility-sensitive, probabilistic framework is original in this setting and advances beyond prior active learning and question-generation approaches that lack compositional semantics or goal-directed utilities.

Significance
For computational cognitive science, the paper provides a compelling account of how humans trade off informativeness and complexity when asking questions. For the broader NIPS audience, significance depends on scalability and integration with language and perception. The core ideas could inspire richer query mechanisms in active learning and interactive AI, but the present evaluation remains domain-specific.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled formulation of question asking as program generation with an explicit utility-complexity trade-off.
- Solid evidence that compositionality, complexity costs, and answer-type preferences are necessary to predict human behavior.
- Generates creative, human-like questions, demonstrating productivity beyond memorization.
- Clear writing and careful experiments; contributes to understanding human inquiry and to the design of query-capable agents.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong reliance on hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar and semantics; unclear path to scaling.
- Limited comparison to alternative modeling paradigms commonly used by the NIPS community.
- Computational strain leading to dropped rules/contexts and approximate normalization, leaving uncertainty about robustness.

Suggestions and minor comments
- Provide statistical tests for model comparisons. A nested model comparison using the approximated log-likelihoods (with bootstrap or paired tests over held-out contexts) would quantify significance of lesion effects.
- Include stronger baselines: (a) retrieval/template models; (b) seq2seq question generators conditioned on board features; (c) a neural semantic parser trained to predict human questions from context.
- Report variance of importance sampling estimates (e.g., effective sample sizes, confidence intervals for Z) and sensitivity to the proposal distribution.
- Discuss pathways to scaling: amortized inference over programs, neurally guided PCFGs, learned priors over complexity from human data, or integration with semantic parsers (e.g., Wang et al., 2015).
- For sequential questioning, consider connections to formal semantics/pragmatics, e.g., Questions Under Discussion and related dialog models: Ginzburg (1995); Rojas-Esponda (2013).
- Clarify the impact of removing the draw rule and dropping two contexts on the evaluation; consider reporting results with/without these components.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-executed, thought-provoking paper that advances our understanding of human question asking and offers a promising framework for richer query generation. I lean toward acceptance, especially on cognitive/computational grounds, while encouraging the authors to strengthen comparative baselines and articulate a clearer path to scaling beyond the Battleship domain.
