This paper proposes a cognitive model to predict which questions people will ask in a given belief state. The model calculates the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of several factors: informativeness (measured by Expected Information Gain, or EIG), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance (distinguishing between questions about the belief state and world-independent logical facts). The model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the goal is to locate ships on a grid. Ablation studies show that the full model outperforms models with fewer features in terms of log-likelihood on held-out human data. However, when evaluated on correlation with human judgments, a model without the EIG feature performs comparably to the full model.

The paper is technically sound.

I believe the work succeeds more as an AI contribution than as a cognitive science one. The authors claim their "results show how people balance informativeness and complexity," but I am not convinced the results strongly support this conclusion about human cognition. In the Battleship domain, the influence of EIG on human question-asking appears to be either minimal (based on the log-likelihood results, which a bootstrap analysis suggests have a 19% chance of being spurious) or non-existent (based on the correlation results). Given that the correlation data is noisy due to its sparsity, these findings are not robust enough to support general conclusions about how people balance these factors.

The paper is well-written and clear, and the methodology is described in sufficient detail to suggest the results are reproducible.

The paper could be strengthened by acknowledging relevant prior work. A central innovation presented is the idea to "model questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer." This formalism is conceptually similar to the notion of a "goal" in Hawkins (2015, "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers"), which itself builds on the concept of a Question-Under-Discussion (QUD) from Roberts (1996). Notably, the Hawkins model also employs a trade-off between expected information gain and question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain. Acknowledging this related work would better situate the paper's contributions.

Despite these criticisms, the paper's core approach is promising. Models that combine a compositional prior over questions with decision-theoretic principles like EIG are valuable for both AI and cognitive science. The primary contribution of this paper is demonstrating the feasibility of this approach for generating complex, compositional questions in a non-trivial domain. This is a significant step forward and highlights a promising direction for future research.
