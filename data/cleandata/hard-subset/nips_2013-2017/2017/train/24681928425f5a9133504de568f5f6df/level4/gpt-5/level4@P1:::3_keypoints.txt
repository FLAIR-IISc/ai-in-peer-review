- Proposal: Model open-ended question generation by representing questions as executable programs in a compositional language.  
- Grammar & features: Probabilistic grammar over programs; log-linear energy with features: expected information gain (EIG), complexity (−log prior), answer type, and relevance.  
- Training & inference: Trained with importance sampling to match human question distributions.  
- Evaluation: Tested in a Battleship 6×6 domain using leave-one-context-out evaluation where players ask one question per board.  
- Main quantitative result: Full model outperforms lesioned variants on held-out log-likelihood, though EIG’s contribution to correlation with human frequencies is weak.  
- Data limitations: Correlation analyses are noisy and constrained by sparse human data.  
- Generation capability: Model can sample novel, compositional questions not seen in training.  
- Relation to prior work: Connects to QUD (Roberts, 1996), Hawkins et al. (2015), and semantic parsing/program induction; needs clearer positioning relative to these.  
- Quality: Technically sound with clear specification, appropriate evaluation, and reproducible detail.  
- Clarity: Well written, organized, with helpful figures and examples.  
- Originality: Core ideas (questions as programs, EIG–complexity tradeoff) are not new, but novelty lies in scaling to a richer domain and generating questions.  
- AI significance: Strong contribution showing compositional question generation in a nontrivial setting.  
- Cognitive significance: Evidence for humans optimizing EIG vs. complexity is ambiguous and not decisive here.  
- Strengths: Clear executable representation, principled probabilistic framework, ability to generate human-like novel questions, and informative lesion analyses.  
- Weaknesses: Overstated cognitive claims, missing key citations, and noisy results due to sparse data.  
- Suggestions: Explicitly situate work with QUD/Hawkins, temper cognitive claims or add analyses isolating EIG, and address data sparsity or collect denser/more domains.  
- Pros for acceptance: Valuable AI demonstration, solid methodology, and useful for interactive systems.  
- Cons for acceptance: Limited conceptual novelty relative to prior work, cognitive claims exceed evidence, and omitted citations.  
- Overall: Demonstrates that a compositional program-based model with simple features can generate interesting, human-like questions in a realistic domain; cognitive implications remain tentative.
