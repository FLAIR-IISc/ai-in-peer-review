- Studies human question-asking in a "battleship" domain using a PCFG over lambda-calculus representations of single-word questions.  
- Scores question goodness as a linear combination of expected information gain, complexity (length/negative log PCFG probability), and answer type.  
- Uses previously published human data manually encoded into the grammar; evaluates with leave-one-out cross-validation and lesioned models.  
- Finds all factors significantly contribute to question goodness, with complexity being the most important predictor.  
- Presents novel questions generated by the model and provides thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluation.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-written and offers a novel integration of linguistic and cognitive theories.  
- Advances computational cognitive science by explaining why informativeness alone is a poor predictor—complexity and answer type matter.  
- Major limitation: unclear scalability—manual semantic encoding and computational cost are already strained in the small battleship domain.  
- To broaden appeal, authors should give a clear application scenario and demonstrate how the approach extends to richer domains.  
- Minor suggestion: use nested model comparison with log-likelihoods for more revealing, statistically testable analyses.  
- Minor suggestion: consider literature on sequential question-asking (Questions under Discussion); relevant refs include Ginzburg (1995) and Rojas-Esponda (2013).
