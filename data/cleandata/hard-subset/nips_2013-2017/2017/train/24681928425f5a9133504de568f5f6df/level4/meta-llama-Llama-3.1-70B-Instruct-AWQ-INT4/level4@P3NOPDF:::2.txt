The authors present an intriguing examination of human question asking in a "battleship" domain, where hidden colored shapes are located in a partially observable grid. They define a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) by hand for this domain, with the task of an agent asking a single-word-answer question that maximizes information gain about the board's state. The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are represented as statements in lambda calculus. Question goodness is evaluated as a linear function of its informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log probability under the PCFG with a uniform distribution over rewrite rules), and "answer type" (e.g., true/false or color).

The authors utilize human data from a previously published paper, which was manually coded into their grammar. They employ a leave-one-out cross-validation approach to compare lesioned versions of the model, finding that all factors contribute non-negligibly to the model's performance, with complexity being the most significant. Furthermore, the authors generate a series of novel questions, demonstrating the model's capabilities.

This work is situated within the important domain of question-asking in linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors provide a novel perspective, integrating modern linguistic and cognitive theories to investigate human question-asking behavior. This approach has the potential to foster innovative research in the active learning literature, enabling models to ask more sophisticated questions. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, with a thorough evaluation of the model from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this paper advances the field of self-directed learning. Previous research found that question informativeness was a poor predictor of human question asking, and this study resolves part of this discrepancy by showing that complexity and answer type are more significant factors. This contribution to the cognitive science literature may be sufficient to justify publication in NIPS.

However, there are significant limitations to this approach, which may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While it may be possible to automate the conversion of human question text to statements in some semantic logic, it is unclear how the approach could scale or extend to more complex domains. The battleship domain is relatively small, with only a few features, and the method is already computationally strained. Nevertheless, the ideas presented are intriguing, particularly in the context of active learning with more sophisticated questions. To increase its appeal to the broader community, the authors may need to provide a clear scenario where their technique is likely to be applicable and provide novel insights.

Minor comments:

1. A nested model comparison with the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models could provide statistical significance tests, potentially revealing more insights.
2. For sequential question asking, there are other models within the linguistics literature, particularly in formal semantics and pragmatics. The Questions under Discussion literature may be of interest, with relevant references including:
Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131-139.
