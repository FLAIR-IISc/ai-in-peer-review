The paper proposes a cognitive model that predicts which questions people ask in particular contexts (belief states). In this model, the unnormalized log-probability of a question is a weighted sum of four factors: informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (approximately question length), answer type, and “relevance” (whether the question targets the belief state or concerns world-independent logical facts). The model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the task is to infer the locations of ships (clusters of grid cells) on a grid. The full model, including all features, outperforms lesioned variants with fewer features on held-out human data in terms of log-likelihood. However, in correlational analyses with human judgments, the model that omits EIG performs about as well as the full model.

The paper appears technically sound overall.

I see it as more successful as an AI contribution than as a cognitive science contribution. The paper claims that “our model predicts what question people will ask” and that “from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity.” I am not convinced that the results substantively illuminate how people trade off these factors. In the Battleship task, the evidence suggests that people are either weakly influenced by EIG (if we trust the log-likelihood improvements, which bootstrap estimates indicate have a 19% chance of being spurious) or not influenced by EIG at all (if we rely on the correlation analyses, which are noisy due to sparse human data). Taken together, I do not think these results support strong conclusions about how people balance informativeness and complexity in general.

The paper is clearly written, and I believe I could reproduce the results.

However, some prior work is missing. One apparent innovation is to “model questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer.” This closely matches the notion of a “goal” used in Hawkins 2015 (“Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.”), which builds on the question-under-discussion (QUD) framework introduced by Roberts 1996 (“Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics.”). The Hawkins questioner model also trades off expected information gain against question complexity, though it is applied to a simpler domain.

I find this class of models—those with a compositional prior over questions that choose among alternatives based on EIG and other factors—highly plausible as components in both cognitive and AI systems, and I expect they will see broader use. In my view, the main contribution of this paper is to demonstrate that one can generate interesting, compositional questions in a nontrivial domain using this approach.
