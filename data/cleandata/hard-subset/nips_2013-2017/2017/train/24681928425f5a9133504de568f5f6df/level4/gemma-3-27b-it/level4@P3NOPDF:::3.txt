This paper presents a cognitive model designed to predict question selection based on underlying belief states. The model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of four features: informativeness (measured as expected information gain, or EIG), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance – representing whether the question pertains to the current belief state or solely to world-independent facts. The authors apply this model to the Battleship domain, where the goal is to locate hidden ships within a grid. Their results demonstrate that the full model, incorporating all four features, achieves superior performance in predicting held-out human question choices (in terms of log-likelihood) compared to ablated versions with fewer features. However, the correlation between model predictions and human judgments was comparable for the full model and a version omitting EIG.

The paper is technically sound and clearly written; I believe the results could be reproduced. However, while the work represents a valuable contribution to AI, I am less convinced of its impact on cognitive science. The authors claim the model "predicts what question people will ask" and reveals "how people balance informativeness and complexity." Yet, the evidence supporting these claims feels weak. Within the Battleship domain, the findings suggest people are either minimally influenced by EIG (if we accept the log-likelihood results, which have a 19% probability – based on bootstrap estimates – of being spurious) or entirely unaffected by it (as suggested by the noisy correlation results stemming from sparse human data). This ambiguity prevents strong conclusions about the cognitive processes underlying the trade-off between informativeness and complexity.

A significant omission in the related work section concerns prior research on question generation as goal-directed behavior. The authors position their approach as novel in "modeling questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer.” This concept directly aligns with Hawkins’ (2015) “Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers,” which frames questions as “goals” and builds upon Roberts’ (1996) foundational work on the “question under discussion” (QUD) in discourse pragmatics. Furthermore, Hawkins’ questioner model also employs EIG and balances it against question complexity, albeit within a simpler domain. Acknowledging these precedents would provide crucial context for the paper's contribution. 

Despite this oversight, I recognize the value of this modeling approach. A compositional prior over questions, coupled with selection based on EIG and other factors, is a sensible component for both cognitive and AI systems, and I anticipate its wider adoption. I believe the primary contribution of this paper lies in demonstrating the feasibility of generating meaningful compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this framework. It successfully showcases a practical application of this approach and suggests its potential for more complex scenarios.
