- Paper models human question-asking in a K-ary answer "battleship" domain using a manually defined PCFG as a prior over questions.  
- Questions are formalized as lambda-calculus statements and evaluated by a linear score combining informativeness, complexity, and answer type.  
- Complexity is measured by question length or negative log probability under the PCFG with uniform rewrites.  
- Human question data from a prior study was manually encoded into the grammar for training/evaluation.  
- Leave-one-out cross-validation and lesioned-model comparisons show all factors matter, with complexity the most influential.  
- The model generates a set of novel questions beyond the human dataset.  
- Paper bridges linguistic and cognitive theories, offering a novel perspective on question-asking and potential inspiration for active learning.  
- Well-written and clearly presented with thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluation.  
- Advances computational cognitive science by explaining why informativeness alone previously predicted human behavior poorly, implicating complexity and question-space size.  
- Limitations: small, simplified domain (battleship), unclear scalability to more complex domains, and computational intensity.  
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods for statistical significance testing.  
- Minor suggestion: consider linguistics literature on sequential question-asking (Questions under Discussion), e.g., Ginzburg (1995) and Rojas-Esponda (2013).
