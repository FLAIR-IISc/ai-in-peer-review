Summary:
The paper proposes a cognitive model that predicts which questions people ask in specific contexts (belief states). The unnormalized log probability of a question is modeled as a weighted sum of four features: informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (roughly question length), answer type, and “relevance” (whether the question refers to the current belief state or to world-independent logical facts). The model is evaluated in a Battleship domain where the task is to locate ships (clusters of grid elements) on a grid.

Empirically, the full model with all features achieves higher held-out log-likelihood on human data than lesioned variants that remove individual features. However, in terms of correlation with human judgments, a model that omits EIG performs about as well as the full model.

Technical assessment:
The paper appears technically sound, and the methodology is clearly described.

Cognitive science claims:
I find the work more compelling as an AI contribution than as a cognitive science result. The paper claims that “our model predicts what question people will ask” and that “from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity.” In the Battleship domain, however, the evidence for a cognitive trade-off is limited. Depending on which evaluation one emphasizes, people appear to be influenced by EIG only modestly (if we credit the log-likelihood comparisons, which the authors estimate via bootstrap have a 19% chance of favoring the wrong model) or not at all (if we focus on correlations with human judgments, which are roughly on par for the EIG-free and full models and are themselves noisy due to sparse human data). These results do not, in my view, support strong conclusions about how people generally balance informativeness and complexity.

Clarity and reproducibility:
The paper is well written and clear. I expect I could reproduce the results based on the information provided.

Relation to prior work:
The paper omits some relevant prior work. Modeling questions as programs that, when executed on a possible world, return an answer aligns with the notion of a “goal” in Hawkins (2015, “Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.”), which itself builds on the question-under-discussion (QUD) framework introduced by Roberts (1996, “Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics.”). Hawkins also models a questioner that trades off EIG against question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain. This connection should be acknowledged and discussed.

Overall assessment:
A compositional prior over questions combined with EIG and related features is a sensible and promising approach for both cognitive and AI models. The main contribution here is to demonstrate that this framework can generate interesting, compositional questions in a non-trivial domain. I expect this line of work to be influential and to see broader use, though stronger cognitive claims will require more decisive evidence about how people trade off informativeness, complexity, and other factors.
