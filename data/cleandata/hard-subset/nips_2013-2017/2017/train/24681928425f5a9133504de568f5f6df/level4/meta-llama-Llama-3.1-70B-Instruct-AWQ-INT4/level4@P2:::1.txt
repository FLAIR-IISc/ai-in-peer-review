This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling human question asking abilities in a game of Battleship, where players ask questions that can be composed into a logical language with Montagovian semantics. The authors develop a probabilistic model of question generation, which uses a log-linear model over four features of the questions: expected information gain (informativeness), answer type (Boolean or numerical), complexity, and relevance to the game board.

The paper's central hypothesis is that active machine learning concepts can be generalized to operate over a complex, compositional space of programs that are evaluated over possible worlds. The authors argue that this approach can lead to more capable active learning machines that can ask rich and interesting questions.

The proposed model is evaluated on a data set of 605 natural language questions asked by human participants in an information-search game. The results show that the model can predict the distribution of questions people asked in novel scenarios and generate genuinely novel questions that were not present in the data set.

The paper's strengths include its novel approach to modeling human question asking abilities, its ability to predict human question frequencies, and its capacity to generate novel questions. However, the task is highly constrained and logically unambiguous, which may limit the paper's interest to the NIPS community.

In terms of originality, the paper's ideas are not entirely new, as they build upon existing work in active learning and compositional semantics. However, the authors' application of these ideas to the task of question asking is innovative and contributes to the field of cognitive science.

The paper's clarity is generally good, with clear explanations of the proposed model and its evaluation. However, some sections could be improved, such as the related work section, which could provide more context and comparison to other approaches.

The paper's significance is moderate, as it contributes to the understanding of human question asking abilities and proposes a novel approach to modeling these abilities. However, the task's simplicity and logical unambiguity may limit the paper's impact on the field.
