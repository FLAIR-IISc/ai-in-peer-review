- Presents a computational model of human question-asking in a Battleship-like game using a hand-crafted PCFG that generates compositional lambda-calculus question programs, scored by a log-linear model with features: Expected Information Gain, complexity, and answer type.  
- Trained on a dataset of human questions manually translated into the formal language.  
- Evaluated with leave-one-out cross-validation and lesion studies; informativeness, complexity, and answer type all help predict human questions, with complexity being most important.  
- Qualitatively demonstrates the model can generate novel, human-like questions.  
- Strength: Very clear, well-written, and connects active learning with cognitive science and linguistics.  
- Strength: Innovative approach modeling questions as programs from a compositional grammar, offering a powerful framework for structured query generation.  
- Strength: Significant cognitive-science contribution explaining why complexity constrains human question asking more than informativeness alone.  
- Strength: Thorough evaluation combining quantitative comparisons and qualitative generative analysis.  
- Weakness: Scalability and generalizability concerns due to hand-crafted grammar tailored to the Battleship domain.  
- Weakness: Manual translation of natural-language questions into the formal language; automating this is a major challenge.  
- Weakness: Computationally intensive method applied to a simple domain raises doubts about scaling to larger state spaces or more complex structures.  
- Weakness: Needs a clearer path to applications in broader NIPS-relevant domains (e.g., RL, robotics, scientific discovery).  
- Minor comment: Suggests nested model comparison using log-likelihoods to test statistical significance of feature contributions.  
- Minor comment: Recommends exploring the Questions Under Discussion (QUD) literature for sequential question-asking, with example references.
