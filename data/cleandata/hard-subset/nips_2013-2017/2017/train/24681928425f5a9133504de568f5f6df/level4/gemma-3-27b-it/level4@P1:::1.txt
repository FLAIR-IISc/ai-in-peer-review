This paper investigates question asking as a form of program generation, modeling human questions in a Battleship-like game as formal programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The authors propose a probabilistic model to predict the questions humans will ask, and to generate novel, human-like questions. The model leverages a compositional grammar and is evaluated using a dataset of natural language questions collected from human players. Results show that the model successfully predicts human question frequencies and can generate novel questions, with both question informativeness (measured by Expected Information Gain) and complexity playing important roles.

The motivation for this work – understanding human question asking as a means to improve active learning – is well-grounded. The approach of framing questions as programs, allowing for compositional semantics, is a natural and potentially powerful one. This builds on previous work in cognitive science that views concepts and reasoning as forms of computation [4, 15, 14]. The paper also appropriately positions itself within the broader landscape of active learning [21] and dialog systems [25, 1], highlighting the limitations of existing approaches in handling richer, more nuanced queries. Related work on visual question generation [16, 23, 11] and visual question answering [22] is also well-cited.

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear articulation of the problem, the elegant formulation of questions as programs, and the experimental validation of the model against human data. The use of a probabilistic grammar allows for both prediction and generation of questions, demonstrating a degree of creativity. The ablation study, comparing different model variants, effectively demonstrates the importance of both informativeness and complexity in generating human-like questions. The inclusion of examples of generated questions (Figure 3) provides valuable qualitative insight.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the task is well-defined, its simplicity might limit the generalizability of the findings. The Battleship domain, while providing a controlled environment, may not fully capture the complexities of real-world question asking. The reliance on a pre-defined grammar, while enabling computational tractability, introduces a degree of artificiality. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but it's worth emphasizing that the degree to which the grammar accurately reflects human cognitive processes remains an open question. Additionally, the connection between the chosen features (informativeness, complexity, answer type) and underlying cognitive mechanisms could be further elaborated.  The analysis could be strengthened by comparing the model’s performance to stronger baselines beyond the ablated variants (e.g., a simple n-gram model).  The discussion on how much domain knowledge is *required* versus preserved from the current architecture could also be expanded.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of question asking as program generation.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict and generate human-like questions.
*   Clear articulation of the problem and a well-defined experimental setup.
*   Insightful ablation study highlighting the importance of informativeness and complexity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The simplicity of the task may limit generalizability.
*   Reliance on a pre-defined grammar introduces a degree of artificiality.
*   The connection to underlying cognitive mechanisms could be more thoroughly explored.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of active learning and cognitive modeling. While the task is relatively simple, the proposed approach is novel and the results are promising. I believe the paper warrants acceptance, with the caveat that the limitations should be acknowledged and future work should explore more complex and realistic scenarios.
