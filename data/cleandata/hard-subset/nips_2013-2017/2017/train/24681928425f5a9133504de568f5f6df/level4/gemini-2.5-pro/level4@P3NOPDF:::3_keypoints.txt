- Proposes a cognitive model predicting which questions people ask from a belief state, with unnormalized log-probability as a weighted sum of four features: EIG (informativeness), complexity (question length), answer type, and relevance (state vs. logical facts).
- Evaluated in the Battleship domain; the full four-feature model achieves higher held-out log-likelihood than ablated variants.
- The contribution of EIG (informativeness) is ambiguous: removing EIG yields similar correlation with human judgments, and log-likelihood gains from EIG are marginal with ~19% chance of being noise.
- Strength: shows viability of a compositional, program-synthesis approach to question generation in a non-trivial domain; technically sound and apparently reproducible.
- Weakness: empirical results are mixed/noisy, so claims about human balancing of informativeness and complexity are not well-supported.
- Relation to prior work: should engage with Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996, QUD), which formalize similar EIG–complexity trade-offs and notions of questions-as-goals.
- Overall: solid AI/engineering contribution applying program synthesis to a complex task, but cognitive-science conclusions are overstated given the evidence.
