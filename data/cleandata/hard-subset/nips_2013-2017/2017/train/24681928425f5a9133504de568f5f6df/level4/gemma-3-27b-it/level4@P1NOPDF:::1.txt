This paper investigates reproducing user questions in a Battleship game, but replaces direct queries about individual squares with questions posed in an artificial language possessing compositional, Montagovian semantics. A log-linear model is employed, utilizing four simple features of each question – expected information gain (informativeness), answer type (Boolean or numerical), and whether the question pertains to board features at all. 

The high-level motivation, framed within active learning, is reasonable. However, the ideas presented feel rather obvious, and the task itself is very simple, highly constrained, and logically unambiguous. Consequently, I do not believe this paper will be of significant interest to the NIPS community.

The paper’s core contribution lies in formulating the Battleship questioning strategy as a problem of compositional semantics and learning to map from game state to questions expressed in this language. However, the simplicity of the game and the constrained nature of the artificial language significantly limit the generalizability and impact of the work. The authors demonstrate that a log-linear model can effectively learn to ask reasonable questions, but this result doesn’t represent a substantial advancement over existing active learning or question generation techniques. The features used – information gain, answer type, and board relevance – are intuitive and commonly employed, and their combination doesn’t introduce novel insights.

Specifically, the compositional semantics, while theoretically interesting, appears tailored to the Battleship domain and doesn’t address the complexities of natural language understanding. The artificial language avoids ambiguity by design, a feature absent in real-world questioning scenarios. This simplification undermines the potential to draw broader conclusions applicable to more challenging active learning problems involving natural language.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation, while thorough for the chosen task, lacks comparison to stronger baselines. The authors compare against a uniform random strategy and a greedy information gain approach. However, more sophisticated active learning strategies or recent advancements in question generation could provide a more informative benchmark. The absence of such comparisons weakens the claim that the proposed model offers a meaningful improvement.

I am also concerned about the limited scope of the reported results. The paper focuses exclusively on a single, well-defined game. Exploring variations in the game’s complexity or evaluating the approach on different, related tasks would strengthen the conclusions.

In summary, while the paper presents a technically sound application of log-linear modeling to a constrained active learning problem, it lacks novelty and broad appeal. The simplicity of the task and the artificial nature of the language limit its relevance to the broader NIPS audience. I recommend rejection.
