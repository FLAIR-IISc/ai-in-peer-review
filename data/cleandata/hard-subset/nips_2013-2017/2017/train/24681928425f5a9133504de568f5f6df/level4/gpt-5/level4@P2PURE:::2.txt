The authors study human question-asking with K-ary answers. They hand-specify a PCFG for a Battleship-like domain with hidden, colored ships on a partially observable grid; some tiles are revealed as containing part of a ship of a specific color or as empty. The agent’s task is to pose a single-word-answer question that yields as much information as possible about the board state. The PCFG provides a prior over questions, which are represented as lambda-calculus expressions. Question quality is scored as a linear function of (i) informativeness, measured as expected information gain, (ii) complexity, measured by length or by the negative log-probability of being generated by the PCFG under uniform rewrite-rule probabilities, and (iii) answer type (e.g., Boolean vs. color). Human data from a prior study were hand-encoded into the grammar. Using leave-one-out cross-validation with lesioned variants of the model, the authors find that each factor contributes non-negligibly, with complexity clearly dominant. They also present a set of novel questions.

Question-asking is central to linguistics and hypothesis testing. This paper offers an innovative synthesis of modern linguistic and cognitive theory to analyze how people ask questions, with the potential to spur active-learning research in which models ask richer questions. The paper is very clear and well written, and the model is evaluated appropriately from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, the work advances self-directed learning. Prior work found that informativeness was a poor predictor of human question selection. This follow-up helps explain why: informativeness is secondary to complexity and may also be affected by the size of the question space available to the model. Thus, the paper makes a substantive contribution to cognitive science, which could be sufficient to justify publication at NIPS.

However, the approach has notable limitations that may constrain its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. Even if mapping natural-language questions to a formal semantic representation could be automated, it is unclear how the method would scale or extend to domains of general interest. The Battleship domain is small, with few features, and the current method already appears computationally strained. The core ideas—active learning with richer question types—are compelling, but the authors should articulate a concrete, broadly relevant scenario where their technique is feasible and yields novel insights.

Minor comments:
1) Although it may not be highly revealing, you could perform nested model comparisons using the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models to obtain tests of statistical significance.
2) For sequential question-asking, there are relevant models in formal semantics and pragmatics, especially from the Questions Under Discussion literature. Pointers include:
- Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459–527.
- Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131–139.
