This paper proposes a computational model of human question-asking, framing the problem as one of program generation. The central idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. These programs, when executed on a possible world state (in this case, a game board configuration), return an answer. The authors define a probabilistic log-linear model over the space of possible programs, which is generated by a context-free grammar. The model favors programs that are both informative, as measured by Expected Information Gain (EIG), and structurally simple. This framework is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions from a "Battleship"-style information-seeking game. The work connects to the field of active learning, which typically involves simpler queries (e.g., label requests), by proposing a method for generating much richer, more structured queries.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel formulation of question-asking as program synthesis. This is an interesting and principled way to move beyond the simple queries common in active learning and could potentially inspire new directions in human-computer interaction and goal-oriented dialogue systems.
*   **Quality:** The proposed model is technically sound. The authors conduct a thorough evaluation, including ablation studies that convincingly demonstrate that both informativeness (EIG) and complexity are crucial components for predicting human questions. The qualitative results, showing the model can generate novel and sensible questions, are also compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The core concepts of representing questions as programs and using a probabilistic model for generation are explained effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Significance and Generalizability:** A key limitation is the reliance on a highly constrained and logically unambiguous experimental domain. The "Battleship" game provides a fully specified world state and a discrete set of objects and relations, which makes it straightforward to define a formal grammar and compute the EIG of any question. It is unclear how this approach would scale to more complex, less structured, real-world domains where defining the space of possible world states and the semantic primitives for a grammar would be significantly more challenging.
*   **Originality:** While the application to question-asking is novel, the core technical components (probabilistic context-free grammars, log-linear models, EIG) are well-established. The contribution lies in the combination and application of these techniques rather than in fundamental methodological innovation.
*   **Quality:** The model's performance relies heavily on a hand-engineered grammar specific to the game domain. This requirement for significant domain-specific knowledge engineering is a major practical hurdle for applying this method to new problems and limits its autonomy as a learning system. The paper acknowledges this limitation but its potential impact on the broader applicability of the framework could be understated.

In summary, the paper presents an elegant and thought-provoking framework for modeling a complex cognitive ability. However, the contribution's impact is constrained by the simplicity of the task domain and the reliance on significant hand-engineering. While the core idea is appealing, the paper provides limited evidence that the approach can generalize beyond the carefully constructed environment, which may limit its broader interest and utility to the NIPS community.
