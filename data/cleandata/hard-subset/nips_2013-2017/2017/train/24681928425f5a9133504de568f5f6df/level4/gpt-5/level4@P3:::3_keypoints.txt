- Proposes a probabilistic, compositional question-generation model that treats questions as executable, typed programs defined by a context-free grammar augmented with lambda, map, and set operators.  
- Uses a log-linear model with features for informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (grammar prior favoring short programs), answer type, and a relevance filter; parameters fit by likelihood maximization using importance sampling.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship-style dataset (605 mapped questions from 40 participants over partially revealed 6×6 boards) with leave-one-context-out cross-validation; full model outperforms ablations in held-out log-likelihood and generates novel human-like questions.  
- Builds on semantics/pragmatics (questions as partitions, QUD) and prior EIG-based models, extending them by providing an executable program grammar enabling combinatorial productivity and quantitative prediction in a richer domain.  
- Strength: precise, executable semantics with principled informativeness measure and a generative grammar that captures compositionality and productivity.  
- Strength: quantitative ablation evaluation shows complexity is critical; full model predicts human distributions better than lesioned variants.  
- Strength: model generates creative, contextually relevant novel questions; paper is clear and reproducible with detailed methods and released data/code.  
- Weakness: evidence for cognitive claims about informativeness is mixed—correlations and bootstrap tests show limited advantage for EIG over information-agnostic models.  
- Weakness: domain-specific engineering—grammar mixes general and Battleship-specific predicates, some contexts/rules were dropped for computational reasons, raising scalability/generalization concerns.  
- Weakness: reliance on importance sampling for training and normalization may affect stability and approximation quality.  
- Suggestion: include targeted behavioral tests manipulating complexity or prior uncertainty, report additional fit metrics (top-k, calibration), and consider hierarchical modeling for frequency data.  
- Suggestion: clarify reusable vs. domain-specific parts of the grammar, demonstrate or analyze transfer to a second domain, and compare alternative complexity measures.  
- Suggestion: report variance/effective sample sizes for importance sampling, convergence checks, and validate with alternative samplers or reduced-space controls.  
- Suggestion: better position contributions relative to QUD and Hawkins et al. (2015), highlighting what is genuinely novel (program-synthesis-based generation, typed functional language, richer quantitative predictions).  
- Overall: technically solid and promising for AI and cognitive modeling if authors temper EIG claims, strengthen prior-theory positioning, and add robustness/sensitivity analyses; recommended for acceptance contingent on these revisions.
