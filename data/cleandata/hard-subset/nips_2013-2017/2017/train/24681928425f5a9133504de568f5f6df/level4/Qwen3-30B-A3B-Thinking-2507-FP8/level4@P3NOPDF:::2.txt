

The paper investigates human question-asking behavior in a K-ary answer domain, focusing on a "battleship" task where agents query a partially observable grid containing hidden colored shapes. The authors hand-construct a PCFG to model question generation, defining questions as lambda calculus statements. Question quality is evaluated via a linear combination of three factors: (1) informativeness (expected information gain), (2) complexity (length or negative log probability under the PCFG with uniform rule weights), and (3) answer type (e.g., true/false vs. color). Human question data, drawn from prior work and hand-coded into the grammar, is used to evaluate model variants via leave-one-out cross-validation. Results show all factors contribute meaningfully, with complexity being the strongest predictor. The authors also generate novel questions demonstrating the model’s capabilities.

The work presents a compelling synthesis of computational linguistics, cognitive theory, and active learning. By unifying formal semantics with cognitive models of question-asking, the paper offers a novel framework for understanding human hypothesis testing. Its clarity, rigorous evaluation (quantitative via cross-validation and qualitative via novel question generation), and theoretical grounding make it a strong contribution to computational cognitive science. Critically, it resolves a key discrepancy in prior literature: while question informativeness alone poorly predicts human behavior, this model shows complexity and answer-type constraints are dominant factors—likely due to the combinatorial explosion of possible questions in the domain. This advances cognitive science by explaining *why* informativeness is secondary, potentially influencing active learning research.

However, the paper’s narrow scope limits its broader impact for NIPS. The battleship domain’s minimal feature space (few colors, small grid) and the PCFG’s hand-crafted nature present significant scalability challenges. The approach is computationally intensive even here, and extending it to domains with richer feature spaces (e.g., natural language or real-world perception) remains unaddressed. While the core idea—active learning with semantically rich questions—is promising, the authors fail to articulate a compelling use case for the wider ML community. Without a clear pathway to domains like robotics, healthcare, or large-scale NLP, the method risks appearing as a niche cognitive model rather than a broadly applicable technique. A concrete example (e.g., "this framework could enable efficient medical diagnosis by prioritizing high-complexity diagnostic questions over exhaustive symptom lists") would strengthen the argument.

Minor suggestions:  
1) A nested model comparison (using log-likelihoods of lesioned models) would provide statistical rigor for the factor importance claims.  
2) For future work on sequential questioning, the authors should engage with formal semantics literature (e.g., Ginzburg, 1995; Rojas-Esponda, 2013) on "Questions under Discussion," which models contextual question dynamics beyond the current scope.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to cognitive science and computational linguistics, and its theoretical insights are valuable. Yet, its limited applicability to core NIPS themes (scalable, general-purpose machine learning) weakens its case for publication. The authors must demonstrate a clear, non-trivial application domain where their method outperforms existing active learning approaches. Without this, the work remains an elegant but isolated cognitive model rather than a broadly influential ML contribution. The current version leans too heavily on a toy domain; a revised submission should explicitly address scalability and provide a concrete target application. As it stands, the paper’s cognitive science merits may justify publication in a specialized venue, but it does not yet meet NIPS’ bar for general ML impact.
