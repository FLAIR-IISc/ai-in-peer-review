The authors investigate human question asking in scenarios with K-ary answers, focusing on a “battleship” domain involving hidden, colored shapes on a partially observable grid. They define a Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) to model question structure, representing questions as statements in lambda calculus. Question quality is quantified using a composite function balancing informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (based on length and PCFG probability), and answer type. The model leverages a human dataset from prior work, which was encoded into their defined grammar. Through leave-one-out cross-validation on lesioned model versions, the authors demonstrate that each factor—informativeness, complexity, and answer type—contributes to question generation, with complexity being the most influential. The work also introduces a set of novel questions generated by the model.

This paper offers a compelling and innovative approach to understanding human question asking, integrating linguistic and cognitive theory to potentially advance active learning research with more sophisticated question-generating models. The presentation is exceptionally clear and the evaluation is thorough, encompassing both quantitative and qualitative analysis. From a computational cognitive science perspective, the work represents a significant contribution to the field of self-directed learning, clarifying why prior studies found informativeness alone to be a weak predictor of human question choice. Specifically, the authors show that complexity plays a crucial role, potentially explaining the discrepancy and accounting for the constrained space of possible questions. This contribution justifies consideration for publication at NIPS.

However, the approach’s scalability and generalizability raise concerns regarding its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While automated conversion of human questions into semantic representations might be feasible, extending this methodology to more complex, real-world domains is unclear. The current “battleship” domain is limited in feature space, and the computational demands are already significant. Despite these limitations, the core idea of active learning through richer questions is promising, and the authors could strengthen the paper by articulating clear, compelling scenarios where their technique could provide novel insights and be broadly applicable.

Minor comments:

1) A nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models could provide statistical tests of significance for their contributions.
2) For future work involving sequential question asking, the authors might consider relevant literature in linguistics, specifically formal semantics and pragmatics, such as research on Questions under Discussion. Relevant references include Ginzburg (1995) and Rojas-Esponda (2013).
