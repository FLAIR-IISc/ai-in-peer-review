- Presents a cognitive model that predicts question selection from belief states.  
- Defines unnormalized log-probability of a question as weighted sum of informativeness (EIG), complexity (question length), answer type, and relevance.  
- Applies the model in the Battleship domain (locating hidden ships on a grid).  
- Full model outperforms ablated variants on held-out human question data by log-likelihood.  
- Model without EIG shows comparable correlation with human judgments to the full model.  
- Paper is technically sound.  
- Contribution to cognitive science is unclear; evidence is insufficient to support claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity.  
- Results suggest human question selection may be weakly influenced or not influenced by EIG (19% bootstrap chance result could be spurious; correlations may be noisy due to sparse data).  
- Paper is well-written and reproducible.  
- Omits relevant prior work—similar ideas appear in Hawkins (2015) and the QUD framework of Roberts (1996).  
- The approach (compositional prior over questions with EIG-based selection) is promising for cognitive and AI applications.  
- Primary contribution is demonstrating feasibility of generating meaningful compositional questions in a non-trivial domain.
