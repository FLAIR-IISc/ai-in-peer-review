1. Frames human question-asking as probabilistic program generation, with questions as compositional programs that return answers when executed on world states.  
2. Uses a hand-crafted grammar and a log-linear model that balances Expected Information Gain (informativeness) and program simplicity (complexity).  
3. Evaluated on a Battleship-like dataset: predicts human question frequencies in held-out contexts and generates novel, plausible questions.  
4. Novelty: extends active learning to rich, compositional queries via probabilistic program synthesis, offering a conceptually elegant model.  
5. Technical quality: sound implementation; ablation studies show both EIG and simplicity are crucial, supporting the simplicity bias hypothesis.  
6. Compelling results: good correlation with human data and ability to generate productive, contextually relevant novel questions.  
7. Limited scope: tested in a constrained, unambiguous toy domain, raising scalability and real-world applicability concerns.  
8. Relies on a domain-specific, hand-crafted grammar, creating a significant knowledge-engineering bottleneck for new domains.  
9. Sidesteps natural language: operates on semantic programs and does not address parsing/generation to/from natural language.  
10. Summary/recommendation: technically strong and novel with convincing results; strengths outweigh limitations, recommend acceptance despite generalizability concerns.
