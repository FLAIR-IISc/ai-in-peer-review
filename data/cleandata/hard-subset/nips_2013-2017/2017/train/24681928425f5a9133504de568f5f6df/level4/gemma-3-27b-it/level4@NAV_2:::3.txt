The paper presents a cognitive model designed to predict question selection based on context and belief states. The model posits that the unnormalized log probability of a question is determined by a weighted sum of several features: informativeness (measured via expected information gain), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance (assessing connection to the underlying belief state). This model is evaluated within the Battleship domain, where the goal is to identify the locations of hidden ships on a grid. The full model, incorporating all features, demonstrated superior performance in predicting held-out human question data (measured by log-likelihood) compared to variants with reduced feature sets. However, correlation analyses revealed that the full model did not significantly outperform versions lacking the informativeness feature, potentially due to data sparsity.

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors demonstrate a feasible approach to modeling question generation and evaluation.

While the work has potential implications for both AI and cognitive science, its primary contribution appears to lie within the realm of artificial intelligence. The authors claim to reveal insights into how humans balance informativeness and complexity in question asking, but the empirical results offer limited support for strong conclusions regarding human cognitive processes. The log-likelihood improvements offered by including EIG are not robust (estimated 19% chance of being due to chance), and the correlation analysis does not convincingly demonstrate a relationship between EIG and human question choices.  Thus, the paper's claim to provide a cognitive account feels somewhat overstated given the nuanced and potentially weak evidence.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and appears reproducible. The methodology is described sufficiently to allow for replication by others in the field.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work. The framing of questions as programs that, when executed, yield answers is reminiscent of Hawkins (2015) [“Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.”], which also employs expected information gain and considers question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain. Furthermore, the underlying concept of representing questions semantically relates to the question-under-discussion (QUD) framework introduced by Roberts (1996) [“Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics.”].  Acknowledging these connections would strengthen the paper’s positioning within the existing literature.

Overall, this work represents a valuable contribution to the field of active learning and question generation. The proposed model offers a promising approach to generating compositional and informative questions, and is likely to be of interest to researchers in both AI and cognitive science. I anticipate that this line of work will inspire future research in both areas. The core strength of the paper lies in demonstrating the feasibility of generating interesting compositional questions within a non-trivial domain using the proposed program-based approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-executed methodology.
*   Novel approach to question generation based on program synthesis.
*   Demonstrates the feasibility of generating compositional questions in a complex domain.
*   Potential for impact in active learning and cognitive modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited evidence supporting strong claims about human cognitive processes.
*   Insufficient discussion of relevant prior work (Hawkins 2015, Roberts 1996).
*   Results are somewhat sensitive to estimation methods (bootstrap estimates).
