Summary
The paper studies question generation in a Battleship-style game, replacing queries about individual squares with questions in an artificial language endowed with compositional (Montagovian) semantics. A log-linear model ranks candidate questions using a small set of features, including expected information gain (informativeness), answer type (Boolean vs. numerical), and whether the question refers to board features. The goal is to reproduce human-like question-asking behavior and to relate it to ideas from active learning.

Strengths
- The motivation from active learning is reasonable: selecting queries to maximize information is a natural lens for question generation.
- The use of a compositional semantic language to define question meaning makes the setup unambiguous and facilitates precise computation of information gain.
- The modeling choice (log-linear over interpretable features) is transparent and allows for straightforward analysis of which properties of questions correlate with performance.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: expected information gain and simple structural features of questions are standard tools; the core ideas feel obvious for this highly controlled setting.
- Narrow, artificial domain: the Battleship environment is simple, logically unambiguous, and noise-free, with a small hypothesis space. This makes the problem comparatively easy and limits the significance of the results.
- Simplistic modeling: the model is essentially a linear combination of a few hand-crafted features. There is no exploration of richer representations, learned semantics, or non-myopic planning.
- Insufficient evaluation to establish interest to the NIPS community. The draft does not describe comparisons against strong baselines (e.g., pure information gain, random, cost-sensitive heuristics), ablations over features, or human behavioral data validating that the model captures human question preferences beyond trivial effects.
- It is unclear whether the learned weights offer insights beyond “ask the most informative question.” Without noise, ambiguity, language pragmatics, or resource constraints, the setting underplays the challenges that make question-asking interesting in AI or cognitive science.
- Inconsistency: the paper mentions “four” features but lists only three; this needs clarification.
- Generalization is not demonstrated: it is unclear how the approach scales to larger boards, richer question languages, partially observed answers, or domains beyond Battleship.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Precisely define the hypothesis space, priors over ship configurations, and the answer model. How is expected information gain computed and approximated?
- What is the fourth feature? If present, how sensitive are results to each feature (feature ablation)?
- What baselines are used? How does the model compare to: (i) choosing the argmax of expected information gain only, (ii) random selection, and (iii) cost/length-penalized information gain?
- Are there human data on which questions people ask? If so, how closely does the model match human behavior beyond informativeness alone?
- How large is the candidate question set? Is there any search or pruning, and does the method scale with language size?
- Do you consider non-myopic strategies (e.g., planning over multiple questions), or is the approach purely greedy?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen evaluation: include robust baselines, feature ablations, sensitivity analyses, and experiments on larger/more complex boards.
- Introduce realistic complexity: noise or ambiguity in answers, partial observability, answer costs, or time/complexity costs for questions; evaluate cost-sensitive trade-offs.
- Broaden the language: allow more diverse compositional constructions and investigate whether the model scales to richer semantics.
- Incorporate or compare to human data to support the claim of reproducing human question-asking behavior; analyze where the model agrees/disagrees with people.
- Explore non-myopic decision-making and planning beyond single-step information gain.
- Discuss connections and differences to established active learning, decision-theoretic question asking, and pragmatic language modeling work; position the contribution clearly relative to that literature.
- Provide code, data, and precise task specification for reproducibility.

Overall assessment
While the motivation is sensible and the setup is clear, the current contribution is too limited in scope, novelty, and empirical depth to be compelling for NIPS. The task is highly constrained and unambiguous, the model is simple and relies on well-known features, and the evaluation (as described) does not establish either scientific insight into human question-asking or algorithmic advances for interactive learning. With substantial extensions—more challenging domains, richer semantics, realistic uncertainty/costs, human validation, and stronger comparative analyses—the work could become more broadly relevant. In its current form, I do not recommend acceptance.
