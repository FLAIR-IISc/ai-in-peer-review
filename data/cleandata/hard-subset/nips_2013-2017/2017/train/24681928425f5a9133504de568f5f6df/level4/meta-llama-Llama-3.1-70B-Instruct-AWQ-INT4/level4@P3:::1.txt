This paper presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking as program generation, which is a promising direction in active learning. The authors propose a probabilistic model that treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. They evaluate their approach using a data set containing natural language questions asked by human participants in an information-search game.

The paper makes several contributions to the field of active learning and cognitive science. First, it introduces a new computational framework that explains how people construct rich and interesting queries within a particular domain. Second, it provides a method for generating meaningful questions/programs from scratch, which is a crucial step towards developing more human-like question asking in machines. Finally, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model in predicting human questions and generating novel, human-like questions that were not present in the training set.

The authors' use of a probabilistic model to predict question usefulness is well-motivated, and their incorporation of features such as informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance is reasonable. The results show that the full model provides the best account of the data, and that lesioning its key components results in lower quality predictions.

However, there are some limitations to the current approach. The system operates on semantic representations rather than on natural language text directly, and some aspects of the grammar are specific to the Battleship domain. Additionally, the model begins with substantial domain knowledge and special purpose structures, which may limit its generalizability to other domains.

Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to modeling human question asking as program generation. While there are some limitations to the current approach, the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model, and the paper makes several contributions to the field of active learning and cognitive science.

Strengths:

* Introduces a new computational framework for modeling human question asking
* Provides a method for generating meaningful questions/programs from scratch
* Demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model in predicting human questions and generating novel, human-like questions
* Uses a probabilistic model to predict question usefulness

Weaknesses:

* System operates on semantic representations rather than on natural language text directly
* Some aspects of the grammar are specific to the Battleship domain
* Model begins with substantial domain knowledge and special purpose structures, which may limit its generalizability to other domains

Arguments for acceptance:

* Presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking as program generation
* Demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model in predicting human questions and generating novel, human-like questions
* Makes several contributions to the field of active learning and cognitive science

Arguments against acceptance:

* Limited generalizability to other domains due to the specificity of the grammar and the model's reliance on domain knowledge
* System operates on semantic representations rather than on natural language text directly, which may limit its applicability to real-world scenarios

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the limitations of the current approach and provide more discussion on the generalizability of the model to other domains. Additionally, they should consider incorporating more features into the model to improve its performance.
