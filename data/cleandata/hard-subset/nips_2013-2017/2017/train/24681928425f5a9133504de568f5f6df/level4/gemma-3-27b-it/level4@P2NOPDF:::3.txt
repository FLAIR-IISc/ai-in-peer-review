This paper introduces a cognitive model designed to predict question selection based on underlying belief states. The model calculates the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of four factors: informativeness (approximated through expected information gain or EIG), complexity (operationalized as question length), answer type, and relevance – whether the question pertains to the current belief state or relies on world-independent knowledge. The model was evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the goal is to locate hidden ships within a grid. Results demonstrate that the full model, incorporating all four features, outperforms ablated versions with fewer features in terms of log-likelihood when predicting held-out human question data. However, the model's performance, as measured by correlation with human judgments, was comparable with and without the inclusion of EIG.

The paper is technically sound and well-written, and the results appear reproducible. However, I believe its primary contribution lies more within the realm of artificial intelligence than cognitive science. While the authors claim their model “predicts what question people will ask” and provides insight into “how people balance informativeness and complexity,” the evidence supporting these claims is weak. The observed data regarding EIG’s influence is inconsistent: log-likelihood results suggest a modest effect, but bootstrap estimates indicate substantial uncertainty (19% chance of being incorrect), while correlation results show no significant impact – a finding potentially hampered by the sparsity of the human data. Consequently, I am unconvinced that the paper provides substantial evidence for *how* people balance informativeness and complexity in question selection.

The work is missing a discussion of relevant prior work. Specifically, the approach of “modeling questions as programs that…output an answer” strongly echoes Hawkins (2015) ("Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers"), which employs the concept of a “goal” and builds upon the earlier notion of the question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts (1996) ("Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics."). Hawkins’ questioner model similarly utilizes EIG and balances it with question complexity, albeit within a simpler domain. Acknowledging this lineage would strengthen the paper's position within the broader research landscape.

Despite these limitations, I recognize the value of this modeling approach – a compositional prior over questions guided by EIG and other relevant factors – as a potentially powerful component in both cognitive and AI systems. I anticipate its increased adoption in future research. The core contribution of this paper, therefore, lies in demonstrating the feasibility of generating meaningful compositional questions for a relatively complex domain using this framework.
