The authors investigate human question-asking behavior in a "battleship" domain, where hidden colored shapes are placed in a partially observable grid. They define a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) by hand, which serves as a prior over questions represented as statements in lambda calculus. The goal is to ask a single-word question that maximizes information gain about the board state. The authors define a question's "goodness" as a linear function of its informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log probability under the PCFG with a uniform distribution over rewrite rules), and "answer type" (e.g., true/false or color).

The authors compare lesioned versions of their model using leave-one-out cross-validation and find that all factors contribute significantly to the model's performance, with complexity being the most important. They also present a set of novel questions generated by their model. This work is an important contribution to the field of linguistics and hypothesis testing, providing a unique perspective that unifies modern linguistic and cognitive theory.

The paper is well-written and clear, and the model is evaluated from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives. As a computational cognitive scientist, I believe this paper advances the field of self-directed learning by resolving part of why question informativeness is a poor predictor of human question-asking behavior. The authors show that complexity and the size of possible questions available to the model play a more significant role.

However, there are limitations to this approach that may limit its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While it may be possible to automate human question text into semantic logic, it is unclear how the approach can be scaled or extended to more complex domains. The battleship domain is relatively small, with only a few features, and the method is already computationally strained. Nevertheless, the ideas presented in this paper, such as active learning with richer questions, are interesting and may be of interest to the broader community.

To increase the paper's appeal, the authors may need to provide a clear scenario where their technique can be applied and provide novel insights. Some possible extensions to consider include exploring more complex domains or integrating their approach with other models from the linguistics literature, such as those from the Questions under Discussion literature.

Minor comments:

1. The authors may consider performing a nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models to obtain tests of statistical significance.
2. For sequential question-asking, the authors may want to explore other models from the linguistics literature, such as those from the Questions under Discussion literature. Some relevant references include Ginzburg (1995) and Rojas-Esponda (2013).
