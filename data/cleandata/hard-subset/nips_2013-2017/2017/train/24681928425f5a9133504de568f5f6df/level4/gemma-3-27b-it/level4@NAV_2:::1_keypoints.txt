- Introduces a computational framework that models human question asking by representing questions as executable programs.
- Uses a probabilistic, log-linear model of question utility with features like Expected Information Gain, complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Evaluates the approach on human questions collected from an information-seeking game, showing good predictive performance.
- Motivation ties active learning to richer, human-like queries and aligns with broader AI goals.
- Compositional program representation is sensible and draws on linguistics and cognitive science.
- Positions the work relative to active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation, noting limitations of prior approaches.
- Experimental setup and dataset provide a solid foundation for evaluation.
- Key strength: demonstrates that both informativeness and complexity matter for human-like question generation.
- Ablation studies effectively show the contribution of individual model components.
- Model can generate novel, human-like questions beyond memorizing training data, indicating generalization.
- Major weakness: task is relatively constrained and may not generalize to open-ended real-world questioning.
- Reliance on a hand-designed grammar could limit scalability and transfer to more complex domains.
- Discussion lacks depth on connections to Bayesian program learning and broader theoretical grounding.
- Limitations section is brief and should better address challenges in scaling to less structured environments.
- Overall: sound and well-presented work with compelling results but somewhat incremental and better suited to a specialized venue given its scope.
