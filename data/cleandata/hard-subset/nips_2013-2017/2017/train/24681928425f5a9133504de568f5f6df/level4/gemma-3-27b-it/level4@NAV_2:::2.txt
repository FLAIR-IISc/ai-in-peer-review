The authors examine human question asking where answers can take on multiple values. They model questions as formal programs that, when executed on a state of the world, output an answer. Their approach is applied to a “battleship” domain with partially observable grids, where the goal is to identify hidden colored shapes by asking single-word answer questions that maximize information gain. The authors define a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) for this domain, using it as a prior over questions expressed in lambda calculus. Question goodness is evaluated through a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (negative log probability under the PCFG), and answer type. The human data originates from a prior study and was translated into their grammar. The authors compare model variants through leave-one-out cross-validation, finding that all factors contribute to performance, with complexity being particularly important. They also present novel questions generated by the model.

This work addresses an important problem in both linguistics and active learning: understanding and modeling human question asking. The authors provide a novel and compelling perspective, unifying cognitive and linguistic theory to explain how people formulate questions, potentially fostering new research in active learning that moves beyond simple label requests. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear, and the model is evaluated rigorously through both quantitative and qualitative analyses.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, the paper advances the field of self-directed learning. Prior research indicated that question informativeness alone is a poor predictor of human question asking; this work elucidates why, demonstrating the importance of complexity and the role of a richer program space. This contribution to the cognitive science literature is substantial and potentially justifies publication in NIPS.

However, the approach has significant limitations that may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While the potential exists to automate the translation of natural language questions into semantic logic, it remains unclear how the method would scale or generalize to domains of wider interest. The battleship domain is relatively small, and the approach is already computationally demanding. That said, the core ideas—active learning with richer, more nuanced questions—are compelling and warrant consideration. The authors may need to articulate a clear scenario or application relevant to the broader NIPS community where their technique could deliver novel insights. 

Minor comments:

1) A nested model comparison with likelihood ratio tests could provide statistical support for the differences observed between the lesioned models.
2) Future work might benefit from considering related research in formal semantics and pragmatics, particularly the “Questions under Discussion” framework, which explores the dynamics of question-answering sequences. Relevant references include: Ginzburg (1995) and Rojas-Esponda (2013).
