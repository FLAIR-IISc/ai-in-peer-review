Summary
This paper models human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as lambda-calculus–style programs executed on a “Battleship”-like world to return single-word answers (Boolean, number, color, or location). A hand-built probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) defines a compositional space of question programs for the domain. A log-linear model assigns probabilities to programs using features for expected information gain (EIG), complexity (negative log probability under the PCFG with uniform rewrite choices), and answer type; a relevance feature filters out non–board-referential expressions. Parameters are learned via importance-sampled gradient ascent. Using a dataset of 605 human questions from prior work, the model is evaluated with leave-one-context-out cross-validation to predict question frequencies and to generate novel questions. Lesion studies suggest all feature groups matter, with complexity being especially critical.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects active learning (typically restricted to label queries) to richer, open-ended queries, drawing on compositional semantics and the idea of questions as partitions over possible worlds. It aligns with program-induction perspectives in cognitive science and semantic parsing for question answering. Relative to dialog and visual question generation systems, this approach targets intentional, information-seeking questions grounded in a formal semantics.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized presentation with careful definitions of the grammar, features, and inference.
- Conceptually novel framing of question asking as program synthesis, bridging active learning, formal semantics, and cognitive modeling.
- Both quantitative prediction of human question distributions and qualitative generation of novel, sensible questions.
- Insightful cognitive result: complexity substantially moderates informativeness, helping explain why EIG alone poorly predicts human behavior in prior work.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Domain specificity and scalability: The Battleship grammar is hand-engineered and the mapping from natural language to programs was hand-coded. It is unclear how this approach scales to richer domains without substantial knowledge engineering.
- Computational burden: Even in a small domain, inference requires large importance-sampled sets and approximations to the partition function; some contexts were excluded for tractability. This raises questions about applicability to larger hypothesis spaces or real-time settings.
- Natural language interface: The paper sidesteps the core challenge of semantic parsing in open domains, limiting immediate practical impact for broader NIPS applications.
- Generalization: While the grammar captures all questions in this dataset, it is uncertain how much of the machinery would transfer to new tasks and whether the same features would dominate outside this controlled environment.

Evaluation by NIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically sound and honest about limitations. The generative framework, feature design, and optimization are appropriate; approximations are standard for this setting. Some statistical comparisons across lesioned models could be strengthened (see suggestions).
- Clarity: Very clearly written, with interpretable figures and examples. The decomposition into grammar, model, features, and optimization is easy to follow.
- Originality: Representing open-ended question asking as probabilistic program generation is a fresh and compelling synthesis of ideas across fields.
- Significance: For cognitive science and self-directed learning, the findings are meaningful and likely to stimulate follow-up work. For general NIPS impact, significance depends on demonstrating scalability or applicability to broader domains (e.g., task-oriented dialogue with compositional world models).

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, unified account of question asking that integrates compositional semantics and active learning.
- Strong evidence that complexity, alongside informativeness, is key to human-like question generation.
- Capable of producing novel, context-sensitive questions not seen in training.
- Clear writing and thorough ablations.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar and hand-coded annotations.
- Computationally intensive inference even in a small domain; limited scalability.
- Lacks an end-to-end natural language interface or demonstration in a domain of wider NIPS interest.
- Limited assessment of statistical significance for model comparisons.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a statistically principled comparison among lesioned models (e.g., nested model comparisons on approximated log-likelihoods or bootstrap-based tests over normalizers).
- Outline a concrete path to broader domains: which grammar components are reusable versus domain-specific, and what engineering effort is required? A small demonstration in a second domain would strengthen the significance claim.
- Discuss how to integrate a semantic parser to replace hand-coding, and the expected hit to performance/noise tolerance.
- For multi-turn settings, connect to formal semantics/pragmatics approaches to discourse and Questions Under Discussion (e.g., Ginzburg, 1995; Rojas-Esponda, 2013) to model sequential question selection.

Minor comments
- Consider conducting nested model comparisons or bootstrap hypothesis tests to assess the statistical significance of differences among lesioned models.
- For future work on sequential question asking, leveraging formal semantics/pragmatics frameworks (Questions Under Discussion) may provide principled mechanisms for context updates and dialogue management:
  - Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459–527.
  - Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of SemDial (DialDam), 17, 131–139.

Overall assessment
A thoughtful, well-executed paper with clear cognitive and methodological contributions. It is strongest as a computational cognitive science advance and as a conceptual blueprint for richer active learning. For broader NIPS impact, the authors should better substantiate scalability and generality, or present a compelling application scenario beyond the Battleship domain.
