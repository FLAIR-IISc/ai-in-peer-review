This paper presents a computational model of human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The central idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional language which, when executed, return an answer. The authors define a probabilistic context-free grammar for a "Battleship"-like game domain, which serves as a prior over the space of possible questions. The probability of asking a particular question is then modeled as a log-linear function of several features, including its expected information gain (EIG), complexity (derived from its derivation in the grammar), and the type of its answer (e.g., Boolean, number). The model's parameters are fit to a dataset of human-generated questions from a previous study, where questions were manually translated into the formal program representation. The authors perform a leave-one-out cross-validation and lesion studies, demonstrating that features for informativeness, complexity, and answer type all contribute to predicting human questions, with complexity being a particularly critical factor. Finally, the model is shown to be capable of generating novel, contextually relevant questions not seen in the training data.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper offers an innovative and compelling perspective on question asking by unifying ideas from cognitive science, formal linguistics, and machine learning. Framing rich, compositional queries as programs is a significant conceptual advance over the simple label-request queries typical in most active learning literature. This work has the potential to inspire new research directions in building more sophisticated and human-like active learning agents.
*   **Quality:** The model is evaluated thoroughly through both quantitative and qualitative means. The quantitative analysis, which includes model comparisons via lesion studies, provides strong evidence for the contributions of the model's different components. In particular, the finding that question complexity is a better predictor of human behavior than raw informativeness is an important contribution to cognitive science, clarifying results from previous work. The qualitative examples of novel, generated questions effectively demonstrate the model's generative and creative capacity.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It presents a complex framework involving program synthesis, probabilistic modeling, and Bayesian inference in a clear and accessible manner, making the work understandable to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
*   **Scalability and Generalization:** A primary concern is the scalability of the approach to larger, more complex domains beyond the constrained "Battleship" game. The grammar is hand-engineered for this specific task, and the computational methods for inference are already strained, as evidenced by the need to exclude certain game contexts from the analysis. The paper would be stronger if the authors discussed a potential roadmap for applying these ideas to domains with larger state spaces or more varied semantics, which would be of greater interest to the broader NIPS community.
*   **Dependence on Manual Engineering:** The current framework requires natural language questions to be manually translated into the formal program representation. This reliance on a pre-defined semantic logic and manual coding could limit the practical applicability of the model. While this is a reasonable simplification for a cognitive modeling paper, discussing how this gap between natural language and the "language of thought" might be bridged (perhaps by integrating with modern semantic parsing techniques) would enhance the paper's contribution.

**Minor Comments:**
1.  The comparison between the full and lesioned models is based on log-likelihoods. It might be possible to formalize this comparison using nested model tests to establish the statistical significance of each feature set's contribution.
2.  For future work, especially regarding sequential question asking, the authors may find the linguistics literature on "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) relevant. This work provides formal models of how dialogue context constrains which questions are relevant to ask next. The following are good starting points:
    *   Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy, 18*(5), 459-527.
    *   Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. In *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17*, 131-139.
