- Introduces a model treating questions as executable programs to predict and generate human-like questions in a Battleship-style game.  
- Uses a log-linear model with features: expected information gain (EIG), program complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated on 605 human questions across 18 game contexts with cross-validation and bootstrap analyses.  
- Full model outperforms variants on held-out log-likelihood; removing complexity causes the largest performance drop.  
- Generates novel, contextually relevant compositional questions (e.g., “Are all ships horizontal?”).  
- Cognitive-science claims are overstated; evidence for an EIG–complexity trade-off is inconsistent.  
- Technical approach is sound and rigorously evaluated.  
- Correlation results: model energy vs human frequency ρ=0.64, EIG-agnostic ρ=0.65, EIG alone shows negligible correlation.  
- Sparse human data (many one-off questions) limits robustness of correlation-based conclusions.  
- Relies on domain-specific grammar engineering (ship size, color primitives), raising generalization concerns.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and reproducible, with detailed supplementary materials.  
- Builds on prior work (Hawkins 2015; Roberts 1996); formalism isn’t wholly novel but application and compositionality are.  
- Offers a principled, scalable alternative to canned or neural question-asking systems requiring large datasets.  
- Strengths: novel program-synthesis application, rigorous model comparisons, compositional question generation, well-motivated features.  
- Weaknesses: overstated cognitive claims, missing foundational citations, domain-specific engineering, and sparse human data.  
- Recommendation: Accept — strong AI/technical contribution; revise to temper cognitive claims and add missing citations.
