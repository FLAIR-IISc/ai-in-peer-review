- Paper models human question-asking in a battleship-like grid using a hand-specified PCFG and lambda-calculus logical forms.  
- Questions are scored by a linear combination of informativeness (expected info gain), complexity (length or -log PCFG prob), and answer type (e.g., boolean vs color).  
- Human questions from prior work were manually mapped into the grammar for evaluation.  
- Leave-one-out cross-validation on ablated models shows all components matter, with complexity clearly the dominant factor.  
- The authors introduce a set of novel questions produced by their model.  
- The framing integrates linguistic theory and cognitive modeling and is exceptionally clear and well written.  
- Evaluation includes appropriate quantitative and qualitative analyses.  
- From a cognitive science perspective, the work advances understanding of self-directed learning and explains why informativeness alone is a poor predictor.  
- Main limitation: scalability and transfer are unclear—small, feature-limited battleship domain and seemingly computationally heavy method.  
- Recommendation: present a concrete, broadly relevant application to demonstrate scalability and wider impact.  
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods for statistical significance testing.  
- Minor pointers: cite sequential question-asking/QUD literature (e.g., Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
