The authors examine human question asking in scenarios where answers are drawn from a finite set of possibilities. They define a Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) by hand for a “battleship” domain – a partially observable grid concealing colored shapes, where the agent seeks to identify the shapes’ locations and colors. The task is to formulate questions with single-word answers that maximize information gain about the board’s state. This PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are formally defined as statements in lambda calculus. Question quality is quantified as a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (measured by question length or negative log-probability under the PCFG, assuming a uniform distribution over rewrite rules), and answer type (e.g., true/false versus color identification). Human question data, originally published in a prior work, was manually coded into the defined grammar. The authors evaluated lesioned versions of their model using leave-one-out cross-validation, demonstrating that each factor contributes significantly to performance, though complexity proved the most influential. They also presented a set of novel, model-generated questions.

Question-asking is a central area within linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors provide an innovative perspective, unifying modern linguistic and cognitive theory to explore human question formulation. This approach has the potential to inspire new research in active learning, fostering models capable of asking more sophisticated questions. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, and the model is assessed rigorously from both quantitative and qualitative standpoints.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this work advances the field of self-directed learning. Previous research found that question informativeness alone was a poor predictor of human question choices. This paper helps resolve this discrepancy, demonstrating that complexity is a crucial factor, potentially explaining the limited predictive power of informativeness in models with a restricted question space. This contribution is valuable to the cognitive science literature and may independently justify publication in NIPS.

However, the approach suffers from significant limitations that may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While automating the mapping of human question text to semantic logic representations may be feasible, it remains unclear how the method could scale to domains of greater complexity and relevance to the wider machine learning community. The battleship domain is relatively small, featuring few distinct features, and the current implementation is already computationally demanding. Nevertheless, the underlying ideas – leveraging richer questions within an active learning framework – are promising. To broaden the paper’s impact, the authors should provide a concrete scenario of interest to the broader NIPS community where their technique is likely to be applicable and yield novel insights. 

Minor comments:

1) It would be beneficial to conduct a nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods (LLs) of the lesioned models to obtain tests of statistical significance.
2) For future work involving sequential question asking, the authors should consider relevant models from the linguistics literature focusing on question-asking in formal semantics and pragmatics, particularly research stemming from the Questions under Discussion framework. Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy, 18*(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17*, 131-139.
