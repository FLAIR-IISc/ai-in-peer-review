- Introduces a cognitive model that predicts which questions are asked given belief states, using an unnormalized log-probability as a weighted sum of four factors: informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship-style task; the full model achieves better held-out log-likelihood than ablated variants.  
- However, a model omitting EIG correlates with human judgments about as well as the full model.  
- Overall the paper is technically sound.  
- Stronger contribution to AI than to cognitive science; claims about revealing how people trade off informativeness and complexity are not well supported.  
- Evidence for EIG’s influence is weak: the log-likelihood improvement has a 19% bootstrap chance of being incorrect and correlation results are noisy due to sparse human data.  
- Paper is clear, well written, and likely reproducible.  
- Missing relevant prior work, notably Hawkins (2015) and the QUD framework from Roberts (1996), which also model EIG vs. complexity tradeoffs.  
- The compositional-question + selection-by-EIG approach is promising and the main contribution is demonstrating generation of interesting, compositional questions in a non-trivial domain.
