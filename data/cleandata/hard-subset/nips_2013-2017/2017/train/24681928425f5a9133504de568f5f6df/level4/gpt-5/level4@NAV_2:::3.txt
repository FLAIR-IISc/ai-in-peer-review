The paper introduces a cognitive model aimed at predicting which questions people will ask in particular contexts (belief states). In this framework, the unnormalized log-probability (energy) of a question is a linear function of several features: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (approximately question length), answer type, and a “relevance” indicator (whether the question refers to the current belief state rather than world-independent logical facts). The model is instantiated in a Battleship-like domain where the goal is to infer the positions and sizes of ships on a grid. The full model incorporating all features achieves higher held-out log-likelihood on human data than lesioned variants. However, in terms of correlation with human frequencies, the model that omits EIG performs about as well as the full system.

The paper appears generally technically sound.

I see this as more compelling as an AI contribution than as a cognitive science result. The paper claims that “We find that our model predicts what question people will ask” and that “from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity.” I am not convinced that the current results substantively clarify how people trade off these factors. In this Battleship task, people seem to be influenced by EIG only weakly (if one trusts the log-likelihood comparison, which bootstrap estimates suggest has a 19% chance of reversing) or not at all (if one trusts the correlation analyses, which are themselves noisy due to sparsity in the human data). These findings do not, in my view, support broad conclusions about how people balance informativeness and complexity in general.

The paper is well written and clear. I believe the results would be reproducible.

Some relevant prior work is missing. One apparent novelty is to “model questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer.” This aligns closely with the notion of a “goal” in Hawkins (2015, “Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers”), which itself builds on the question-under-discussion (QUD) framework from Roberts (1996). The Hawkins model also trades off expected information gain with question complexity, though in a simpler setting.

Overall, I think a model that imposes a compositional prior over questions and selects among them via EIG and related factors is a sensible building block for both cognitive and AI systems, and I expect such approaches to become more prevalent. I take the paper’s main contribution to be a demonstration that this method can generate interesting, compositional questions in a nontrivial domain.
