- Investigates human question asking in a “battleship” domain using a hand-crafted PCFG that generates lambda-calculus question statements.  
- Task: agent asks a single-word-answer question to maximize information about a partially observable grid with hidden colored shapes.  
- Question goodness modeled as a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity, and answer type.  
- Complexity measured by question length or negative log probability under the PCFG with uniform rule distribution.  
- Human data from prior work was hand-coded into the grammar; the model also generates novel questions.  
- Evaluation via leave-one-out cross-validation on lesioned models shows all factors matter, with complexity being most significant.  
- Strong contribution: integrates linguistic and cognitive theories, clearly written, with solid quantitative and qualitative analyses.  
- Cognitive-science impact: explains why informativeness alone poorly predicted human behavior by highlighting the role of complexity.  
- Major limitation: scalability is unclear—small domain, few features, and noticeable computational strain limit broader applicability.  
- Recommendation: authors should identify a larger, community-relevant domain where the technique would provide novel insights.  
- Minor comments: use nested model comparisons with log-likelihoods for statistical tests; consider formal semantics/pragmatics (Questions under Discussion literature) for sequential questioning.
