### Summary

This paper presents a computational model for generating context-sensitive, goal-directed questions. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional language based on lambda calculus. The model defines a probability distribution over the space of possible programs, favoring questions that are both informative and concise. The informativeness of a question is quantified by its Expected Information Gain (EIG) in a Bayesian framework, while its complexity is captured by its length under a probabilistic context-free grammar.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game, where the goal is to identify the location of hidden objects on a grid. The authors demonstrate that a log-linear model combining features for EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance can predict the distribution of human-asked questions. The full model outperforms lesioned versions (e.g., one that is agnostic to information gain or complexity) on a log-likelihood metric. Qualitatively, the model is also shown to generate novel, plausible, and contextually relevant questions that were not observed in the human training data.

### Detailed Review

This is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution by applying the program synthesis paradigm to the problem of open-ended question generation. The approach of framing questions as programs that are scored based on a trade-off between informativeness and complexity is elegant and powerful.

#### Quality

The paper is technically solid. The probabilistic model is clearly formulated, and the use of importance sampling for intractable computations is appropriate. The experimental setup, using a leave-one-out cross-validation approach on the game contexts, is methodologically sound.

However, the strength of the empirical support for some of the central claims is mixed. The authors conclude that their results "show how people balance informativeness and complexity." While the full model outperforms lesioned models on the log-likelihood metric, the results from the correlation analysis are less conclusive. The full model achieves a Spearman's rho of 0.64, while the information-agnostic model achieves a very similar 0.65. This suggests that, by this metric, EIG is not a critical component for predicting human question frequencies. The authors should discuss this apparent contradiction more directly. The weak and conflicting evidence makes it difficult to draw strong conclusions about the cognitive plausibility of the EIG/complexity trade-off as modeled here. The contribution seems stronger as a novel AI system for question generation than as a definitive cognitive model of human question asking.

#### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the model is described in sufficient detail, and the experiments are easy to understand. The figures, particularly Figure 3 which showcases generated questions, are effective at illustrating the model's capabilities. The authors have done an excellent job of presenting a complex framework in an accessible manner, and the work appears to be reproducible.

#### Originality

The central idea of modeling questions as programs that partition a space of possible worlds has roots in formal semantics and pragmatics. A significant weakness of the paper is the omission of highly relevant prior work that tempers the perceived novelty of the core framework. The authors should discuss the relationship of their work to:

*   **Roberts (1996, "Information structure in discourse..."):** This work introduced the concept of a Question Under Discussion (QUD), which formalizes questions as partitions over a set of possible worlds, a concept functionally identical to the paper's "program execution" semantics.
*   **Hawkins et al. (2015, "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers."):** This work also models a questioner using a probabilistic model that trades off EIG with question complexity. While applied to a simpler domain, it shares the core conceptual framework of the current paper.

Acknowledging this prior work is crucial. That said, the primary contribution of this paper is not the invention of this trade-off, but rather the demonstration that this approach can be scaled to a significantly more complex, non-trivial domain to generate a rich, compositional set of open-ended questions. This is a novel and important engineering achievement that significantly advances the state of the art from prior demonstrations.

#### Significance

This work is significant for both the active learning and computational cognitive science communities. It presents a powerful and generalizable framework for moving beyond simple label queries in active learning, paving the way for machines that can inquire more like humans. The ability to generate novel, creative questions is particularly impressive and addresses a major limitation of existing dialogue and question-generation systems that often rely on templates or re-ranking existing questions.

I expect the core idea—a compositional grammar for queries combined with a utility function based on EIG and complexity—to be influential and widely adopted in future AI systems that need to reason about information gathering. The main contribution is successfully building a system that can generate interesting compositional questions for a complex domain, which is a substantial step forward.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Novel Application:** Presents a novel and successful application of program synthesis to generate a rich set of open-ended, goal-directed questions.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically solid, well-executed, and clearly explained.
3.  **Generativity:** The model's ability to generate creative, contextually relevant questions not seen in the training data is a key strength and an important advance over previous systems.
4.  **Significance:** The framework has the potential to be highly influential for building more sophisticated active learning and dialogue agents.

### Arguments for Rejection:

1.  **Missing Key Citations:** The paper fails to cite and discuss highly relevant prior work (e.g., Hawkins et al. 2015; Roberts 1996) that introduced similar core ideas, which overstates the conceptual novelty of the approach.
2.  **Weak Support for Cognitive Claims:** The empirical evidence for the specific trade-off between EIG and complexity in human cognition is mixed and contradictory across different evaluation metrics within the paper itself.

### Recommendation

Despite the weaknesses, particularly the missing citations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the negatives. The technical contribution of successfully implementing this framework in a complex domain and demonstrating its generative power is significant enough for acceptance. I strongly recommend that the authors revise the paper to thoroughly discuss the relationship of their work to the prior art mentioned above. This would more accurately frame their contribution and strengthen the paper considerably.
