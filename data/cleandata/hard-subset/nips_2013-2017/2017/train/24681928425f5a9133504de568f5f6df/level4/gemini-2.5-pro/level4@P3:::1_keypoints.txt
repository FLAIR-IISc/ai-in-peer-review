- Represents human questions as compositional programs executed on a world state, enabling formal answers.  
- Defines a probabilistic log-linear model over programs generated by a context-free grammar.  
- Model scores favor informative questions (high Expected Information Gain) and simple ones (low program length).  
- Evaluated on a "Battleship"-like game dataset; predicts human question frequencies in held-out contexts.  
- Can generate novel, contextually relevant, human-like questions not seen in training.  
- Extends active learning from simple label queries to a rich, compositional inquiry space.  
- Contrasts with data-driven dialogue/question-generation systems by using a structured, knowledge-rich model that works with limited data.  
- Builds on cognitive-science ideas of representing concepts as programs (language of thought).  
- Novel and elegant conceptual contribution framing question asking as probabilistic program generation.  
- Sound methodology: grammar-defined hypothesis space, log-linear scoring, and informative lesion studies showing importance of EIG and complexity.  
- Strong generative ability demonstrating productive, compositional question formation rather than memorization.  
- Clear, well-written, and well-motivated presentation.  
- Main weakness: evaluated in a highly constrained, toy domain, raising scalability and generalizability concerns.  
- Reliance on hand-crafted grammars and manual translation from natural language sidesteps semantic parsing challenges.  
- Features used are relatively simple (EIG, complexity, answer type); richer cognitive/pragmatic features might improve performance.  
- Pro acceptance arguments: novel framework, solid experiments, creative output, and high-quality presentation.  
- Con acceptance arguments: limited to simple domain, heavy domain engineering, and primarily a cognitive modeling contribution with limited immediate large-scale ML impact.
