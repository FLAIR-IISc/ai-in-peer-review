- Introduces a cognitive model that predicts question selection from belief states using a weighted sum of EIG, complexity (question length), answer type, and relevance.
- Implements questions as programs that output answers when executed on possible worlds.
- Applied to the Battleship domain; full model outperformed ablated versions on held-out human data by log-likelihood.
- When evaluated by correlation with human judgments, the model without EIG performed comparably to the full model.
- Evidence for humans using EIG is weak: bootstrap indicates a 19% chance the EIG effect is spurious.
- Correlation results are noisy and limited by sparse human data, undermining strong conclusions about how people balance informativeness and complexity.
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, clear, and results appear reproducible.
- Related work is inadequately cited: similar ideas appear in Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996), where goals/QUDs and EIG+complexity trade-offs are discussed.
- Main contribution is demonstrating feasibility of generating meaningful compositional questions in a non-trivial domain, making the approach promising for AI and cognitive research.
