

This paper introduces a cognitive model for human question asking that treats questions as executable programs over possible world states. The core innovation is a probabilistic framework where question likelihood is determined by a weighted combination of informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance (whether the question references the current belief state). Evaluated on a Battleship game dataset where participants asked questions to identify ship configurations, the full model with all features outperformed variants in predicting held-out human question distributions (log-likelihood), though correlation with human frequencies was modest (Spearman’s ρ=0.64). Crucially, the complexity-agnostic variant performed significantly worse, suggesting humans prioritize concise questions beyond pure informativeness.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The technical approach is sound, with thorough evaluation of model variants via leave-one-out cross-validation. The grammar-based compositional representation (inspired by cognitive science and programming language theory) enables novel question generation, a key advance over prior neural approaches that require large datasets. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies (e.g., 0.64 Spearman correlation) and generates novel, contextually relevant questions (e.g., "Are all ships horizontal?"), demonstrating feasibility in a non-trivial domain.  
*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the grammar, features, and optimization. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including grammar rules and feature definitions.  
*Originality*: While modeling questions as programs builds on Hawkins et al. (2015) and Roberts (1996), this work extends it significantly by: (1) introducing a compositional grammar for open-ended question generation (not just fixed templates), (2) demonstrating applicability to a complex cognitive task (human question asking), and (3) showing how complexity and informativeness trade off in practice. This differs from prior NIPS work on visual question generation (e.g., Mostafazadeh et al., 2016) which lacks compositional structure and goal-directedness.  
*Significance*: The model provides a concrete, scalable method for generating human-like questions in active learning contexts—a step toward more natural human-AI interaction. It establishes a framework that could inform future work in cognitive science (e.g., modeling how humans balance cognitive effort and information gain) and AI (e.g., building question-answering agents).

**Weaknesses**  
*Originality/Cognitive Interpretation*: The paper overstates cognitive claims: "our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity." The evidence is domain-specific (Battleship game) and indirect—the model’s success with complexity suggests humans optimize for it, but the sparse human data limits conclusions about *general* cognitive mechanisms. The correlation between EIG and human frequency is negligible (as noted in the paper), making the "balancing" interpretation tenuous.  
*Related Work*: The paper fails to cite key precursors: Hawkins et al. (2015) used EIG and complexity trade-offs in a simpler question-answering task, while Roberts (1996) formalized question-under-discussion (QUD) semantics, which underpins the "relevance" feature. This omission weakens the novelty claim.  
*Significance*: While technically promising, the domain-specific grammar (e.g., ship-size predicates) limits immediate applicability to broader settings. The paper acknowledges this but understates the effort required for domain adaptation.

**Pro/Con Summary**  
*Pro*:  
- First model to generate *novel, compositional* questions via program synthesis (not template-based).  
- Demonstrates a viable technical approach for human-like question generation in non-trivial settings.  
- Rigorous evaluation of feature importance (complexity is critical).  
*Con*:  
- Cognitive claims are overstated; evidence is limited to a single game domain.  
- Misses key citations (Hawkins 2015, Roberts 1996) that contextualize the "program" approach.  
- Sparse human data limits confidence in cognitive interpretations.

**Recommendation**  
Accept. The paper makes a clear technical contribution to modeling question generation with a novel compositional approach, validated in a challenging domain. While the cognitive claims require qualification, the core method is sound, well-executed, and advances the state of the art in active learning and program synthesis. The missing citations should be addressed in revision, but this does not undermine the paper’s technical merits. The model’s ability to produce novel, human-like questions represents a significant step toward more natural AI question-asking systems.
