

The paper introduces a cognitive model designed to predict when specific questions are posed within particular contexts (belief states). Under this framework, the unnormalized log probability of a question is defined as a weighted sum of four components: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (approximately question length), answer type, and "relevance" (whether the question pertains to the belief state rather than world-independent logical facts). This model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the objective is to identify ship locations (clusters of grid elements) within a grid. The full model, incorporating all four features, achieves superior predictive performance on held-out human data (measured by log-likelihood) compared to ablated variants omitting individual features. However, in terms of correlation with human judgments, the model excluding expected information gain (EIG) performs comparably to the full model.

The paper is generally technically sound. Nevertheless, its primary contribution appears stronger as an AI project than as a cognitive science contribution. While the authors claim the model "predicts what questions people will ask" and demonstrates "how people balance informativeness and complexity" from a cognitive science perspective, the evidence does not convincingly support this interpretation. In the Battleship domain, the results are ambiguous: log-likelihood improvements suggest a minor role for EIG (with bootstrap estimates indicating a 19% probability the observed effect is spurious), while correlation metrics—compromised by sparse human data—imply EIG may have negligible influence. Consequently, the findings do not robustly inform how people balance these factors in general.

The paper is well-written, clear, and reproducible. However, it omits critical prior work. A key claimed innovation—modeling questions as programs that execute on a possible world state to produce answers—corresponds directly to Hawkins' (2015) concept of a "goal" (in "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers?") and to Roberts' (1996) notion of a "question-under-discussion" (QUD, in "Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics"). Hawkins' questioner model similarly employs EIG and question complexity but applies to a much simpler domain.

Despite the cognitive science limitations, this approach—featuring a compositional prior over questions and selection based on EIG and other factors—represents a coherent and promising framework for both cognitive and AI modeling. I view the paper's main contribution as demonstrating the feasibility of generating meaningful compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this method.
