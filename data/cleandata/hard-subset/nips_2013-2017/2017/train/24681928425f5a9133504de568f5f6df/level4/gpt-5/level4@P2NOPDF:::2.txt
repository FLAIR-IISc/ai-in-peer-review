This paper studies how people ask K-ary (categorical) questions. The authors hand-specify a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) for a Battleship-style grid world with hidden colored ships, where some tiles are revealed as ship segments of a given color or as empty. The agent’s task is to ask a single-word-answer question that maximizes information about the underlying board. The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are represented as lambda-calculus expressions. Question quality is modeled as a linear function of three components: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (approximated by string length or by the negative log probability under the PCFG with a uniform prior over rewrite rules), and answer type (e.g., Boolean vs. color). Human question data from a prior study were annotated to fit the grammar. Using leave-one-out cross-validation with ablations (“lesions”) of each component, the authors show that all factors matter, with complexity clearly the strongest. They also present a set of novel, model-generated questions.

Question asking is a central topic in linguistics and hypothesis testing. This paper provides an innovative synthesis of modern linguistic and cognitive theory to analyze how people pose questions, and it could stimulate new work in active learning where agents pose more sophisticated queries. The paper is very clearly written, and the evaluation combines appropriate quantitative analyses with informative qualitative examples.

From a computational cognitive science standpoint, the work advances our understanding of self-directed learning. Prior findings suggested that question informativeness alone is a weak predictor of human question choice. This follow-up clarifies that informativeness is secondary to complexity and that the size of the available question space likely plays a role. As such, the paper makes a substantive contribution to the cognitive science literature and may by itself justify publication at NIPS.

That said, there are important limitations that could curb its impact for the broader NIPS audience. It is unclear how readily the approach scales or transfers to domains of wider interest, even if mapping natural-language questions to logical forms could be partially automated. The Battleship domain is small and low-dimensional, yet the current method already appears computationally demanding. The high-level idea—active learning with richer question types—remains compelling, but to appeal to a broader audience the authors should articulate a concrete, higher-impact application where their framework is tractable and yields insights unavailable to simpler methods.

Minor comments:
1) Consider a nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods of the ablated models (e.g., likelihood-ratio tests) to assess statistical significance.
2) For sequential question asking, there is relevant work in formal semantics and pragmatics, especially in the Questions under Discussion tradition. Two useful pointers:
- Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459–527.
- Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of SemDial (DialDam), 17, 131–139.
