This paper presents a cognitive model of question asking, where questions are represented as programs that are executed on a belief state to produce an answer. The model defines a probability distribution over the space of possible questions, framed as a log-linear model where a question's energy is a weighted sum of its features. These features include informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity (related to program length), answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number), and relevance (whether the question pertains to the task). The model is evaluated on its ability to predict human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game, where players must identify the location of hidden ships on a grid. The primary result is that the full model, using all features, outperforms lesioned versions (e.g., without informativeness or complexity) in predicting held-out human data based on log-likelihood. However, when evaluated on the correlation with human question frequencies, an information-agnostic model performs comparably to the full model.

**Quality and Significance**

The paper is technically sound and presents a compelling framework. The primary contribution is demonstrating the feasibility of generating interesting, compositional questions for a non-trivial domain by combining a programmatic representation with a probabilistic model sensitive to information gain and complexity. This approach of using a compositional prior over questions and selecting them based on EIG and other factors is a promising direction for building more sophisticated components for both AI and cognitive models.

However, the paper's claims about its contribution to cognitive science seem stronger than what the results support. The authors state, "our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity." I am not convinced that the findings presented here provide strong evidence for how this balance is struck. The evidence for the role of EIG in guiding human question-asking is mixed. While the log-likelihood results show a slight advantage for the full model over the information-agnostic one, the authors report that this advantage holds in only 81% of bootstrap samples, indicating a lack of statistical robustness. Furthermore, the correlation results show that the information-agnostic model performs just as well as the full model (Spearman's ρ of 0.65 vs. 0.64). Given the noise and sparsity of the human data, these results do not seem sufficient to draw firm conclusions about how people weigh these factors. Consequently, the work succeeds more as a contribution to AI—by building a system that can generate useful questions—than as a cognitive science project that explains human behavior.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide enough detail about the model, the grammar, and the experimental setup that the results appear to be reproducible.

**Originality**

A weakness of the paper is the omission of some relevant prior work. The central idea to "model questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer" is conceptually similar to the notion of a "goal" used in Hawkins et al. (2015, "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers"), which in turn is based on the concept of a question-under-discussion (QUD) from Roberts (1996, "Information structure in discourse..."). The model presented by Hawkins et al. also uses expected information gain and trades it off against question complexity. While the present paper applies this framework to a significantly more complex and interesting domain, the core theoretical components are not entirely novel and the related work should be acknowledged.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
*   Presents a novel and powerful framework for question asking as program generation.
*   Successfully demonstrates that this approach can generate complex, context-sensitive, and human-like questions in a non-trivial domain.
*   The overall approach is a valuable contribution to the field of active learning and AI, showing how to move beyond simple label queries.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The cognitive science claims about modeling how humans balance informativeness and complexity are not strongly supported by the mixed empirical results.
*   The paper omits key prior work on formal models of question asking that also trade off information gain and complexity.

In summary, this paper introduces a valuable computational framework and demonstrates its effectiveness in a challenging domain. The main contribution lies in showing that a programmatic approach to question generation is viable and promising. While its claims as a cognitive model of human behavior are overstated, its contribution as an AI system for intelligent question asking is significant.
