This paper explores the problem of reproducing user questions in a simplified Battleship game, framing it as an active learning task. The core idea is to replace direct queries about individual squares with questions posed in an artificial language possessing compositional, Montagovian semantics. A log-linear model is employed, leveraging four features of these questions: expected information gain (informativeness), answer type (Boolean vs. numerical), and indicators of board-related content.

While the motivation, viewed through the lens of active learning and grounding language in a game environment, is reasonable, the presented work ultimately feels incremental and lacks sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The underlying concepts are, in my assessment, rather straightforward and the task itself is significantly simplified and highly constrained. The logical ambiguity inherent in natural language question answering is largely absent here, due to the explicitly defined semantics and limited scope of the artificial language. 

Specifically, the simplification of Battleship to this formulation significantly reduces the complexity of the problem. The compositional semantics, while a key component, are applied within a remarkably restricted space, diminishing the challenges typically associated with semantic parsing and question generation. The four features used in the log-linear model appear intuitive, but their selection and weighting don’t represent a significant methodological advance. The paper doesn’t demonstrate substantial insight into *why* these features are effective, nor does it explore more sophisticated feature engineering or model architectures. 

Furthermore, the evaluation lacks a compelling baseline. While the paper demonstrates performance on reproducing questions, it remains unclear how this compares to simpler strategies, such as randomly generating questions satisfying basic grammatical constraints.  A stronger comparison to a more nuanced baseline, or an ablation study to determine the contribution of each feature, would strengthen the results. 

The presentation is clear and the technical details are adequately described. However, clarity does not compensate for a lack of substantial contribution. The paper reads more like a proof-of-concept demonstration than a significant research advance. The potential connections to broader active learning or natural language processing research areas are not fully realized, and the limitations of the highly constrained setting are not sufficiently acknowledged or addressed.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses a potentially interesting problem, the simplicity of the task, the lack of novelty in the approach, and the limited scope of the evaluation lead me to believe that this work is unlikely to be of significant interest to the NIPS community. I recommend rejection.
