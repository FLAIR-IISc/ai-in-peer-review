- Introduces a computational model of human question-asking in K-ary answer domains using a hand-crafted PCFG and lambda-calculus questions in a "Battleship" grid domain.  
- Questions scored by three factors: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (statement length or negative log PCFG probability), and answer type (e.g., yes/no vs. color).  
- Human question data were hand-coded into the grammar and used to train the model; model variants compared via leave-one-out cross-validation.  
- Results show all three factors contribute to predicting human questions, with complexity the strongest predictor.  
- Model can generate novel, domain-appropriate questions, not just reproduce human examples.  
- Theoretical advance: unifies linguistic and cognitive frameworks and explains why informativeness alone fails to predict human questioning.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and rigorously evaluated with quantitative and qualitative analyses.  
- Contribution is significant for computational cognitive science, resolving a persistent puzzle about human question selection.  
- Major limitation: scalability—Battleship is small and method is computationally intensive.  
- Major limitation: reliance on manual grammar engineering and hand-coding of questions raises concerns about generalization to real-world, large, or ambiguous domains.  
- Authors should identify high-impact application domains where PCFG-handling of rich question structures outperforms existing active learning methods.  
- Minor suggestions: perform nested model comparisons using lesioned log-likelihoods; engage with "Questions under Discussion" formal semantics for sequential questioning.  
- Overall: technically sound and valuable, but needs clearer justification of broader applicability to maximize impact.
