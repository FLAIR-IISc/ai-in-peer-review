The paper introduces a cognitive model designed to predict question selection based on underlying belief states. The model calculates the unnormalized log probability of a question using a weighted sum of informativeness (measured as expected information gain – EIG), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance to the current belief state versus world knowledge. This model was evaluated in the Battleship domain, a task involving locating hidden ships within a grid. Results demonstrate that the complete model, incorporating all features, outperformed ablated versions in predicting held-out human question data based on log-likelihood. However, the model’s performance, as measured by correlation with human judgments, was comparable with and without the inclusion of EIG, a discrepancy potentially attributable to noise in the relatively sparse human data and indicated by bootstrap estimates suggesting a 19% chance the log-likelihood results are spurious.

While technically sound, the paper’s contribution appears stronger as an artificial intelligence endeavor than a cognitive science one. The authors claim to predict human question choices and to demonstrate how people balance informativeness and complexity. However, the findings offer limited insight into the cognitive mechanisms underlying this balance. The Battleship results suggest either a minor influence of EIG, or no influence at all, making strong conclusions about human question-asking strategies difficult to support.

The paper is clearly written and the results appear reproducible. 

A significant omission is related work. The approach of modeling questions as programs that yield answers when executed on a world state aligns with the concept of “goals” as defined by Hawkins (2015) and builds upon the question-under-discussion (QUD) framework introduced by Roberts (1996). Hawkins (2015) also utilizes EIG and complexity trade-offs, albeit within a simpler domain.

Despite this, the proposed model – one with a compositional prior over questions and selection based on EIG and other factors – represents a valuable component for both cognitive and AI modeling, and is likely to see further application. The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating the feasibility of generating meaningful compositional questions within a complex, non-trivial domain using this approach.
