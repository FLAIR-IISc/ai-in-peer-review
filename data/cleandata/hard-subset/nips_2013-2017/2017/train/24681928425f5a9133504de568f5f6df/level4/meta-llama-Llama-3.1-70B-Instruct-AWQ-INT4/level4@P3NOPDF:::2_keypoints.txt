- Presents a study of human question-asking in a "battleship" domain using a hand-built PCFG and lambda-calculus question representations, where an agent asks single-answer questions to maximize information gain.
- Treats the PCFG as a prior and scores questions as a linear combination of expected information gain (informativeness), complexity (length / negative log PCFG prob), and answer type.
- Uses previously published human data manually coded into the grammar and evaluates models with leave-one-out cross-validation, showing all factors matter and complexity is the strongest predictor.
- Demonstrates the model can generate novel questions and provides both quantitative and qualitative evaluations.
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-written and offers a novel integration of linguistic and cognitive theories relevant to active learning and question-asking.
- Advances computational cognitive science by explaining why informativeness alone poorly predicts human questions—complexity and answer type are major factors—and may justify publication.
- Major limitation: scalability and applicability to broader, more complex domains are unclear given the small battleship domain and existing computational strain.
- Suggests further work to show concrete scenarios of applicability and novel insights to appeal to the broader NIPS community.
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods for statistical significance testing.
- Minor suggestion: consider additional literature on sequential question asking (Questions under Discussion), e.g., Ginzburg (1995) and Rojas-Esponda (2013).
