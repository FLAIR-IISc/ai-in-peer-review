- Paper studies human question-asking in a K-ary answer "battleship" domain using a manually defined PCFG and lambda-calculus question representations.  
- PCFG serves as a prior over question space; questions are statements in lambda calculus.  
- Question quality is modeled as a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log PCFG prob), and answer type.  
- Human data were manually coded into the grammar and evaluated with leave-one-out cross-validation of ablated models.  
- All three factors (informativeness, complexity, answer type) contribute to predicting human questions; complexity is the strongest predictor.  
- The paper includes qualitative analysis of novel questions generated by the model.  
- The approach unifies linguistic and cognitive theory and has potential to inspire work on more human-like active learning agents.  
- Paper is very clear, well-written, and appropriately evaluated with quantitative and qualitative methods.  
- Significant contribution to computational cognitive science: explains why informativeness alone poorly predicts human questions by highlighting complexity and question space size.  
- Major limitation: manual translation of natural language to logical forms raises scalability and automation concerns.  
- Major limitation: battleship domain is small and the method is already computationally strained, leaving unclear applicability to larger ML-relevant domains.  
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons using ablated-model log-likelihoods for formal significance testing.  
- Minor suggestion: consider literature on semantics/pragmatics for sequential question-asking, e.g., the Questions under Discussion (QuD) framework (Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
