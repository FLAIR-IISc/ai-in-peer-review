- Novel model unifying PCFG-based compositional semantics with Bayesian cognitive modeling to explain human question-asking.
- Uses a "battleship"-style domain where single-word-answer questions aim to maximize information about a hidden board.
- Questions are generated by a manually constructed PCFG producing lambda-calculus logical forms as a structured prior.
- Question utility is modeled as a linear combination of expected information gain, complexity (negative log PCFG probability), and answer type.
- Evaluated on hand-coded human questions with leave-one-out cross-validation and lesioned models showing all three factors contribute, with complexity most important.
- Paper is clear, well-written, and includes both quantitative and qualitative evaluations.
- Significant contribution to computational cognitive science by explaining why information-gain-only models fall short and highlighting representational complexity.
- Major scalability/generalizability concern: reliance on hand-crafted grammar in a simple domain limits extension to complex, open-ended tasks.
- Practical deployment issue: experiments require manual translation of natural language to logical forms; automatic parsing is a hard open problem.
- Computational-cost concern: method is already strained at small scale, casting doubt on applicability to larger state/action spaces.
- Paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of paths to scalability or concrete application scenarios where this approach outperforms existing methods.
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods for formal significance tests.
- Minor suggestion: consider the Questions Under Discussion (QUD) literature for modeling sequential/question-discourse constraints (e.g., Ginzburg 1995; Rojas-Esponda 2013).
