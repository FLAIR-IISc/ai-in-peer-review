

This paper proposes a cognitive model predicting human question-asking behavior in the Battleship domain, where agents infer ship placements in a grid. The model defines the log-unnormalized probability of a question as a weighted sum of four factors: expected information gain (EIG), question complexity (length), answer type, and "relevance" (whether the question references the current belief state). The full model outperforms ablated versions in predicting held-out human question-asking data (measured by log-likelihood), though a model omitting EIG correlates nearly as well with human judgments. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible.

The primary contribution lies in demonstrating that a compositional question-generation framework—treating questions as executable programs over possible worlds—can produce non-trivial, human-like questions in a complex domain. This approach aligns with existing work on goal-oriented question models (Hawkins, 2015) and question-under-discussion (QUD) theory (Roberts, 1996), though the paper underemphasizes these connections. The model’s strength is its practical implementation for generating structured questions, not its novel cognitive insights.

However, the paper overstates its cognitive science contribution. While it claims to show "how people balance informativeness and complexity," the evidence is ambiguous. The log-likelihood advantage of the full model (including EIG) is marginal (bootstrap p=0.19 for the difference), suggesting the EIG effect may not be robust. Conversely, the correlation analysis—which is noisy due to sparse human data—implies EIG might not significantly influence behavior. This contradiction prevents a clear conclusion about how people balance these factors. The results do not establish whether humans *use* EIG, *ignore* it, or weigh it differently in this domain. Without stronger evidence, the cognitive claims remain speculative.

The paper’s AI-oriented contribution is more compelling. It successfully demonstrates that EIG-based question generation, combined with compositional priors and complexity penalties, yields plausible questions in a non-trivial setting. This framework is a promising component for future cognitive and AI systems, and the authors rightly note its potential for broader application. The Battleship domain serves as a valid testbed for the model’s mechanics, but the domain’s simplicity limits generalizability.

Critical gaps require attention. First, the paper’s claim of novelty for "modeling questions as programs" conflates two concepts: (1) the compositional program representation (which appears in Hawkins, 2015), and (2) the specific integration of EIG and complexity. Hawkins already uses EIG to select questions in a simpler domain (e.g., guessing numbers), making the current model’s innovation primarily its scalability to Battleship. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this overlap and clarify how their implementation advances prior work. Second, the paper omits key cognitive science literature on question-asking (e.g., work on pragmatic reasoning in dialogue systems), weakening the contextual framing.

The model’s reliance on human data from a single domain (Battleship) further limits its cognitive claims. Without evidence from diverse contexts (e.g., social reasoning or collaborative problem-solving), the model cannot support general claims about human cognition. The paper’s focus on predictive accuracy over mechanistic explanation also misses an opportunity to probe *why* people ask certain questions—e.g., whether relevance or answer type drives behavior more than EIG.

In summary, the paper is a technically solid AI contribution showcasing a viable question-generation framework. Its cognitive science claims, however, are unsupported by the evidence presented. The results are consistent with multiple interpretations (EIG matters, EIG doesn’t matter, or the effect is context-dependent), and the sparse data prevents definitive conclusions. The authors should temper their cognitive assertions and clarify the model’s relationship to Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996). 

Despite these limitations, the work is valuable as a practical demonstration of compositional question generation. The model’s ability to produce structured, contextually relevant questions in a non-trivial domain advances both AI and cognitive modeling. The authors have successfully built a system that *can* generate human-like questions, even if it doesn’t yet explain *how* humans do it. Future work should address the evidence gaps and extend the model to richer domains to strengthen cognitive claims. For now, the paper’s strongest contribution is as a methodological step toward scalable question-answering systems, not a cognitive theory.
