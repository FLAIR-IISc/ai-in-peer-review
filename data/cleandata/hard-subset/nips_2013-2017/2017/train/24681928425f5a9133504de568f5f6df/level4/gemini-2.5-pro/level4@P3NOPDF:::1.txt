This paper presents a model for predicting user questions in a modified version of the game Battleship. In this setting, instead of querying individual grid squares, users ask questions from a structured, artificial language with compositional logical semantics. The authors employ a log-linear model to rank potential questions based on a small set of simple features, namely expected information gain (informativeness), answer type (Boolean or numerical), and whether the question is grounded in the board's configuration.

The paper's motivation, framed within the context of active learning and goal-oriented dialogue, is sound. By studying how humans seek information in a constrained environment, the work aims to shed light on principles of efficient inquiry. The experimental setup is well-controlled and the problem is clearly defined, which allows for a straightforward analysis.

However, the primary weakness of this work lies in its limited novelty and the simplicity of the task. The core idea of modeling question-asking as a process of maximizing information gain is a foundational concept in active learning and decision theory and has been explored extensively. The application of a simple log-linear model with a few hand-crafted features for this task is also a well-established and standard approach. The paper does not appear to introduce a new modeling technique, a significant theoretical insight, or a novel feature representation that advances the state of the art.

Furthermore, the chosen task, while methodologically clean, is overly simplistic and highly constrained, which limits the broader impact of the findings. The use of an artificial, logically unambiguous language sidesteps the most significant challenges in human-computer interaction and natural language understanding, such as parsing, semantic ambiguity, and grounding. The Battleship domain itself is a toy problem, and it is unclear how the conclusions drawn from this highly structured environment would generalize to more complex, realistic scenarios where the space of possible queries is vast and unstructured. The simplicity of the domain makes the problem of selecting an optimal question far more tractable than it would be in real-world applications that the NIPS community typically engages with.

Consequently, the contributions feel incremental at best. The paper confirms established principles (e.g., that humans value informativeness in their questions) within a new but simplified domain, without offering a significant new perspective or methodology.

In summary, while the paper is clearly written and the experimental premise is reasonable, the contributions are not substantial enough for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The ideas presented are largely straightforward applications of existing concepts to a toy problem that lacks the complexity and ambiguity characteristic of the challenges typically addressed by the machine learning community. Therefore, I do not believe this paper will be of significant interest to the NIPS readership.
