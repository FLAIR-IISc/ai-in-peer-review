

The paper introduces a cognitive model for predicting question-asking behavior in specific contexts (belief states). The model defines the unnormalized log-probability of a question as a weighted sum of four components: informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (question length), answer type, and "relevance" (whether the question pertains to the belief state rather than world-independent facts). Applied to the Battleship game—where players infer ship locations in a grid—the full model (incorporating all features) achieves higher log-likelihood on held-out human data than lesioned variants missing one or more features. However, correlation with human judgments is similar between the full model and a version excluding EIG, suggesting EIG may not be critical for predictive accuracy in this domain.

The technical approach is sound, and the paper is well-written with clear reproducibility. That said, the work appears stronger as an AI contribution than as cognitive science. While the authors claim their model "predicts what questions people will ask" and demonstrates "how people balance informativeness and complexity" from a cognitive science perspective, the evidence for such claims is weak. The Battleship results present a contradiction: log-likelihood improvements suggest EIG influences behavior (though bootstrap estimates indicate a 19% probability the observed advantage is not statistically significant), while correlation metrics (compromised by sparse human data) imply EIG has negligible impact. This ambiguity prevents meaningful conclusions about how people trade off these factors in general. The paper’s cognitive science claims thus overreach the data.

The work also overlooks key prior art. A stated innovation—"modeling questions as programs that execute on possible worlds to output answers"—directly mirrors Hawkins’ (2015) "goal" framework for question-answering, which similarly uses EIG and question complexity. Hawkins’ approach also builds on Roberts’ (1996) question-under-discussion (QUD) concept. While Hawkins’ model operates in a simpler domain, the core methodology is not novel. The paper should explicitly acknowledge this lineage to position its contribution accurately.

Despite these gaps, the model’s approach—using a compositional prior over questions, optimized via EIG and other factors—is conceptually compelling and likely to influence future work in both cognitive science and AI. The paper’s primary contribution lies in demonstrating that this framework can generate meaningful compositional questions for non-trivial domains like Battleship, rather than revealing new insights into human cognition. For cognitive science, the results offer limited mechanistic understanding of question-asking behavior; for AI, they provide a useful methodological template. The work would benefit from stronger alignment with cognitive theory and clearer articulation of its limitations relative to existing frameworks.
