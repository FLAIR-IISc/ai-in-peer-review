- Summary: Models question generation in a Battleship-style game using a compositional artificial language and a log-linear ranker with features including expected information gain, answer type, and board-feature references.

- Strength: Motivating framework aligns with active learning—selecting queries by information maximization is reasonable.

- Strength: Compositional semantics makes question meanings unambiguous and enables precise information-gain computation.

- Strength: Transparent, interpretable log-linear model facilitates analysis of which question properties matter.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—using information gain and simple structural features is standard and unsurprising in this controlled setting.

- Weakness: Narrow, artificial, noise-free domain with a tiny hypothesis space limits the significance and challenge of results.

- Weakness: Simplistic modeling—hand-crafted linear features only, with no learned semantics or non-myopic planning explored.

- Weakness: Insufficient evaluation—no comparisons to strong baselines (pure IG, random, cost-sensitive), no feature ablations, and no robust human-data validation.

- Weakness: Unclear whether learned weights provide insight beyond “ask the most informative question” given lack of noise, ambiguity, pragmatics, or resource constraints.

- Weakness: Inconsistency in the paper’s feature count (claims four features but lists three) needs correction.

- Weakness: Generalization and scalability are not demonstrated—unclear how approach handles larger boards, richer languages, partial observability, or scaling of candidate sets.

- Question: Precisely define hypothesis space, priors, and answer model; how is expected information gain computed/approximated?

- Question: What is the fourth feature, and how sensitive are results to each feature (request feature ablation)?

- Question: What baselines are used—how does the model compare to (i) argmax IG, (ii) random selection, and (iii) cost/length-penalized IG?

- Question: Are there human question-asking data, and does the model match human behavior beyond informativeness?

- Question: How large is the candidate question set, is there search/pruning, and does the method scale with language size?

- Question: Is the strategy non-myopic (multi-step planning) or purely greedy?

- Suggestion: Strengthen evaluation with robust baselines, feature ablations, sensitivity analyses, and experiments on larger/more complex boards.

- Suggestion: Add realistic complexity—noise, ambiguity, partial observability, answer costs, or time/complexity penalties and evaluate cost-sensitive trade-offs.

- Suggestion: Broaden the language to richer compositional constructions and test scaling of semantics.

- Suggestion: Incorporate or compare to human data and analyze model–human agreements and disagreements.

- Suggestion: Explore non-myopic decision-making and planning beyond single-step IG.

- Suggestion: Better position the work relative to active learning, decision-theoretic question asking, and pragmatic language modeling literature.

- Suggestion: Provide code, data, and precise task specification for reproducibility.

- Overall assessment: Sensible motivation and clear setup, but current scope, novelty, and evaluation are too limited for NIPS; major extensions and stronger empirical validation are required.
