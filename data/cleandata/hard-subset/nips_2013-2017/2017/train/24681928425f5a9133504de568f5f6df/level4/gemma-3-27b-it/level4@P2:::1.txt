This paper investigates question asking as a form of program generation, focusing on a simplified “Battleship” game where players learn hidden information by posing questions expressed in a compositional, logic-based language. The authors propose a probabilistic model, utilizing a log-linear framework with features representing expected information gain, answer type, and relevance to the game board, to predict and generate human-like questions.

The motivation, connecting active learning with richer, more human-inspired querying strategies, is sound. However, the core ideas presented feel incremental. The approach of modeling questions as programs and utilizing compositional semantics, while conceptually interesting, builds heavily on established work in linguistics and cognitive science (e.g., Fodor, 1975; Chouinard, 2005). Furthermore, the task itself, though well-defined, is highly constrained and lacks the ambiguity inherent in real-world question asking scenarios. 

The paper demonstrates the model’s ability to predict question frequencies and generate novel questions within the game context. The results show that complexity, alongside informativeness and answer type, plays a crucial role in generating human-like questions, a finding supported by the comparative analysis of model variants. However, the extent to which these findings generalize beyond this specific, simplified domain remains unclear. The success of the model is also predicated on a carefully designed grammar and pre-defined features, raising concerns about the scalability and adaptability to more complex environments.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Formulation:** The paper clearly frames question asking as a program generation problem, providing a formal perspective on a traditionally cognitive process.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions, providing quantitative evidence for its predictive capabilities.
*   **Feature Analysis:** The ablation study highlights the importance of complexity, alongside informativeness, in modeling human question asking behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The core ideas build heavily on established concepts in linguistics and cognitive science, lacking significant conceptual breakthroughs.
*   **Constrained Task:** The “Battleship” game is a simplified domain with limited ambiguity, potentially overstating the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Grammar Dependency:** The performance relies heavily on a pre-defined grammar, raising questions about scalability to more complex scenarios.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The paper does not compare to a variety of baselines beyond the feature ablation, and would be stronger if it considered other relevant machine learning methods.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a formal and computationally tractable approach to modeling question asking.
*   Offers empirical insights into the factors influencing human question generation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The core ideas are not particularly novel and build heavily on existing literature.
*   The limited scope and simplified task may restrict the generalizability of the findings.
*   The paper's relevance to the core NIPS audience may be limited, as it leans heavily on cognitive science concepts and a specific game-playing context. 

**Overall:** I believe this paper is a solid piece of work, but ultimately falls short of the originality and impact expected for NIPS. The problem setting feels contrived, and the methodological contributions are incremental. I recommend **rejection**.
