This paper presents an innovative investigation into human question asking in scenarios with K-ary answers, focusing on the interplay between informativeness, complexity, and answer type. The authors tackle this problem within a defined “battleship” domain – a partially observable grid containing hidden colored shapes – and model question generation using a Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG). Questions are formalized as statements in lambda calculus, and their “goodness” is evaluated as a linear combination of expected information gain, grammatical complexity (measured by length and PCFG probability), and answer type characteristics. Crucially, the PCFG was built by hand-coding data from a prior study of human question asking into a formal grammar. The model was evaluated through leave-one-out cross-validation of lesioned versions, demonstrating that all three factors contribute to question selection, with complexity playing the dominant role. The authors also present a set of novel questions generated by their model.

Question asking is a central topic in both linguistics and cognitive science, with implications for active learning. This work offers a valuable perspective by unifying linguistic and cognitive theory to analyze human questioning behavior. The potential to inform more sophisticated question-asking strategies within active learning models is significant. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, and the evaluation strategy appropriately combines quantitative and qualitative analysis. 

From a computational cognitive science viewpoint, this paper represents progress in the field of self-directed learning. Prior research demonstrated that question informativeness alone is a weak predictor of human question choices. This work sheds light on why, revealing that complexity considerations are paramount, and that the limited predictive power of informativeness may be due to the restricted space of possible questions. This contribution to the cognitive science literature is substantial and, in my opinion, sufficient to justify publication at NIPS.

However, the approach has significant limitations that may constrain its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While the authors demonstrate a method for translating human questions into a formal semantic representation, the scalability and generalizability of this method to more complex domains remain unclear. The battleship domain is relatively small, with a limited feature space, and the current implementation already experiences computational strain. The core idea – incorporating richer, more linguistically-motivated questions into active learning – is compelling, but the authors need to articulate a clear scenario where this technique could provide novel insights in a domain of wider interest to the NIPS community. A discussion of potential applications beyond the battleship domain, and a roadmap for adapting the methodology, would strengthen the paper considerably.

**Minor Comments:**

1) Performing a nested model comparison, using the log-likelihoods (LLs) of the lesioned models, would allow for statistical testing of the differences in model fit. This would provide a more rigorous assessment of the relative contributions of each factor.

2) For future work considering sequential question asking, the authors should be aware of existing literature in formal semantics and pragmatics, particularly the “Questions under Discussion” framework. These approaches offer alternative models of question generation and interpretation. I recommend exploring the following references:

*   Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy, 18*(5), 459-527.
*   Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17*, 131-139.
