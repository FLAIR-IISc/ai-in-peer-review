The paper presents a cognitive model that is intended to predict when certain questions are asked in certain contexts (belief states). Under this model, the unnormalized log probability of a question is defined as the weighted sum of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (roughly, question length), answer type, and "relevance" (whether the question refers to the belief state or just world-independent logical facts). This model is applied to the Battleship domain where the task is to figure out where within a grid a number of ships (clusters of grid elements) are located. The full model with all of the features mentioned above did better at predicting held-out human data (in terms of log-likelihood) than lesioned versions with fewer features. In terms of correlation with human judgments, the model that didn't take into account EIG did about as well as the full model.

The paper seems generally technically sound. However, I have some reservations about its contribution to cognitive science. While the paper states that "We find that our model predicts what question people will ask" and "from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity," I'm not convinced that we learn much about how people balance these factors. In the Battleship domain, it seems that people are either influenced by EIG a little (if we believe the log-likelihood results, which according to bootstrap estimates have a 19% chance of being wrong) or not at all (if we believe the correlation results, which are also pretty noisy due to sparse human data). I don't think these results allow us to conclude much about how people balance these factors in general.

The paper is well-written and clear. I could probably reproduce the results. However, I think the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the current approach and the potential avenues for future research.

One area for improvement is the discussion of prior work. The paper mentions some related work in the field, but it seems to be missing some important references. For example, the idea of modeling questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer, is not new and has been explored in previous work (e.g., Hawkins, 2015). Additionally, the concept of a question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts (1996) is relevant to the paper's approach. I think it would be helpful to discuss these prior works in more detail and explain how the current paper builds upon or differs from them.

Overall, I think the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of artificial intelligence, particularly in the area of question generation. The model presented in the paper is well-suited for generating interesting compositional questions for non-trivial domains, and I expect it to be used more in the future. However, I'm not convinced that the paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of human cognition, and I think the authors could have done more to situate their work in the context of prior research in cognitive science.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a technically sound model for question generation that is well-suited for non-trivial domains.
* The model is able to generate interesting compositional questions that are similar to those asked by humans.
* The paper is well-written and clear, making it easy to follow and understand.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's contribution to cognitive science is limited, and the results do not provide significant insights into how people balance informativeness and complexity.
* The paper is missing some important references to prior work in the field.
* The discussion of limitations and future research directions is limited.

Recommendation:

I would recommend accepting the paper, but with the condition that the authors revise the paper to address the issues mentioned above. Specifically, I would like to see a more detailed discussion of prior work and a clearer explanation of how the current paper builds upon or differs from previous research. Additionally, I would like to see a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the current approach and the potential avenues for future research.
