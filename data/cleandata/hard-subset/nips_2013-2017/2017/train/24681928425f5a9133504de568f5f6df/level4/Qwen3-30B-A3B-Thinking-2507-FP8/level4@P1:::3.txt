

The paper introduces a computational model that treats questions as executable programs operating on possible world states, with the goal of predicting and generating human-like questions in a Battleship-style game. The model uses a log-linear framework where question probability is determined by weighted features: expected information gain (EIG), program complexity (log-grammar probability), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and relevance (whether the question references the game state). Evaluated on 605 human questions across 18 game contexts, the full model (incorporating all features) significantly outperformed variants in predicting held-out human question distributions (log-likelihood), with the complexity-agnostic variant showing the largest drop. While the model generates novel, contextually relevant questions (e.g., "Are all ships horizontal?"), the cognitive science claims require nuance.

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with rigorous evaluation via cross-validation and bootstrap analysis. The model’s ability to generate novel questions demonstrates a meaningful contribution to program synthesis. However, the paper’s cognitive science claims are overstated. The log-likelihood advantage of the full model over the EIG-agnostic variant (81% of bootstrap samples) suggests EIG plays a role, yet the correlation between predicted energy and human question frequencies (Spearman’s ρ=0.64) is comparable to the EIG-agnostic model (ρ=0.65), and the correlation between EIG alone and human frequencies is negligible [19]. This inconsistency undermines the assertion that the model reveals *how* humans balance informativeness and complexity. The sparsity of human data (many questions asked once) limits the robustness of these conclusions. The model also relies on domain-specific grammar engineering (e.g., ship size, color primitives), which may not generalize without significant reconfiguration.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear, with well-structured explanations of the program-based question representation, grammar design, and probabilistic model. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail to reproduce the experiments. The writing effectively bridges cognitive science and computational modeling.

**Originality**: The core idea—modeling questions as executable programs for inference—builds on Hawkins et al. (2015) [19] ("Why do you ask?") and Roberts (1996) [18] ("Information structure in discourse"). While the application to a non-trivial domain (Battleship with compositional questions) is novel, the formalism itself is not. The paper should explicitly acknowledge these foundational works. The model’s compositionality (e.g., using lambda expressions for "Are all ships the same size?") is a clear technical advance over prior neural approaches [16, 20, 22], which require large image-question datasets and lack intentional question generation.

**Significance**: The work makes a strong contribution to *AI* by demonstrating a scalable framework for generating contextually relevant, compositional questions. It provides a principled alternative to canned or stereotyped question-asking systems [1, 25]. For *cognitive science*, the significance is weaker: the model does not advance understanding of human question-asking mechanisms beyond confirming that humans prefer concise, informative questions. The cognitive claims would be strengthened by showing cross-domain generalization or direct behavioral evidence of the EIG-complexity trade-off.

**Strengths**:  
- Novel application of program synthesis to open-ended question generation.  
- Rigorous comparison of model variants via log-likelihood and qualitative novelty assessment.  
- Clear demonstration of compositional question generation (e.g., novel questions like "What is the column of the top left of the tiles that have the color of the bottom right corner?").  
- Well-motivated features (EIG, complexity, answer type) grounded in cognitive principles.

**Weaknesses**:  
- Overstated cognitive science contribution; evidence for human EIG-complexity trade-offs is inconsistent.  
- Domain-specific grammar limits generalizability; unclear how much knowledge engineering is required for new domains.  
- Missing citations to foundational work on program-based questions (Hawkins et al., 2015; Roberts, 1996).  
- Sparse human data limits confidence in correlation-based claims.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a valuable technical contribution to AI and active learning, demonstrating a scalable, compositional approach to question generation. While the cognitive science claims require qualification, the primary focus on computational modeling is well-executed and significant. The work provides a strong foundation for future extensions to broader domains and could inspire new AI dialogue systems. The missing citations to Hawkins and Roberts should be added in revision.
