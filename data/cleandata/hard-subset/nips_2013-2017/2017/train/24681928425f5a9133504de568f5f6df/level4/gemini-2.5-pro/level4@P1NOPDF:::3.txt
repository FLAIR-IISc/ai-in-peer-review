This paper presents a cognitive model designed to predict which questions people ask in specific contexts or belief states. The model calculates the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of several factors: informativeness (expected information gain, or EIG), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance (i.e., whether the question pertains to the current belief state or to world-independent facts).

The model is evaluated in the Battleship domain, where the goal is to locate ships on a grid. The full model, incorporating all features, outperforms ablated versions in predicting held-out human data as measured by log-likelihood. However, when measured by correlation with human judgments, a model that excludes EIG performs just as well as the full model.

The paper is technically sound and the methodology is clearly described, suggesting the results are reproducible. Its primary contribution lies in demonstrating that a model with a compositional prior over questions, guided by EIG and other pragmatic factors, can generate interesting and relevant questions in a non-trivial domain. This approach is a promising component for future AI and cognitive systems, and this work serves as an important proof of concept.

However, the paper is more successful as an AI engineering achievement than as a cognitive science study. The authors claim that "our model predicts what question people will ask" and that "from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity." The evidence presented, however, is insufficient to support strong conclusions about the cognitive mechanisms of human inquiry. In the Battleship domain, the results regarding the influence of EIG are ambiguous: the log-likelihood results suggest a weak effect (which, by the authors' own bootstrap estimates, has a 19% chance of being spurious), while the correlation results suggest no effect at all. Given this noisy and conflicting evidence from a single task, it is difficult to generalize about how people balance these factors.

Furthermore, the paper overlooks key prior work. The central idea to "model questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer" is presented as a novel contribution. However, this formalism is conceptually equivalent to the notion of a "goal" used in Hawkins et al. (2015, "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers"), which itself builds on the concept of a Question-Under-Discussion (QUD) from Roberts (1996, "Information structure in discourse..."). The questioner model in Hawkins et al. also employs a trade-off between expected information gain and question complexity. While the present work applies this framework to a significantly more complex and interesting domain, the paper should acknowledge these foundational ideas.

In summary, this is a well-executed paper that demonstrates a powerful method for computational question generation. Its main value is in showing the viability of using structured probabilistic models to explore a rich hypothesis space of questions in a complex setting. The claims about modeling human cognition are not fully supported by the ambiguous experimental results, but the work remains a valuable contribution to the AI literature on inquiry and information-seeking.
