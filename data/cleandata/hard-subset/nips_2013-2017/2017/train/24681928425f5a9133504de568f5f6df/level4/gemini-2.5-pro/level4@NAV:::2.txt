This work investigates human question-asking in contexts with K-ary answers. The authors manually construct a Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) for a "battleship"-style domain featuring concealed, colored shapes on a grid with partial observability. The agent's objective is to formulate a single-word-answer question that maximally reduces uncertainty about the board's state. This PCFG functions as a prior over the space of possible questions, which are represented as lambda calculus expressions. The quality of a question is modeled as a linear combination of three components: its informativeness (measured by expected information gain), its complexity (defined by its length or negative log probability under a uniform-rule PCFG), and its "answer type" (e.g., boolean vs. color). The model is evaluated on human data from a prior study, which the authors hand-encoded into their proposed grammar. Through leave-one-out cross-validation comparing ablated versions of their model, the authors demonstrate that all three factors make a non-negligible contribution, with complexity being the most significant predictor. The paper concludes by showcasing a set of novel questions generated by their model.

The paper addresses question-asking, a significant area of research at the intersection of linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors present a novel framework that integrates contemporary linguistic and cognitive theories to model how humans ask questions. This approach holds considerable promise for advancing the active learning literature, potentially enabling models to generate more complex and human-like queries. The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and clear. The evaluation is thorough, incorporating both quantitative and qualitative analyses. From a computational cognitive science viewpoint, this work makes a valuable contribution to the study of self-directed learning. It builds upon prior findings that showed question informativeness alone is insufficient to predict human question-asking behavior. This paper offers a compelling explanation for this discrepancy, suggesting that informativeness is secondary to question complexity and is constrained by the vast space of possible questions. This insight constitutes a meaningful contribution to cognitive science, which on its own could warrant publication.

Despite its strengths, the proposed approach has significant limitations that may restrict its appeal to the broader machine learning audience. While the manual encoding of human questions into semantic logic could potentially be automated, the paper does not address how the framework would scale or generalize to more complex domains of wider interest. The "battleship" domain is constrained, with few features, yet the methodology already appears to be computationally intensive. Nevertheless, the core concepts—particularly active learning with a richer set of questions—are compelling. To broaden its impact, the authors should articulate a clear and compelling application scenario where their technique could be applied to provide novel insights for the general community.

Minor comments:
1) A nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods from the lesioned models could provide formal tests of statistical significance for the contribution of each component.
2) For future work on sequential question-asking, the authors might consider engaging with existing models in formal semantics and pragmatics. The "Questions under Discussion" (QuD) framework is particularly relevant. The following references serve as an entry point to this literature:
Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459-527.
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131-139.
