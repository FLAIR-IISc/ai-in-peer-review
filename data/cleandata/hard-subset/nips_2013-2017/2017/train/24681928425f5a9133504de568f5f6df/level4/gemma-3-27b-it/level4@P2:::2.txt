The authors investigate human question asking in scenarios requiring K-ary answers. They introduce a framework modeling questions as formal programs – specifically, statements in lambda calculus – within a “battleship” domain. This domain involves hidden colored shapes on a partially observable grid, where the agent aims to identify ship configurations by asking questions with single-word answers. A probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) is defined over these programs, serving as a prior, and question quality is assessed via a linear function incorporating informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (negative log probability under the PCFG), and answer type. The model is evaluated on a pre-existing dataset, with questions hand-coded into their grammar. Comparisons between models with ablated components, using leave-one-out cross-validation, demonstrate the contribution of each factor, with complexity proving most important. The paper concludes with examples of novel, generated questions.

This work presents an innovative perspective on human question asking, unifying linguistic and cognitive theory to examine how people formulate inquiries. This offers potential for advancing active learning research by enabling models to pose more sophisticated questions. The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and the evaluation is robust, combining quantitative and qualitative analysis. From a computational cognitive science viewpoint, this research builds upon previous findings suggesting informativeness alone is insufficient to explain human questioning behavior. By highlighting the importance of complexity, the authors contribute valuable insights to the field of self-directed learning and provide a compelling rationale for publication at NIPS.

However, significant limitations restrict the broader appeal of this work to the NIPS community. While the approach demonstrates promise, scaling beyond the constrained “battleship” domain remains a substantial challenge. The domain’s small feature space already strains computational resources, raising concerns about applicability to more complex, real-world problems. Furthermore, while the authors acknowledge the need for translating natural language questions into their semantic logic, the feasibility and automation of this process are unclear. A critical path to broader acceptance lies in demonstrating the technique's potential in domains of wider interest and showcasing novel insights attainable only through their method. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The work presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling question asking as program generation, bridging insights from linguistics, cognitive science, and active learning.
*   **Clarity & Soundness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly explaining the model and evaluation process. The technical aspects are sound, and the results are well-supported.
*   **Cognitive Relevance:** The findings contribute meaningfully to our understanding of human question asking, resolving a known discrepancy in previous research regarding the role of informativeness.
*   **Strong Evaluation:** The model is evaluated rigorously with both quantitative (cross-validation) and qualitative (novel question generation) analyses.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scalability:** The computational complexity and domain specificity of the approach raise concerns about its applicability to more complex, real-world scenarios.
*   **Domain Dependence:** The reliance on a manually defined grammar for a specific domain (“battleship”) hinders generalizability.
*   **Missing Statistical Tests:** While the authors provide comparison between model variants, the difference between the models lacks statistical significance.

**Minor Comments:**

1.  Performing nested model comparison with likelihood ratio tests between the lesioned models would strengthen the quantitative analysis and provide statistical significance to the comparisons.
2.  Future work could benefit from engaging with the broader literature on question-asking in formal semantics and pragmatics, such as the "Questions under Discussion" framework (e.g., Ginzburg, 1995; Rojas-Esponda, 2013).
