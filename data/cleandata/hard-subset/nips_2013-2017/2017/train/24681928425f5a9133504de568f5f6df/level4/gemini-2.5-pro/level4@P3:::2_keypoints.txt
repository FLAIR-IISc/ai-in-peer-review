- Introduces a novel model of human question-asking as probabilistic program generation in a compositional lambda-calculus language.
- Defines a log-linear probability over question-programs that favors informativeness (EIG), structural simplicity (PCFG complexity), and accounts for answer types.
- Uses a hand-designed PCFG and evaluates on human questions from a Battleship-like game with leave-one-out cross-validation.
- Full model fits human question distributions well; lesion studies show complexity is the strongest predictor, with informativeness and answer type also important.
- Model is generative and can produce novel, contextually relevant questions not seen in the data.
- Strength: Original conceptualization unifying active learning, cognitive science, and formal linguistics by treating questions as programs.
- Strength: Technically sound with thorough quantitative prediction, qualitative generation, and effective lesion analyses.
- Strength: Exceptionally clear and well-written exposition of a complex model.
- Strength: Significant cognitive-science insight explaining why EIG alone often fails by showing complexity constrains question selection.
- Weakness: Scalability and generalizability limited by reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar requiring expert effort.
- Weakness: Computational tractability concerns—approximations and context exclusions were necessary even in the small domain.
- Weakness: Lacks automated grounding from natural language to program representations; human data was manually translated.
- Minor: Recommend nested model comparisons on reported log-likelihoods for formal significance testing between full and lesioned models.
- Minor: Suggests leveraging Questions under Discussion (QUD) literature for modeling sequential question-asking and dialogue context.
