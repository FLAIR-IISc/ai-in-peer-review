

The authors investigate human question-asking behavior where answers are K-ary. They manually define a PCFG for a "battleship" domain, featuring hidden colored shapes in a partially observable grid (where some tiles reveal partial ship segments of specific colors or indicate emptiness). The agent's task is to pose a single-word question that maximizes information about the board state. The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, defined as lambda calculus statements. Question quality is modeled as a linear combination of three factors: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (measured by question length or negative log probability under the PCFG with uniform rule selection), and "answer type" (e.g., true/false or color-based answers). Human question data, sourced from a prior publication, was hand-coded into the authors' grammar. The authors evaluate lesioned model variants via leave-one-out cross-validation, demonstrating that all factors contribute non-negligibly (with complexity being the most significant). They also present a series of novel questions.

Question-asking represents a key domain in linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors offer an innovative synthesis of modern linguistic and cognitive theory to examine human question-asking, with potential to advance active learning research by enabling models to formulate more sophisticated questions. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, with rigorous evaluation from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this work advances self-directed learning. Previous research identified question informativeness as a poor predictor of human behavior; this paper resolves part of this discrepancy (complexity is secondary to informativeness and may stem from the limited question space available to the model). Thus, the contribution to cognitive science literature is substantial and potentially sufficient for NIPS publication.

However, the approach faces serious scalability limitations that may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. While automating human questions into semantic logic statements may be feasible in constrained domains, it remains unclear how this method could extend to domains of wider interest. The battleship domain is small (with few features), and the approach is already computationally intensive. The authors should clarify a compelling scenario where their technique applies to broader communities and yields novel insights.

Minor comments:  
1) Nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods of lesioned models could provide statistical significance tests.  
2) For sequential question-asking, the linguistics literature offers relevant models (e.g., Questions under Discussion framework). Key references:  
Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459-527.  
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131-139.
