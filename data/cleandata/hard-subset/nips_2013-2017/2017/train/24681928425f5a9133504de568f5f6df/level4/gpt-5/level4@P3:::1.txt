Summary
The paper proposes a computational model of human question asking by representing questions as executable programs. A probabilistic context-free grammar defines a compositional space of question programs (lambda calculus/LISP-like), and a log-linear model assigns probabilities to programs using features that capture informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (−log prior under the grammar), answer type (Boolean, Number, Color, Location), and a relevance filter. Informativeness is computed via Bayesian updating over a Battleship-like hypothesis space and averaging expected entropy reduction across possible answers.

The model is trained to predict the distribution of questions humans asked in an information-search game with 605 questions across 18 contexts. Evaluation uses leave-one-context-out cross-validation. The full model outperforms ablated variants that omit informativeness, complexity, or answer-type features, both in approximate log-likelihood of human questions and in rank correlation between model energy and empirical frequencies. The system can also sample novel questions not in the training set, filtered for semantic equivalence.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself between active learning (label queries and interventions), program-based approaches to reasoning and semantic parsing, and recent neural question generation work (dialog systems, VQG). Unlike goal-directed dialog with limited templates or large-scale neural generation trained on massive corpora, this work emphasizes goal-driven question utility and compositionality in a small-data regime. It is aligned with cognitive science perspectives on compositional semantics and questions as partitions over possible worlds.

Strengths
- Clear and principled framing: Questions as programs provides a clean bridge between compositional semantics, active learning, and cognitive modeling.
- Interpretable features: Combining EIG with a complexity prior and answer-type preferences explains why high-EIG but unwieldy questions are dispreferred.
- Empirical evaluation: Leave-one-context-out prediction and ablation studies show that both informativeness and complexity matter; complexity is particularly critical.
- Generativity: The grammar supports creation of novel, context-appropriate questions; qualitative samples are sensible and sometimes clever.
- Connection to theory: The approach operationalizes classic ideas about questions (partitions, informativeness) in a concrete generative model.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Narrow, engineered domain: The grammar contains significant Battleship-specific primitives; this limits generality and risks overfitting domain regularities.
- Approximate inference: Likelihoods and gradients rely on importance sampling from a uniform PCFG proposal over a huge program space; stability and variance are not fully characterized. Some contexts/rules (e.g., draw) were dropped for computational reasons.
- Evaluation metrics: Predictive performance is summarized via approximate log-likelihood and rank correlations; human judgments of the sampled questions’ quality are not collected, and the “creativity” evaluation is qualitative.
- Limited baselines: There are no comparisons to alternative generative models (e.g., retrieval/nearest-neighbor over prior questions, template-based recombination, or simple neural conditional generators), nor to utility-driven policies without program grammars.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but some technical details are deferred to supplements (full grammar, hypothesis space sizes). A clearer discussion of computational cost, variance of importance sampling estimates, and sensitivity to grammar design would help.

Originality
- The representation of questions as executable programs is not entirely new in semantics/QA, but applying it to open-ended question generation with a learned utility- and complexity-aware distribution is novel in this precise form. However, the modeling choices (log-linear over features, EIG, PCFG prior) are relatively standard.

Significance
- As a cognitive model, the work offers useful insight: people balance informativeness with complexity and type preferences. As a machine learning contribution, the impact is tempered by the limited domain, manual grammar engineering, and lack of broader-domain demonstrations or competitive baselines. The bridge to natural language (semantic parsing/generation) is acknowledged but not realized.

Suggestions for improvement
- Broaden evaluation: Test in at least one additional domain with different primitives (e.g., a simple relational or visual domain) to assess portability of the grammar and features.
- Strengthen baselines: Include retrieval/template baselines, a conditional neural generator (e.g., seq2seq over linearized contexts-to-questions), and a utility-only policy without a grammar to quantify the added value of compositional program generation.
- Human evaluation: Collect ratings from human judges on the informativeness and clarity of sampled questions in held-out contexts.
- Robustness analysis: Report variance of importance sampling estimates, learning curves, sensitivity to grammar choices and length limits, and computational costs.
- Language interface: Demonstrate a small end-to-end pipeline by mapping natural language to programs (with a basic semantic parser) and back for the generated questions.
- Utility variants: Explore alternative objectives (e.g., cost-sensitive utilities, answerer workload models) beyond EIG and analyze cases where EIG correlates poorly with human frequencies.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant formulation with interpretable components.
- Empirical evidence that complexity and informativeness jointly explain human question preferences.
- Demonstrates compositional generalization and novel question generation.

Cons for acceptance
- Domain-specific grammar and narrow task limit generality.
- Lack of strong ML baselines and end-to-end language handling.
- Evaluation relies on approximate likelihoods and qualitative samples; no human validation of generated outputs.
- Computational constraints required pruning parts of the task.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents a thoughtful and interpretable model with solid cognitive motivation and interesting results in a controlled domain. However, for NIPS, the methodological novelty and empirical scope are limited. Strengthening the evaluation with broader domains, competitive baselines, and human assessments of generated questions, and demonstrating an end-to-end link to natural language, would substantially increase its impact and suitability.
