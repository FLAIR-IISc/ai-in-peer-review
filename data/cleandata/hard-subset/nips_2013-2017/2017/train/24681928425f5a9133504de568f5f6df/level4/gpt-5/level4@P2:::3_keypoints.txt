- Proposes a probabilistic model that treats questions as executable programs over possible world states using a typed, compositional grammar.
- Uses a lambda-calculus/LISP-style grammar to define a large hypothesis space of question-programs.
- Employs a log-linear model with features for informativeness (EIG), complexity (−log PCFG probability), answer type, and relevance to the game state.
- Learns parameters via importance sampling and gradient ascent with approximated partition functions.
- Evaluated on a Battleship-style dataset of 605 natural-language questions across 18 ambiguous board contexts.
- Predicts held-out human question distributions (log-likelihood) and generates novel, context-appropriate questions.
- Ablations show removing complexity causes a large performance drop; removing informativeness or type yields smaller but noticeable declines.
- Mean correlation between model energies and human frequencies is 0.64; an information-agnostic variant sometimes matches this correlation.
- System can synthesize creative, context-appropriate questions not seen in training.
- Positions work between active learning and cognitive modeling, relating to QUD semantics and prior EIG-based models.
- Connects to semantic parsing and question generation literature but emphasizes goal-directed informativeness and compositional generation.
- Main novelty: scaling a compositional, program-based question generator to a richer domain and matching human distributions.
- Methodologically sound: grammar covers dataset, model and sampling training are appropriate, and evaluations use leave-one-context-out fits with ablations.
- Caveat: informativeness effect is mixed—benefit is modest/uncertain and sometimes matched by an information-agnostic model.
- Caveat: complexity feature depends on PCFG rule probabilities, potentially conflating cognitive brevity preferences with grammar design choices.
- Recommends robustness checks using alternative complexity proxies (token count, tree depth) or matched grammars.
- Clarity is good overall, but needs clarification on program-equivalence threshold/procedure, computational budget/convergence diagnostics, and inclusion of key grammar rules in the main text.
- Originality: idea not wholly new but advances the field by using a general typed grammar, probabilistic optimization to match human distributions, and creative generation in a complex setting.
- Significance for AI: meaningful step toward agents that ask targeted, compositional questions transferable across domains.
- Significance for cognitive science: suggestive evidence that people balance informativeness and complexity, but not definitive given the dataset limitations.
- Strengths: clear probabilistic model, prediction and generation capabilities, informative ablations, and tractable engineering via importance sampling.
- Weaknesses: modest support for EIG-complexity trade-off, single-domain evaluation with domain-specific grammar, and potential post-hoc grammar fitting.
- Suggestions: report effect sizes/CIs, test alternative proposal distributions and explicit length/depth penalties, add a second domain or portability tests, and address dropped contexts/stochastic rule biases.
- Additional suggestion: evaluate or report mapping from programs to natural language (e.g., user study on fluency/answerability).
- Acceptance arguments: technically solid, reproducible, advances program-based question generation, and demonstrates creative, human-like outputs.
- Rejection arguments: limited cognitive evidence, single-domain handcrafted grammar, and some methodological choices that may confound conclusions.
- Overall: strong AI contribution with moderate cognitive evidence; recommend tempering behavioral claims and adding robustness analyses.
