

This paper proposes a cognitive model predicting when humans ask specific questions within belief-state contexts, defined by a log probability combining informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (question length), answer type, and relevance (connection to belief state vs. world facts). Applied to the Battleship domain—where players infer ship locations in a grid—the full model outperforms lesioned variants in predicting held-out human question data (log-likelihood). However, models excluding EIG correlate equally well with human judgments, suggesting EIG’s role is ambiguous.  

The work is technically sound and well-written, with clear reproducibility. Its primary contribution, however, lies more in AI than cognitive science. While the paper claims "our model predicts what questions people will ask" and demonstrates "how people balance informativeness and complexity," the evidence is weak. Log-likelihood improvements (with bootstrap confidence intervals indicating only 19% probability that EIG’s effect is non-zero) conflict with correlation results (which are noisy due to sparse human data). This inconsistency prevents strong conclusions about human cognitive mechanisms. The model may describe *what* questions are asked, but not *how* people balance factors like EIG and complexity in general.  

The paper overlooks key prior work. The core innovation—"modeling questions as programs executing on possible worlds"—directly parallels Hawkins (2015), who frames questions as "goals" provoking informative answers, and Roberts (1996), who formalizes the question-under-discussion (QUD). Hawkins also employs EIG and complexity trade-offs, though in a simpler domain. The current model extends this framework to Battleship, but the novelty is not sufficiently distinguished from established concepts.  

That said, the approach itself is compelling. A compositional question-generation model using EIG and complexity as selection criteria is a valuable framework for both cognitive and AI systems. The paper’s strongest contribution is demonstrating its feasibility in a non-trivial domain: generating meaningful, compositional questions for Battleship via this methodology. This suggests broader applicability for future work in dialog systems and cognitive modeling.  

In summary, the paper makes a modest but useful advance in applying question-generation models to complex domains. While the cognitive science claims are overstated due to ambiguous empirical support, the technical execution is robust, and the methodology aligns with emerging AI trends. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should explicitly contextualize their work against Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996), clarify the limited evidence for EIG’s cognitive role, and temper claims about human balancing mechanisms. The work is a solid step toward scalable question-generation systems but does not substantially advance our understanding of human cognition.
