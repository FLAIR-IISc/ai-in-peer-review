- Models human question-asking in a Battleship-like domain using a hand-crafted PCFG over lambda-calculus question representations.  
- Agents ask single-word-answer questions to maximize information about a partially observable grid of hidden colored ships.  
- Question quality is a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log-probability under the PCFG), and answer type (e.g., yes/no vs. color).  
- Human data from a prior study was hand-coded into the formalism and evaluated with leave-one-out cross-validation on lesioned model variants.  
- Results show all three factors significantly contribute to predicting human questions, with complexity being the strongest predictor.  
- Qualitative analysis demonstrates the model can generate novel, human-like questions.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, with thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluation.  
- Significant cognitive-science contribution: reconciles prior findings by showing simplicity preferences can outweigh pure informativeness in question-asking.  
- Core idea—integrating structured questions into active learning—has potential to inspire more human-like question-generation in agents.  
- Major limitation: manual mapping of human text to logical forms, with unclear prospects for automation and scaling.  
- Domain and model are small and computationally strained, raising concerns about feasibility in larger, real-world ML problems.  
- To broaden impact, authors should outline a roadmap or use case for applying the technique to domains of wider interest.  
- Minor suggestions: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods for formal significance tests, and connect future sequential-question work to QUD and formal pragmatics literature (refs provided).
