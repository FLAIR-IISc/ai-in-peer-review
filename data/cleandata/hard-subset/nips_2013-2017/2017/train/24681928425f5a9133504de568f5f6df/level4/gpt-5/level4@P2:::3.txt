This paper proposes a probabilistic model of human question generation that treats questions as executable programs over possible world states. A typed, compositional grammar (lambda calculus/LISP style) defines a large hypothesis space of question-programs. A log-linear model assigns probabilities to programs using features that capture informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (−log probability under the grammar, favoring shorter/ simpler programs), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and a relevance filter (ensuring the program references the game state). Parameters are learned with importance sampling and gradient ascent. The model is evaluated on a Battleship-style dataset of 605 natural-language questions collected across 18 ambiguous board contexts, predicting held-out human question distributions (log-likelihood) and generating novel questions not seen in training. Ablations indicate that removing complexity leads to a large performance drop; removing informativeness or type yields smaller but noticeable declines. Correlations between model energies and human frequencies average 0.64, while an information-agnostic variant sometimes achieves similar correlations. The system can synthesize creative, context-appropriate questions.

Relation to prior work. The paper positions itself between active learning and cognitive modeling. The idea of representing questions as goal-like procedures over worlds aligns with Question-Under-Discussion (QUD) semantics (Roberts, 1996) and with Hawkins et al. (2015), who optimize EIG and penalize complexity in a simpler domain. Program-based question representations also connect to semantic parsing/program induction (e.g., Wang et al., 2015; Johnson et al., 2017) and question generation in vision (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016), while differing by explicitly modeling goal-directed informativeness and compositional generation rather than imitation from large paired datasets. The paper’s main novelty is scaling a compositional, program-based generator to a richer domain and demonstrating that it can both predict and produce human-like questions.

Quality. Methodologically, the approach is sound and clearly specified. The grammar covers the dataset; the log-linear model and sampling-based training are appropriate; and the evaluation uses leave-one-context-out fits with ablations. The importance sampling and partition function approximations are acknowledged. Two caveats merit attention: (1) the informativeness effect is mixed—log-likelihood suggests a benefit with bootstrap uncertainty (∼19% of samples favor the information-agnostic model), and correlations show the information-agnostic variant can match the full model in some contexts; (2) the complexity feature depends on the PCFG’s rule probabilities, potentially entangling a cognitive preference for brevity with design choices in the grammar. Robustness checks using alternative complexity proxies (e.g., token count, tree depth) or matched grammars would strengthen the claims.

Clarity. The paper is well organized and readable. The formalism and training details are adequate for reproduction. A few clarifications would help: describe the threshold and procedure for deciding program equivalence via mutual information; report computational budget and convergence diagnostics; and summarize key grammar rules in the main text (not only in the supplement) to guide readers unfamiliar with the domain.

Originality. Treating question generation as program synthesis is not entirely new from a semantic perspective (QUD) or from cognitive modeling (Hawkins et al., 2015), but this work advances the idea by (i) using a general, typed grammar that composes rich predicates, (ii) optimizing a probabilistic model over that grammar to match human distributions, and (iii) demonstrating creative question generation in a substantially more complex setting. The integration and empirical validation are novel and valuable.

Significance. For AI, the paper is a meaningful step toward agents that ask targeted, compositional questions beyond label queries, with a flexible representation that could transfer across domains. For cognitive science, the evidence that people balance informativeness against complexity is suggestive but not definitive in this dataset; stronger tests would require tightly controlled manipulations, larger samples, or multiple domains. Still, the work offers a promising computational account of question productivity and constraints.

Strengths
- Clear, well-specified probabilistic model over a compositional question space.
- Demonstrates both prediction of human question distributions and generation of novel, context-sensitive questions.
- Ablations identify complexity as crucial; informativeness and type also contribute.
- Solid engineering: tractable inference via importance sampling; careful handling of answer types and relevance.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Cognitive claim about informativeness-complexity trade-off is not strongly supported: EIG’s added value is modest/uncertain. Suggest reporting effect sizes, CIs, and alternative proposal distributions; include an explicit length/depth penalty to test robustness against grammar-induced complexity.
- Generalization is limited to a single domain with a domain-specific grammar; adding a second domain or demonstrating portability of core operators would strengthen significance.
- The grammar was designed to cover the dataset, risking post hoc fit; preregistered constraints or out-of-domain tests would mitigate this.
- Two contexts and one stochastic rule were dropped for computational reasons; discuss potential biases and whether results replicate when reintroducing approximations for these cases.
- The mapping from programs to natural language is not addressed; even a small user study rating the fluency/answerability of generated questions would be informative.

Arguments for acceptance
- Technically solid, clearly written, and reproducible.
- Advances an underexplored but important direction: program-based, goal-directed question generation.
- Demonstrates creative, human-like questions and quantitative prediction in a nontrivial domain.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited evidence for the cognitive claim about balancing EIG and complexity.
- Single-domain evaluation with handcrafted grammar; unclear generality.
- Some methodological choices (complexity via PCFG probability, dropped contexts) may confound conclusions.

Overall, I see this as a strong AI contribution with moderate cognitive evidence. With tempered claims about human behavior and added robustness analyses, it would make a valuable addition to the program.
