- Novel modeling approach: treats human questions as formal programs executed on world states.
- Paper is well-written and evaluated both quantitatively and qualitatively.
- Unifies modern linguistic and cognitive theory, with potential to advance active learning and self-directed learning.
- Resolves prior puzzle that informativeness is a poor predictor by showing complexity is primary and question-space size matters.
- Model predicts the distribution of human questions in novel scenarios and generates novel, human-like questions absent from the dataset.
- Full model (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance) provides the best account of the data.
- Authors perform careful model comparisons across lesioned variants; a nested log-likelihood comparison could test significance.
- Computationally strained method with unclear scalability to larger or more complex domains.
- Evaluation limited to a small Battleship domain with few features, raising generalizability concerns.
- Approach depends on hand-coding question text into a grammar, which may not be feasible for larger datasets.
- Minor suggestion: explore sequential question-asking models from linguistics (e.g., Questions under Discussion).
- Strengths: novel approach, careful evaluation, and significant contribution to cognitive science.
- Weaknesses: scalability and reliance on hand-coded grammar.
- Arguments for acceptance: innovation, solid empirical support, and contribution to literature.
- Arguments against acceptance: practicality concerns about scaling and grammar engineering.
