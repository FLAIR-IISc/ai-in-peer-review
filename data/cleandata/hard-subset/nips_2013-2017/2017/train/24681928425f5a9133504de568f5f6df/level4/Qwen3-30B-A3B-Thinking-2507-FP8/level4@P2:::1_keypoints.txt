- Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs in a simple lambda-calculus/LISP-like language whose structure captures compositional question semantics.
- Introduces a probabilistic generative model predicting human question choices using four features: expected information gain (EIG), question complexity, answer type, and relevance to the game board.
- Evaluates the model on a dataset of 605 natural-language questions from a Battleship-style game where players query to discover hidden ship configurations.
- Connects to prior work in active learning, program synthesis, and compositional semantics, and to formal semantics and program induction for visual reasoning.
- Could have better engaged related work in visual question generation and goal-directed dialogue systems, which address question generation under different constraints.
- Strength: clear, principled formalization of questions as programs that captures compositional structure.
- Strength: probabilistic model effectively integrates informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance; ablation studies show each feature is necessary.
- Strength: model generates novel, human-like questions not seen in training, demonstrating creative potential.
- Weakness: domain is highly specific and constrained (Battleship), raising questions about broader applicability and scalability to open-ended or real-world settings.
- Weakness: limited evidence that this advances active learning beyond the specific game context or integrates with complex ML systems.
- Originality is limited: modeling questions as programs has precedent; novelty lies mainly in implementation and dataset application rather than a new theoretical advance.
- Paper is well-written, technically sound, with clear figures and adequate supplementary material for reproducibility.
- Limitation: domain-specific grammar would require significant re-engineering for other domains; evaluation confined to a single game context.
- Conclusion and recommendation: technically solid but incremental and narrow in scope, with limited significance for the broader NIPS community — arguments for rejection outweigh acceptance, though the paper has noteworthy strengths (formalization, feature integration, generative capability).
