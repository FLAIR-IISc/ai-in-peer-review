This paper presents a computational model of human question asking, framing the problem as one of program generation. The core idea is to represent questions as programs within a compositional grammar. A log-linear model is used to define a probability distribution over these programs, scoring potential questions based on a weighted combination of features, including their expected information gain (EIG), complexity (program length), and answer type. The model is evaluated on its ability to predict human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game, where players must ask questions to resolve ambiguity about hidden ship configurations. The main experimental result shows that the full model, which includes all features, better predicts held-out human data in terms of log-likelihood compared to ablated versions that lack key components like informativeness or complexity.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic framework is well-defined, and the application of program synthesis to this problem is thoughtfully executed. The experimental methodology, including the leave-one-out cross-validation and model lesion studies, is appropriate for assessing the contributions of different model components.

*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly articulate their motivation, the formal structure of the model, and the experimental results. The level of detail provided appears sufficient for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

*   **Originality:** While the application is novel, the paper could better contextualize its core theoretical ideas. The central premise of modeling questions as executable programs that partition a space of possible worlds is closely related to the notion of a question-under-discussion (QUD) in formal semantics (e.g., Roberts, 1996) and has been implemented in previous cognitive models. For instance, Hawkins et al. (2015) also proposed a model of question asking that balanced expected information gain against question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain. Acknowledging this lineage would help clarify the paper's specific contribution.

*   **Significance:** The work's primary contribution is as a compelling AI project that demonstrates how to generate sophisticated, compositional questions in a complex domain. This is a valuable advance over prior work in active learning and dialogue systems, which are often limited to simpler queries. However, its significance as a cognitive science paper is less certain. The authors claim to show "how people balance informativeness and complexity," but the supporting evidence is ambiguous. The log-likelihood improvement from including the EIG feature is noted to be statistically marginal (failing to hold in 19% of bootstrap samples), and the rank-correlation analysis shows the information-agnostic model performs comparably to the full model. Given the data sparsity and these mixed results, strong conclusions about the cognitive trade-offs in human question-asking seem premature.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel and powerful framework for question generation by framing it as program synthesis.
*   **Pro:** It successfully demonstrates the ability to generate creative, complex, and contextually appropriate questions in a non-trivial domain, advancing the state of the art in goal-directed question asking.
*   **Pro:** The work is technically solid and very clearly communicated.
*   **Con:** The claims regarding originality could be better situated with respect to prior work in both computational cognitive science and formal semantics.
*   **Con:** The cognitive science claims about how humans balance competing factors are not robustly supported by the quantitative results, which are mixed and somewhat inconclusive.
