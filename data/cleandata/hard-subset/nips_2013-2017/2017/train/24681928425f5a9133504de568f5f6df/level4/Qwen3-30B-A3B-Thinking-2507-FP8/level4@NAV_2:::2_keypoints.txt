- Paper models human questions as lambda-calculus programs that execute on world states to yield single-word answers.  
- Uses a probabilistic generative framework with features for informativeness (EIG), complexity (negative log PCFG probability), answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship-style game; model predicts human question frequencies (Spearman ρ = 0.64) and generates novel human-like questions.  
- Ablation studies show all features matter, with complexity being especially critical for matching human behavior.  
- Provides the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking, formalizing the tradeoff between informativeness and complexity.  
- Strong clarity and technical quality: precise definitions, clear model specification, and thorough empirical validation.  
- Original contribution by combining compositional linguistics, probabilistic programming, and cognitive theory.  
- Advances cognitive science and fits NIPS emphasis on foundational contributions and significance.  
- Narrow domain (Battleship) limits generalizability to broader NIPS audience.  
- Grammar and features are highly domain-specific, making scaling to natural language or more complex domains unclear.  
- Computational burden (e.g., importance sampling over ~150,000 questions) may hinder practical deployment.  
- Paper does not sufficiently articulate scenarios where the method yields novel insights beyond this domain.  
- Recommendation: demonstrate extension to more general tasks or clarify domain-agnostic components (logical connectives, set operations).
