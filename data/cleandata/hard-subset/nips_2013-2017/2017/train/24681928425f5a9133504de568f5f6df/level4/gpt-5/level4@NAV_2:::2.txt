The paper investigates human question generation with single-word answers drawn from a small set of types. The authors hand-specify a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) for a Battleship-like domain in which colored ships are partially hidden on a grid and the agent may ask one question to reduce uncertainty about the underlying configuration. Questions are represented as lambda-calculus programs, and a prior over questions is induced by the PCFG. Question utility is modeled via a log-linear energy combining (i) informativeness measured as expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian hypothesis-updating model, (ii) complexity operationalized as the negative log-probability of the program under the uniform PCFG (favoring concise programs), and (iii) answer-type preferences (e.g., Boolean, number, color, location). Human questions from a prior study are mapped into this formal language. Using leave-one-context-out cross-validation, the authors compare the full model to ablations that remove informativeness, complexity, or type features, finding that all contribute (with complexity being especially critical). They also showcase sampled questions that were not present in the training data.

Question-asking is central to linguistics and hypothesis testing. This work offers a novel synthesis of contemporary linguistic and cognitive perspectives to explain how people formulate questions, and it suggests avenues for active learning systems that can select richer queries than simple labels. The presentation is exceptionally clear and well-structured, and the empirical evaluation includes both quantitative model fit and qualitative inspection of generated questions.

From a computational cognitive science standpoint, the paper advances our understanding of self-directed learning. Prior work reported that informativeness alone was a weak predictor of human question choice; this study clarifies that complexity constraints are primary (and that the size of the candidate question space may modulate informativeness effects). As such, the contribution to cognitive science is substantive and could, in itself, merit acceptance at NIPS.

That said, there are significant limitations that may curb appeal to a broader NIPS audience. While mapping natural language questions to executable semantic programs could in principle be automated, the pathway to scaling this approach to domains of wider machine learning interest remains unclear. The Battleship setting is relatively constrained with few latent factors, yet the inference is already computationally intensive. The conceptual thrust—active learning with richer queries—is compelling, but the authors may strengthen the case by articulating a concrete, larger-scale application where their framework is practical and yields insights beyond existing approaches.

Minor comments:
1) You could treat the ablations as nested models and use the held-out log-likelihoods to conduct formal tests of the significance of each feature class’s contribution.
2) For sequential and discourse-sensitive question-asking, there is relevant work in formal semantics and pragmatics (e.g., Questions Under Discussion). Two pointers:
- Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459–527.
- Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of SemDial (DialDam), 17, 131–139.
