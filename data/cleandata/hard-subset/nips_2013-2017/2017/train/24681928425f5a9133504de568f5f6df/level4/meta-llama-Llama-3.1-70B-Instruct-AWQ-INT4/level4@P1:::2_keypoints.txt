- Paper proposes a novel probabilistic grammar-based model of human question asking in a "battleship" domain, treating questions as lambda-calculus programs.  
- Authors define a PCFG prior over questions and a "battleship" task where questions have single-word K-ary answers about a hidden grid.  
- Question goodness is modeled as a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log PCFG probability), and answer type.  
- The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and reproducible for expert readers.  
- Human data were reused from prior work and hand-coded into the grammar for evaluation.  
- Model evaluated both quantitatively and qualitatively, including lesioned variants via leave-one-out cross-validation.  
- All model factors contributed non-negligibly to prediction, with complexity being the most important factor.  
- The work explains why informativeness alone was a poor predictor in previous studies—complexity dominates and question space size matters.  
- Contribution is significant to computational cognitive science and advances understanding of self-directed learning and active question asking.  
- Originality lies in generating questions as formal programs/partitions over worlds and producing questions from scratch.  
- Major limitation: scalability and generalization are unclear—battleship is small and the approach is computationally strained.  
- Authors should provide scenarios where the method is applicable to broader NIPS-relevant domains and demonstrate scalability.  
- Minor suggestions: perform nested model comparisons for statistical tests and consider sequential/question-under-discussion models from linguistics in future work.  
- Acceptance pros: novel approach, advances self-directed learning, improves on prior work, and has potential impact on active learning research.  
- Acceptance cons: limited scalability, computational strain in current domain, and unclear broader applicability.
