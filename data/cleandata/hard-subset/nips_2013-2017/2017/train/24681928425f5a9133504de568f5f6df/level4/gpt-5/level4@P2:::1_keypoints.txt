- Summary: Models human question-asking as generation of executable programs in a typed LISP-like language, selecting questions with a log-linear model over features including EIG, complexity, answer-type indicators, and relevance.
- Summary: Trained on a Battleship-style task (605 questions across 18 ambiguous boards) using importance sampling; full model correlates with human frequencies (avg ρ=0.64) and can generate novel questions.
- Relation to prior work: Connects to active learning, formal semantics of questions, program-induction, and question generation, emphasizing compositional, goal-directed question generation over template or neural VQG approaches.
- Strength: Clear computational framework combining compositional programs, executable semantics, and an explicit EIG utility with a cognitive complexity penalty.
- Strength: Empirically shows informativeness alone is insufficient and that complexity strongly influences human question choices.
- Strength: Generates novel, human-like questions via a grammar rather than memorized templates, demonstrating productivity.
- Weakness: Heavy domain engineering (bespoke grammar, hand-specified types, small noise-free environment) limits obvious transfer to richer domains.
- Weakness: Limited evaluation scale and scope (605 questions, dropped contexts/operators for tractability), potentially biasing results.
- Weakness: Narrow baselines—only ablations of the model; missing comparisons to direct EIG optimization, RL policies, or neural program generators.
- Weakness: Indirect link to natural language—no parser/generator or end-to-end NL evaluation of sampled questions.
- Weakness: Reliance on approximate likelihoods and rank correlations without human judgments of sampled questions’ usefulness or naturalness.
- Quality: Technical components are sound for the setting, but importance-sampling choices and dropped elements require diagnostics (variance, ESS, seed stability) to increase confidence.
- Clarity: Well written and organized; would benefit from pseudocode for training/inference, hypothesis-space size details, and a compact grammar summary in the main text.
- Originality: Thoughtful integration of known ideas (LOT, EIG, program semantics) with novelty in framing rather than new algorithms.
- Significance: Offers cognitive insight on informativeness–complexity tradeoffs but limited ML impact due to narrow domain, lack of NL interface, and modest scale.
- Suggestions: Demonstrate transfer to another domain, add stronger baselines (length-regularized EIG, RL over grammar, neural generators), and include human evaluations of sampled questions.
- Suggestions: Provide sensitivity analyses for the importance sampler and relevance filter, and explore coupling with a semantic parser for NL mapping.
- Pros for acceptance: Principled executable semantics and compositionality, empirical evidence for complexity’s role, and ability to generate novel context-appropriate questions.
- Cons for acceptance: Heavy domain engineering, limited generalization/scale, missing stronger baselines, and no end-to-end natural language evaluation.
- Overall recommendation: Borderline reject for NIPS—interesting cognitive modeling and formulation but limited scope and ML novelty; needs broader domains, stronger baselines, and NL integration to be competitive.
