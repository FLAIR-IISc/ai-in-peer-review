The paper presents a cognitive model designed to predict the types of questions asked in specific contexts, referred to as belief states. This model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of several key factors, including informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (roughly, question length), answer type, and relevance (whether the question pertains to the belief state or world-independent logical facts). The authors apply this model to the Battleship domain, where the task is to locate ships (clusters of grid elements) within a grid. The full model, incorporating all of these features, outperforms lesioned versions with fewer features in predicting held-out human data in terms of log-likelihood. However, when considering correlation with human judgments, the model that neglects expected information gain (EIG) performs similarly to the full model.

From a technical standpoint, the paper appears to be sound. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the underlying concepts and provide a thorough explanation of their model.

While the paper is well-written and clear, its contribution to cognitive science is somewhat limited. The authors claim that their model predicts human question-asking behavior and provides insight into how people balance informativeness and complexity. However, the results are not entirely convincing in this regard. The Battleship domain appears to be a relatively simple task, and the influence of EIG on human question-asking behavior is unclear. The log-likelihood results suggest a possible effect, but with a 19% chance of being incorrect according to bootstrap estimates. In contrast, the correlation results, which are also noisy due to sparse human data, indicate little to no effect of EIG. These findings do not provide a clear understanding of how people balance these factors in general.

One notable omission in the paper is a discussion of prior work in this area. The authors' approach to modeling questions as programs that output answers when executed on the state of a possible world is not entirely novel. This concept is similar to the notion of a "goal" used in Hawkins (2015) and is based on the idea of a question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts (1996). The questioner model by Hawkins also incorporates expected information gain and trades it off with question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain.

Despite these limitations, the model presented in this paper has potential as a component for cognitive and AI models. The use of a compositional prior over questions and the incorporation of EIG and other factors make sense in this context. The main contribution of this paper is demonstrating the feasibility of generating interesting, compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this approach.
