This paper proposes a cognitive model to predict which questions humans ask in a given belief state. The model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of four features: informativeness (expected information gain or EIG), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance (distinguishing between questions about the belief state and world-independent logical facts). This framework is evaluated in the Battleship game domain, where the goal is to locate hidden ships on a grid. The authors demonstrate that their full model outperforms lesioned versions (models with fewer features) in predicting held-out human data, as measured by log-likelihood. However, the results regarding the contribution of informativeness are mixed: a model without EIG performed nearly as well as the full model when evaluated by correlation with human judgments.

The paper is generally well-written, clear, and technically sound. The methodology is presented with sufficient detail that the results should be reproducible. The primary contribution of this work is demonstrating that a compositional model of question generation, which balances factors like EIG and complexity, can be successfully applied to a non-trivial domain. This type of model, which employs a compositional prior over questions, is a promising approach for both AI and cognitive modeling, and this paper serves as a valuable proof of concept. I expect this approach to see wider use in the future.

While the paper succeeds as an AI engineering project, its conclusions as a cognitive science contribution are not fully supported by the results. The authors claim that "from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity." However, the evidence for the role of informativeness is ambiguous in the Battleship domain. According to the log-likelihood metric, the full model provides a better fit, but the authors' own bootstrap analysis suggests a 19% probability that this improvement over a model without EIG is due to chance. Furthermore, when measured by correlation with human judgments, the model without EIG performs comparably to the full model. Given the noise in the human data and these mixed results, it is difficult to draw strong conclusions about the general cognitive principles of how humans balance these specific factors.

Additionally, the paper could be strengthened by situating its contributions more thoroughly within existing literature. The core idea of modeling "questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer" is conceptually similar to the notion of a "goal" in Hawkins et al. (2015, "Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.") and builds on the theory of Questions-Under-Discussion (Roberts, 1996). The questioner model by Hawkins et al. also presents a trade-off between expected information gain and question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain. Acknowledging and differentiating from this prior work would clarify the novel contributions of the current paper.
