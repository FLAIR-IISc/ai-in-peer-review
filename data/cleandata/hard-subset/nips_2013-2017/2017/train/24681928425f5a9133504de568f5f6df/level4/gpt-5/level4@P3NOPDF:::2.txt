Summary
This paper studies human question-asking in a “battleship” domain with partially observable colored ships on a grid. The authors specify a PCFG as a prior over questions, represent questions as lambda-calculus expressions, and score each question using a linear combination of expected information gain (EIG), complexity (length or negative log-probability under the PCFG), and answer type (e.g., yes/no vs. color). Human data from a prior study are mapped into the grammar. Through leave-one-out cross-validation with lesioned models, the authors show that all components contribute to model fit, with complexity emerging as the strongest predictor. They also present generated questions illustrating qualitative behavior.

Strengths
- Timely and interesting problem: question-asking as a unifying topic across linguistics, cognition, and active learning.
- Clear modeling framework linking a generative prior over questions to decision-theoretic informativeness and pragmatic answer types.
- Transparent, well-written exposition; the paper is easy to follow.
- Careful evaluation: both quantitative (lesion studies, cross-validation) and qualitative (novel generated questions).
- Cognitive science contribution: helps reconcile prior findings that informativeness alone is a weak predictor of human question-asking by showing complexity exerts a larger influence and that the size/composition of the hypothesis space matters.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scalability and generality: The approach relies on a hand-engineered PCFG and hand-coding of human questions into the grammar. It is unclear how this pipeline scales to richer domains, broader vocabularies, or more naturalistic question forms. The current battleship domain is small, yet search and evaluation are already computationally demanding.
- External relevance: Without a clear path to domains of interest to the broader NeurIPS community (e.g., interactive ML, robotics, data labeling, scientific discovery), the impact may remain primarily within cognitive science and formal semantics.
- Modeling choices: While the ablations indicate all components matter, the dominance of complexity raises questions about whether informativeness is underweighted due to the grammar, search, or the range of available questions. More analysis could clarify whether this is a property of human behavior or of the modeling assumptions.
- Data handling: The reliance on previously collected data and hand mapping into the grammar may introduce biases or ceiling effects; it would help to know how robust results are to alternative codings or modest changes in the grammar.

Significance
From a computational cognitive science standpoint, the paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding self-directed learning and question generation, offering a principled account of why EIG alone underpredicts human behavior. This theoretical advance could be sufficient for acceptance. For broader ML impact, the paper would benefit from a more convincing narrative about how the approach can be adapted or automated in larger-scale, real-world settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify complexity: Precisely define the complexity term(s) used in the final model (length vs. PCFG surprisal). If both are tried, report their relative contributions, correlations, and whether one subsumes the other.
- Statistical tests: In addition to cross-validated predictive performance, consider nested model comparisons on held-out log-likelihoods to test whether each component (informativeness, complexity, answer type) significantly improves fit. This would provide a more formal assessment of the ablation results.
- Ablation diagnostics: Report how the weight on informativeness changes with grammar size or search budget. If expanding the grammar increases the utility of EIG, it would support the claim that prior negative findings reflect limited question spaces rather than human insensitivity to information.
- Computational profile: Provide details on search/inference complexity, pruning strategies, and runtimes as the grammar grows. A discussion of approximate search (e.g., beam search, Monte Carlo) and its effect on model predictions would help address scalability concerns.
- Automation path: Sketch a concrete route to reduce hand-engineering—for example, using learned or semi-automatically induced grammars, semantic parsers to map natural language to logical forms, or neural priors over questions constrained by compositional structure. Even a small proof-of-concept in a richer domain would broaden relevance.
- Broader application: Present a specific scenario (e.g., interactive data collection, scientific hypothesis generation, robotic exploration, or tutoring systems) where the model’s ability to ask structured, compositional questions offers a clear advantage over standard active learning. A targeted case study would make the impact more compelling for the NeurIPS audience.
- Error analysis: Include qualitative analysis of where the model diverges from human choices (e.g., when complexity overwhelms informativeness or when answer type dominates), to disentangle model misspecification from genuine human preferences.

Minor comments
1) You can conduct nested model comparisons using held-out log-likelihoods from the lesioned models to test statistical significance of each component’s contribution.
2) For sequential question-asking and discourse dynamics, the formal semantics/pragmatics literature on Questions Under Discussion may offer useful extensions:
- Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459–527.
- Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131–139.

Overall assessment
A clear and well-executed paper that advances the cognitive modeling of question-asking and connects linguistic structure with decision-theoretic utility. While its current scope and hand-engineering limit immediate scalability, the core ideas are promising. Strengthening the path to automation and demonstrating applicability beyond the toy domain would substantially increase its appeal to the broader NeurIPS community.
