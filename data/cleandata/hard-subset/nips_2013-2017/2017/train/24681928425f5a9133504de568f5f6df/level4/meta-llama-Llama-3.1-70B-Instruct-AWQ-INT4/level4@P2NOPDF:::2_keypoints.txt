- Paper models human question-asking in a "battleship" grid using a hand-built PCFG prior over lambda-calculus question representations.  
- Task: pick a single-word question to maximize information gain about the hidden board.  
- Question goodness is a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or PCFG-based negative log-probability), and answer type (e.g., boolean or color).  
- Leave-one-out cross-validation on lesioned models shows all three factors significantly contribute, with complexity the most important.  
- The model generates novel questions and provides a unifying perspective linking linguistic and cognitive theory.  
- Paper is well-written, evaluated quantitatively and qualitatively, and advances understanding of why informativeness alone poorly predicts human question-asking.  
- Limitations: scalability and extension to more complex domains are unclear—battleship is small and the method is already computationally strained.  
- To broaden appeal, authors should present clear application scenarios or novel insights and consider extensions to larger domains or integration with other linguistic models.  
- Minor suggestion: perform nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods of lesioned models for statistical tests.  
- Minor suggestion: consider sequential question-asking literature (Questions under Discussion), e.g., Ginzburg 1995 and Rojas-Esponda 2013.
