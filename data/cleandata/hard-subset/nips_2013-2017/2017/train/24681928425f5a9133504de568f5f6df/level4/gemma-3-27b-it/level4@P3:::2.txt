This paper presents an innovative approach to modeling human question asking, framing questions as formal programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The authors tackle a challenging problem – moving beyond simple label requests in active learning towards richer, more human-like inquiries – within the context of a “Battleship” game where agents must infer hidden colored shapes on a grid. They define a probabilistic model, grounded in a custom-designed probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG), to generate and evaluate questions based on informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (grammar-based probability), and answer type. The model is evaluated by predicting human question choices and generating novel questions, demonstrating a compelling ability to capture key aspects of human questioning behavior.

This work builds on a rich history of research in active learning (Settles, 2009) and represents a significant step toward more sophisticated active learning agents. While traditional active learning focuses on simple queries, this paper aligns with the goal of developing agents capable of asking questions that directly target critical information gaps, echoing insights from cognitive science regarding human learning (Chouinard, 1973). The authors' framing of questions as programs is a novel and insightful approach, connecting to formal linguistics (Fodor, 1987; Jacobson, 1995) and the broader “language of thought” hypothesis. The clarity of the presentation and the combined quantitative and qualitative evaluation further strengthen the paper. From a computational cognitive science perspective, this work provides valuable insights into the interplay between informativeness and complexity in human question asking, extending previous findings that suggested informativeness alone was insufficient (Gureckis & Markant, 2017).

The strengths of this paper lie in its original framing of the problem, the technical soundness of the proposed model, its clear and compelling evaluation, and its contributions to understanding the cognitive mechanisms underlying question asking. The authors successfully demonstrate that a model incorporating complexity considerations can better predict human question behavior. The generated novel questions are also genuinely interesting and illustrative of the model's capabilities.

However, the paper’s applicability to broader NIPS audiences remains a concern. The current implementation is tied to a specific, relatively small domain (Battleship). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the computational strain involved in scaling this approach to more complex environments isn’t fully addressed. A key question for the broader community is whether the core principles of this approach – program-based question generation and the weighting of informativeness and complexity – can be effectively transferred to more general problem settings.  The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar, although well-motivated, is another potential bottleneck for broader applicability. Demonstrating the feasibility of automating grammar construction or adapting to new domains with minimal manual effort would significantly increase the impact of this work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The framing of questions as programs is a genuinely innovative approach.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The model is well-defined, mathematically grounded, and evaluated rigorously.
*   **Cognitive Insight:**  The results provide valuable insights into the cognitive factors influencing human question asking, resolving previous inconsistencies in the literature.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Domain Specificity:** The current implementation is limited to the Battleship domain and scalability is a concern.
*   **Manual Grammar Construction:** The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar hinders broader applicability.
*   **Limited Generalizability:** It’s unclear how easily the approach could be applied to domains of greater interest to the wider NIPS community.



**Minor comments:**

1.  Consider performing nested model comparison with likelihood ratio tests to assess the statistical significance of the differences between the lesioned models.
2.  The authors might benefit from acknowledging relevant work in formal semantics and pragmatics related to question-answering and the "Questions under Discussion" literature (Ginzburg, 1995; Rojas-Esponda, 2013) for a more complete contextualization of their work.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the fields of active learning and computational cognitive science. While the domain specificity and reliance on a hand-crafted grammar raise some concerns, the originality of the approach, the technical quality of the work, and the cognitive insights it provides justify consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors briefly addressing the scalability concerns and outlining a potential path towards generalization to broader application areas.
