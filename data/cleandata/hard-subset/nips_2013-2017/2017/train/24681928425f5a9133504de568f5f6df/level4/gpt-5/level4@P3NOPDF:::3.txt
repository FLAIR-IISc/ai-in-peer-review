Summary
The paper proposes a cognitive model that predicts which questions people ask in a given belief state. The unnormalized log probability of a question is a weighted sum of expected information gain (EIG), complexity (roughly question length), answer type, and a “relevance” term distinguishing queries tied to the current belief state from world-independent logical facts. The model is instantiated in a Battleship domain (locating ships on a grid), and evaluated on held-out human data. The full model outperforms lesioned variants in predictive log-likelihood, although a version without EIG performs comparably to the full model in correlation with human judgments.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and a coherent, compositional prior over questions as executable programs.
- Technically sound modeling and evaluation; the paper is well written and, in principle, reproducible.
- Demonstrates that nontrivial, compositional questions can be generated and scored in a realistic domain.
- Ablation analysis indicates that multiple features contribute to predictive performance in log-likelihood.

Concerns and limitations
- Cognitive claims outpace the evidence. The paper asserts that the model reveals how people balance informativeness and complexity. However, the results are mixed: (a) log-likelihood favors the full model over lesioned variants, but the bootstrap indicates a non-negligible chance (~19%) that this advantage is spurious; (b) rank correlation with human judgments is roughly unchanged when EIG is removed. Given the sparsity of human data and noise in both metrics, it is difficult to draw strong conclusions about how people trade off EIG and complexity in general.
- Inconsistency across evaluation metrics is not fully analyzed. Why does EIG help in likelihood but not in correlation? Is this due to calibration vs. ranking, dataset sparsity, or collinearity with other features?
- Some features are under-specified. The “relevance” feature (belief-state vs. world-independent) needs a clearer operational definition and examples. The “answer type” feature could be correlated with complexity; it would help to unpack their distinct contributions.
- Potential overfitting and identifiability. With several partially collinear features, fitted weights may be unstable. It is unclear how regularization was chosen and whether confidence intervals over weights were computed.
- Limited generality. All results come from a single task/domain. It is unclear whether the learned trade-offs or even the feature set would transfer to other question-asking contexts.

Missing related work
The paper’s “questions as programs/goals” framing closely parallels prior work:
- Hawkins (2015), “Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers,” models a questioner with an EIG-complexity trade-off (in a simpler domain).
- The approach builds on the Question Under Discussion (QUD) framework (Roberts, 1996).
Related traditions worth acknowledging include: optimal experimental design and active learning as models of inquiry; and pragmatic/question-asking work in cognitive science and linguistics that formalizes informativeness and complexity trade-offs. Positioning the present model relative to these literatures would clarify what is novel (e.g., scaling to a compositional program space in Battleship; adding the “relevance” feature; the particular inference machinery).

Suggestions for improvement
- Calibrate cognitive claims. Frame the results as demonstrating that a compositional question prior plus feature-based utility can predict human questions in Battleship, while evidence for an EIG-complexity trade-off in human behavior remains tentative. Highlight where the model provides clear gains and where the evidence is equivocal.
- Analyze where EIG helps. Provide qualitative and quantitative error analyses: which question types or belief states show the largest improvements with EIG? Are gains localized to highly uncertain states or to questions that disambiguate specific ship configurations?
- Report uncertainty and identifiability. Provide standard errors/credible intervals for feature weights and model comparisons (e.g., bootstrap CIs for log-likelihood differences, Bayes factors or WAIC/LOO-CV). Discuss collinearity among EIG, complexity, and answer type. Show sensitivity to regularization strength and to alternative complexity measures (e.g., program depth vs. surface length).
- Clarify and justify “relevance.” Define how relevance is computed relative to belief states, illustrate with examples, and quantify its independent contribution (e.g., via nested models, partial dependence plots).
- Reconcile metrics. Report both calibration-oriented metrics (log-likelihood) and ranking metrics (Spearman/Kendall), with confidence intervals. Consider top-k accuracy and pairwise choice prediction. Analyze why EIG affects likelihood but not rank—e.g., does EIG mostly sharpen probabilities among already top-ranked questions?
- Strengthen baselines and generalization tests. Compare against:
  - Non-compositional or template-based baselines that capture length and answer type.
  - Heuristic active-learning baselines (uncertainty sampling, information density).
  - Participant- and board-level cross-validation to test generalization across people and grids.
- Expand related work. Explicitly connect to Hawkins (2015) and QUD (Roberts, 1996), and situate the approach within optimal experimental design and active learning literatures. Clarify the novel contributions relative to these.
- Reproducibility. Include code/data, full grammar/prior over question programs, hyperparameters, and details of inference (e.g., search limits, stochasticity, pruning). Discuss computational cost and scalability.

Overall assessment
As an AI/ML contribution, the paper is solid: it shows that a compositional, program-like representation of questions combined with feature-based scoring can generate and predict human-like questions in a nontrivial domain. As a cognitive science contribution, the evidence for specific psychological trade-offs—particularly the role of EIG versus complexity—is suggestive but not conclusive given the mixed metrics and statistical uncertainty. With a more careful positioning of claims, expanded related work, clearer feature definitions, stronger uncertainty quantification, and targeted analyses that diagnose when and how EIG matters, this would be a compelling paper. I view the primary contribution as demonstrating that one can generate interesting, compositional questions at scale in Battleship using this framework, laying groundwork for broader applications and deeper cognitive tests.
