The paper introduces a cognitive model designed to predict question selection based on contextual belief states. The model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of informativeness (measured by expected information gain, or EIG), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance – whether the question pertains to the current belief state or world-independent facts. Applied to the Battleship domain, where the goal is to localize hidden ships within a grid, the complete model incorporating all features outperformed ablated versions with fewer features in terms of log-likelihood on held-out human data. However, when assessed via correlation with human judgments, the model excluding EIG performed comparably to the full model.

The paper appears technically sound. While successful as an AI endeavor, its contribution to cognitive science is less clear. The authors claim the model "predicts what question people will ask" and demonstrates “how people balance informativeness and complexity.” However, the results offer limited insight into *how* people achieve this balance. In the Battleship domain, the data suggest people are either marginally influenced by EIG (a finding with a 19% chance of being spurious, according to bootstrap estimates) or are unaffected by it (as indicated by noisy correlation results stemming from sparse human data). These findings do not strongly support generalized conclusions about the human balancing of informativeness and complexity. 

The paper is well-written, clear, and the results appear reproducible. 

A notable omission is related work. The paper presents “modeling questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer” as an innovation, yet this aligns with the concept of a “goal” as defined by Hawkins (2015, “Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.”) and builds upon the notion of a question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts (1996, “Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics.”). Hawkins’ questioner model also leverages EIG and balances it with question complexity, though within a simpler domain.

I believe this class of model – incorporating a compositional prior over questions and selecting based on EIG and other factors – is promising for both cognitive and AI research and will likely see increased use. I view the paper's primary contribution as demonstrating the feasibility of generating meaningful compositional questions within a non-trivial domain using this approach.
