- Presents a computational model of human question asking as program generation using a compositional grammar.  
- Uses a log-linear model scoring programs by features including expected information gain (EIG), complexity (program length), and answer type.  
- Evaluated in a "Battleship"-like game; full model predicts held-out human questions better than ablated versions.  
- Technically sound probabilistic framework and well-executed program synthesis application.  
- Experimental methodology is appropriate (leave-one-out cross-validation, lesion studies).  
- Manuscript is exceptionally well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproduction.  
- Novel application to generate compositional questions in a complex domain, advancing AI question generation.  
- Needs better contextualization with prior work (e.g., QUD literature, Roberts 1996; Hawkins et al. 2015).  
- Cognitive claims about how people balance informativeness and complexity are weakly supported by mixed quantitative results.  
- Quantitative issues: EIG improvement is marginal (fails in 19% of bootstrap samples) and rank-correlation shows info-agnostic model performs comparably.  
- Pros: novel framework, generates creative/contextual questions, technically solid and clear.  
- Cons: insufficient literature situating and inconclusive support for cognitive-science claims.
