The authors investigate human question-asking behavior in a K-ary answer setting by manually defining a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) for a "battleship" domain. This domain features a partially observable grid containing hidden colored shapes, where the agent's task is to pose a single-word answer question that maximizes information gain about the board's state. The PCFG serves as a prior over questions, which are formalized as lambda calculus statements. The quality of a question is evaluated based on a linear function of its informativeness, complexity (measured by length or negative log probability under the PCFG with uniform rewrite rules), and "answer type" (e.g., true/false or color).

The authors leverage human data from a previously published study, which was manually encoded into their grammar. Through leave-one-out cross-validation, they compare lesioned versions of the model, finding that all factors contribute substantially to its performance (with complexity being the most influential). Furthermore, the authors present a set of novel questions.

The paper offers a unique perspective on question-asking, bridging modern linguistic and cognitive theories to investigate human inquiry. This innovative approach has the potential to inspire new research in active learning, where models can pose more sophisticated questions. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented, with a thorough evaluation of the model from both quantitative and qualitative standpoints.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this study advances the field of self-directed learning. Previous research found that question informativeness was a poor predictor of human question-asking behavior. This paper resolves part of this discrepancy, suggesting that complexity and the size of possible questions available to the model may be contributing factors. As such, the paper contributes significantly to the cognitive science literature, potentially justifying its publication in NIPS.

However, the approach has notable limitations that may restrict its appeal to the broader NIPS audience. Although it may be possible to automate the conversion of human questions to semantic logic statements, it is unclear how the method could be scaled or extended to more complex domains. The battleship domain is relatively small, with limited features, and the current method is already computationally intensive.

Minor comments:

1. A nested model comparison using log-likelihoods of the lesioned models may provide statistical significance tests, offering additional insights.
2. For sequential question-asking, the linguistics literature on formal semantics and pragmatics, particularly the Questions under Discussion framework, may provide valuable insights and alternative models. References to this literature include Ginzburg (1995) and Rojas-Esponda (2013), which may serve as useful starting points for future research.
