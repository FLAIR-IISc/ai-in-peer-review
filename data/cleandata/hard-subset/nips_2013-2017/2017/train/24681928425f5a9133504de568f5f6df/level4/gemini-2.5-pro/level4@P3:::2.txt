### Summary

This paper introduces a novel computational model of human question-asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The authors propose that meaningful questions can be represented as programs in a compositional language (based on lambda calculus) that, when executed on a world state, return an answer. The model defines a probability distribution over this space of programs using a log-linear model, favoring questions that are both informative and structurally simple.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game, where players must ask questions to resolve ambiguity about hidden objects on a grid. The core of their model is a hand-designed Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG) that defines the space of possible questions. The probability of a given question is a weighted function of several features: its informativeness (Expected Information Gain), its complexity (negative log-probability under the PCFG), and its answer type (e.g., Boolean, number, color).

Using leave-one-out cross-validation, the authors demonstrate that their full model provides a good quantitative fit to the distribution of human questions. Lesion studies reveal that while informativeness and answer type are important, question complexity is the most critical factor in predicting human behavior. Qualitatively, the model is shown to be generative, capable of producing novel and contextually relevant questions that were not observed in the human data.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Novel Framework:** The paper's primary strength is its innovative conceptualization of question-asking as program synthesis. This approach elegantly unifies ideas from active learning, cognitive science (compositionality and the "language of thought"), and formal linguistics. It represents a significant step beyond typical active learning paradigms that are often restricted to simple label queries, opening a path toward modeling more sophisticated, human-like information-seeking behaviors.

2.  **Technical Quality and Thorough Evaluation:** The proposed model is technically sound and well-realized. The evaluation is commendable for its depth, combining both quantitative prediction of human data with qualitative generation of novel questions. The lesion analysis is particularly effective, providing clear evidence for the contribution of each component of the model (informativeness, complexity, and answer type) and rigorously supporting the paper's central claims.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and organized. The authors do an excellent job of explaining a complex model and its theoretical underpinnings, making the work accessible and its contributions easy to appreciate.

4.  **Significance for Cognitive Science:** This work makes a significant contribution to the computational cognitive science of learning and reasoning. It provides a compelling explanation for a known puzzle in the literature: why Expected Information Gain (EIG) alone is often a poor predictor of human information-gathering strategies. By demonstrating the crucial role of complexity as a constraint on EIG, the paper offers a more nuanced and accurate model of human self-directed learning.

### Weaknesses and Opportunities for Improvement

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its potential for broader impact and scalability beyond the well-defined but constrained domain presented. While the framework is conceptually powerful, its practical application in a wider range of machine learning problems faces several challenges that limit its immediate significance to the broader NIPS community.

1.  **Scalability and Generalizability:** The model relies on a hand-crafted grammar tailored specifically to the "Battleship" domain. It is unclear how this approach would generalize to new, more complex domains without significant and expert-driven knowledge engineering for each new task. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of a potential roadmap for reducing this manual effort, perhaps through grammar induction or by leveraging more domain-general semantic primitives.

2.  **Computational Tractability:** As the authors note, the method is already computationally strained in this relatively small domain, requiring them to exclude certain contexts and approximate the intractable normalization constant. This raises serious concerns about whether the approach can scale to problems with larger state spaces or more expressive grammars, which are common in real-world applications of interest to the machine learning community.

3.  **Grounding in Natural Language:** The model operates on semantic program representations, and the human data was manually translated into this form. Bridging the gap from natural language text to these formal representations remains a major open problem. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the lack of an automated path from text to program limits the model's applicability as a system that could interact with humans or process natural language data directly.

The paper would be more compelling for a general NIPS audience if the authors could provide a clearer vision for how these challenges might be overcome, or if they could identify a class of real-world problems where this structured, program-based approach offers a distinct advantage over existing end-to-end neural methods, despite the engineering overhead.

### Minor Comments

1.  For the lesion studies, it might be informative to perform a nested model comparison using the reported log-likelihoods. This would allow for formal statistical tests of significance between the full model and its lesioned variants, strengthening the claim that each component provides a non-negligible contribution.

2.  For future work, particularly on sequential question-asking, the authors may find the "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) literature from formal semantics and pragmatics to be highly relevant. This framework provides a rich perspective on how dialogue context constrains the relevance and form of questions. The following are good entry points to that literature:
    *   Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy, 18*(5), 459-527.
    *   Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17*, 131-139.
