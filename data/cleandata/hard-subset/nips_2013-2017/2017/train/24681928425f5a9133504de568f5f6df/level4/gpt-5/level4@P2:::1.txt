Summary
The paper models human question-asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a small, typed, LISP-like language (lambda calculus with sets, mapping, logical and arithmetic operators). Executing a program on a hypothesized world returns an answer, allowing the model to evaluate a question’s informativeness. A probabilistic context-free grammar defines the space of possible questions; a log-linear (energy-based) distribution over programs selects questions using a small set of features: expected information gain (EIG), a binary offset for zero EIG, a complexity penalty derived from the grammar (−log q(x)), answer type indicators (Boolean, Number, Color, Location), and a relevance filter for questions that actually refer to the board.

The model is trained to match the empirical distribution of questions in a Battleship-style information search task (605 questions across 18 ambiguous board states). Training uses importance sampling to approximate gradients and partition functions. The authors assess (a) how well the model predicts which questions people ask in held-out contexts (leave-one-context-out) and (b) whether it can generate novel, human-like questions. The full model outperforms variants that omit informativeness, complexity, or answer-type features; complexity is especially critical. Correlations between predicted energies and human frequencies average ρ=0.64 across contexts. The model can also sample plausible new questions not seen in the data.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to active learning (queries beyond labels), formal semantics of questions (partitions over possible worlds), program-induction views of concepts, and question generation in vision/dialog. Unlike goal-directed dialog systems with fixed templates or neural VQG trained on large paired corpora, this approach emphasizes compositional, goal-directed question generation driven by EIG in a small domain. It is close in spirit to program-based QA and LOT-style concept learning, but targets question generation rather than answering.

Strengths
- Clear computational framework: compositional representation plus an explicit utility signal (EIG) and a cognitively motivated complexity penalty.
- Demonstrates that informativeness alone is insufficient; complexity strongly shapes human question choices.
- Generates novel questions via a grammar rather than memorizing templates, addressing productivity.
- Bridges active learning and cognitive modeling with an executable semantics that enables principled evaluation via possible worlds.

Weaknesses
- Domain and supervision are heavily engineered: a bespoke grammar, hand-specified types, and a small, noise-free game environment. It is unclear how much transfers to richer domains.
- Evaluation is limited in scope: 605 questions, 16 contexts used for modeling, with two contexts and a probabilistic “draw” operator removed for tractability. This may bias results and understate real-world complexity.
- Baselines are narrow. The study compares only ablations of its own features. Comparisons to alternative query policies (e.g., direct EIG maximization with length cost, RL/policy gradient over a grammar, or neural program generators) are missing.
- The link to natural language is indirect. The model operates over semantic programs with no parser or generator showing end-to-end NL behavior.
- Reported metrics rely on approximate likelihoods and rank correlations; no human evaluation of sampled questions’ usefulness or naturalness is provided.

Quality
The technical components are sound for the chosen setting: the grammar is expressive enough to cover the dataset; the EIG computation follows a standard Bayesian ideal observer; and the learning objective is appropriate. However, several practical choices (importance sampling with a uniform PCFG proposal, dropping contexts/operators for tractability) warrant more analysis of variance, convergence, and sensitivity. The partition function and expectations are approximate; additional diagnostics (effective sample sizes, stability across seeds) would strengthen confidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The core ideas—questions as programs, grammar-based generation, and log-linear selection—are clearly presented. Providing pseudocode for training/inference, more detail on the hypothesis space sizes per context, and a compact summary of the grammar in the main text (not only in the supplement) would improve readability.

Originality
Modeling question generation as program synthesis within a cognitive, utility-driven framework is a thoughtful combination of known ideas (LOT, EIG, program semantics). The novelty lies more in the integration and application to human question asking than in new algorithms. Within NIPS, similar ingredients have appeared in program induction and semantic parsing; the contribution here is an interesting reframing rather than a technical advance.

Significance
The work offers insight into how informativeness and complexity trade off in human question asking and points toward richer query spaces for active learning. However, the impact on the NIPS community may be limited by the narrow, hand-specified domain, lack of end-to-end NL, and modest scale. As a cognitive modeling paper, it is compelling; as a machine learning contribution, it feels incremental.

Suggestions for improvement
- Demonstrate transfer: apply the same framework (with minimal engineering) to at least one substantially different domain.
- Add stronger baselines: a length-regularized EIG optimizer; a learned policy over grammar rules (e.g., RL); a neural program generator trained to match question distributions.
- Include human evaluations of sampled questions’ usefulness and naturalness, beyond correlation with frequency.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for the importance sampler and ablations of the relevance filter.
- Explore coupling with a semantic parser to map between natural language and programs.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled framework with executable semantics and compositionality.
- Empirical evidence that complexity matters alongside informativeness.
- Ability to generate novel, contextually appropriate questions.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy domain engineering; limited generalization and scale.
- Missing comparisons to stronger, modern baselines.
- No end-to-end natural language evaluation; modest ML novelty.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject for NIPS: interesting cognitive modeling and a neat formulation, but limited scope and ML impact. The work would be strengthened by broader domains, stronger baselines, and a tighter NL interface.
