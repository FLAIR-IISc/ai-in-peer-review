This paper proposes a cognitive model designed to predict the timing and context of certain questions (in relation to belief states). The model defines the unnormalized log probability of a question as a weighted sum of four key factors: informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (approximated by question length), answer type, and relevance (whether the question pertains to the belief state or general logical facts). The authors apply this model to the Battleship domain, where the objective is to locate ships within a grid. The full model, incorporating all of these features, outperforms its lesioned versions with fewer features in predicting human data (based on log-likelihood). However, when considering correlation with human judgments, the model without expected information gain (EIG) performs similarly to the full model.

The paper appears to be technically sound. While it is successful as an AI project, its contribution to cognitive science is less convincing. The authors claim that their model predicts human questioning behavior and sheds light on the balance between informativeness and complexity from a cognitive science perspective. However, the results do not provide conclusive evidence regarding how people balance these factors. In the Battleship domain, the impact of EIG on human questioning behavior is either minimal (based on log-likelihood results, which have a 19% chance of being incorrect according to bootstrap estimates) or negligible (based on correlation results, which are noisy due to sparse human data). These findings do not allow for general conclusions about how people balance these factors.

The paper is well-written, clear, and reproducible. However, it lacks discussion of prior work. The authors' approach to modeling questions as programs that output answers when executed on the state of a possible world is similar to the notion of "goals" introduced by Hawkins (2015) and based on Roberts' (1996) concept of question-under-discussion (QUD). Hawkins' questioner model also utilizes expected information gain and trades it off with question complexity, albeit in a simpler domain.

This type of model, which incorporates a compositional prior over questions and chooses based on EIG and other factors, is promising for cognitive and AI models. Its potential for future applications is significant. The primary contribution of this paper lies in demonstrating the feasibility of generating interesting compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this approach.
