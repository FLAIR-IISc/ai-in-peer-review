This paper investigates human question-asking in a "battleship"-style domain where players must identify hidden, colored shapes on a partially observable grid. The task is to ask a single question that yields a categorical (K-ary) answer to maximally reduce uncertainty about the board's state.

The authors' primary contribution is a computational model of question generation based on a hand-crafted Probabilistic Context-Free Grammar (PCFG). This grammar serves as a prior over a space of possible questions, which are formally represented as statements in lambda calculus. The model scores the quality of a potential question as a linear combination of three factors:
1.  **Informativeness:** The expected information gain about the board state.
2.  **Complexity:** The simplicity of the question, measured by its length or, alternatively, its negative log-probability under the PCFG prior.
3.  **Answer Type:** A preference for certain types of answers (e.g., true/false vs. color).

To evaluate their model, the authors used human data from a previously published study, which they manually translated into the logical forms defined by their grammar. Using leave-one-out cross-validation, they compared the full model to "lesioned" versions where each of the three factors was removed. The results demonstrate that all components contribute meaningfully to the model's predictive power, with question complexity being the most significant factor. Finally, the paper showcases several novel questions generated by the model, illustrating its qualitative behavior.

**Strengths and Contributions**

Question-asking is a fundamental aspect of intelligence, connecting linguistics, cognitive science, and machine learning domains like active learning and hypothesis testing. This paper offers an innovative perspective that unifies modern linguistic theory with cognitive modeling to formally examine how people select questions. This approach has the potential to foster significant new research, particularly in the active learning community, by providing a framework for agents that can ask more sophisticated, compositional questions.

The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and presents a thoughtfully designed model. The evaluation is appropriate and convincing, combining quantitative analysis through model comparison with a qualitative inspection of the model's behavior.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this work makes a notable advance in the study of self-directed learning. It helps resolve a puzzle from previous research, which found that informativeness alone is a poor predictor of human question-asking. This paper compellingly argues that this discrepancy arises because question complexity is a more dominant factor. This represents a significant contribution to the cognitive science literature, which in itself could be sufficient to justify publication at NIPS.

**Limitations and Suggestions**

Despite its strengths, the work has significant limitations that may reduce its impact on the broader NIPS audience. The primary concern is scalability and generalizability. While the manual translation from natural language questions to logical forms was feasible for this study, it is unclear how this process could be automated. Furthermore, it is not obvious how the overall framework would extend to more complex, high-dimensional domains of interest to the wider machine learning community. The "battleship" domain is relatively small, yet the authors note that their method is already computationally demanding.

That being said, the core ideas—particularly the integration of richer, compositional questions into active learning frameworks—are compelling and timely. To strengthen the paper's appeal to a NIPS audience, the authors could provide a clearer roadmap for future work. Discussing a compelling, large-scale scenario where their technique could be applicable (e.g., interactive debugging, scientific discovery, or more complex dialogue systems) would help demonstrate the potential for broader impact and provide novel insights.

**Minor Comments**

1.  Regarding the ablation study, it may be possible to perform a nested model comparison using the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models. This would allow for formal tests of statistical significance for each model component, strengthening the quantitative results.

2.  For future work on sequential question-asking, the authors might find the "Questions under Discussion" (QUD) framework from formal semantics and pragmatics to be highly relevant. This body of literature offers formal models for how discourse context and goals shape questioning strategies. The following references may serve as useful pointers:
    *   Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. *Linguistics and Philosophy, 18*(5), 459-527.
    *   Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. *Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17*, 131-139.
