The paper presents a cognitive model designed to predict when certain questions are asked in specific contexts (belief states). Under this model, the unnormalized log probability of a question is defined as the weighted sum of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (roughly, question length), answer type, and "relevance" (whether the question refers to the belief state or just world-independent logical facts). This model is applied to the Battleship domain, where the task is to determine the location of a number of ships (clusters of grid elements) within a grid. The full model, incorporating all of the features mentioned above, outperformed lesioned versions with fewer features in predicting held-out human data (in terms of log-likelihood). However, in terms of correlation with human judgments, the model that did not account for expected information gain (EIG) performed similarly to the full model.

The paper appears to be generally technically sound.

While the paper succeeds more as an AI project than as a cognitive science project, its contributions are notable. The authors claim that "We find that our model predicts what question people will ask" and "from a CogSci standpoint, our results show how people balance informativeness and complexity." However, I am not convinced that we gain significant insights into how people balance these factors. In the Battleship domain, it seems that people are either slightly influenced by EIG (if we believe the log-likelihood results, which, according to bootstrap estimates, have a 19% chance of being incorrect) or not at all (if we believe the correlation results, which are also somewhat noisy due to sparse human data). I do not think these results allow us to draw general conclusions about how people balance these factors.

The paper is well-written and clear, and I could likely reproduce the results.

However, the paper lacks some prior work. One of the apparent innovations of the paper is to "model questions as programs that, when executed on the state of a possible world, output an answer." This concept is similar to the notion of a "goal" used in Hawkins 2015 ("Why do you ask? Good questions provoke informative answers.") and is based on the notion of a question-under-discussion (QUD) introduced by Roberts 1996 ("Information structure in discourse: Towards an integrated formal theory of pragmatics."). The questioner model by Hawkins also utilizes expected information gain and trades it off with the complexity of questions, but is applied to a much simpler domain.

I believe that this type of model (which has a compositional prior over questions and chooses based on EIG and other factors) makes sense as a component for cognitive and AI models and is likely to be used more in the future. I view the main contribution of this paper as demonstrating that it is possible to generate interesting compositional questions for a non-trivial domain using this approach.
