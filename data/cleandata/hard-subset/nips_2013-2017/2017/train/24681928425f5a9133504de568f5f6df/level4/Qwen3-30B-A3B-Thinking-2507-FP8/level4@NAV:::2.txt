

The authors investigate human question asking with K-ary answers, defining a hand-crafted PCFG for a "battleship" domain featuring hidden colored shapes in a partially observable grid (where tiles reveal ship segments of specific colors or emptiness). The agent's task is to formulate single-word-answer questions maximizing information about the board state. The PCFG served as a prior over questions, formalized as lambda calculus statements. Question quality was quantified as a linear combination of informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (length or negative log probability under uniform PCFG rewrite rules), and "answer type" (e.g., true/false vs. color). Human data, sourced from a prior publication, was hand-coded into the grammar. Lesioned model variants were compared via leave-one-out cross-validation, revealing all factors contributed non-negligibly (with complexity being dominant). The authors also generated novel questions.

Question asking is a significant domain in linguistics and hypothesis testing. The authors offer an innovative synthesis of modern linguistic and cognitive theory to examine human question-asking, with potential to advance active learning through more sophisticated question-asking models. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, with robust evaluation spanning quantitative and qualitative dimensions.

From a computational cognitive science perspective, this work advances self-directed learning. Previous studies found question informativeness a poor predictor of human behavior; this paper partially resolves this by showing complexity (and potential constraints from the question space size) is more influential. This contribution to cognitive science literature sufficiently warrants publication in NIPS.

However, significant limitations may restrict broader NIPS appeal. While automating human question text to semantic logic statements might be feasible, scalability to domains of wider interest is unclear. The Battleship domain is small (few features), and the method is already computationally intensive. The authors should clarify a compelling scenario where their technique applies to broader communities and yields novel insights.

Minor comments:  
1) Nested model comparisons using log-likelihoods of lesioned models could provide statistical significance tests.  
2) For sequential question asking, future work should consider existing formal semantics/pragmatics models (e.g., Questions under Discussion literature). Key references:  
Ginzburg, J. (1995). Resolving questions, part I. Linguistics and Philosophy, 18(5), 459-527.  
Rojas-Esponda, T. (2013). The roadsigns of communication. Proceedings of Semdial (DialDam), 17, 131-139.
