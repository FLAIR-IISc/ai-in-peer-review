This paper presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking, treating questions as formal programs that can be executed on the state of the world to output an answer. The authors propose a probabilistic generative model that predicts which questions people will ask, based on a combination of features such as informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model is evaluated on a data set of natural language questions asked by humans in an information-search game, and the results show that the model can accurately predict the distribution of human questions and generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper is well-written and clear, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model from both quantitative and qualitative perspectives. The approach is innovative and has the potential to foster novel research in the active learning literature, particularly in the area of question asking.

From the perspective of computational cognitive science, this paper advances the field of self-directed learning by providing a predictive model of open-ended human question asking. The authors' finding that complexity is a crucial factor in human question asking, in addition to informativeness, is a significant contribution to the cognitive science literature.

However, there are some limitations to the approach that may limit its interest to the broader NIPS audience. The method relies on a hand-coded grammar for the "battleship" domain, which may not be easily scalable or extensible to other domains. The computational demands of the approach are also significant, and it is unclear how it would perform on more complex domains.

To increase the paper's appeal to the broader NIPS audience, the authors may need to provide a clear scenario of interest to the community where their technique is likely to be applicable and provide novel insights. Additionally, the authors may want to consider exploring ways to automate the process of converting human question text to statements in some semantic logic, which would make the approach more practical and widely applicable.

Minor comments:

1. The authors may want to consider performing a nested model comparison with the log-likelihoods of the lesioned models to obtain tests of statistical significance.
2. For sequential question asking, the authors may want to explore other models within the linguistics literature of question-asking in formal semantics and pragmatics, such as the Questions under Discussion literature.
