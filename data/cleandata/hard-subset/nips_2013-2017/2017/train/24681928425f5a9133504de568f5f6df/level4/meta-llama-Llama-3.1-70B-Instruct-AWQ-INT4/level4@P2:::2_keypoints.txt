- Novel approach: models questions as executable programs and uses a probabilistic generative model to predict human question asking.
- Model features: predicts questions using informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Evaluation: tested on human natural-language questions from an information-search game.
- Results: accurately predicts distribution of human questions and can generate novel, human-like questions.
- Clarity and evaluation: paper is well-written with thorough quantitative and qualitative analyses.
- Cognitive science contribution: advances self-directed learning modeling and shows complexity is crucial alongside informativeness.
- Main limitation: relies on a hand-coded grammar for the "battleship" domain, limiting scalability and extensibility.
- Computational cost: method is computationally demanding and unclear how it scales to more complex domains.
- Applicability concern: may not be broadly applicable or of wide interest to the NIPS community without a clearer target scenario.
- Suggested improvement: provide a clear NIPS-relevant application scenario and novel insights for that community.
- Suggested improvement: explore automating conversion of human question text into semantic logic representations.
- Minor comment: perform nested model comparisons (lesioned models' log-likelihoods) to test statistical significance.
- Minor comment: for sequential question asking, consider models from linguistics such as the Questions Under Discussion literature.
- Strengths summary: novel approach, evaluated on real human data, predicts and generates human-like questions.
- Weaknesses summary: hand-coded grammar, high computational demands, limited cross-domain applicability.
- Recommendation and score: Accept with minor revisions; rating 7/10.
