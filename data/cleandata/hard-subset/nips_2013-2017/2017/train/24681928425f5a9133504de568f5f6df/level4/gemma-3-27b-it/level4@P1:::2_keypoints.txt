- Paper models human single-word question asking in a Battleship domain using a PCFG prior over questions represented in lambda calculus.  
- Task: pick one-word questions that maximize information gain about a partially observed grid of colored shapes.  
- Question quality scored by a linear function combining informativeness, complexity, and answer type.  
- Human question data from prior work was parsed into the proposed grammar for evaluation.  
- Leave-one-out cross-validation shows all factors help prediction, with complexity being especially important.  
- Authors generate novel, plausible questions from the model and provide qualitative examples.  
- Clear, well-written paper with thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluation.  
- Advances cognitive science by explaining why informativeness alone poorly predicts human question asking and highlighting complexity’s role.  
- Innovative framing of question asking as program generation, relevant to linguistics and active learning.  
- Major limitation: unclear scalability and generalizability beyond the small Battleship domain.  
- Computationally demanding approach may not scale to richer, real-world domains.  
- Requires substantial domain knowledge and manual grammar construction, limiting practical applicability.  
- Minor suggestion: use nested model comparisons and likelihood ratio tests to assess significance between lesioned models.  
- Suggestion for future work: connect to formal semantics/pragmatics literature on sequential questioning (e.g., Questions under Discussion).
