- Introduces a cognitive-style model where question probability is a weighted sum of EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Defines questions as programs executed on possible worlds to generate answers.  
- Evaluated in a Battleship grid domain for inferring ship locations.  
- Full model outperforms lesioned variants on held-out human data by log-likelihood.  
- Models excluding EIG achieve nearly the same correlation with human judgments.  
- Results are conflicting: log-likelihood suggests a small EIG effect, but correlation and sparse data imply no EIG influence.  
- Bootstrap analysis indicates a 19% chance the observed log-likelihood difference is due to chance.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, and reproducible.  
- Contribution is stronger for AI than for cognitive science; claims about predicting human question behavior are not fully supported.  
- Omits relevant prior work, notably Hawkins (2015) and Roberts (1996) QUD framing.  
- Despite gaps, the compositional prior + EIG/complexity trade-off is a promising framework and produces meaningful compositional questions.
