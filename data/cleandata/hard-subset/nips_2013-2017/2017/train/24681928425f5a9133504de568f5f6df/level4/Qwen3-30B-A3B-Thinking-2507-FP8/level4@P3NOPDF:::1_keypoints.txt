- Proposes generating strategic questions in a Battleship-like game using a Montague-style compositional logical language and a log-linear model over four features.  
- Task is overly simplified: perfect observability and fixed rules make it unchallenging for language or learning research.  
- Core contributions lack novelty and depth; the model and features are trivial in this constrained setting.  
- The log-linear model offers no substantive ML innovation beyond a basic heuristic.  
- Features like “board relevance” are trivial to define here and do not demonstrate meaningful design complexity.  
- Fails to show how the framework advances active learning, compositional language modeling, or practical applications in complex domains.  
- No empirical comparisons to non-logical baselines or alternative question-generation strategies.  
- Conflates linguistic structure with strategic value; feature engineering treats strategically different questions as equivalent.  
- Authors do not justify why the four features capture meaningful distinctions or generalize beyond this narrow task.  
- No evaluation against human strategies or more sophisticated language models to assess real-world benefit.  
- “Compositionality” is superficial—logical structure is imposed on an artificial language with no connection to natural language pragmatics or ambiguity.  
- Problem space is too constrained to inform active learning issues like observation uncertainty or language generation challenges like noisy inputs.  
- Paper fails to articulate a clear research gap and provides no supporting related work showing necessity of compositional semantics in active learning.  
- Technical contribution is minimal and results are weak: accuracy on a synthetic dataset without analysis or baseline comparisons.  
- Overall, not aligned with NIPS priorities (novel algorithms, theoretical grounding, empirical breakthroughs) and better suited to a game-theory or linguistics venue.
