- Introduces a cognitive model that predicts question selection using a weighted combination of EIG (informativeness), complexity (question length), answer type, and relevance (belief-state vs. world-independent).
- Tested in the Battleship domain (locating hidden ships on a grid).
- Full model (all features) outperforms ablated versions on held-out human data by log-likelihood.
- Correlation with human judgments is similar with and without EIG, so EIG’s role is unclear.
- Results give limited evidence for how people trade off informativeness and complexity in question selection.
- Battleship findings are ambiguous: log-likelihood suggests slight EIG influence but bootstrap indicates a 19% error chance; noisy correlations likely reflect limited human data.
- Work is technically sound, clearly written, and appears replicable.
- Contribution seems stronger for AI than for cognitive science; authors’ claim that the model “predicts what people will ask” is overstated.
- Significant omission of related prior work (e.g., Hawkins 2015 and Roberts 1996 QUD framework).
- Promising approach overall: compositional prior plus EIG/other factors successfully generates meaningful compositional questions in a non-trivial domain.
