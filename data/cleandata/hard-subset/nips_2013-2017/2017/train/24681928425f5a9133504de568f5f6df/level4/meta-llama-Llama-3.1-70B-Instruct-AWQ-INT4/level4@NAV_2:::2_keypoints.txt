- Task: model human question-asking in a "battleship" domain where answers are single-word (K-ary) and the agent asks to maximize information about a partially observed grid.  
- Representation: questions defined as lambda-calculus statements and generated by a hand-designed PCFG acting as a prior.  
- Question goodness: modeled as a linear combination of expected information gain (informativeness), complexity (length/negative log PCFG prob), and answer type (e.g., yes/no vs. color).  
- Data: human questions came from a previously published dataset and were hand-coded into the grammar.  
- Evaluation: compared lesioned models using leave-one-out cross-validation; all factors contributed, with complexity being the dominant predictor.  
- Outputs: model produced qualitatively novel questions consistent with the framework.  
- Strengths: innovative unification of linguistic and cognitive theory, clear writing, and appropriate quantitative and qualitative evaluation.  
- Contribution to cognitive science: advances understanding of self-directed learning and explains why informativeness alone poorly predicts human question-asking (complexity can dominate).  
- Limitation — scalability: domain is small with few features, method is computationally strained, and scalability/generalization to broader AI domains is unclear.  
- Recommendation: authors should present a clear scenario where the technique scales or provides novel insights for the broader community.  
- Minor comment 1: perform nested model comparisons on lesioned-model log-likelihoods to test statistical significance.  
- Minor comment 2: for sequential question asking, consider literature on Questions under Discussion and formal pragmatics (references suggested).
