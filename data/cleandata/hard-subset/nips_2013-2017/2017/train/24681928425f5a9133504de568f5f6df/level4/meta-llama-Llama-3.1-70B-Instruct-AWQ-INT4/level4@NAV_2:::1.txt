This paper presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking as a form of program generation, which is a key aspect of active machine learning. The authors propose a probabilistic model that treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. This approach is evaluated using a data set of natural language questions asked by human participants in an information-search game, and the results show that the model can predict the frequencies of human questions and generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and results. The authors provide a thorough review of related work and clearly situate their contribution within the context of active machine learning and cognitive science. The use of a log-linear model over four simple features of the questions (informativeness, answer type, complexity, and relevance) is a reasonable choice, and the results demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance informativeness and complexity when generating questions. The authors show that their model can produce questions that are both informative and concise, which is an important aspect of human question asking. The paper also provides a clear and detailed explanation of the model's components, including the grammar, probabilistic generative model, and optimization procedure.

However, the paper's contribution may be seen as limited by some reviewers. The task of generating questions in a game of battleship is relatively simple and highly constrained, and the logical semantics of the questions are unambiguous. Some reviewers may question the generalizability of the approach to more complex and open-ended question-asking tasks. Additionally, the paper's reliance on a log-linear model over simple features may be seen as overly simplistic by some reviewers.

Overall, this paper presents a well-motivated and clearly explained approach to modeling human question asking as a form of program generation. While the contribution may be seen as limited by some reviewers, the paper's results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their model.
