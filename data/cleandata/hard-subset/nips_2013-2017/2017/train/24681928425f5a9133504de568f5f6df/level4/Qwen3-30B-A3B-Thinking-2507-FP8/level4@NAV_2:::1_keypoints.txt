- Model: Treats questions as executable programs in a lambda-calculus language within a probabilistic generative model using features for informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and board relevance.
- Evaluation: Tested on a Battleship dataset of 605 human questions, predicts question frequencies with Spearman 0.64 and generates novel human-like questions.
- Technical quality: Sound log-linear probabilistic model with rigorous cross-validation and lesion analyses showing each component’s necessity.
- Domain limitation: Task is highly constrained to a single game with unambiguous rules, limiting claim generality about human-like questioning.
- Reliance on prior knowledge: Model depends on predefined domain knowledge (e.g., ship properties) which is acknowledged but not addressed for broader applicability.
- Clarity: Exceptionally clear exposition, detailed formalism and grammar specs, good visualizations; minor clarifications needed for EIG computation and the filter feature.
- Originality: Novel formalization of questions as executable programs and compositional program synthesis for goal-directed, context-sensitive question generation.
- Relation to prior work: Extends probabilistic programming approaches and differs from VQG/active learning by generating novel questions without large datasets and capturing context-dependent utility.
- Significance: Advances cognitive science modeling of question asking but limited impact for broader ML/NIPS audience due to narrow domain and lack of demonstrated practical benefits.
- Pros: Novel formalism, strong quantitative fit, rigorous ablations, clear technical execution.
- Cons: Narrow domain, domain-specific assumptions, no evidence of generalization to real-world questioning or improvement in ML tasks.
- Overall: Technically rigorous and theoretically valuable for cognitive modeling but falls short on broader significance and applicability for a NIPS-level contribution.
