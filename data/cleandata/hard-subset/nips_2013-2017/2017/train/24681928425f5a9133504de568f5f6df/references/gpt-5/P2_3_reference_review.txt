Summary and relation to prior work
The paper models question asking as program generation: questions are lambda/LISP-style programs that, when executed on a hypothesized world state, return an answer. A PCFG defines a compositional hypothesis space over programs; a log-linear model over program features (informativeness via expected information gain, complexity via −log PCFG probability, answer type, and a relevance filter) defines a distribution over questions conditioned on context. Parameters are learned to maximize likelihood of human-authored questions from a Battleship-like information search task (605 questions across 18 contexts; two contexts dropped for computation), using importance sampling to approximate expectations and normalizers. Ablations remove informativeness, complexity, or answer-type features. The model predicts human question distributions in leave-one-context-out settings and samples novel questions judged qualitatively reasonable.

This connects to: (i) active learning (beyond label queries), (ii) formal semantics (questions as partitions over worlds), (iii) semantic parsing/program induction for question answering, and (iv) cognitive models of compositional, program-like concepts. Relative to prior work on VQG and dialog systems that rely on large supervised corpora or canned templates, the contribution is a generative, compositional approach to producing goal-directed, context-sensitive questions with an explicit utility model.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formalization is coherent: defining questions as executable programs permits computing answer distributions and EIG over a hypothesis set, and a log-linear model over interpretable features is appropriate for density estimation. The ablations are informative and show that complexity control is essential.
- Inference/optimization: Approximations are heavy. Expectations and Z are estimated by importance sampling from the PCFG with uniform rewrite choices. It appears a fixed pool of 150k sampled programs is reused for gradient estimates and Z; this risks bias and high variance. No diagnostics (ESS, variance) or sensitivity to proposal or sample size are reported.
- Data and evaluation: The dataset is small (≈591 questions after removing “draw” programs) and from a single, highly engineered domain with a handcrafted grammar and primitives. Held-out evaluation is across 16 contexts from the same domain. LL improvements are reported (with bootstrap over Z) but magnitudes are not given; correlations between model energy and human question frequencies average 0.64 for the full model, with the information-agnostic variant at 0.65, which undercuts the claim that informativeness improves rank prediction (authors instead rely on LL). Novel question generation is evaluated qualitatively without human judgments or blind ratings.
- Assumptions/limitations: EIG relies on a manually specified hypothesis space and exact execution of programs; 14 questions were removed due to computational complexity. A relevance feature requires manual marking of “board-referencing” constructs.

Clarity
The paper is clear and well organized, with compelling examples that make the program representations intuitive. Important implementation details are deferred or ambiguous: size of hypothesis spaces per context, whether the sampler is resampled per gradient step, computational cost, and precise form of the importance estimator (the text suggests Z ≈ E[p(x;θ) q(x)] rather than the standard E[p/q], likely a typo). The discrepancy between LL and correlation-based evaluations should be discussed more directly.

Originality
The central idea—treating question asking as compositional program synthesis optimized for expected information—is novel in the NeurIPS community. While grounded in known strands (LOT, formal semantics, EIG, PCFGs), the combination to generate human-like questions and to fit a predictive model of human question frequencies appears original. Related work is generally well covered.

Significance
As a cognitive/computational account of human question asking, the work is interesting and could seed a line of research on richer active querying. For core ML impact, current significance is modest: the approach is domain-specific, relies on handcrafted grammars/primitives, and is evaluated only on predicting human question distributions, not on improving downstream learning or interacting with natural-language systems. Demonstrations in additional domains or integration with a learned semantic parser would raise impact.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation of questions as executable programs; leverages compositionality and computability.
- Interpretable feature-based model with meaningful ablations; strong evidence that complexity control matters beyond informativeness alone.
- Public dataset; qualitative samples show nontrivial, creative new questions.
- Bridges active learning, formal semantics, and program induction in an interesting way.

Cons
- Heavy dependence on handcrafted, domain-specific grammar and relevance annotations; unclear generalization to new domains.
- Small, single-domain dataset; dropped contexts and removed operators due to computational issues.
- Evaluation limited: no human evaluation of generated questions; no comparison to simple baselines beyond ablations (e.g., length prior + type priors, template libraries); mixed signals between LL and correlation metrics.
- Importance sampling/inference details and diagnostics are thin; estimator typo; potential bias from fixed proposal pool.
- Informativeness feature yields only modest gains in reported correlations; claims could be better substantiated.

Questions/requests for authors
- Do you resample programs each gradient step? Provide ESS/variance diagnostics and sensitivity to the proposal and sample size.
- Report absolute LL gains and CIs across contexts; reconcile LL vs correlation results for the information-agnostic model.
- Quantify hypothesis space sizes and runtimes per context; can you handle the “draw” operator with approximate inference?
- Add baselines: e.g., a template bank tuned per context, or a unigram/PCFG prior with only length/type features; human evaluation of sampled questions’ usefulness.
- Demonstrate transfer to a second domain with minimal new engineering, or interface with a learned semantic parser to map NL to programs.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is conceptually elegant and clearly written, with a nice integration of ideas and suggestive results. However, the empirical evaluation is narrow and the approach currently relies on substantial domain-specific engineering and approximate inference without sufficient diagnostics. Strengthening the evaluation (additional domains, stronger baselines, human judgments, clearer inference details) would make this a stronger fit for NeurIPS.
