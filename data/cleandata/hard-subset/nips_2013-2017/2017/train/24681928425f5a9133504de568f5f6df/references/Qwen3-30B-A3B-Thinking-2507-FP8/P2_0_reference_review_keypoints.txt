- Proposes modeling human question asking as program synthesis, representing questions as executable programs evaluated on possible worlds.  
- Key contributions: compositional grammar for question programs, probabilistic model using informativeness/complexity/answer type/relevance, and evaluation in a Battleship-style domain.  

- Methodologically rigorous with a well-defined probabilistic model and cognitively grounded features.  
- Comprehensive evaluation including quantitative log-likelihood comparisons and qualitative novel-question generation.  
- Ablation studies show each feature matters, especially complexity.  

- Evaluation limited to a single Battleship domain, raising generalizability concerns.  
- Computational complexity is high (requires importance sampling) and scalability is not thoroughly addressed.  
- Unclear how model handles larger hypothesis spaces or more complex game states.  

- Exceptionally well-organized and clearly written with precise grammar descriptions and illustrative examples.  
- Figures effectively demonstrate model capabilities and the paper distinguishes itself from related work.  
- Reliance on supplementary materials (not provided) slightly hampers full evaluation.  

- The connection between semantic/programmatic representations and natural language generation is discussed but remains abstract.  

- Modeling questions as executable programs is a novel and original insight for question asking.  
- Combines compositionality and computability to generate open-ended, human-like questions without large datasets.  
- Differentiates from dialog systems and VQG by modeling question generation rather than using canned or data-hungry approaches.  

- Domain-specific grammar (designed for Battleship) limits scope and the paper doesn't fully argue domain-general applicability.  

- Advances active learning by modeling rich, goal-directed, human-like questioning and balancing informativeness vs complexity.  
- Demonstrates ability to generate novel useful questions, with potential applications in tutoring and human-AI collaboration.  
- Makes a concrete contribution to computational cognitive science.  

- Significance constrained by narrow evaluation domain and the semantic vs natural language gap, which limits practical application.  

- Overall assessment: high-quality, technically sound, and novel work deserving acceptance with minor revisions.  

- Arguments for acceptance: novel program-generation framing, comprehensive evaluation and ablations, ability to generate human-like questions, strong cognitive grounding, clear contribution to the field.  
- Arguments against acceptance: single-domain evaluation, domain-specific grammar, unclear scalability, and lack of natural language integration.  

- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Revision requests: clarify which grammar components are domain-general vs domain-specific and how to extend to new domains; discuss computational complexity and scalability; more explicitly address bridging semantic program representations to natural language.
