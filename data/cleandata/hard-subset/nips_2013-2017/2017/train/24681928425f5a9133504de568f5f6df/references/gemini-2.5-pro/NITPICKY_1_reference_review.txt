This paper presents a computational framework for modeling context-sensitive, goal-directed human question asking. The central idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional language, recasting the problem of question generation as one of program synthesis. The authors develop a probabilistic, log-linear model defined over a space of programs generated by a custom grammar. The model's features include the expected information gain (EIG) of a question, its complexity, its answer type, and its relevance to the game domain. The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game. The authors show that their full model provides a better fit to the human data (in terms of log-likelihood) than several ablated versions and can generate novel, human-like questions.

**Strengths:**
1.  The core proposal to model question asking as probabilistic program generation is novel and compelling. It provides an elegant formalism for moving beyond the simple label queries common in active learning and captures the compositional and productive nature of human inquiry.
2.  The paper presents a complete, end-to-end modeling effort, from the formal grammar definition to the probabilistic model, optimization procedure, and both quantitative and qualitative evaluations.
3.  The qualitative results in Figure 3 are impressive, demonstrating that the framework can generate semantically coherent, contextually relevant, and non-trivial questions that were not observed in the human data. This highlights the generative power of the approach.
4.  The ablation study is a good first step towards understanding the contribution of different factors (informativeness, complexity, answer type) that guide human question asking.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while built on a promising idea, suffers from a substantial number of methodological and presentational issues that undermine confidence in the results and conclusions.

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The manuscript lacks the technical precision expected for a NIPS publication.
    *   Critical details regarding the experimental setup are relegated to footnotes and lack justification. For instance, Footnote 7 states that two of the 18 contexts were dropped "for computational reasons." Footnote 4 notes that 14 questions and a corresponding grammar rule were removed due to computational complexity. These are significant data exclusions that could bias the results and call into question the scalability and robustness of the proposed method. The reasons for this computational intractability are not adequately explained.
    *   The optimization procedure (Section 4.4) uses fixed hyperparameters (100,000 iterations, learning rate of 0.1) without any justification, sensitivity analysis, or discussion of convergence.
    *   The approximation of the intractable partition function and model expectations via importance sampling is a critical component. However, the choice of a uniform PCFG as the proposal distribution is not justified. This can be a highly inefficient choice if the target distribution is peaked, leading to high-variance estimates. The paper provides no analysis of the quality or variance of this approximation, making it difficult to trust the resulting log-likelihood values.

2.  **Evaluation and Interpretation of Results:** The quantitative evaluation is not entirely convincing and contains a significant contradiction that the authors fail to address.
    *   While the full model shows the best performance in terms of log-likelihood (Table 1), the Spearman's rank correlation results (page 6) are puzzling. The information-agnostic model achieves a slightly *higher* correlation (0.65) than the full model (0.64). This directly contradicts the central claim that informativeness (EIG) is a critical feature for predicting human questions. This discrepancy is a major flaw and is not discussed or explained.
    *   The paper reports that EIG alone has no correlation with question frequency, yet the model relies on it. The inclusion of the `f_EIG=0` feature feels like an ad-hoc fix to penalize uninformative questions, rather than a principled integration of information gain.

3.  **Domain Specificity and Generalizability:** The entire framework is highly tailored to the Battleship game. The grammar (Table SI-1), the semantic primitives (e.g., `size`, ship colors), and the hypothesis space are all domain-specific. While the authors claim "many aspects of our grammar are domain general," this seems overstated. The path to applying this method to a new domain is unclear and would seemingly require substantial manual engineering of a new grammar and hypothesis space representation. This severely limits the significance of the work for the broader machine learning community and weakens the claim that this represents a general step "toward more capable active learning machines."

4.  **Lack of Polish:** The manuscript is riddled with minor but distracting errors that suggest a lack of careful preparation.
    *   The conference attribution on page 1 contains a typo ("st Conference").
    *   The reference list is inconsistently formatted and contains clear errors (e.g., references [12] and [22] begin with single-letter typos).
    *   The arbitrary collapsing of "Orientation" into "Boolean" answer types (Section 4.5) is an unprincipled simplification.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and conceptually elegant framework for a challenging problem: generating rich, goal-directed questions.
*   The approach successfully integrates ideas from program synthesis, Bayesian inference, and cognitive science.
*   The qualitative results demonstrate a capacity for creative generation that is lacking in current systems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The quantitative evaluation is flawed, containing a key contradiction regarding the importance of informativeness that is left unaddressed.
*   The method's robustness is questionable, given the unexplained exclusion of certain data points and contexts due to "computational reasons."
*   Key methodological choices (e.g., hyperparameters, proposal distribution for importance sampling) are not justified, hindering reproducibility and trust in the results.
*   The extreme domain specificity limits the immediate impact and generalizability of the contribution.
*   The manuscript suffers from a general lack of precision and polish, with numerous minor errors that collectively undermine its credibility.

In conclusion, while the paper's core idea is interesting, the execution and presentation are not up to the standards of a top-tier conference like NIPS. The combination of an unaddressed contradiction in the quantitative results, a lack of methodological rigor, and concerns about generalizability and reproducibility leads me to recommend rejection. The work is promising, but it requires significant refinement, more careful analysis, and a more transparent and rigorous presentation before it can be considered a solid scientific contribution.
