- Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs, reframing question asking as program synthesis and demonstrating this on a Battleship-like dataset.  
- Positions the work against active learning, dialog systems, and VQG, arguing for greater flexibility and lower data needs; cites relevant linguistics and cognitive science.  
- Novel idea: representing questions as programs is an insightful and original contribution.  
- Strong theoretical grounding via lambda calculus, LISP, compositionality, and computability.  
- Empirical results on the Battleship dataset show the model predicts human question frequencies and can generate plausible novel questions.  
- Paper provides a detailed probabilistic model, grammar, features, optimization procedure, and useful supplementary material.  
- Thoughtful and accurate analysis of related work situating the contribution in multiple fields.  
- Major concern about domain specificity: the model and grammar appear tailored to the Battleship game, limiting generalizability.  
- Model complexity (probabilistic grammar, feature engineering, importance sampling) may hinder scalability to larger, diverse domains.  
- Quantitative evaluation is limited to a single dataset, with potential data sparsity and insufficient baseline comparisons.  
- No formal human evaluation of generated questions to assess quality, relevance, or usefulness.  
- The introduced f∅(x) “filter” feature seems like a pragmatic workaround, not a principled component of the model.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, strong theory, promising empirical results, and clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited generalizability, scalability concerns, weak quantitative baselines, and lack of human evaluation.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions due to limitations that can be addressed.  
- Suggested revisions: discuss generalizability strategies, add comparisons to simple baselines, conduct human evaluations of generated questions, and clarify or replace the f∅(x) filter.
