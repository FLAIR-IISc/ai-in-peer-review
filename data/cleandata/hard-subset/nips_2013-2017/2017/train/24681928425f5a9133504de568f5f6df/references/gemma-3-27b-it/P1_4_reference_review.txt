## NIPS 2017 Review: Question Asking as Program Generation

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The authors argue that framing questions as programs – which, when executed on a world state, yield an answer – provides a powerful formalism for understanding human inquiry. They introduce a probabilistic model to predict and generate questions within the context of a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequency and synthesize novel, human-like questions. The core idea is a significant departure from traditional active learning approaches that focus on simple label requests, aiming to capture the richer, more targeted questioning strategies humans employ.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper appropriately positions itself within the active learning literature (Settles, 2009), highlighting the limitations of current methods that largely focus on label acquisition. It differentiates itself from AI dialog systems (Serban et al., 2017) which often rely on pre-defined question sets, and visual question answering systems (Strub et al., 2017) that typically exhibit stereotyped questioning patterns.  The authors connect their work to broader themes in cognitive science, citing research on compositionality (Fodor, 1987; Marcus, 1988) and the “language of thought” (Piantadosi et al., 2016), offering a compelling theoretical grounding for their approach. The work builds on a previously published dataset (Gureckis et al., 2017) that provides a unique and valuable resource for studying human question-asking behavior.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The framing of question asking as program generation is a highly original and promising approach. It offers a computationally tractable way to model the semantic richness of human questions.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper effectively leverages concepts from linguistics, cognitive science, and formal program synthesis to build a coherent and well-motivated model. The connection to the idea of questions as world-partitioning mechanisms (Groenendijk & Stokhof, thesis) is insightful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on a challenging, human-generated dataset. The results showing the ability to predict question frequencies and generate novel questions are encouraging.
* **Detailed Model Description:** The paper provides a comprehensive description of the probabilistic model, including the grammar, features, and optimization procedure.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge this, the current implementation is heavily tied to the “Battleship” game.  Extending the grammar and model to more complex domains may prove challenging.  The removal of the tile drawing question from the dataset suggests limitations in the current system's ability to handle certain types of queries.
* **Complexity of the Model:**  The model is quite complex, with a large number of features and parameters. This makes it difficult to fully understand the contribution of each component and potentially increases the risk of overfitting.
* **Importance Sampling Approximation:** The reliance on importance sampling for approximating the normalizing constant introduces potential sources of error and uncertainty in the model's predictions. The authors mention bootstrapping to address this, which is good, but a more robust approximation technique might be considered.
* **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** While the paper presents examples of generated questions, a more in-depth qualitative analysis comparing these questions to human-generated questions would be beneficial.  How do the generated questions *feel* compared to the human questions – are they truly natural and insightful?
* **Feature Engineering:** The features, while thoughtfully designed, require significant engineering and domain knowledge. This reduces the generality of the approach.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a truly novel and potentially transformative approach to modeling human question asking.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the model on a challenging dataset.
* The theoretical grounding and detailed model description are impressive.
* The work has the potential to impact both the active learning and cognitive science communities.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The domain specificity of the current implementation limits its immediate applicability to other problems.
* The model's complexity and reliance on importance sampling introduce potential drawbacks.
* Further qualitative analysis of the generated questions is needed to assess their human-likeness.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The core idea of modeling question asking as program generation is innovative and well-motivated, and the empirical results are promising. While the domain specificity and model complexity are legitimate concerns, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work.  

**Recommendation:**  **Accept** with minor revisions. 

I recommend the authors address the following in a revision:

*   Provide a more detailed discussion of the challenges and potential solutions for extending the model to new domains.
*   Consider exploring more robust approximation techniques for the normalizing constant.
*   Include a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the generated questions, perhaps through a human evaluation study.
*   Discuss how the observed feature weights can inform our understanding of human questioning strategies.
