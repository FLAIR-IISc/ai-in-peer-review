### Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

#### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking. The central thesis is that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed on a world state, return an answer. This recasts the problem of deciding what question to ask as a problem of probabilistic program synthesis. The authors develop a model built on a compositional grammar that defines a space of possible questions (programs). A log-linear model is used to define a probability distribution over this space, with features capturing a question's informativeness (Expected Information Gain), its complexity (program length), and its answer type (e.g., Boolean, number). The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The results demonstrate that the model can successfully predict the frequency distribution of human questions in held-out contexts and, compellingly, can generate novel, contextually appropriate, and human-like questions that were not in the original dataset. This work provides a bridge between active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science, offering a new perspective on building machines that can learn by asking rich, structured questions.

#### Strengths
1.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Core Idea:** The formulation of question asking as probabilistic program generation is highly original and powerful. It moves beyond the simple label queries of traditional active learning and the stereotyped outputs of many VQG systems. It provides a natural and elegant way to account for key properties of human inquiry: compositionality (building complex questions from simpler concepts), generativity (creating entirely new questions), and goal-directedness (selecting questions that are useful in a specific context). This is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Strong Experimental Validation and Ablation Study:** The paper’s claims are well-supported by a rigorous experimental evaluation. The leave-one-out cross-validation setup is appropriate for the dataset size. Crucially, the ablation study provides deep insight into the factors driving human question asking. The finding that the `complexity-agnostic` model performs extremely poorly (negative correlation with human data) is a key result, demonstrating that people do not simply maximize information gain but trade it off against question simplicity. This empirically validates a central hypothesis of the paper and provides a valuable insight for the field.

3.  **Compelling Qualitative Results:** The ability to generate novel, non-trivial questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?", "Are blue and purple ships touching and red and purple not touching?") is a very strong demonstration of the model's expressive power. This goes beyond just fitting a distribution over existing data and shows true generative capability. These examples effectively showcase the model's capacity for "creativity" within the constrained domain, which is a major step forward from systems that can only select from a pre-defined set of questions.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the model is described logically from its core principles to its implementation details, and the results are presented in a clear and convincing manner. The authors are also honest and upfront about the limitations of their work, which strengthens the paper's scientific quality.

#### Weaknesses
1.  **Scalability and Generalizability:** The primary limitation is the reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar. While the authors correctly point out that many components are domain-general (logic, arithmetic, set operations), the semantic primitives (`size`, `Blue`, `Red`, etc.) are tied to the Battleship game. The effort required to port this framework to a new, more complex domain is unclear and potentially substantial. This raises questions about the scalability of the approach as a general-purpose method for question asking in AI systems. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of what a principled process for developing such grammars for new domains might look like.

2.  **Bypassing Natural Language Processing:** The model operates on semantic representations (programs) and bypasses the challenging problem of parsing natural language questions into these forms (or generating language from them). While this is a reasonable simplification to isolate the core problem of question generation, it makes the system an incomplete model of human question asking and limits its immediate practical applicability. While the authors cite work on semantic parsing (Wang et al., 2015), a brief discussion of how their system might be integrated with such a front-end would strengthen the paper's connection to the broader NLP and AI communities.

3.  **Inference Approximation:** The model relies on importance sampling to approximate the partition function and the model expectations during training. The proposal distribution is a simple uniform PCFG. It is possible that this proposal distribution is not effective at exploring the space of high-utility but structurally complex programs, potentially leading to inaccurate gradient estimates and a biased model. While this is a common challenge in such models, a brief analysis of the variance of the importance sampling estimator or a discussion of alternative inference strategies could add to the technical depth.

#### Summary and Recommendation

This paper presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the study of active learning and goal-directed inquiry. The central idea of modeling question asking as program generation is a powerful abstraction that elegantly captures the compositional and generative nature of human questions. The experimental results, particularly the ablation studies and the generation of novel questions, provide strong support for the authors' claims. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and provides valuable insights into the trade-off between informativeness and complexity in human cognition. While the reliance on a domain-specific grammar limits its immediate generalizability, the work lays a strong conceptual and methodological foundation for future research. It is highly likely to inspire other researchers and open up new avenues in active learning, program synthesis, and human-computer interaction.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Originality:** Proposes a novel and elegant framework for a challenging problem.
*   **Significance:** Represents a significant step beyond standard active learning and provides a computational account of a key aspect of human intelligence. The insights about the complexity-informativeness trade-off are broadly relevant.
*   **Quality:** The model is well-formulated, and the experimental validation is rigorous and insightful.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar raises concerns about the scalability and general applicability of the approach.
*   The work sidesteps the natural language interface, which is a critical component of a complete question-asking system.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a foundational idea with the potential for high impact.

**Recommendation: Accept**
