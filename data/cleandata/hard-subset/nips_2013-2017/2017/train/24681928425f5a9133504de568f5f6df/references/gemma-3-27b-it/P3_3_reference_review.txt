## NIPS 2017 Review: Question Asking as Program Generation

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The central idea is that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed on a state representing a “possible world,” yield an answer. This reframes question asking as a program synthesis problem. The authors demonstrate their approach on a dataset of natural language questions collected during a "Battleship"-like game, showing that their model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of active learning, noting the limitations of existing methods that primarily focus on simple label requests. It contrasts its approach with AI dialog systems, pointing out their reliance on pre-defined question sets and lack of genuine flexibility. The authors also acknowledge related work in visual question generation (VQG) but emphasize the advantage of their approach in handling novel scenarios without extensive training data.  The connection to formal linguistic approaches like partitioning possible worlds and the “Language of Thought” hypothesis are well highlighted. They appropriately differentiate themselves from work requiring large paired image-question datasets.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Conceptual Insight:** The core idea of representing questions as programs is a significant conceptual leap. It provides a powerful formalism for understanding and modeling the compositional and computational aspects of human questioning.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the Battleship dataset demonstrate that the model can successfully predict human question frequencies. This is a non-trivial achievement given the complexity and open-endedness of the task.
* **Generative Capabilities:** The ability to synthesize novel, human-like questions represents a significant step toward more intelligent and flexible question-asking agents. The examples provided (Figure 3) are compelling.
* **Well-Defined Probabilistic Model:** The use of a probabilistic model with feature engineering provides a principled approach for learning from the data and making predictions. The consideration of informativeness (EIG) *and* complexity is particularly insightful.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology.  The motivation is effectively presented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity of Grammar:** While the authors acknowledge this, the grammar is currently tailored to the Battleship domain.  A key challenge for the field is generalizing this approach to more complex and diverse domains, and the extent to which the current grammar facilitates this is unclear.  The exclusion of questions requiring a “draw” operation highlights this limitation.
* **Computational Complexity:** The importance sampling used to approximate the normalizing constant introduces computational challenges.  While acknowledged, the scalability of this approach to larger, more complex domains needs further investigation.
* **Limited Scope of “Human-Likeness” Evaluation:** The evaluation of generated questions relies primarily on qualitative assessment (demonstrated through examples).  A more rigorous quantitative evaluation of "human-likeness" would strengthen the claims. Metrics beyond just frequency prediction would be useful.
* **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The performance heavily relies on carefully engineered features.  While justified and effective, this raises questions about the model’s ability to learn directly from raw data without such domain-specific knowledge. It would be interesting to see an ablation study showing the contribution of each feature.
* **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While the paper compares to variants of their own model, a comparison to simpler baselines (e.g., a purely frequency-based model, or a model that only considers EIG) would provide a clearer understanding of the value added by the proposed framework.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and insightful approach to modeling question asking.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the viability of the proposed framework.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the fields of active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive science.
* The writing quality is high and the presentation is clear.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The domain specificity of the current implementation may limit its broader applicability.
* The computational complexity could pose challenges for scaling the approach.
* The evaluation of generated questions is somewhat subjective.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The idea of modeling questions as programs is clever and has the potential to unlock new avenues for research in active learning and AI. While there are limitations, the authors acknowledge them and the empirical results are compelling. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

*   **Discuss Generalization:** Elaborate on the potential for generalizing the grammar and approach to other domains.
*   **Scalability Analysis:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the importance sampling method.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation of Generation:**  Explore metrics for evaluating the "human-likeness" of generated questions beyond frequency prediction.
* **Baseline Comparison:** Include a comparison to simpler baseline models.
* **Ablation Study:** Perform an ablation study to assess the importance of each feature.
