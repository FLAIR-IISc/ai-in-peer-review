

# NIPS 2017 Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs that operate on possible world states. The authors argue that humans ask rich, context-sensitive questions that go beyond simple label queries, and that modeling questions as programs (using a grammar based on lambda calculus) captures the compositionality and computability of human question generation. They evaluate their model using a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like information search game, where players had to identify ship configurations on a grid. The probabilistic model incorporates features for informativeness (measured by Expected Information Gain), complexity (log probability under a grammar), answer type (Boolean, Number, Color, Location), and relevance (whether the question refers to the game board). The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and can generate novel human-like questions not present in the training data.

The paper makes a clear contribution by bridging insights from linguistics (compositionality), cognitive science (language of thought), and machine learning (program synthesis) to create a model of human question asking that goes beyond existing active learning approaches and neural question generation systems.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Conceptual Framework**: The core idea of modeling questions as programs is highly original and provides a compelling theoretical lens for understanding human question asking. This connects well with cognitive science (language of thought) and computational linguistics (compositionality), which is a significant advance over previous work that treated questions as simple label requests or used neural networks without compositional understanding.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper carefully grounds its approach in established concepts from linguistics (compositionality), cognitive science (language of thought), and information theory (Expected Information Gain), demonstrating a deep understanding of the interdisciplinary nature of the problem.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors conduct thorough ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each feature in their model (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance). The quantitative evaluation using log-likelihood on held-out contexts and the qualitative generation of novel questions provide strong evidence for the model's effectiveness.

4. **Demonstrated Productivity**: The paper successfully shows that the grammar-based approach can generate novel questions not present in the training data, addressing a key challenge in modeling human question asking behavior (the productivity of language).

5. **Clear Distinction from Related Work**: The authors provide an excellent analysis of related work, clearly differentiating their approach from existing active learning algorithms, goal-directed dialog systems, and neural question generation methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Domain Specificity**: The most significant limitation is the heavy reliance on domain-specific knowledge for the grammar and features. The grammar is tailored to the Battleship game, with functions like "size" and "color" specific to that domain. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't sufficiently address how much of the grammar would need to be re-engineered for new domains. The claim that "many aspects of our grammar are domain general" (e.g., logical connectives, set operations) is somewhat overstated - these are general constructs, but the specific application to the game requires substantial domain knowledge.

2. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The paper only evaluates on one specific game (Battleship-like), which limits the generalizability of the findings. It would be valuable to see how the model performs on a different domain (e.g., a different information search game) to demonstrate broader applicability.

3. **Natural Language Gap**: The model operates on semantic representations rather than directly on natural language, which is a significant limitation for practical applications. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't provide a clear path forward for bridging this gap (beyond a brief mention of "recent tools in computational linguistics"). This is particularly important since the paper's title emphasizes "question asking" which implies natural language generation.

4. **Computational Complexity**: The optimization procedure is computationally intensive (100,000 gradient ascent iterations with 150,000 samples per iteration), which may limit scalability for more complex domains or larger datasets.

5. **Sparse Data Challenge**: The human question data is relatively sparse (many questions were only asked once), which makes the correlation analysis less reliable as a measure of fit. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide a thorough analysis of how sparsity affects their model's performance.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Domain Generalization Analysis**: Add a brief analysis or experiment demonstrating how much of the grammar would need to be changed for a different domain. For example, could the same grammar structure be applied to a different game with minimal changes? This would strengthen the argument about domain generality.

2. **Natural Language Generation Component**: Consider adding a brief discussion or small experiment on how to bridge the semantic representation to natural language. The paper mentions this as a future direction but doesn't provide any concrete steps.

3. **Comparison to Alternative Methods**: While the paper compares model variants, it would be valuable to include a comparison to a simple neural language model trained on the same dataset (e.g., an LSTM) to demonstrate the advantages of the program-based approach over purely data-driven methods.

4. **More Detailed Analysis of Novel Questions**: The paper shows some novel questions generated by the model, but a more detailed analysis of why these questions are considered "clever" or "useful" would strengthen the qualitative evaluation.

5. **Clarify the Role of Domain Knowledge**: The paper acknowledges that "some aspects of our grammar are specific to the Battleship domain," but it's not clear how much of the grammar is domain-specific versus domain-general. A more detailed breakdown of which grammar rules are domain-specific would be helpful.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to understanding human question asking from a computational perspective. The core idea of modeling questions as programs is novel, well-motivated, and grounded in established theories from multiple disciplines. The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides convincing evidence for the model's effectiveness in predicting human question behavior and generating novel questions.

The main limitations relate to domain specificity and the gap between semantic representations and natural language, which are important for practical applications. However, these are reasonable limitations for a first step in this direction, and the authors acknowledge them appropriately.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. It addresses an important gap in active learning literature by moving beyond simple label queries to model richer human-like question asking. The framework has significant potential for future work in human-computer interaction, educational technology, and AI systems that need to interact with humans more naturally.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. It represents a meaningful advance in modeling human question asking behavior with a novel theoretical framework that connects multiple disciplines. While there are limitations regarding domain specificity and the natural language gap, these are acknowledged by the authors and represent natural directions for future work rather than flaws in the current contribution. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field that aligns well with NIPS' focus on machine learning and cognitive science.

The paper's strength lies in its ability to bridge cognitive science, linguistics, and machine learning to create a model that not only predicts human question asking but can also generate novel questions, demonstrating a level of creativity and productivity that is rare in current AI systems. This is exactly the kind of interdisciplinary work that NIPS aims to highlight.
