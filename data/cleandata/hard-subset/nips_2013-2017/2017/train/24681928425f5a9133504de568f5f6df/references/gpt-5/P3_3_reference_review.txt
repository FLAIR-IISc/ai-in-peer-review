Summary
The paper proposes modeling question asking as program generation: each question is a typed, executable program over possible world states. A PCFG-like grammar (LISP/lambda-calculus style with sets, map, logical/comparison ops) defines the compositional hypothesis space. A log-linear (energy-based) model over programs uses features for informativeness (Expected Information Gain under a Bayesian ideal observer over Battleship boards), complexity (−log PCFG probability), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and a relevance filter. Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human questions using importance sampling to approximate the partition function and feature expectations. On a dataset of 605 human-authored questions across 18 Battleship contexts, the full model achieves the best held-out log-likelihood versus ablations (removing EIG, complexity, or type features), and correlates with human frequencies (avg Spearman 0.64). It also samples novel, seemingly human-like questions.

Relation to prior work
The work bridges active learning/Bayesian experimental design (EIG), probabilistic languages of thought, and program-based semantic parsing/visual reasoning (e.g., executable programs for CLEVR). It contrasts with neural VQG and goal-directed dialog systems that lack explicit reasoning about informativeness. It relates to pragmatic models of question asking (e.g., Rational Speech Acts) that weigh informativity and cost.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation: questions as executable, typed programs captures compositionality and precise semantics, enabling evaluation via partitions over hypotheses.
- Integrates informativeness with cost/complexity, providing a plausible account of why humans do not simply maximize EIG.
- Grammar is expressive (lambda/mapping/sets) yet constrained enough to enable learning and search; covers the dataset.
- Empirical evaluation includes LOOCV across contexts and ablations showing the importance of complexity (removing it hurts substantially) and contributions from EIG and answer types.
- Novel question generation with equivalence checking by answer partitions shows genuine productivity rather than template reuse.
- The work is careful about limitations (domain specificity, natural language interface) and positions itself as a step toward richer active learning.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical
- Scalability: EIG relies on enumerating large hypothesis spaces; two contexts were dropped and stochastic draw rules removed. The approach may struggle in domains with larger or continuous state spaces without approximate Bayesian design (e.g., sampling, amortization).
- Learning/inference: The likelihood is approximated with importance sampling from a uniform PCFG proposal. It appears a fixed set of 150k programs is reused for 100k gradient steps. This risks bias/overfitting to the proposal pool; convergence diagnostics, variance estimates, and sensitivity to pool size are not reported. Computational cost reporting is limited.
- Baselines: Missing several strong and informative baselines: (a) grammar-only (PCFG prior) without features, (b) EIG-only with a simple length penalty, (c) nearest-neighbor/template reuse across contexts, (d) simple neural language models trained on the small dataset (authors argue data scarcity, but a lightweight seq2seq or n-gram baseline would contextualize gains).
- Metrics: While LL comparisons favor the full model, the rank correlation with human frequencies is slightly higher for the information-agnostic variant (0.65 vs 0.64). This deserves analysis: does EIG help likelihood but hurt rank ordering, or is the difference within estimation noise? Confidence intervals for correlations would help.
- Relevance feature f∅ is ad hoc; alternatively the grammar could be restricted to “board-referential” constructs. As-is, this effectively encodes domain knowledge as a feature.
- Equivalence for novelty: Treating questions differing only by arguments (e.g., (size Blue) vs (size Red)) as equivalent is debatable because informativeness is context-dependent. The MI-based equivalence threshold is not specified; results might be sensitive to it.

Clarity
- Some implementation details are deferred: exact hypothesis priors, EIG computation details for large spaces, proposal distribution probabilities, whether proposal resampling occurs across iterations, and runtime/memory. Reference formatting has inconsistencies.
- The mapping from natural language to programs is performed offline; while acknowledged, it limits reproducibility for end-to-end settings.

Originality
- The central idea—treating question generation as program synthesis evaluated by Bayesian design—is novel and well-motivated. While related to executable semantic parsers and LOT models, applying this to open-ended question asking (generation, not just interpretation) with quantitative human data is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
- The work provides a concrete, testable account of human question asking and suggests a route to more capable, goal-directed question-asking agents. However, current evaluation is limited to a single, engineered domain; significance for broader ML would be bolstered by demonstrations in additional domains or by end-to-end language interfacing.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Report computational cost, convergence diagnostics, and sensitivity to the size of the proposal pool. Do you resample proposals during training? How stable are results across random seeds?
- Add baselines: PCFG-only, EIG(+length)-only, nearest-neighbor, and a small neural baseline (even if weak) to contextualize gains and isolate the contribution of the learned features versus the grammar.
- Provide CIs or bootstrap for rank correlations; analyze why the information-agnostic model yields slightly higher rank correlations.
- Detail the MI threshold and equivalence procedure; reconsider argument-based equivalence or at least analyze its effect.
- Include a human evaluation of generated questions (usefulness/clarity ratings) and, if feasible, an online task showing that model-suggested questions improve task performance.
- Discuss or prototype approximate EIG for large H (e.g., particle approximations, amortized inference) and show how the approach scales.
- Clarify the relevance feature and consider constraining the grammar to board-referential programs instead.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant, principled framework; bridges cognitive modeling and ML.
- Solid ablation evidence that complexity matters in human-like question asking.
- Demonstrates compositional generalization and productive question generation.

Cons for acceptance
- Scalability and computational approximations insufficiently validated; dropped contexts.
- Limited domain and lack of end-to-end NL interface; manual semantic mapping.
- Missing baselines and some mixed quantitative signals (correlation).

Overall
A thoughtful and technically sound step toward modeling and generating rich, goal-directed questions via program synthesis and Bayesian design. I lean toward borderline accept, contingent on clarifying computational details and strengthening baselines; even as-is, it offers valuable ideas likely to influence work at the cognition–ML interface.
