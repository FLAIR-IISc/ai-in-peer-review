- Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs and formalizes question asking as program synthesis.  
- Introduces a probabilistic model combining informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance to generate questions.  
- Evaluated on a dataset of 605 human questions in a Battleship-like game.  
- Demonstrates the model predicts human question frequencies on held-out contexts and generates novel, human-like questions.  
- Integrates formal language theory, probabilistic modeling, and information theory coherently.  
- Provides clear mathematical formulation (Equations 1–4) and justifies feature choices.  
- Experimental evaluation includes thorough ablation studies and both quantitative (log-likelihood, Spearman 0.64) and qualitative analyses.  
- Clear organization, effective concrete examples, and informative figures and supplementary grammar details.  
- Acknowledges domain-specific limitations and discusses future directions.  
- Novelty: modeling questions as compositional programs differs from dataset-driven or canned-template approaches.  
- Potential impact on active learning, human–computer interaction, and cognitive science.  
- Weakness: evaluation restricted to a single domain, raising questions about generalizability.  
- Weakness: connection between program representations and natural language generation is underdeveloped.  
- Weakness: computational complexity (importance sampling over large grammar) not thoroughly discussed.  
- Weakness: does not address handling of ambiguous or poorly formed human questions.  
- Clarity issues: some technical details (optimization/importance sampling, set-operation handling) need clearer explanation.  
- Recommendation: accept — significant, well-executed contribution despite domain and practical limitations.  
- Suggested minor improvements: clearer discussion of program-to-NL mapping and explicit statement of computational requirements.
