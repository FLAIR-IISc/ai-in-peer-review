

# NIPS Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that can be executed on possible world states to generate answers. The authors propose that questions can be modeled as syntactically well-formed programs (using a grammar-based approach inspired by lambda calculus), and that human question selection can be captured through a probabilistic generative model that balances informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The approach is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions collected from human players in a Battleship-like information-search game, where participants asked questions to identify hidden ship configurations. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

This work builds on but significantly differs from previous approaches to question generation. Unlike neural approaches that require large datasets of question-image pairs (e.g., Visual Question Generation), or goal-directed dialog systems that rely on canned questions, this paper offers a cognitive-science-inspired model that captures the compositional nature of human question asking and can generate questions without prior examples of those specific questions. It also differs from previous work on program synthesis for question answering (e.g., [24, 12]) by focusing on the *generation* of questions rather than answering them.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their model (Section 4), including the grammar, probabilistic model, and feature design. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with both quantitative metrics (log-likelihood comparisons across model variants, Spearman's rank correlation with human question frequencies) and qualitative analysis of novel questions generated by the model. The authors appropriately conduct ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of each feature component (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance), which strengthens their claims about the model's components.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately (Section 6), noting the domain specificity of their grammar and the need for domain knowledge. The authors also provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including the grammar rules and feature definitions.

One minor technical concern: The authors note that the grammar was designed based on the full set of contexts, but had to remove contexts 1 and 2 for computational reasons (Section 4.6). This could potentially introduce a bias in the grammar, though the authors make clear that the grammar was designed to express all questions in the human data set.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly establishes the research problem and the significance of modeling rich human question asking. The related work section effectively distinguishes this work from previous approaches in active learning, dialog systems, and question generation. The technical sections (Sections 4 and 5) are particularly clear, with well-explained concepts and sufficient examples to illustrate the program representation.

The figures (particularly Figure 3 showing novel generated questions) are helpful for understanding the model's capabilities. The supplementary materials appear to provide adequate detail for replication. The paper successfully bridges cognitive science and machine learning concepts, making it accessible to both communities.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution to the field. While there has been work on program synthesis for question answering [24, 12] and on question generation for visual tasks [16, 23], this is the first work to model *human question asking* as program generation with a probabilistic model that predicts human question frequencies. The key insight—that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed, produce answers—provides a fresh perspective on the cognitive process of question asking.

The paper's approach differs fundamentally from previous work in that it focuses on the *generation* of questions rather than answering them, and it models the cognitive constraints (informativeness, complexity) that guide human question selection. The demonstration of the model's ability to generate novel questions (beyond the training data) is particularly novel and impressive.

The paper also makes a strong contribution to computational cognitive science by providing a formal model of how people balance informativeness and complexity when producing questions, which was previously not captured in predictive models.

## Significance

The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in AI: how to develop systems that can learn by asking rich, human-like questions rather than simply requesting supervised labels. This has significant implications for active learning, human-computer interaction, and cognitive modeling. The ability to generate novel, human-like questions (as demonstrated in Figure 3) represents a meaningful step toward more human-like question asking in machines.

The work provides a new framework for understanding human question asking that could inform future research in cognitive science. The approach is potentially extensible to other domains, though the authors appropriately acknowledge the current domain specificity. The paper's significance is further enhanced by its clear demonstration of how to bridge cognitive science concepts with machine learning techniques.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths
1. Novel theoretical framework that models questions as programs with clear computational semantics
2. Thorough experimental evaluation with both quantitative metrics and qualitative analysis
3. Demonstrated ability to generate novel human-like questions beyond the training data
4. Clear ablation studies showing the importance of each model component
5. Strong connection to cognitive science and computational linguistics
6. Well-written and well-organized paper with sufficient detail for reproducibility

### Weaknesses
1. Current domain specificity (Battleship game) limits immediate applicability to broader settings
2. The natural language interface is not fully developed (the model works with program representations, not natural language directly)
3. The paper doesn't fully explore how to scale this approach to more complex domains
4. The grammar includes some domain-specific elements that may require substantial re-engineering for new domains

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant and original contribution to the field of active learning and computational cognitive science. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides a compelling new perspective on human question asking. While the current domain specificity is a limitation, the authors appropriately acknowledge this and provide a clear path forward for extension to other domains. The work represents a meaningful step toward more human-like question asking in machines, which has important implications for AI development.

The paper's contribution is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers on question generation and active learning, as evidenced by the thorough related work section that carefully distinguishes this work from existing approaches. The model's ability to generate novel human-like questions (Figure 3) is particularly impressive and demonstrates a level of creativity that surpasses current neural approaches.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field that advances both the technical understanding of question asking and the development of more human-like AI systems.
