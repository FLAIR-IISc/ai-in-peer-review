

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that can be executed on possible world states. The authors model questions as syntactically well-formed programs in a simple programming language (based on lambda calculus), and propose a probabilistic generative model that uses features related to question informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model is evaluated on a dataset of human questions from a Battleship-style game, where players asked questions to identify ship configurations. The authors claim their model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel human-like questions.

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically interesting connection between question asking and program synthesis, and the compositional nature of questions is well-documented in the data. The grammar-based approach for question generation has potential for modeling the productivity of human question asking, as it can generate novel questions not present in the training data. The evaluation methodology using cross-validation and the careful design of question features are methodologically sound.

## Weaknesses

Despite the theoretical appeal, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses that prevent it from meeting NIPS standards for scientific contribution:

1. **Lack of comparison to established baselines**: The most significant weakness is the absence of comparison to existing active learning methods or question generation approaches. The paper claims "most work on active machine learning has focused on relatively simple types of information requests," but fails to benchmark against relevant methods like those in [21, 25, 1, 22, 20]. Without such comparisons, the claim that this approach represents progress toward "more human-like question asking in machines" is unsubstantiated. How does this method compare to standard active learning techniques that query for labels? How does it compare to neural question generation approaches in [16, 23, 11, 20]?

2. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The evaluation is restricted to a single game domain (Battleship), which severely limits the generalizability of the claims. The authors acknowledge "some aspects of our grammar are specific to the Battleship domain" and note the need for "substantial domain knowledge." This undermines the claim of developing a general framework for human-like question asking. For a NIPS paper, the method should be validated across multiple domains or with a more generalizable approach.

3. **Questionable novelty**: The paper's core idea of modeling questions as programs is not new. Previous work in computational linguistics (e.g., [24, 12]) and cognitive science (e.g., [17, 5, 13]) has already explored this approach. The authors cite these works but fail to clearly distinguish their contribution. The paper's novelty appears to be limited to applying this framework to a specific game domain, not to the core idea itself.

4. **Insufficient validation of "human-like" claims**: The paper claims the model can "generate novel human-like questions," but the evaluation of this claim is purely qualitative and limited to a few examples. There is no quantitative measure of how human-like the generated questions are, nor is there any user study comparing human judgments of the generated questions versus human questions. The paper lacks a proper evaluation of the "human-like" aspect.

5. **Overstated significance**: The paper makes broad claims about "a long term goal of artificial intelligence (AI) is to develop algorithms with a similar capacity to learn by asking rich questions" and that this work "represents a step toward more capable active learning machines." These claims are not substantiated by the limited scope of the evaluation. The paper does not demonstrate any meaningful improvement over existing methods for active learning or question asking.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting theoretical framework but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and significance. The lack of comparison to established baselines, limited scope of evaluation, and insufficient validation of the "human-like" claim are serious shortcomings. The method appears to be a specialized application of existing program synthesis techniques rather than a significant advancement in the field.

The paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. It does not provide evidence that this approach outperforms existing methods in active learning or question generation tasks. The evaluation is limited to a single game domain with no generalizability claims supported by empirical evidence.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the theoretical idea has some merit, the lack of proper validation against established baselines, the narrow scope of evaluation, and the overstatement of significance prevent this work from meeting NIPS standards for scientific contribution. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to include proper comparisons to relevant baselines, demonstrate generalizability across multiple domains, and provide more rigorous validation of their "human-like" claims before it could be considered for publication.
