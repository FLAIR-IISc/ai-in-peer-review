Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a computational framework for human question asking by representing questions as executable programs in a small, typed, LISP-like DSL. A PCFG defines a compositional hypothesis space over programs; a log-linear model with features for expected information gain (EIG), complexity (−log PCFG probability), answer type, and a relevance filter is trained to match human question distributions in a Battleship-like game. EIG is computed via Bayesian belief updating over a hypothesis space of board configurations conditioned on partial observations. Learning is performed with importance sampling over programs generated from the PCFG, using the samples both to approximate normalizers and gradients. The model is evaluated via leave-one-context-out prediction of human questions and qualitative synthesis of “novel” questions.

This bridges strands from active learning (beyond label queries), semantic representations/programs in QA and VQA, Bayesian cognitive models of inquiry, and program induction. Compared to prior QA-as-program work (e.g., semantic parsing/executing questions), the novelty lies in generating questions (not answers) to match human distributions, and in combining EIG with compositional representations. It is close in spirit to rational/pragmatic models of question asking in cognitive science (e.g., Hawkins et al.), adding a concrete, executable grammar and a parametric mixture of utility and complexity.

Strengths
- Clear, compositional formulation: modeling questions as executable programs is principled and affords precise semantics, computability, and generativity.
- Interpretable objectives: the mixture of information gain and complexity provides a plausible cognitive account and yields a measurable trade-off.
- Empirical signal: the model predicts human question frequencies better than ablated variants; the critical role of complexity is convincingly shown (complexity-agnostic variant fails badly).
- Productivity: the grammar captures unseen but human-like questions across contexts, and the sampling procedure can synthesize novel programs with nontrivial structure.
- Relates cognitive modeling and ML: casts question asking as program synthesis with probabilistic modeling and approximate inference.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Heavy handcrafting and domain specificity: The DSL and grammar are engineered to cover all questions in this specific task; mapping NL to programs is manual and ambiguous items were excluded. This may limit generality and inflates apparent coverage. There is no test in a second domain.
- Role of EIG is unclear: Despite the narrative emphasis on informativeness, the information-agnostic model achieves similar (even slightly higher) correlation (0.65 vs. 0.64). The bootstrap advantage is modest (81%). This weakens the claim that EIG is central to human-like question generation, beyond what complexity and type priors already capture.
- Approximate inference details: Importance sampling over a very large program space (150k samples) with a uniform PCFG as proposal raises variance concerns. There are no reports of effective sample sizes, variance of Z estimates, or convergence diagnostics over 100k gradient steps. No regularization is described, and computational cost/runtime are omitted. Dropping two contexts and 14 questions due to intractability further underscores scalability issues.
- Evaluation gaps: The “novel question” assessment is purely qualitative with no human judgments of usefulness/naturalness. Baselines are limited to ablations; there is no comparison to retrieval from similar contexts, simple heuristics (e.g., shortest EIG-optimal queries under beam search), or learning PCFG rule probabilities from data. The claim that the grammar accounts for productivity could be strengthened by such baselines.
- Utility misspecification: Utility is equated to EIG with a global complexity penalty and coarse answer-type priors. Human question costs include answerability, cognitive load for the respondent, and pragmatic coordination, which are not modeled beyond the complexity term. The “relevance filter” feature appears ad hoc.
- Reproducibility: Crucial implementation details are deferred to supplements or prior work (exact grammar, hypothesis enumeration per context, mapping procedure and inter-annotator agreement, equivalence detection between programs). For a NeurIPS audience, more is needed to reproduce results.

Quality
Technically sound overall, but the statistical estimation/inference section needs tightening: report variance/ESS, sensitivity to sample size and max program length, and regularization. The decision to drop contexts/questions biases evaluation and should be quantified. Claims about informativeness need more careful support.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. However, the paper relies heavily on supplemental materials and previous work for key details. Including the core grammar, a schematic of EIG computation conditioned on observed tiles, and concrete program-NL mappings would improve clarity and reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. Representing questions as programs is known in QA, and EIG for question selection is standard in active learning and cognitive models. The contribution is the integration for open-ended question generation with human data and a compositional grammar. The emphasis on complexity costs is a useful addition.

Significance
Promising but limited by domain specificity and evaluation scope. The ideas could inspire richer active learning/querying mechanisms and cognitive models, but evidence of broader applicability and stronger baselines would increase impact.

Reasons to accept
- Elegant, compositional framework that can synthesize nontrivial queries.
- Empirical evidence that complexity constraints are crucial for modeling human question choices.
- Bridges cognitive modeling and ML with executable semantics and probabilistic learning.

Reasons to reject
- Limited and domain-specific evaluation; hand-engineered grammar and excluded items undermine generality.
- Weak empirical support for the central role of EIG; ablations suggest complexity and type priors dominate.
- Insufficient details and diagnostics for approximate inference and optimization; scalability concerns.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report ESS/variance of importance sampling, runtime, sensitivity to sample size, and regularization. Include convergence diagnostics.
- Provide inter-annotator agreement and guidelines for NL-to-program mapping; analyze robustness to alternative parses.
- Add baselines: nearest-neighbor retrieval across contexts; beam search maximizing EIG with MDL penalty; learning PCFG rule probabilities from data; a simple neural sequence model over programs (even with limited data).
- Obtain human evaluations of novelty/usefulness for sampled questions.
- Demonstrate portability to a second domain or an expanded hypothesis space; clarify how much domain knowledge is reusable.
- Analyze the marginal contribution of EIG per question (partial correlations) and when/why it helps or hurts.

Overall: Interesting and thought-provoking, but currently borderline due to evaluation and generality limitations.
