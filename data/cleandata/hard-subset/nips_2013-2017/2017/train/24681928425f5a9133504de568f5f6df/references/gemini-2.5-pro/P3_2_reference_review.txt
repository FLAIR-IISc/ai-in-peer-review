### Paper Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel framework for modeling goal-directed, context-sensitive human question asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. When executed on a possible world state (e.g., a "Battleship" game board configuration), these programs return an answer. The problem of deciding which question to ask is thus recast as generating the most "useful" program from a vast space defined by a grammar. The authors propose a log-linear model to score candidate programs, where the score (or energy) is a weighted combination of several features: Expected Information Gain (EIG), program complexity, answer type, and relevance to the game. By fitting the feature weights to a dataset of human questions from a "Battleship"-style game, the model learns to predict the distribution of human-asked questions in new contexts. The work is positioned as a significant step beyond traditional active learning, which typically uses simple label queries, and data-driven dialogue systems, which often lack goal-directedness and compositional creativity.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The central contribution—modeling rich, open-ended question asking as probabilistic program synthesis—is highly original and significant. It provides a principled, computational framework that bridges ideas from active learning (EIG), cognitive science (compositionality, "language of thought"), and programming languages. This approach elegantly formalizes the intuitive trade-offs humans make when asking questions and has the potential to inspire a new direction in active learning and human-machine interaction, moving beyond simple queries toward more collaborative, human-like information seeking.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The probabilistic model is well-conceived and technically sound. The choice of a log-linear model combining features for informativeness (EIG), complexity (a penalty from the PCFG), and human biases (answer type) is a powerful and interpretable way to capture the competing constraints on question generation. The use of a lesion study, comparing the full model against ablated versions (complexity-agnostic, information-agnostic, etc.), provides strong evidence for the necessity of each component. In particular, the finding that complexity is a critical factor, and that EIG alone is a poor predictor of human questions, is a key insight.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a compelling evaluation on a challenging dataset. The model not only achieves a solid quantitative fit to human question frequencies in held-out contexts (Spearman's ρ = 0.64) but also excels in its qualitative, generative capabilities. The examples of novel, human-like questions generated by the model (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") are particularly impressive, demonstrating that the framework captures the productive and creative nature of human inquiry rather than simply memorizing patterns from the training set.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The "Language of Thought" Assumption / Sidestepping NLU:** The model operates entirely on semantic representations (the LISP-like programs), bypassing the difficult problem of parsing natural language questions into these forms. The dataset questions were manually translated into the formal language. While this is a reasonable simplification for an initial investigation, it is a major limitation for building a practical system. The title "Question Asking as Program Generation" might be slightly overstated, as it models the generation of question *semantics*, not the surface-form questions themselves. A more thorough discussion of how this framework could be integrated with modern semantic parsing techniques (e.g., [24] in their own references) would strengthen the paper and clarify the path toward a complete system.

2.  **Domain Specificity and Scalability:** The grammar, which defines the space of all possible questions, is hand-crafted for the Battleship domain. While the authors correctly note that many components (logical operators, arithmetic) are domain-general, a significant portion of the primitives (`size`, `color`, references to ships) are highly specific. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but the amount of expert knowledge and engineering required to adapt this framework to a new domain remains a critical open question. For the NIPS audience, where generalizability is highly valued, this is a notable weakness. A discussion of potential avenues for learning or bootstrapping parts of the grammar would be beneficial.

3.  **Inference and Computational Cost:** The model relies on importance sampling over a large set of 150,000 generated programs to approximate the partition function and gradients for training. While this is a standard technique for intractable models, the quality of this approximation in such a vast combinatorial space is not fully analyzed. Furthermore, computing EIG for each sampled question requires iterating over the entire space of possible hypotheses, which was computationally prohibitive for two of the game contexts. This suggests that the approach, in its current form, may be limited to domains with relatively small and enumerable hypothesis spaces.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and elegant conceptual framework for a fundamental AI problem.
*   The synthesis of ideas from active learning, program induction, and cognitive science is a significant contribution.
*   The methodology is sound, and the experimental results, particularly the generation of novel questions, are compelling and strongly support the paper's claims.
*   The work opens up numerous avenues for future research in more sophisticated active learning and goal-oriented dialogue.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar limits the immediate generalizability of the approach.
*   The model sidesteps the natural language understanding component, which is a critical part of the overall question-asking process.
*   Computational costs associated with EIG calculation and inference over the program space may limit scalability.

**Conclusion**

Despite the limitations regarding domain specificity and the lack of a natural language front-end, this paper represents a high-quality, original, and thought-provoking contribution. The core idea of framing question asking as program generation is powerful and well-executed. The authors are honest about the limitations, and the work serves as an excellent proof-of-concept that successfully models complex human behavior and provides a clear roadmap for developing more intelligent active learning agents. The insights generated—particularly the crucial balance between informativeness and complexity—are significant for both machine learning and cognitive science. This paper is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to stimulate valuable discussion and future research. I recommend acceptance.
