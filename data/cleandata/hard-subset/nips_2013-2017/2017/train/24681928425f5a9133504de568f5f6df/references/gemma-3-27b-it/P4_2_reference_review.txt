This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, yield an answer. This recasts question asking as a program synthesis problem, leveraging compositionality and computability. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions asked by humans in a game context, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper builds on a significant body of work in active learning (e.g., [21]), dialog systems ([25, 1]), and visual question generation ([16, 22, 20]). It distinguishes itself by moving beyond simple label requests and aiming for a more expressive, program-based representation of questions. Related to this, the work acknowledges and contrasts itself with recent advances in neural question generation, arguing that their approach better captures the intentional, goal-directed nature of human inquiry. The paper also cites relevant work in linguistics and cognitive science on compositionality ([10], [4, 15, 14]) and formalizing question meaning ([6, 18, 9]).

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framing of question asking as program generation is a conceptually novel and potentially powerful approach. It offers a way to integrate ideas from program synthesis and cognitive science into the study of human inquiry.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The authors present a well-defined probabilistic model with a grammar for generating questions and a method for estimating informativeness based on Bayesian belief updating and Expected Information Gain.  The optimization procedure using importance sampling appears reasonable, given the intractability of exact computation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the human question dataset demonstrate that the model can predict question frequencies and generate plausible novel questions. The quantitative results (Table 1) suggest the full model outperforms simpler variants.
*   **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and methodology. The example questions and the explanation of the grammar are particularly helpful.
*   **Connection to Cognitive Science:** The explicit links to cognitive science concepts like compositionality and the language of thought ([3]) add depth and motivation to the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge it, the reliance on the Battleship domain introduces potential limitations. The grammar, although intended to be general, is heavily tailored to this specific task.  The transferability of the model to other domains remains an open question.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The importance sampling method, while necessary, introduces approximation errors and computational cost. The authors mention scaling limitations (e.g., dropping contexts 1 and 2 due to large hypothesis spaces).
*   **Feature Engineering:** The model relies on hand-engineered features, including the informativeness measures. While justified, this approach could benefit from more automated feature learning.
*   **Qualitative Evaluation:**  The qualitative evaluation of generated questions (Figure 3) is limited. A more systematic evaluation with human judges would strengthen the claims about the “human-likeness” of the generated questions.
*   **Limited Baselines:** While ablation studies are presented, the comparison to alternative approaches is somewhat limited. Exploring other generative models or alternative representations of questions would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated framework for modeling human question asking.
*   The technical approach is sound and the empirical results are promising.
*   The work has the potential to contribute to both machine learning and cognitive science.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The domain specificity of the approach raises concerns about its generalizability.
*   The reliance on hand-engineered features and the computational complexity of the model could limit its scalability.
*   The qualitative evaluation of generated questions is somewhat weak.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially significant contribution. While the domain specificity and computational complexity are concerns, the authors have presented a compelling case for their approach. The clear writing, thorough analysis, and connection to relevant research areas make this a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance.
