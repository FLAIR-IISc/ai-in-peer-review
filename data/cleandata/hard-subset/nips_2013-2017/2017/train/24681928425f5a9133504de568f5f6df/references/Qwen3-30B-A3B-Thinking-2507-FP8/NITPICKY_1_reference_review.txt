

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes modeling human question asking as program generation, where questions are represented as programs that, when executed on a world state, produce an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic model based on a context-free grammar for question programs, incorporating features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. They evaluate the model on a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship-like game, showing that their full model outperforms variants that omit key features.

## Strengths
The paper makes a compelling case for modeling questions as programs, which aligns with cognitive science perspectives on compositionality. The grammar-based approach effectively captures the productivity of human question asking, allowing for novel questions not present in training data. The evaluation design using leave-one-out cross-validation is appropriate for the task, and the demonstration of novel, human-like questions is impressive. The paper also thoughtfully addresses the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding domain specificity.

## Weaknesses

**1. Technical inconsistencies and errors:**
The paper contains several technical errors that undermine its credibility. The energy function is incorrectly defined as "p(x;θ) = exp(−E(x))∑ x∈X exp(−E(x))" (Section 4.3), when it should be "p(x;θ) = exp(−E(x))/Z" where Z = ∑_{x∈X} exp(−E(x)). This is a fundamental error in the model specification that would affect all subsequent results.

**2. Arbitrary modeling choices:**
The paper arbitrarily limits question length to "100 or fewer functions" (Section 4.5) without justification. This appears to be a computational convenience rather than a theoretically grounded constraint, potentially biasing the results. Similarly, the removal of the "draw" rule from the grammar (Section 4.4) is mentioned without explaining how this affects the model's ability to generate questions that would be natural to humans.

**3. Insufficient methodological details:**
The paper claims "the full model's log-likelihood advantage over the complexity-agnostic model held in 100% of the bootstrap samples" but fails to provide the bootstrap procedure details. Without knowing the number of bootstrap samples or the confidence intervals, this claim is unverifiable. The paper also does not describe how the model parameters were initialized or why gradient ascent with 100,000 iterations at a learning rate of 0.1 was appropriate.

**4. Inadequate evaluation of novelty:**
The paper claims "the model is capable of asking novel (and clever) human-like questions" but provides no rigorous evaluation of this claim. The examples in Figure 3 are presented as novel, but the paper does not demonstrate how these questions were determined to be novel (beyond a simple string match) or how they compare to human questions in terms of quality. The paper also fails to provide a statistical test for the significance of the Spearman's correlation (0.64) between predicted energy and question frequencies.

**5. Overstated novelty claims:**
The paper claims to present "the first predictive model to date of open-ended human question asking," but this is inaccurate. The Visual Question Generation (VQG) work [16] and recent neural approaches to question generation [23, 11] already provide predictive models for question generation, even if they are not specifically for goal-directed question asking. The paper also fails to adequately differentiate its approach from program synthesis for question answering [24, 12].

**6. Formatting and consistency issues:**
The paper contains several formatting inconsistencies. The reference "[see 21, for a review]" (Introduction) should be "[21] for a review." The phrase "st Conference" (Abstract) should be "The Conference." The paper references "Figure 1B and 1C" and "Figure 3" but these figures are not provided in the text. The paper also mentions "Table SI-1" and "Table SI-2" without providing these tables.

**7. Misrepresentation of features:**
The paper states "The remaining features described below are independent of the context" (Section 4.5), but the Expected Information Gain (EIG) feature is explicitly dependent on the game context, creating a contradiction in the paper's own description.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents an interesting approach to modeling question asking, the technical errors, arbitrary modeling choices, insufficient methodological details, and overstated novelty claims significantly undermine its contribution. The fundamental error in the energy function definition is particularly concerning, as it would invalidate all reported results. The paper also fails to adequately differentiate itself from existing work in program synthesis and question generation.

The paper's strengths in modeling compositionality and demonstrating novel question generation are overshadowed by these technical flaws and methodological weaknesses. Given the stringent standards of NIPS, these issues are too significant to overlook. The paper would need substantial revision to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication.

**Recommendation: Reject**
