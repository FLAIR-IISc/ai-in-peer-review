

# Peer Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs that can be evaluated on possible world states. The authors argue that humans ask rich, context-sensitive questions that go beyond simple label requests in active learning, and they model this using program synthesis. They collect a dataset of 605 natural language questions from 40 human participants playing a Battleship-like game, where players had to identify hidden ship configurations by asking questions. The authors develop a probabilistic model that generates questions by treating them as programs in a simple programming language, incorporating features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

The work connects to several relevant areas: program synthesis (e.g., Lake et al., 2015), Bayesian active learning (e.g., Settles, 2010), and cognitive science approaches to language (e.g., Fodor, 1975; Marcus, 2001). It makes a clear distinction from previous question generation work, which typically uses neural networks trained on large datasets (e.g., VQG) or limited sets of canned questions in goal-directed dialog systems.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-structured probabilistic model. The use of Expected Information Gain (EIG) to measure question informativeness is grounded in Bayesian reasoning, and the model's ability to incorporate multiple factors (informativeness, complexity, answer type) provides a more complete account of human question asking than previous work. The evaluation is thorough, with both quantitative (log-likelihood comparisons across model variants) and qualitative (novel question generation) assessments. The authors carefully acknowledge limitations, particularly regarding domain specificity and the need for domain knowledge.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant limitation is the domain-specific nature of the grammar and features. While the authors acknowledge this, the grammar is tightly coupled to the Battleship game mechanics (e.g., ship sizes, colors, orientations), making it unclear how much would need to be re-engineered for new domains. The paper doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how much domain knowledge is required to adapt the framework to new contexts. The model's reliance on EIG requires a well-defined Bayesian model of the world, which might not be feasible for complex real-world domains with uncertain or incomplete knowledge.

Additionally, the paper's claim that "people can ask intelligent questions in a novel scenario with no (or very limited) practice" (Section 2) is not sufficiently supported by the data. The dataset comes from participants who had extensive instructions and practice rounds, which likely provided them with domain-specific knowledge that would be unavailable in truly novel scenarios.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear sections that build logically from motivation to model to evaluation. The use of examples to illustrate the program representation (e.g., "(size Blue)" for "How long is the blue ship?") makes the core idea accessible. The supplementary materials provide additional detail on the grammar and features, enhancing reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**
The grammar details (Section 4.2) are somewhat dense and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how the rewrite rules generate meaningful questions. The connection between the program representation and natural language is not fully explained, making it difficult to see how this approach would interface with real-world natural language processing systems.

The description of the EIG calculation (Section 4.5) could be more precise. The paper states "the agent must identify a single hypothesis h in the space of possible configurations H," but it doesn't clarify how the hypothesis space is defined or how the prior is established. For readers unfamiliar with Bayesian active learning, this could be a stumbling block.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
The core idea of modeling questions as executable programs is highly original and represents a significant conceptual advance over previous approaches to question generation. The paper makes a clear distinction from prior work, particularly from neural network-based approaches (e.g., VQG) and goal-directed dialog systems that use canned questions. The use of compositionality to explain the productivity of human question asking (the ability to generate novel questions not seen before) is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses:**
While the program representation is novel, the probabilistic model itself (log-linear with features) is somewhat standard in the machine learning literature. The novelty is more in the choice of features and the domain-specific adaptation of the model to question asking rather than the modeling framework itself.

The paper could better distinguish itself from recent work on program induction for language understanding (e.g., Lake et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2015). While it references these works, it doesn't explicitly explain how this approach differs from or builds upon them in terms of the specific problem of question asking.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses a fundamental aspect of human learning—question asking—that has been largely overlooked in machine learning. By providing the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking, it opens up new possibilities for developing more human-like AI systems that can learn through questioning. The ability to generate novel, human-like questions (Figure 3) demonstrates the model's creative potential, which could be valuable for applications in education, human-AI collaboration, and more natural interactive learning systems.

**Weaknesses:**
The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of the dataset (Battleship-like game) and the domain-specific nature of the model. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper doesn't sufficiently address how to overcome these limitations for broader applicability. The claim that "the model can interface with recent tools in computational linguistics" (Section 6) is vague and lacks concrete examples or references to specific approaches.

The paper doesn't provide a clear roadmap for how this work could be extended to more complex, real-world domains where question asking might be more challenging (e.g., medical diagnosis, scientific discovery).

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the modeling of human question asking. The core idea of representing questions as executable programs is elegant, well-motivated, and represents a meaningful advancement over previous work. The empirical evaluation is thorough, and the model's ability to generate novel, human-like questions is impressive.

However, the domain-specific nature of the grammar and the need for substantial domain knowledge to define the model are significant limitations that need to be addressed in future work. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of the generalizability of the approach and a clearer path to extending it to broader domains.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, and the limitations it identifies are appropriately acknowledged. The minor revisions I'd suggest are:

1. Expand the discussion of domain generalization in Section 6 to better address how much domain knowledge would be required for new domains and what aspects of the grammar could be preserved across domains.

2. Clarify the connection between the program representation and natural language processing in the main text (not just in the supplementary materials).

3. Provide a more detailed explanation of the Bayesian model used for EIG calculation, particularly how the hypothesis space is defined and the prior established.

The paper's core contribution is original, well-executed, and significant for the field of cognitive science and human-AI interaction, making it a strong candidate for NeurIPS.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel conceptual framework for modeling human question asking as program generation
- Strong empirical validation showing the model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel questions
- Clear connection to cognitive science and human learning
- Thorough evaluation with multiple model variants and careful consideration of limitations

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Domain-specific grammar may limit broader applicability
- Requires substantial domain knowledge to implement
- Limited to a single game context (Battleship-like), though this is acknowledged as a limitation
- The probabilistic model itself is somewhat standard, with novelty concentrated in feature design

The arguments for acceptance outweigh the arguments for rejection, particularly given the paper's strong empirical validation and clear path for future work. The limitations are acknowledged and seem surmountable with further research.
