

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs that operate on possible world states. The authors argue that this computational approach offers a more expressive framework than existing active learning methods, which typically limit queries to simple label requests. They evaluate their model on a dataset of human questions asked in a Battleship-style information search game.

The paper's main contribution is the formulation of question asking as program generation, with a probabilistic model that incorporates features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The authors claim their model successfully predicts human question frequencies in the training data and can generate novel human-like questions.

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting framework, I have significant concerns about its methodological soundness and the evidence supporting its claims. The core approach of modeling questions as programs is not entirely novel—similar ideas appear in computational linguistics (e.g., [12, 24]) and cognitive science (e.g., [5, 13]). The authors fail to adequately distinguish their contribution from these prior works, particularly in terms of how their approach advances beyond existing program synthesis methods for question generation.

The evaluation strategy is particularly problematic. The model is evaluated solely on predicting human question frequencies in the same Battleship domain, without demonstrating how this approach would improve active learning performance in practical machine learning tasks. The paper claims to be "the first predictive model to date of open-ended human question asking," but this is misleading—previous work on visual question generation (e.g., [16, 23]) already produces human-like questions, albeit with different constraints.

Most concerning is the lack of rigorous comparison against established baselines. The authors test variants of their model (e.g., lesioning features) but don't compare against standard active learning approaches or existing question generation methods. The "full model" outperforms the "complexity-agnostic" model, but this doesn't demonstrate that their approach is better than existing methods—it only shows that their specific feature engineering is better than their own ablated versions. Without comparisons to established methods, it's impossible to assess whether this framework represents a meaningful advance.

The paper also suffers from limited scope. The model is evaluated exclusively on a single game domain with specific constraints (e.g., questions must have one-word answers), which severely limits generalizability. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide evidence that the approach would work in more complex, real-world scenarios. The claim that their model "may eventually enable more human-like question asking in machines" is speculative with no empirical support.

The technical evaluation is also methodologically questionable. The authors report a Spearman correlation of 0.64 between model predictions and human question frequencies, but they don't explain why this correlation is meaningful for the task. In fact, they note that "there is, surprisingly, no correlation at all between question generation frequency and EIG alone," suggesting that their model's success may be due to features other than informativeness. The paper also doesn't provide evidence that the model's predictions would actually improve learning performance in active learning settings.

I'm particularly concerned about the paper's overstatement of significance. The authors claim to have made "a step toward more capable active learning machines," but the paper provides no evidence that their question generation approach would actually improve learning performance in any real active learning scenario. The connection between their question generation framework and improved active learning is purely theoretical, with no empirical validation.

While the paper is well-written and the probabilistic model is technically sound within its narrow scope, it falls short of the high standards expected for NIPS. The approach is not sufficiently novel, the evaluation is inadequate (lacking comparison to established baselines), and the claimed significance is unsupported by evidence. The paper presents an interesting theoretical framework but fails to demonstrate that it advances the state of the art in active learning or question generation.

**Strengths:**
- Well-organized presentation of the theoretical framework
- Clear explanation of the programmatic representation of questions
- Adequate description of the probabilistic model and features
- Useful demonstration of the model's ability to generate novel questions

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient novelty relative to prior work in program synthesis and question generation
- No rigorous comparison against established active learning or question generation methods
- Limited evaluation to a single domain with specific constraints
- Overstated significance without empirical evidence of improved learning performance
- Theoretical claims about "human-like" question asking lack validation

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to modeling question asking but fails to provide sufficient evidence that it represents a meaningful advance over existing methods. The lack of rigorous comparison against established baselines and the limited scope of evaluation prevent this work from meeting NIPS' standards for methodological rigor and significance.
