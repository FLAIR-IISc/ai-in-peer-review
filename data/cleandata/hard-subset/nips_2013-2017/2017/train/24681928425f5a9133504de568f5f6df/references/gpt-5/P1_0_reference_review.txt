Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a probabilistic model of human question generation that represents questions as executable programs in a typed, LISP-like language. A context-free grammar defines a compositional hypothesis space over programs/questions. A log-linear model scores questions using features for expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, program complexity (via −log probability under the grammar), answer type priors, and a relevance flag. Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human questions via importance sampling over the grammar. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural-language questions collected in a Battleship-like game across 18 contexts (with 2 very large contexts excluded from model evaluation), using leave-one-context-out evaluation to predict human question distributions and to sample novel, “human-like” questions.

The work draws on and connects several lines: active learning and query selection (Settles), formal semantics of questions as partitions over worlds (Groenendijk & Stokhof; Roberts), cognitive modeling with languages of thought and probabilistic program induction (Goodman, Lake, Tenenbaum), and program-based semantics for QA (e.g., Wang et al.). It differs from goal-oriented dialog systems and VQG by explicitly optimizing over the informativeness of executable, compositional queries, aiming to capture human-like tradeoffs between informativeness and complexity.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling framing of questions as executable programs, enabling principled computation of informativeness and reuse of compositional building blocks.
- Probabilistic generative model that predicts human question frequencies and can synthesize novel, context-sensitive questions; explicit features and ablations make the contribution transparent.
- Empirical findings are intuitive and interesting: complexity plays a large role; EIG alone does not suffice; answer-type preferences matter.
- Methodology is grounded in Bayesian belief updating and information-theoretic objectives, connecting cognitive plausibility and machine learning.
- The grammar can express all questions in the dataset, and novelty filtering via answer-distribution equivalence is thoughtful.

Weaknesses
- Narrow domain and small dataset (605 questions, 16 contexts used for modeling): limits conclusions about generality and scalability. Many components (predicates, constants) are hand-engineered for Battleship.
- Evaluation relies heavily on approximate log-likelihood with importance sampling and correlations with sparse human frequencies; significance claims are modest for some ablations (e.g., full model exceeds information- and type-agnostic variants in only ~81%/88% bootstrap samples).
- The correlation of the full model’s energy with human frequencies is only marginally better (and sometimes slightly worse) than the information-agnostic variant, which complicates the claim that informativeness materially improves fit beyond complexity and type biases.
- Importance sampling from a uniform PCFG may have high variance and bias against rare-but-good questions; no diagnostics on estimator variance, effective sample size, or sensitivity to sample size are provided.
- Complexity is operationalized via −log q(x) under a uniform PCFG (i.e., effectively length); cognitive and computational complexity need not align with this proxy. No analysis of alternative priors (e.g., description length with typed penalties, cost for lambda/mapping/set) is provided.
- Limited baselines: no comparison to simpler heuristics (e.g., n-gram or template-based question models conditioned on context features), to neural sequence models over the program strings, or to pragmatic/RSA-style models for question choice.
- Generated questions are only qualitatively assessed; no human evaluation of usefulness or naturalness, nor automatic metrics beyond answer-distribution novelty.

Quality
The approach is technically sound and grounded in established principles (Bayesian inference, EIG, log-linear modeling over a grammar). However, key computational details are deferred to supplements (full grammar, EIG computation specifics, hypothesis space sizes) and some choices (proposal distribution, length cap, optimization stability) lack diagnostics. The ablations are appropriate, but stronger baselines and statistical testing would increase confidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The motivation, data, model, and results are clearly described. Some clarity gaps: precise grammar rules and typing are in the supplement; details of the hypothesis space enumeration, runtime, and the mapping from natural language to programs are only sketched; and the choice of complexity proxy could be justified more fully.

Originality
Modeling question asking as program generation with a compositional grammar and combining EIG, complexity, and answer-type features in a predictive generative model is novel relative to prior VQG/dialog work and extends formal semantic and cognitive modeling traditions to question generation. The contribution is more methodological and conceptual than algorithmically groundbreaking, but is original and timely.

Significance
The work opens a path toward richer, goal-directed query generation for active learning and dialog by grounding questions in executable semantics. That said, the current impact is constrained by domain specificity and scale. Demonstrations beyond a single synthetic domain and stronger baselines would better establish significance for the NIPS community.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide diagnostics for importance sampling: effective sample size, variance across runs, sensitivity to 150k sample size and to the length cap.
- Explore alternative complexity priors (e.g., MDL with operator-specific costs; depth constraints; cognitive priors) and report their effect.
- Add baselines: (i) a purely complexity+type-only model; (ii) a neural model over programs (e.g., LSTM conditioned on context features); (iii) simple template/n-gram models. Even if weak, they contextualize the gains.
- Include a small human study rating the utility/naturalness of generated questions versus human questions.
- Report more details on the Bayesian ideal observer: hypothesis space sizes per context, pruning strategies, and runtime.
- Discuss generalization to another domain with minimal re-engineering to demonstrate portability.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and well-motivated formulation bridging ML, semantics, and cognition.
- Transparent probabilistic model with interpretable features and ablations.
- Empirical evidence that complexity and type preferences shape human question choices; model predicts distributions and produces sensible novel questions.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scale and domain specificity; modest quantitative gains over ablations; no strong ML baselines.
- Evaluation relies on approximate likelihoods without sufficient diagnostics.
- Generalization beyond the Battleship domain remains untested.

Overall recommendation: borderline accept (weak accept)
I find the central idea compelling and relevant to NIPS audiences interested in active learning, program induction, and interactive ML. While the empirical scope is limited and some methodology could be strengthened, the paper offers a clear, principled framework and concrete results that could spur further research on compositional query generation. Strengthening evaluation and baselines would make this a solid accept.
