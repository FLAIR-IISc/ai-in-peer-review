Summary
This paper models human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as lambda-calculus/LISP programs that execute on possible-world states (Battleship boards) to produce answers. A PCFG defines the space of compositional programs; a log-linear energy model over programs, with features for expected information gain (EIG), complexity (-log PCFG probability), answer type, and a “relevance” filter, defines a distribution over questions. Parameters are fit by maximizing the likelihood of human questions collected from an information-search game (605 questions across 18 contexts; 2 contexts dropped for computational reasons). Approximate inference uses importance sampling from the PCFG to estimate gradients and normalizing constants. The model predicts human question frequencies in held-out contexts and can sample novel, “human-like” questions.

Strengths
- Ambitious, timely problem: formalizing open-ended, goal-directed question asking with compositional semantics is a worthwhile direction for both ML and cognitive science.
- Elegant unification: compositional programs + EIG operationalize informativeness over rich question spaces; the PCFG provides a generative prior over well-typed questions.
- Behavioral validation on a curated dataset; the framework generates plausible novel questions beyond the training set.
- The analysis highlights a real phenomenon: EIG alone is insufficient; complexity/conciseness matters.

Major weaknesses
- Conditioning on context is not formally specified. The probability model p(x;θ) is defined context-free (Eq. 2–4), but EIG is context-dependent. The derivations and likelihood (Eq. 3) omit c, and it is unclear whether the trained distribution is p(x|c;θ) or p(x;θ). This is a central modeling gap.
- Incorrect or ambiguous normalization/gradient math:
  - Z estimator: “Z ≈ E_q[p(x;θ)/q(x)].” Since p includes Z, this is circular. The unbiased IS estimator is Z = E_q[exp(-E(x))/q(x)] over unnormalized scores.
  - Gradient sign: For p ∝ exp(-E), ∂ log L/∂θk = -E_D[fk] + E_p[fk]. The paper states N E_D[fk] − N E_p[fk] and uses “gradient ascent,” which appears sign-inconsistent. This should be clarified and corrected.
- Importance sampling/inference details are underspecified and likely inconsistent:
  - “150,000 questions is sampled in order to approximate the gradient at each step.” If resampled each step, 100k iterations × 150k samples is infeasible; if fixed, the proposal may not cover the target as θ changes (and the importance weights degrade).
  - No diagnostics (effective sample size, variance) or runtime/memory budgeting are provided. IS over a massive discrete space with changing targets needs more care (e.g., adaptive proposals, tempering).
- EIG computation and hypothesis space handling are under-specified. Exact enumeration over Battleship configurations can be very large; two contexts were dropped; details are deferred to [19]. Without clear computational procedure (e.g., pruning, caching, symmetry reduction), this part is not reproducible.
- The complexity feature uses -log q(x) under a PCFG with uniform rewrite choices. This entangles the sampling proposal with the learned energy and makes performance sensitive to grammar design. The “complexity” prior may largely reflect hand-engineered syntactic probabilities rather than a principled MDL/description-length measure.
- The “relevance” feature f∅ relies on a “b marker” in grammar rules, apparently hand-authored and domain-specific. This undermines claims of generality and risks leaking domain knowledge into the model.
- Evaluation gaps/contradictions:
  - The full model is claimed to be best on held-out log-likelihood (Table 1; not present), but the reported Spearman correlations show the information-agnostic model slightly higher (0.65 vs. 0.64). This discrepancy is not discussed.
  - No absolute likelihood/perplexity numbers or confidence intervals; bootstrap details (sampling procedure, sample size) are missing.
  - The novelty generation filtering uses “mutual information of answer distributions” to detect equivalence; exact procedure, thresholds, and whether MI is computed exactly over H are not specified. Also, equating (size Blue) and (size Red) as “equivalent” can be context-dependent given priors/ambiguity.
- Reproducibility: crucial pieces are in the supplement (Table SI-1/SI-2), which are not provided; several implementation decisions (e.g., truncation to ≤100 functions, type system and operator overloading like = on vectors) are not fully described.

Clarity and presentation issues
- Numerous typos/infelicities: “within in,” “less then,” inconsistent formatting of equations/footnotes (e.g., 3/4/5 markers in text), the dangling “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017), Long Beach, CA, USA.” line in the introduction.
- References are incomplete, inconsistent, and in multiple cases malformed or missing years and venues (e.g., “L, J. Johnson...”; “d, F. Strub, V. Harm...”; many arXiv citations without numbers; missing years across the list). This needs significant cleanup.
- Claims like “first predictive model to date of open-ended human question asking” appear overstated given prior work on question evaluation/generation (e.g., Hawkins et al. 2015; RSA-based pragmatic models) and program-based QA.

Originality and significance
- The combination—question-as-program with EIG, trained to human data—is interesting and could be impactful if the statistical and computational issues were handled rigorously and the approach generalized beyond Battleship. As is, heavy domain-specific engineering (grammar, relevance filter) and unclear inference limit significance.

Pros
- Clear, motivating problem and bridge between ML and cognitive science.
- Compositional program representation well-suited to rich question spaces.
- Empirical dataset and qualitative examples are appealing.
- Evidence that balancing informativeness and complexity matters.

Cons
- Core probabilistic formulation errors/omissions (context conditioning, Z estimator, gradient sign).
- Inference methodology is opaque or implausible at stated scales; no diagnostics.
- Heavy dependence on hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar marks and complexity prior.
- Evaluation lacks key quantitative details; contradictions not reconciled.
- Numerous presentation and reference issues.

Questions for authors
- Please formalize p(x|c;θ) and update all derivations accordingly. How is the context incorporated into the energy and normalization?
- Clarify the normalization estimator and gradient sign. Did you use ascent or descent? Provide exact optimization objective and verified gradients.
- Do you resample the 150k programs each iteration? If not, how do you mitigate importance weight degeneracy as θ changes? Provide ESS statistics and runtime.
- Precisely describe EIG computation, hypothesis space sizes per context, and any approximations/pruning.
- Provide the full grammar (SI-1/SI-2), type system, and rules for f∅ marking. How much of this transfers to new domains?
- Include the missing quantitative results (Table 1), with CIs, and reconcile the correlation vs. log-likelihood findings.

Recommendation: Reject
The paper tackles an exciting topic with a promising framing, but the current version has significant technical inconsistencies in the probabilistic formulation and inference, insufficient methodological detail for reproducibility, heavy domain-specific engineering, and numerous presentation issues. With corrected math, clearer conditioning on context, robust and well-diagnosed inference, and thorough cleanup of the manuscript and references, a revised version could be much stronger.
