This paper proposes a framework for modeling human question asking as program generation, with the goal of enabling more human-like question asking in machines. The core idea – representing questions as programs executable on a world state – is conceptually interesting and attempts to bridge the gap between simple active learning queries and the richness of human inquiry. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions collected during a spatial reasoning game, demonstrating an ability to predict human question frequencies and synthesize novel, human-like questions.

While the central premise is intriguing, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from imprecise definitions and questionable experimental methodology to numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and a generally verbose writing style. The cumulative effect of these flaws significantly undermines the paper's impact and justifies a rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Framing:** The idea of framing questions as programs is a novel and potentially fruitful avenue for research in active learning and human-computer interaction.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate a quantifiable link between their model and human question-asking behavior, even if the magnitude of the effect is modest.
*   **Dataset Utilization:** The paper effectively leverages an existing dataset to provide a concrete evaluation setting.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Imprecise Definitions:** The notion of a "program" is loosely defined. While the authors gesture towards lambda calculus and LISP, the practical implementation appears ad-hoc and lacks formal rigor. The connection between these theoretical foundations and the actual program structures used is not clearly articulated.
*   **Overly Complex Grammar:** The grammar presented (Tables SI-1 and SI-2 in the supplementary material) is unnecessarily complex and appears tailored specifically to the Battleship domain. The justification for the inclusion of lambda expressions and set operators feels tenuous and adds complexity without clear benefit. The rule “L → (draw C)” being excluded demonstrates a fragility in the design.
*   **Questionable Evaluation:** The leave-one-out cross-validation scheme is not sufficiently robust. The small size of the dataset (605 questions) raises concerns about the statistical significance of the reported results. The evaluation metric (log-likelihood) is appropriate, but the lack of comparison to simpler baselines weakens the conclusions. The reported Spearman's rank correlation of 0.64 is also not sufficiently impressive, given the complexity of the model.
*   **Ambiguous Feature Engineering:** The features used in the probabilistic model (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance) are not clearly motivated or explained. The implementation of the "informativeness" feature, based on Expected Information Gain, relies on several assumptions that are not thoroughly justified.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study:** The ablation study, while present, is insufficient. The description of the performance differences between the models lacks statistical rigor and does not provide a clear understanding of the contribution of each feature.
*   **Formatting and Writing Issues:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, missing periods, awkward phrasing). The writing style is often verbose and lacks conciseness. The frequent use of parenthetical remarks disrupts the flow of the text. Reference formatting is inconsistent.
* **Missing Details:** The implementation details regarding the importance sampling approximation are glossed over. The number of samples (150,000) feels arbitrary and is not justified.

**Specific Concerns:**

*   Equation 1 lacks clarity regarding the scope of the summation (K is not defined).
*   The definition of the energy function in Equation 1 is missing details on the form of the features `f_k(x)`.
*   The discussion of the "filter" feature `f∅(x)` is confusing and requires clarification.
*   The phrase “Although it is straightforward to represent those questions with this rule…” regarding the excluded rule, indicates a prior design flaw.
*   The claim of "strong agreement" between model predictions and human frequencies is overstated, given the relatively modest correlation coefficient of 0.64.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The core idea of framing question asking as program generation is novel.
*   The paper provides an empirical evaluation of the proposed framework.

**Con:**

*   The technical details are imprecise and lack rigor.
*   The experimental methodology is questionable.
*   The writing style is verbose and contains numerous minor errors.
*   The significance of the results is limited.
*   The complexity of the model does not appear to justify the modest improvement over simpler approaches.



**Overall:**

Despite the interesting central idea, the paper is fundamentally flawed by a lack of technical rigor, insufficient experimental validation, and numerous minor issues. The cumulative effect of these weaknesses renders the paper unsuitable for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should significantly revise the paper to address the concerns raised above before resubmitting it to a different venue. A more rigorous formalization of the programming language, a more comprehensive evaluation with stronger baselines, and a more concise and polished writing style are essential for improving the quality of the work.
