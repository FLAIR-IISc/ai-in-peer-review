

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper presents a highly novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The authors propose that questions can be represented as executable programs that, when run on possible world states, produce answers. This "language of thought" approach captures the compositionality and computability of human questions, offering a fresh perspective on how humans formulate rich, goal-directed inquiries. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-style information-search game, demonstrating both predictive accuracy for human question patterns and the ability to generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper's core innovation lies in reframing question asking as program synthesis rather than as a linguistic or dialog problem. This is a significant departure from existing work on active learning (which focuses on label acquisition), goal-directed dialog systems (which use canned questions), and visual question generation (which relies on image-question pairs). The authors effectively distinguish their work from prior efforts, noting that their approach enables open-ended, context-sensitive question generation without requiring large training datasets.

The technical contribution is strong: the authors develop a context-free grammar for generating question programs, define a probabilistic generative model that incorporates key features like informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (via grammar probability), answer type, and relevance. Their ablation studies convincingly demonstrate that all these features are necessary to capture human question patterns, with the complexity feature being particularly important - a finding that challenges the common assumption that humans optimize purely for information gain.

The paper's strengths are numerous. First, the conceptual novelty is exceptional - this is a genuinely new way of thinking about question asking that could reshape active learning research. Second, the evaluation is thorough and well-designed, showing strong quantitative performance in predicting human question distributions and qualitative success in generating novel, meaningful questions (Figure 3). Third, the work has clear potential for broader impact: by modeling questions as programs, it bridges cognitive science and AI, offering a pathway to more human-like question-asking in machines.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately: the grammar is domain-specific to the Battleship game, and the model operates on semantic representations rather than natural language. However, the authors rightly argue that many grammar components (logical connectives, set operations, arithmetic) are domain-general, suggesting the approach could generalize with appropriate domain knowledge engineering.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold conceptual contributions, I find this paper exceptionally compelling. It doesn't just incrementally improve existing methods but fundamentally reframes a core aspect of human learning. The program generation approach opens up new research directions for active learning that could significantly advance how machines interact with humans to acquire knowledge.

The minor weaknesses are outweighed by the paper's conceptual significance. The domain-specific grammar is a limitation, but the authors clearly identify it as such and suggest future work to address it. The computational complexity of the inference process is acknowledged but doesn't undermine the core contribution. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a strong case for its novelty and significance.

This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by introducing a new framework that could transform how we think about question asking in both humans and machines. It's not just a methodological refinement but a conceptual leap that repositions question asking as a problem of program synthesis rather than language generation. The ability to generate novel, human-like questions (Figure 3) demonstrates the power of this approach in a way that previous work on question generation cannot match.

Given the paper's exceptional novelty, bold conceptual framework, and clear potential for impact, I strongly recommend acceptance. The work is not merely incremental - it establishes a new direction for active learning research that could lead to more human-like AI systems capable of sophisticated knowledge acquisition through questioning. While the current model is domain-specific, the framework it establishes is general enough to inspire future work across multiple domains.

The paper's significance is amplified by its connection to cognitive science - it provides the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking, bridging computational approaches with cognitive phenomena. This interdisciplinary contribution is exactly the kind of work that NIPS should celebrate.

In summary, this paper represents a significant conceptual advance that should be accepted for NIPS. It's not just a good technical paper - it's a paper that changes how we think about a fundamental aspect of learning.
