

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation, where questions are represented as syntactically well-formed programs that output answers when executed on possible world states. The authors evaluate their approach using a dataset of natural language questions from a Battleship-style information-search game, demonstrating that their probabilistic model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions. This work sits at the intersection of cognitive science, active learning, and program synthesis, offering a formal computational account of how humans construct semantically meaningful questions.

## Strengths

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant contribution by reframing question asking as program synthesis, which is a fresh perspective compared to existing active learning approaches that focus on label queries and neural question generation methods that require large datasets. The use of compositionality and computability as core principles aligns with linguistic and cognitive science traditions while offering a novel computational approach. This work clearly distinguishes itself from prior NIPS work on question generation (e.g., VQG datasets [16, 23]) by focusing on goal-directed, context-sensitive question asking rather than image captioning-style generation.

**Quality:** The model is theoretically sound with a clear probabilistic foundation (Equations 1-2) that incorporates multiple relevant features (informativeness, complexity, answer type). The evaluation is thorough, including both quantitative metrics (log-likelihood, Spearman correlations) and qualitative generation of novel questions. The authors carefully consider alternative model variants (lesioning key components), demonstrating that complexity and answer type features are crucial for modeling human behavior, not just pure informativeness.

**Significance:** The work addresses an important gap in active learning research by modeling richer forms of human-like question asking. The ability to generate novel, contextually appropriate questions has potential applications in human-AI interaction, educational technology, and cognitive modeling. The paper's conclusion that "people balance informativeness and complexity when producing semantically coherent questions" provides a testable hypothesis for cognitive science.

## Weaknesses

**Limited scope of evaluation:** The model is evaluated exclusively on a single domain (Battleship game), which restricts the generalizability of the findings. While the authors acknowledge this limitation (Section 6), they don't sufficiently address how the model would transfer to more open-ended domains or whether the grammar would need significant modification. The paper would benefit from discussing how the model might adapt to other domains with different question structures.

**Over-reliance on domain-specific knowledge:** The grammar and features are heavily tailored to the Battleship domain, which raises questions about the model's broader applicability. While the authors note that some aspects (logical connectives, set operations) are domain general, the specific implementation appears too constrained for general question asking. A more compelling case for transferability would strengthen the significance claim.

**Insufficient comparison to related work:** The paper mentions prior work on program-based question answering [24, 12] but doesn't sufficiently distinguish their contribution from these approaches. The discussion of "computational work in cognitive science" [17, 5, 13] could be more precise about how this work advances beyond previous program-based cognitive models.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The theoretical contribution is strong, the model is well-constructed, and the evaluation is thorough for the domain studied. However, the limited scope of evaluation and over-reliance on domain-specific knowledge prevent it from making a broader impact on the field.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand evaluation to additional domains:** The authors should attempt to apply their model to a different question-asking task (e.g., a different game or a simple educational task) to demonstrate transferability. Even a brief analysis of how the grammar would need to change for another domain would strengthen the paper.

2. **Clarify the connection to broader applications:** The paper should more explicitly address how this work could contribute to more general AI systems that engage in natural language interaction. What are the specific barriers to applying this approach beyond the Battleship domain?

3. **Refine comparison to related work:** The authors should more precisely differentiate their work from program-based question answering systems [24, 12] and prior cognitive models [17, 5, 13], emphasizing the novel aspects of modeling *question asking* rather than question answering.

4. **Address the natural language interface gap:** The paper acknowledges that the system operates on semantic representations rather than natural language directly, but this limitation needs more discussion. How could this be bridged to create a more complete human-AI interaction system?

This is a promising contribution that advances our understanding of human question asking through a computational lens. With the suggested improvements, it could make a stronger case for broader significance in the NIPS community. The work demonstrates a clear path toward more human-like question asking in AI systems, which is an important direction for future research.
