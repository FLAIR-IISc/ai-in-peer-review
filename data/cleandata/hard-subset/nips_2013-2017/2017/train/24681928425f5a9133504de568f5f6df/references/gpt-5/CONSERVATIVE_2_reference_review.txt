Summary
This paper models human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as compositional programs in a small, LISP-like language with a hand-designed grammar. A log-linear model over program features (informativeness via expected information gain, a complexity prior via the PCFG probability, answer type indicators, and a relevance filter) defines a distribution over questions conditioned on a game context (a Battleship-like grid world). Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human questions (translated into programs) using importance sampling over programs to approximate gradients and normalization. The model is evaluated on a 605-question dataset across 18 contexts via leave-one-out splits: (i) predictive log-likelihood of human questions; (ii) correlation between model “energy” and human frequency; and (iii) qualitative samples of “novel” questions.

Relation to prior work: The framing connects to work in formal semantics (questions as partitions), Bayesian experimental design/EIG and cognitive modeling of question asking (e.g., Hawkins et al.), program induction as a language of thought, and program-based question answering/semantic parsing. Compared to prior visual/VQG and dialog systems, the contribution is a compositional, semantically grounded space of questions and a simple probabilistic model of which questions humans choose.

Strengths
- Clear, compositional formalism: Representing questions as executable programs is principled and aligns with both formal semantics and program induction traditions.
- Simple, interpretable probabilistic model: The log-linear combination of informativeness, complexity, and answer-type features yields readable insights (e.g., complexity matters).
- Cognitive relevance: Addresses a real phenomenon (human question asking) beyond label queries; links to Bayesian belief update and EIG.
- Some predictive success: Reasonable rank correlations on several contexts; full model outperforms some lesioned variants in (approximate) log-likelihood; qualitative samples are sensible.
- Dataset availability and transparent assumptions (possible-world belief, answer partitions).

Weaknesses
- Heavy hand-engineering and narrow domain. The grammar, hypothesis space, and features are tailored to a small Battleship task; 14 questions and 2 contexts are dropped for computational reasons. Generalization to new domains is unclear and likely requires substantial knowledge engineering.
- Limited and partially contradictory evidence for the core claim (informativeness drives questions). The information-agnostic model achieves equal or higher average rank correlation (0.65) than the full model (0.64), and prior work notes low correlation between EIG and human frequency. The observed gains are mainly due to the complexity prior and type priors, which weakens the “active learning” contribution.
- Baselines are insufficient. All comparisons are ablations of the same model. Missing are: (i) a pure PCFG prior over programs; (ii) retrieval/template baselines from other contexts; (iii) simple heuristics (asking about most uncertain attribute/ship); (iv) non-program baselines (n-gram or neural LMs trained on the questions). Without these, it is hard to assess the incremental value of the proposed feature set and grammar beyond prior frequency and simplicity.
- Evaluation methodology concerns.
  - Importance sampling details are under-specified and potentially impractical: “150,000 questions… at each step” over 100,000 iterations is not credible; likely a fixed proposal set is reused—please clarify. Bias/variance of the Z estimator and gradient estimates are not quantified; no sensitivity analyses.
  - Approximations and exclusions (dropping contexts and question types) limit the validity of leave-one-out conclusions.
  - The normalizing constant and model likelihoods are approximate; only bootstrap over Z is reported. No per-context statistical comparisons or uncertainty over correlations.
  - “Novel question” evaluation is purely qualitative; no human judgments of naturalness/utility.
- Mapping from natural language to programs is manual and not evaluated. There is no end-to-end pathway from NL input to program nor back to NL for deployment; this limits ML significance and practical relevance.
- Clarity and completeness issues. Key grammar tables and many necessary details are in the supplement. Reference list has numerous incomplete entries. Important implementation choices (hypothesis space sizes, runtime, convergence, number of parameters, inter-annotator reliability for program translations) are missing.

Quality
The paper is technically sound at a high level but relies on substantial hand-crafted structure and approximate inference with limited diagnostics. Claims about informativeness are not strongly supported relative to simpler priors.

Clarity
Generally well written, but crucial details are deferred to the supplement; the evaluation and optimization procedures need clarification. References need fixing.

Originality
Representing questions as programs for generation is an interesting recombination of known ideas (LOT, EIG, PCFGs, program-based semantics). Novelty is moderate, with substantial overlap to prior cognitive and semantic parsing work.

Significance
As a cognitive modeling paper, it offers a neat formalism and some predictive value. For NeurIPS, impact on ML practice seems limited: small domain, no end-to-end NL, minimal comparisons to modern baselines, and limited evidence that informativeness materially improves predictions.

Questions for authors
- Do you resample 150k programs every gradient step, or reuse a fixed pool? Provide compute/runtime and diagnostics for the importance sampler (ESS, variance).
- How sensitive are results to the proposal distribution q, grammar rule probabilities, and the prior p(h)? Any robustness analyses?
- What is the inter-annotator agreement for translating NL questions into programs? How many programs are ambiguous?
- Can you report per-context log-likelihoods and correlations with uncertainty, including the two dropped contexts if feasible?
- Add baselines: PCFG-only prior, template retrieval, and a simple uncertainty heuristic; ideally a small neural LM trained on the question corpus.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually interesting framing with interpretable components.
- Some predictive alignment with human data; complexity vs informativeness trade-off is insightful.
- Potentially useful direction for program-based question generation.

Cons for acceptance
- Domain-specific hand-engineering; weak generalization story.
- Evidence that informativeness helps is limited; performance gains are mostly due to priors.
- Missing key baselines and robust evaluation; methodological ambiguities.
- Limited ML significance and no end-to-end NL component.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The work is intriguing and could be impactful with stronger evaluation, broader baselines, clearer methodology, and at least one additional domain or an end-to-end NL component.

Confidence
High.
