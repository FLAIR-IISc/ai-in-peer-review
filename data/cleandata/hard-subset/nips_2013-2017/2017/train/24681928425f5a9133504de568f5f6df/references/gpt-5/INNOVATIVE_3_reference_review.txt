Summary
This paper proposes a bold and timely reframing of question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a typed, compositional language (lambda/LISP-style), which when run on a possible world return an answer. A probabilistic generative model over this program space (with a log-linear energy) selects questions conditioned on context using features for informativeness (expected information gain under a Bayesian ideal observer), complexity (−log probability under a PCFG prior over programs), answer type, and a relevance filter. The model is trained via approximate maximum likelihood using importance sampling over programs generated by the grammar. On a Battleship-style information search dataset of 605 human questions across contexts, the model predicts human question distributions in held-out contexts (leave-one-out), performs ablations showing the importance of complexity and informativeness, and can sample novel, “human-like” questions not seen in training.

Relation to prior work
The work connects threads from active learning (beyond label queries), semantic/pragmatic theories of questions as partitions over possible worlds, program induction and semantic parsing, and neural question generation for images/dialog. Unlike prior dialog systems that rely on canned templates or large paired datasets, this approach emphasizes compositional expressivity, executability, and goal-directed information gathering. It also dovetails with probabilistic program induction and the rational analysis tradition in cognitive science.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Casting question asking as program synthesis over a compositional, executable space is original and potentially field-shaping for interactive ML and cognitive modeling.
- Principled utility: Informativeness is formalized via EIG under a Bayesian hypothesis space, yielding a coherent cost–utility trade-off when combined with a complexity prior.
- Generativity and productivity: The grammar enables synthesis of questions not seen in the data, addressing limitations of retrieval-based approaches.
- Empirical validation: Leave-one-out predictions and ablations demonstrate that complexity matters (not just EIG), aligning with human preferences for concise questions.
- Clear bridge to future extensions: The approach could naturally integrate with semantic parsing, neurally guided program synthesis, and more general interactive agents.

Weaknesses and questions
- Domain specificity and scalability: The grammar contains Battleship-specific predicates; the method’s scalability to richer, open-world domains is unclear. What fraction of the grammar is reusable across domains in practice?
- Approximate inference concerns: Importance sampling from a uniform PCFG proposal may be a poor fit for the true posterior over “useful” questions; estimates of Z and gradients could be high variance. Have you tried learned proposals, AIS, or MCMC-in-program-space?
- Computational cost of EIG: Computing EIG over large hypothesis spaces limits applicability; two contexts were dropped and a stochastic rule removed. How does runtime scale, and can amortized inference or particle filters help?
- Mapping from natural language to programs: Human questions were “translated into programs.” Was this manual? What was the annotation protocol and inter-annotator agreement? This mapping is critical for reproducibility and for extending beyond this domain.
- Evaluation breadth: Correlations are moderate and data are sparse; qualitative samples are compelling but cherry-picking risk exists. A human study rating the naturalness/usefulness of generated questions would strengthen claims.
- Baselines: Beyond ablations, comparisons to stronger alternatives (e.g., ranking grammar-generated questions purely by EIG, retrieval plus paraphrase, neural sequence models conditioned on context abstractions) would contextualize gains.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a coherent probabilistic formulation and reasonable engineering choices for a difficult synthesis problem. The ablation results are informative and support the main claims. However, the reliance on approximate importance sampling, removal of some contexts, and limited baseline comparisons temper the strength of the empirical case. Clarifying the NL-to-program annotation and providing more robust uncertainty estimates for likelihoods would improve rigor.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The motivation is compelling, and the model is described at a level sufficient for expert readers. That said, key details appear in the supplement (full grammar, types), several references are incomplete, and the annotation procedure needs explicit description. A brief complexity analysis and pseudocode for the training/evaluation loop would help reproducibility.

Originality
High. The framing of question generation as program synthesis over a compositional space, combined with a Bayesian EIG objective and a complexity prior, is a novel combination that goes beyond prior work in active learning and question generation.

Significance
Potentially high. If extended to more realistic domains (with semantic parsing and scalable EIG approximations), this could influence interactive ML, human-in-the-loop systems, and cognitive models of inquiry. Even as a domain-specific study, it provides a template for principled, goal-directed question generation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report the natural language to program annotation process and agreement statistics; release code.
- Explore better proposals for importance sampling (e.g., learned PCFG rule probabilities, neurally guided generators) or AIS/SMC for Z and gradient estimation.
- Provide runtime/memory scaling and ablation on proposal size.
- Add human evaluation of generated questions (informativeness, clarity).
- Include baseline ranking by EIG alone over the same candidate set and a retrieval-based baseline.
- Discuss integration with semantic parsers to close the loop with natural language.
- Consider alternative utility functions beyond EIG (e.g., expected regret, answerer’s cost).

Decision: Weak Accept
This paper introduces a fresh and impactful perspective—questions as programs—and demonstrates that a principled balance of informativeness and complexity predicts human questions and yields creative, context-sensitive generation. Despite limitations in scalability, annotation clarity, and evaluation breadth, the conceptual contribution is strong and worth disseminating at NeurIPS.

Confidence: 0.7 (familiar with program synthesis/probabilistic modeling; less with the specific Battleship dataset)
