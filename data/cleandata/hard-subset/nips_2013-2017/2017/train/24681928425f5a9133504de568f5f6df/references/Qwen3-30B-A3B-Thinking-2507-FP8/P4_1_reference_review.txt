

# Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework modeling human question asking as program generation. The authors represent questions as syntactically well-formed programs that, when executed on possible world states, produce answers. This reframes question asking as program synthesis, addressing a gap in active learning literature that has largely focused on simple label queries rather than rich, human-like questions. The approach builds on concepts from cognitive science (compositionality, "language of thought") and computational linguistics (question as program), while addressing limitations in existing AI dialog systems that rely on canned questions or large datasets for visual question generation. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship-style information-search game, where human players asked questions to identify ship configurations.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound probabilistic model with a well-structured log-linear approach. The authors provide rigorous evaluation through cross-validation, model variants (lesioning key features), and bootstrap analysis. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies (Spearman correlation 0.64) and generates novel human-like questions not present in the training data. The evaluation of features (informativeness via EIG, complexity, answer type, relevance) demonstrates their individual importance through controlled ablation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (domain-specific grammar, semantic vs. natural language representation), demonstrating honesty in evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The grammar for program generation is well-explained with concrete examples, and the model components (features, optimization, evaluation) are presented with appropriate mathematical formalization. The experimental setup (Battleship game context) is clearly described, and the figures effectively illustrate key concepts (e.g., novel questions generated by the model). The supplementary materials provide necessary technical details without overwhelming the main text.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant original contribution by framing questions as executable programs that can be evaluated over possible worlds. While prior work has modeled questions as programs for question answering (e.g., [24, 12]), this is the first to apply this approach to human question generation. The paper clearly differentiates itself from visual question generation work (e.g., [16, 23]) by focusing on goal-directed question asking rather than image captioning. The demonstration of compositionality (15% of human questions were novel across contexts) provides strong evidence for the approach's novelty.

**Significance:** The work advances the state of the art in modeling human question asking behavior, providing a framework that could eventually enable machines to ask richer questions. The insights about the balance between informativeness and complexity offer valuable cognitive science insights. The ability to generate novel human-like questions demonstrates potential for practical applications in human-computer interaction. The approach bridges cognitive science and machine learning, potentially benefiting both fields.

## Weaknesses

**Generalizability:** The grammar and model are somewhat domain-specific to the Battleship game. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't provide a clear path for adapting to new domains. The need for substantial domain knowledge (e.g., ship sizes, board configurations) may limit broader applicability, though the authors note that some grammar elements (logical connectives, set operations) are domain-general.

**Natural Language Interface:** The model operates on semantic representations rather than directly on natural language text, requiring translation from natural language to programs. While the authors mention potential integration with computational linguistics tools, they don't explore this connection in depth.

**Data Sparsity:** The human data is relatively sparse (many questions were asked only once), which limits the strength of correlation analysis as a measure of fit. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't provide methods to address it in the model.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Novel framing of question asking as program generation with clear theoretical grounding
- Strong empirical evaluation demonstrating successful prediction of human question frequencies
- Ability to generate novel, human-like questions not present in training data
- Rigorous ablation study demonstrating the importance of key features (informativeness, complexity, answer type)
- Clear connection to cognitive science and linguistics literature
- Well-structured, clearly written paper with appropriate technical depth

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Domain-specific grammar limits immediate generalizability to new tasks
- Requires semantic representation rather than direct natural language processing
- Data sparsity may limit the robustness of correlation-based evaluation
- Limited discussion of how to extend to domains beyond the Battleship game

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant contribution to modeling human question asking behavior by framing it as program synthesis. The technical approach is sound, the evaluation is thorough, and the work demonstrates clear novelty in the context of active learning and human-computer interaction. While the domain-specific nature of the grammar is a limitation, the paper provides a solid foundation for future work on extending the approach to new domains. The insights about the balance between informativeness and complexity are valuable for both cognitive science and machine learning. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, addressing an important problem in AI with a novel technical approach that advances the field. The strengths of the work substantially outweigh the limitations, making it a strong candidate for acceptance.
