

# Peer Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The authors argue that humans ask rich, context-sensitive questions to efficiently learn about the world, and they formalize this process by modeling questions as executable programs that operate on possible world states. The framework uses a context-free grammar to generate syntactically valid question programs, combined with a probabilistic model that weights questions based on their informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance to the game context. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of 605 natural language questions collected from human participants playing a Battleship-like game. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies in held-out contexts and generates novel, human-like questions that were not present in the training data.

This work builds on several strands of previous research: (1) active learning literature that has traditionally focused on simple label queries [21], (2) work on visual question generation [16, 23, 11] that requires large datasets of paired images and questions, and (3) computational cognitive science research modeling concepts as programs [17, 5, 13]. The paper makes a significant contribution by formalizing human question asking as program synthesis in a goal-directed manner, moving beyond the limitations of previous approaches that lack the representational capacity for rich, context-sensitive questioning.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear formalization of their approach with a well-motivated probabilistic model that incorporates multiple features capturing different aspects of human question asking. The evaluation methodology is rigorous, including cross-validation, comparison with ablated model variants, and qualitative analysis of generated questions. The authors carefully address limitations, such as the domain-specific nature of their grammar and the need for semantic representations rather than direct natural language processing.

The model's ability to generate novel questions that were not present in the training data (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") is particularly impressive and demonstrates the model's capacity for productive question asking. The authors also provide thorough analysis of why certain features (like complexity) are important, showing that pure informativeness maximization would produce unhelpful questions (e.g., overly complex programs).

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The problem statement, motivation, and technical approach are presented in a logical sequence with helpful examples. The grammar and program representation are clearly explained through concrete examples (e.g., "How long is the blue ship?" as (size Blue)). The evaluation is presented in a straightforward manner with well-chosen figures (Figures 2 and 3) that effectively illustrate the model's performance.

The supplementary materials (which include grammar rules and additional examples) are well-organized and enhance the paper's clarity. The authors make appropriate use of supplementary materials for technical details that would clutter the main text, such as the grammar rewrite rules.

## Originality
This work represents a significant advance in modeling human question asking. Previous work on active learning has focused on simple label queries [21], while visual question generation systems require massive datasets and lack the goal-directed flexibility of human questioning [16, 23, 11]. The paper's core insight—modeling questions as executable programs that can be evaluated over possible world states—is novel and provides a compelling framework for understanding human question asking.

The compositionality aspect (recognizing that questions are built from conceptual building blocks) and the computability aspect (questions as programs that evaluate to answers) are particularly novel contributions that move beyond previous approaches. The authors also demonstrate the importance of complexity in human question asking, which had been overlooked in prior work.

## Significance
This paper addresses a fundamental gap in active learning: the ability to ask rich, informative questions rather than simply requesting labels. The framework provides a new approach for developing machines that can learn more like humans by asking targeted, context-sensitive questions. The successful generation of novel, human-like questions (Figure 3) demonstrates the practical potential of the approach.

The work has potential to impact multiple areas: cognitive science (by providing a computational model of human question asking), natural language processing (by offering a new approach to question generation), and active learning (by extending the scope of queries beyond simple labels). The framework's domain-general aspects (logical connectives, set operations, arithmetic) suggest potential for extension to other domains, which the authors acknowledge as an important direction for future work.

## Strengths
1. Novel framework that models questions as executable programs, capturing both compositionality and computability.
2. Comprehensive evaluation with cross-validation, ablation studies, and qualitative analysis of generated questions.
3. Demonstrates the importance of complexity as a feature in human question asking, beyond mere informativeness.
4. Successfully generates novel, human-like questions that weren't present in the training data.
5. Clear connection to cognitive science and computational linguistics literature.
6. Well-organized and clearly written with appropriate supplementary materials.

## Weaknesses
1. The grammar is somewhat domain-specific to the Battleship game, which limits the immediate applicability to other domains (though the authors acknowledge this as a limitation and discuss domain general aspects).
2. The model operates on semantic representations rather than directly on natural language, which creates a gap between the model and practical applications where natural language is needed (though the authors acknowledge this as a future direction).
3. The dataset is relatively small (605 questions), though the authors demonstrate the model's ability to generalize to novel contexts.
4. The paper would benefit from more discussion of how the model could be extended to handle questions with more complex natural language structures beyond the limited answer types (Boolean, Number, Color, Location).

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Presents a novel and well-motivated framework for modeling human question asking.
- Provides the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking.
- Demonstrates strong empirical results with both quantitative and qualitative evaluation.
- The work bridges cognitive science and machine learning in a meaningful way.
- The approach has clear potential for extending active learning to more human-like question asking.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The domain-specific nature of the grammar may limit immediate applicability to broader contexts.
- The lack of direct natural language processing might be seen as a limitation for practical applications.
- The dataset size is relatively small for the complexity of the model.

## Recommendation
I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a significant contribution to the field that advances our understanding of human question asking and provides a promising new direction for active learning. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The authors have carefully evaluated their approach and provided a clear path for future work.

The minor limitations noted (domain-specific grammar, semantic representation rather than natural language) are acknowledged by the authors and are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's strong contributions. The work is clearly within NIPS scope as it addresses fundamental machine learning challenges with a novel computational approach.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement
1. In the discussion of limitations (Section 6), consider adding a concrete example of how the grammar might be extended to a new domain (e.g., a different game or real-world scenario).
2. The paper could briefly mention how the model's program generation could interface with recent advances in natural language generation (e.g., [24]) to produce natural language questions directly.
3. In Figure 2, consider adding a brief explanation of the "low correlation" contexts to help readers understand why some contexts had weaker matches.
4. The paper could more explicitly connect to recent work on neural program synthesis [e.g., 12, 24] to highlight how this work differs from data-driven approaches.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field with clear significance, technical merit, and novelty. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
