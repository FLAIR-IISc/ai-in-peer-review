

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes modeling human question asking as program generation, where questions are represented as executable programs over a possible world state. The authors introduce a grammar-based probabilistic model that captures compositionality and computability, and evaluate it on a data set of human questions from a Battleship-like game. The model predicts human question frequencies better than variants that omit key features like informativeness, complexity, or answer type. The work represents an interesting computational approach to question asking, bridging cognitive science and machine learning.

## Strengths
The paper makes a valuable contribution by formalizing question asking as program generation, which provides a clear computational framework for modeling human question asking. The grammar-based approach elegantly captures compositionality and computability. The evaluation is thorough, with careful comparison of different model variants using both quantitative (log-likelihood) and qualitative (novel question generation) metrics. The demonstration of novel, human-like questions generated by the model is particularly compelling.

## Weaknesses

**1. Technical Inconsistencies and Missing Details:**
- The paper defines EIG(x) = ∑_{d∈Ax} p(d;x)[I[p(h)]− I[p(h|d;x)]], but never explicitly defines I[·] as Shannon entropy, which is critical for understanding the calculation. This omission makes the technical description incomplete.
- In Section 4.3, the authors state "The prior p(h) is specified first by a uniform choice over the ship sizes, and second by a uniform choice over all possible configurations given those sizes." However, this is inconsistent with the description in Section 3, which states "Every board had exactly three ships which were placed in nonoverlapping but otherwise random locations." The model's prior should reflect this randomness, but the paper doesn't clarify how it's implemented.
- The claim that "the grammar can produce syntactically coherent programs that have no reference to the game board at all" is problematic. The grammar should inherently produce questions about the game board, yet the paper doesn't explain how the grammar avoids generating irrelevant programs beyond the "filter" feature.

**2. Methodological Concerns:**
- The evaluation uses "leave one out cross-validation" but doesn't justify why this is appropriate for the data set. With 605 question-context pairs and 18 contexts per player, a more robust validation strategy (e.g., leaving out entire players or contexts) would be preferable to avoid data leakage.
- The paper claims the model "outperformed the information-agnostic and type-agnostic models," but the log-likelihood improvements (0.65 vs. 0.64 for information-agnostic) are minuscule. The paper should provide statistical significance testing for these small differences.
- The "complexity-agnostic" model performed poorly (Spearman's correlation of -0.36), but the paper doesn't explain why complexity is so critical. Without this explanation, the claim that "people also optimize for complexity" lacks supporting evidence.

**3. Formatting and Presentation Issues:**
- The reference list contains multiple formatting errors: "arXiv preprint, ," and "Learning End-to-End Goal-Oriented Dialog, A. Bordes, J. Weston, arXiv preprint, ," suggest incomplete reference entries.
- The paper references "Table SI-1" multiple times but doesn't include these tables in the main text, making it difficult to assess the grammar details without supplementary materials.
- In Section 4.2, the example "Are all ships the same size?" is represented as "(= (map (λ x (size x)) (set Blue Red Purple)))", but the paper doesn't clarify how the set is defined (e.g., does it include all ship colors?).
- The paper states "We define X to be the set of questions with 100 or fewer functions" (Section 4.4), but this arbitrary limit isn't justified or discussed.

**4. Overstated Claims:**
- The paper claims "this is the first predictive model to date of open-ended human question asking," but this is an overstatement. The VQG work (e.g., [16]) provides a predictive model for question generation, albeit in a different context. The paper should clarify how this work differs from existing predictive question generation approaches.
- The paper states "the grammar over programs provides one account of the productivity of the human behavior," but the evidence for this is weak. The paper notes that "about 15% of the human questions did not appear in any of the other contexts," but doesn't demonstrate that the grammar can generate these novel questions, only that it can model their distribution.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents an interesting approach to modeling human question asking as program generation, with a well-constructed grammar and probabilistic model. However, the technical details are often incomplete, the evaluation lacks sufficient statistical rigor, and the paper makes some overstated claims about the novelty and scope of the work. The formatting inconsistencies and missing details in the main text (referencing supplementary materials without providing them) undermine the paper's credibility.

While the core idea is promising, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this approach advances the state of the art beyond existing work on program synthesis for question generation. The minor methodological issues, combined with the lack of clear evidence for the claimed advantages, prevent this from being a strong contribution to NIPS.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains significant technical omissions and methodological weaknesses that prevent it from being a robust contribution to the field. While the core idea is interesting, the execution lacks the necessary detail and rigor expected for NIPS. The minor flaws in this paper are substantial enough to outweigh its novel contributions.
