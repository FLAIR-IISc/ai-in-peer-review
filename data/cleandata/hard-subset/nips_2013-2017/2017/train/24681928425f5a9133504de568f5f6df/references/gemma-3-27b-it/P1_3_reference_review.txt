## NIPS 2017 Review: Question Asking as Program Generation

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, yield an answer. This reframes the problem as program synthesis, allowing for a compositional and computable approach to understanding how humans formulate questions. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions collected during a Battleship-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

**Related Work:**

The paper effectively positions its work within the broader context of active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation. It correctly points out the limitations of current active learning methods in handling rich queries, and the rigidity of existing dialog systems. The comparison to visual question generation is also apt, highlighting the advantage of their approach in dealing with novel scenarios without extensive training data, mirroring human learning capabilities.  They correctly identify the VQG dataset as requiring large amounts of paired data, unlike their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel and Insightful Framework:** The central idea of modeling questions as programs is a significant contribution. It provides a formal and computationally tractable way to represent the semantics of questions and their role in information seeking.
* **Compositionality and Computability:**  Highlighting these two key elements provides a compelling argument for why this approach aligns well with cognitive science principles and human question asking.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The evaluation on the collected dataset demonstrates the model’s predictive power and its ability to generate plausible questions. The strong correlation between predicted energies and human question frequencies is noteworthy.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanation of the grammar and probabilistic model is clear, and the examples are helpful.
* **Strong Baseline Comparison:**  The ablation study, comparing the full model to variants missing key features, provides valuable insights into the importance of each component (informativeness, complexity, answer type).
* **Novel Question Generation:** The examples of generated questions demonstrate the model’s ability to go beyond simply re-producing training data and offer potentially useful queries.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity of Grammar:** While the authors acknowledge limitations, the grammar, despite some general components, is tailored to the Battleship domain.  The scalability and adaptability of this grammar to more complex domains are uncertain. The removal of draw-related questions to simplify computation indicates a potential fragility.
* **Computational Complexity:** The reliance on importance sampling to approximate the normalizing constant introduces computational challenges, especially as the grammar expands. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of training and inference.
* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the empirical results are promising, the dataset is relatively small (605 questions) and limited to a single game context. Further evaluation on more diverse datasets would be crucial.
* **EIG Calculation Assumptions:** The Bayesian ideal observer framework used for calculating EIG relies on strong assumptions about the agent’s prior knowledge and beliefs. The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions isn't fully explored.
* **Lack of Qualitative Analysis of Generated Questions:** While examples are shown, a more systematic evaluation of the generated questions’ semantic coherence and actual usefulness (beyond simply being novel) would be valuable.  Are the generated questions *good* questions, or just syntactically valid ones?



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and theoretically sound approach to modeling human question asking. The authors convincingly demonstrate the feasibility and potential of their framework through empirical validation.  While limitations related to domain specificity and computational complexity exist, the contribution is significant and represents a valuable step towards more intelligent and human-like active learning systems.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and insightful framework for modeling human question asking.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the model’s predictive power.
* The ablation study provides valuable insights into the importance of different features.
* The work addresses a challenging and important problem in AI and cognitive science.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The grammar is somewhat domain-specific and its scalability is unclear.
* The computational complexity of the model may be a barrier to wider adoption.
* The evaluation dataset is limited in size and diversity.

**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  Despite the limitations, the novelty, theoretical grounding, and empirical validation make this a significant contribution to the field. The weaknesses are acknowledged by the authors and do not fundamentally undermine the strength of the work.  I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS program.
