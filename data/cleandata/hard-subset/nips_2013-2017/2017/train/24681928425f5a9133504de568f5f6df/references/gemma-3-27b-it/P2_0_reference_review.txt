## NIPS 2017 Review: Question Asking as Program Generation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and interesting framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The approach is well-motivated, grounded in cognitive science, and demonstrates promising results on a challenging dataset. While the technical contributions are incremental and the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the paper meets the NIPS criteria for acceptance, leaning towards a strong accept.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors propose a framework where questions are treated as programs that, when executed on a "world state," yield an answer. This recasts question asking as a program synthesis problem. They motivate this by arguing that existing active learning techniques are limited in their expressiveness compared to human questions, and that their approach offers a more natural representation of human inquiry. The paper clearly situates itself within the active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive science literature. It correctly points out the limitations of current dialog systems (canned responses) and visual question generation (data hungry and stereotyped).  The connection to formal linguistics and cognitive science (compositionality, language of thought) is well-articulated.

**2. Strengths:**

*   **Novelty & Insightful Framework:** The core idea of modeling questions as programs is a significant conceptual contribution. It provides a fresh perspective on a long-standing problem in AI and cognitive science.
*   **Strong Motivation:** The authors persuasively argue for the need to move beyond simple label-requesting active learning algorithms towards more expressive question asking.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The model demonstrates the ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, plausible questions. The fact that it can generalize *to* novel questions is impressive, and suggests the model is learning something about the underlying structure of human questioning.
*   **Well-Defined Grammar:** The grammar presented for program generation is a core element of the approach and appears well-designed for the given task.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the complex ideas accessible.

**3. Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Technical Contribution:** While the framework is novel, the core technical advances – the grammar and probabilistic model – feel somewhat standard.  The use of a log-linear model with feature engineering is not particularly cutting-edge.
*   **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The experimental evaluation would be strengthened by comparison to stronger baselines. While the authors compare against variations of their own model, a comparison to a state-of-the-art language model (even without the program generation constraint) would be valuable.
*   **Dataset Limitations:** The Battleship dataset, while well-defined, is relatively small and domain-specific. The generalizability of the findings to other domains is unclear.
*   **Subjectivity in Novel Question Evaluation:** The qualitative evaluation of generated questions relies on subjective judgment ("human-like"). A more rigorous evaluation metric, even if imperfect, would be desirable.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While addressed to some extent, the computational cost of inference (importance sampling) is a concern, and could limit the scalability of the approach.



**4. Evaluation of NIPS Criteria:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are reasonably well-supported by the experimental results. The authors acknowledge limitations. However, the technical contributions, while solid, are not groundbreaking.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The authors do a good job of explaining the complex concepts.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of modeling questions as programs is novel and represents a significant contribution.
*   **Significance:** The work has the potential to be impactful, particularly in the areas of active learning, human-computer interaction, and cognitive science. A more expressive and human-like question asking capability would be valuable in many applications.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel and insightful framework for modeling question asking.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict human question behavior and generate plausible questions.
*   Strong grounding in cognitive science.
*   Well-written and clear presentation.

**Con:**

*   Incremental technical contribution.
*   Limited baseline comparison.
*   Dataset is relatively small and domain-specific.
*   Subjective evaluation of generated questions.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** Despite some weaknesses, the novelty and potential significance of this work outweigh its limitations. The paper presents a compelling framework for understanding human question asking and has the potential to inspire further research in this important area. I believe it is a valuable contribution to NIPS. I encourage the authors to consider expanding the evaluation to more diverse datasets and exploring more advanced technical approaches in future work.
