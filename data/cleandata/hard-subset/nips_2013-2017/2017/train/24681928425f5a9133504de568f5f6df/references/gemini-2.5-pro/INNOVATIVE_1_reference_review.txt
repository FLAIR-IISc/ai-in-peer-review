This paper introduces a novel and exciting framework for modeling goal-directed, context-sensitive human question asking, proposing that questions can be understood as programs generated to maximize information gain. The authors recast the problem of asking a good question as one of program synthesis. They develop a probabilistic model defined over a compositional grammar that represents questions in a LISP-like "language of thought." The probability of a question is determined by a log-linear model incorporating features for informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-style information-seeking game. The results demonstrate that the model can successfully predict the frequency of human-asked questions in held-out contexts and, perhaps more impressively, can generate novel, human-like questions that are contextually appropriate. This work provides a significant conceptual bridge between cognitive science theories of compositionality and machine learning, particularly active learning.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The central thesis—that question asking can be formalized as probabilistic program generation—is a bold, elegant, and powerful idea. It moves the field of active learning far beyond its traditional focus on simple label queries and offers a principled, computational framework for understanding the rich, compositional, and generative nature of human inquiry. This is not an incremental improvement on an existing method but rather the proposal of a new and promising research direction. The connection to the "language of thought" hypothesis from cognitive science is well-motivated and provides a strong theoretical underpinning.

2.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration of the Core Idea:** The paper provides a compelling demonstration that this framework is not just a theoretical construct but a viable computational model. The ablation studies clearly show that both informativeness (EIG) and complexity are crucial for predicting human behavior. The finding that EIG alone is a poor predictor, but the combination of features in the full model achieves strong correlations with human data, is a significant insight into the cognitive constraints on question asking. This result highlights that people do not simply optimize for information but balance it against cognitive costs, a nuance often missed in purely rational-agent models.

3.  **Impressive Generative Capabilities:** The qualitative results presented in Figure 3 are a major strength. The model's ability to synthesize novel, clever, and contextually useful questions that were not present in the human data is a powerful testament to the generativity of the programmatic approach. Questions like "Are all the ships horizontal?" or "What is the column of the top left of the tiles that have the color of the bottom right corner of the board?" showcase a level of compositional reasoning that is absent in contemporary neural question generation models, which often produce stereotyped or grammatically awkward outputs without this kind of deep semantic grounding.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Domain Specificity and Scalability:** The most significant limitation is the reliance on a hand-engineered grammar specific to the Battleship domain. The authors are transparent about this, noting that the model begins with substantial domain knowledge. While some grammatical components are domain-general (e.g., logical connectives, set operators), the semantic primitives are tailored to the game's objects and properties. This raises valid concerns about the scalability of the approach and the engineering effort required to adapt it to new, more complex domains. Future work must address how these grammars might be learned or constructed more automatically.

2.  **Disconnection from Natural Language:** The model operates on semantic program representations, effectively sidestepping the difficult problem of semantic parsing from raw text. The human questions in the dataset had to be manually translated into this formal language. While this is a reasonable simplification for an initial investigation of the core idea, a complete and practical system would need to bridge this gap, likely by integrating a semantic parser. This is a non-trivial challenge that limits the immediate applicability of the system.

3.  **Simplicity of the Domain:** The Battleship game, while an excellent controlled environment for this study, is a relatively simple, symbolic world. It is an open question how this framework would handle the noise, ambiguity, and vastness of real-world perceptual domains, such as question asking about images or videos, where defining the set of possible worlds and executing programs over them is substantially more challenging.

### Recommendation

Despite the limitations regarding domain-specificity and the lack of a natural language front-end, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The sheer novelty and significance of the core idea—framing question asking as program generation—far outweigh these weaknesses. This paper is not presenting a perfect, finished system, but rather a groundbreaking new paradigm for active learning and goal-directed dialogue. The weaknesses identified are not fundamental flaws in the approach but rather natural and exciting avenues for future research that this work opens up.

This is exactly the kind of high-risk, high-reward paper that NIPS should champion. It introduces a major new concept, supports it with a thoughtful computational model and solid empirical evidence, and has the potential to inspire a great deal of follow-up work in machine learning, NLP, and computational cognitive science. It advances the state of the art not by inches, but by pointing toward a whole new landscape.
