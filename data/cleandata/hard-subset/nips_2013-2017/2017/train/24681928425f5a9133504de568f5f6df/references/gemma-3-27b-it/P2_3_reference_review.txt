## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea – representing questions as executable programs – is insightful and offers a promising avenue for bridging the gap between current active learning approaches and the richness of human inquiry. While the experimental evaluation is limited to a single game context, the results are encouraging and demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach. I believe this paper meets the NIPS acceptance criteria, albeit with some caveats detailed below.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The authors propose modeling questions not as simple label requests, but as programs that, when executed, yield an answer based on a world state. This elegantly reframes question asking as a program synthesis problem. They demonstrate this framework on a dataset of questions from a "Battleship"-like game, showing they can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions. The paper effectively positions itself against existing work in active learning (which typically focuses on simple queries), dialog systems (often canned responses), and visual question generation (reliant on large datasets). It appropriately highlights the limitations of these approaches and argues for the benefits of a compositional, computable, and context-sensitive approach to question asking.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Originality:** The central idea of framing questions as programs is genuinely novel and provides a strong theoretical foundation for modeling human inquiry. This moves beyond simply *asking* questions to representing *the meaning* of a question in a formally executable way.
* **Strong Theoretical Motivation:**  The authors ground their approach in cognitive science, drawing connections to compositionality, the language of thought, and Bayesian belief updating.  This theoretical grounding adds significant value.
* **Promising Results:** Despite the limited dataset, the model’s ability to predict human question frequencies and generate plausible novel questions is impressive. The quantitative results, particularly the advantage over simpler models, are convincing.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is well-written and the core concepts are explained clearly. The breakdown of the probabilistic model and feature engineering is particularly well-presented.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature, including active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive science.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently restricted to a single, albeit well-defined, game context. The generalizability of the model to other domains is unclear and a major concern.  While the authors acknowledge this, it’s a significant limitation.
* **Domain Knowledge Dependency:** The grammar and features are designed specifically for the Battleship game. Extending this to new domains may require substantial manual effort and knowledge engineering, as the authors acknowledge. The scalability of the approach is therefore questionable.
* **Complexity of the Model:** The probabilistic model, while theoretically sound, appears complex with numerous features.  A clearer justification for the inclusion of *every* feature would be beneficial. It's hard to assess the relative contribution of each without more detailed ablation studies.
* **Importance Sampling Concerns:** The reliance on importance sampling to approximate the gradient introduces potential bias and instability. While the authors acknowledge this, a more robust approximation technique or a sensitivity analysis would be valuable.



**4. Addressing the NIPS Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, though the reliance on approximations (importance sampling) is a point of concern. The claims are generally well-supported by the results, but the limited scope of the evaluation weakens the overall confidence. It’s a reasonably complete piece of work for a first exploration of this idea.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors do a great job of explaining complex concepts in an accessible manner. 
* **Originality:** The approach is highly original, offering a fundamentally different perspective on question asking.  The combination of program synthesis, probabilistic modeling, and cognitive science principles is novel.
* **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant. If the approach can be generalized to other domains, it could lead to more intelligent and efficient active learning algorithms, as well as a deeper understanding of human cognition.  



**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* A genuinely novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem.
* Demonstrates promising results in a controlled environment.
* Well-written and clearly presented.
* Addresses a gap in current active learning techniques.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of evaluation – concerns about generalizability.
* Domain-specific grammar and features raise scalability questions.
* Complexity of the model and reliance on approximations.



**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding generalizability and scalability by:

* **Discussing potential strategies for adapting the grammar to new domains.** Could a more generic grammar be defined, or could domain-specific knowledge be incorporated more efficiently?
* **Conducting further analysis of the model complexity and feature importance.** A more thorough ablation study would help clarify the contribution of each feature.
* **Acknowledging the limitations of importance sampling and exploring alternative approximation techniques.**




This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and has the potential to inspire further research on intelligent question asking and active learning. While the current evaluation is limited, the core ideas are strong and warrant further investigation.
