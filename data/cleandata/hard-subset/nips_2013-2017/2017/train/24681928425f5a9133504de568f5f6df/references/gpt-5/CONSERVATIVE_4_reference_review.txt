Summary
This paper models human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as typed, executable programs in a small lambda/LISP-like language; a context-free grammar defines the hypothesis space. A log-linear model over program features (informativeness via expected information gain, complexity via a PCFG prior, answer type priors, and a “relevance” filter) induces a distribution over questions conditioned on a Battleship-style game context. Inference and learning rely on importance sampling over programs using the grammar as proposal. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 human questions across 18 contexts (two dropped for computational reasons). The authors report improved held-out log-likelihood over ablations and show a few qualitative samples of “novel” questions.

Relation to prior work: The approach connects to the semantics-of-questions literature (partition semantics), semantic parsing as program execution, probabilistic program induction, and Bayesian experimental design (EIG). It positions itself relative to active learning (label queries), dialog systems, and visual question generation, arguing for richer, compositional question representations.

Strengths
- Clear, formal proposal for viewing questions as executable programs, with an explicit grammar and a probabilistic model over that space.
- Uses a cognitively motivated feature set (informativeness vs. complexity) and shows that complexity/conciseness helps explain human preferences.
- The grammar-based representation plausibly accounts for compositional productivity and can synthesize plausible new questions.
- The paper is generally well written and situates itself in both ML and cognitive science literatures.

Weaknesses
- Heavy domain engineering and limited scope: the grammar and features are tuned to a single, small Battleship domain; the system does not operate on natural language, and mapping from NL to programs is assumed.
- Limited empirical validation: only one narrowly-scoped dataset (605 questions), two contexts and 14 questions dropped for computational reasons, and no comparison to strong baselines beyond ablations (e.g., sequence models over program strings, retrieval+recombination, semantic parsers trained to predict question distributions).
- The reported quantitative gains are fragile: correlations between model energy and human frequencies for the full model (0.64) are on par with the information-agnostic model (0.65), suggesting EIG adds little beyond complexity and type priors; the main driver of fit appears to be a length/PCFG prior.
- Inference/learning methodology is weakly justified: importance sampling with a uniform PCFG proposal over a vast program space is likely to have high-variance estimates of Z and feature expectations; bootstrapping the same approximate normalizer does not address estimator bias; it is unclear whether samples are refreshed per iteration.
- The “relevance” filter and the EIG=0 indicator are hand-crafted safety valves that, together with the grammar prior, heavily constrain the hypothesis space; it is not shown how sensitive results are to these choices.
- Novelty claims are not rigorously evaluated: “novel questions” are selected examples; there is no human study assessing usefulness, grammaticality, or preference relative to human questions; the equivalence heuristic that treats programs differing only by arguments as equivalent is questionable in context.
- Reproducibility concerns: key details are deferred to supplementary tables; some citations are incomplete; dropping contexts reduces the main evaluation set; no variance bars or ESS for IS are reported.

Quality
The modeling framework is coherent and technically sound at a high level, but the empirical methodology is not sufficiently rigorous for NeurIPS. The reliance on a hand-designed grammar and features in a single toy domain, combined with approximate and potentially high-variance estimation, makes it difficult to assess robustness. Claims that the model predicts human question distributions better than alternatives rest on ablations rather than comparisons with competitive baselines. The correlation analysis suggests complexity dominates; the contribution of EIG appears marginal here.

Clarity
Writing is clear and accessible. However, important details are missing or only in the supplement: full grammar rules, size of the enumerated hypothesis spaces per context, whether IS samples are resampled each iteration, effective sample sizes, and the precise computation of EIG. Figures/tables referenced (e.g., Table 1, Figures 2–3, SI-1/2) are critical to interpreting the results.

Originality
The framing “questions as programs” is interesting but largely synthesizes existing ideas: program representations for meaning, Bayesian experimental design for informativeness, and log-linear modeling with a PCFG prior. The novelty lies in applying this synthesis to human question asking and proposing a generative model over questions. Methodologically, the learning/inference components are fairly standard.

Significance
Given the limited domain, small dataset, absence of strong baselines, and fragile quantitative gains, the work’s impact for the NeurIPS community is likely modest at this stage. As a cognitive modeling paper, it is intriguing; as an ML contribution, it falls short on demonstrating generality and state-of-the-art advances.

Questions for the authors
- How stable are results to changing the proposal q(x)? Do you resample per iteration? What are the effective sample sizes for Z and feature expectations?
- How much does the “relevance” filter contribute? What happens if it is removed or replaced by stronger typing constraints?
- Why does the information-agnostic model match or exceed the full model in rank correlation? Can you provide metrics (e.g., top-k coverage, calibration) where EIG clearly helps?
- How sensitive are results to grammar design choices? Could you show transfer to a second domain with minimal added engineering?
- The equivalence criterion that treats questions differing only by arguments as equivalent seems dubious in context; can you justify or revise this?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add competitive baselines: e.g., a neural sequence model over program strings with a length penalty; retrieval-and-recombine baselines; or a semantic parser trained to predict question distributions.
- Report variance/ESS for IS, use stronger normalizer estimators (AIS/NCE), and clarify resampling strategy.
- Provide human evaluation of generated “novel” questions.
- Demonstrate generalization to at least one additional domain and a basic NL interface.
- Remove hand-tuned features where possible (e.g., replace f∅ with stricter typing).

Recommendation
Reject.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation; interesting synthesis.
- Some empirical signal that complexity and answer-type priors matter.

Cons
- Narrow, highly engineered setting; small dataset; dropped contexts.
- Weak baselines; limited, approximate evidence; EIG appears to add little.
- Inference/learning methodology and novelty insufficient for NeurIPS.

Reviewer confidence: High.
