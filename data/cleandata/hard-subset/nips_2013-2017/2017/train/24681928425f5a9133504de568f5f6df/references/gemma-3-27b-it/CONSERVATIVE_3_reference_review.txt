This paper proposes a framework for modeling human question asking as program synthesis, aiming to bridge the gap between current active learning paradigms and the richer, more targeted questions humans pose. The core idea – representing questions as executable programs – is conceptually interesting and attempts to ground question generation in a formal, computational framework. The authors evaluate this model on a dataset of questions asked during a game, demonstrating its ability to predict question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

While the premise is intriguing, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing due to a lack of rigorous validation and a reliance on a relatively narrow, albeit well-defined, domain. The primary concern is the extensive engineering effort seemingly required to define the grammar and features, coupled with limited evidence that this complexity genuinely captures the underlying cognitive processes. The presented results, while positive, are largely correlational. Showing that the model *predicts* human question frequencies is not the same as demonstrating it *explains* why humans ask those questions, or that it generalizes beyond this specific game. 

The authors acknowledge limitations regarding domain specificity and the need for natural language integration. However, the core issue remains the substantial hand-crafting of the system. The grammar, while allowing for the representation of the dataset’s questions, feels overly tailored. The numerous features, including the detailed EIG calculation, introduce significant complexity. The reliance on importance sampling to address intractability also raises concerns about the reliability of the results. While approximating expectations is necessary, the reliance on a potentially biased proposal distribution (the uniform grammar) could skew the results.

The comparison to alternative models is a strength, demonstrating the importance of features beyond just informativeness. However, the ablation study feels incomplete. The “complexity-agnostic” model’s poor performance is expected, but the limited gains from including the answer type features suggest that this aspect of the model may not be essential. Furthermore, the lack of comparison to simpler baselines—such as a model predicting question frequency based solely on context similarity—weakens the conclusions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Framing:** The perspective of framing question asking as program synthesis is a potentially fruitful avenue for research.
*   **Detailed Model:** The authors provide a comprehensive description of their probabilistic model, including the grammar, features, and optimization procedure.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation on the human question dataset provides some evidence for the model’s ability to capture human question asking behavior.
*   **Ablation Study:** The inclusion of an ablation study, though limited, provides some insights into the importance of different model components.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Domain Specificity:** The reliance on a single, highly structured game limits the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Engineering Complexity:** The significant amount of manual effort required to define the grammar and features raises concerns about scalability and the potential for overfitting.
*   **Limited Generalization:** The model's ability to generate novel questions doesn’t necessarily imply a deeper understanding of human question asking strategies.
*   **Lack of Strong Baselines:** The absence of comparison to simpler baseline models makes it difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach.
*   **Approximation Concerns:** The reliance on importance sampling to handle intractability could introduce bias into the results.
*   **Correlational Results:** The demonstrated correlation between model predictions and human question frequency doesn't establish causality or a comprehensive explanation of the underlying cognitive processes.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful perspective on question asking.
*   The empirical results, while not conclusive, demonstrate the model’s ability to capture some aspects of human question asking behavior.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The heavy reliance on domain-specific engineering and the lack of strong baselines weaken the claims of originality and significance.
*   The limited generalizability and the correlational nature of the results raise concerns about the robustness and explanatory power of the model.
*   The computational complexity and the reliance on approximations raise practical concerns about the scalability of the approach.



**Overall:** I lean towards rejection. The paper presents an interesting idea, but it lacks the rigor and validation necessary to justify acceptance at NIPS. The extensive engineering effort, coupled with the limited generalizability and the lack of comparison to simpler baselines, make it difficult to believe that this approach represents a significant advance in our understanding of question asking or active learning. Substantial revisions, including a more thorough evaluation on additional domains and a comparison to stronger baselines, would be required for reconsideration.
