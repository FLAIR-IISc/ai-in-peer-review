- Paper proposes modeling human questions as executable programs using a typed compositional grammar and a log-linear model trading off EIG, complexity, type preferences, and relevance, trained on a Battleship-like dataset.
- Evaluation uses leave-one-context-out prediction, ablations, and shows the full model best predicts held-out log-likelihoods and correlates with human question frequencies (avg Spearman 0.64), and can synthesize novel questions.
- Strength: Clear, principled formulation grounding compositionality and meaning in executable programs with Bayesian utility evaluation.
- Strength: Quantitative predictive model that explains human distributions by combining EIG with a cognitively plausible complexity penalty.
- Strength: Compositional generalization—grammar can generate novel, useful questions and avoids trivial paraphrases via equivalence checking.
- Strength: Strong connections to Bayesian cognitive modeling, program induction, question semantics, and contrasts with VQG/active learning.
- Strength: Transparent ablations showing the importance of complexity in matching human behavior.
- Weakness: Heavy domain engineering—grammar and hypothesis space tailored to Battleship and NL-to-program mapping done offline, unclear portability.
- Weakness: Small, narrow dataset (605 questions, 18 contexts) with many singletons limits statistical power and diversity.
- Weakness: Mixed evaluation signals—model energy correlation similar for information-agnostic variant (0.65 vs 0.64), raising questions about EIG’s incremental effect on rank-order predictions.
- Weakness: Inference approximations heavy (importance sampling with large proposals, long optimization), with potential entanglement between proposal distribution and complexity prior.
- Weakness: No human evaluation of generated questions to verify usefulness, understandability, or one-word answer assumptions.
- Weakness: Dropped 14 questions and 2 contexts for tractability may bias results; sensitivity analysis needed.
- Weakness: Complexity prior (−log q(x)) reflects proposal PCFG probabilities, not independently measured cognitive cost; alternative penalties should be tested.
- Quality: Technically sound with coherent probabilistic formulation and appropriate ablations, but needs more details on bootstraps, CIs, and robustness diagnostics.
- Clarity: Well organized and readable but crucial grammar/specification moved to supplement; NL-to-program mapping and equivalence criteria need clearer exposition.
- Originality: Novel and timely idea combining program-based question generation with Bayesian evaluation, distinct from prior work.
- Significance: Conceptually significant for cognitive and interactive ML, but practical impact limited by domain specificity and data scale.
- Key requested clarifications: annotation procedure and agreement for NL→program mapping; sensitivity to program length and proposal grammar; bootstrap protocol details; handling of answer types and ambiguity; incorporation of answerer cost/noise.
- Pros summary: Elegant, principled framework; explains complexity vs. informativeness tradeoffs; generates novel questions; well positioned in literature.
- Cons summary: Heavy engineering and single domain, approximate inference issues, lacks human evaluation, and complexity prior tied to proposal distribution.
- Recommendation: Weak accept—promising contribution that would benefit from robustness checks, clearer reporting, and a small cross-domain or human-evaluation demonstration.
