### Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional language (a LISP-like lambda calculus) that, when executed on a world state, produce an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic log-linear model to predict which questions humans will ask in a given context. The model's energy function is a weighted combination of features including the question's expected information gain (EIG), its complexity (derived from a probabilistic context-free grammar), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number), and its relevance to the game. The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in an ambiguous "Battleship"-like game. Through a series of ablation studies, the authors demonstrate that humans balance informativeness with complexity and have preferences for certain answer types. The model is also shown to be capable of generating novel, contextually relevant questions not seen in the human data. This work connects ideas from active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science to provide one of the first predictive models of open-ended, goal-directed inquiry.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed probabilistic model is well-defined, and the use of a log-linear model over a grammatically-defined space of programs is a principled approach. The methodology for evaluating the model via leave-one-out cross-validation and ablation studies is rigorous and provides clear support for the central claims. The authors are honest about the limitations, particularly the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar and the abstraction away from natural language parsing.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is explained step-by-step, and the results are presented logically. The use of examples (e.g., different questions about ship size) effectively illustrates the concepts of compositionality and computability. The figures and tables are informative and support the main text well.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is its originality. While program synthesis has been applied to question *answering* and concept learning, its application to question *asking* is novel and insightful. It represents a significant conceptual advance over traditional active learning, which is typically confined to simple label queries. The framework successfully unifies information-theoretic goals (EIG) with cognitive constraints (simplicity/complexity), providing a fresh perspective on a fundamental aspect of intelligence.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant. It opens up a new research direction for active learning, suggesting a path toward machines that can learn more efficiently by asking richer, more human-like questions. For cognitive science, it offers a powerful, quantitative model of human inquiry that goes beyond descriptive accounts. While the current implementation is domain-specific, the core principles—representing queries as programs and balancing informativeness with complexity—are general and likely to inspire future work in machine learning, NLP, and human-computer interaction.

### Detailed Comments

**Strengths:**

1.  **Conceptual Framework:** The core idea of treating questions as programs is powerful. It elegantly captures the compositionality, generativity, and computability of human inquiry in a way that previous approaches in active learning and dialogue systems have not.
2.  **Balancing Information and Complexity:** The paper's most compelling result is the empirical demonstration that human question asking is not a simple maximization of information gain. The ablation study, which shows the complexity-agnostic model performing extremely poorly (Spearman's ρ = -0.36), provides strong evidence that a simplicity bias is a crucial component of human question selection. This is an important finding for both AI and cognitive science.
3.  **Generative Capability:** The ability of the model to generate novel, plausible, and even clever questions (as shown in Figure 3) is a strong validation of the framework. It proves the model is not merely memorizing or re-weighting existing questions but has captured underlying principles of how to construct a good query.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Scalability and the Hand-Crafted Grammar:** The authors rightly point this out as a limitation, but its significance cannot be understated. The current grammar is tailored to the Battleship domain. Extending this framework to new, more complex domains would require substantial, non-trivial knowledge engineering. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of potential paths to mitigate this, such as grammar induction techniques or integrating large-scale knowledge bases to automatically populate the primitive functions and objects.
2.  **Inference and Optimization:** The optimization relies on importance sampling with a fixed proposal distribution (a uniform PCFG). This can be inefficient if the learned model distribution `p(x;θ)` becomes very different from the proposal `q(x)`. It would be interesting to know if more advanced methods were considered, such as adaptive importance sampling or MCMC methods for approximating the model expectations. The large number of samples (150,000) and iterations (100,000) suggests that the optimization process is computationally intensive, which could be a barrier to scaling.
3.  **The Gap to Natural Language:** The model operates on a "language of thought" representation, sidestepping the difficult problem of semantic parsing. While this is a reasonable simplification for an initial investigation, the title "Question Asking as Program Generation" might be interpreted as a full model of the process. It would be more precise to frame it as a model of *semantic query formulation*. A brief discussion on the challenges of interfacing this model with a state-of-the-art semantic parser would make the paper's scope and future challenges clearer.
4.  **Correlation vs. Log-Likelihood:** There is a slight tension in the results reported on page 5. The full model has the best log-likelihood, but the information-agnostic model has a slightly higher Spearman's correlation (0.65 vs. 0.64). While log-likelihood is the more principled measure of model fit for the density estimation task, this small discrepancy is curious and might warrant a brief comment. It could suggest that while EIG is critical for correctly assigning probability mass across the entire distribution (improving likelihood), the rank ordering of the most common questions is heavily dominated by the complexity/prior terms.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Pro:** Introduces a highly original and significant new framework for question asking, bridging active learning and program synthesis.
*   **Pro:** Provides strong empirical evidence for a key cognitive insight: that people balance informativeness and complexity when asking questions.
*   **Pro:** The methodology is sound, the paper is exceptionally clear, and the results are compelling, including the generation of novel questions.
*   **Pro:** Likely to be highly influential and to stimulate new research in several subfields of AI and cognitive science.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

*   **Con:** The reliance on a domain-specific, hand-engineered grammar raises questions about the generalizability and scalability of the approach in its current form.
*   **Con:** The computational intensity of the inference procedure could be a practical limitation.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of the core idea and the quality of the execution, far outweigh the limitations. This is a thought-provoking paper that opens up a promising new avenue for research.
