### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed, human-like question asking, framing it as a problem of program generation. The central thesis is that rich, compositional questions can be represented as programs that, when executed on a world state, return an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic model over a space of LISP-like programs defined by a context-free grammar. This model, a log-linear energy-based model, predicts which question a person is likely to ask in a given context by balancing several key factors: the question's informativeness (measured by Expected Information Gain), its complexity (a penalty for longer programs), and its answer type (capturing human biases for, e.g., boolean questions).

The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in an ambiguous "Battleship"-like game. Through leave-one-out cross-validation, the authors demonstrate that their full model, incorporating all features, provides the best prediction of human question frequencies. A key finding is that a model without a complexity penalty performs very poorly, highlighting that humans do not simply optimize for information gain but are constrained by a preference for simplicity. The model is also shown to be generative, capable of producing novel, contextually relevant, and human-like questions not seen in the training data. The work positions itself as a step towards more sophisticated active learning agents and as a cognitive model of human inquiry.

### Review

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed approach is sound and well-executed.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The methodology is rigorous. The use of a probabilistic model over a grammar-defined space is appropriate for the problem. The optimization procedure, using gradient ascent with importance sampling to handle the intractable partition function, is a standard and valid approach for this class of models.
    *   The experimental evaluation is strong. The use of leave-one-context-out cross-validation is a robust way to assess generalization. The inclusion of an ablation study (lesioning different model components) provides clear and convincing evidence for the contribution of each feature set. The finding that the complexity-agnostic model fails dramatically is a powerful result that supports the central claims of the paper.
    *   The authors are careful and honest about the limitations of their work, particularly the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar and the fact that the model operates on semantic representations rather than raw text. This transparency strengthens the paper.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The approximation of the partition function `Z` and the model expectations via importance sampling is a potential source of variance and instability. While the authors state their parameters (150,000 samples, 100,000 iterations), a brief discussion of convergence or the stability of the gradient estimates would have further strengthened the technical claims.
    *   The authors note that 14 questions using a `draw` function were excluded due to computational complexity. While a small fraction of the data, this does slightly temper the claim that the grammar can represent "all 605 questions," though the reason for exclusion is well-justified.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, effectively contrasting the proposed approach with existing work in active learning and dialogue systems.
    *   The model is described in a clear, step-by-step manner, moving logically from the core concepts of compositionality and computability to the grammar, the probabilistic model, and the feature definitions.
    *   The connection to cognitive science concepts (e.g., "language of thought") and formal semantics provides valuable context and intellectual depth. The results are presented logically and support the conclusions drawn. The qualitative examples of generated questions are particularly effective at illustrating the model's capabilities.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   As is common in conference papers, full details of the grammar are relegated to supplementary materials. While understandable, this means a full appreciation of the model's expressive power requires consulting an external document. This is a minor point.

**Originality:** The work is highly original and presents a significant conceptual advance.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The core idea of treating goal-directed question *asking* as program *generation* is novel and insightful. While program synthesis has been applied to question *answering* and concept learning, its application to generating inquiries in an active learning setting is a new and promising research direction.
    *   This work provides a compelling alternative to the dominant end-to-end neural network approaches to question generation. It emphasizes structure, compositionality, and data efficiency, which are current weaknesses in many deep learning systems.
    *   The synthesis of ideas from active learning (EIG), probabilistic programming (generation from a grammar), and cognitive science (balancing utility and complexity) is a novel and powerful combination.

**Significance:** The paper has high potential significance for both AI/ML research and computational cognitive science.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper directly addresses a major limitation in current active learning and AI systems: the inability to formulate complex, strategic queries. It provides a concrete computational framework for moving beyond simple label requests towards more human-like information seeking.
    *   The finding that people balance informativeness with complexity is a crucial insight. It suggests that simply maximizing EIG is insufficient for building human-like agents and that incorporating cognitive constraints is essential. This result is important for researchers in both human-computer interaction and cognitive modeling.
    *   The framework is likely to inspire future work. One can imagine extending this approach to more complex domains, learning parts of the grammar from data, or integrating it with natural language parsing and generation front-ends. It opens up a rich new problem space.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The primary barrier to broader impact, as the authors note, is the reliance on a domain-specific, hand-engineered grammar. The scalability of this approach to more open and complex domains remains an open question. While this does not detract from the paper's conceptual contribution, it is a practical limitation.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and important idea, executes it with technical rigor, and presents the results with outstanding clarity. It makes a significant contribution to the fields of active learning, probabilistic modeling, and computational cognitive science. The work is thought-provoking and likely to have a lasting impact. I strongly recommend acceptance.
