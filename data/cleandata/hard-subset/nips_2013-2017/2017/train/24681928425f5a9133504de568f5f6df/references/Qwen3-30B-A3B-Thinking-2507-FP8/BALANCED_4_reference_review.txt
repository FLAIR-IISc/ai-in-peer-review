

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that, when executed on possible world states, output answers. The authors argue that this programmatic approach captures compositionality and computability in human questions, and they evaluate their model on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-style game. The core contribution is a probabilistic generative model that predicts human question frequencies and generates novel human-like questions, using features related to informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (via grammar-based length), answer type, and relevance.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a compelling case for modeling questions as programs, which is a novel and theoretically sound approach that bridges cognitive science and machine learning. The evaluation is well-designed, using leave-one-out cross-validation to show that the full model (with all features) outperforms variants that omit key components (informativeness, complexity, or answer type features). The model successfully predicts human question frequencies (with Spearman's correlation of 0.64) and generates novel, human-like questions that were not present in the training data. The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of limitations and potential extensions, acknowledging the domain-specific nature of their grammar while arguing for the domain-general aspects of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant limitation is the narrow scope of evaluation. The model is tested exclusively on a single game domain (Battleship) with 605 questions, which limits the generalizability of the findings. While the authors acknowledge this, they do not provide a clear path to extending the approach to more complex domains or natural language interfaces. The paper also lacks a thorough comparison with recent neural approaches to question generation (e.g., the VQG data set work), which might have provided a stronger baseline. The model's dependence on a Bayesian ideal observer for calculating Expected Information Gain (EIG) may not perfectly align with human cognition, and the paper does not sufficiently address this potential mismatch. Additionally, while the grammar-based approach enables compositionality, the paper doesn't demonstrate how the model would handle the full complexity of natural language questions with semantic ambiguity or pragmatic context.

**Quality:**
The technical approach is sound, with appropriate use of a log-linear model and importance sampling for optimization. The evaluation methodology is rigorous, using cross-validation and bootstrapping to assess model performance. However, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees about the model's performance or convergence properties. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their current implementation (e.g., the grammar being specific to the Battleship domain), but don't provide a clear roadmap for addressing these limitations in future work.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good explanations of the key concepts (compositionality, computability, and the grammar-based approach). The technical details of the model and evaluation are presented accessibly, though some aspects of the importance sampling implementation could be clarified. The supplementary materials provide necessary detail about the grammar rules and question features.

**Originality:**
This is a genuinely novel contribution that distinguishes itself from prior work on active learning (which focuses on label queries) and question generation (which typically relies on large datasets of paired images and questions). The programmatic framing of questions is a significant conceptual advance, and the authors effectively position their work against related literature.

**Significance:**
The work addresses an important gap in active learning literature by moving beyond simple label queries to richer, more human-like question asking. The ability to generate novel, contextually appropriate questions is a significant step toward more capable active learning systems. However, the current scope limits the immediate impact, as the model is constrained to a single domain with limited natural language capabilities.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Expand the evaluation to include a second domain (even if simplified) to demonstrate broader applicability.
2. Include a comparison with a strong neural baseline (e.g., a transformer-based question generator trained on the same data) to better establish the relative advantages of the programmatic approach.
3. Provide more detailed discussion of how the model might interface with natural language generation systems to produce human-readable questions.
4. Address the potential mismatch between the Bayesian ideal observer used for EIG calculation and actual human cognition more explicitly.
5. Consider including a qualitative analysis of why the model generates certain novel questions (beyond the examples provided), to better understand its reasoning.

**Conclusion:**
This is a strong, technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of human question asking. While the current scope is limited to a specific domain, the framework has clear potential for broader application. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation across multiple domains and a stronger comparison with recent neural approaches. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the suggestions above, as this work represents an important step toward more human-like active learning systems.
