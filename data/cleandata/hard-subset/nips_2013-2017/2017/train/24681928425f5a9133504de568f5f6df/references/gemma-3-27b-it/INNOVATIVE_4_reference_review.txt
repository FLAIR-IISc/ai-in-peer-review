This paper presents a fascinating and ambitious framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea – recasting questions as executable programs that query a world state – is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from traditional active learning approaches that focus on simple label requests. The authors connect this idea to established concepts in linguistics, cognitive science, and AI, creating a compelling intellectual foundation. The evaluation on the “Battleship” game dataset demonstrates the method's ability to predict human question frequencies *and* generate novel, plausible questions, which is particularly impressive. 

The strengths of the paper are considerable. The conceptual leap to framing questions as programs is insightful and opens up new avenues for research in both AI and cognitive modeling. The grammar-based approach provides a structured way to generate and evaluate questions, addressing a key challenge in open-ended question generation. The empirical results, while not exhaustive, are encouraging and provide initial validation for the proposed framework. The inclusion of both prediction and generation tasks strengthens the evaluation. The authors are also careful to acknowledge limitations.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. The probabilistic model, while functional, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The features used (informativeness, complexity, answer type) are intuitively reasonable, but their specific formulation and weighting appear largely driven by experimentation rather than a deep theoretical justification. The dependence on importance sampling for approximating the normalizing constant in the probabilistic model introduces potential instability and makes the results sensitive to the choice of proposal distribution. While the authors address this with bootstrapping, the computational cost and potential for bias remain concerns. The reliance on a specific domain (Battleship) is a limitation; the generalizability of the grammar and model to other scenarios is unclear, despite the authors’ acknowledgement of this. The description of the features, while present, could be more detailed (e.g. how exactly EIG is calculated).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The program generation perspective on question asking is a genuinely new and promising idea.
*   **Bold approach:** The authors tackle a difficult problem—modeling human-like question asking—with a computationally grounded and theoretically motivated framework.
*   **Positive empirical results:** The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates plausible new questions.
*   **Interdisciplinary connection:** The work bridges AI, cognitive science, and linguistics.
*   **Potential impact:** This research could inspire new approaches to active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Model limitations:** The probabilistic model feels somewhat ad-hoc and relies on approximations.
*   **Domain specificity:** The evaluation is limited to a single domain (Battleship).
*   **Limited theoretical depth:** While the connection to existing literature is strong, the theoretical justification for the specific model components could be strengthened.
*   **Computational Cost:** The complexity of the optimization process and the importance sampling may limit the scalability of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its limitations, I strongly believe that this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The core idea is highly original and has the potential to reshape our understanding of question asking and active learning. The authors have demonstrated the feasibility of their approach with encouraging empirical results. While further research is needed to address the limitations and explore the generalizability of the framework, the boldness and novelty of this work outweigh its shortcomings. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the reviewer's comments in a future version. This paper pushes the boundaries of current research and opens up exciting new directions for exploration.
