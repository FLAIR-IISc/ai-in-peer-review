

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs that generate answers based on possible world states. The authors argue that traditional active learning systems focus on simple label queries, while humans ask rich, context-sensitive questions. By modeling questions as programs, they create a formalism that captures the compositional nature of human questioning. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship-style game where participants identified ship configurations. The approach uses a probabilistic generative model that incorporates features for informativeness (based on Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The paper demonstrates that their full model outperforms variants that omit key features in predicting human question frequencies and generating novel human-like questions.

This work relates to previous NIPS contributions in active learning [21], program induction [12, 13], and cognitive science [4, 5, 14, 15]. It extends prior work on modeling questions as programs for question answering [12, 24] to the question asking task. Unlike neural approaches to question generation [11, 16, 20, 23], which require large datasets and lack goal-directed creativity, this approach models question asking as program synthesis with explicit computational constraints.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-structured probabilistic model with clear theoretical foundations. The authors carefully define the program grammar, features, and evaluation methodology. The use of Expected Information Gain for informativeness is well-motivated and properly implemented.

2. **Novel Framework**: The program generation approach offers a genuinely novel perspective on modeling human question asking. By framing questions as executable programs, the authors capture compositionality and computability in a way that distinguishes their approach from existing work.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation through multiple model variants (lesioning key components), cross-validation, and both quantitative (log-likelihood, Spearman correlation) and qualitative (novel question generation) assessments. The results consistently demonstrate the importance of all key features.

4. **Predictive Success**: The model successfully predicts human question frequencies (Spearman correlation of 0.64) and generates novel, contextually appropriate questions not present in the training data, demonstrating the model's creative capacity.

5. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper provides the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking, offering insights into how humans balance informativeness and complexity when producing questions.

## Weaknesses
1. **Domain Specificity**: While the authors argue that many grammar components are domain-general, the model is evaluated solely on a Battleship game. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the approach generalizes to other domains without significant re-engineering.

2. **Natural Language Interface**: The model operates on semantic representations rather than natural language, with no discussion of how to bridge this gap to produce human-readable questions. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't propose a concrete solution.

3. **Dataset Size**: The dataset of 605 questions from 40 participants across 18 contexts is relatively small for a machine learning model, potentially limiting the generalizability of the results.

4. **Comparison to Recent Neural Methods**: The paper doesn't compare with recent transformer-based approaches to question generation [11, 20], though it acknowledges the limitation. A comparison would strengthen the claim of novelty.

## Quality Assessment
The paper is technically sound with a well-constructed model, appropriate evaluation methodology, and careful analysis of model components. The theoretical foundations are solid and properly connected to prior work in cognitive science and linguistics. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and provide honest evaluation of their model's performance.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The technical concepts (grammar, program representation, probabilistic model) are explained with concrete examples. The figures effectively illustrate key points, and the supplementary materials provide necessary technical details. The paper provides sufficient information for experts to reproduce the results.

## Originality
This work makes a significant contribution to the originality category. The framing of question asking as program generation represents a novel approach that differs substantially from existing active learning methods (focused on label queries) and neural question generation approaches (which require large datasets and lack goal-directed creativity). The connection to compositional semantics and program synthesis in cognitive science is particularly innovative.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important gap in active learning systems by modeling rich, human-like question asking. This has potential implications for educational technology, human-AI collaboration, and cognitive modeling. While the immediate application is domain-specific, the theoretical framework provides a foundation for more capable active learning machines. The ability to generate novel questions demonstrates the model's creative potential, suggesting broader applicability.

## Recommendation
The paper makes a strong technical contribution with clear theoretical foundations, thorough evaluation, and novel insights into human question asking. The limitations regarding domain specificity and natural language generation are acknowledged but could be addressed in future work. The work represents a meaningful advancement in modeling human-like question asking and is well-suited for NIPS.

Pro: Novel framework that successfully models human question asking through program generation; strong technical evaluation; clear connection to cognitive science.

Con: Limited to a single domain; no natural language interface; relatively small dataset; lacks comparison with recent neural approaches.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria with strong technical soundness, clarity, originality, and significance. I recommend acceptance.
