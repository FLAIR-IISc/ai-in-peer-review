This paper introduces a novel framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking as probabilistic program generation. The central idea is to represent questions not as selections from a predefined set, but as executable programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. These programs, when run on a possible world state, return an answer. The problem of deciding which question to ask is thus framed as synthesizing the most "useful" program. The authors propose a probabilistic log-linear model to score potential questions, combining features such as Expected Information Gain (EIG), program complexity, and answer type. The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in an ambiguous "Battleship"-like game. The framework is compared against ablated versions of itself and demonstrates a strong ability to predict the frequency of human questions and to generate novel, plausible questions not seen in the training data.

This work carves out a unique and valuable niche. It distinguishes itself from standard active learning, which typically focuses on simple label queries, by tackling a much richer and more structured query space. It also moves beyond contemporary dialogue and question-generation systems, which are often either template-based, require massive datasets, or are not explicitly goal-directed in the same information-seeking sense. The core idea connects program synthesis, Bayesian active learning, and cognitive science, offering a fresh perspective on a fundamental aspect of intelligence.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Conceptual Novelty:** The primary strength of this paper is its bold and highly novel central thesis: question asking as program synthesis. This is a significant conceptual leap that reframes the problem of active learning. Instead of merely selecting a point to query, the agent constructs a *method* for extracting information. This is a powerful, generative view that aligns much more closely with the flexibility and creativity of human inquiry. This is precisely the kind of paradigm-shifting idea that pushes the field forward.

2.  **Compositionality and Generativity:** The program-based representation elegantly captures the compositional nature of language and thought. By defining a grammar, the model can generate a combinatorial explosion of potential questions from a finite set of primitives. This allows it to account for the productivity of human question-asking, including generating entirely novel questions that are contextually appropriate, as demonstrated impressively in Figure 3. The ability to generate questions like "Are all the ships horizontal?" or "What is the column of the top left of the tiles that have the color of the bottom right corner of the board?" is a compelling demonstration of the framework's power.

3.  **Psychologically Plausible Model:** The paper does an excellent job of building a model that is not only computationally interesting but also cognitively plausible. The finding that a pure EIG-maximization approach is insufficient and that a penalty for complexity is critical to matching human behavior is a key insight. Humans do not ask monstrously complex questions, even if they would be maximally informative. The model's combination of informativeness, complexity, and biases for certain answer types provides a much more nuanced and realistic account of the trade-offs involved in human cognition.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation (for its scope):** Given the novelty of the task, the evaluation is well-conceived. Predicting the distribution of human questions in held-out contexts is a challenging test, and the reported correlation of 0.64 is respectable. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the importance of each component of the model, especially the complexity feature. The qualitative results—the generated novel questions—are perhaps the most convincing evidence of the model's success.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on a Hand-Engineered Grammar:** The most significant limitation is the model's reliance on a manually specified grammar and semantic primitives. While the authors acknowledge this, it is a crucial point. The system's "knowledge" of what constitutes a valid question is hard-coded. This sidesteps the difficult problem of learning these concepts from data and limits the framework's scalability and applicability to new domains, which would require significant feature engineering.

2.  **Disconnection from Natural Language:** The model operates entirely on semantic program representations, bypassing the challenge of parsing natural language questions into programs or generating natural language text from them. While this is a reasonable simplification to isolate the core problem of question selection, it leaves a major gap to be bridged before this could become a practical, end-to-end system.

3.  **Computational Tractability:** The authors note that inference is intractable and requires importance sampling over a very large space of potential programs. They also had to exclude two contexts due to the size of the hypothesis space. While this is a common issue in probabilistic modeling, it does raise questions about the scalability of the approach to more complex domains with larger state spaces and more expressive grammars.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    1.  The core idea of modeling question asking as program synthesis is exceptionally novel, ambitious, and opens up a significant new direction for research in active learning and AI.
    2.  The model successfully captures the critical principles of compositionality and generativity in human inquiry, moving far beyond existing approaches.
    3.  The work provides a strong, cognitively plausible account of how humans balance informativeness and complexity when asking questions.
    4.  The qualitative results are highly compelling, demonstrating the generation of creative and intelligent novel questions.

*   **Arguments for Rejection:**
    1.  The heavy reliance on a domain-specific, hand-engineered grammar limits the generalizability and practical applicability of the current system.
    2.  The model does not engage with raw natural language, which is a major simplification of the overall problem.
    3.  The inference procedure is computationally expensive and appears to have scalability limitations.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

This paper is a clear example of high-risk, high-reward research. While the methodological framework has limitations—most notably the hand-engineered grammar—the central concept is a genuine breakthrough. It offers a new and exciting paradigm for thinking about active learning and intelligent inquiry. The authors successfully demonstrate that this paradigm can not only predict human behavior but also generate novel, human-like outputs. The weaknesses, while significant, feel like engineering challenges that can be addressed in future work, rather than fundamental flaws in the core idea. For a conference like NIPS, which should value and promote bold new directions, the conceptual contribution of this paper far outweighs its current practical limitations. This is work that is likely to inspire many others to build upon its ideas.
