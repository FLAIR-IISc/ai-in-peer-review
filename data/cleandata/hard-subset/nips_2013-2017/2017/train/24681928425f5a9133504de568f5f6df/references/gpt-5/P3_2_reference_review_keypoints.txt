- Proposal: represent human questions as executable programs in a typed LISP-like DSL, with a PCFG over programs and a log-linear model using EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance features.  
- Inference: EIG computed by Bayesian belief updating over board hypotheses; learning via importance sampling over PCFG-generated programs.  
- Evaluation: leave-one-context-out prediction of human questions and qualitative synthesis of novel questions.  

Strengths:
- Compositional formulation: executable-program representation gives precise semantics and generativity.  
- Interpretable objectives: mixture of information gain and complexity yields a measurable cognitive trade-off.  
- Empirical signal: model outperforms ablations; complexity term shown to be critical.  
- Productivity: grammar can synthesize unseen, human-like questions.  
- Cross-disciplinary relevance: connects cognitive models, active learning, program induction, and QA semantics.  

Weaknesses:
- Heavy handcrafting/domain specificity: DSL and grammar engineered for this task, ambiguous NL mappings excluded, no second-domain test.  
- Unclear role of EIG: information-agnostic model attains similar correlation, weakening claims about EIG’s centrality.  
- Approximate inference concerns: importance sampling with uniform PCFG proposal may have high variance; no ESS/variance/convergence diagnostics; dropped contexts/questions suggest scalability issues.  
- Evaluation gaps: novel-question assessment lacks human judgments; baselines limited to ablations and miss simple retrieval/heuristic comparisons.  
- Utility misspecification: utility modeled only as EIG + global complexity, ignoring answerability, respondent cost, and pragmatic factors; relevance filter seems ad hoc.  
- Reproducibility issues: key implementation details (grammar, hypothesis enumeration, NL-to-program mapping, equivalence detection) deferred to supplements or prior work.  

Quality:
- Technically sound but needs more rigorous reporting on estimation/inference (variance, sensitivity, regularization) and quantification of dropped data bias.  

Clarity:
- Generally well written but relies heavily on supplemental material; core grammar, EIG schematic, and concrete NL-program mappings should be included.  

Originality:
- Moderate: combines known ideas (questions-as-programs, EIG) but contributes an integrated generative question model with emphasis on complexity.  

Significance:
- Promising but limited by domain specificity and evaluation scope; broader applicability currently unproven.  

Reasons to accept:
- Elegant compositional framework able to synthesize nontrivial queries.  
- Empirical evidence that complexity constraints are important for modeling human question choice.  
- Bridges cognitive modeling and ML with executable semantics and probabilistic learning.  

Reasons to reject:
- Domain-specific, hand-engineered grammar and excluded items undermine generality.  
- Weak empirical support for EIG’s central role; complexity/type priors dominate.  
- Insufficient diagnostics for approximate inference and scalability concerns.  

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report ESS/variance of importance sampling, runtime, sensitivity to sample size, convergence diagnostics, and regularization.  
- Provide inter-annotator agreement and guidelines for NL-to-program mapping; test robustness to alternate parses.  
- Add baselines: nearest-neighbor retrieval, beam search EIG+MDL heuristics, learned PCFG rule probs, and simple neural sequence models over programs.  
- Obtain human evaluations of novelty/usefulness for sampled questions.  
- Demonstrate portability to a second domain or larger hypothesis space and clarify reusable domain knowledge.  
- Analyze marginal contribution of EIG per question (e.g., partial correlations) and characterize when EIG helps or hurts.
