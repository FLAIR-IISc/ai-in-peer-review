This paper proposes a framework for modeling human question asking as program generation, with the core idea being that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed, yield an answer within a given world state. The authors evaluate this framework on a dataset of questions asked during a "Battleship"-like game, demonstrating that their model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

While the idea of framing question asking as program synthesis is conceptually interesting and aligns with cognitive science perspectives on compositionality and computability, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant advance over existing approaches. The reliance on a specific game context (Battleship) and a relatively small dataset raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. More importantly, the paper doesn't sufficiently establish *why* this program-based approach is superior to simpler, more established methods for modeling question asking – e.g., probabilistic models directly over natural language questions, or approaches focused on information gain without the intermediary of program synthesis. 

The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the domain-specificity of the grammar and the need for initial domain knowledge. However, they downplay the practical challenges of scaling this approach to more complex domains. The current grammar, while capable of representing the observed questions, seems specifically tailored to the Battleship game and requires significant engineering effort. A more compelling demonstration would involve applying this framework to a different domain with minimal modification.

The experimental evaluation, while thorough, largely focuses on predicting question frequencies within the dataset. Generating novel questions is presented qualitatively, and while the examples are reasonable, a quantitative evaluation of their quality and usefulness (compared to human-generated questions) is lacking. The comparison against alternative models (lesion studies) is helpful in identifying the importance of different features, but these alternatives are all variations *within* the proposed framework – they don’t compare against fundamentally different approaches to question modeling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Conceptualization:** The idea of framing question asking as program generation offers a fresh perspective.
*   **Compositionality & Computability:**  The emphasis on these cognitive principles is well-motivated.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The model demonstrates a reasonable ability to predict question frequencies.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Domain Specificity:** The reliance on the Battleship game limits generalizability.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Established Methods:** The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate an advantage over simpler baselines.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** Extending this framework to more complex domains poses significant challenges.
*   **Qualitative Evaluation of Novel Question Generation:**  A quantitative assessment is needed.
*   **Limited Dataset Size:** The relatively small size of the dataset may limit the robustness of the findings.
*   **Complexity of Implementation:** The need for a specialized grammar and program execution engine adds significant overhead.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel, cognitively-motivated approach to question asking.
*   The empirical results provide some support for the proposed framework.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The domain specificity and limited generalizability of the findings.
*   The lack of a convincing comparison to established methods.
*   The scalability concerns and implementation complexity.
*   The qualitative nature of the novel question generation evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the core idea is interesting, the paper doesn’t provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate a significant contribution to the field. The reliance on a specific game, the lack of comparison to simpler baselines, and the scalability concerns raise serious doubts about the practical impact of this work. The authors need to either demonstrate broader applicability or provide a more compelling argument for why this program-based approach is superior to existing methods. I believe this paper is more appropriate for a cognitive science venue than NIPS.
