This paper presents a fascinating and ambitious framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea – representing questions as executable programs that query a world state – is genuinely novel and offers a compelling perspective on the cognitive processes underlying information seeking. This is a significant departure from typical active learning approaches that focus on simple label requests and aligns well with observations of human learning which often involves richer, more targeted queries. The authors successfully demonstrate the feasibility of their approach by predicting human question frequencies in a “Battleship”-like game and generating novel, plausible questions.

The strength of this work lies in its boldness and conceptual leap. The authors aren’t incrementally improving existing methods; they’re proposing a fundamentally different way to think about question asking. The probabilistic model, while complex, is well-motivated and the incorporation of features like informativeness, complexity, and answer type demonstrates a careful consideration of the problem. The experimental results, while not exhaustive, provide encouraging evidence that the model captures aspects of human behavior. The generated questions, as shown in Figure 3, are surprisingly human-like. 

However, the paper isn’t without its limitations. The grammar, while sufficient for the current domain, feels somewhat hand-crafted and its generalizability to other scenarios isn’t fully explored. The computational complexity of the model is also a concern – the reliance on importance sampling to approximate the normalizing constant suggests potential scalability issues. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of computational efficiency would be beneficial. The evaluation is also primarily focused on prediction accuracy within the context of the game; exploring how well the generated questions would actually *aid* a human player in solving the game (e.g., reducing the number of turns) would strengthen the results. Furthermore, the reliance on a pre-defined world state and rules simplifies the problem; real-world question asking often operates in much more ambiguous and open-ended environments. The statement that the grammar *can* represent all questions in the dataset is true, but the reliance on lambda expressions and set operators feels somewhat at odds with the goal of mimicking human intuition – do humans genuinely reason about questions in this way?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The program generation framework is a truly novel approach to modeling question asking, offering a fundamentally new perspective.
*   **Strong Conceptual Foundation:** The connection between compositionality, computability, and human question asking is well-articulated and convincingly argued.
*   **Promising Results:** The model demonstrates an ability to predict human questions and generate plausible novel questions, indicating its potential.
*   **Bold Approach:** The paper tackles a challenging problem with an ambitious and potentially impactful approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The model’s computational cost may limit its scalability.
*   **Domain Specificity:** The grammar and evaluation are tied to a specific game domain, raising concerns about generalizability.
*   **Limited Evaluation:** The evaluation primarily focuses on prediction accuracy, lacking a direct measure of question usefulness. 
*   **Hand-Crafted Elements:** Some aspects of the grammar appear hand-crafted, reducing the model’s elegance and generalizability.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, I strongly lean towards acceptance. The paper’s novelty and boldness are compelling. While the methodology isn’t perfect, the authors have presented a significant conceptual advance with promising preliminary results. The paper has the potential to open up new avenues of research in active learning, cognitive science, and AI. I believe that the potential benefits of this work outweigh its current shortcomings. The authors should be encouraged to address the scalability concerns and explore the generalizability of their approach in future work.
