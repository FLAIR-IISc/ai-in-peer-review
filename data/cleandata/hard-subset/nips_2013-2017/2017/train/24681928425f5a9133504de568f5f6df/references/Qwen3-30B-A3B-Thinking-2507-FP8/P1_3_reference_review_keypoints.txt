- Proposes modeling human question asking as program generation: questions are compositional programs evaluated on world states.  
- Collected a dataset of 605 natural-language questions from 40 players in a Battleship-like information-search game.  
- Develops a probabilistic, log-linear model with features for informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity (grammar length), answer type, and relevance.  
- Uses a grammar-based question generation framework to represent and sample programmatic questions.  
- Evaluated with leave-one-out cross-validation across 16 contexts and compared against ablated models.  
- Model predicts human question frequencies with strong performance (Spearman correlation ≈ 0.64) and generates novel, human-like questions.  
- Technical approach is sound, well-structured, and appropriately evaluated.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized; supplementary materials detail grammar and features.  
- Original contribution: framing question asking as program synthesis distinct from prior VQG and dialog systems.  
- Significance: advances modeling of goal-directed, context-sensitive question asking and potential for richer active learning systems.  
- Strengths: novel theoretical framework, solid dataset and evaluation, strong empirical results, generation of novel questions, ablation comparisons.  
- Weaknesses: domain-specific (Battleship), lacks natural language generation component, contains domain-specific grammar elements, no discussion of scalability or computational complexity.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — technically sound, original, significant, and likely to inspire follow-up work.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, strong empirical validation, capability to generate novel questions, theoretical grounding, rigorous ablations.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited domain generality, program vs. text gap, no demonstrated scalability to complex domains.
