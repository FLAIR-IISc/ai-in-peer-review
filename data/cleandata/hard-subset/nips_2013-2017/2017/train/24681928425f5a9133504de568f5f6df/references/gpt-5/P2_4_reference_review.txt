Summary
The paper proposes a computational framework that treats question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a small, typed, compositional language (lambda-calculus/LISP style). A probabilistic, log-linear model over programs is defined with features for informativeness (expected information gain over a Bayesian hypothesis space), complexity (negative log prior from a probabilistic grammar), answer type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and a relevance filter. Parameters are learned to match human question frequencies in a Battleship-like information-search game (605 questions across 18 contexts; 16 used in modeling due to tractability). The model predicts the distribution of human questions in held-out contexts and can sample novel, human-like questions. Ablations suggest complexity and answer-type features are important; informativeness contributes by log-likelihood but less clearly by rank correlation.

Relation to prior work
The work intersects active learning (beyond label queries), formal semantics of questions as partitions, program induction/LOT, and question generation (dialog/VQG). Unlike goal-directed dialog systems and neural VQG trained on large corpora, this model emphasizes compositional semantics and Bayesian experimental design to predict human question choices in a new, small-data task. Modeling questions as programs is known in QA/semantic parsing, but applying this to question generation with EIG and predicting human distributions is a novel combination.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent: a PCFG defines the space; a log-linear model reweights programs via features; EIG is computed over a well-specified hypothesis space; training proceeds via gradient ascent with importance sampling. However:
  - Estimation relies on a fixed proposal q from the grammar; both the partition function and expected feature values are approximated from 150k samples. There is limited analysis of variance/consistency of these estimates or sensitivity to q. If the learned model diverges from q, importance sampling can be high-variance.
  - The complexity feature is −log q(x), meaning the learned distribution is effectively q(x) reweighted by exp(−θ·features). This is reasonable, but the choice of q strongly shapes both the complexity prior and the proposal; potential bias deserves analysis.
  - The EIG computation is principled, but expensive; two contexts and a rule (draw) were dropped, along with 14 human questions, due to computational intractability. This raises concerns about scalability and completeness.
  - Evaluation: Log-likelihood comparisons are approximate; bootstrapping Z is helpful, but confidence intervals on the main metrics (LL, Spearman) and sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., length cap, grammar design) are not reported. The full model’s average Spearman ρ = 0.64 is solid, but the information-agnostic model’s ρ = 0.65 is slightly higher, complicating the claim that informativeness is a key driver (though LL does favor the full model). Clarification on this discrepancy is needed.
- Completeness and honesty: The paper is candid about limitations (domain specificity, lack of NL interface). Some implementation details (full grammar, hypothesis space sizes, prior choices, sampling diagnostics) seem to be in the supplement; providing more in the main paper would aid reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and uses concrete examples. The modeling components are explained intuitively; figures (referenced) appear informative. Some references are garbled; ensure citations are complete. Key implementation details (full grammar, exact hypothesis enumeration/sampling, computational costs) should be summarized more in the main text to permit reproduction. A brief discussion of how equivalence between questions is assessed (mutual information over answer partitions) is good; adding an algorithmic outline would help.

Originality
Moderately high. Representing questions as programs is not new in QA/semantic parsing, and EIG is standard in Bayesian experimental design; the novelty lies in (a) using a typed program space to model and generate human questions, (b) jointly balancing informativeness with compositional complexity and answer-type priors, and (c) demonstrating predictive power on a small, open-ended dataset. This is a distinctive angle relative to neural question generation and dialog work.

Significance
Conceptually significant for bridging active learning, program induction, and cognitive models of question asking. Practically, the scope is limited: a single synthetic domain, small dataset, and reliance on hand-crafted grammar/semantics limit immediate ML impact. The work could influence future systems that ask informative, compositional questions, but demonstrations beyond the Battleship domain and towards natural language (or additional domains with minimal re-engineering) would strengthen the case.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of questions as executable programs with a generative grammar.
- Integration of EIG with a learned complexity prior explains human preferences for concise, semantically rich questions.
- Predicts human question distributions in held-out contexts; ablations highlight the critical role of complexity and answer-type priors.
- Generates novel, plausibly human-like questions without large paired datasets; uses semantic equivalence to ensure novelty.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar and hypothesis space; unclear portability to new domains.
- Scalability issues: dropping two contexts and 14 questions; costly EIG computations; no discussion of more scalable inference (e.g., SMC/MCMC over programs, annealed/bridge sampling for Z).
- Importance sampling from the same PCFG that defines the complexity prior risks bias/high variance; no diagnostics or alternatives presented.
- Mixed evidence for informativeness: correlation favors information-agnostic model; claims should be reconciled.
- No natural language grounding; the mapping from text to programs is assumed rather than learned.
- Limited quantitative evaluation of “human-likeness” of generated questions (no human judgments).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide diagnostics for the importance sampler (effective sample size, variance) and consider more robust Z estimation (AIS/bridge sampling).
- Analyze sensitivity to the grammar prior q and the complexity feature; report results with alternative priors/length penalties.
- Clarify the informativeness result: why does ρ degrade when including EIG? Provide per-context analysis and LL vs. ρ trade-offs.
- Attempt a second domain with minimal new engineering to demonstrate portability.
- Add a small NL mapping (even heuristic semantic parser) or show how the approach interfaces with existing parsers.
- Include human evaluation of generated questions’ usefulness/naturalness.

Recommendation
Borderline reject for NeurIPS main track. The paper is elegant and thought-provoking with solid cognitive modeling, but the current scope (single engineered domain, scalability limitations, approximate evaluation, and limited ML impact) falls short of the NeurIPS bar. I encourage resubmission with stronger demonstrations of scalability and portability, improved inference, and clearer evidence that informativeness contributes beyond a well-shaped complexity prior.
