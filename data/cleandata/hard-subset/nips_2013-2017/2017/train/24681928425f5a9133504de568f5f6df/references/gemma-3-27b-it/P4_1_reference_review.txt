This paper proposes a novel framework for understanding human question asking by modeling questions as programs. The core idea is that questions can be represented as syntactically well-formed programs that, when executed, yield an answer based on a given world state. The authors demonstrate this approach by applying it to a dataset of questions collected during a game where players try to identify hidden ships on a grid. They show that their probabilistic model, leveraging features like informativeness and complexity, can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper builds upon existing work in active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation. However, it distinguishes itself by focusing on the *generation* of questions, rather than answering them, and by representing questions at a more fundamental, programmatic level. Unlike traditional active learning, which typically asks for simple labels, and dialog systems that rely on canned responses, this framework aims to capture the richness and creativity of human questioning. It also differentiates itself from visual question generation by requiring no pre-existing image-question pairs; questions are generated in novel scenarios. The connection to formal linguistics (compositionality, partitions over possible worlds) and cognitive science (language of thought) provides a strong theoretical foundation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of modeling questions as programs is a significant departure from existing approaches and offers a compelling new perspective on the problem of question asking.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The probabilistic model appears well-motivated and incorporates relevant features (informativeness, complexity, answer type). The use of a grammar-based approach to ensure syntactic validity and facilitate program generation is sensible.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation on the collected dataset demonstrates the model's ability to predict human question frequencies and generate plausible novel questions. The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the importance of different features.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the key concepts clearly. The motivation is well-articulated, and the connection to related work is explicitly discussed.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The work draws upon insights from linguistics and cognitive science, providing a strong theoretical foundation for the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge the domain specificity of their grammar, it remains a limitation. The extent to which the approach can be generalized to other domains without substantial modifications is unclear.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The optimization process, involving importance sampling, is computationally expensive, as noted by the authors. This may hinder scalability to larger datasets or more complex grammars.
*   **Evaluation Limitations:** Although the generated questions appear plausible, a more rigorous evaluation of their quality and usefulness would be beneficial. This could involve human evaluation of the generated questions or assessing their impact on learning performance in the game.
*   **Feature Engineering:** The features used in the model (informativeness, complexity, answer type) are relatively hand-engineered. Exploring the use of learned representations or end-to-end learning approaches could potentially improve performance.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While ablation studies are performed, comparison with simpler baselines beyond the feature ablation could strengthen the results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to modeling question asking as program generation.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the model’s ability to predict human question frequencies and generate plausible novel questions.
*   The work is grounded in relevant theoretical frameworks from linguistics and cognitive science.
*   The potential impact of this work on active learning and AI dialog systems is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The domain specificity of the grammar and the computational complexity of the optimization process are limitations.
*   The evaluation of generated questions could be more rigorous.
*   The reliance on hand-engineered features may limit the scalability and generalizability of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and promising approach to modeling human question asking. While some limitations exist, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field and has the potential to inspire further research in active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive science. I recommend acceptance.
