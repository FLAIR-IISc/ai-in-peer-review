This paper proposes a novel framework for understanding human question asking by modeling questions as programs. The central idea is compelling: framing questions as executable programs that map world states to answers offers a powerful lens for analyzing their compositionality, computability, and informativeness. The authors evaluate this framework using a dataset of questions collected during a Battleship-like game, demonstrating that their probabilistic model can predict human question frequencies and even synthesize novel, human-like questions.

The paper is well-motivated and connects to a rich body of work in active learning, cognitive science, and natural language processing. The authors effectively highlight the limitations of existing approaches – simplistic queries in active learning, canned responses in dialog systems, and the data hunger of deep learning methods for question generation – and position their work as a step towards more human-like question asking in machines. The connection to linguistic theories of compositionality and the ‘language of thought’ is insightful. The description of the dataset is clear, and the grounding in a game provides a concrete context for evaluation. The technical details, including the grammar and probabilistic model, are presented reasonably well, although some aspects (e.g., the optimization procedure) could benefit from more elaboration.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The program-as-question framework is a genuinely new and promising approach to modeling human question asking.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The work is well-grounded in theories from linguistics and cognitive science, providing a strong theoretical justification for the approach.
* **Empirical validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that the model can predict human question frequencies and generate plausible questions, suggesting that it captures important aspects of human question asking behavior.
* **Clear writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core ideas and methodology.
* **Well-defined problem:** Focusing on a specific game domain allows for a concrete and well-defined evaluation of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the model:** While the grammar is clearly defined, the overall model with its multiple features (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance) feels somewhat complex and lacks a clear justification for *why* these specific features were chosen. The ablation study helps, but a more principled approach to feature selection would be beneficial.
* **Computational cost:** The importance sampling used for approximate inference appears computationally expensive, and the limitations imposed on question length (100 functions) suggest potential scalability issues.
* **Domain specificity:** The model is tailored to the Battleship game and the extent to which it generalizes to other domains is unclear. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but more discussion of potential adaptation strategies would be valuable.
* **Limited baselines:** The comparison to alternative models is somewhat limited. While the ablation study is helpful, comparing against simpler models (e.g., an n-gram model of question sequences) would provide a stronger baseline.
* **Evaluation metrics:** The evaluation relies heavily on log-likelihood, which is a useful metric, but doesn't fully capture the *quality* of the generated questions. More human-centered evaluation metrics (e.g., asking humans to rate the helpfulness or clarity of the generated questions) would strengthen the empirical validation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling human question asking.
* The experimental results demonstrate that the model can predict human behavior and generate plausible questions.
* The work addresses a challenging problem that is relevant to both AI and cognitive science.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The model is complex and potentially computationally expensive.
* The evaluation is somewhat limited and lacks strong baselines.
* The domain specificity raises concerns about generalizability.
* The justification for the feature set isn’t fully developed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with a novel and promising contribution. While the model has some limitations, the core idea is compelling and the empirical results are encouraging. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses identified above in future work. Specifically, clarifying the feature selection process, investigating scalability, and exploring more robust evaluation metrics would significantly strengthen the paper. A discussion of how the approach might generalize to other domains would also be valuable.
