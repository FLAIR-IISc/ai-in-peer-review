This paper proposes a framework for modeling human question asking as program generation, leveraging compositionality and computability to predict and even synthesize questions within a game context. While the premise is intriguing and potentially valuable for advancing active learning beyond simple label requests, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing as a NIPS submission. My primary concern stems from the limited scope of the evaluation and the lack of compelling evidence demonstrating a significant advance over existing approaches.

The core idea – representing questions as programs – isn’t entirely novel. The connection to program synthesis is acknowledged, but the paper doesn't deeply engage with this existing literature, nor does it convincingly demonstrate that this program-centric approach offers a substantial advantage over more traditional methods for modeling question asking (e.g., those based on information gain or Bayesian optimization). The authors claim this formalism offers a “new approach”, but the implemented model largely relies on features that are standard in active learning, such as informativeness (EIG), albeit framed within the program generation context.

The experimental evaluation, centered around the “Battleship”-like game, is a significant weakness. The dataset, while novel, appears quite constrained. The success of the model in predicting human questions feels largely attributable to the limited action space and the explicit modeling of game-specific knowledge. The authors acknowledge that the grammar is tailored to this domain and express uncertainty about the generalizability of the approach, further undermining its significance. The comparison to alternative models, while present, isn’t comprehensive enough to isolate the benefits of the proposed program-based representation. The ablation study is helpful, but the dominance of complexity as a key feature seems counterintuitive – a focus on complexity rather than pure informativeness raises concerns about the model’s alignment with true human reasoning.

Clarity is generally good; the paper is well-written and the motivation is clearly presented. However, the numerous supplementary materials (tables, rules) suggest a level of complexity that could have been streamlined within the main text. The notation is also occasionally dense.

Originality lies in the specific combination of program synthesis, probabilistic modeling, and a human question-asking dataset. However, the core components – probabilistic grammars, information gain – are not new. The key contribution seems to be applying these techniques to the specific problem of modeling human questions in this game, rather than presenting a fundamentally new algorithmic or theoretical breakthrough.

Regarding significance, the paper’s impact is unclear. While the idea of modeling questions as programs is interesting, the limited scope of the evaluation prevents a strong claim about its broader applicability. The generated questions, while “human-like” according to the authors, aren’t demonstrably superior to questions generated by existing techniques or, frankly, by a human playing the game. The claim that this work will enable "more human-like question asking in machines" feels overstated given the current results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of program synthesis to model human question asking.
*   Creation of a new dataset of human questions in a structured environment.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict and generate plausible questions within the defined domain.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of the evaluation and potential lack of generalizability.
*   Lack of a convincing demonstration that the program-based representation offers a significant advantage over existing approaches.
*   Reliance on domain-specific knowledge and a tailored grammar.
*   The crucial role of the complexity feature is questionable.
*   Limited discussion of the connection to the broader program synthesis literature.



Overall, I believe this paper is not ready for NIPS. While the ideas presented are interesting and the work is technically sound, the limited scope, lack of compelling evidence of significant improvement, and domain specificity outweigh the potential benefits. I recommend rejection. A revised version focusing on a more general framework, a more thorough comparison to existing methods, and a demonstration of broader applicability would be more suitable for consideration.
