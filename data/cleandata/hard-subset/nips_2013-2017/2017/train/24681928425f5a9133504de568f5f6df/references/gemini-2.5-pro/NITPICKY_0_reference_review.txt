**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a computational framework for modeling human goal-directed question asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. When executed on a given world state (e.g., a game board configuration), these programs produce an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic model over this program space, defined by a context-free grammar. The probability of a given question is determined by a log-linear model incorporating features for the question's expected information gain (EIG), complexity, and answer type. The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game. The authors show that their model can predict the frequency of human questions in held-out contexts and generate novel, contextually relevant questions that were not observed in the training data. This work positions itself as an advancement over traditional active learning (which uses simple queries) and data-intensive deep learning models for dialogue, aiming for a more cognitive and generalizable model of inquiry.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Framing:** The central contribution—modeling rich, goal-directed question asking as program synthesis—is a compelling and significant idea. It offers a path to move beyond the simple label requests typical in active learning and provides a formal, interpretable framework for a complex cognitive ability. This approach has the potential to influence future work in active learning, human-computer interaction, and computational cognitive science.

2.  **Impressive Qualitative Results:** The model's ability to generate novel, complex, and contextually appropriate questions (as shown in Figure 3) is a strong demonstration of its generative power. Questions like "What is the column of the top left of the tiles that have the color of the bottom right corner of the board?" showcase a level of creativity and compositionality that is lacking in many contemporary question-generation systems.

3.  **Principled Model Structure:** The model is built on well-established principles from cognitive science and linguistics, namely compositionality and computability. The use of a probabilistic model that balances informativeness (EIG) against complexity is well-motivated and intuitive.

**Weaknesses**

While the high-level idea is commendable, the paper suffers from numerous methodological and presentational issues that undermine the credibility of its claims.

1.  **Systematic Exclusion of Data:** The authors repeatedly report excluding data for reasons of computational convenience. Footnote 4 states that 14 questions were removed because they involved a rule that led to "exponentially complex computations." Footnote 7 reveals that two entire experimental contexts (contexts 1 and 2) were dropped because they had "especially large hypothesis spaces." This practice of curating the dataset to fit the model's limitations is a serious methodological flaw. It raises questions about the model's scalability and generalizability. A robust model should be capable of handling the complexities of the data, not avoiding them.

2.  **Contradictory and Unexplained Quantitative Results:** The quantitative evaluation contains a critical, unaddressed issue. In the analysis of Spearman's rank correlation, the "information-agnostic model" (which lacks EIG features) achieves a correlation of 0.65, slightly *outperforming* the full model's 0.64. This result directly contradicts the paper's central narrative that informativeness is a key component driving human question asking. The authors fail to comment on or explain this finding, which significantly weakens their conclusions about the importance of EIG within their framework.

3.  **Lack of Technical Detail and Reproducibility:** The paper is not self-contained. The grammar, which is the foundational structure defining the entire space of questions, is relegated to the supplementary materials (Tables SI-1 and SI-2). Without these rules, it is impossible for a reader to fully grasp the model's representational capacity or evaluate its design choices. Furthermore, the "relevance" feature `f_∅(x)` is poorly described as a "filter" that marks questions referencing the game board with a "b marker," which feels like an ad-hoc solution to a grammar that over-generates irrelevant expressions.

4.  **Questionable Approximation Methods:** The model relies on importance sampling with a proposal distribution derived from a uniform PCFG to approximate the intractable partition function and gradients. This is a notoriously high-variance estimation technique, yet the authors provide no analysis of the approximation quality or its stability. The choice of 150,000 samples for the approximation appears arbitrary.

5.  **Numerous Formatting and Professionalism Issues:** The manuscript is unpolished and contains several errors that should have been caught before submission.
    *   The reference list is poorly formatted and incomplete. References [12] and [22] are truncated, author name conventions are inconsistent (e.g., "J.A. Fodor" vs. "Bordes, A."), and formatting varies significantly between entries.
    *   A prominent footer on the first page ("1st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)...") is highly inappropriate for a manuscript under review.
    *   Footnote 1 provides a GitHub link, which is a potential violation of the double-blind review process.
    *   There are typographical errors in the text (e.g., "less then 4 blocks" instead of "less than").

**Recommendation: Reject**

Arguments for acceptance:
*   The paper introduces a novel and promising conceptual framework for question asking.
*   The qualitative examples of generated questions are compelling.

Arguments against acceptance:
*   The systematic exclusion of computationally challenging data points constitutes a major methodological weakness.
*   A key quantitative result (the rank correlation) contradicts the paper's main claims, and this contradiction is ignored by the authors.
*   The paper is not self-contained, with the core grammar relegated to the supplement.
*   The manuscript suffers from a substantial number of formatting errors, incomplete references, and other signs of carelessness, failing to meet the professional standards expected for a top-tier conference like NIPS.

While the core idea is interesting, the execution, evaluation, and presentation are deeply flawed. The unaddressed contradiction in the results and the practice of cherry-picking data are particularly concerning. The paper, in its current state, does not provide a sufficiently rigorous or convincing piece of scientific work.
