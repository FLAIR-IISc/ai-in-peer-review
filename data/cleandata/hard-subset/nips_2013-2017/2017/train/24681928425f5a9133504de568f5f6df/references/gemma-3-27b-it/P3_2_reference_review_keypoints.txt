- Overall: Novel, compelling framework modeling questions as programs; strong conceptual contribution that warrants acceptance.

- Main idea: Formalizes questions as executable programs and frames question asking as program synthesis to predict and generate human questions.

- Application/dataset: Demonstrated on a “Battleship”-like dataset, showing prediction of question frequencies and generation of plausible novel questions.

- Relation to prior work: Links to active learning, dialog systems, VQG limits, and cognitive-science ideas of compositionality and formal question semantics.

- Strength — Novelty: Modeling questions as programs is highly innovative and opens new research directions.

- Strength — Motivation: Well-motivated by limitations of existing active learning and question-generation approaches.

- Strength — Theory: Solid theoretical grounding in compositionality, computability, and Bayesian belief updating.

- Strength — Empirical: Model captures human question patterns and outperforms ablations; generated questions look promising.

- Strength — Writing: Clear, well-written presentation with accessible cognitive-science connections.

- Weakness — Evaluation scope: Experiments confined to a single, relatively constrained domain; generalizability untested.

- Weakness — Dataset size: Small number of questions per context limits statistical power and risks overfitting.

- Weakness — Grammar complexity: Grammar is complex and simplifying assumptions obscure trade-offs between expressiveness and tractability.

- Weakness — Importance sampling: Reliance on importance sampling for normalization may introduce variance and sensitivity in likelihood estimates.

- Suggestions — Dataset details: Provide more dataset statistics (question length, type distribution, excluded examples).

- Suggestions — Feature analysis: Report learned feature weights to show which factors drive human-like question choice.

- Suggestions — Generated question eval: Conduct systematic human evaluations of generated questions’ relevance and informativeness.

- Suggestions — Generalization: Describe concrete strategies for adapting the grammar and model to other domains and knowledge representations.

- For acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound framework with promising empirical results and potential impact on multiple fields.

- Against acceptance: Limited, small-scale evaluation and potential estimation issues from importance sampling.

- Recommendation: Accept, with encouragement to address evaluation, dataset, grammar, and importance-sampling concerns in future work.
