## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and compelling framework for understanding human question asking by modeling questions as programs. The approach is well-motivated, grounded in cognitive science principles, and demonstrates promising results on a challenging dataset. While the experimental evaluation is relatively modest, the conceptual contribution is significant and warrants acceptance to NIPS.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper posits that human questions can be formalized as programs that, when executed on a world state, yield an answer. This reframes question asking as a program synthesis problem.  The authors leverage this insight to build a probabilistic model that predicts which questions humans will ask in a “Battleship”-like game context. They demonstrate their model’s ability to both predict question frequencies and generate novel, plausible questions.

This work builds upon several areas. It connects to active learning, by moving beyond simple label requests towards more expressive queries. It also draws from the field of dialog systems, but distinguishes itself by focusing on *goal-directed* question asking rather than open-ended conversation. The authors appropriately highlight the limitations of existing visual question generation (VQG) systems that require large datasets and lack the flexibility of human questioning. Crucially, the paper connects to cognitive science research on compositionality and the "language of thought," bringing these theoretical perspectives into a computational framework.  The approach also echoes work in formalizing question meaning through partitions over possible worlds.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The core idea of modeling questions as programs is highly innovative. It provides a powerful lens for understanding the underlying cognitive processes involved in question asking and opens up new avenues for research in active learning and AI.
* **Strong Motivation:**  The authors effectively motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of existing active learning methods and the richness of human question asking.
* **Sound Theoretical Framework:** The grounding in compositionality, computability, and Bayesian belief updating provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed model.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the human question dataset demonstrate that the model can capture patterns in human question asking and generate plausible new questions. The strong performance compared to ablation studies reinforces the importance of the key components of the model.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally very well written and clearly explains the technical details of the approach. The connection to cognitive science principles is presented in an accessible manner.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is primarily focused on a single dataset (the “Battleship” game). While challenging, this domain is relatively constrained. It would be beneficial to see how the model generalizes to other domains with different knowledge representations and query possibilities.
* **Dataset Limitations:** The data set, while valuable, has a relatively small number of questions per context. This limits the statistical power of the evaluation and could contribute to overfitting.  The authors acknowledge this limitation.
* **Grammar Complexity:** While the grammar is well-defined, it appears somewhat complex. The authors mention simplifying assumptions (e.g., excluding questions asking for visual demonstrations) to manage computational complexity.  It would be helpful to discuss the trade-offs between grammar expressiveness and computational tractability in more detail.
* **Importance Sampling Challenges:** The reliance on importance sampling to approximate the normalizing constant introduces potential sources of error and variance in the estimated likelihoods. While the authors report bootstrapping to address this, the sensitivity to the importance sampling procedure should be further investigated.

**4. Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

* **Section 3: Dataset Details:** It would be helpful to provide more detailed statistics about the dataset, such as the average question length, the distribution of question types, and examples of questions that were excluded and why.
* **Section 4.5: Feature Importance:** While the ablation studies demonstrate the importance of different features, it would be insightful to analyze the learned weights of these features. This could reveal which aspects of question informativeness, complexity, and answer type are most strongly correlated with human behavior.
* **Novel Question Generation:** The generated questions in Figure 3 are promising. However, a more systematic evaluation of the quality and usefulness of these questions would be beneficial. Could human participants judge the relevance and informativeness of the generated questions in specific contexts?
* **Future Work:** The authors mention extending the approach to new domains. It would be valuable to discuss specific strategies for adapting the grammar and probabilistic model to different knowledge representations and query types.



**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound framework for understanding human question asking.
* The experimental results demonstrate the feasibility and promise of the approach.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the fields of active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive science.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The evaluation is limited to a single dataset.
* The dataset is relatively small, which could limit the statistical power of the results.
* The reliance on importance sampling introduces potential sources of error.

**6. Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept.** Despite the limitations of the evaluation, the conceptual contribution of this paper is significant enough to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The authors have provided a compelling framework for understanding human question asking that has the potential to inspire future research in several areas. The strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses in future work, particularly by exploring the generalizability of the approach to other domains.
