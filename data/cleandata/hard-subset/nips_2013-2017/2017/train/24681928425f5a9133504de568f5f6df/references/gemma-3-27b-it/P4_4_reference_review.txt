This paper proposes a novel framework for understanding human question asking by modeling questions as programs. The core idea is that questions can be represented as syntactically well-formed programs that, when executed on a world state, produce an answer. This recasts question asking as a program synthesis problem, enabling a probabilistic model to predict and generate human-like questions within a game context (Battleship). The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of human questions collected during gameplay, demonstrating its ability to predict question frequencies and generate novel questions.

The work builds upon the established field of active learning, differentiating itself by moving beyond simple label requests to richer, more nuanced queries. It also connects to work in dialog systems and visual question generation, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches in capturing the goal-directed and compositional nature of human questioning. The paper appropriately references relevant work, including studies on linguistic semantics and cognitive science relating to compositionality and the language of thought.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The framing of question asking as program generation is a genuinely novel and insightful approach. It provides a strong theoretical foundation and opens up new avenues for modeling and understanding human inquiry.
* **Technical Soundness:** The probabilistic model, utilizing a context-free grammar and importance sampling for optimization, is well-defined and appears technically sound. The use of Expected Information Gain as a feature demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of question informativeness.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation on the human-generated question dataset provides valuable insights into the model's performance. The quantitative results (log-likelihood comparisons) and qualitative examples of generated questions are compelling. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the importance of each feature.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology. The connection between the theoretical framework and the empirical results is well-articulated.
* **Significance:**  This work has the potential to significantly impact the field of active learning by broadening the scope of possible queries. It could also lead to more human-like dialog systems and AI agents capable of engaging in more meaningful interactions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity:** While the grammar incorporates some domain-general elements, the approach is still tailored to the Battleship game.  Extending it to other domains might require substantial modifications and knowledge engineering. The authors acknowledge this limitation.
* **Grammar Complexity:** Although the grammar is sufficient to represent the observed questions, it may not be scalable to more complex domains or natural language.  The reliance on a hand-crafted grammar could be a bottleneck.
* **Computational Cost:** The importance sampling method, while necessary due to the intractable normalization constant, introduces computational cost.  The authors mention optimization strategies, but further discussion of scalability would be beneficial.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to alternative models is comprehensive in terms of feature ablation, but lacks comparison to state-of-the-art approaches in related fields like question generation or dialog management. A comparison with simpler baselines beyond feature ablation might also be useful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling human question asking.
* The technical implementation is sound and the empirical evaluation is well-executed.
* The results demonstrate the model's ability to both predict and generate human-like questions.
* The work has the potential to significantly advance the field of active learning and human-computer interaction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The domain specificity of the approach may limit its generalizability.
* The computational cost of the inference procedure could be a concern for large-scale applications.
* The lack of comparison to broader baselines in related fields.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a compelling and innovative approach to modeling human question asking. The strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses, and it makes a valuable contribution to the field. The novelty of the idea and the thorough empirical evaluation warrant acceptance. While the domain specificity and computational cost are limitations, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.
