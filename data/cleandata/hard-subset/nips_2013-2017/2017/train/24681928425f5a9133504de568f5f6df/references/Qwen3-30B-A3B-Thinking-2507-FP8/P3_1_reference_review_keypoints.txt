- Summary: Models human question asking by representing questions as executable programs over world states, evaluated on a Battleship-like game using features for informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance.

- Strength: Novel conceptual framework modeling questions as compositional, executable programs linking cognitive science and computational linguistics.

- Strength: Strong theoretical grounding in compositionality, language of thought, and information theory (Expected Information Gain).

- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation with ablation studies, quantitative log-likelihood metrics, and qualitative generation of novel questions.

- Strength: Demonstrates productivity—grammar-based model generates novel, human-like questions not in training data.

- Strength: Clearly distinguishes this approach from active learning, dialog systems, and neural question generation.

- Weakness: Heavy domain specificity—grammar and features are tailored to Battleship and may require substantial re-engineering for new domains.

- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope—only tested on one game, limiting claims of generalizability.

- Weakness: Natural language gap—the model works on semantic representations, with no concrete method for mapping to/from natural language.

- Weakness: Computational complexity—training involves expensive optimization and sampling that may not scale to larger domains or datasets.

- Weakness: Sparse data challenge—many human questions occur only once, weakening correlation-based fit and analysis.

- Suggestion: Add an analysis or experiment showing how much of the grammar transfers to a different domain to support claims of domain generality.

- Suggestion: Provide a discussion or small experiment on bridging semantic programs to natural language generation/understanding.

- Suggestion: Compare against a simple neural baseline (e.g., LSTM) trained on the same data to highlight advantages over data-driven methods.

- Suggestion: Offer a deeper qualitative analysis of generated novel questions to explain why they are useful or clever.

- Suggestion: Clarify and break down which grammar rules are domain-specific versus domain-general.

- Overall assessment: Strong, well-motivated, interdisciplinary paper that meaningfully advances computational models of human question asking despite limitations.

- Recommendation: Accept—significant contribution with reasonable limitations that suggest future work rather than flaws.
