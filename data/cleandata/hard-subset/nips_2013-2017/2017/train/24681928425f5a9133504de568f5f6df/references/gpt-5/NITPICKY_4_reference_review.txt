Summary
This paper proposes a computational framework for question asking by modeling questions as executable programs over possible worlds. A PCFG/Lambda-calculus–style grammar defines a compositional question space; a log-linear model over program features (expected information gain, complexity via PCFG prior, answer type, and a “relevance” flag) predicts which question a human will ask in a given Battleship-like context. The model is trained by maximum likelihood with expectations approximated via importance sampling from the grammar. Evaluations include leave-one-context-out prediction of human question distributions and qualitative generation of novel questions. Ablations suggest complexity and EIG matter, and the model generates human-like, compositional queries.

Strengths
- Clear, appealing formulation of questions as executable, typed programs, capturing compositionality and enabling principled informativeness computations.
- Sensible feature design (EIG and complexity) providing a rational account of trade-offs observed in human question asking.
- Use of a real open-ended dataset with natural language questions mapped to programs; capability to synthesize novel questions is compelling.
- Ablation study indicates the role of complexity beyond informativeness alone.
- The approach plausibly bridges active learning and semantic parsing/program induction.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Probabilistic formulation and normalization:
  • Equation for the normalizing constant is incorrect. Z = sum_x exp(−E(x)) but the estimator is given as E_q[p(x;θ)/q(x)], which is circular because p(x;θ) itself depends on Z. It should be Z ≈ E_q[exp(−E(x))/q(x)]. This is a serious error.
  • Conditioning on context is not made explicit in the math. All equations define p(x;θ), yet features like EIG depend on the context c. The correct model is p(x|c;θ) with a context-specific normalizer Z(c). The gradient in Eq. 4 should sum over contexts with features f(x,c) and expectations under p(x|c;θ). As written, the optimization objective and gradient are misleading/incomplete.

- Importance sampling and optimization details:
  • A single proposal (150k sampled programs) reused across all iterations and contexts risks high bias/variance, especially given context-dependent EIG and a changing target with θ. No diagnostics (ESS, variance of weights) or convergence checks are provided.
  • The proposal q is the same PCFG used to define the complexity feature fcomp = −log q(x). This double use is acceptable, but the consequences should be analyzed (e.g., whether the learned complexity weight collapses to reweighting the proposal).
  • Limiting programs to ≤100 functions is arbitrary; the impact on coverage and bias is not analyzed.

- Dataset handling and evaluation:
  • Two contexts and 14 questions are dropped; this should be justified with quantitative impact analyses. Removing the (draw C) rule and corresponding questions materially changes the target distribution.
  • Quantitative results are underspecified. Table 1 is referenced but not shown; absolute log-likelihood/perplexity numbers, standard errors, and the magnitude of differences are missing. The “bootstrapped advantage” percentages are not accompanied by CIs or ΔLL magnitudes.
  • Reported correlations are puzzling: the information-agnostic model achieves higher average Spearman (0.65) than the full model (0.64), contradicting the claim that EIG improves predictive quality (while LL might still improve, this discrepancy should be discussed).
  • No comparisons to strong baselines: e.g., RSA-style questioner models, neural semantic parsers with length/complexity regularization, nonparametric program priors, or simpler heuristic baselines (EIG-only, length-only) are not presented quantitatively.

- Equivalence and novelty detection:
  • Using “mutual information of answer distributions” as an equivalence test is ill-specified. Two programs can have identical MI(H;A) yet different partitions; equivalence should require identical answer partitions across hypotheses.
  • Claiming (size Blue) is equivalent to (size Red) is generally false in this task unless symmetry/group averaging is explicitly assumed. This undermines the novelty filtering.

- EIG computation:
  • The hypothesis space size, enumeration/pruning strategy, and computational complexity are not detailed. Dropping contexts suggests scalability issues; no approximate inference strategy (e.g., MCMC over hypotheses) is discussed.

- Modeling choices:
  • The “relevance” feature f∅ is a band-aid for allowing non-board-referential programs in the grammar. It would be cleaner to restrict the grammar or impose a hard constraint during sampling, especially since EIG already downweights irrelevant programs (EIG=0).
  • The answer-type features are coarse; it would be informative to analyze learned weights and their stability across contexts.

- Reproducibility and clarity:
  • Key elements are deferred to supplementary tables (grammar rules), figures are referenced but not included here. Pseudocode for training/inference would help.
  • Many references are malformed or incomplete; several contain typos or missing years/pages. Multiple typographical errors in the text (e.g., “within in”, “less then”), and formatting glitches (“st Conference ...”) detract from clarity.

Originality
Modeling question generation as program synthesis with an EIG-driven log-linear model over a typed grammar is a nice synthesis of ideas from semantic parsing, program induction, and active learning. However, the high-level ingredients (compositional semantics, EIG, PCFG complexity prior) are known; the contribution is in combining them for human question asking and showing predictive value.

Significance
Potentially impactful for both cognitive modeling of questions and for building agents that ask informative queries. However, the empirical scope is narrow (single domain, small dataset) and technical flaws limit confidence in the reported gains. Demonstrating robustness on additional domains and fixing the probabilistic issues would increase significance.

Quality
As noted, the normalization error, context-conditioning omission, underspecified IS procedure, and equivalence criterion are substantive issues. Several ablation and evaluation inconsistencies need resolution.

Clarity
Generally readable at a high level, but marred by mathematical inaccuracies, missing details, and formatting/reference problems.

Pros
- Elegant, compositional representation of questions as programs
- Principled incorporation of informativeness and complexity
- Ability to generate novel, non-trivial questions
- Ablation suggests meaningful trade-offs

Cons
- Incorrect partition function estimator; missing conditioning on context in equations
- Weakly specified and potentially biased importance sampling setup
- Questionable equivalence test; inappropriate equivalence example
- Sparse, inconsistent quantitative reporting; missing baselines
- Domain-specific engineering; dropped contexts/questions
- Numerous formatting and citation errors

Questions for authors
1) Please correct the Z estimator and clarify p(x|c;θ) with context-specific normalization and gradients. Provide pseudocode for training/inference.
2) How many hypotheses per context? How is EIG computed efficiently? Any approximations?
3) Provide full quantitative results (LL, ΔLL, CIs) and reconcile the correlation discrepancy (info-agnostic > full).
4) Justify novelty/equivalence detection rigorously (identical partitions over H) and correct the (size Blue) ≡ (size Red) claim.
5) Add baselines (RSA questioner, heuristic EIG-only, length-only, neural decoder with diversity/length penalty).
6) Analyze sensitivity to the proposal size, sample reuse, and program length cutoff.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The core idea is interesting and promising, but the paper contains critical technical errors, underspecified inference, questionable evaluation choices, and significant presentation/formatting issues. With corrected math, stronger baselines, clearer and complete quantitative results, and improved rigor in novelty/equivalence handling, this work could be competitive in a future submission.
