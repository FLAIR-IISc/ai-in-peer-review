## NIPS 2017 Review: Question Asking as Program Generation

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The authors argue that humans construct questions to directly target critical parameters in a learning task, and propose representing these questions as syntactically well-formed programs that, when executed, yield an answer within a specific world state. They evaluate this model on a dataset of natural language questions collected during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions. The core idea is to bridge the gap between simple active learning queries (e.g., label requests) and the richness of human questioning through a formal, computational lens.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper effectively situates itself within the active learning literature, highlighting the limitations of current approaches that primarily focus on simple queries. It contrasts its work with dialog systems, noting their reliance on pre-defined question sets and lack of flexibility. It also acknowledges related work in visual question generation and computational linguistics, but emphasizes the novelty of its approach in modeling *intentional*, goal-directed question asking in a novel scenario with limited prior examples, a capability lacking in existing systems. The connection to formalizing question meaning as a partition over possible worlds and to concepts as programs is well noted and properly credited.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The central idea of framing question asking as program generation is a significant and original contribution. It offers a compelling and potentially powerful way to represent and model the complexities of human inquiry.
* **Formalization:** The use of a formal grammar (based on lambda calculus and LISP) provides a clear and rigorous framework for representing and manipulating questions.
* **Empirical Validation:** The evaluation on the human question dataset is well-motivated and provides evidence that the model can capture patterns in human question asking behavior. The ability to generate novel, plausible questions is particularly encouraging.
* **Strong Theoretical Grounding:**  The authors connect their work to well-established concepts in linguistics, cognitive science (compositionality), and Bayesian reasoning (information gain).
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity:**  While the authors acknowledge the domain specificity of the grammar, the reliance on the Battleship game limits the generalizability of the approach. Extending this framework to more complex domains will be a significant challenge.
* **Complexity of the Model:**  The model involves several components (grammar, probabilistic model, feature engineering) which, while individually well-explained, contribute to overall complexity.  Simplifying the model or identifying key features would enhance its accessibility.
* **Importance Sampling Concerns:**  The use of importance sampling for approximating the normalizing constant is a potential limitation. The quality of the approximation depends heavily on the choice of the proposal distribution.  A more robust approach to approximation might be needed.
* **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** While the authors provide a few example generated questions, a more comprehensive qualitative analysis of the generated questions, including error analysis and comparisons to human questions, would be valuable.
* **Feature Engineering:** The choice of features, particularly those related to informativeness, relies on a Bayesian ideal observer. This assumption, while reasonable, may not fully capture the cognitive processes involved in human question asking.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis and experimental results are well-presented and support the claims. It is a complete piece of work given the scope. The authors address the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, the complexity of the model requires careful reading. Providing more intuitive examples of program representations could improve clarity.
* **Originality:** The idea of modeling question asking as program generation is highly original and represents a significant departure from traditional active learning approaches.
* **Significance:**  The results are promising and suggest that this approach could lead to more intelligent and human-like question asking machines. The potential impact on active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive science is significant.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*  The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful framework for modeling human question asking.
*  The empirical results demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the approach.
*  The paper is well-grounded in theoretical principles and related work.
*  The authors identify clear directions for future research.

**Con:**

*  The domain specificity of the grammar limits the generalizability of the approach.
*  The model complexity and reliance on importance sampling are potential concerns.
* The limited qualitative analysis of generated questions leaves some questions unanswered.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the domain specificity and model complexity are valid concerns, the novelty, theoretical grounding, and empirical results are strong enough to justify acceptance. The work makes a significant contribution to the field and opens up exciting new avenues for research in active learning, dialog systems, and computational cognitive science. I believe that the potential benefits of this research outweigh its limitations.
