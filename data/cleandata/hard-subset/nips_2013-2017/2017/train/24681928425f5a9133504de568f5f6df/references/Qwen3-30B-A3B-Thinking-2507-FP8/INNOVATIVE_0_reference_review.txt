

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs that, when run on possible world states, produce answers. The core insight is to model questions as syntactically well-formed programs (e.g., "(size Blue)" representing "How long is the blue ship?"), recasting question asking as program synthesis. This approach bridges cognitive science, linguistics, and machine learning, offering a fundamentally new perspective on question asking that differs significantly from existing work in active learning, dialogue systems, and visual question generation.

The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship-like information-search game. They design a context-free grammar for generating meaningful questions as programs, and develop a probabilistic generative model that uses four key features: informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain), complexity (log probability under the grammar), answer type (Boolean, Number, Color, Location), and relevance (whether the question refers to the game board). The model successfully predicts human question frequencies (with a Spearman correlation of 0.64) and generates novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

The paper's most significant contribution is its bold conceptual leap: modeling questions as programs. This is a novel approach that distinguishes itself from previous work in several ways. Unlike active learning methods that query for labels or causal interventions, or dialogue systems that use canned questions, this framework captures the compositionality and computability of human question asking. It also differs from neural question generation approaches that require large datasets of paired questions and images, as the authors' model can generate meaningful questions in novel scenarios with minimal prior exposure.

The paper's strengths are its conceptual novelty and the clear articulation of how this framework advances our understanding of human question asking. The authors demonstrate that compositionality is essential to modeling human question asking (15% of human questions were unique to specific contexts), and they provide evidence that humans balance informativeness with complexity when generating questions. The model's ability to generate novel, human-like questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") shows promise for future applications.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical detail to be reproducible. The methodology is sound, though the evaluation is limited to a single domain (the Battleship game). However, as a reviewer prioritizing novelty over methodological perfection, I find this limitation acceptable given the paper's conceptual contribution.

The main weaknesses are:
1. The model's domain-specific grammar requires substantial knowledge engineering for each new domain, limiting its immediate applicability.
2. The evaluation is confined to a single game, without testing in real-world active learning scenarios.
3. The paper doesn't address how to bridge the gap between program representations and natural language generation.

Despite these limitations, this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field. It introduces a new conceptual framework that could influence multiple areas: cognitive science (by providing a formal model of human question asking), computational linguistics (by connecting question meaning to program semantics), and machine learning (by extending active learning to richer query spaces).

Compared to recent NIPS papers, this work stands out for its bold theoretical contribution rather than incremental methodological improvements. While some NIPS papers focus on improving existing models with better architectures or training procedures, this paper proposes a fundamentally new way of conceptualizing a core cognitive ability.

The paper is well-positioned to advance the field by opening new avenues for research on human-like question asking in AI. The framework could inspire work on program synthesis for other cognitive tasks, and the authors have laid the groundwork for extending this approach to new domains.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper's conceptual novelty and potential to open new research directions outweigh its methodological limitations. It represents a significant contribution to understanding human question asking and offers a promising path toward more human-like question asking in machines. The framework is clearly articulated, the evaluation is appropriate for the novel approach, and the results demonstrate the model's ability to capture key aspects of human question asking. This paper should be accepted for NIPS as it introduces a significant new concept that will likely influence future research in cognitive science, linguistics, and machine learning.
