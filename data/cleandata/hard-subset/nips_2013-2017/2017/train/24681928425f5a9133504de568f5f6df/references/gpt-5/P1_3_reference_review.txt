Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a computational framework that models question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a LISP-like language; when run on a hypothesized world state, they return an answer of a specific type (Boolean/Number/Color/Location). A probabilistic context-free grammar defines the space of compositional, computable questions. A log-linear model over program features (Expected Information Gain, complexity via grammar prior, answer type, and a relevance filter) assigns probabilities to questions. Parameters are fit by maximizing the likelihood of human-produced questions using importance sampling to approximate gradients and the partition function. Evaluation uses a dataset of 605 natural language questions collected from 40 participants in a “Battleship” information-search task across 18 contexts (16 used for cross-validation due to computational constraints). The model predicts human question distributions (log-likelihood, Spearman correlations) and samples novel, human-like questions.

The work connects to: active learning (beyond label queries) and information-theoretic query selection; cognitive science accounts of compositional semantics and “language of thought”; semantic parsing/program induction approaches to question answering; and recent visual question generation/goal-driven dialogue. The conceptual novelty is turning question asking (not answering) into program synthesis optimized for informativeness and constrained by cognitive realism (complexity, type preferences).

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of questions as executable programs, enabling evaluation over hypothesized worlds and direct computation of informativeness (EIG).
- Compositional grammar that can represent the full diversity of questions in the dataset and generate novel ones; demonstrates productivity beyond template reuse.
- Thoughtful probabilistic modeling: combines informativeness with a complexity prior and type preferences, aligning with cognitive constraints.
- Empirical evaluation with leave-one-context-out: the full model outperforms lesioned variants in likelihood and generates plausible new questions.
- Bridges AI (active learning, program synthesis) and cognitive modeling; provides a concrete framework others could extend.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Quality (soundness and evaluation)
- Approximations: The importance sampling scheme is central but under-specified. It appears the proposal q is a uniform PCFG; a fixed pool of 150k samples is used “at each step.” Clarify whether the pool is resampled or reused; if reused, does it bias estimates as θ changes? If resampled, the computation is extremely heavy (100k iterations).
- Partition function estimator: The text gives Z ≈ E_q[p(x;θ)/q(x)], but p(x;θ) includes 1/Z; the standard IS estimator is Z = E_q[exp(−E(x))/q(x)]. Please correct and clarify how Z and gradients are actually estimated.
- Dataset limitations: Only 16 contexts used for evaluation; 14 questions removed due to the draw rule; two most complex contexts omitted. This raises concerns about scalability and robustness of EIG computation.
- Natural-language mapping: The paper evaluates on programs translated from typed questions, but the translation process is not described. Was it manual? How was inter-annotator agreement ensured? This affects reproducibility and claims about predicting “natural language” questions.
- Baselines: Ablations (no EIG, no complexity, no type) are informative, but there is no EIG-only baseline with the same grammar prior, nor comparisons to simpler template families, or to data-driven sequence models (even at the program level). Also, an EIG-only ranker is discussed in the related work; implementing it as a baseline would strengthen claims.
- Mixed signals in metrics: The full model has higher likelihoods, but the reported average Spearman correlation (0.64) is slightly lower than the information-agnostic model (0.65). Please explain this discrepancy (e.g., likelihood captures tails better; correlations are noisy due to sparsity).

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, with helpful examples. However:
  - Provide the full grammar (core and extended) in the main text or a more extensive summary, as this is central to reproducibility.
  - Explicitly describe the natural language → program annotation protocol and make mappings available.
  - Detail how the hypothesis space H is constructed per context, and why contexts 1 and 2 are intractable.
  - Explain how equivalence of programs is computed (MI threshold?) and how argument-renaming equivalence is detected.
  - Report learned feature weights and their variability to aid interpretability.
  - Fix the partition function formula and clarify the importance sampling procedure.

Originality
- The idea of modeling question asking as program generation and optimizing over compositional programs for EIG is original within active learning and cognitive modeling. Prior work modeled question meaning as partitions or used programs for answering, but not for generating context-sensitive questions at this level of generality. The combination with a grammar-based complexity prior is a novel and plausible cognitive constraint.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: reframes active learning to support rich, goal-directed queries beyond labels.
- Empirically promising: predicts human distributions and produces novel questions. However, current scope is narrow (single domain, handcrafted grammar, manual mapping), and computational scalability is a concern. The work is likely to inspire follow-on research integrating semantic parsing, grammar induction, and approximate EIG in larger domains.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an EIG-only baseline with the same complexity prior (grammar probability) and a “reuse human questions” baseline to quantify productivity gains.
- Report per-context likelihoods/correlations with confidence intervals and show that the full model’s advantage is statistically reliable.
- Provide analyses of complexity: length vs. depth vs. number of primitives; test which better predicts human preferences.
- Describe and release code, grammars, and the program annotations.
- Discuss strategies to scale EIG (e.g., amortized inference over hypotheses, MCMC over H, variational approximations) and to generalize across domains (grammar transfer, meta-learning).
- Explore light-weight NL interfaces (e.g., semantic parser trained with weak supervision from the dataset) to close the language gap.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled formulation of question asking as program generation with a compelling cognitive/AI bridge.
- Solid ablation evidence that complexity and type preferences matter beyond informativeness.
- Generates creative, context-appropriate questions; demonstrates compositional productivity.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited scope and scalability; handcrafted, domain-specific components; manual NL-to-program mapping.
- Incomplete methodological details about importance sampling and partition function; potential formula error.
- Missing stronger baselines; mixed correlation results.

Overall
A thought-provoking and well-executed first step toward modeling and generating rich, human-like questions. With clarifications and stronger baselines, this would be a strong NIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance for its conceptual novelty and cross-disciplinary impact, while noting the scope and scalability limitations.
