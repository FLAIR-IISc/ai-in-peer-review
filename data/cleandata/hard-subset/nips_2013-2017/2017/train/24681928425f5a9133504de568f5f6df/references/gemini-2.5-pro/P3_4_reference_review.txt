### Paper Summary

This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a LISP-like language. This "question asking as program generation" approach recasts the problem of deciding what to ask as a program synthesis problem. The authors develop a probabilistic log-linear model defined over a space of programs generated by a compositional grammar. This model scores potential questions (programs) based on a weighted combination of features, including their Expected Information Gain (EIG), complexity (program length), and answer type (e.g., Boolean, number). The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The results demonstrate that the model can accurately predict the frequency distribution of human questions in unseen game contexts and can also generate novel, syntactically and semantically sensible questions that were not observed in the human data. Ablation studies show that both informativeness (EIG) and complexity are critical components for explaining human question-asking behavior.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Framework:** The central contribution of this paper is its novel and highly original formulation of question asking as probabilistic program generation. This is a significant conceptual advance over existing approaches. It elegantly connects active learning (via EIG), program synthesis (via the grammar), and cognitive modeling. By moving beyond simple label queries, this work opens up a compelling research direction for creating more flexible and human-like AI systems that can actively formulate their own lines of inquiry. This is a thought-provoking paper that could inspire a new subfield of research.

2.  **Principled and Interpretable Model:** The model is well-conceived and its components are highly interpretable. The choice of features—informativeness, complexity, and answer type—is well-motivated by principles from information theory and cognitive science. The finding that complexity is a crucial factor, and that pure EIG maximization is a poor predictor of human behavior, is an important insight. This stands in contrast to many black-box neural models for question generation, where the internal reasoning is opaque.

3.  **Strong Experimental Design and Results:** The use of a controlled "Battleship" game environment is an excellent choice for studying goal-directed questioning. The ablation study provides strong evidence for the necessity of each of the model's key components (informativeness, complexity, type). The quantitative results, particularly the rank correlation between model predictions and human frequencies, are solid. Most impressively, the qualitative results showing the model generating novel and clever questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") powerfully demonstrate the generative and compositional nature of the framework.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the model is described in a logical progression from the grammar to the probabilistic formulation and inference, and the results are presented persuasively.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The "Language of Thought" Assumption and the Natural Language Gap:** The model operates entirely on semantic representations (programs), bypassing the challenge of parsing natural language into these forms. The 605 human questions were manually translated into the formal language. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, its significance should not be understated. It means the system in its current form cannot take natural language as input, nor does it generate natural language text as output. Bridging this "semantic gap" with modern parsing and generation tools is a major research challenge in itself and is critical for any practical application.

2.  **Scalability and Domain Specificity of the Grammar:** The grammar, which defines the entire hypothesis space of questions, is hand-crafted specifically for the Battleship domain. While the authors argue that many components (logical operators, set functions) are domain-general, a significant portion is tailored to the objects and relations of the game (e.g., `size`, `color`, board coordinates). The engineering effort required to design a new grammar for a different, potentially more complex, domain is likely to be substantial. This raises questions about the framework's scalability and generalizability beyond toy domains.

3.  **Computational Tractability:** The computation of EIG requires iterating over the entire space of possible world hypotheses, and the model's normalization constant `Z` requires sampling a large number of programs. This was manageable in the 6x6 grid world (though two contexts were still excluded for computational reasons). It is unclear how this approach would scale to problems with vastly larger or continuous state spaces where enumerating or sampling hypotheses becomes intractable. The importance sampling scheme used for inference might also struggle with more complex grammars where the uniform proposal distribution is a poor match for the true posterior.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   Presents a highly novel and significant conceptual framework for a fundamental AI problem.
*   The work is a strong synthesis of ideas from active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science.
*   The model is principled, interpretable, and backed by strong quantitative and qualitative results in its domain.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   It has the potential to be highly influential and open up new research avenues.

### Arguments for Rejection

*   The heavy reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar is a major limitation for scalability and generalizability.
*   The model sidesteps the difficult problem of translating between natural language and its formal program representation.
*   Computational costs may make the approach intractable for more complex, real-world problems.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that introduces a powerful and elegant new idea. The framing of question asking as program generation is a significant contribution that provides a new lens through which to view active learning and human-computer interaction. While the limitations regarding scalability and the natural language interface are real, they are characteristic of foundational work that establishes a new paradigm. The strengths—in particular, the novelty of the idea, the interpretability of the model, and the compelling results—far outweigh the weaknesses. This work is a perfect fit for NIPS, as it is likely to stimulate discussion and inspire follow-up research at the intersection of machine learning, cognitive science, and AI. I strongly recommend acceptance.
