### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking, framing the problem as one of program generation. The central idea is that a question can be represented as a program which, when executed on a possible world state, yields an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic log-linear model over a space of programs defined by a compositional grammar. This model predicts the questions humans are likely to ask in a given context by balancing several factors, primarily the question's informativeness (modeled as Expected Information Gain) and its complexity (derived from a probabilistic context-free grammar). The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions from a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The results show that the full model, incorporating informativeness, complexity, and answer type features, provides a better fit to human data than ablated versions. The paper also demonstrates the model's ability to generate novel, plausible questions not seen in the training set.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper presents an ambitious and conceptually interesting approach. However, the technical execution and presentation are marred by a substantial number of flaws and omissions that undermine confidence in the work.

*   **Methodological Omissions and Lack of Transparency:** Crucial details of the methodology are either omitted or relegated to footnotes, preventing a thorough evaluation.
    *   The core grammar, which defines the entire hypothesis space of questions, is not provided in the main text but in supplementary materials. This is a critical component of the model and its absence makes the paper difficult to assess. The authors state it is "context-free with a few exceptions" (Sec 4.2), but these exceptions are never specified.
    *   Footnote 4 reveals that 14 questions and a corresponding grammar rule were removed due to computational complexity. This contradicts the claim in Section 4.2 that the grammar "is sufficient to represent all 605 questions" and feels like a post-hoc adjustment to fit the model's limitations.
    *   Footnote 7 states that two entire contexts were dropped "for computational reasons" because they had large hypothesis spaces. This is a significant concern. The model's performance may be artificially inflated by excluding the most challenging scenarios. A robust model should be able to handle these cases, even if computationally expensive.
*   **Technical Soundness:** There are several points that raise concerns about the technical rigor of the work.
    *   The gradient equation (4) appears to be incorrect. The first term, representing the expectation over the data, should be a sum over all N data points, or the second term should be divided by N if the first term is an average. This is a fundamental error in a core equation of the optimization procedure.
    *   The "relevance" feature `f∅(x)` (Sec 4.5) feels like an ad-hoc patch. A well-designed, domain-specific grammar should not be generating questions like `(+ 1 1)` that are syntactically valid but semantically irrelevant. This suggests the grammar is too general and is being corrected by a feature rather than being properly constrained.
    *   The complexity feature `fcomp(x) = − log q(x)` is derived from a PCFG with *uniform* rule probabilities. This is a simplistic choice that essentially penalizes the number of rule applications. More justification for this specific choice over other complexity metrics is needed.
*   **Evaluation:** The evaluation is not as thorough as it could be.
    *   The log-likelihoods in Table 1 are based on importance sampling approximations of the normalizing constant Z. The stability and accuracy of this approximation are not discussed. The bootstrap results show that the full model's advantage over the information-agnostic and type-agnostic models is not overwhelmingly significant (81% and 88% of samples, respectively), which may be sensitive to the quality of this approximation.
    *   The process for generating "novel" questions is unclearly described. The claim that "(size Blue) is equivalent to (size Red)" is confusing; these are distinct questions. If this refers to structural equivalence, it should be stated precisely. The qualitative results are based on a few cherry-picked examples, which may not be representative of the model's typical output.

**Clarity:**

The paper suffers from a general lack of precision and numerous presentation errors, which detracts significantly from its readability and professionalism.

*   The manuscript is littered with typographical errors (e.g., "within in a particular domain" in the introduction).
*   The reference list is poorly formatted and contains clear errors (e.g., a stray "L," in reference [12], "d," in [22]) and incomplete entries (e.g., [1], [11], [23] are missing publication years/venues). Citation style is inconsistent throughout the text.
*   A misplaced template artifact ("st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...") appears as a footnote on the first page.
*   The explanation for why 115 potential questions were excluded from the original dataset (from 40 players * 18 contexts = 720) is vague ("ambiguous or extremely difficult to address computationally"). This lack of detail is frustrating for the reader.

**Originality:**

The core idea of modeling questions as programs is novel in the context of active learning and provides a fresh perspective that connects to work in cognitive science and semantic parsing. The combination of a compositional grammar with a probabilistic model that balances EIG and complexity is a strong and original contribution. The work is well-situated with respect to related literature.

**Significance:**

The paper addresses an important and challenging problem: enabling machines to ask richer, more human-like questions. If the technical execution were more rigorous, the work would represent a significant step forward. However, the model is evaluated in a single, highly constrained, toy-like domain ("Battleship"). The grammar contains many domain-specific primitives, and it is unclear how this approach would generalize to more complex, real-world domains. The claims about advancing active learning are therefore speculative and not fully substantiated by the presented evidence.

### Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The central framing of question asking as program synthesis is highly novel and elegant, providing a powerful new direction for research in active learning and computational cognitive science.
2.  The model successfully integrates informativeness (EIG) with a notion of complexity, and the ablation study convincingly demonstrates that both are necessary to explain human behavior.
3.  The model shows a capacity for productivity, generating sensible and clever questions that were not observed in the human data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The paper is replete with errors in formatting, citations, and typography, as well as a fundamental error in a core mathematical equation, indicating a lack of care unbecoming of a top-tier conference publication.
2.  Critical methodological details are either missing from the main paper or glossed over in footnotes (the full grammar, exclusion of difficult data points and entire contexts), preventing proper scrutiny and reproducibility.
3.  Several modeling choices appear ad-hoc (the uniform PCFG for complexity, the relevance filter) and lack sufficient justification.
4.  The evaluation is confined to a single, artificial domain, which severely limits the claims of generalizability and broader impact on the field of active learning.

### Recommendation

**Reject.**

While the paper is founded on a creative and promising core idea, the execution is deeply flawed. The sheer number of presentation errors, methodological omissions, and technical inaccuracies is unacceptable for a NIPS paper. The work feels preliminary and rushed. The exclusion of the most complex data and contexts for "computational reasons" calls into question the robustness and scalability of the proposed method. Before this work can be considered a significant contribution, the authors must address the technical sloppiness, provide a more transparent and complete description of their methodology, and validate their approach in a more compelling manner. The accumulation of many "minor" flaws amounts to a major weakness that cannot be overlooked.
