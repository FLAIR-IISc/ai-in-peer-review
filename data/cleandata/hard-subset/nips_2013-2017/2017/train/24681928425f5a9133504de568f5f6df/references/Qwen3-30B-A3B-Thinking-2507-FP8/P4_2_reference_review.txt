

# Review: Question Asking as Program Generation

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs that evaluate to answers on possible world states. The authors argue that human questions exhibit compositionality (reusing conceptual building blocks) and computability (being executable programs), which can be captured through a probabilistic program generation model. They evaluate their approach on a dataset of 605 natural language questions collected from a Battleship-style information-search game, where players asked context-sensitive questions to identify hidden ship configurations. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel human-like questions not present in the training data.

The work relates to and extends several lines of previous research: it builds on active learning [21] by moving beyond simple label queries to richer question types; it connects to program induction [12, 13, 17] by modeling questions as programs; and it addresses limitations in existing question generation work [11, 16, 20, 23] by focusing on goal-directed, context-sensitive questioning rather than open-ended generation from large datasets.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-structured probabilistic model with clear theoretical foundations. The authors properly account for key aspects of human question asking through a comprehensive set of features (informativeness via EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance). The ablation study (Table 1) effectively demonstrates the importance of each component.

2. **Novelty**: The core insight of modeling questions as executable programs represents a significant conceptual advance. While previous work has modeled questions as programs for question answering [12, 24], this paper is the first to use program generation to model human question asking behavior. The demonstration of compositionality (Section 4.1) and the ability to generate novel questions (Figure 3) provide strong evidence of the model's novelty.

3. **Evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation through both quantitative (log-likelihood comparisons, Spearman's correlations) and qualitative (novel question generation) methods. The leave-one-out cross-validation design is appropriate for the task, and the authors acknowledge data sparsity limitations.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized with clear explanations of the key concepts (compositionality, computability), the grammar, and the probabilistic model. The examples effectively illustrate how questions map to programs, and the supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for replication.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Domain Generalizability**: While the authors note that some grammar components are domain-general, the model's current implementation is tightly coupled to the Battleship domain. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how to adapt the approach to new domains without substantial re-engineering, which limits the broader applicability of the framework.

2. **Natural Language Gap**: The model operates on semantic representations rather than directly on natural language, creating a disconnect from practical natural language processing applications. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide a clear path to bridging this gap.

3. **Data Constraints**: The dataset (605 question-context pairs) is relatively small, and many questions were asked only once (sparse data). This limits the statistical power of the evaluation, though the authors appropriately acknowledge this limitation.

## Pro and Con for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Presents a novel, theoretically grounded framework for modeling human question asking that significantly advances beyond previous work
- Demonstrates both predictive accuracy (0.64 Spearman correlation) and generative capability (novel question generation)
- Provides a clear connection to cognitive science and linguistics literature
- Includes thorough ablation study showing the necessity of each component
- Well-written with clear technical explanations and sufficient detail for replication

**Con:**
- Model is currently limited to a specific domain (Battleship) with unclear generalization path
- Operates on semantic representations rather than natural language, limiting immediate practical applications
- Dataset size and sparsity constrain the statistical evaluation
- Some domain-specific grammar elements may not generalize well

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality, technically sound paper that makes a significant contribution to the computational modeling of human cognition. The core idea of modeling questions as executable programs is novel and well-supported by the evidence presented. While the current implementation is domain-specific, the framework provides a promising foundation for future work on human-like question asking in AI systems. The paper's strengths in theoretical contribution, methodological rigor, and evaluation substantially outweigh its weaknesses, particularly given the novelty of the approach.

The work advances the state of the art in computational cognitive science and has potential implications for developing more human-like active learning systems. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, provides a thorough evaluation, and acknowledges limitations appropriately. The model's ability to generate novel human-like questions represents a particularly compelling contribution.

Given the paper's novelty, technical soundness, and potential significance for both cognitive science and AI, I recommend acceptance for NIPS. The work makes a clear contribution to understanding human question asking and provides a foundation for more sophisticated active learning systems.
