### Summary

This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling rich, goal-directed question asking, drawing inspiration from human cognition. The central thesis is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional language. When executed on a possible world state, these programs produce an answer. This recasts the problem of question asking as one of probabilistic program generation. The authors develop a log-linear model over a space of programs defined by a hand-crafted grammar. This model learns to score potential questions based on a combination of features, including their expected information gain (EIG), complexity, and the type of answer they produce.

The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The authors demonstrate that their model can successfully predict the distribution of questions humans ask in unseen game contexts. Ablation studies show that both informativeness and a penalty for complexity are crucial for matching human behavior. Furthermore, the model is generative, capable of producing novel, contextually relevant, and human-like questions that were not observed in the training data. This work positions itself as a significant extension to traditional active learning, which typically focuses on simple label queries, and offers a more structured, goal-directed alternative to data-intensive neural approaches for question generation.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of framing goal-directed question asking as probabilistic program generation is highly original and powerful. It provides a formal, interpretable, and extensible framework for a complex cognitive phenomenon that is central to intelligence. This work moves beyond the dominant paradigm of simple queries in active learning and could inspire a new line of research into building more inquisitive and efficient learning agents. The connection to program induction in cognitive science is well-motivated and provides a strong conceptual foundation.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed model is technically sound and well-executed. The choice of a log-linear model over a program space is principled, and the selected features (EIG, complexity, answer type) are well-justified. The experimental design is rigorous, featuring a leave-one-out cross-validation setup and a thorough ablation study that clearly demonstrates the importance of each component of the model. The finding that a complexity penalty is essential for explaining human data, even more so than information gain in some cases, is a particularly insightful result.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly motivate the problem, explain their formalism with illustrative examples, and walk the reader through the model, inference, and experimental results. The connection between natural language questions and their program representations is made very clear, which is crucial for understanding the contribution.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The model is not merely a theoretical construct but is carefully validated against a challenging dataset of human behavior. The quantitative results, showing strong predictive performance on held-out data (average Spearman's ρ = 0.64), are compelling. The qualitative results are perhaps even more impressive, demonstrating that the model can generate novel and sophisticated questions (e.g., "Are blue and purple ships touching and red and purple not touching (or vice versa)?") that are both contextually useful and not present in the training data, highlighting the framework's generative power.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

1.  **Domain Specificity and Scalability:** The primary limitation, as the authors rightly acknowledge, is the reliance on a hand-engineered grammar specific to the Battleship domain. This raises questions about the framework's scalability and generality. While many primitives (logic, arithmetic) are domain-general, a significant engineering effort appears necessary to adapt the system to a new task.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper could be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the path toward greater generality. For instance, the authors could provide a clearer delineation of which grammar rules they consider truly domain-general versus domain-specific. A discussion on the potential for learning parts of the grammar from data (e.g., from a corpus of domain-specific text or through interaction) would make the future vision for this research more concrete.

2.  **The "Language-to-Program" Gap:** The model operates entirely on the semantic level of programs, abstracting away the challenge of parsing natural language into these formal representations. While this is a reasonable simplification for a focused study, it is a non-trivial gap to bridge for building a complete question-asking system.
    *   **Suggestion:** While a full semantic parser is beyond the scope of this work, the authors could briefly analyze the raw question data to characterize the mapping. For example, how many distinct natural language phrasings in the dataset correspond to a single program? Demonstrating this "many-to-one" mapping would further motivate their choice to work at the program level and provide valuable insights for future work on integrating a natural language front-end.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The inference procedure relies on sampling a very large set of programs (150,000) to approximate the partition function and gradients. This seems computationally intensive, especially given that EIG must be calculated for each sampled program. This could be a bottleneck for scaling to more complex domains with larger hypothesis spaces or richer grammars.
    *   **Suggestion:** It would be helpful to include a brief discussion of the computational cost and runtime of the current approach. The authors could also speculate on more efficient inference strategies. For example, could a search-based method like MCMC, which proposes local modifications to promising programs, be more efficient than the current importance sampling scheme?

### Decision

This paper presents a creative, elegant, and significant contribution to the study of active learning and artificial intelligence. The framing of question asking as program generation is a powerful idea that is executed with technical rigor and validated convincingly against human data. While the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar limits its immediate applicability, the work provides a strong foundation and a clear new direction for research. It is a complete and thought-provoking piece of work that is well-suited for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
