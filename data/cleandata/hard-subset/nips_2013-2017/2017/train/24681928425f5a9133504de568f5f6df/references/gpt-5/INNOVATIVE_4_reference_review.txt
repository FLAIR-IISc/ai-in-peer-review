Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for question asking: represent questions as executable programs and model human question generation as probabilistic program synthesis. Using a LISP-like grammar over semantic primitives (sets, logic, arithmetic, lambda/mapping), the authors define a large compositional space of possible questions. A log-linear model over program features—expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, a complexity prior derived from the PCFG, answer-type preferences, and a relevance filter—assigns probabilities to questions in a given Battleship-like game context. Parameters are learned via maximum likelihood, using importance sampling to approximate the partition function and feature expectations. On a dataset of 605 human-generated questions across 18 contexts, the model predicts context-sensitive question distributions in leave-one-context-out evaluation and can sample novel questions judged to be human-like. Ablations show that complexity costs and answer-type preferences matter alongside informativeness; EIG alone does not explain human frequencies.

Relation to prior work
The work connects several lines: active learning (mostly label- and intervention-queries), formal semantics of questions as partitions, cognitive science’s language-of-thought and compositional concept learning, and program-induction-based semantic parsing and visual reasoning. Compared to VQG and dialog systems, the focus here is goal-directed, compositional question generation with minimal data, in contrast to large-scale neural generative models trained on extensive corpora. Unlike question answering as program execution, this paper emphasizes question asking as program generation conditioned on utility. It also complements pragmatic models of question utility (e.g., RSA-style accounts) by operationalizing informativeness and production costs within a synthesis model.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: reframing question asking as program generation is bold and compelling, bridging active learning, formal semantics, and program synthesis.
- Compositionality: the grammar transparently captures reuse of semantic building blocks; it explains productivity beyond memorizing observed questions.
- Principled objective: EIG under an explicit hypothesis space, combined with a cost/complexity prior, yields an interpretable account of human preferences.
- Empirical signal: holds-out predictions achieve reasonable rank correlations, and ablations indicate key ingredients (notably complexity) are necessary.
- Generativity: sampled questions are context-sensitive and often clever, going beyond the training set without massive data.

Weaknesses
- Domain specificity and supervision: the grammar is partly hand-engineered for Battleship and relies on mapping human NL questions to programs; generalization to new domains and to natural language parsing is not demonstrated.
- Limited scale: small dataset (605 questions), removal of two complex contexts and one probabilistic operator, and reliance on importance sampling raise concerns about robustness and variance.
- Evaluation depth: predictive metrics are limited (approximate log-likelihood and Spearman correlations); no human evaluation of sampled questions’ quality/usefulness beyond illustrative examples; baseline space is narrow (e.g., no pragmatic RSA baseline, no neural sequence baselines conditioned on symbolic context).
- Utility assumptions: EIG presumes a fixed goal (identify the full board) and specific priors; human goals and priors may vary. The relevance filter is somewhat ad hoc.

Quality
Overall technically sound for a modeling paper. The probabilistic formulation, inference via importance sampling, and ablations are appropriate. However, details about the proposal distribution’s variance, convergence diagnostics, and sensitivity to grammar/proposal choices are limited. Dropping contexts and operators is understandable but should be quantified (how much does this affect results?). A stronger set of baselines and human evaluations of generated questions would improve confidence.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized, with intuitive examples and candid discussion of limitations. Some crucial grammar details reside in the supplement; including a compact subset in the main text would help. A few references appear incomplete or malformed. Clarify the NL-to-program annotation process and its reliability.

Originality
High. Framing question asking as probabilistic program synthesis with an EIG-driven utility and a compositional grammar is a fresh and promising direction, distinct from existing active learning and question generation work.

Significance
Moderate to high as a conceptual contribution. The approach could influence interactive learning, dialog, and cognitive modeling. Empirical scope is narrow; broader validation would raise impact. Still, as a foundational step, it opens an exciting research direction.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Detail the NL-to-program mapping: annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, coverage of paraphrase.
- Provide sensitivity analyses: effect of proposal distribution, sample size for importance sampling, maximum program length, and prior over hypotheses.
- Add baselines: simple “max-EIG then shortest” heuristic; pragmatic RSA-based question production; and a neural baseline conditioned on a symbolic context representation.
- Evaluate generated questions with humans for perceived usefulness, naturalness, and answerability cost; report inter-rater reliability.
- Discuss portability: what fraction of the grammar is domain-general vs domain-specific? Demonstrate transfer to a second domain with minimal additions, or sketch a principled procedure for extending the grammar.
- Consider integrating semantic parsing to close the loop with natural language, or using a neural proposal over programs guided by the grammar.
- Beyond EIG, consider explicit answerer-cost models and utility functions capturing intermediate goals.

Pros
- Bold, original framing with clear compositional machinery
- Interpretable, principled utility-cost tradeoff
- Empirical evidence that complexity matters, not just informativeness
- Demonstrates generative capacity for novel, context-appropriate questions

Cons
- Narrow domain with hand-engineered grammar and manual mapping
- Small dataset; dropped contexts/operators
- Limited baselines and evaluation breadth; no human eval of samples
- Utility assumptions may not capture human pragmatics fully

Recommendation
Accept (weak accept to accept). Despite limited empirical scope, the conceptual advance is significant and well motivated. This work stakes out an important direction for interactive learning and provides a concrete, extensible modeling framework that others can build upon.
