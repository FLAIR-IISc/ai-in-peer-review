**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel and ambitious framework for modeling goal-directed question asking, recasting the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The central idea is that human questions can be understood as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, return an answer. The authors develop this idea within a "Battleship"-like game, where participants ask natural language questions to resolve ambiguity. They define a compositional grammar (inspired by LISP and lambda calculus) capable of representing these questions as semantic programs. A probabilistic model is then defined over this space of programs. The probability of a given question is determined by a log-linear model combining features for its informativeness (Expected Information Gain), its complexity (a penalty for longer programs), and its answer type. The model is trained on a dataset of human-generated questions and evaluated on its ability to predict question frequencies in held-out contexts and to generate novel, plausible questions. The results show that the full model, which balances informativeness and complexity, provides a good fit to the human data and can generate creative and useful new questions.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and the boldness of its core idea. It presents a significant conceptual shift away from the dominant paradigms in active learning and dialogue systems.

1.  **A New Paradigm for Active Learning:** Most active learning research has remained confined to simple queries, typically requesting labels for unlabeled data points. This work shatters that limitation by proposing a rich, structured, and compositional space of possible queries. The idea of "question asking as program generation" is a powerful and generative one that could inspire a completely new direction of research in active learning, moving it closer to the kind of inquiry that characterizes human learning.

2.  **Compositionality and Generativity:** The program synthesis framework provides a compelling and computationally grounded account of the compositionality and productivity of human questioning. Unlike models that select from a predefined set of questions or learn shallow patterns from massive datasets, this model can construct entirely new, syntactically valid, and semantically meaningful questions from a set of conceptual primitives. The demonstration that the model can generate clever, non-trivial questions that were not in the human training set is a powerful validation of the approach.

3.  **Principled Model of Cognitive Constraints:** The paper does an excellent job of formalizing the trade-offs involved in asking a good question. It moves beyond the simple notion of maximizing information gain by integrating a well-motivated complexity penalty derived from the grammar. The ablation studies convincingly show that both informativeness (EIG) and complexity are critical for explaining human behavior, reflecting a plausible cognitive model where people seek informative answers but are constrained by a preference for simplicity.

**Weaknesses**

The paper's weaknesses are primarily limitations of scope inherent in introducing such a novel framework. From my perspective, these do not detract from the value of the core contribution and are acceptable trade-offs for exploring a bold new idea.

1.  **Domain-Specific Grammar:** The grammar that defines the space of questions is hand-engineered for the Battleship domain. This naturally raises questions about the framework's scalability and generalizability. While a valid concern, this is a necessary simplification for a first-of-its-kind investigation. The authors rightly point out that many components of the grammar (logic, arithmetic, set operations) are domain-general. The primary contribution here is the framework itself, not a universally applicable grammar. Demonstrating the core concept in a clean, controlled domain is the right first step.

2.  **Decoupling from Natural Language:** The model operates on semantic programs, not raw text, sidestepping the difficult problem of semantic parsing. Again, this is an acceptable simplification. The goal of the paper is to model the cognitive process of formulating a query, not to build an end-to-end NLP system. The proposed model could readily be combined with existing work on semantic parsing in the future. Focusing on the semantic representation allows for a clearer investigation of the core problem.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

This work is exactly the kind of submission that NIPS should champion: it is bold, creative, and conceptually deep. It tackles a fundamental problem in intelligence—how to ask good questions—with a genuinely new approach. While other papers may present more polished methodologies or marginal SOTA improvements on established benchmarks, this paper has the potential to open up an entirely new subfield at the intersection of active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science.

The authors are transparent about the current limitations, such as the hand-crafted grammar. However, these are acceptable constraints for a paper introducing a new paradigm. The value here is not in a perfect, end-to-end system, but in the power and elegance of the core idea. The framework is theoretically sound, the experiments provide strong support for its claims, and the qualitative results (the generated novel questions) are impressive. This paper is thought-provoking and is highly likely to inspire and influence future research. It prioritizes a significant conceptual leap over incremental refinement, and for that, it deserves high praise.
