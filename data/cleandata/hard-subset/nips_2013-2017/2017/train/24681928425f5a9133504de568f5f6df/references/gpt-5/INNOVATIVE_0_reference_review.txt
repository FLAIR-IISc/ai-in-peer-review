Summary
This paper proposes a bold, computational account of human question asking by treating questions as executable programs over possible world states. A probabilistic context-free grammar (with lambda calculus, mapping, and set operators) defines a compositional space of question-programs. A log-linear model over programs uses features for expected information gain (EIG), program complexity (via grammar probability), answer type, and a relevance filter. Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human-authored questions in a Battleship-style information search task, using importance sampling to approximate intractable expectations. The model predicts which questions humans ask in context (leave-one-context-out), and can also synthesize novel, human-like questions. Ablations suggest complexity and informativeness features both matter; the full model achieves higher held-out log-likelihoods and reasonable rank correlations with human frequencies, and can generate plausible new questions.

Relation to prior work
The approach bridges ideas from active learning (beyond label queries), program induction and semantic parsing (questions as programs), and pragmatic/partition semantics of questions in linguistics and cognitive science. It goes beyond goal-directed dialog systems with limited templates and beyond neural VQG systems that rely on large paired datasets and fixed styles. Related cognitive work on informativeness and questioning is cited; connections to recent program-based visual reasoning and languages of thought are appropriate. Overall, the paper differentiates itself by focusing on question generation as program synthesis guided by EIG and complexity, and by predicting human questions in novel contexts.

Strengths
- Novel framing: Questions as programs with compositional, executable meaning is a compelling and general abstraction that could shape future work in active learning and HCI.
- Human-aligned utility: Integrates Bayesian EIG with an explicit complexity/conciseness prior, capturing the informativeness–cost trade-off observed in people.
- Generative capability: The grammar enables productivity; the model samples genuinely novel, context-sensitive questions and checks semantic equivalence via answer partitions.
- Empirical evidence: Leave-one-context-out fits, ablations showing the necessity of complexity (and benefits of EIG and answer-type features), and moderate-to-strong rank correlations on several contexts.
- Clarity about limitations and scope; discussion is thoughtful and well-situated.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Domain specificity and scale: The Battleship grammar contains domain-specific symbols; it is unclear how much knowledge engineering is required to transfer to new tasks. The dataset is small (605 questions), and two complex contexts and some draw-type questions were dropped due to computational issues.
- Evaluation depth: Quantitative evaluation relies on approximate log-likelihoods and correlations; generative evaluation is qualitative (no human judgments of naturalness/utility). Comparisons to alternative question-generation baselines (e.g., retrieval, simple EIG-only rankers, or neural sequence models constrained by answer types) are limited.
- Inference approximations: Importance sampling with a fixed proposal (uniform PCFG) and reusing a finite question pool may lead to weight degeneracy as parameters change. No diagnostics for variance, ESS, or sensitivity to the proposal are reported.
- Utility choice: The model uses entropy reduction; alternative task losses (e.g., Bayes risk, goal-specific utilities) or pragmatic models (answerer cost, ambiguity aversion) are not explored.
- Natural language interface: The pipeline operates in a semantic space and does not map to/from text; integration with a semantic parser or surface realizer is deferred.

Quality
Technically sound within scope, with reasonable approximations for an exploratory study. Claims are mostly supported: ablations substantiate feature importance; predictive performance is plausible. However, the evaluation is narrow and approximate; stronger baselines, robustness diagnostics, and human evaluation of generated questions would strengthen evidence.

Clarity
Well-written and organized; the motivation and modeling choices are clear. Some key details are in the supplement (full grammar, figure references), and the EIG computation over large hypothesis spaces merits more computational detail. Reporting more implementation diagnostics would aid reproducibility.

Originality
High. The core idea—casting question asking as program synthesis with a learned utility over executable semantics—is fresh and conceptually significant, beyond incremental extensions of existing dialog or VQG systems.

Significance
Promising direction that can influence active learning, interactive ML, and cognitive modeling. While current results are in a constrained domain, the framework’s compositionality and the empirical alignment with human questioning suggest broader potential.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: an EIG-only ranker over the same program space; retrieval from nearest contexts; a simple neural language model constrained by answer types.
- Provide inference diagnostics: effective sample sizes, variance of Z estimates, and sensitivity to proposal distribution; consider resampling or adaptive proposals.
- Expand evaluation: human judgments of sampled questions’ usefulness and naturalness; report failure cases and error analysis.
- Explore alternative utilities: task loss (Bayes risk), answerer costs, or pragmatic reasoning to model preferences for yes/no vs numeric questions beyond base rates.
- Demonstrate partial transfer: reuse the domain-general subset of the grammar in a second, small domain to probe portability.
- Bridge to language: a prototype semantic parser or surface realizer to show end-to-end feasibility.

Reasons to accept
- Bold, original formulation with clear conceptual contribution.
- Compositional, executable semantics enabling genuine generativity.
- Empirical evidence that complexity + EIG captures human question preferences.

Reasons to reject
- Narrow, domain-specific evaluation with small dataset.
- Approximate and somewhat limited quantitative validation; lack of strong baselines and human eval of generations.
- Computational scalability and transfer remain unproven.

Verdict
Lean accept. Despite limitations, the paper opens a new, intellectually rich direction—question asking as program generation—that is likely to spur follow-up work. The novelty and conceptual ambition outweigh the incompleteness of the current validation.
