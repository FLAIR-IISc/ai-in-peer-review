- Summary: Models human question asking by representing questions as executable programs evaluated on world states; tested on 605 natural questions from a Battleship-like game using a probabilistic generative model with informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance features.

- Contribution: Reframes question asking as program synthesis emphasizing compositionality and computability, enabling generation of novel, context-sensitive questions with minimal training.

- Dataset and evaluation: Evaluated on a small, domain-specific dataset collected from a game; uses leave-one-out cross-validation and bootstrapping for statistical validation.

- Results: Full model outperforms ablated variants and can generate novel, human-like questions not seen in training.

- Originality (High): Novel approach modeling questions as programs rather than statistical templates, addressing productivity of human question asking.

- Technical quality (High): Well-defined probabilistic model, appropriate feature set, rigorous evaluation, and robust statistical testing.

- Significance (High): Advances active learning and human-AI interaction by moving beyond simple label queries toward rich, human-like question asking; promising for broader applications.

- Clarity (High): Exceptionally well-written with clear exposition, precise grammar description, and reproducible supplementary material.

- Weakness — Domain specificity (Moderate): Current implementation is tightly coupled to the Battleship domain and lacks analysis of how much grammar redesign is needed for other domains.

- Weakness — Natural language gap (Moderate): Model outputs program representations, not natural language; translation to natural language remains a significant unresolved engineering challenge.

- Weakness — Scalability (Moderate): Unclear how the approach scales to more complex domains with much larger hypothesis spaces (e.g., medicine, science).

- Arguments for acceptance: Fundamentally new approach, strong empirical validation, ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel questions, theoretically grounded and well-presented.

- Arguments against acceptance: Limited immediate applicability due to domain specificity, unresolved natural language generation step, and limited discussion of scalability.

- Recommendation: Strong accept — significant contribution with solid execution and high potential for follow-on research despite acknowledged limitations.
