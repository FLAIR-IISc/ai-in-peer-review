### Summary

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed, context-sensitive human question asking. The central idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. This approach recasts the problem of deciding which question to ask as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The authors define a space of possible questions using a formal grammar and construct a log-linear probabilistic model over this space. The model's energy function for a given question (program) is a weighted combination of several features, most notably its Expected Information Gain (EIG) in a given context and its complexity (related to program length).

The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game, where the goal was to resolve ambiguity about a hidden board configuration. The authors demonstrate that their model can predict the distribution of human questions in held-out game contexts. Ablation studies show that both informativeness (EIG) and complexity are critical components for matching human behavior. Furthermore, the model is shown to be generative, capable of synthesizing novel, contextually relevant, and human-like questions that were not present in the training data.

This work connects to several lines of research. It extends the scope of active learning, which traditionally focuses on simple label queries [21], to a much richer and more structured query space. It also provides a structured, goal-directed alternative to recent end-to-end neural network models for question generation [16, 22], which are typically less interpretable, less data-efficient, and not explicitly optimized for information seeking. The framework draws heavily on the "language of thought" hypothesis and program induction models from cognitive science [13, 17], applying these concepts for the first time to the problem of question *generation*.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-defined, combining a probabilistic context-free grammar with a log-linear model over program features. The choice of features—informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance—is well-motivated by principles of both rational decision-making and cognitive constraints. The optimization procedure, which uses importance sampling to handle the intractable partition function and gradient estimation, is a standard and appropriate method for this class of models. The experimental evaluation is rigorous, employing leave-one-out cross-validation and insightful lesion studies that clearly demonstrate the contribution of the different model components. The finding that a complexity-agnostic model performs very poorly is a particularly strong result, highlighting that human question asking is not simply about maximizing information gain.

A minor weakness is the reliance on approximations for both training and evaluation (log-likelihood). While the authors use bootstrapping to assess the stability of their model comparisons, a discussion of the variance of the importance sampling estimators or convergence properties of the optimization would strengthen the claims. Additionally, the exclusion of two contexts and a small number of questions for computational tractability is a pragmatic choice but represents a limitation on the completeness of the evaluation.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The core idea of "question asking as program generation" is introduced intuitively and motivated with clear examples. The description of the model architecture, from the grammar to the probabilistic formulation and feature definitions, is logical and easy to follow. The figures, particularly Figure 3 which showcases novel generated questions, are effective in illustrating the model's capabilities. The connection to prior work is clearly articulated, situating the contribution effectively within the fields of active learning, AI, and cognitive science.

A small point for improvement would be to include a more detailed summary of the grammar or key production rules in the main text, as its full definition is in the supplementary materials. This would make the paper more self-contained for a reader trying to understand the full expressive capacity of the question language.

**Originality:**
The paper's primary contribution is highly original. While program synthesis has been applied to question answering [24] and concept learning [13], this work is the first to formalize goal-directed question *asking* as probabilistic program generation. This represents a significant conceptual advance over existing approaches. The synthesis of Bayesian inference (for EIG), formal language theory (the grammar), and probabilistic modeling to tackle a fundamental aspect of intelligence is novel and compelling. It provides a powerful, interpretable, and compositional framework that stands in contrast to the dominant paradigm of end-to-end deep learning models for language generation tasks.

**Significance:**
The work is significant for multiple research communities. For machine learning, it charts a path toward more sophisticated active learning agents that can probe their environment with complex, structured queries, moving beyond simple label requests. This could have a substantial impact on sample-efficient learning and human-AI interaction. For artificial intelligence and computational linguistics, it presents a functional, goal-directed model of question generation that is grounded in semantics and utility, a key departure from models trained only on large dialogue corpora. For computational cognitive science, it offers the first predictive, generative model of open-ended human question asking, providing a quantitative account of how people balance informativeness and simplicity in inquiry.

The main limitation affecting the broader significance is the current reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar. The authors rightly acknowledge this, and it poses a clear challenge for scaling the approach to new, more complex domains. The model also operates on semantic program representations rather than natural language directly, meaning it would require an effective semantic parser to be integrated into an end-to-end system. Despite these challenges, the foundational ideas presented in the paper are likely to inspire future research exploring these extensions.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Presents a highly original and elegant formalization of question asking as probabilistic program generation.
*   The framework successfully integrates principles from active learning (EIG), cognitive science (compositionality), and programming languages (grammars).
*   The model is technically sound and rigorously evaluated with strong quantitative and qualitative results.
*   The work provides a compelling, interpretable alternative to black-box neural models for question generation.
*   The contribution is significant for multiple fields, including machine learning, AI, and cognitive science, and addresses a key limitation in current active learning systems.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar raises questions about the scalability and generalizability of the approach to more open-ended domains.
*   The model does not parse natural language, operating instead on an intermediate semantic representation, which limits its immediate practical application as an end-to-end system.
