- Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs in a simple language to capture compositional, context-sensitive question asking.  
- Uses a probabilistic generative model with features for informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated in a Battleship-style game and successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions.  
- Technically sound and thoroughly executed: detailed grammar, model description, cross-validation, ablation studies, and qualitative analyses.  
- Quantitatively strong: full model outperforms ablated versions, with log-likelihood advantage in 100% of bootstrap samples.  
- Qualitatively strong: generates contextually appropriate novel questions (e.g., “Are all the ships horizontal?”).  
- Main limitation: grammar and implementation are domain-specific, raising questions about generalization to other domains.  
- Does not show how to automatically generate or adapt the grammar for new domains, implying substantial knowledge engineering.  
- Paper is clearly written and organized, though some technical grammar details may be dense and key tables are in the supplement.  
- Original and novel contribution: first predictive, compositional program-based model of open-ended human question asking.  
- Cognitive-science contribution: formalizes how people trade off informativeness and complexity when asking questions.  
- Significant potential impact for enabling richer AI question asking and applications in education and human-AI collaboration.  
- Weaknesses: narrow evaluation scope, focus on predicting frequencies rather than question utility for learning, and modest linkage to broader active learning literature.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept (NeurIPS), final score 8/10, justified by strong contributions despite domain-specific limitations.
