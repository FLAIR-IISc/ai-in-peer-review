- Proposes a framework treating questions as compositional programs evaluated over possible-world states, using a PCFG and log-linear model with EIG, complexity, answer-type, and relevance features.  
- Connects active learning, formal semantics (questions as partitions), and probabilistic program induction to generate open-ended, goal-directed questions.  
- Formulation is technically coherent: typed-programs PCFG, log-linear learning, and importance sampling for approximations are appropriate.  
- Empirically, the model achieves higher held-out log-likelihood and generates plausible novel questions; the complexity prior is essential.  
- Informativeness (EIG) shows mixed evidence: LL favors it but rank correlation with human frequencies is not stronger than info-agnostic variants.  
- Major limitations: hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar and world model; two contexts and 14 questions removed for computational reasons.  
- Approximation/inference details are underspecified (150k sample handling, importance-weight variance, sensitivity to proposal q) and diagnostics are missing.  
- Lacks comparisons to simple data-driven baselines (template reuse, sequence models, nearest-neighbor) to contextualize gains.  
- Novelty of sampled questions not quantitatively evaluated—no human judgments or task-based utility measures.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized, but key computational and variance details are deferred to the supplement and SEs/variance are not reported.  
- Original and noteworthy combination of compositional program generation with utility/complexity tradeoffs for question asking.  
- Significance is moderate: conceptually interesting and likely to inspire follow-ups, but limited by narrow domain, small dataset, and mixed informativeness results.  
- Pros: principled formalization, importance of complexity priors demonstrated, generates semantically novel questions, provides a generative predictive account beyond label queries.  
- Cons: heavy hand-engineering, mixed evidence for informativeness, missing strong baselines and human evaluations, and incomplete approximation diagnostics.  
- Suggested improvements: add simple baselines, quantify importance-sampling variance and sensitivity, run human evaluations of sampled questions, test transfer with domain-general grammar subsets, and explore alternative complexity priors.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept (medium confidence).
