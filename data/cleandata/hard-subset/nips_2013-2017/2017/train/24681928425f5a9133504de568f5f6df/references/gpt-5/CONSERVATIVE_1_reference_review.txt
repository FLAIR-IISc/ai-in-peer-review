Summary
This paper models question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a small, typed, lambda/LISP-like DSL; executing a program on a hypothesized world yields an answer. A context-free grammar (with some extensions) defines the space of programs. A log-linear model over programs is trained to predict which questions humans ask in a Battleship-like information search game, with features for Expected Information Gain (EIG), complexity (−log PCFG probability), answer type, and a relevance filter. Training and evaluation rely on importance sampling from the grammar to approximate expectations and the partition function. On a dataset of 605 questions over 18 contexts (two dropped), the full model outperforms ablations in approximate log-likelihood and yields moderate correlations with human question frequencies. The model can also sample “novel” questions deemed plausible by qualitative inspection.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned between active learning, semantic parsing/QA as program execution, and cognitive models of communication (e.g., partition semantics, Bayesian/RSA frameworks). The DSL/PCFG approach echoes prior “language of thought” and program induction work. The main novelty claimed is a predictive, generative model of human question choice across contexts, trading off informativeness and complexity.

Strengths
- Clear, interpretable formalization: representing questions as typed programs is principled and connects to established traditions (semantic parsing, partition semantics, program induction).
- Compositional generalization: a hand-crafted grammar allows generating questions not seen in training and captures productivity beyond simple retrieval baselines.
- Sensible feature design: combining EIG with a complexity prior is psychologically and practically motivated; the finding that complexity is crucial is interesting.
- Dataset and task are concrete; qualitative samples are sometimes clever and context-appropriate.
- Paper is generally well written and situates itself in relevant literature.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological soundness and evaluation
- Heavy reliance on hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar and semantics. While some operators are domain-general, much of the apparatus (entities, predicates, typing, lambda constructs) is tailored to Battleship. It is unclear how much carries over without substantial knowledge engineering.
- Inference/estimation is approximate and potentially high-variance. The model uses a fixed proposal (uniform PCFG) to sample 150k programs, then optimizes a log-linear model by importance sampling expectations over this fixed pool for 100k iterations. This risks biased gradients (optimizing over a finite subset) and unstable normalization. No diagnostics (ESS, variance) or sensitivity analyses are reported. No regularization is used; no parameter recovery or stability analysis is shown.
- Evaluation is narrow and partially excludes data. Two contexts were dropped “for computational reasons,” and 14 questions were removed due to a probabilistic operator. This weakens claims of generality and raises questions about scalability of EIG computation (already challenging even in a toy domain).
- Mixed evidence for informativeness. The average Spearman correlation of the full model is 0.64, while the information-agnostic model reportedly achieves 0.65, suggesting EIG contributes little to rank prediction of human frequencies. The paper emphasizes log-likelihood improvements, but these are approximate and depend on the same sampler used for training; the bootstrap over Z helps but does not address proposal mismatch or gradient bias. The narrative that “informativeness matters” is undercut by these results and previous findings cited by the authors that EIG alone does not correlate with human frequencies.
- Weak baselines. The ablations are all within the same modeling framework. Missing are: (a) a “grammar-only” prior (no learned weights); (b) a retrieval baseline using nearest contexts; (c) a template or type-frequency baseline; (d) simple discriminative models over hand-crafted features computed from program semantics; (e) RSA-style pragmatic models of questions, which are highly relevant. Without these, it is hard to assess how much the proposed training actually helps beyond the PCFG and length/type biases.
- Qualitative generation lacks human evaluation. “Human-like” is asserted without behavioral validation, naturalness judgments, or task utility assessment.

Clarity
- Core ideas are clear, but key technical details are deferred to supplemental tables (grammar rules, typing). The paper should include enough to reproduce the grammar and feature computations.
- The optimization section needs more transparency: whether the sampled pool is fixed across iterations/fits, variance of estimators, convergence behavior, and sensitivity to the 150k size.
- The contradiction between correlation numbers (info-agnostic > full) and the claim that EIG helps should be addressed directly.

Originality
- Modeling questions as executable programs is not wholly new (semantic parsing/QA, partition semantics), but applying it to predict human question asking via a log-linear model over program features is a reasonable integration. The conceptual contribution is incremental rather than radical.

Significance
- As presented, the work is a proof of concept in a constrained domain with substantial hand-engineering and computational limitations. The approach may inspire future work, but current results fall short of a robust, general method for active question generation in ML or dialog systems. Claims about enabling “more human-like question asking in machines” are premature.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the proposal distribution and the 150k sample size? Did you try adaptive proposals or resampling per iteration?
2. Are feature weights stable across contexts (e.g., cross-fit variance)? Any regularization used?
3. Can you report a “grammar prior only” baseline and a simple retrieval baseline?
4. Why does the information-agnostic model achieve higher average Spearman correlation than the full model? Can you reconcile this with your log-likelihood results?
5. What fraction of the learned questions’ probability mass concentrates on programs not present in the sampled pool? Any ESS diagnostics for IS?
6. Can you release the full grammar and code to ensure reproducibility?

Pros
- Clear, interpretable formalization; principled connection to prior theory.
- Demonstrates compositional generation beyond memorization.
- Sensible feature trade-offs; complexity clearly important.
- Well-written; dataset available.

Cons
- Strong domain specificity and hand-engineering; limited scalability.
- Approximate, potentially biased estimation with limited diagnostics.
- Mixed evidence for the informativeness feature; weak external baselines.
- Partial data exclusion; lack of human evaluation for generation.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. Interesting formulation with some cognitive insights, but methodological limitations, narrow evaluation, and weak baselines reduce confidence in the general validity and impact for NIPS. Strengthening the inference, baselines, and evaluation (including human judgments and an out-of-domain test) would substantially improve the paper. Confidence: medium.
