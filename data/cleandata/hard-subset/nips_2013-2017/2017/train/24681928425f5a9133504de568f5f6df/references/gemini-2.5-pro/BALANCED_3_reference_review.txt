### Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed, open-ended question asking, framing it as a problem of program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. When executed on a possible world state, these programs return an answer. This formalism recasts the challenge of deciding what question to ask into a search and inference problem over a space of programs defined by a grammar.

The authors propose a probabilistic log-linear model to score the "usefulness" of a question (program). The score, or energy, of a program is a weighted combination of several features: its expected information gain (EIG) with respect to a distribution over possible world states (a standard concept from active learning), its complexity (a penalty for longer programs), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number), and its relevance to the task. The model's parameters are fit to maximize the likelihood of a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game.

This work builds upon and extends several distinct lines of research. It significantly generalizes classic active learning, which typically considers a very restricted query space (e.g., requests for labels), to a rich, compositional space of queries. It connects to the "probabilistic language of thought" hypothesis in cognitive science, which models concepts as programs, by applying this paradigm to the active process of inquiry rather than just representation. Finally, it provides a compelling alternative to contemporary data-driven approaches in AI dialogue and visual question generation. Unlike deep learning models that require vast datasets and often generate stereotyped or non-goal-directed questions, this approach demonstrates impressive generativity and contextual sensitivity from a small dataset, reflecting a deeper integration of reasoning and planning into the question-generation process.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Significant Conceptual Framework:** The central contribution—modeling questions as programs and question asking as probabilistic program synthesis—is highly original, elegant, and powerful. It provides a formal, interpretable framework for a complex cognitive phenomenon that has been difficult to capture computationally. This reframing has the potential to be highly influential, bridging active learning, program induction, and cognitive science.

2.  **Strong Empirical Grounding and Evaluation:** The model is not merely theoretical; it is rigorously tested against human data. The quantitative results show that the full model provides a good fit to the distribution of human questions, outperforming several well-chosen ablated versions (e.g., complexity-agnostic, information-agnostic). The finding that a combination of informativeness, complexity, and answer-type preference is needed to explain human behavior is a significant result in itself.

3.  **Demonstrated Generativity and Creativity:** Perhaps the most compelling evidence for the model's success is its ability to generate novel, contextually relevant, and insightful questions that were not present in the human training data (Figure 3). Questions like "Are all the ships horizontal?" or the complex Boolean query "Are blue and purple ships touching and red and purple not touching (or vice versa)?" demonstrate that the model has captured underlying principles of what makes a good question, rather than just memorizing patterns.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clearly articulated, the model is described with sufficient detail, and the experiments are easy to understand. The authors are transparent about the model's limitations, which adds to the credibility of the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Domain Specificity and Scalability:** The primary limitation is the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar tailored to the "Battleship" domain. The grammar contains domain-specific primitives (e.g., `size`, `color`, ship names) and structures. While the authors correctly note that many components (logical operators, lambda calculus) are general, it is unclear how the approach would scale to new, more complex domains without substantial and potentially prohibitive manual engineering of a new grammar. This challenge is acknowledged but is a significant barrier to broader applicability.

2.  **Detachment from Natural Language:** The model operates purely on semantic program representations, bypassing the difficult problems of parsing natural language questions into these forms and generating fluent text from them. This makes it a model of the cognitive process of selecting a query, but not a full, end-to-end question-asking system. While this is a reasonable simplification for a foundational research paper, it limits the immediate practical utility.

3.  **Computational Intractability:** The model relies on importance sampling over a very large set of generated programs to approximate the partition function and gradients for training. This appears to be computationally intensive and may not scale to domains with more complex grammars or larger hypothesis spaces. While an acceptable approximation for this study, it raises questions about the feasibility of the approach in more complex, real-world scenarios.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Analysis of Learned Weights:** The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the learned feature weights (`theta`). What are the relative weights assigned to EIG, complexity, and different answer types? This would provide valuable insight into the trade-offs the model learned and how they might reflect human cognitive biases (e.g., a strong penalty for complexity, a preference for Boolean questions).

*   **Roadmap for Grammar Learning:** To address the domain-specificity weakness, the authors could elaborate on future directions for learning parts of the grammar. Could grammar primitives be induced from a corpus of text and a corresponding world model? Could large language models be used to propose new rules or primitives for a new domain, which could then be refined and incorporated into this framework? A brief discussion of this would make the path to scaling the approach clearer.

*   **Error Analysis:** The correlation between model predictions and human frequencies varies significantly across contexts (Figure 2). An analysis of the contexts where the model performs poorly could be very insightful. Are there specific types of questions or game situations that the current feature set fails to capture? For example, does the model struggle with questions that involve more complex spatial or strategic reasoning?

### Final Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the study of intelligent inquiry. The framing of question asking as program generation is a powerful idea that is well-executed and convincingly evaluated against human data. The work is thought-provoking and is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research at the intersection of machine learning, AI, and cognitive science. While the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar limits its immediate applicability, this is a characteristic of foundational research that opens up new avenues rather than incrementally improving existing ones. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
