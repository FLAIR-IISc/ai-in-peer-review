## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, yield an answer. This recasts the problem into one of program synthesis, allowing for a more structured and computationally tractable approach to understanding human questioning behavior. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions collected from humans playing a spatial reasoning game (Battleship-like), demonstrating its ability to predict question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader fields of active learning, dialog systems, and computational linguistics. It distinguishes itself from existing work by moving beyond simple label requests (active learning) and canned questions (dialog systems), and by focusing on goal-directed question asking in a novel scenario, circumventing the need for massive datasets required by VQG systems.  The connection to formal linguistics (compositionality, partition over possible worlds) and cognitive science (language of thought) is well articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The program generation perspective on question asking is a significant and genuinely novel contribution. It provides a principled way to represent and reason about questions beyond natural language processing alone.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed probabilistic model, while complex, is well-defined and the optimization procedure is clearly explained. The use of importance sampling to address the intractability of the normalization constant is a sensible approach.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The results on the human question dataset are encouraging. The model's ability to predict question frequencies and generate novel questions demonstrates its capacity to capture aspects of human questioning behavior. The ablation studies (lesioning features) effectively highlight the importance of different components.
* **Clarity of Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core concepts are explained clearly, and the authors provide sufficient detail to understand the technical aspects of the approach.
* **Strong Connection to Cognitive Science:** Grounding the work in concepts like compositionality and the "language of thought" adds depth and motivates the approach from a cognitive perspective.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge this, the reliance on the Battleship-like game context is a limitation. The grammar, while somewhat general, is still tailored to this specific domain. It's unclear how easily the approach would generalize to other areas.
* **Complexity of the Model:** The model is complex, involving multiple features, a probabilistic grammar, and importance sampling.  This complexity makes it difficult to fully understand the contribution of each component and hinders reproducibility.  
* **Limited Qualitative Analysis of Generated Questions:** While the paper shows some examples of generated questions, a more thorough qualitative evaluation, perhaps with human judgment on question quality and relevance, would be beneficial.
* **Informativeness Metric:** The EIG metric, while a good starting point, might not fully capture the nuanced ways humans evaluate question usefulness. It assumes a rational agent with a clear goal, which may not always be the case. 
* **Scaling:** The authors mention limitations in scaling due to the computational cost of evaluating the grammar. This is a significant concern for applying the method to more complex domains.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and promising framework for modeling question asking.
* The technical contribution is significant, and the empirical results are encouraging.
* The work is well-motivated and grounded in relevant research from multiple fields.
* The ablation studies provide valuable insights into the importance of different features.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The domain specificity of the approach limits its generalizability.
* The complexity of the model hinders understanding and reproducibility.
* The qualitative evaluation of generated questions is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is **Strong Accept**. While the domain specificity and model complexity are valid concerns, the novelty, technical soundness, and promising empirical results outweigh these weaknesses. The paper presents a significant step forward in understanding human question asking and has the potential to impact both active learning and computational cognitive science. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work and suggest avenues for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically a more detailed qualitative analysis of the generated questions and a clearer discussion of the challenges of scaling the approach to more complex domains.



**Rating:** 8/10
