

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes modeling human question asking as program generation, where questions are represented as programs that can be executed on possible world states to produce answers. The authors develop a grammar-based approach to generate questions in a Battleship-like game domain, using a probabilistic model with features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 human questions from 40 players in 18 game contexts.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a technically sound formal framework with clear definitions of the program grammar and probabilistic model. The evaluation methodology is rigorous, with proper ablation studies comparing different feature sets. The grammar successfully captures compositionality in human questions, and the model demonstrates strong correlation (Spearman's 0.64) with human question frequencies. The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly regarding domain specificity.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite its technical merits, the paper's contribution lacks sufficient significance for NIPS. The most concerning issue is the extremely narrow scope of the approach - it only works for a single, highly constrained domain (a Battleship-like game with specific rules about ship configurations). The grammar is tailored specifically to this domain, with features like "ship size" and "color" that would need complete re-engineering for any other application. The paper fails to demonstrate how this approach would generalize to other domains, despite claiming to model "open-ended human question asking."

The novelty claim is overstated. The core idea of modeling questions as programs has been explored in prior work, such as Lake et al.'s "Human-level concept learning through probabilistic program induction" (Science, 2015) and Wang et al.'s "Building a Semantic Parser Overnight" (ACL, 2015). The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from these existing works. The evaluation is limited to a single game domain, with no comparison to established active learning methods (e.g., query-by-committee, uncertainty sampling) that might serve as more relevant baselines.

Most critically, the paper fails to demonstrate that this approach actually improves performance on the learning task. The authors claim to model "human-like question asking," but they don't show that their model leads to faster or more accurate learning in the game compared to standard active learning approaches. The evaluation focuses on predicting human question frequencies rather than showing that the questions generated lead to better learning outcomes.

The paper also makes unsubstantiated claims about "human-like" question asking. The questions generated by the model (e.g., "What is the column of the top left of the tiles that have the color of the bottom right corner of the board?") are more complex than typical human questions, and the paper doesn't establish that these are actually preferred by humans or lead to better learning.

**Significance concerns:**
The paper's contribution is largely limited to a specific game domain with no clear pathway to broader application. For a NIPS paper, the significance should be demonstrated through a generalizable approach that advances the state of the art in active learning or question answering. The current work doesn't meet this bar. The authors acknowledge the domain-specific nature of their grammar ("some aspects of our grammar are specific to the Battleship domain"), yet fail to provide sufficient evidence that this is a necessary limitation or how to overcome it.

**Recommendation:**
While technically sound within its narrow scope, this paper does not make a significant contribution to the field of active learning or question answering that would justify publication at NIPS. The approach is too domain-specific, lacks meaningful comparison to established methods, and fails to demonstrate practical advantages over simpler alternatives. The authors should substantially expand the evaluation to multiple domains and compare their approach to standard active learning baselines before considering this work suitable for NIPS.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it demonstrated generalizability to multiple domains, provided clear evidence that the questions generated actually improve learning performance, and more thoroughly distinguished its approach from existing work in program synthesis for question generation. As presented, the contribution is incremental at best and lacks the broader significance expected for NIPS.
