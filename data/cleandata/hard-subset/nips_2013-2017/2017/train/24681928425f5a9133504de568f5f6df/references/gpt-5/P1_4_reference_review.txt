Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a computational framework for human question asking that models questions as executable programs. A typed, compositional grammar (lambda-calculus/LISP style) defines a space of programs/questions; a log-linear generative model over this space trades off informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (via a PCFG-based length/structure prior), answer type preferences, and a relevance filter. The model is trained to match the distribution of human questions in a Battleship-like game (605 natural-language questions from 40 participants across 18 contexts, two dropped for computation). Evaluation uses leave-one-context-out: fit on 15 contexts, predict on the held-out. Comparisons include ablations removing informativeness, complexity, or type features. The full model best predicts held-out log-likelihoods (with bootstrap support), achieves an average Spearman correlation of 0.64 between energy and human frequencies, and can synthesize novel, context-sensitive questions. The work connects to active learning (beyond label queries), Bayesian experimental design (EIG), program induction and languages of thought, and semantic parsing-based question answering. Related efforts in VQG/visual dialog and goal-directed dialog are discussed, highlighting this work’s emphasis on intentional, goal-directed, compositional question generation.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation: modeling questions as programs over possible worlds grounds meaning, compositionality, and computability, enabling Bayesian evaluation of expected utility.
- Predictive quantitative model: a log-linear mixture of EIG, complexity, and type features fits human question distributions and explains the observed lack of correlation with EIG alone by introducing a cognitively plausible complexity penalty.
- Compositional generalization: the grammar captures productivity; the model can generate novel, useful questions not seen in training, with equivalence checking to avoid trivial paraphrases.
- Solid connections to prior literature in Bayesian cognitive modeling, program induction, and question semantics; positions the work relative to VQG/visual dialog and active learning.
- Transparent limitations and ablations: the importance of complexity is convincingly demonstrated (complexity-agnostic model performs poorly).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Domain specificity and knowledge engineering: The grammar and hypothesis space are tailored to Battleship; natural language is mapped to programs offline rather than learned. It is unclear how much of the grammar is reusable and how robust the approach is across domains. Suggestion: demonstrate portability by adding a second domain with minimal new rules, or quantify the proportion of domain-general constructs reused.
- Data scale and diversity: The dataset (605 questions, 18 contexts) is small, with many singletons, limiting statistical power. Suggestion: augment with new contexts or a new task, and report additional metrics (top-k recall of human questions, KL divergence over question types).
- Evaluation signals: The correlation between model energy and human frequencies averages 0.64, while the information-agnostic variant reports 0.65, raising questions about the incremental contribution of EIG to rank-order predictions (despite better log-likelihood). Clarify this discrepancy and include confidence intervals for correlations; analyze contexts where EIG helps most.
- Approximations and stability: Importance sampling over 150k proposals and 100k gradient steps is heavy; Z and expectations are approximated with the same proposal distribution used also as the complexity measure (−log q(x)), potentially entangling modeling and inference. Suggestion: test robustness to alternative proposal grammars and to different length cutoffs; report variance of Z estimates and convergence diagnostics.
- Human evaluation of generated questions: Qualitative examples are compelling, but there is no user study verifying that generated questions are judged as helpful/understandable or that they have appropriate one-word answers. Suggestion: collect human ratings on novelty, usefulness, and answerability for sampled questions.
- Removal of “draw” questions and contexts: Dropping 14 questions and 2 contexts for computational reasons may bias evaluation. Provide sensitivity analyses including/down-weighting these cases, or approximate the stochastic semantics differently (e.g., via enumerating bounded draws).
- Cognitive plausibility of complexity prior: −log q(x) reflects the PCFG’s production probabilities under uniform rule selection, not empirically measured cognitive cost (e.g., working memory, answerer effort). Suggestion: explore alternative length/structure penalties (token count, nesting depth, number of predicates) and compare fit.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a coherent probabilistic formulation and appropriate ablations. Claims are mostly supported by quantitative results, though more detail on bootstrap procedures, confidence intervals, and robustness would improve confidence. The computational burden and the reliance on approximations are acknowledged; further diagnostics would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The formal definition of the grammar and features is clear, though crucial grammar tables are deferred to the supplement; include at least a compact core in the main text. Clarify how natural language questions were mapped to programs and how equivalence classes were determined (mutual information threshold, handling of symmetry). Resolve the correlation vs. log-likelihood discrepancy explicitly in the main text.

Originality
The central idea—treating question asking as program generation with Bayesian evaluation—is novel and timely, distinct from prior active learning (label queries), VQG/dialog systems, and even prior RSA-style models of question meaning/utility. The combination of a compositional program space with a generative model of question preferences is a new contribution.

Significance
Conceptually significant: it advances a rigorous, generative view of question asking that could impact interactive ML, human-in-the-loop systems, and cognitive modeling. Practical impact is currently limited by domain specificity and data scale; demonstrating cross-domain generalization or integrating learned semantic parsing would broaden significance.

Detailed questions for authors
- How are natural language strings mapped to programs (annotation procedure, inter-annotator agreement)? Could errors there affect evaluation?
- How sensitive are results to the maximum program length and to the proposal grammar? Any signs of mode dropping in the importance sampler?
- What is the exact bootstrap protocol for comparing log-likelihoods? Across which sources of randomness is resampling performed?
- How is answer type determined for complex lambda/set expressions? Any ambiguous cases?
- Can the model incorporate answerer cost or answer ambiguity (e.g., human errors) in its utility?

Pros
- Elegant, principled framework unifying compositionality and Bayesian utility.
- Predicts human question distributions and explains complexity vs. informativeness tradeoff.
- Generates novel, context-appropriate questions.
- Strong connections to prior work; clear positioning.

Cons
- Heavy domain engineering; limited dataset and single domain.
- Evaluation relies on approximations; some metrics favor ablations.
- No human evaluation of generated outputs; dropped items for tractability.
- Complexity prior tied to proposal distribution rather than independent cognitive measure.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a compelling, well-motivated framework with solid initial evidence. Addressing robustness, clarifying the correlation/likelihood results, and adding a small cross-domain demonstration or human evaluation would further strengthen the contribution.
