- Proposes modeling human question asking as program generation, representing questions as executable programs targeting world states.  
- Evaluates the model on a Battleship-like dataset, showing it predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions.  
- Positions the work against active learning and dialog systems, emphasizing novelty in modeling intentional, goal-directed questions.  
- Connects to formal semantics (questions as partitions), concepts-as-programs, compositionality, and Bayesian information gain.  
- Key strength: novel and original framework that meaningfully extends beyond simple active learning queries.  
- Key strength: rigorous formalization using a lambda-calculus/LISP-inspired grammar.  
- Key strength: empirical validation demonstrating plausible question generation and alignment with human data.  
- Key strength: strong theoretical grounding across linguistics, cognitive science, and Bayesian reasoning.  
- Key strength: clear and well-written presentation.  
- Main weakness: grammar and evaluation are highly domain-specific (Battleship), limiting generalizability.  
- Main weakness: model complexity (grammar, probabilistic model, feature engineering) reduces accessibility and clarity.  
- Main weakness: reliance on importance sampling for normalizing constant may give poor approximations depending on the proposal distribution.  
- Main weakness: limited qualitative analysis and error analysis of generated questions.  
- Main weakness: informativeness features depend on a Bayesian ideal observer, which may not reflect human cognition.  
- Overall: reviewer recommends acceptance, citing significant novelty and promising results despite concerns about generality and complexity.
