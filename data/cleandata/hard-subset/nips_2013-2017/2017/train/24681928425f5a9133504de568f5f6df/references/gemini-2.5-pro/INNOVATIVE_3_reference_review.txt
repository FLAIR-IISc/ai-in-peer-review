**Summary**
This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling context-sensitive, goal-directed human question asking. The core and most significant idea is to formalize questions as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. Under this framework, the problem of deciding what question to ask is recast as a problem of program synthesis. The authors develop a probabilistic generative model over the space of possible programs, defined by a custom grammar. The model's probability distribution is log-linear, with an energy function that is a weighted sum of several features, including the question's Expected Information Gain (EIG), its complexity (derived from the grammar), and its answer type. The model's parameters are fit to a dataset of human-generated questions from a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The authors evaluate their model by predicting the distribution of human questions in held-out game contexts and by demonstrating its ability to generate novel, plausible questions that were not in the original dataset.

**Strengths**
The primary strength of this paper is its profound novelty and ambition. The central contribution—reframing question asking from a selection or retrieval problem to one of program synthesis—is a significant conceptual leap for the field of active learning. Most work in this area, even sophisticated approaches, is confined to querying for labels or simple features. This paper proposes a path toward machines that can ask the kind of rich, compositional, and creative questions that humans use to learn. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-level conceptual contribution that can open up new avenues of research.

A second major strength is the model's explanatory power. By modeling question generation as a trade-off between informativeness (EIG) and complexity, the authors provide a compelling account of human behavior. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate that neither EIG nor complexity alone is sufficient; the model's success hinges on balancing these factors. The finding that the complexity-agnostic model performs exceptionally poorly is particularly insightful, highlighting that humans are not simply myopic information-maximizers but are constrained by a preference for cognitive simplicity. This provides a valuable insight for both machine learning and cognitive science.

Finally, the qualitative results are excellent. The ability of the model to generate novel, non-trivial, and contextually useful questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") that were not asked by humans in the dataset is a powerful demonstration of its generative capacity. This shows the model is not merely memorizing or interpolating between seen examples but has captured some of the underlying compositional structure of intelligent inquiry.

**Weaknesses**
The most apparent weakness is the reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar. The grammar is tailored to the Battleship game, with primitives like `size(S)` and `touch(S1, S2)`. While the authors correctly point out that many components are domain-general (logical operators, set functions, etc.), a significant engineering effort would be required to port this system to a new domain. This limits the immediate practical applicability of the work and raises questions about how the necessary domain knowledge could be acquired more automatically in the future.

Secondly, the computational tractability of the approach is a concern. The authors rely on importance sampling to approximate the intractable partition function and gradients, and they had to exclude certain contexts and a grammar rule due to computational complexity. While this is a reasonable approach for a proof-of-concept, it suggests that scaling this method to more complex domains with vastly larger program spaces could be a major challenge.

Lastly, the model operates on semantic representations ("language of thought") and bypasses the difficult problem of parsing natural language into these programmatic forms and verbalizing programs back into natural language. While this is a deliberate and reasonable simplification to focus on the core problem of question generation, it is a non-trivial gap that must be bridged for any real-world application.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of question asking as program generation is a powerful and original contribution that could significantly influence future work in active learning, dialog systems, and AI more broadly.
2.  **Conceptual Shift:** The paper moves the field beyond incremental improvements in standard active learning settings and tackles the much more ambitious goal of rich, human-like inquiry.
3.  **Strong Explanatory Power:** The model provides valuable cognitive insights into the trade-offs humans make when asking questions, particularly the balance between informativeness and complexity.
4.  **Impressive Generative Results:** The demonstration of generating novel, creative questions validates the power of the compositional framework.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
1.  **Domain Specificity:** The hand-crafted grammar limits the generalizability of the current implementation.
2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The reliance on approximate inference in a vast search space raises questions about the method's applicability to more complex problems.
3.  **Incomplete System:** The model does not interface with natural language directly, which is a major component of a full question-asking system.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper is a quintessential example of the kind of bold, high-risk, high-reward research that NIPS should champion. While it has clear limitations in terms of methodological completeness and domain-generality, these are far overshadowed by the profound novelty and potential impact of its core idea. The reframing of question asking as program generation is a significant conceptual leap that provides both a powerful new tool for machine learning and a compelling cognitive model of human intelligence. This work opens up a genuinely new research direction that others are likely to build upon. The weaknesses are acceptable for a foundational paper introducing a new paradigm. I strongly recommend its acceptance.
