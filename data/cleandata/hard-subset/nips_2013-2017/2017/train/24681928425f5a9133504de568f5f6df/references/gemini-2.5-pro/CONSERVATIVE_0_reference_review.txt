**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human goal-directed question asking by framing it as a program generation task. The central idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. These programs, when executed on a world state (in this case, a game board configuration), produce an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic model over the space of possible programs, defined by a hand-crafted grammar. The probability of a program (question) is determined by a log-linear model incorporating features such as Expected Information Gain (EIG), program complexity, and answer type. The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game, with the goals of predicting the frequency of human questions and generating novel, plausible questions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The approach of modeling question asking as probabilistic program synthesis is original and offers a conceptually interesting alternative to dominant end-to-end neural approaches. It moves beyond simple label requests in active learning to a much richer query space.

2.  **Compositionality and Interpretability:** The framework inherently captures the compositional nature of questions, allowing complex queries to be built from simpler conceptual primitives. The resulting programs are interpretable, which is a significant advantage over opaque deep learning models.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its motivation, model structure, and experimental setup. The connection to cognitive science principles like the "language of thought" provides a solid conceptual grounding.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Competitive Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this work is the complete absence of comparisons to established machine learning baselines. The authors only compare their full model against ablated versions of itself. This makes it impossible to assess whether this complex, bespoke machinery provides any real advantage over simpler, more conventional methods. For instance, a standard sequence-to-sequence model (or even a simpler classifier over a fixed set of question templates) could be trained to predict a question given a representation of the game context. Without such a baseline, the reported performance (e.g., log-likelihood, correlation) is difficult to interpret. The authors argue that neural models are data-hungry, but this does not preclude testing a reasonably sized model on their dataset to provide a point of comparison.

2.  **Dependence on a Hand-Crafted Grammar:** The entire framework is predicated on a manually designed, domain-specific grammar. While the authors claim some components are domain-general (e.g., logical operators), the core semantic primitives (`size`, `color`, `touching`, etc.) are tied directly to the Battleship game. This raises serious concerns about the scalability and generalizability of the approach. The amount of expert knowledge and engineering required to define such a grammar for any new, reasonably complex domain seems prohibitive. This limitation severely curtails the paper's claim of being a step toward "more capable active learning machines" in a general sense.

3.  **Limited and Niche Evaluation:** The model is evaluated on a single, relatively simple toy-world dataset. While this dataset is interesting, validation in such a constrained environment is insufficient to support the broad claims made. The success of the method may be heavily dependent on the specific properties of this game. Furthermore, the quantitative evaluation is not entirely convincing. A Spearman's correlation of 0.64 is moderate, and the qualitative evaluation relies on a few cherry-picked examples of generated questions. It is unclear how many nonsensical or low-quality novel questions the model generates.

4.  **Computational Intractability:** The methodology relies on approximating the partition function and gradients via importance sampling over a large set of programs (150,000 samples). The authors also note they had to remove a grammar rule because it led to "exponentially complex computations." This suggests that the approach may not scale to domains with richer grammars and larger program spaces, further limiting its practical significance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly original and thought-provoking conceptual framework for active learning and question asking.
*   The focus on structured, interpretable, and compositional representations is a valuable contribution in an era dominated by black-box models.
*   The work could inspire new research directions at the intersection of program synthesis, cognitive science, and active learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The empirical evaluation lacks rigor due to the absence of any comparison to standard machine learning baselines.
*   The heavy reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar makes the approach unscalable and difficult to generalize, limiting its practical impact.
*   The evaluation is confined to a single, small-scale toy domain, which is insufficient to validate the method's effectiveness.
*   The inference procedure appears computationally expensive and has known scaling issues.

**Conclusion**

While the paper introduces a clever and original idea, it currently reads more as a proof-of-concept or a computational cognitive science paper than a contribution to the general machine learning community at NIPS. The lack of rigorous empirical validation against established methods is a critical flaw. For the claims to be convincing, the authors would need to demonstrate the superiority of their approach over standard baselines, and ideally, show its applicability in more than one highly constrained, game-like domain. As it stands, the work is too preliminary and its practical utility and scalability are unproven.
