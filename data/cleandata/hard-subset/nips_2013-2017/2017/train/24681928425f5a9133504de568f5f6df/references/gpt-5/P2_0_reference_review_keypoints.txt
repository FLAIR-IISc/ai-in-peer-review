- Proposes a framework that treats questions as executable, typed programs in a lambda-calculus grammar and scores them with a log-linear model.  
- Uses features for informativeness (EIG), complexity (negative log PCFG prob), answer type, and task relevance; parameters fit by approximate MLE with importance sampling.  
- Evaluated on 605 human questions from a Battleship-style information-search game using leave-one-context-out fits; model outperforms ablations and can sample novel questions.  
- Novelty: generates questions (not just answers) by composing executable semantics and optimizing utilities, combining ideas from LoT, active learning, and program induction.  
- Modeling choices are sensible and grounded in prior work; hand-engineered features and EIG are appropriate for small data.  
- Approximate inference/learning is under-specified: importance sampling proposal mismatch, unclear resampling/iterations, no convergence diagnostics or sensitivity analyses.  
- NL-to-program translation process is not described: annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, and error rates are missing.  
- Computational limits: two contexts with large hypothesis spaces and 14 questions using a probabilistic operator were dropped, suggesting scalability issues and potential evaluation bias.  
- Informativeness contribution is weak: full model better on held-out log-likelihood but slightly worse rank-correlation with human frequencies than an info-agnostic variant; complexity appears more decisive.  
- Quantitative evaluation is limited to a single, small, sparse domain; novel question generation lacks human evaluation.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized; grammar and feature descriptions are understandable but rely on supplementary material.  
- Missing reproducibility details: NL-to-program protocol, exact sampling/training procedure, code, learned feature weights and their variability.  
- Originality is strong: the specific combination of program-based question generation guided by EIG is novel and interesting.  
- Significance is conceptually promising for goal-directed question generation but practically limited by domain specificity, manual semantic representations, and scalability.  
- Ablations convincingly show the importance of complexity and answer type in modeling human questions.  
- Pros: clear compositional formulation, integration of rational utility with learned program prior, insightful ablations, and public dataset.  
- Cons: single small domain, under-specified annotation, thin approximate inference details, weak evidence for EIG improving ranking, no human study or strong generative baselines.  
- Suggested improvements: release NL-to-program protocol and IAA, run robustness analyses (seeds, proposals, sample sizes), report feature weights with CIs, compare stronger baselines, add human evaluations, explore cost-sensitive utilities and amortized inference, and test transfer to another domain or NL interface.  
- Overall: thoughtful, original, and potentially impactful work but empirically narrow and missing key methodological details—borderline accept.
