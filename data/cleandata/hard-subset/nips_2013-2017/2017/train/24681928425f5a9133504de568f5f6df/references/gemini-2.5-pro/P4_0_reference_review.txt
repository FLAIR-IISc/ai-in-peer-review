### Summary

This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The central idea is that the semantic content of a question can be represented as a program which, when executed on a world state, returns an answer. This formulation naturally captures the compositional and generative nature of human inquiry. The authors develop a system comprising three main parts: 1) a LISP-like programming language and an associated grammar that defines the space of possible questions, 2) a set of features to evaluate the quality of a question, including its expected information gain (EIG), complexity, and answer type, and 3) a log-linear probabilistic model that learns to weight these features to predict the distribution of questions asked by humans in a specific context.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions collected from human participants playing a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The authors demonstrate that their model can predict the frequencies of human questions in unseen game contexts and can also generate novel, contextually relevant questions that were not in the original dataset. This work relates to prior research in several areas. It extends the scope of active learning, which typically focuses on simple label queries, to a much richer query space. It also provides a more structured, goal-directed alternative to data-driven dialogue systems and visual question generation models, which often lack explicit reasoning about informativeness. Finally, it builds upon a line of work in cognitive science that models concepts and reasoning as probabilistic program induction (e.g., Lake et al., 2015; Goodman et al., 2008).

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed probabilistic model is well-defined and principled. The choice of a log-linear model to combine features of informativeness, complexity, and form is a standard and sound approach. The features themselves are well-motivated: EIG captures the goal-directed nature of question asking, while the complexity penalty, derived from the grammar, reflects a natural preference for simplicity. The optimization procedure, using gradient ascent with importance sampling to handle the intractable partition function, is an appropriate method for this class of model.

The claims are well-supported by the experimental results. The leave-one-out cross-validation provides a solid basis for model comparison. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate that each component of the model—informativeness, complexity, and answer type—contributes positively to its predictive performance. The finding that the complexity-agnostic model performs very poorly is particularly insightful, highlighting that humans do not simply maximize information gain but trade it off against question complexity. The authors are careful to report not just mean performance but also the robustness of their findings using bootstrapping. The work is presented as a complete piece of research, from data collection and formalization to model implementation and thorough evaluation.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, and the core idea of "questions as programs" is introduced intuitively. The structure of the paper follows a logical progression from the dataset, to the model's components (grammar, probabilistic model, features), to the evaluation. The authors provide concrete examples of questions and their corresponding program representations (e.g., "How long is the blue ship?" as `(size Blue)`), which greatly aids reader comprehension. The description of the model and the optimization process is sufficiently detailed for an expert to understand the methodology. The figures and tables are clear and effectively support the main results. Figure 3, in particular, provides a compelling qualitative demonstration of the model's generative capabilities.

### Originality

The paper's main contribution is its originality. The formulation of question asking as probabilistic program synthesis is a highly novel approach that diverges significantly from prior work in active learning and dialogue systems. While the component ideas (program induction, EIG, log-linear models) are not new in isolation, their combination to create a predictive model of open-ended, goal-directed question asking is a unique and powerful synthesis. The work is clearly distinguished from related approaches. Unlike standard active learning, it operates over a compositional, semantically rich query space. Unlike many neural question generation models, it does not require massive datasets and incorporates an explicit, goal-directed objective function (EIG), allowing it to generate useful questions in novel scenarios. Related work is adequately cited and contextualized.

### Significance

The results presented are significant and likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners. For the machine learning community, particularly in active learning and human-AI interaction, this paper opens up a new avenue for developing machines that can learn more efficiently by asking more sophisticated, human-like questions. The framework provides a blueprint for moving beyond simple label requests. For computational cognitive science, the paper offers the first quantitative, predictive model of open-ended human question asking, providing strong evidence that people balance informativeness and simplicity when formulating queries.

The primary limitation, as the authors forthrightly acknowledge, is the reliance on a domain-specific, hand-engineered grammar. Extending the approach to new and more complex domains would require significant knowledge engineering effort. Furthermore, the model operates on semantic representations and bypasses the challenging problem of parsing natural language into programs. Despite these limitations, the conceptual contribution is substantial and provides a strong foundation upon which future work can build, for instance by integrating grammar induction techniques or semantic parsers.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **High Originality:** The core idea of modeling question asking as probabilistic program generation is novel, elegant, and powerful.
2.  **Significant Contribution:** The work represents a substantial step toward more capable active learning agents and provides a new computational framework for studying human cognition.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model is principled, and the experimental evaluation is rigorous and convincing, including well-designed ablation studies.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible and easy to follow.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  **Generalizability:** The approach relies on a hand-crafted grammar for a constrained task, raising questions about its scalability and applicability to more open-ended domains without substantial manual effort.
2.  **Scope:** The system sidesteps the natural language understanding component, operating purely on pre-defined semantic primitives. This limits its immediate practical application as a natural language interface.
