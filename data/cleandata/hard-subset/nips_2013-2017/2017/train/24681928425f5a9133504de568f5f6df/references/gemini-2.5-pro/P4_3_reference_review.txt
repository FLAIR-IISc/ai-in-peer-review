**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The central idea is to represent questions as executable programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. When executed on a possible world state, a program returns an answer. This formalism recasts the challenge of deciding *what question to ask* into a problem of synthesizing a useful program.

The authors evaluate their model using a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. They define a grammar that specifies the space of possible questions (programs) and propose a probabilistic log-linear model to score these programs. The model's score for a given question is a weighted combination of several features, most notably its Expected Information Gain (EIG) for resolving uncertainty about the game state, its complexity (a penalty for longer programs), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, number), and its relevance to the game.

Model parameters are fit to the human data via gradient ascent, aiming to maximize the likelihood of the questions people actually asked. The authors demonstrate that their full model outperforms several ablated versions (e.g., one that is agnostic to information gain, another to complexity), highlighting the importance of balancing these factors. The model successfully predicts the frequencies of human questions in held-out game contexts and, perhaps more impressively, can generate novel, contextually appropriate, and semantically complex questions that were not observed in the human data.

This work connects to and extends several areas. It generalizes traditional active learning, which typically queries for simple labels, to a rich, structured space of programmatic queries. It contrasts with contemporary dialog systems by focusing on the flexible, goal-directed generation of novel questions rather than selecting from pre-specified templates or mimicking patterns in large, non-goal-oriented corpora. The approach is also philosophically grounded in cognitive science research on compositionality and the "language of thought," applying ideas from concept learning as program induction to the problem of question generation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's core contribution—framing context-sensitive question asking as probabilistic program generation—is highly original and significant. It provides a formal, interpretable, and powerful framework for a complex cognitive ability that is largely beyond the reach of current AI systems. This work opens a promising new research direction for active learning, moving the field beyond simple label requests toward more sophisticated, human-like information gathering. The potential for other researchers to build upon these ideas is substantial.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed model is technically sound. The use of a log-linear model over a program space defined by a grammar is appropriate and well-executed. The choice of features—combining Bayesian informativeness (EIG) with a complexity penalty and priors over answer types—is well-motivated and provides a principled way to model the trade-offs inherent in human cognition. The optimization procedure is standard, and the ablation study provides clear evidence for the contribution of each component of the model.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining their formal framework, and detailing the experimental setup. The distinction between their approach and related work in active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation is made explicit and convincing. The examples provided, both of human questions and model-generated ones, are illustrative and effectively convey the model's capabilities.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough. The quantitative results, showing strong correlations with human question frequencies and outperforming ablated models, provide solid support for the authors' claims. The qualitative results are particularly compelling; the ability of the model to generate novel, clever, and contextually useful questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") demonstrates that it has captured meaningful structure about the task and is not merely memorizing patterns.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Generalizability and Scalability:** The model relies on a hand-crafted grammar tailored to the "Battleship" domain. While the authors correctly note that many components (e.g., logical operators, arithmetic) are domain-general, a significant amount of domain-specific engineering is still required to define the core predicates (e.g., `size`, `color`). This raises questions about the effort required to apply this framework to new domains and its scalability to more complex environments with larger state and action spaces. The authors acknowledge this as a key area for future work.

2.  **Scope (Natural Language Interface):** The system operates entirely on the level of semantic program representations. It bypasses the challenging but crucial problems of parsing natural language questions into these program forms and of generating natural language text from a selected program. While the paper's focus is on the cognitive problem of *what to ask* rather than the linguistic one of *how to phrase it*, this gap limits the model's immediate applicability as a complete question-asking agent.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The model's inference is computationally intensive. The normalization constant and expected feature values are intractable and must be approximated using importance sampling over a large number of sampled programs. The authors note that they had to exclude two game contexts from their cross-validation procedure "for computational reasons" due to large hypothesis spaces. This suggests that the approach may face significant computational hurdles in more complex, open-ended domains.

**Arguments Pro/Con Acceptance**

*   **Pro:** The paper presents a highly novel, technically sound, and well-executed idea that opens up a new avenue for research in active learning and AI. The results are strong, and the potential impact is high. The clarity of the writing makes a complex topic accessible.
*   **Con:** The reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar limits the immediate generalizability of the approach. The model does not address the natural language interface, which is a key component of a full question-asking system.

In summary, while the weaknesses regarding scalability and the lack of a natural language interface are valid, they are characteristic of foundational work that introduces a new paradigm. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and potential significance are substantial. The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field by formalizing and providing the first predictive model of open-ended, goal-directed question asking.
