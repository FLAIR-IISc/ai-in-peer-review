- Proposes modeling human question asking as program generation where questions are programs executed on world states to yield answers.  
- Demonstrates the approach on a Battleship-like dataset, predicting human question frequencies and generating novel human-like questions.  
- Positions the work relative to active learning, dialog systems, VQG, and formal linguistic ideas like partitioning possible worlds and Language of Thought.  
- Novel conceptual contribution: representing questions as executable programs captures compositional and computational aspects of questioning.  
- Strong empirical validation: model predicts human question frequencies on the Battleship dataset.  
- Generative capability: model can synthesize novel, plausible human-like questions (qualitative examples shown).  
- Well-defined probabilistic model combining informativeness (EIG) and complexity with feature engineering.  
- Paper is clear and well-written with effective motivation and explanations.  
- Limitation: grammar is domain-specific to Battleship and excluding operations (e.g., “draw”) questions limits generality.  
- Scalability concern: importance sampling for normalizing constant may not scale to larger, more complex domains.  
- Evaluation of generated questions is largely qualitative; lacks rigorous quantitative measures of human-likeness beyond frequency prediction.  
- Heavy reliance on hand-engineered features raises questions about learning from raw data; no ablation study provided.  
- Missing comparisons to simpler baselines (e.g., frequency-only or EIG-only models) to quantify added value.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel idea, promising empirical results, potential cross-disciplinary impact, and strong presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited generality, computational scalability issues, and somewhat subjective generation evaluation.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions; the contribution is significant despite limitations.  
- Suggested revision: discuss how the grammar and approach could generalize to other domains.  
- Suggested revision: provide a detailed scalability/complexity analysis of the importance sampling method.  
- Suggested revision: include quantitative metrics for evaluating generated-question human-likeness beyond frequency prediction.  
- Suggested revision: add comparisons to simpler baselines and an ablation study to quantify feature contributions.
