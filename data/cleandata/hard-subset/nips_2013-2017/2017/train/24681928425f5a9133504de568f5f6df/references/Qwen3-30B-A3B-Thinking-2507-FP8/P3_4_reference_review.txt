

# Peer Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that, when executed on possible world states, produce answers. The authors argue that humans construct questions through compositional building blocks (e.g., predicates like size(x)) and that these questions can be viewed as programs evaluating to specific answers (e.g., True/False, numbers, colors). They collect a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like game, where players sought to identify hidden ship configurations. The authors develop a probabilistic model that uses a context-free grammar to generate question programs, incorporating features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel human-like questions.

This work connects to several relevant areas: program synthesis for question answering (e.g., Lake et al., 2015), cognitive science approaches to compositionality (Fodor, 1981), and active learning (Settles, 2010). Unlike previous work on visual question generation (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016) or goal-directed dialog systems (Bordes et al., 2016), this paper focuses on the question-asking process itself rather than question answering or dialogue generation.

## Strengths

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, the formalization of questions as executable programs is elegant and well-motivated, capturing both compositionality (the ability to combine concepts) and computability (the ability to evaluate questions on possible worlds). This approach bridges cognitive science and machine learning in a novel way. Second, the model successfully predicts human question frequencies (Spearman's ρ = 0.64) while also generating novel, human-like questions that weren't in the training data. Third, the authors carefully evaluate the necessity of each component through model variants (lesioning informativeness, complexity, and answer type features), demonstrating that all contribute meaningfully to the model's performance. The complexity-agnostic model performed particularly poorly (ρ = -0.36), highlighting an important insight about human question asking: people balance informativeness with simplicity.

The experimental design is strong, using leave-one-out cross-validation across 16 contexts with 40 participants each. The paper's acknowledgment of limitations—particularly the domain-specific nature of the grammar—is refreshing and honest. The supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is strong, several concerns warrant attention. First, the model's domain specificity is a significant limitation. The grammar and features are tailored to the Battleship game, and it's unclear how much engineering would be required to adapt this to other domains. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide a clear roadmap for generalization. For instance, how would the grammar change for a medical diagnosis domain versus a spatial reasoning game? The paper claims some features (logical connectives, set operations) are domain-general, but doesn't demonstrate this with evidence.

Second, the evaluation is limited to a single domain. While the Battleship game provides a controlled environment, it's unclear whether the model would generalize to more complex, real-world scenarios. The paper doesn't address how the model would handle noisy or incomplete world state representations, which are common in practical applications.

Third, the model's computational complexity isn't fully explored. The paper notes they had to limit the grammar to prevent exponential complexity (removing the "draw" rule), but it's unclear how this limitation would scale to more complex domains. The use of importance sampling for gradient ascent is reasonable, but the paper doesn't discuss how long training would take for more complex grammars.

Fourth, while the model generates novel questions, it's unclear how these questions would be implemented in a practical AI system. The paper mentions potential integration with computational linguistics tools, but doesn't provide specific mechanisms or discuss the natural language generation challenges.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper with a strong theoretical foundation and thorough experimental evaluation. The core insight—modeling questions as programs—is original and well-executed, providing a new perspective on human question asking that bridges cognitive science and machine learning. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of active learning by moving beyond simple label queries to more complex, human-like question asking.

While the domain specificity is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and provide a clear path for future work. The model's ability to generate novel human-like questions is particularly impressive and represents a step toward more human-like question-asking AI systems.

The paper meets all four NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound (with clear formalization and evaluation), well-written, original in its approach, and significant for advancing our understanding of human question asking and for potential applications in more capable active learning systems.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of active learning and computational cognitive science. While the domain-specific nature of the grammar is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and the core framework is sufficiently general to inspire future work. The model's ability to generate novel, human-like questions that weren't in the training data is particularly compelling.

The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of how the grammar could be generalized to other domains, perhaps with a brief example of how the grammar might be adapted for a different task. However, this is a minor issue that doesn't detract from the paper's overall quality.

This work provides a valuable framework for modeling human-like question asking, and I believe it will be of interest to the NIPS community.
