

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that can be executed on possible world states. The authors model question generation as a probabilistic program synthesis problem, where questions are constrained by a grammar that enables compositional and computable representations. The approach is evaluated on a human question dataset collected from a Battleship-style information-search game, where participants asked natural language questions to resolve ambiguous game contexts. The authors demonstrate that their model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

The work builds on related research in computational linguistics (e.g., Fodor's Language of Thought), program induction (Lake et al.), and active learning, but makes a distinctive contribution by framing question asking as program generation rather than as simple label requests or canned questions. It differs from VQG approaches (Mostafazadeh et al.) by focusing on goal-directed question asking rather than image captioning.

## Strengths
1. **Novel and Well-Motivated Framework**: The paper presents a compelling theoretical foundation for modeling question asking through program synthesis. The compositionality and computability insights are well-articulated and provide a principled way to model human question generation.

2. **Thorough Evaluation**: The authors conduct a rigorous evaluation through both quantitative (log-likelihood comparisons across model variants) and qualitative (novel question generation) methods. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of key features (informativeness, complexity, answer type).

3. **Strong Empirical Results**: The model successfully predicts human question frequencies (Spearman's correlation of 0.64) and generates novel, human-like questions that were not present in the training data. The generated questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") demonstrate genuine creativity and relevance.

4. **Clear Theoretical Contributions**: The paper makes important contributions to computational cognitive science by explaining how people balance informativeness and complexity when generating questions. This provides a first predictive model for open-ended human question asking.

## Weaknesses
1. **Domain Specificity**: The model is tightly coupled to the Battleship game domain. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't sufficiently address how their approach would generalize to other domains. The grammar and features are highly specific to this particular game, making it unclear how much domain knowledge would need to be re-engineered for new applications.

2. **Lack of Natural Language Interface**: The paper represents questions as semantic programs rather than natural language, and the authors don't adequately address how this would interface with natural language processing systems. The gap between the semantic representation and natural language output is a significant limitation for practical applications.

3. **Limited Comparison to Program Induction Approaches**: The paper doesn't sufficiently compare to existing program induction work (e.g., Lake et al. 2015, Wang et al. 2015), which also models questions as programs. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the paper's claim of novelty.

4. **Data Limitations**: The dataset is relatively small (605 questions from 40 participants), and the authors don't adequately address how this might impact the generalizability of their findings. The paper could benefit from discussing how the model might perform with larger, more diverse datasets.

5. **Complexity of the Grammar**: The grammar used is quite complex (with lambda expressions, set operators, etc.), and it's unclear how this would scale to more natural language scenarios. The paper could better justify the choice of grammar features.

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to computational cognitive science and active learning. The framework is theoretically sound, the evaluation is thorough, and the results are compelling. The work represents a significant step toward modeling human-like question asking in machines.

The main weaknesses relate to domain specificity and the lack of a clear path to natural language generation. These limitations are acknowledged by the authors but require more substantial discussion to strengthen the paper's broader impact.

## Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a novel and important contribution to understanding human question asking through a computational lens. While the domain specificity is a limitation, the framework itself is generalizable in principle, and the authors provide a clear path for future work in extending to other domains. The quality of the research, clarity of presentation, and significance of the contribution meet NeurIPS standards. With minor improvements addressing the domain generalization and natural language interface issues, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the field.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Expand the discussion of how the grammar could be generalized to other domains, perhaps by providing examples of how the grammar might be extended for a different task.
2. Include a more detailed comparison to existing program induction approaches (e.g., Lake et al., Wang et al.) to better establish the novelty of the contribution.
3. Add a brief discussion of how the semantic program representations could be mapped to natural language (perhaps referencing recent work in program-to-text generation).
4. Consider including a small-scale experiment demonstrating how the model might be extended to a different domain (even if just as a proof of concept).
5. Address the data limitations more thoroughly and discuss potential ways to collect more diverse question data in future work.
