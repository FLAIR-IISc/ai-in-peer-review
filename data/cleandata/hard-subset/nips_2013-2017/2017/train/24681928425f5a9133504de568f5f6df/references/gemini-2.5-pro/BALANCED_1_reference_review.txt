### **Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper presents a novel computational framework for goal-directed question asking, modeling it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The central idea is that a meaningful question can be represented as a program which, when executed on a world state, returns an answer. The authors develop a compositional grammar based on a LISP-like language to define the space of possible questions. To predict which questions humans are likely to ask in a given context, they propose a log-linear model that scores programs based on a weighted combination of features. These features include the question's expected information gain (EIG), its complexity (as a penalty for longer programs), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, number), and its relevance to the task.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions from a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The authors demonstrate that their model can predict the frequency of human questions in held-out contexts and can also generate novel, plausible, and contextually relevant questions. This work positions itself at the intersection of active learning, computational cognitive science, and AI. It distinguishes itself from standard active learning, which typically involves simple label queries, by enabling a much richer query space. It also contrasts with data-hungry deep learning approaches for question generation (e.g., VQG), which are less goal-directed and require extensive training data, by proposing a structured, knowledge-rich model that can operate effectively in novel scenarios. The framework draws inspiration from cognitive science literature on compositionality and the "language of thought."

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its highly original and compelling reconceptualization of question asking as program synthesis. This is a significant departure from dominant paradigms in both active learning and dialogue systems. By framing questions as structured, executable programs, the authors provide a powerful and interpretable formalism for reasoning about what makes a "good" question. This approach has the potential to inspire new research directions in creating more sophisticated and human-like active learning agents.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed model is technically sound and well-executed. The use of a formal grammar, a probabilistic log-linear model, and features grounded in information theory (EIG) and cognitive plausibility (simplicity preference) is principled and elegant. The ablation studies convincingly demonstrate the necessity of each key component (informativeness, complexity, and answer type), with the removal of the complexity feature leading to a particularly dramatic drop in performance. This highlights the crucial insight that humans do not simply maximize information gain but balance it against conciseness.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the model is built up in logical steps, and the experimental results are presented effectively. The use of concrete examples (e.g., `(size Blue)`) greatly aids in understanding the program representation. The authors are also commendably transparent about the model's limitations.

4.  **Strong Empirical and Qualitative Results:** The model achieves a strong quantitative fit to the human data, with an average rank correlation of 0.64 between model predictions and human question frequencies in held-out contexts. Even more impressively, the qualitative results in Figure 3 show the model's ability to generate novel, clever, and contextually useful questions that were not produced by human participants. This demonstrates true generative capacity beyond simply memorizing or recombining seen examples.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Generalizability and Scalability of the Grammar:** The primary weakness of the current work is its reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar. While the authors correctly note that some components are domain-general (logical connectives, set operators), many primitives (`size`, `color`, ship-related concepts) are tied to the Battleship game. This raises significant questions about the framework's scalability and the amount of expert knowledge required to apply it to new domains. While this is an acceptable limitation for a foundational paper, the work would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of potential paths forward. For example, could grammar primitives be learned from data, perhaps through program induction techniques (e.g., related to Lake et al., 2015) or by integrating with large language models to propose candidate functions?

2.  **Computational Cost:** The inference procedure relies on importance sampling over a very large set of generated programs (150,000) to approximate the partition function and gradients. This appears to be computationally intensive. While necessary for this model, it may become a bottleneck in more complex domains with even larger program spaces. A brief discussion of these computational challenges and potential avenues for more efficient inference (e.g., variational methods, MCMC with structure-aware proposals) could be beneficial.

3.  **The "Language of Thought" to Natural Language Gap:** The model operates on semantic representations (programs) and bypasses the challenges of natural language understanding (parsing text to programs) and generation (producing text from programs). The authors acknowledge this limitation. The paper could be further strengthened by framing this more explicitly as a contribution to the "what to ask" (semantics and pragmatics) problem, which can be seen as a necessary precursor to the "how to say it" (NLU/NLG) problem. Positioning the work as a "semantic core" that could interface with existing semantic parsers would help clarify its role in a complete question-asking system.

### **Arguments for and against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel, elegant, and conceptually significant framework for question asking.
*   The approach bridges active learning, AI, and cognitive science in a compelling way.
*   Technically sound methodology with strong quantitative and qualitative results.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and honest about its limitations.
*   High potential for impact, likely to be cited and built upon by researchers in multiple fields.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar raises concerns about generalizability and scalability.
*   The inference method is computationally expensive.
*   The model does not engage with natural language text directly, which may limit its immediate practical application.

### **Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper. The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. The conceptual contribution of framing question asking as probabilistic program generation is significant and refreshing. While the limitations regarding scalability and the natural language interface are real, they represent avenues for future research rather than fatal flaws in the current work. This paper introduces a new and powerful way of thinking about a fundamental problem in intelligence and is an excellent fit for NIPS. It is a complete and high-quality piece of research that is likely to have a lasting impact on the field.
