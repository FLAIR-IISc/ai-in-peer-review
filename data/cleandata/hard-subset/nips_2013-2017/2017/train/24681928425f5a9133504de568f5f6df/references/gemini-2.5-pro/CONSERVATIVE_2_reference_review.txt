### Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question-asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that a question can be represented as a program in a LISP-like language, which, when executed on a world state, yields an answer. The authors develop a context-free grammar to define the compositional space of possible questions (programs). A log-linear probabilistic model is then used to score and sample from this space, aiming to predict which questions humans are likely to ask in a given context. The model's features include the question's expected information gain (EIG), its complexity (derived from the grammar), and its answer type. The framework is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions from a "Battleship"-like game, where the model is tasked with predicting question frequencies in held-out game contexts and generating novel, human-like questions. The authors claim this is the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking and represents a step toward more capable active learning systems.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The central contribution—modeling questions as programs—is a compelling and elegant way to capture the compositional, generative, and computable nature of human inquiry. This formalism provides a principled way to move beyond simple label requests, which is a significant limitation of most contemporary active learning research.

2.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper correctly identifies a key gap between the simple queries used in machine learning and the rich, goal-directed questions humans use to learn. The ambition to bridge this gap is commendable and highly relevant to the NIPS community.

3.  **Methodological Soundness (within its own confines):** The use of a log-linear model over a structured program space is a standard and reasonable approach. The chosen features (informativeness, complexity, answer type) are well-justified from a cognitive and information-theoretic perspective. The inclusion of ablation studies to test the contribution of different feature sets is a good experimental practice.

4.  **Qualitative Results:** The examples of generated novel questions are qualitatively impressive. They suggest that the model has learned to combine concepts in meaningful and contextually relevant ways, going beyond simple retrieval or recombination of training examples.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Competitive Baselines:** This is a major weakness of the paper. The proposed model is only compared against ablated versions of itself. While this demonstrates the utility of the chosen features, it fails to establish the superiority of the overall program-generation approach. A rigorous evaluation would require comparison against established, albeit potentially simpler, methods. For example:
    *   A retrieval-based baseline: Could a model that retrieves the most similar question from the training data (based on context similarity) and re-ranks it by EIG perform comparably?
    *   A standard sequence model: The authors dismiss deep learning approaches as data-hungry and inflexible, but this claim is not substantiated with experiments. A fine-tuned sequence-to-sequence model (e.g., T5, BART) that takes the board state as input and generates a question program could serve as a powerful modern baseline. Even if such a model performed poorly, analyzing its failure modes would strengthen the authors' argument for their structured approach. Without such a comparison, the claims about the model's performance are difficult to contextualize.

2.  **Limited and Highly-Engineered Domain:** The evaluation is confined to a single, "toy" domain (the Battleship game). The grammar, which is the cornerstone of the model, is hand-crafted specifically for this domain. While the authors claim many components are domain-general (e.g., logic, arithmetic), the semantic primitives (`size`, `color`, `touching`, etc.) are entirely domain-specific. This raises significant concerns about the scalability and generalizability of the approach. The amount of expert knowledge and engineering required to define such a grammar for a new, more complex domain seems prohibitive. The paper provides no clear path for how this framework could be extended beyond such constrained environments, weakening its claim as a general step towards more capable AI.

3.  **Evaluation Metrics and Results:** The quantitative evaluation is not entirely convincing. The primary metric is the log-likelihood on held-out data, which is approximated via importance sampling and subject to variance. More critically, the reported Spearman's rank correlation (ρ=0.64) is modest. The fact that the "information-agnostic" model achieves a slightly *higher* correlation (ρ=0.65) is counter-intuitive and contradicts the log-likelihood results. This discrepancy is not addressed by the authors and casts doubt on the claim that the model successfully balances informativeness and complexity. Furthermore, EIG calculation relies on enumerating a hypothesis space, which is only feasible in this small, discrete game. This is a well-known limitation of such approaches and further curtails the practical significance of the method for real-world active learning.

4.  **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims to provide "the first predictive model to date of open-ended human question asking." Given the highly constrained domain, the hand-engineered grammar, and the lack of competitive baselines, this claim feels overstated. The work is better framed as a promising proof-of-concept in a controlled setting, rather than a validated general model of human question-asking or a readily applicable active learning technique.

**Arguments for Acceptance**

*   The core idea of treating question asking as program synthesis is novel, conceptually elegant, and could inspire future work.
*   The paper addresses a significant and difficult problem in both active learning and human-computer interaction.
*   The qualitative results demonstrate that the framework can produce creative and contextually relevant outputs.

**Arguments for Rejection**

*   The evaluation lacks any comparison to established baseline methods, making it impossible to assess the practical value of the proposed approach.
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar severely limits the scalability and generalizability of the method.
*   The quantitative results are not overwhelmingly strong and contain unexplained inconsistencies (e.g., the Spearman correlation results).
*   The claims of generality and being the "first" model of its kind are not fully supported by the limited experimental evidence.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces an interesting and thought-provoking idea for modeling question asking. However, the work in its current form feels more like a position paper or a preliminary cognitive science study than a rigorous NIPS contribution. The lack of comparison to standard machine learning baselines and the severe limitations imposed by the hand-engineered, toy-world evaluation framework prevent a clear assessment of its significance for the field. While the novelty of the idea is appealing, the empirical validation is not yet up to the standards expected for NIPS. I would recommend rejection at this stage but would be very interested to see a future version of this work that addresses the evaluation shortcomings on a more complex task and with proper baselines.
