This paper proposes a novel framework for understanding human question asking, modeling questions as programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The core idea is compelling: by framing question asking as program synthesis, the authors aim to capture the compositional and computational aspects of human inquiry. They evaluate this framework on a dataset of questions asked during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating the model's ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper is well-motivated and clearly written. The connection to active learning is appropriately highlighted, and the authors effectively position their work relative to existing approaches in dialog systems, visual question generation, and cognitive science. The introduction effectively conveys the intuition behind the proposed approach and its potential benefits.  The description of the dataset is thorough and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.  The grammar and probabilistic model are presented with reasonable clarity, though a deeper understanding would benefit from access to the supplementary materials. The experimental results are convincing, showing that the full model outperforms ablated versions and generates plausible questions. 

However, several weaknesses limit the impact of this work. First, the scope is relatively narrow. The “Battleship” game, while providing a controlled environment, is a simplified domain.  It's unclear how well the proposed framework would generalize to more complex, open-ended scenarios. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but a discussion of potential adaptations or challenges in broader domains would be beneficial. Second, the evaluation primarily focuses on prediction accuracy and qualitative assessment of generated questions. While the generated questions *seem* plausible, a more rigorous evaluation of their informativeness or utility within the game context would strengthen the claims. For instance, comparing the performance of a human playing the game with access to the model-generated questions versus a human playing with a standard questioning strategy would be insightful. Third, the reliance on a handcrafted grammar, despite its expressiveness, feels somewhat limiting. While the authors acknowledge the need for domain knowledge, exploring ways to learn or adapt the grammar from data could improve scalability. Fourth, the optimization details (importance sampling, learning rate, number of iterations) are provided but could benefit from further justification or sensitivity analysis.  Is the performance highly sensitive to these hyperparameters? Finally, the paper lacks a discussion of computational complexity. How does the complexity of program synthesis scale with the size and complexity of the domain?

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Framework:** The program synthesis perspective on question asking is a fresh and promising approach.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results provide evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed model.
*   **Strong Connection to Cognitive Science:** The work is grounded in insights from cognitive science and linguistics.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Domain:** The “Battleship” game is a simplified domain, potentially limiting generalizability.
*   **Evaluation Rigor:** Evaluation relies heavily on prediction accuracy and qualitative assessment; more rigorous evaluation of generated question utility is needed.
*   **Handcrafted Grammar:** Reliance on a manually defined grammar.
*   **Computational Complexity:** Lack of discussion regarding scalability.
*   **Optimization Details:** Limited justification or sensitivity analysis of optimization hyperparameters.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful framework for understanding human question asking.
*   The experimental results are promising and support the key claims.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The connection to relevant fields (active learning, cognitive science) is strong.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited domain and lack of more rigorous evaluation raise concerns about generalizability.
*   The reliance on a handcrafted grammar may limit scalability.
*   The lack of discussion regarding computational complexity is a weakness.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. While the limitations are noteworthy, the novelty of the approach and the promising experimental results outweigh these concerns.  I recommend **acceptance**, but with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above in future work. Specifically, exploring the generalization to more complex domains and developing more robust evaluation metrics would significantly strengthen the impact of this research.  A discussion of computational complexity and strategies for grammar learning would also be beneficial.
