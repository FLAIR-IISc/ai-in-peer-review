Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a computational framework for open-ended question asking by representing questions as executable programs in a simple, typed, LISP-like language. A context-free grammar (augmented with lambda, map, and set operators) defines a compositional hypothesis space of question-programs. A log-linear model over programs assigns probabilities based on features: Expected Information Gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, a complexity prior derived from the grammar (−log q(x)), answer-type indicators, and a relevance indicator. Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human-produced questions using importance sampling to approximate the partition function and expected feature values. Evaluation is conducted on a Battleship-style information-search dataset (605 natural-language questions over 18 contexts, with 2 contexts dropped), translated into programs. The model is tested via leave-one-context-out prediction of human question distributions and qualitative generation of novel questions.

The paper connects to several strands of work:
- Active learning, which typically limits queries to label requests [Settles 2010].
- Semantics of questions as partitions over worlds [Groenendijk & Stokhof 1984] and pragmatic models of informative questions [Hawkins et al. 2015].
- Program-based representations in cognitive science and LOT [Goodman et al. 2015; Lake et al. 2015] and program-based QA [Johnson et al. 2017; Wang et al. 2015].
- Question generation in dialogue/VQG [Mostafazadeh et al. 2016; Serban et al. 2016; Bordes & Weston 2017], which generally lacks explicit goal-directed informativity and compositional program structure.

Strengths
- Clear, interpretable formalization: Representing questions as programs directly encodes compositionality and computability, aligning with formal semantics and LOT traditions.
- Principled objective: The EIG feature operationalizes goal-directed information gathering; the complexity feature captures a concise-question bias observed in human data.
- Productivity: The grammar explains the ability to produce and predict questions not seen in training, addressing a limitation of retrieval-based or fixed-template systems.
- Empirical support via ablations: Lesioning complexity, EIG, or answer-type features degrades performance; complexity appears crucial (complexity-agnostic model performs poorly), and EIG/type also contribute.
- Quantitative fit: Held-out correlations between model energies and human question frequencies are reported (average Spearman 0.64 for the full model, versus −0.36 without the complexity feature).
- Qualitative generation: The system synthesizes novel, context-relevant questions that are nontrivial and interpretable.
- Dataset availability is noted, facilitating reproducibility for the data component.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality (technical soundness)
- Inference details are underspecified and potentially problematic. The text states that 150k questions are sampled “to approximate the gradient at each step” over 100k iterations, which seems computationally infeasible unless the same sample is reused; clarify whether the sample is fixed across iterations, provide runtime/compute budget, and include convergence diagnostics or variance reduction techniques. Describe how Z is estimated and stabilized (bootstrap details are briefly mentioned).
- The proposal distribution q is both the PCFG prior used for complexity and the importance sampler. This coupling can bias both learning and evaluation; include sensitivity analyses to alternative priors/proposals and report how results change with different PCFG rule probabilities or a length-based prior.
- The EIG computation relies on an ideal observer over a large hypothesis space; two contexts and 14 questions (draw operator) are dropped for tractability. Quantify how many hypotheses are considered per context, describe approximations (if any) to EIG, and report robustness to these omissions.
- Mapping from natural language to programs is nontrivial but not described. Specify annotation procedure, inter-annotator agreement, coverage of the grammar, and how ambiguities were resolved. Without this, reproducibility and validity could be affected.

Clarity
- The core ideas are well motivated, but some crucial numbers are missing. Table 1 (log-likelihoods) is referenced but not shown; include exact values and confidence intervals. Report additional metrics such as top-k accuracy for predicting the most frequent questions per context.
- References and formatting have several inconsistencies/incomplete entries; fix these for clarity.
- Explicitly detail the grammar in the main text or ensure the supplement is self-contained and accessible; state the exact maximum program length and proportion of mass excluded.

Originality
- The framing of question asking as program generation with an explicit EIG/complexity trade-off appears novel relative to prior work focused on answering or templated question generation. The approach builds on established ideas (LOT, program induction, question semantics) but applies them innovatively to generation.

Significance
- The contribution is conceptually interesting and may influence research on active learning and interactive systems, but empirical scope is limited. The evaluation is confined to a single, bespoke domain with a small dataset. There is no human evaluation of the generated questions (e.g., usefulness/clarity judgments) and no comparison to alternative generative baselines (e.g., retrieval + EIG reranking, sequence models conditioned on context abstractions, or pragmatic RSA-style generators).
- Portability is unclear. While many operators are domain-general, the grammar contains domain-specific symbols and the model includes a hand-crafted relevance feature. Provide either a second domain study or an ablation showing what portion of the grammar transfers unchanged; analyze how much new engineering is required.

Additional suggestions
- Add baselines: EIG-only ranker over a canonical question set; PCFG-only prior; RSA-style pragmatic generator; a simple neural baseline over serialized contexts and questions; and a retrieval baseline with concept substitution via the grammar.
- Report calibration (e.g., probability assigned to human questions) and sample efficiency.
- Include a small human study rating generated questions’ usefulness and well-formedness.
- Analyze sensitivity to grammar size, maximum depth/length, and proposal distribution.
- Discuss how to connect to natural language via a learned semantic parser; even a preliminary experiment would strengthen impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and interpretable framework that unifies compositionality and goal-directed information seeking.
- Empirical evidence that balancing informativeness and complexity predicts human question distributions.
- Demonstrated productivity and generation of novel, interpretable questions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical scope (single domain, small dataset), with no human evaluation of generated outputs and no strong baselines beyond ablations.
- Important methodological details (annotation, inference scalability, numerical results) are missing or unclear.
- Portability and generality outside the Battleship domain are not demonstrated; aspects of the grammar and features are domain-specific.

Overall, the paper offers a promising and well-motivated framework with interpretable modeling, but would benefit from stronger empirical validation, clearer methodology, and broader evaluation to meet the standards of generality and rigor expected at NeurIPS.
