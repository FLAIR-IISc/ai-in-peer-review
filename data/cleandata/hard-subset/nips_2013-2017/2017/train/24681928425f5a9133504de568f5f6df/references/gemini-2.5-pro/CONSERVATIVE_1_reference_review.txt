**Summary:**
This paper proposes a computational framework for modeling goal-directed, human-like question asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The central idea is that complex questions can be represented as programs in a compositional language (based on LISP), which, when executed on a possible world state, return an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic log-linear model over the space of programs defined by a hand-crafted grammar. The model's energy function incorporates features for a question's informativeness (Expected Information Gain), its complexity (derived from a probabilistic context-free grammar), and its answer type. The model's parameters are fit to a dataset of human-generated questions from a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The authors evaluate their model using leave-one-out cross-validation, demonstrating that their full model predicts human question frequencies better than ablated versions that lack key features (informativeness, complexity, or answer type). They also provide qualitative examples of novel, plausible questions generated by the model. This work connects ideas from active learning, computational cognitive science, and program synthesis to tackle the problem of generating rich, context-sensitive queries.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound within its own defined scope. The probabilistic formulation is clear, and the use of a log-linear model to combine features like EIG and complexity is a reasonable approach. The optimization procedure, while relying on approximations for the intractable partition function, follows standard practice. The ablation study is a good methodological choice, as it provides evidence for the contribution of each component within the proposed framework.

However, the work's primary weakness lies in its empirical evaluation and the lack of comparison to established methods. The paper compares the full model only against lesioned versions of itself. While this demonstrates that, for instance, adding a complexity term improves this specific model, it fails to establish that the entire program generation framework is superior to simpler, more conventional baselines. For a NIPS paper, one would expect a comparison against alternative machine learning approaches. For example:
1.  A retrieval-based baseline: Given a new context, find the most similar context in the training set and reuse its most frequent question.
2.  A simple generative model: An n-gram model or a simple recurrent neural network trained on the text of the questions, perhaps conditioned on a feature vector representing the game context.
3.  A baseline that ranks all syntactically valid questions from the grammar purely by EIG. The authors state that EIG alone has no correlation with human frequency, but this should be demonstrated as a formal baseline model within the evaluation.

Without these comparisons, the claim that this complex machinery is necessary or superior for this task is not sufficiently supported. The work feels more like a position paper or a proof-of-concept for a cognitive science audience than a rigorously validated machine learning method.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the model is described in sufficient detail, and the connection between the theoretical framework and the experimental task is well-articulated. The figures and examples are helpful in illustrating the core concepts.

**Originality:**
The core idea of modeling question asking as probabilistic program synthesis is highly original and intellectually interesting. It represents a novel synthesis of concepts from formal semantics, program induction, and Bayesian inference to address a challenging problem in active learning and AI. While program induction has been explored at NIPS before (e.g., Lake et al., 2015), its application to goal-directed *question generation* is new and compelling. The paper does a good job of situating itself relative to related work in dialogue systems and visual question generation, clearly highlighting its unique focus on compositional, goal-directed inquiry.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is high; developing machines that can ask intelligent, rich questions would be a major advance for AI and active learning. However, the current paper's impact is limited by its methodology. The framework's reliance on a manually-designed, domain-specific grammar is a significant practical limitation. The authors acknowledge that extending the model to new domains would require non-trivial knowledge engineering. This reliance on hand-crafted symbolic structures runs counter to the prevailing trend in machine learning towards end-to-end learning from data and raises questions about the scalability and generalizability of the approach.

Furthermore, the evaluation is conducted on a single, constrained toy-world domain ("Battleship"). While this allows for controlled analysis, it makes it difficult to assess how the approach would fare in more complex, real-world scenarios. The claims of moving towards "more capable active learning machines" feel premature given the limited scope of the experiments and the absence of comparisons to any existing active learning algorithms.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly original and elegant conceptual framework for a challenging and important problem.
*   The idea of framing question asking as program synthesis is a novel contribution that could inspire future work.
*   The paper is very clearly written and the proposed model is technically sound.
*   The qualitative results showing the generation of novel, human-like questions are compelling.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The most significant weakness is the complete lack of comparison to any established baseline methods, making it impossible to judge the practical utility of the proposed framework. The evaluation is limited to internal ablation studies.
*   The model relies heavily on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar, which raises serious concerns about its scalability and applicability to new problems.
*   The empirical validation is conducted on a single, relatively simple toy domain, and the claims about advancing active learning are not substantiated by the results.

**Conclusion:**
While I find the conceptual contribution of this paper to be novel and thought-provoking, its significant empirical weaknesses prevent me from recommending acceptance. The core problem is the lack of rigorous validation against any standard or simple baselines. For a contribution to be accepted at NIPS, it must not only be original but must also demonstrate its value through strong empirical evidence. By only comparing against ablated versions of itself, the paper fails to show that this complex, program-synthesis approach offers a tangible benefit over more established techniques. The reliance on heavy domain engineering further limits its practical significance for the broader machine learning community. The work is promising as a research direction, but in its current form, it reads more like a cognitive science paper than a contribution to neural information processing systems that has been thoroughly vetted.
