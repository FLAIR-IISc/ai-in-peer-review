Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a simple, typed, lambda-calculus/LISP-like language generated by a largely context-free grammar. A log-linear model over program features defines a distribution over questions; features include expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer for the Battleship domain, a complexity prior derived from the PCFG (-log q(x)), answer-type indicators (Boolean/Number/Color/Location), and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit by maximum likelihood using importance sampling over 150k sampled programs and leave-one-context-out evaluation on a dataset of 605 human questions across 18 ambiguous Battleship contexts [19] (two contexts removed for tractability, plus “draw” questions dropped). The full model outperforms ablations in log-likelihood and generates novel, context-appropriate questions beyond the training set.

The work connects to active learning [21], but extends beyond label/query types to richer, compositional queries. It builds on partition semantics in linguistics [6] and prior cognitive models of concept/program induction [17, 13], and is related to program-based question answering [24, 12] and recent visual question generation and dialogue systems [16, 20, 22, 25], while focusing specifically on intentional, goal-directed question asking in a low-data regime. The empirical task and Bayesian observer follow prior work on information-seeking in games [7, 19].

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of questions as executable programs, capturing compositionality and computability. The grammar covers all human questions in the dataset.
- A simple, interpretable probabilistic model that integrates informativeness (EIG), a learnable complexity prior, and base-rate preferences over answer types, yielding good predictive performance on human question frequencies and plausible novel generations.
- Careful ablations: removing complexity severely degrades performance; type and informativeness features also matter by log-likelihood comparisons. This diagnoses key ingredients in human question choice.
- Demonstrates productivity: model can synthesize valid, human-like questions not seen in training, addressing a limitation of many neural systems that rely on large paired corpora [16, 23, 11, 20].
- Provides resources (dataset link) and a concrete testbed for question generation research.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope and scalability: evaluation is confined to a single toy domain with hand-engineered grammar and Bayesian world model. Two contexts and “draw” questions are removed due to computational intractability, raising concerns about scalability to larger hypothesis spaces and general domains.
- Informativeness vs. correlation: while the full model has the best held-out log-likelihood, its average rank correlation with human frequencies (0.64) is marginally worse than the information-agnostic variant (0.65), suggesting limited incremental predictive value from EIG for ranking. This tension could be analyzed more deeply.
- Complexity prior: -log q(x) from the PCFG is a syntactic probability rather than a cognitively motivated complexity measure (e.g., MDL or description length). It may encode design choices of the grammar more than human cognitive cost.
- Approximate inference: results rely on importance sampling with a PCFG proposal. The paper does not report diagnostics (ESS, variance, sensitivity to sample size, or convergence across runs). The normalizer Z and gradient estimates may have high variance.
- Natural language interface: the system assumes gold program representations; no model is provided for mapping from or to natural language. This limits practical applicability to human-computer interaction settings.
- Evaluation of novelty: qualitative showcase of five sampled questions per context is informative but lacks human evaluation of usefulness, answerability, or “human-likeness.”
- Relevance filter and answer types: adding a hand-coded relevance feature and answer-type indicators risks baking in preferences that could also be learned or derived from utility/cost models.
- Reproducibility details: key grammar rules and observer details are deferred to the supplement; runtime and computational cost are not reported.

Assessment by criteria
Quality: Generally sound. The modeling choices are well-motivated, and the ablation study is informative. However, the dependence on hand-crafted grammars and observer, the lack of inference diagnostics, and limited quantitative evaluation of generated questions reduce robustness. The removal of contexts for tractability indicates scaling challenges.

Clarity: Clear narrative and motivation; the notion of questions-as-programs is explained with concrete examples. Some critical details (full grammar, EIG computation specifics, hypothesis space sizes) are deferred to the supplement. Reference formatting has several inconsistencies.

Originality: Modeling open-ended question asking as program synthesis with a probabilistic prior over programs is novel in this context. While it builds on established ideas (partition semantics, program induction, active learning), the integration and application to predicting human questions constitute a meaningful contribution.

Significance: The work advances understanding of human question asking and proposes a potentially extensible framework for active learners that ask compositional, goal-directed questions. Current impact is limited by domain specificity and scalability; broader significance would be strengthened by demonstrations in additional domains and a language interface.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide inference diagnostics (ESS, variance) and sensitivity to the number of sampled programs; report runtimes and memory.
- Analyze the discrepancy between log-likelihood and correlation metrics; add a utility model with explicit cognitive cost and compare against EIG alone.
- Replace or complement -log q(x) with a learned complexity prior (e.g., from human judgments) or MDL-based measures; report how grammar re-weighting affects results.
- Include a simple baseline that optimizes EIG with a length penalty to better isolate the benefit of the learned prior.
- Add human evaluation for generated questions (usefulness, answerability, naturalness).
- Demonstrate portability: instantiate the approach in a second domain with minimal new engineering; or learn parts of the grammar from data.
- Move essential grammar rules and the observer specification into the main text or an accessible appendix; fix reference formatting.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually compelling framework linking active learning, compositional semantics, and program induction.
- Interpretable model with informative ablations; evidence of predicting human question distributions and generating novel, sensible questions.
- Provides a new lens on question asking that could inspire future work in interactive ML.

Arguments against acceptance
- Narrow, hand-engineered domain; limited scalability and generality demonstrated.
- Heavy reliance on approximations without diagnostics; two contexts removed due to tractability.
- Limited empirical validation of generated questions; modest gains from EIG on ranking.
