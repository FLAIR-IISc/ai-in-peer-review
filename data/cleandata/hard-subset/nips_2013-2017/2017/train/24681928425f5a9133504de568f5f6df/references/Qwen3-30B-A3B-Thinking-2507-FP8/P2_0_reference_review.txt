

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, output an answer. The authors frame question asking as program synthesis, with key contributions including: (1) a compositional grammar for generating questions as programs; (2) a probabilistic model that predicts human question choices based on features like informativeness (measured through Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance; and (3) an evaluation showing their model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions in a Battleship-style game domain. The work sits at the intersection of computational cognitive science, active learning, and program synthesis, building on prior work in program induction [12, 13, 17] while addressing a gap in modeling rich, goal-directed human questioning.

## Quality

**Strengths**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear methodological components. The authors provide a rigorous probabilistic model with well-defined features (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance) that are grounded in cognitive science principles. The evaluation is comprehensive, including both quantitative (log-likelihood comparisons across model variants using leave-one-out cross-validation) and qualitative (novel question generation) assessments. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the importance of each key feature, with the full model outperforming all variants (complexity-agnostic model performed particularly poorly, highlighting the importance of question complexity). The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., domain specificity, semantic vs. natural language representation), and the supplementary materials (referenced but not included in the provided text) likely provide additional technical details.

**Weaknesses**: The evaluation is limited to a single domain (Battleship game), which raises questions about generalizability. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't provide evidence that their approach would scale to other domains. The computational complexity of the model (requiring importance sampling to approximate intractable normalizing constants) is noted but not thoroughly discussed in terms of practical scalability. The paper doesn't provide evidence of how the model would handle more complex game states or larger hypothesis spaces.

## Clarity

**Strengths**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The key concepts (compositionality, computability) are introduced early and effectively illustrated with concrete examples (e.g., "How long is the blue ship?" → (size Blue)). The grammar description is precise and accessible, with the use of programming language examples making the technical content understandable. The figures (particularly Figure 3 showing novel questions) effectively demonstrate the model's capabilities. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work in active learning, AI dialog systems, and visual question generation.

**Weaknesses**: The supplementary materials referenced throughout (e.g., Table SI-1, SI-2) are not included in the provided text, which slightly limits the ability to fully evaluate the grammar details. While the paper discusses the "language of thought" representation, the connection to natural language generation is somewhat abstract and could be more explicitly addressed.

## Originality

**Strengths**: The core insight of modeling questions as executable programs is highly original in the context of human question asking. While program synthesis has been used in question answering [12, 24], this paper is the first to apply it to the problem of *question asking*—a fundamentally different task where the goal is to generate the question itself. The combination of compositionality (using a grammar to build questions from conceptual building blocks) and computability (questions as programs that evaluate to answers) is a novel contribution to computational cognitive science. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work: unlike goal-directed dialog systems [1, 25] that use canned questions, or VQG models [16, 23] that require large datasets, this work models open-ended human-like question asking without extensive training data.

**Weaknesses**: The paper's domain-specific grammar (designed for Battleship) is somewhat limited in scope. While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation, they don't provide a sufficiently detailed argument for how much of their framework is domain-general versus domain-specific. The approach shares similarities with program induction frameworks [13, 17], but the application to question asking is novel.

## Significance

**Strengths**: The work addresses a significant gap in active learning literature by modeling rich, human-like questioning rather than simple label queries. The ability to generate novel, useful questions (as shown in Figure 3) represents a substantial advance over existing systems, which typically produce stereotyped questions [22] or lack goal-directedness. The framework provides a concrete computational model of how humans balance informativeness and complexity when asking questions, contributing to computational cognitive science. The paper's long-term vision of enabling more human-like question asking in machines has clear potential applications in educational technology, intelligent tutoring systems, and human-AI collaboration.

**Weaknesses**: The significance is somewhat constrained by the narrow domain of evaluation (Battleship game). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the paper doesn't provide strong evidence that the approach would generalize to more naturalistic or diverse question-asking scenarios. The gap between semantic program representations and natural language (which the authors note as a limitation) represents a significant barrier to practical application.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the intersection of computational cognitive science and active learning. The core idea of modeling questions as executable programs is novel and well-motivated, and the evaluation demonstrates clear advantages over alternative approaches. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and makes a concrete contribution to the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Novel approach to modeling human question asking as program generation
- Comprehensive evaluation with clear ablation studies
- Demonstrated ability to generate novel, human-like questions
- Strong theoretical grounding in cognitive science principles
- Clear contribution to computational cognitive science

**Arguments Against Acceptance**:
- Evaluation limited to a single domain (Battleship game)
- Domain-specific grammar may limit generalizability
- No evidence of how the model would scale to more complex domains
- Semantic representation rather than direct natural language processing

## Final Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant and original contribution to the field, and the technical quality is high. The domain limitation is acknowledged by the authors, and the framework's potential for extension to other domains is promising. I would ask the authors to address the following minor points in revision:

1. Clarify in the discussion how much of the grammar is domain-specific versus domain-general, and provide more specific examples of how the framework might be extended to new domains.
2. Add a brief discussion of computational complexity and scalability for larger domains.
3. More explicitly address the gap between semantic program representations and natural language generation.

The paper advances the state of the art in modeling human-like question asking and provides a valuable framework for future work in active learning and computational cognitive science. It meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level and would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS.
