## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed on a world state, produce an answer. This recasts question asking as a program synthesis problem. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions asked during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper positions itself within the intersection of active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive science. It correctly points out the limitations of existing active learning methods (focused on simple queries) and dialog systems (reliant on pre-defined question sets). It also acknowledges advancements in visual question generation but highlights the challenges of generalizing to novel scenarios. The connection to work on compositional semantics and the “language of thought” is well established.  The authors effectively differentiate their approach by focusing on goal-directed question asking in a dynamic, ambiguous environment.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The framing of question asking as program generation is a genuinely novel and insightful approach. It provides a strong theoretical grounding for modeling human inquiry.
* **Technical Soundness:** The probabilistic model, based on a context-free grammar and expected information gain, is well-defined and appears technically sound. The use of importance sampling to approximate intractable computations is a practical and reasonable choice.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The quantitative results demonstrate the model’s ability to predict human question frequencies, and the qualitative examples of generated questions are compelling. The comparison to ablations highlights the importance of complexity and informativeness.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core concepts are clearly explained, and the motivations for design choices are well articulated. The inclusion of examples (e.g., Figures 2 and 3) significantly enhances understanding.
* **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental problem in AI – enabling machines to ask intelligent, informative questions.  Success in this area could significantly improve active learning, human-computer interaction, and cognitive modeling.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge the limitations, the current grammar is still tailored to the Battleship domain. The extent to which this framework can be generalized to other domains without significant re-engineering remains unclear.
* **Grammar Design:** The grammar, while sufficient for the data, feels somewhat *ad hoc*. A more systematic derivation of the grammar, perhaps from linguistic principles, could strengthen the theoretical foundation.
* **Limited Scalability:** The use of importance sampling indicates that the model may struggle to scale to more complex domains with larger hypothesis spaces.
* **Evaluation Limitations:** The dataset, while valuable, is relatively small. The predictive performance, although significant, is limited by the sparsity of the data. The qualitative evaluation relies on human judgment which is inherently subjective.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention computational issues, the costs associated with the program execution and importance sampling are not fully discussed.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a truly novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling human question asking.
* The empirical results provide strong support for the proposed framework.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact multiple fields, including AI and cognitive science.
* The writing is clear and concise, making the ideas accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The domain specificity of the current implementation limits its immediate applicability.
* The scalability of the approach remains a concern.
* The relatively small dataset and reliance on subjective evaluation weaken the empirical support.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The idea of modeling question asking as program generation is both insightful and technically well-executed. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper far outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this work is of high quality and is appropriate for acceptance to NIPS. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*  Discuss the potential for generalizing the grammar to other domains.
*  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational costs associated with the model.
*  Explore alternative techniques for approximating the normalizing constant in the probabilistic model.
*  Consider expanding the evaluation to include a wider range of tasks and datasets.
