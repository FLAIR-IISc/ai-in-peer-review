Summary
This paper proposes “question asking as program generation”: questions are represented as executable programs over possible worlds. A context-free grammar (augmented with lambda, map, and set operators) defines a space of well-typed, computable question-programs for a Battleship-like domain. A log-linear model over programs predicts which question a human will ask in a given context. Features include expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, a complexity prior derived from the PCFG (−log q), answer-type indicators, and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit by maximizing likelihood of 605 human questions across 18 contexts using importance sampling over programs. Evaluation uses leave-one-context-out: (i) held-out log-likelihood and rank correlations between model energies and human frequencies, and (ii) qualitative assessment of sampled “novel” questions.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to active learning (mostly label queries), semantic theories of questions as partitions, program-induction views of concepts, and program-based semantic parsers for question answering. Compared to goal-directed dialog and VQG systems, this approach targets intentional, context-sensitive question selection and productivity via a compositional, executable representation. Closest antecedents are RSA-style pragmatic models of question evaluation and program-based QA; the novelty here is generating questions (not just interpreting/answering them) and jointly modeling informativeness and complexity.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of a rich query space via programs; compositionality and computability are well-motivated and domain-general in spirit.
- Probabilistic model integrates EIG with a learned complexity prior and answer-type biases, capturing realistic trade-offs beyond pure information maximization.
- Leave-one-context-out evaluation shows the full model outperforms ablations in held-out log-likelihood; complexity is especially important.
- The framework yields diverse, interpretable novel questions; equivalence classes via answer partitions are a thoughtful touch.
- Engages both ML and cognitive science communities with a coherent proposal.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited empirical scope: a single, hand-engineered domain with a bespoke grammar; two contexts dropped due to computational constraints and one grammar rule removed. This raises questions about scalability of EIG computation and generality of the approach.
- Evaluation is partly qualitative. The “novel question” claim lacks human judgments (e.g., usefulness, naturalness) or downstream utility (e.g., improved task identification vs baselines).
- Ambiguity about semantic parsing: it appears the 605 NL questions were manually mapped to programs, which limits end-to-end realism and reproducibility.
- Potential circularity and sampling bias: the complexity feature uses −log q where q is also the proposal for importance sampling. If q poorly covers human-like regions, both modeling and estimation may be biased. No diagnostics (ESS, variance) or convergence analyses are reported for importance sampling or training.
- Mixed evidence on informativeness: the rank correlation section reports the information-agnostic model at 0.65 vs 0.64 for the full model, seemingly contradicting earlier claims; this discrepancy needs clarification (metrics vs LL, variance).
- Results lack absolute numbers for LL improvements, confidence intervals for correlations, and ablation detail (e.g., effect sizes across contexts). Reproducibility depends on SI grammar and code, which are not included.
- The cost model is rudimentary (complexity via PCFG). Human costs likely depend on answerer model, presuppositions, and cognitive effort beyond length; related pragmatic models (e.g., RSA for questions) are not quantitatively compared.

Quality
Conceptually sound and technically plausible, but the inference is approximate and under-documented. The dependence on a hand-crafted grammar, dropped contexts, and manual semantic mapping undercut claims of generality. Evaluation is decent for a cognitive modeling paper but light for NeurIPS standards (lack of stronger baselines and human eval).

Clarity
Generally well-written, with concrete examples. However, critical details (complete grammar, hypothesis space sizes, EIG computation and caching, sampling diagnostics, exact LL numbers, and parser mapping) are relegated or missing. Reference formatting is inconsistent.

Originality
Moderately high. Framing question generation as program synthesis with a learned utility over programs is novel in this context. The components (EIG, PCFG priors, program semantics) are known, but the combination and application to human question asking and generation is original.

Significance
Moderate. The work advances the conceptual discussion of rich queries in active learning and offers a concrete modeling tool for cognitive science. Impact on mainstream ML is limited by domain specificity, computational scaling, and lack of integration with natural language or real interactive tasks.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide code, grammars, and the mapping from NL to programs; report IS diagnostics (ESS), training convergence, and robustness to the proposal grammar.
- Clarify the correlation vs log-likelihood discrepancy; report absolute LLs with CIs across contexts.
- Add stronger baselines: (i) a neural sequence model conditioned on a symbolic context encoding; (ii) a heuristic EIG−λ·length policy; (iii) an RSA-style questioner with explicit answerer model and cost.
- Include human evaluation of sampled questions (usefulness, naturalness) and a task-utility analysis (e.g., expected reduction in identification time).
- Stress-test generalization: port the grammar skeleton to a second domain with minimal engineering; analyze sensitivity to grammar variants and maximum program length.
- Discuss prospects for coupling with a semantic parser to close the NL loop.

Pros
- Elegant, interpretable framework; principled integration of information and complexity.
- Evidence that compositional program priors matter for human-like question generation.
- Clear qualitative demonstrations of novel, context-sensitive questions.

Cons
- Narrow domain; computational and practical limitations (dropped contexts, manual mapping).
- Limited quantitative evaluation and baselines; unclear importance sampling reliability.
- Unclear generalization to natural language and larger hypothesis spaces.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Confidence: High
