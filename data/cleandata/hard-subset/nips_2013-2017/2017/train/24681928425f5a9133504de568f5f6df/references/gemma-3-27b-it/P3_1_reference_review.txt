## NIPS 2017 Review: Question Asking as Program Generation

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, yield an answer. This reframes question asking as a program synthesis problem and allows for a probabilistic model to predict and even generate human-like questions within a "Battleship"-like game context. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of human questions collected during gameplay, demonstrating its ability to predict question frequencies and synthesize novel questions.

**Related Work:**

The paper effectively positions itself within the fields of active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation (VQG). It rightly points out the limitations of existing active learning approaches which typically focus on simple label requests, and highlights the lack of genuine flexibility in goal-directed dialog systems reliant on canned responses. The comparison to VQG is also well-made; while VQG generates questions, it requires large paired image-question datasets and lacks the human ability to ask intelligent questions in novel scenarios. The authors accurately represent the state-of-the-art and situate their work as a step towards more human-like, goal-directed question asking.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framing of question asking as program generation is a genuinely novel and insightful approach. It provides a powerful theoretical lens for understanding the compositional nature of questions and their computational requirements.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The authors build on established concepts from linguistics (compositionality), cognitive science (language of thought), and computer science (program synthesis) to create a principled framework.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The evaluation on a real human dataset is crucial. The model’s ability to predict question frequencies and generate plausible new questions demonstrates its effectiveness, particularly given the complexity of the task and the open-ended nature of human questioning.
*   **Well-Defined Grammar:** The grammar for program generation is clearly defined and appears sufficient to capture the nuances of the questions observed in the dataset.  The incorporation of lambda expressions and set operators adds to the expressiveness of the framework.
*   **Feature Engineering:** The thoughtfully chosen features, particularly the inclusion of both informativeness (EIG) and complexity, are essential for modeling human question asking behavior.  The ablation studies in the results section clearly demonstrate the importance of these factors.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge the domain-specificity of some grammar elements, the reliance on a specific game context remains a limitation.  Scaling this approach to more complex, real-world scenarios will be a significant challenge.  How generalizable are the underlying principles to domains lacking a readily-defined "world state" like Battleship?
*   **Computational Complexity:** The importance sampling method used to approximate the normalizing constant in the probabilistic model is computationally expensive, as noted by the authors. This limits the scalability of the approach.  Further optimization or alternative approximation techniques would be valuable.
*   **Limited Qualitative Evaluation of Generated Questions:**  While the authors provide examples of generated questions, a more rigorous qualitative evaluation – perhaps involving human ratings of relevance, clarity, and “human-likeness” – would strengthen the claims. Simply stating the questions are "clever" isn’t sufficiently convincing.
*   **EIG Calculation Limitations:** The reliance on a Bayesian ideal observer to calculate EIG might be overly strong. Human question asking isn’t always perfectly rational; cognitive biases and heuristics likely play a role.  Exploring alternative approaches to modeling question usefulness could be beneficial.
*   **Dataset Size:** Although the dataset is valuable, 605 question-context pairs may be relatively small for training a complex probabilistic model.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to modeling question asking.
*   The empirical results are encouraging, demonstrating the model’s ability to predict and generate human-like questions.
*   The work has the potential to contribute significantly to the fields of active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive science.
*   The authors clearly identify the limitations of their approach and suggest directions for future research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The domain specificity of the current implementation may limit the broader applicability of the framework.
*   The computational complexity of the model could hinder its scalability.
*   The qualitative evaluation of generated questions is somewhat limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, combined with the solid theoretical foundation and promising empirical results, outweighs the identified weaknesses. While challenges remain regarding scalability and generalization, the authors have presented a compelling framework for understanding and modeling human question asking.

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017, with the following minor revisions:

1.  **Expand the discussion of domain generalization:** Address more directly how the approach could be adapted to more complex, real-world scenarios.
2.  **Provide a more rigorous qualitative evaluation of generated questions:** Incorporate human ratings or other objective metrics to assess the quality and “human-likeness” of the generated questions.
3.  **Briefly discuss potential optimizations to reduce computational complexity.**
