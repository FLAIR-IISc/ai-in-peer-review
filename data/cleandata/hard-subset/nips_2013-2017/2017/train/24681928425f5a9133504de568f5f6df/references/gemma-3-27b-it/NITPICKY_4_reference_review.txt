This paper proposes a framework for modeling human question asking as program generation, aiming to bridge the gap between simple active learning queries and the rich, nuanced questions humans pose. The core idea—representing questions as executable programs—is conceptually appealing and offers a formal foundation for studying question formulation. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions collected during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, plausible questions.

Despite the promising premise, I find several significant issues that, in my assessment, outweigh the paper's contributions. While the core idea is interesting, the execution is riddled with methodological shortcomings and a lack of rigor that ultimately undermine the claims made.

First, the probabilistic model, while described at length, feels overly complex and lacks sufficient justification for its specific components. The choice of features – particularly the interplay between EIG and complexity – feels ad-hoc and lacks a clear theoretical grounding. The importance sampling optimization is acknowledged to be approximate, but the implications of this approximation for the reliability of the reported results are not adequately discussed. The reliance on 150,000 samples is computationally expensive and raises concerns about scalability.

Second, the grammar itself, while aiming for expressiveness, feels unnecessarily convoluted. The inclusion of lambda expressions and set operators, while potentially powerful, adds significant complexity without a clear demonstration of their necessity. Table SI-1 and SI-2, crucial for understanding the grammar, are relegated to the supplementary materials, hindering a clear understanding of the model’s capabilities and limitations. The explanation of how the grammar handles questions requiring visual reasoning (e.g., referencing tile coordinates) is also inadequate.

Third, the evaluation is not entirely convincing. While the correlation between model predictions and human question frequencies is reported, the sparsity of the data, as acknowledged by the authors, limits the interpretability of these correlations. The qualitative evaluation of generated questions feels subjective and lacks a more systematic assessment. The claim of “novel” questions is also debatable, as many appear to be minor variations of questions already present in the dataset. Showing a few examples does not constitute a strong evaluation of generative capabilities.

Fourth, the paper suffers from numerous minor inconsistencies and stylistic issues. For example, the use of “cf.” in the introduction is inappropriate. Equation numbering is sporadic. The phrasing is often imprecise (e.g., “a small number of questions were excluded”). More importantly, the paper references the supplementary materials extensively (e.g., “Table SI-1”) without providing sufficient context within the main text. The length constraints imposed on questions (100 functions) appear arbitrary and could significantly impact the model’s expressiveness. The fact that the auxiliary feature f∅(x) had to be used because of programs with no relevance to the game is concerning and illustrates a flaw in the initial grammar design. The omission of contexts 1 and 2 from the evaluation due to large hypothesis spaces is a further limitation.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Methodological flaws in model design and optimization.
*   Overly complex and poorly justified probabilistic model.
*   Weak evaluation and subjective assessment of generated questions.
*   Numerous minor inconsistencies and stylistic issues.
*   Lack of rigorous theoretical analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The core idea of modeling question asking as program generation is novel and potentially promising.
*   The dataset used provides a valuable resource for studying human question asking.
*   The model demonstrates some ability to predict human question frequencies.

**Overall:**

While the paper presents an interesting idea, the numerous methodological shortcomings and lack of rigor make it difficult to assess its true contribution. The paper feels incomplete and would require substantial revision to address the issues raised above. I, therefore, recommend **rejection**. The potential of the idea is overshadowed by the execution and presentation. The reliance on hand-engineered features, approximate inference, and a complex grammar without sufficient justification weakens the overall impact of the work.
