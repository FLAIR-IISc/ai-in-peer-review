

# Peer Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that can be executed on possible world states to generate answers. The authors argue that traditional active learning approaches focus on simple label queries, whereas humans ask rich, compositional questions that target critical parameters in a learning task. The paper models questions as programs in a simple programming language (based on lambda calculus), with a grammar that enables compositional question generation. A probabilistic generative model then predicts which questions humans would ask by balancing four key features: informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain), complexity (favoring shorter programs), answer type (Boolean, Number, Color, Location), and relevance (ensuring questions reference the game board).

The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by human participants in a Battleship-like game. The model successfully predicts question frequencies in held-out contexts (achieving a Spearman correlation of 0.64 with human question frequencies) and generates novel, human-like questions that were not present in the training data.

This work connects to several important areas: cognitive science (the "language of thought" hypothesis), computational linguistics (compositional semantics), and active learning. It builds on prior work in program synthesis (e.g., Lake et al. 2015) and question answering as program execution (e.g., Wang et al. 2015), but makes a significant contribution by applying these techniques to model *question asking* rather than question answering.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach**: The paper makes a compelling case for modeling questions as executable programs rather than as natural language strings. This approach elegantly captures compositionality (e.g., combining size and comparison operators) and computability (questions as functions from world states to answers), which are central to human question asking but absent in prior work.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper carefully develops the theoretical underpinnings, connecting to linguistic theory (compositionality), cognitive science (language of thought), and information theory (EIG). The probabilistic model is well-formulated and grounded in a clear information-theoretic framework.

3. **Rigorous Evaluation**: The evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare against several ablated models, demonstrating the necessity of each key feature (informativeness, complexity, answer type). The leave-one-out cross-validation provides a strong test of generalizability, and the qualitative generation of novel questions demonstrates the model's capability to produce human-like questions.

4. **Clear Contributions**: The paper clearly articulates how it differs from related work. It distinguishes itself from previous active learning approaches (which focus on label queries), visual question generation (which requires large image-question datasets), and goal-directed dialog systems (which use canned questions).

5. **Good Balance of Theory and Experimentation**: The paper successfully combines theoretical insights with empirical validation, providing a predictive model that can be evaluated on real human question data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Domain Specificity**: The grammar and model are designed specifically for the Battleship game domain. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't sufficiently address how to extend the approach to more complex domains. The paper would benefit from discussing concrete strategies for domain adaptation or transfer.

2. **Natural Language Gap**: The model operates on semantic representations rather than natural language. While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation, they don't explore how to bridge the gap between semantic programs and natural language generation (e.g., using recent neural paraphrasing approaches). This is a significant hurdle for practical applications.

3. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The evaluation is confined to a single domain (Battleship game). While this is a well-chosen test case, the paper would be stronger with at least one additional domain to demonstrate broader applicability.

4. **Technical Details in Supplementary Materials**: Some key technical details (e.g., the exact grammar rules, the set of features) are relegated to supplementary materials. While this is common practice, some of these details would be helpful in the main text for readers to fully understand the model's capabilities.

5. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Neural Approaches**: The paper compares to traditional active learning and dialog systems but doesn't adequately address recent neural approaches to question generation (e.g., Jain et al. 2016, Vijayakumar et al. 2016), which have made significant progress in generating diverse questions.

## Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The model is well-formulated, the evaluation methodology is rigorous, and the authors carefully acknowledge limitations. The paper presents a complete piece of work rather than a position paper.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and organized. The core concepts are clearly explained, and the figures effectively illustrate key points. The main text provides sufficient detail for experts to understand the model, though some technical details are in supplementary materials.

**Originality**: The approach of modeling question asking as program generation is highly original. While prior work has modeled questions as programs for *answering* (e.g., Wang et al. 2015), this paper is the first to apply this framework to *question generation*. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work.

**Significance**: This work makes a significant contribution to bridging the gap between cognitive science and machine learning. It provides a new computational framework for understanding human question asking that could lead to more human-like question-asking agents. While the immediate application is limited to the Battleship domain, the theoretical framework has broad potential for future work in active learning and cognitive modeling.

## Specific Recommendations

1. **Address Domain Generalization**: The authors should expand their discussion on how to extend this approach to new domains. What aspects of the grammar are domain-specific versus domain-general? How much knowledge engineering would be required for a new domain?

2. **Bridge the Natural Language Gap**: Consider discussing how to integrate the semantic question representations with natural language generation techniques. This would make the model more practically applicable.

3. **Expand Evaluation**: Add at least one additional domain to demonstrate the model's broader applicability. Even a brief experiment with a different game or scenario would strengthen the significance claim.

4. **Clarify Technical Details**: Move key technical details (e.g., the grammar rules) from supplementary materials to the main text to improve accessibility.

5. **Compare to Recent Neural Approaches**: Add a brief comparison to recent neural question generation work (e.g., Jain et al. 2016) to better position the paper's contribution.

## Final Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking. The theoretical foundation is sound, the evaluation is rigorous, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over ablated models. While the domain specificity and natural language gap are limitations, these are acknowledged by the authors and represent opportunities for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current approach.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it bridges cognitive science and machine learning with a novel computational approach that advances our understanding of human-like question asking. The work has potential to influence both cognitive science and AI research.

**Overall Rating**: Strong Accept (8/10)

**Pro Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Novel theoretical framework for modeling human question asking
- Rigorous evaluation with clear demonstration of model advantages
- Strong connection to cognitive science and linguistics literature
- Demonstrates ability to generate novel human-like questions
- Well-written and well-structured paper

**Con Arguments for Acceptance**:
- Domain-specific grammar limits immediate applicability
- Natural language gap is not addressed in the current framework
- Limited evaluation scope to a single domain

The paper makes a substantial contribution to understanding the computational basis of human question asking, and the framework has potential for broader application with future work. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS.
