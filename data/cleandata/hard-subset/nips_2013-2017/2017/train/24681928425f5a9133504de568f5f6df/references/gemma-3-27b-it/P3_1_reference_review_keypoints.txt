- Summary: Models human question asking as program generation where questions are executable programs; evaluated on a Battleship-like dataset to predict question frequencies and generate novel questions.

- Related work: Positions the approach against active learning, dialog systems, and VQG, arguing it overcomes limitations like canned responses and large paired-data needs.

- Novelty: Framing question asking as program synthesis is original and captures compositional question structure.

- Theoretical foundation: Builds on linguistics, cognitive science, and program synthesis for a principled framework.

- Empirical validation: Tested on real human data showing good prediction of question frequencies and plausible question generation.

- Grammar expressiveness: Well-defined grammar with lambda expressions and set operators that captures observed question types.

- Feature engineering: Uses informativeness (EIG) and complexity features; ablation studies show their importance.

- Domain specificity: Current grammar and implementation are tied to the game context, raising generalization concerns to real-world domains.

- Computational complexity: Importance sampling for the partition function is expensive and limits scalability.

- Limited qualitative evaluation: Generated questions lack rigorous human-rated assessments of relevance, clarity, and human-likeness.

- EIG modeling limitation: Reliance on a Bayesian ideal observer may not capture human heuristics and biases in question asking.

- Dataset size: Only 605 question-context pairs, which may be small for complex probabilistic modeling.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound, empirically promising, and impactful across active learning, dialog, and cognitive science.

- Arguments for rejection: Concerns about domain generality, computational scalability, and limited qualitative evaluation.

- Overall assessment: Strong contribution whose merits outweigh weaknesses; promising framework deserving acceptance with minor fixes.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

- Requested revisions: Expand discussion of domain generalization, add rigorous human-based qualitative evaluation of generated questions, and discuss possible optimizations to reduce computational cost.
