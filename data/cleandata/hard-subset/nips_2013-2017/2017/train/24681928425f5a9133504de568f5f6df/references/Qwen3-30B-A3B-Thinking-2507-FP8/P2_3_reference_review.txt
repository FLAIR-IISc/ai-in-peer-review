

# Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs. The authors argue that human questions are compositional and computable, and thus can be modeled as program synthesis rather than simple label queries. They collect a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship-like game where players must identify ship configurations. The authors develop a probabilistic model that generates questions by combining compositional elements (using a grammar-based program representation), with features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies (Spearman correlation of 0.64) and generates novel human-like questions not present in the training data.

## Strengths

**Originality (High):** This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by framing question asking as program generation, a perspective that differs fundamentally from previous work. Unlike prior active learning approaches that focus on simple label queries or neural question generation systems that require massive datasets, this work provides a principled, cognitively-motivated approach grounded in compositionality and computability. The connection to cognitive science literature (e.g., "language of thought" [3]) and linguistic principles (compositionality [10]) is well-established and provides a strong theoretical foundation.

**Quality (High):** The paper is technically sound with rigorous evaluation methodology. The authors conduct thorough ablation studies comparing the full model against variants that omit key components (informativeness, complexity, answer type features). Their results consistently show that all features contribute meaningfully to predicting human behavior (the full model outperforms all variants in 81-100% of bootstrap samples). The model's ability to generate novel, contextually relevant questions (Figure 3) demonstrates its capacity for productive question generation, a key human capability that previous systems fail to capture.

**Clarity (High):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain their technical approach using concrete examples (e.g., how "How long is the blue ship?" maps to the program (size Blue)). The grammar description (Section 4.2) and feature definitions (Section 4.5) are particularly well-structured and accessible. The evaluation methodology is transparent, and the supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for reproduction.

**Significance (High):** This work addresses a fundamental gap in active learning research: moving beyond simple label queries to rich, context-sensitive questioning. The framework provides a new theoretical lens for understanding human question asking and represents a significant step toward more human-like machine question asking. The paper's potential to influence both active learning and computational cognitive science is substantial, as evidenced by its clear connections to foundational work in linguistics and cognitive science.

## Weaknesses

**Domain Specificity (Moderate):** While the authors acknowledge that the grammar is somewhat domain-specific to the Battleship game, they don't sufficiently address the generalizability challenge. The paper states that "many aspects of our grammar are domain general," but doesn't provide evidence for this claim. Future work could explore how much of the grammar would need to be redesigned for a new domain (e.g., medical diagnosis vs. game playing), which is crucial for assessing the approach's broader applicability.

**Natural Language Generation Gap (Moderate):** The model operates on semantic program representations rather than directly generating natural language. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't discuss how to bridge this gap (e.g., using recent advances in program-to-text generation [24]). For the work to be practically useful in real-world applications, this remains an important missing piece.

**Dataset Size (Moderate):** The dataset of 605 questions across 18 contexts is relatively small, which limits the statistical power of the evaluation. While the authors acknowledge the sparsity of human data (many questions were asked only once), a larger dataset would strengthen the claims about the model's ability to capture human question patterns.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of active learning and computational cognitive science. It successfully addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a principled, cognitively-grounded approach to modeling human question asking. The technical execution is sound, the evaluation is thorough, and the results are compelling.

The paper stands out from previous NIPS work on active learning [21] and question generation [16, 23] by focusing on the compositional, context-sensitive nature of human questions rather than simple label queries or neural network-based generation. It advances the field by showing that questions can be meaningfully represented as programs that can be evaluated against possible world states.

The most compelling aspect is the model's ability to generate novel questions that reflect human-like creativity ("Are all the ships horizontal?" or "What is the top left of all the ship tiles?"). This demonstrates the model's productive capacity, a hallmark of human language that previous systems struggle to replicate.

## Recommendation

This paper is well-suited for NIPS and should be accepted. It makes a clear, significant contribution to the field with strong theoretical foundations, rigorous evaluation, and novel insights into human question asking. The work is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a meaningful gap in active learning research.

The minor weaknesses regarding domain specificity and natural language generation are acknowledged by the authors and represent important directions for future work rather than flaws in the current contribution. Given the paper's strong theoretical grounding, clear evaluation, and significant potential impact, it represents a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

The paper advances the state of the art in active learning by demonstrating that rich, human-like question asking can be modeled as program generation, and it provides a framework that could guide future work in this important area.
