Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a computational framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs (lambda-calculus/LISP-style expressions) that, when run on a hypothesized world state, return answers of specific types (Boolean, Number, Color, Location). A context-free grammar (with some extensions such as lambda abstraction, map, and set operations) defines a compositional hypothesis space of programs/questions. A log-linear model over features assigns probabilities to questions in a given context; features include Expected Information Gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, a complexity penalty derived from the PCFG prior (−log q(x)), answer-type indicators, and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit via gradient ascent using importance sampling over the large program space, and EIG is computed relative to a Battleship-like hypothesis space. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans across 18 ambiguous board contexts (from [19]), predicting held-out question distributions (leave-one-context-out) and generating novel, “human-like” questions.

The work relates to active learning [21], but extends beyond label queries by modeling richer question types and their utility. It connects to formal semantics of questions as partitions [6, 18], Bayesian pragmatic models of question asking [9, 19], and program-based concept modeling and neuro-symbolic reasoning [5, 12, 13, 17]. It contrasts with goal-oriented dialog systems [25, 1, 20, 22] and VQG [16, 23, 11], arguing for intentional, compositional question generation with limited data. It also draws on language-of-thought and compositional semantics traditions [3, 4, 10, 14, 15].

Quality (technical soundness and evaluation)
- Modeling: The exponential family formulation with features and a PCFG-derived complexity prior is technically sound. The EIG-based utility with Bayesian belief updating over explicit hypothesis spaces is standard and appropriate for this domain. The gradient derivation and use of importance sampling to approximate expectations and Z are correct in principle.
- Approximations and scalability: The approach requires extensive sampling (150k programs per step, 100k iterations) and drops two contexts due to hypothesis space size and one probabilistic rule (draw), removing 14 questions. This highlights computational fragility and raises concerns about scalability beyond toy domains or when richer stochastic primitives are included.
- Data and mapping: The dataset is modest (605 questions, 40 participants). Natural language questions are translated to programs, but the mapping procedure is not described in detail here (ambiguity resolution, inter-annotator agreement, coverage). This matters for reproducibility and for assessing potential biases introduced by annotation.
- Baselines and ablations: Lesion studies (removing EIG, complexity, or type features) are informative. The full model outperforms ablations in log-likelihood, especially vs. the complexity-agnostic variant. However, reported rank correlations show full (0.64) vs. information-agnostic (0.65), suggesting EIG’s contribution to ranking is limited; this tension is acknowledged via bootstrap on likelihood but could be discussed more. Comparisons to additional baselines (e.g., pure EIG, pure complexity, frequency-based or n-gram-style templating) would strengthen claims.
- Generalization: Leave-one-context-out within the same domain is reasonable, and the model can synthesize novel questions with meaningful partitions. Transfer to new domains remains untested.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, with intuitive examples of the grammar and evaluation. The overall workflow (grammar, features, sampling-based training, EIG) is well explained. Some critical details are deferred to the supplement (full grammar, EIG computation specifics, hypothesis space sizes), and the NL-to-program mapping lacks detail. Reporting of computational costs, convergence diagnostics, and sensitivity analyses would help.

Originality
Viewing question asking as program synthesis, and combining a LOT-style PCFG with explicit EIG and a learned complexity trade-off for predicting human question distributions, is a novel and well-motivated synthesis. Prior work has modeled questions as partitions and used programs for answering, but program generation for open-ended question asking with predictive evaluation against human data appears new.

Significance
The work contributes to computational cognitive modeling of question asking and suggests a path toward richer, goal-directed active learning in machines. Its significance for broad ML lies in demonstrating a principled, compositional search space and utility-driven selection that can generate human-like, intentional queries with limited data. Practical impact is limited by domain specificity, manual grammar engineering, and computational scalability; bridging to end-to-end natural language and larger domains remains future work.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation of questions as executable programs with a compositional grammar.
- Probabilistic model that captures a trade-off between informativeness and complexity, aligning with human data.
- Demonstrated ability to generate novel, context-sensitive, human-like questions with explicit partition-based equivalence checking.
- Lesion studies that show the importance of complexity and type features; solid evidence that EIG alone does not explain behavior.
- Well-situated in prior literature across active learning, formal semantics, and LOT/program induction.

Cons
- Limited dataset and domain; manual grammar tailored to Battleship; two contexts and a probabilistic rule excluded for computational reasons.
- Incomplete description of natural language to program translation and potential annotation biases.
- Scalability concerns for EIG computation and program-space sampling; unclear how approach extends to larger, noisier hypothesis spaces or richer stochastic primitives.
- Mixed evidence for EIG’s contribution in rank correlation; lack of stronger baselines (e.g., heuristic, template, or simple neural models adapted to this setup).
- No empirical bridge to surface natural language generation/parsing; reliance on semantic forms constrains applicability.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide details on the NL-to-program annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement, coverage, and examples of ambiguous cases.
- Report computational costs (runtime, samples used for EIG/hypothesis enumeration), convergence behavior, and sensitivity to the proposal PCFG and sample sizes.
- Include additional baselines: pure EIG, pure complexity, frequency/template methods, and possibly a simple sequence model constrained to the grammar.
- Explore learning rule probabilities or featureized PCFG expansions rather than uniform sampling; analyze sensitivity to grammar design choices.
- Evaluate transfer to at least one additional domain with minimal grammar engineering, or quantify what components are domain-general vs. domain-specific.
- Consider modeling answerer cost/noise or cognitively plausible resource constraints beyond length/complexity, and report ablations for the relevance filter.

Overall assessment
A technically sound and well-argued synthesis that advances computational modeling of question asking via program generation, with convincing in-domain results and thoughtful analysis of the informativeness–complexity trade-off. Its main limitations are domain specificity, computational scalability, and limited baselines. If positioned as a cognitive/computational modeling contribution with a clear path toward broader ML, it is a meaningful step forward.
