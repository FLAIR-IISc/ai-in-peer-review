## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and compelling framework for understanding and modeling human question asking by recasting it as program generation. The approach is theoretically sound, empirically grounded in a well-defined (though admittedly limited) dataset, and demonstrates promising results in predicting and generating human-like questions. While there are areas for improvement, particularly regarding the complexity of the model and its scalability, I believe this work represents a significant step forward and warrants acceptance to NIPS.

**Summary:** The authors propose a framework where questions are treated as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, yield an answer. This allows for a more nuanced representation of questions than traditional active learning approaches, which typically focus on simple label requests. They develop a probabilistic model based on a context-free grammar to generate and evaluate questions, learning from a dataset of questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like game.  The model successfully predicts the frequency of human questions and can generate novel, plausible questions.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Originality:** The core idea of framing question asking as program generation is a fresh perspective, moving beyond simple label acquisition in active learning and offering a computational lens for understanding human inquisitiveness. This is a clear departure from most existing work in active learning and dialog systems.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The authors ground their work in concepts from linguistics (compositionality) and cognitive science (language of thought, program induction), lending theoretical weight to their approach. The connection to formal semantics and program synthesis is well-articulated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The evaluation on the human question dataset provides a strong empirical basis for the model.  The ability to predict question frequencies *and* generate novel questions is a noteworthy achievement.  The quantitative and qualitative results both demonstrate the model’s ability to capture aspects of human question asking.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The authors clearly explain their methodology and the rationale behind their design choices. The supplementary material further enhances clarity.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity:** The Battleship game, while providing a controlled environment, is a relatively limited domain. The grammar and features may be somewhat tailored to this game, potentially limiting the generalizability of the approach to other problem spaces. The authors acknowledge this limitation.
* **Model Complexity & Scalability:** The optimization procedure, relying on importance sampling, appears computationally expensive. The authors had to limit question length and even exclude certain question types (e.g., requesting a visual demonstration) due to computational constraints.  Scaling this approach to more complex domains with larger state spaces remains a challenge.
* **Feature Engineering:** While the authors explore several features, there’s a degree of manual feature engineering involved (e.g., defining informativeness through EIG). Future work could explore end-to-end learning of features, potentially leveraging deep learning techniques.
* **Limited Comparison to Baselines:**  The comparisons to alternative models are present but could be more extensive.  While the ablation studies are useful, a comparison to a more sophisticated language model (e.g., a pre-trained transformer) adapted for question generation would be valuable.



**Review Criteria Breakdown:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the experimental setup is rigorous. The results are compelling, though limited by the domain. The authors address limitations honestly.
* **Clarity (9/10):** Excellent writing and organization. The concepts are explained clearly, and the paper is easy to follow. The supplemental material provides helpful details.
* **Originality (9/10):**  Highly original. The framing of question asking as program generation is a novel contribution that significantly departs from existing approaches.
* **Significance (8/10):** This work has the potential to significantly impact the fields of active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive science. The ability to model human question asking could lead to more intelligent and interactive AI systems.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and impactful idea.
* The theoretical foundations are strong.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The domain specificity limits generalizability.
* The model’s complexity and scalability are concerns.
* Comparison to stronger baselines would be beneficial.



**Final Recommendation:** **Accept.** Despite the limitations, the originality, theoretical soundness, and promising results of this work make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. The weaknesses are acknowledged and do not fundamentally undermine the strengths of the paper. I believe this work has the potential to inspire further research in this exciting area.
