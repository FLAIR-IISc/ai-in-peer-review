Summary
This paper models human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a typed, LISP-like language defined by a context-free grammar with lambda abstraction, mapping, and set operations. A probabilistic log-linear model over programs assigns higher probability to questions that balance Expected Information Gain (EIG), complexity (via PCFG prior), answer type preferences, and a “relevance” filter. The model is trained on a Battleship-like task (605 natural-language questions from 40 participants across 18 contexts, with 16 contexts ultimately used) by maximizing the likelihood of human questions, approximating the partition function and feature expectations via importance sampling over programs generated by the grammar. The model predicts the distribution of human questions in held-out contexts and samples novel, human-like questions. Ablations suggest that complexity and informativeness features both matter.

Relation to prior work: The paper bridges active learning (typically label queries) with richer, goal-directed question generation; connects to formal semantics of questions as partitions over worlds; and leverages ideas from program induction and semantic parsing. It contrasts with VQG systems and end-to-end dialog models that lack explicit informativeness objectives. Compared to prior cognitive models of question asking that reason with EIG or pragmatic utility, this work contributes a generative, compositional program space and a learning-based weighting of features to match human data.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation: Compositional programs provide a precise semantics for questions, enabling utility (EIG) computation over hypotheses.
- Integrates multiple pressures: Informativeness, complexity, type preferences, and relevance are combined in a simple, learnable energy-based model.
- Predictive evaluation: Leave-one-context-out fits show the full model outperforms ablations in log-likelihood; reported average rank correlation ≈ 0.64 with human frequencies.
- Productivity: The grammar accounts for novel human questions across contexts, addressing limitations of retrieval-based baselines.
- Qualitative generation: Samples include plausible, creative questions that generalize beyond the training set.
- Relevance to NeurIPS: Intersects active learning, program induction, and computational cognitive modeling; suggests a route to more purposeful question-asking agents.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality/technical soundness
- Inference stability: The target distribution changes during training, yet expectations are approximated using a fixed set of 150k samples from a uniform PCFG proposal. This can lead to severe weight degeneracy and biased gradients as θ drifts. No diagnostics (ESS, variance, resampling) or alternative proposals are reported. The choice of 100k gradient steps with static samples is unconventional; convergence behavior and sensitivity to random seeds are not discussed.
- EIG modeling assumptions: The prior over hypotheses and likelihood are hand-specified (largely uniform), which may not match human priors or perceptual constraints. The surprising lack of correlation between EIG and human frequency (as also noted in [19]) suggests misspecification; the model compensates with a strong complexity feature, but this may obfuscate the role of pragmatic factors (answerability, cognitive cost, presuppositions).
- Mixed metrics: While the full model wins in log-likelihood (with bootstrap support), the information-agnostic variant achieves a slightly higher average rank correlation (0.65 vs 0.64). This inconsistency weakens the quantitative claim that informativeness improves predictive ranking; fuller reporting (exact LL values, CIs) would help.
- Handcrafted features and filter: The “relevance” feature is a hand-coded marker in the grammar (b), which builds in domain knowledge. It would be informative to show results without this filter, or to learn a soft relevance prior from data.
- Evaluation scope: The novelty of generated questions is assessed qualitatively with a few examples. No human evaluation (e.g., usefulness or naturalness ratings) or downstream impact on task performance is provided.
- Generalization: The grammar contains domain-specific primitives; portability to new domains is discussed but not demonstrated. The amount of knowledge engineering required remains unclear.
- Mapping from language to programs: The work assumes gold semantic forms; the non-trivial problem of parsing free-form questions into programs is deferred, limiting practical applicability.

Clarity
- Generally well written and accessible. However:
  - Optimization and importance sampling details need more transparency (resampling, proposal tuning, variance control).
  - Report exact LL numbers and standard errors across folds; include per-context correlation distributions.
  - Specify how natural language questions were mapped to programs (annotation protocol, inter-annotator agreement).

Originality
- Modeling question asking as program synthesis with a learnable energy over program features is novel and interesting. The compositional generative space is a substantial advance over fixed-template or label-query approaches. While related in spirit to program-based QA and semantic parsing, the focus on question generation and human prediction is distinct.

Significance
- Conceptually significant for connecting active learning, formal semantics, and program induction; practically promising for agents that ask purposeful questions. However, the empirical demonstration is limited to a single, small domain with hand-engineered semantics, and lacks end-to-end language or user studies. Significance would be stronger with evidence of transfer, a learned parser, or measurable gains in an interactive task.

Pros
- Principled, compositional formalism; clear link to utility.
- Empirical evidence that complexity matters alongside informativeness.
- Predicts distributions over open-ended questions and produces plausible novel ones.
- Bridges multiple threads of NeurIPS-relevant research.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on hand-engineered grammar and features; limited domain.
- Inference/optimization methodology under-specified; potential biases.
- Limited quantitative evidence for informativeness in ranking; no human eval of generated questions.
- No language interface; practicality remains speculative.

Recommendation: Weak reject
This is an intriguing and promising direction with a solid conceptual foundation, but the current empirical scope and methodological gaps make it borderline for NeurIPS. Strengthening evaluations and addressing inference and generalization concerns would elevate the contribution.

Suggestions for improvement
- Improve inference: Use adaptive importance sampling, SMC, or MCMC over program space; report ESS, variance, and convergence diagnostics; resample during training; compare different proposals.
- Report full quantitative results: Exact log-likelihoods with CIs; per-context correlations; ablation effect sizes; sensitivity to sample size and grammar depth limits.
- Model pragmatics: Incorporate an answerer model and explicit cognitive/response costs (e.g., RSA-style utility), potentially explaining the EIG–frequency discrepancy.
- Language interface: Add a semantic parser from text to programs (even weakly supervised), or evaluate few-shot mapping; report annotation reliability.
- Broaden evaluation: Human judgments of generated questions (usefulness, naturalness); measure impact on task performance in an interactive setting; attempt a second domain to test portability.
- Reduce hand-crafting: Explore learning reusable, domain-general primitives or inducing grammars from data; compare to neural program synthesis/neural module baselines conditioned on context.
