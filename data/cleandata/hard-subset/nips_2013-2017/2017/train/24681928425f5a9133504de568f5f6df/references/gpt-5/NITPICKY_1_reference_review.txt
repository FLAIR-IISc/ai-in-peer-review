Summary
This paper proposes a probabilistic model of human question asking by representing questions as executable programs in a small lambda/LISP-like language. A context-free grammar defines a compositional question space; questions are scored via a log-linear energy function with features for informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (−log PCFG probability), answer type, and a hand-coded “relevance” filter. The model is trained with importance sampling and gradient ascent to predict the distribution of questions in an information-seeking “Battleship”-style task (605 questions across 18 contexts). Evaluations include leave-one-context-out log-likelihood, rank correlations between model energies and human frequencies, and qualitative generation of novel questions.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of questions-as-programs as a unifying representation, with compositionality and computability.
- An explicit generative grammar that can cover the full dataset and produce novel questions.
- Principled use of Bayesian belief updating and EIG for informativeness.
- Ablation analysis suggests importance of complexity and type features; LOOCV setup is appropriate for context-sensitive prediction.
- Interesting qualitative samples demonstrating nontrivial forms of compositional generalization.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological and technical
- Importance sampling for Z is incorrectly specified: Z ≈ E_x~q[p(x;θ)/q(x)] is circular because p(x;θ) depends on Z. The unbiased IS estimator is Z = Σ exp(−E(x)), approximated as E_x~q[exp(−E(x))/q(x)]. This is a serious error and calls into question likelihood estimates and any bootstrap comparisons based on them.
- Heavy reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-informed grammar designed “based on the full set of contexts,” including those held out. This leaks test information and weakens the generalization claim; the LOOCV evaluates parameter generalization, not representation generalization.
- The “relevance” feature f∅ is a manual board-reference marker; this is effectively a hand-coded filter. If the grammar admits many irrelevant programs, the model’s performance partly hinges on this engineered feature rather than learned preferences.
- EIG computation details are underspecified. The prior p(h) is described as uniform over sizes and placements, but it must be conditioned on currently revealed tiles; details of the hypothesis space pruning, enumeration, or sampling, and answer sets Ax are missing in the main text. Two contexts were dropped due to large hypothesis spaces, raising scalability concerns.
- The complexity feature reuses the same PCFG (uniform productions) used as the IS proposal q. This couples the prior used for sampling, the complexity penalty, and the estimator variance in ways that are not analyzed. −log q(x) is not just length; it encodes grammar branching idiosyncrasies. A simpler length/MDL or node-count measure would be more interpretable and robust.
- Optimization details are incomplete: whether the 150k-question sample is fixed across iterations, whether gradients are stabilized or regularized, variance of the IS estimators, and convergence diagnostics are not reported. No sensitivity analysis to sample size or learning rate.
- The EIG=0 indicator adds an arbitrary discontinuity; justification and learned weights are not reported.
- “Orientation” is coerced into Boolean (Horizontal = True, Vertical = False), an arbitrary choice that may distort EIG/type features.
- Equivalence testing for novel questions uses mutual information of answer distributions; computational load and thresholds are not described.

Evaluation and claims
- The average Spearman correlation of the full model is 0.64, while the information-agnostic variant achieves 0.65 (higher). This undercuts the claim that EIG improves prediction; the text emphasizes log-likelihood gains but provides no numbers. Clarify the apparent inconsistency between rank correlation and log-likelihood.
- Baselines are weak. All comparisons are internal ablations. Missing: a pure EIG-only model; a PCFG-only prior; a retrieval/template baseline; or a modern neural question generator fitted to the same data (with explicit constraints).
- Qualitative samples are interesting but anecdotal. No human evaluation of novelty/quality or utility, and no quantitative diversity/quality metrics.
- The dataset is small (605 questions from 40 participants) and further pruned (14 questions and 2 contexts dropped). This limits statistical power and external validity.

Clarity and presentation
- Many essential details are deferred to the supplement (grammar rules, EIG details). The main text lacks enough information for reproduction.
- Numerous citation and formatting issues: several references are incomplete or malformed (missing years, truncated author lists, placeholders like “arXiv preprint,” “L,” “d,” “st Conference…”), and there are typos and inconsistencies (e.g., “within in,” footnote markers misplaced).
- Some claims are ambiguous or overstated (e.g., “very strong agreement” in some contexts without reporting variability; “no correlation at all between EIG alone and human frequency” without statistics).

Quality
Interesting idea with some principled components, but technical correctness is compromised by the Z estimator error, unclear EIG computation, and test leakage through grammar design. Evaluation lacks strong baselines and quantitative rigor for generation.

Originality
Moderately original in framing question asking as program generation and learning a probabilistic model over that space; related to prior work on program induction and semantic parsers, but the specific application to question generation is novel.

Significance
Potentially impactful direction, but current empirical scope is narrow (single toy domain, small dataset) and methodological issues reduce confidence.

Reproducibility
Low from the main text alone. Critical components are in the supplement; code is not provided; several implementation details are missing.

Pros
- Clear, compelling representation (questions as programs)
- Sensible feature set; informative ablations
- Compositional generalization beyond observed questions

Cons
- Incorrect IS estimator for Z; unclear inference details
- Grammar engineered with test leakage; heavy hand-tuning (relevance filter)
- Weak baselines; small dataset; dropped contexts/questions
- Presentation issues and incomplete references

Recommendation
Reject. The paper presents an appealing idea and some promising results, but the combination of a core technical error in likelihood estimation, leakage via grammar design, insufficiently specified EIG/inference, weak baselines, and numerous presentation flaws outweighs the contributions. I encourage the authors to correct the estimator, decouple grammar design from test contexts, strengthen baselines and evaluation (including human judgments for generation), and improve clarity and citations.
