

# Peer Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, output an answer. The authors argue that this approach reframes question asking as program synthesis, leveraging compositionality (building questions from conceptual primitives) and computability (questions as executable programs). They evaluate their model on a dataset of 605 natural language questions collected from humans playing a Battleship-like game, where players asked questions to resolve ambiguous board configurations. The model uses a probabilistic generative approach with features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The results demonstrate that the full model outperforms variants that omit key components and can generate novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

This work builds on prior research in cognitive science (e.g., Fodor's "language of thought" [3]) and computational linguistics (e.g., program synthesis for question answering [24, 12]), but makes a significant contribution by formalizing question asking as program generation with a focus on compositionality and context sensitivity. Unlike prior work on question generation (e.g., [16, 23]) that requires large datasets of paired questions and images, this approach enables question generation in novel scenarios with minimal training.

## Strengths

**Originality (High):** The paper makes a genuinely novel contribution by modeling questions as programs rather than relying on statistical language models or canned question templates. This approach directly addresses the "productivity" of human question asking—the ability to generate novel questions in new contexts—which existing approaches cannot capture. The compositionality aspect is particularly compelling, as it aligns with cognitive science frameworks for understanding human language and concept formation.

**Quality (High):** The technical approach is sound, with a well-defined probabilistic model and clear evaluation methodology. The authors carefully consider relevant features (informativeness, complexity, etc.) and rigorously test their model against plausible variants. The use of leave-one-out cross-validation and bootstrapping for statistical significance adds robustness to the evaluation. The model's ability to generate novel, contextually relevant questions (Figure 3) demonstrates the practical utility of the approach.

**Significance (High):** The paper addresses a fundamental gap in active learning—moving beyond simple label queries to rich, human-like question asking. This has implications for human-AI collaboration, educational technology, and more natural human-computer interaction. The framework could potentially be extended to other domains, making it a promising foundation for future work.

**Clarity (High):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The key concepts (compositionality, computability) are clearly explained with concrete examples. The grammar description is precise, and the supplementary materials provide sufficient detail for replication. The evaluation results are presented clearly with appropriate figures and statistical analysis.

## Weaknesses

**Domain Specificity (Moderate):** While the authors acknowledge that some grammar elements are domain-specific, the model's current implementation is tightly coupled to the Battleship game. The paper doesn't sufficiently explore how much of the grammar would need to be redesigned for other domains, or whether the core principles (compositionality, computability) would generalize without significant domain engineering.

**Natural Language Generation Gap (Moderate):** The model generates program representations, not natural language questions. While the paper mentions bridging this gap via "recent tools in computational linguistics" [24], this remains a significant technical hurdle for practical deployment. The paper would benefit from more concrete discussion of this translation challenge.

**Scalability (Moderate):** The grammar and model are designed for a specific domain with limited possible questions. The paper doesn't address how the approach would scale to more complex domains with larger hypothesis spaces (e.g., medical diagnosis or scientific inquiry).

## Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of human question asking and active learning. The core idea of modeling questions as programs is compelling, well-executed, and clearly differentiates from prior work. The evaluation is thorough, and the ability to generate novel questions demonstrates the model's utility.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), makes a novel contribution (Originality), and addresses a meaningful problem with potential for impact (Significance).

## Arguments for Acceptance

* Presents a fundamentally new approach to modeling human question asking
* Successfully demonstrates the ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel questions
* Clearly differentiates from related work in cognitive science and AI
* Provides a theoretically grounded framework with strong empirical validation
* Well-written and well-organized with clear methodology and evaluation

## Arguments Against Acceptance

* Domain specificity limits immediate applicability to broader settings
* Natural language generation from program representations remains unaddressed
* Limited discussion of scalability to more complex domains

## Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to active learning and human-AI interaction, with a novel theoretical framework that is well-supported by empirical evidence. While the domain specificity is a limitation, it's a common challenge in computational cognitive science papers that can be addressed in future work. The authors have laid a solid foundation that will likely inspire significant follow-on research.

This paper is clearly within NIPS scope, addressing active learning and cognitive modeling with a computational approach. The work represents a meaningful advancement in the field that will be of interest to both cognitive scientists and machine learning researchers.
