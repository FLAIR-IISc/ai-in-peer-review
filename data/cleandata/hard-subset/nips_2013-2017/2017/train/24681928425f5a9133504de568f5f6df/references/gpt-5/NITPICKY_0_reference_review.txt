Summary
This paper proposes a computational framework for question asking that represents questions as executable programs in a simple LISP-like language. A probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) generates candidate programs, and a log-linear model over features chooses among them. Key features include expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, a complexity prior (-log probability under the grammar), answer-type indicators, and a “relevance” flag. The model is trained on a Battleship-style dataset of 605 natural language questions mapped to programs across 18 contexts, and evaluated by leave-one-context-out likelihood and correlations with human question frequencies; it also samples novel “human-like” questions.

Relation to prior work is drawn to active learning, formal semantics of questions (partitions), program induction/Lot, and QA systems that interpret questions as programs. The core claim is that question asking can be cast as program synthesis, balancing informativeness and complexity.

Strengths
- Clear, principled framing of questions as executable programs; compositionality is well-motivated and grounded in formal semantics and LOT.
- Integrates EIG with a concise complexity prior, yielding a plausible account of human trade-offs.
- Grammar-based generation yields productivity beyond memorizing previously seen questions; ablations suggest complexity is essential.
- Qualitative samples are interesting and context-appropriate; the equivalence check via MI is thoughtful.
- The dataset and task are clear, and LOOCV across contexts is a reasonable test of generalization within-domain.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Importance sampling estimator typo: The paper states Z ≈ E_q[p(x;θ) q(x)], but standard IS requires Z = ∑x exp(-E(x)) ≈ (1/M)∑m exp(-E(x_m))/q(x_m) = E_q[exp(-E)/q]. As written, the estimator is incorrect. If this is a typo, it should be corrected; if not, it is a serious flaw affecting all likelihoods and gradients.
- IS/optimization details are under-specified: A single fixed set of 150k samples is reused for all 100k gradient steps. This truncates support and biases E_model expectations toward the proposal sample, potentially invalidating likelihood estimates and gradients. No reporting of effective sample size, variance of estimates, convergence diagnostics, or sensitivity to random seeds. Learning rate 0.1 for 100k steps without regularization is unusual; no evidence of stability or overfitting checks.
- Inconsistent dataset handling: The paper first claims the grammar can represent all 605 questions; later it removes the (draw C) rule and “the corresponding 14 questions,” and also drops contexts 1–2 due to computational cost. Final N, and which questions/contexts are included in each evaluation, must be made precise.
- Evaluation inconsistency: The reported average Spearman correlation for the full model is 0.64, but the information-agnostic model achieves 0.65, contradicting the earlier claim that the full model “outperformed” information-agnostic on prediction quality (beyond log-likelihood). This discrepancy needs to be reconciled.
- Mapping from natural language to programs is assumed; the process, inter-annotator reliability, coverage, and error analysis are not described. This is central to reproducibility and validity.
- EIG computation is domain-specific and expensive (leading to dropped contexts), with a hand-chosen uniform prior over hypotheses. The paper does not report runtimes, size of H per context, or scaling strategies. The likelihood p(d;x) is not explicitly defined (it should be ∑h p(d|h;x)p(h)).
- Hand-engineered features: The “relevance” indicator f∅ and answer-type features are powerful heuristics that conflate modeling and filtering. It is unclear whether the model would learn these preferences from EIG/complexity alone. The decision to coerce orientation to Boolean (Horizontal=True, Vertical=False) is ad hoc and semantically dubious.
- Complexity feature equals -log q(x) where q is a uniform PCFG proposal; thus the “complexity prior” depends on arbitrary grammar branching probabilities rather than a length or description-length measure. This potentially double counts syntactic idiosyncrasies and does not reflect human cognitive complexity.
- Novel question generation is evaluated only qualitatively; no human judgments or task-based utility evaluation are provided.
- Claims of generality vs. domain specificity: Many primitives and constructs are tailored to Battleship. The extent to which the system transfers is not tested.
- Writing/formatting issues: Several typos (“within in,” “pragmantics”), dangling footnote markers, inconsistent counts, and numerous malformed citations (missing years, authors, proceedings; e.g., “d, F. Strub...”, “st Conference...”). Figures/tables referenced (Table 1, Fig. 2–3, SI tables) are not present, hindering assessment. The bibliography is not in a standard NIPS style.

Quality
Conceptually sound and well-motivated, but the core inference procedure and evaluation have significant gaps: an apparent IS estimator error, lack of convergence/variance analysis, unclear data inclusion, and missing details on program annotation. The quantitative improvements are mixed (correlation vs. likelihood) and not robustly supported.

Clarity
Generally readable, but key methods are deferred to the supplement; numerous citation/formatting issues; critical mathematical definitions and experimental details are missing or inconsistent.

Originality
Moderately original combination of program-based semantics with active learning for question generation. Related to prior work on program interpretation of questions and EIG-based question selection, but applied here to generation with a grammar.

Significance
Promising direction, but limited by a small, domain-specific dataset, hand-crafted grammar/features, and incomplete evaluation. Impact on NeurIPS audience would be modest without stronger demonstrations and corrected methodology.

Pros
- Elegant program-as-question framework; compositional and computable.
- Incorporates EIG and a complexity prior; ablation shows complexity matters.
- Grammar enables productivity and some creative question generation.

Cons
- Potentially incorrect IS estimator; insufficient inference details and diagnostics.
- Inconsistent dataset handling; dropped rules/contexts; unclear N.
- Mapping from NL to programs undocumented.
- Mixed quantitative results; no human evaluation of generated questions.
- Heavy domain dependence; hand-tuned features (relevance, types).
- Numerous formatting and citation errors.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and clarify the IS estimator and gradient estimation; report ESS, variance, convergence, and sensitivity to proposal and seeds; consider adaptive sampling or SMC/MCMC over programs.
- Precisely specify included contexts/questions; report runtimes and hypothesis space sizes.
- Document the NL-to-program annotation protocol and provide inter-annotator agreement; release code and annotations.
- Replace -log q(x) with a more principled complexity measure (e.g., program length/description length), and analyze trade-offs with EIG.
- Provide stronger baselines (retrieval, neural seq2seq to programs, language-model priors), and human evaluations of generated questions’ usefulness/naturalness.
- Reconcile correlation vs likelihood results; add confidence intervals/bootstraps on correlations.
- Fix bibliography and typos; include all referenced figures/tables in the submission.

Recommendation: Reject (current form). The idea is interesting and relevant, but methodological issues, inconsistent reporting, limited evaluation, and presentation quality together warrant rejection at this time. I would be inclined to reconsider a substantially revised version. Confidence: high.
