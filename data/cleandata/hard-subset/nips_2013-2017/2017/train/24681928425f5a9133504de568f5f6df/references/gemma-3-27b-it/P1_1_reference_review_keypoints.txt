- Proposes modeling questions as executable programs that return answers when run on world states, framing question asking as program synthesis.
- Evaluated on a Battleship-like dataset; model predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions.
- Positions work relative to active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation; builds on compositionality, language-of-thought, and program synthesis literature.
- Novel and intriguing framework offering a new lens for studying human question asking and building intelligent systems.
- Strong theoretical foundation grounded in compositionality, computability, and Bayesian belief updating.
- Empirical validation shows promising results in predicting human behavior and generating plausible questions.
- Clear presentation with helpful examples illustrating the program representation of questions.
- Thorough and well-situated related work discussion.
- Domain specificity: grammar and feature engineering are heavily tailored to Battleship and exclude some question types.
- Computational complexity: importance sampling for normalization is expensive and may limit scalability.
- Reliance on hand-engineered features raises concerns about robustness and whether features could be learned automatically.
- Limited qualitative analysis; more in-depth human evaluation of question quality and relevance is needed.
- Small dataset (605 pairs) limits assessment of generalization to larger, more diverse settings.
- Technical approach is sound; importance sampling is a pragmatic solution to computational challenges.
- Clarity: paper is mostly clear but would benefit from simplified notation and more intuitive explanations of the energy function and feature weights.
- Originality: modeling questions as programs is a significant and novel contribution.
- Significance: potential to impact understanding of cognition and improve active learning, knowledge discovery, and human-computer interaction.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, promising empirical results, and well-written presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy domain specificity, computational limitations, and reliance on hand-engineered features.
- Overall assessment: strong, innovative paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions on notation, computational trade-offs, and discussion of generalization.
