

# Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that, when executed on possible world states, output answers. The authors formalize question asking as a program synthesis problem, with a grammar-based approach to capture compositionality and computability. They evaluate their model on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-style information-search game, using a probabilistic model that incorporates features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance.

**Strengths**

The paper makes a significant contribution by formalizing question asking as program generation, which captures the compositionality and computability of human questions in a way that previous work does not. The grammar design is elegant and effectively captures the structure of human questions in the domain. The evaluation is thorough, with quantitative results showing that the full model (with all features) outperforms variants that omit key components. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies (with Spearman's correlation of 0.64) and generates novel, human-like questions not seen in training. The authors provide clear examples of the model's creative outputs, demonstrating its ability to produce semantically meaningful questions that target critical information.

The work is well-motivated by the limitations of existing active learning and dialog systems, which either focus on simple label requests or produce stereotyped questions. The connection to cognitive science (via the "language of thought" framework) is well-articulated, and the paper makes a compelling case for the importance of compositionality in human question asking.

**Weaknesses**

The primary limitation is the narrow scope of the evaluation. The model is tested only on a single domain (Battleship game), which raises questions about generalizability. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't sufficiently address how to extend the approach to other domains without substantial re-engineering. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the domain-specific versus domain-general aspects of their grammar and features.

The connection to broader NIPS themes is somewhat weak. While the work has cognitive science implications, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how it advances machine learning or artificial intelligence beyond the specific context. A more explicit discussion of how this framework could be integrated with modern neural approaches (e.g., combining program synthesis with neural question generation) would strengthen the paper's significance.

The paper also doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches to question generation, such as those using transformers or sequence-to-sequence models. While the authors correctly note that these approaches require large datasets and don't capture intentional question asking, a brief comparison would help contextualize their contribution.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement**

1. Extend the evaluation to at least one additional domain (even if synthetic) to demonstrate the framework's flexibility. The supplementary materials could include examples of how the grammar would need to be modified for a different domain.

2. Include a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the proposed approach and recent neural question generation methods. How might program synthesis be combined with neural architectures to leverage the strengths of both?

3. Provide a clearer articulation of the paper's contribution to the broader machine learning community. How might this framework be used to improve active learning systems, or to enable more human-like interaction in AI systems?

4. The paper mentions that the model operates on semantic representations rather than natural language directly. It would be valuable to discuss the challenges of bridging this gap and how recent advances in natural language generation might help address this.

5. The computational complexity of the model isn't fully discussed. For practical deployment, it would be helpful to know how the model scales with question complexity and the size of the hypothesis space.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes a novel contribution to modeling human question asking. The core idea of representing questions as programs is elegant and well-executed, and the evaluation demonstrates the model's ability to capture human question patterns and generate novel questions. The main limitation is the narrow domain scope, which prevents a full assessment of the framework's broader impact.

While the paper is interesting and contributes to cognitive science, its significance to the broader machine learning community is somewhat limited by the domain-specific evaluation. With additional evaluation on a second domain and clearer articulation of the broader implications, this work could be a strong candidate for NIPS.

**Decision: Weak Accept**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding human question asking, but the narrow evaluation scope and limited connection to broader ML themes prevent a stronger recommendation. With the suggested improvements, particularly expanding the evaluation to additional domains, this would be an excellent paper for NIPS.
