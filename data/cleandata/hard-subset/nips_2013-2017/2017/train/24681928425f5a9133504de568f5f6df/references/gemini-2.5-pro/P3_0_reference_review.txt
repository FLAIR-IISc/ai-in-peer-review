**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. The problem of deciding which question to ask in a given context is then framed as probabilistic program synthesis. The authors propose a log-linear model to score candidate programs (questions) based on a combination of features, including their expected information gain (EIG), complexity, and the type of answer they produce. The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game. The results demonstrate that the model can successfully predict the frequency distribution of human questions in held-out contexts and can also generate novel, contextually relevant questions that were not observed in the training data. This work provides a significant conceptual advance over typical active learning approaches, which are limited to simple label queries, and data-driven dialogue systems, which often lack goal-directed reasoning.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—framing intelligent question asking as probabilistic program generation—is highly original and significant. It bridges cognitive science theories of compositional thought with the formalisms of program synthesis and the goals of active learning. This provides a powerful new lens through which to view and potentially build more sophisticated AI agents that can actively seek information in a human-like way. This is a substantial departure from and improvement upon the state-of-the-art in both active learning and goal-directed dialogue.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed model is technically sound and elegant. The use of a context-free grammar to define the space of questions, combined with a log-linear model to score them, is a principled approach to balancing multiple competing objectives (informativeness, simplicity). The features chosen are well-motivated: EIG captures the goal-directed nature of the task, while the complexity penalty reflects a well-known cognitive bias towards simplicity. The use of importance sampling to handle the intractable partition function is a standard and appropriate technique for this class of model.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is strong and provides compelling support for the authors' claims. The leave-one-out cross-validation setup is appropriate. The ablation study (lesioning models) is particularly effective, clearly demonstrating that informativeness, complexity, and answer-type preference are all necessary components for explaining the human data. The finding that the complexity-agnostic model performs extremely poorly is a key result, highlighting that humans do not simply maximize EIG. Furthermore, the qualitative analysis of newly generated questions convincingly showcases the model's generative and creative capacity.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the methodology is explained step-by-step, and the results are presented logically. The authors do an excellent job of connecting their computational framework to broader ideas in linguistics and cognitive science (e.g., compositionality, language of thought), making the work accessible and impactful for a wide audience.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Reliance on a Hand-Crafted Grammar and Semantic Representation:** The most significant limitation is that the model operates not on natural language, but on hand-translated programs. The grammar itself is domain-specific and presumably required considerable expert engineering to cover all questions in the dataset. The paper acknowledges this, but it tempers the immediate applicability of the method as a general-purpose ML tool. For this to become a truly scalable active learning system, it would need to be coupled with a semantic parser that can map natural language to programs. A discussion of the challenges and potential paths toward building such an end-to-end system would strengthen the paper's outlook. How was the grammar developed? Was it an iterative process of covering the observed questions, or were there a priori principles?

2.  **Scalability of Inference:** The inference procedure relies on sampling a large number of programs (150,000) to approximate the gradient. While feasible for the 6x6 Battleship domain, the space of programs and the cost of evaluating EIG for each one could grow exponentially in more complex domains. The authors note they had to exclude two contexts due to large hypothesis spaces. This suggests potential scalability issues. It would be beneficial to discuss the computational complexity of the approach and potential avenues for more efficient inference, such as structured search over the program space instead of simple sampling.

3.  **Generality of Features:** While the current features work well, the informativeness feature (EIG) relies on a full Bayesian belief over a discrete and enumerable hypothesis space. In many real-world active learning problems, the hypothesis space is continuous or too large to enumerate. The authors could briefly comment on how their framework might extend to such scenarios, perhaps by using variational approximations of information gain or other utility functions that are more tractable in complex settings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Proposes a genuinely new and powerful paradigm for active learning and question asking.
*   **Significance:** Addresses a fundamental limitation of current AI systems and takes a concrete step toward more human-like learning agents. The work has high potential to inspire future research at the intersection of ML, NLP, and cognitive science.
*   **Soundness:** The methodology is principled and the empirical results are strong and convincing.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic clear and accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Practicality:** The reliance on a hand-engineered semantic grammar and representation limits its immediate, off-the-shelf applicability to new domains.
*   **Scalability:** The current inference method may not scale to more complex problems, a point that could be discussed more deeply.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper presenting a highly innovative and thought-provoking approach to a fundamental problem in AI. The strengths—in particular, its originality, technical quality, and the strength of its empirical evidence—far outweigh the weaknesses, which primarily relate to the scope and future scalability of the work. This paper is a perfect fit for NIPS and is likely to have a significant impact on the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
