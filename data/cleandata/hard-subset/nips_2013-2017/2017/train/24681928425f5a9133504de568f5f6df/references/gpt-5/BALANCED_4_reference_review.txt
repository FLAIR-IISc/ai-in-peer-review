Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a computational framework for open-ended, goal-directed question asking by modeling questions as executable programs over possible world states. A context-free grammar (with lambda abstractions, mapping, and set operators) defines a compositional space of question-programs; a log-linear model over features then assigns probabilities to questions given a context. Key features include expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian hypothesis space over Battleship boards, a complexity penalty derived from the grammar prior, answer-type preferences, and a relevance filter. Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human-produced questions via importance sampling over programs. Evaluation uses a dataset of 605 human questions across 18 partially revealed Battleship board contexts, with leave-one-context-out testing. The model predicts held-out question distributions better than lesioned variants and can sample novel, human-like questions.

The work connects to active learning (EIG-based querying), semantic parsing and program execution for question answering, program induction in cognitive science, and generative question asking (e.g., VQG) but differs by targeting intentional, compositional question generation with explicit utility and complexity trade-offs. The framing resonates with prior NeurIPS work on probabilistic program induction and visual reasoning via executable programs, while focusing on question asking rather than answering.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formalization: representing questions as programs enables compositionality, executability, and a unified way to compute informativeness.
- Interpretable probabilistic model: log-linear features (EIG, complexity, type) align with cognitive intuitions and yield analyzable contributions via ablations.
- Empirical evidence of productivity: the grammar explains human-like generation beyond memorization of surface forms.
- Quantitative evaluation: leave-one-context-out likelihood and rank correlations on human frequencies; bootstrapped comparisons of lesioned models.
- Demonstration of novel question generation with semantic equivalence checks, not just rephrasing.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Limited domain and engineered grammar: substantial Battleship-specific primitives and a hand-crafted relevance filter. Generalization beyond this domain is untested.
- Small dataset (605 questions; 18 contexts) with exclusions (ambiguous questions; removal of the draw rule and dropping two contexts), risking bias and overfitting to domain idiosyncrasies.
- Evaluation metrics are thin: correlations and approximate likelihoods with importance sampling; no human evaluation of the quality/usefulness of generated questions, and no task-level utility (e.g., downstream learning speed).
- EIG’s role is unclear: average correlation with human frequencies is slightly higher for the information-agnostic model than the full model, suggesting the informativeness feature is not the main driver in practice.
- Approximation concerns: reliance on a fixed proposal grammar for both sampling and defining a complexity feature (-log q) raises potential circularity; stability across random seeds and proposal choices is not reported.
- Annotation details are sparse: how natural-language questions were mapped to programs, inter-annotator agreement, and handling of paraphrases/equivalences are not fully described.

Clarity
- Overall well-written, but key implementation details and grammar tables are deferred to the supplement; several references lack years or complete bibliographic information.
- Computational costs and runtimes for EIG over large hypothesis spaces are not reported; two contexts were dropped due to tractability.

Originality
- Modeling question asking as program synthesis with a probabilistic, utility-aware prior is novel and timely. However, many components (EIG, grammar priors, log-linear energies) are standard; the novelty lies in their integration for question generation.

Significance
- Conceptually interesting and potentially influential for human-like active learning and interactive systems. Practical significance is limited by the narrow domain, small data, and lack of end-to-end natural language or broader benchmarks.

Reasons to accept
- Elegant, compositional formalism with interpretable probabilistic modeling.
- Demonstrates productivity and some predictive power on human data.
- Bridges active learning and program induction with a clear cognitive motivation.

Reasons to reject
- Domain- and grammar-specific with limited evidence of generalization.
- Modest, approximate evaluation without human judgment or task-level gains.
- Mixed evidence for the central role of informativeness vs. complexity priors.
- Small, curated dataset and missing methodological details reduce confidence.

Suggestions for improvement
- Extend beyond Battleship: instantiate a domain-general core and show adaptation to at least one additional, structurally different domain.
- Provide a semantic parsing component (even weakly supervised) and report end-to-end results from natural language to executed programs.
- Add human evaluation of generated questions (usefulness, clarity, answerability) and measure downstream learning performance when agents use model-generated questions.
- Strengthen evaluation metrics: top-k accuracy, calibration, diversity, and ablations on grammar size; report sensitivity to the proposal distribution and random seeds.
- Compare against stronger baselines, including neural question generators adapted to the task (with or without explicit EIG), and rational models of question asking.
- Clarify annotation procedures, inter-annotator reliability, and equivalence criteria; include full grammar and code.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject
The paper presents a thoughtful and potentially impactful framework, but the current empirical scope and evaluation are too limited for NeurIPS. With broader domains, stronger end-to-end and human evaluations, and clearer evidence that the model’s informativeness computations drive behavior beyond complexity/type priors, this line of work could merit acceptance in a future iteration.
