- Proposes modeling human question asking as program generation: questions are syntactic programs executed on possible worlds to yield answers.  
- Presents a probabilistic generative model that balances informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance to predict question selection.  
- Uses a grammar-based, lambda-calculus-inspired program representation to capture compositional question structure.  
- Evaluated on 605 human questions from a Battleship-like game; model predicts human question frequencies and produces novel, human-like questions.  
- Contrasts with neural question-generation and program-synthesis-for-answering approaches by focusing on question generation without large paired datasets.  
- Paper is technically sound with clear mathematical formulation, feature design, and reproducible details (grammar, features).  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough: quantitative metrics (log-likelihood, Spearman correlation), qualitative analysis, and ablation studies.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations, notably domain-specific grammar and removed contexts for computational reasons, which could bias the grammar.  
- Exceptionally well-written and organized; technical sections and figures effectively communicate concepts and examples.  
- Original contribution: first to model human question asking as program generation and to predict human question frequencies with this framework.  
- Demonstrates the model’s ability to generate novel questions beyond the training set, highlighting creativity and generalization.  
- Significant implications for active learning, human-computer interaction, and computational cognitive science despite current domain constraints.  
- Strengths: novel theoretical framework, strong experiments, novel question generation, clear ablations, cognitive-science tie-ins, and reproducibility.  
- Weaknesses: limited immediate generalizability beyond Battleship, natural language interface not fully developed, scalability to complex domains unexplored, and grammar requires domain re-engineering.  
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, original, well-executed work with meaningful implications and a clear path for future extension.
