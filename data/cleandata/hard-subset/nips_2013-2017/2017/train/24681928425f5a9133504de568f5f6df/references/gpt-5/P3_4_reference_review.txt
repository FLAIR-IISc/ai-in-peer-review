Summary
This paper proposes a computational framework for human question asking in an active learning setting by modeling questions as executable programs in a small, typed, LISP-like DSL. A probabilistic context-free grammar (with lambda abstraction, map, set operations) defines the space of questions; a log-linear model over programs assigns probabilities using features for expected information gain (EIG), complexity (via −log prior under the grammar), answer type, and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit by approximate maximum likelihood using importance sampling over programs drawn from the grammar. Evaluation uses a dataset of 605 natural language questions (mapped to programs) posed by humans in a Battleship-like information search task across 18 contexts. The model predicts question distributions under leave-one-context-out, achieves moderate correlations between predicted energies and human frequencies, and can generate novel human-like questions.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects several strands: partition semantics and information-theoretic accounts of questions in pragmatics; program induction and “language of thought” approaches in cognitive science; semantic parsing and neuro-symbolic QA that execute programs; and active learning based on EIG. Compared to dialog and visual question generation work that relies on large paired corpora, this approach aims to produce goal-directed questions in novel contexts by reasoning over a structured hypothesis space. The closest antecedents are (i) formal semantics work modeling questions as partitions and (ii) cognitive models of concept learning as program induction; the novelty here is applying these ideas to question generation and fitting a probabilistic model to human data.

Strengths
- Clear, compelling conceptual framing: questions-as-programs elegantly captures compositionality, answer types, and executability.
- A concrete, executable DSL and grammar that covers the dataset and supports nontrivial compositional constructs (lambda, map, sets).
- Principled utility feature via EIG over a Bayesian hypothesis space, enabling context-sensitive predictions.
- Learning a distribution over programs (not just ranking by EIG) to account for human preferences (complexity, answer-type priors).
- Empirical grounding in a dataset of genuine human-generated questions; mapping to program semantics reduces paraphrase noise.
- Lesion studies suggest multiple ingredients (informativeness, complexity, type) matter; generative samples are plausible and insightful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evaluation scope and robustness: Dataset is small (605 questions; many singletons), domain-specific, and two contexts plus 14 questions removed for computational reasons. The main quantitative metric (Spearman correlation) is moderate on average (0.64) and, by authors’ own report, the information-agnostic model attains a similar correlation (0.65), weakening claims that EIG improves rank-order predictions. The likelihood-based comparison is approximate (both gradient and Z estimated by importance sampling), and effect sizes vs. alternatives are not reported in absolute terms.
- Complexity feature depends on the grammar prior: Using −log q(x) where q is a uniform PCFG is a strong inductive bias tied to the specific grammar design. This risks circularity: the same proposal distribution drives both sampling and the learned penalty. Simpler, grammar-agnostic measures (token length, AST depth) or learned rule probabilities would be informative controls.
- Inference approximations: The model relies on a fixed (or unspecified if resampled) set of 150k proposals to approximate expectations and Z, potentially missing high-utility but low-probability programs. Variance of the estimators and sensitivity to sample size are not reported. The normalizer approximation is reused for model comparison, which can be unstable as θ changes.
- Utility model limitations: EIG is computed over a hand-specified hypothesis space with strong domain knowledge and assumes literal, costless answers. Many pragmatic factors (answerer effort, cognitive load beyond length, preference for Boolean forms) are handled via ad hoc features rather than a unified utility or a pragmatic reasoning model (e.g., RSA for questions). The need for a relevance “filter” feature indicates the grammar generates many ill-posed programs; learning to avoid such programs structurally might be preferable.
- Generalization: While some grammar components are domain-general, the evaluation is restricted to a single task. It remains unclear how much engineering is required to port the system to new domains or scales (e.g., larger boards, continuous attributes).
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to simple but strong heuristics, such as EIG plus length penalty, or uniform-over-type priors, would help isolate the contribution of the grammar-based complexity. No comparison to neural question generation tailored to the domain (even if out-of-the-box systems are not goal-directed).
- Reporting clarity: Key details are relegated to SI (grammar rules, hypothesis-space size, EIG computation specifics). References contain several incomplete citations.

Quality
Technically sound at a conceptual level with executable semantics and coherent probabilistic modeling. However, the experimental evidence is limited in scope, and the approximations (importance sampling of a vast program space; approximate Z) are not thoroughly stress-tested. The lesion study supports the role of complexity and type, but quantitative gains from informativeness are mixed in the rank-correlation metric.

Clarity
Well written and organized. The exposition of the DSL and the EIG-based utility is clear. Reproducibility would benefit from including the full grammar, hypothesis enumeration details, and inference stability analyses in the main text. Some citations are malformed.

Originality
The formulation of question asking as program generation and the learned distribution over questions combining EIG and complexity is novel within ML venues. It extends ideas from formal semantics and cognitive science into a generative, fitted model for human question asking.

Significance
Conceptually significant for bridging active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive models of inquiry. Practical impact is limited by domain specificity, small-scale evaluation, and computational constraints; nevertheless, it opens a promising direction for goal-directed question generation.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Report absolute likelihoods and confidence intervals for model comparisons; clarify whether the proposal set is resampled each gradient step and analyze sensitivity to the number of proposals.
- Add baselines: EIG + length; length-only; grammar-length (token count) vs. −log q; and learned PCFG rule probabilities.
- Analyze ablations on the relevance filter and EIG=0 indicator; how many sampled programs are irrelevant?
- Provide per-context correlations and explore why informativeness does not correlate with human frequencies in this task (as noted).
- Consider a unified utility (e.g., RSA with answerer cost) instead of separate type/complexity features.
- Demonstrate transfer to a second domain, even in a small pilot.
- Fix and complete references; include the full grammar in the paper or an appendix.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a thoughtful and original framework with solid conceptual contributions and encouraging, if limited, empirical results. With stronger experimental validation and clearer analysis of approximations and baselines, this could be a notable contribution.

Confidence
High (expert in program induction, probabilistic modeling, and active learning).
