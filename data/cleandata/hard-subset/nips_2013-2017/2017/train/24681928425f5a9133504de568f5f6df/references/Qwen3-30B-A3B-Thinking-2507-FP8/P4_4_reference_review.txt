

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs that operate on possible world states. The authors model questions as syntactically well-formed programs, recasting question asking as program synthesis. They develop a probabilistic generative model that predicts human question frequencies in a Battleship-like information-search game, using features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance. The model successfully predicts question frequencies in held-out contexts and generates novel human-like questions not present in the training data. The work builds on cognitive science concepts of compositionality and computability, while bridging to active learning and program synthesis approaches in machine learning.

The paper clearly differentiates from related work: unlike traditional active learning (focused on label queries), goal-directed dialog systems (using canned questions), or neural VQG approaches (requiring large paired datasets), this work models question asking as program generation that captures human-like creativity and context sensitivity without requiring extensive training data.

## Strengths
**Technical Soundness and Quality**: The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound approach. The probabilistic model with carefully designed features (informativeness, complexity, etc.) is theoretically grounded in cognitive science and machine learning. The evaluation methodology is rigorous, using leave-one-out cross-validation and comparing against meaningful model variants (information-agnostic, complexity-agnostic, type-agnostic). The model achieves strong predictive performance (Spearman's correlation of 0.64) and successfully generates novel human-like questions. The implementation details, including grammar design and optimization procedures, are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

**Originality**: The core insight of modeling questions as executable programs is novel in the context of question asking research. While previous work has modeled question meaning as partitions over possible worlds [6, 9] or used programs for question answering [24, 12], this paper uniquely applies program synthesis to generate questions, capturing human compositionality and creativity without requiring large datasets. The approach provides the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation, methodology, and evaluation are clearly presented with appropriate technical detail. The explanation of how questions map to programs (e.g., "(size Blue)" representing "How long is the blue ship?") is particularly effective. The supplementary materials (mentioned in the paper) likely provide additional detail for reproducibility.

**Significance**: The work addresses a fundamental aspect of human cognition that has been largely overlooked in machine learning: the ability to ask rich, context-sensitive questions. The framework could significantly advance active learning systems, cognitive modeling, and human-AI interaction. The ability to generate novel human-like questions without large datasets represents a meaningful step toward more human-like AI question asking.

## Weaknesses
The paper's main limitation is its narrow scope to a specific game domain (Battleship). While the authors acknowledge this and discuss potential domain generalization, the model's applicability to broader question-asking scenarios remains largely untested. The paper also does not sufficiently address how the model would scale to more complex domains with richer semantics.

The evaluation relies on a single dataset with limited context diversity (only 18 contexts per participant), which may limit generalizability. While the authors address the sparsity of human question frequencies, the correlation-based evaluation metric (Spearman's rank correlation) has limitations in sparse data scenarios.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Novel framework modeling questions as executable programs with strong theoretical grounding
- First predictive model of open-ended human question asking
- Demonstrated ability to generate novel human-like questions
- Rigorous evaluation with clear comparison to meaningful model variants
- Strong technical soundness with well-designed features and methodology
- Clear connections to cognitive science and active learning literature

## Arguments against Acceptance
- Limited scope to a single game domain with potential scaling challenges
- Evaluation based on a single dataset with limited context diversity
- The model still requires substantial domain-specific grammar engineering

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding human question asking through a novel computational lens. The approach is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear advances over existing work. While the domain-specific nature of the model is a limitation, the framework provides a strong foundation that could be extended to other domains. The paper's novelty, technical quality, and potential impact on active learning and cognitive modeling make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The work represents a meaningful step toward enabling machines to ask rich, human-like questions.
