This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling question asking, framing questions as programs that, when executed, yield answers. The core idea is to leverage compositionality and computability to understand how humans formulate questions, particularly in a game context (Battleship). The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of human questions from this game, demonstrating its ability to predict question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

This work builds upon active learning literature [21], which traditionally focuses on simple label requests, by considering richer, more informative queries. It also connects to work in dialog systems [25, 1, 20], but argues these systems lack the flexibility and creativity of human question asking.  Relatedly, the paper acknowledges VQG [16] and visual question answering [22], but differentiates their work by focusing on goal-directed question asking in novel scenarios without extensive pre-training. The paper appropriately positions itself within these existing bodies of work, highlighting its unique contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The framing of question asking as program generation is a significant and original contribution. It offers a computationally tractable way to model the semantic structure and informativeness of questions.
* **Technical Soundness:**  The probabilistic model, employing a grammar and feature engineering, is reasonably well-defined and the optimization process is described with sufficient detail. The use of importance sampling to address the intractable normalization constant is a practical approach.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments on the Battleship dataset demonstrate the model’s predictive power and ability to generate plausible questions. The comparison against ablated models (removing key features) provides valuable insights into the importance of different components.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the work, and the different components of the model are explained systematically. 
* **Dataset:** Utilizing and further analyzing the existing dataset [19] is a good approach. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Grammar Complexity:** While the grammar is described, its full complexity (especially with lambda expressions and set operators) isn’t immediately apparent and may require careful examination of the supplementary material. The justification for specific grammatical choices could be stronger.
* **Feature Engineering:**  The choice of features (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance) feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic exploration of feature spaces or a learned feature representation might be beneficial. The Bayesian ideal observer approach to informativeness, while sensible, might introduce strong assumptions about human rationality.
* **Domain Specificity:** The evaluation is limited to the Battleship game, raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it’s a significant caveat.
* **Qualitative Evaluation:** The qualitative assessment of generated questions relies on subjective judgment. A more rigorous evaluation, potentially involving human evaluation of question quality or utility, would be desirable.
* **Limited Baselines:** The comparison with ablated models is helpful, but it would be stronger to include comparisons with alternative question generation methods (e.g., sequence-to-sequence models).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to modeling question asking, with the potential to significantly advance our understanding of human cognition and improve active learning algorithms.
* The technical details are sound and the empirical evaluation provides convincing evidence of the model’s effectiveness in the chosen domain.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The domain specificity of the evaluation raises concerns about generalizability.
* The feature engineering process feels somewhat arbitrary.
* A stronger comparison with existing question generation methods is needed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that proposes a promising new framework for modeling question asking. While the domain specificity and feature engineering choices are limitations, the novelty and technical soundness of the work outweigh these concerns. I believe the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and warrants acceptance.
