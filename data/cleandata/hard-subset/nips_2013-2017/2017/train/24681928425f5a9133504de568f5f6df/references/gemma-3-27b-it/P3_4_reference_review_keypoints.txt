- Proposes a novel framework that models human question asking as program generation where questions are executable programs over world states.  
- Evaluates the model on a Battleship-like human question dataset, predicting question frequencies and synthesizing human-like questions.  
- Positions the work relative to active learning, dialog systems, and VQG, emphasizing goal-directed question generation and compositionality.  
- Connects to linguistic and cognitive theories (compositionality, partition semantics, language of thought) for theoretical grounding.  
- Novel and compelling conceptual contribution: reframing question asking as program synthesis.  
- Strong cognitive-science grounding that motivates the modeling choices.  
- Empirical validation shows the model captures aspects of human question behavior and can generate novel questions.  
- Provides a clear, detailed probabilistic model specification (grammar, features, optimization).  
- Includes careful analyses and ablation studies demonstrating the importance of informativeness, complexity, and answer type.  
- Well-written and logically organized presentation.  
- Grammar is domain-specific and hand-crafted for Battleship, limiting generalizability to other domains.  
- Computationally expensive importance sampling (e.g., sampling ~150k questions) raises scalability concerns.  
- Informativeness metric (EIG) assumes a rational Bayesian agent with uniform prior, which may not reflect human biases and heuristics.  
- Limited qualitative analysis of generated questions; more systematic examples and failure cases needed.  
- Small dataset (605 pairs) limits evidence for generalizability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, solid empirical results, and strong theoretical grounding.  
- Arguments against acceptance: domain specificity, computational cost, and potentially unrealistic Bayesian assumptions.  
- Overall assessment: strong, innovative paper whose strengths outweigh weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: explicitly discuss grammar generalization, add detailed qualitative/failure analyses, explore alternative normalizing-constant approximations, and acknowledge/consider more realistic cognitive biases in the informativeness metric.
