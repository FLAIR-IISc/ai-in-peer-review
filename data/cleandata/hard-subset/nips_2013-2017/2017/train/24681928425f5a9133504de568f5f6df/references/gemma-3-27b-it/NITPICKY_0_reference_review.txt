This paper proposes a framework for modeling human question asking as program generation, aiming to bridge the gap between simple active learning queries and the rich questions humans pose. The core idea – representing questions as programs executable on possible world states – is intriguing and offers a novel perspective. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions from a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequencies and synthesize novel, human-like questions.

While the concept is promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from minor stylistic inconsistencies to more substantial methodological concerns. The writing, while generally clear, is often verbose and repetitive. For example, the introduction reiterates the core idea multiple times without adding substantial nuance. The numerous cross-references (e.g., "[cf. 8]") feel unnecessary and disrupt the flow.

A significant weakness lies in the level of detail provided regarding the experimental setup. The description of the “importance sampling” optimization technique is superficial; crucial parameters and implementation details are lacking. The justification for the chosen sample size (150,000 questions) is unclear. Why is this number sufficient? What is the variance in gradient estimates with this sample size? Furthermore, the claim of 100,000 iterations of gradient ascent is followed by a statement about bootstrapping the log-likelihood. This suggests a possible misunderstanding of statistical inference – bootstrapping is for *estimating* variance, not for optimization itself.

The feature engineering aspect, while explored, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The explanation of the "relevance" feature (f∅(x)) is confusing, and its necessity is not fully justified. It’s unclear why questions referencing the game board would *not* automatically be considered relevant. More importantly, the connection between the features and the observed human behavior remains largely speculative. The correlation coefficients reported (average of 0.64) are presented without confidence intervals or baseline comparisons. A correlation of 0.64 isn’t particularly strong and could arise from chance, especially given the dataset's sparseness.

The grammar described in Section 4.2, while presented as a key component, feels unnecessarily complex and potentially restrictive. The use of lambda expressions and set operators, while adding expressiveness, might also introduce unnecessary computational overhead and make the program synthesis problem more difficult. The justification for the specific grammar rules is weak; why these rules and not others? The exclusion of questions involving tile drawing (“L → (draw C)”) due to computational complexity is a significant limitation, suggesting a potential bottleneck in scalability.

The novelty of the generated questions in Figure 3 is debatable. Some appear trivially rephrased versions of existing questions. The qualitative assessment relies heavily on the authors' subjective judgment. A more rigorous evaluation would involve human evaluation of the generated questions' intelligibility, relevance, and “human-likeness.”

The related work section is adequate but could be more comprehensive. The discussion of visual question generation (VQG) briefly mentions the data requirements but doesn't fully address the differences in task setup. VQG typically involves recognizing objects in images, a task fundamentally different from reasoning about hidden information in a game.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of question asking as program generation.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict human question frequencies.
*   Successful synthesis of some novel, human-like questions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous methodological details are lacking or poorly explained.
*   Feature engineering appears ad-hoc and lacks strong justification.
*   Evaluation is weak and lacks statistical rigor.
*   The grammar is potentially overly complex and has limitations.
*   Writing is verbose and repetitive.
*   The significance of the improvement over a simpler baseline is not convincingly demonstrated.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially innovative idea, the paper’s numerous shortcomings outweigh its strengths. The lack of methodological rigor, weak evaluation, and unclear justification for design choices raise significant concerns. I recommend **rejection**. The paper presents a promising direction but requires substantial revisions and a more thorough and rigorous treatment of its methodological and experimental aspects before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. A revised version should address the concerns outlined above, providing more detailed explanations, stronger statistical evidence, and a more compelling argument for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
