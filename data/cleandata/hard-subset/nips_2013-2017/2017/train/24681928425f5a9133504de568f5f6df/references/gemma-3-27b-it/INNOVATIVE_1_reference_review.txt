This paper presents a fascinating and bold idea: framing question asking as program generation. The core insight – that questions can be modeled as programs executed on a world state – is genuinely novel and offers a compelling perspective on how humans formulate queries. The authors successfully demonstrate that this formalism can capture aspects of human question asking in a “Battleship”-like game, predicting question frequencies and even synthesizing novel, human-like questions. This is a significant step towards more intelligent and flexible active learning systems, moving beyond simple label requests toward richer, more targeted information gathering.

The paper's strength lies decisively in its originality and potential impact. The program synthesis framework is a clever and potentially powerful way to approach the problem of question asking, sidestepping the limitations of existing approaches focused on canned responses or purely data-driven generation. The demonstration that the model can *generate* questions, not just predict existing ones, is particularly impressive.  The choice of the Battleship game as a testbed is also astute – it provides a constrained environment with clear semantics, allowing for tractable modeling and evaluation.

However, the work isn't without its weaknesses. While the authors demonstrate the feasibility of their approach, the validation remains relatively limited. The quantitative results (log-likelihood comparisons) are a good start, but a more thorough evaluation of the *quality* of the generated questions is needed. Beyond simply matching human frequencies, do the generated questions lead to efficient strategy in the game? Are they genuinely insightful, or just superficially plausible?  The reliance on a specific grammar, while necessary for tractability, introduces a degree of arbitrariness.  It’s not entirely clear how robust the approach is to variations in the grammar or the underlying domain.  The description of the importance sampling optimization is brief, and a more detailed explanation of its computational cost would be beneficial. Furthermore, the ablation studies, while present, could be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of the individual feature contributions beyond simply reporting the log-likelihood.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High originality:** The program synthesis approach to question asking is a genuinely new idea.
*   **Significant potential:** The framework could lead to more intelligent active learning systems and a better understanding of human cognition.
*   **Demonstrated feasibility:** The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel questions.
*   **Well-chosen testbed:** The Battleship game provides a suitable environment for evaluating the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited validation:**  Evaluation focuses on prediction accuracy rather than the strategic value of the generated questions.
*   **Grammar dependency:** The reliance on a specific grammar introduces a degree of arbitrariness.
*   **Computational complexity:** The optimization procedure isn't fully detailed, and scalability is a concern.
*   **Incomplete ablation study:** More granular analysis of feature contributions is needed.



Despite these weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of this work are substantial. The paper presents a paradigm shift in how we think about question asking, and the authors have taken a significant first step towards realizing this vision. The methodological aspects, while not perfect, are secondary to the core idea, which is exciting and deserves further exploration. I believe the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend **acceptance**. The paper is a valuable contribution to the field and is likely to stimulate further research in this area.
