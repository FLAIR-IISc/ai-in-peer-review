- Overall assessment: Novel, compelling framework treating question asking as program generation; paper merits acceptance.  
- Core idea: Questions are modeled as programs executed on world states, enabling richer representations than traditional active learning.  
- Method: Probabilistic model using a context-free grammar learned from a Battleship-like human question dataset.  
- Results: Model predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, plausible questions.  
- Novelty & originality: Fresh perspective moving beyond simple label queries; significant departure from existing work.  
- Theoretical foundation: Grounded in linguistics and cognitive science (compositionality, program induction) with links to formal semantics.  
- Empirical validation: Quantitative and qualitative results support the model’s ability to capture human question asking.  
- Writing & organization: Clear, well-structured paper with helpful supplementary material.  
- Domain specificity: Evaluation limited to a Battleship-like game, raising concerns about generalizability.  
- Model complexity & scalability: Importance sampling and optimization are computationally expensive; question length and types were restricted.  
- Feature engineering: Relies on manual features (e.g., EIG); end-to-end or deep learning approaches could be explored.  
- Baseline comparisons: Ablations present but stronger comparisons (e.g., pre-trained transformers) are lacking.  
- Review scores: High marks for quality (8/10), clarity (9/10), originality (9/10), and significance (8/10).  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, theoretically strong, empirically promising, and well-written.  
- Rejection concerns: Limited domain, scalability issues, and insufficiently strong baselines.  
- Final recommendation: Accept.
