- Proposes modeling human question-asking as executable programs in a typed LISP-like DSL with a PCFG and a log-linear model using features like EIG, complexity, and answer type.
- Trains parameters by approximate maximum likelihood with importance sampling over program proposals and evaluates on 605 human questions from a Battleship-like task across 18 contexts.
- Connects to partition semantics, program induction/"language of thought", semantic parsing, and active learning, with novelty in fitting a probabilistic program-based question generator to human data.
- Strength: elegant questions-as-programs framing that captures compositionality, answer types, and executability.
- Strength: concrete DSL/grammar supporting nontrivial constructs (lambda, map, sets) that covers the dataset.
- Strength: principled EIG utility over a Bayesian hypothesis space enabling context-sensitive predictions.
- Strength: learns a distribution over programs (not just EIG ranking), accounting for human preferences like complexity and type.
- Strength: empirical grounding in human-generated questions and lesion studies that show multiple factors matter; generative samples are plausible.
- Weakness: small, domain-specific dataset with many singleton questions and removed contexts, limiting evaluation scope and robustness.
- Weakness: main rank-correlation metric is moderate and similar for an information-agnostic model, weakening claims about EIG's predictive power.
- Weakness: complexity feature uses −log prior under a uniform PCFG, creating a strong grammar-dependent inductive bias and potential circularity.
- Weakness: inference relies on a fixed set of 150k proposals and importance sampling for Z, with no reported estimator variance or sensitivity analyses.
- Weakness: EIG computed over a hand-designed hypothesis space assuming literal, costless answers; pragmatic factors are only handled by ad hoc features.
- Weakness: grammar generates many irrelevant programs requiring a relevance filter; structural learning to avoid such programs would be preferable.
- Weakness: limited generalization evidence—unclear how much engineering is needed to port to other domains or scale up.
- Weakness: missing comparisons to simple baselines (e.g., EIG+length) and to neural question-generation methods.
- Reporting: key technical details and some citations are relegated to the SI or malformed; reproducibility would benefit from including the full grammar and inference analyses.
- Quality: conceptually sound and executable but experimental evidence and approximation analyses are limited.
- Originality: novel application of program-based question generation and learning a distribution combining EIG and complexity in ML venues.
- Significance: conceptually important for bridging active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive models, but practical impact limited by scope and computational constraints.
- Suggestions: report absolute likelihoods and CIs, analyze proposal-sample sensitivity, add simple baselines and grammar-agnostic complexity measures, ablate relevance/EIG features, consider unified pragmatic utility (e.g., RSA), demonstrate small-domain transfer, and fix references/include full grammar.
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on stronger experimental validation and clearer analysis of approximations and baselines.
- Confidence: High (reviewer is expert in program induction, probabilistic modeling, and active learning).
