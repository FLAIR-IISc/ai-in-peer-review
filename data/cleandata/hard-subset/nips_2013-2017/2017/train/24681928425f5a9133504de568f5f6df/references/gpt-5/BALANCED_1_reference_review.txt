Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a computational framework in which questions are represented as executable programs over possible world states. A probabilistic grammar (LISP/lambda-calculus–style) defines a compositional hypothesis space of question-programs. A log-linear model over this space trades off expected informativeness (expected information gain under a Bayesian ideal observer), complexity (measured by −log probability under the PCFG), and answer-type priors, with an auxiliary “relevance” feature that filters non-referential programs. Parameters are learned to maximize the likelihood of human questions collected in an information-search “Battleship” game [Rothe et al. 2016], and inference uses importance sampling over the grammar. The model is evaluated by (i) predicting question distributions in held-out contexts and (ii) sampling novel questions deemed “human-like.”

The work connects to: active learning (label/query strategies; Settles 2010), question semantics as partitions over worlds (Groenendijk & Stokhof), pragmatic question choice (Hawkins et al. 2015), program induction/language of thought–style compositionality (Goodman et al. 2008; Piantadosi et al. 2012; Lake et al. 2015), and program-based QA/semantic parsing (Wang et al. 2015; Johnson et al. 2017). The novelty lies in modeling the generation of rich, goal-directed questions (not just answering), with a compositional, computable representation coupled to an information-theoretic utility.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality. Technically sound overall: the program-based formalization, EIG computation over a well-defined hypothesis space, and the log-linear estimation with importance sampling are coherent. The leave-one-context-out evaluation and ablations are appropriate, and the qualitative samples are convincing. However, several choices are hand-engineered (domain-specific grammar primitives, relevance filter), approximations are heavy (importance sampling over a very large X), and some evaluation details are under-specified (e.g., annotation protocol for mapping natural language to programs; inter-annotator agreement; absolute magnitude of LL gains; computational cost).
- Clarity. The paper is clearly written and well motivated. The modeling pipeline is explained accessibly. Important details (full grammar, proposal q, hypothesis space sizes) are deferred to supplement; summarizing key parts in the main text would help reproducibility. The EIG setup and priors are described, but the handling of contexts dropped for tractability should be better justified.
- Originality. Representing questions as programs and treating question asking as program synthesis in a goal-directed, active setting is novel and interesting. While semantic parsers and program-based QA exist, this work addresses generative question choice with a computationally explicit link to informativeness and complexity, which is less explored.
- Significance. As a conceptual step toward richer active learning and cognitively grounded question generation, this is promising. Empirically, the scope is limited to a single, small domain (605 questions across 16 contexts after exclusions), and substantial domain knowledge is embedded in the grammar. The predictive improvements over ablations are suggestive but modest; the correlation analysis even shows a slightly higher average Spearman for the information-agnostic variant, which needs discussion. Broader impact will depend on generalization beyond this toy domain and on integrating with natural language.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formalization of questions as executable programs with a compositional grammar.
- Cogent integration of EIG with a complexity prior, aligning with cognitive constraints and explaining why pure EIG fails to predict human behavior.
- Quantitative evaluation with ablations; strong evidence that complexity is crucial (complexity-agnostic model performs poorly).
- Ability to synthesize novel, context-sensitive questions that are informative and plausibly human-like.
- Solid connection to relevant literature in linguistics, cognitive science, and ML.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Domain specificity and data scale: results are confined to a single, hand-crafted domain with grammar primitives tailored to Battleship; only 16 contexts used for evaluation (two dropped for tractability, plus 14 questions excluded).
- Mapping from natural language to programs is not described in detail: who annotated, ambiguity handling, agreement, coverage of paraphrases.
- The “relevance” feature is a hand-coded filter; it risks baking in task knowledge and obscuring whether the model could learn to avoid degenerate questions.
- Importance-sampling–based learning over 150k samples with a uniform PCFG proposal may be inefficient; no diagnostics on variance, effective sample size, or sensitivity to sample size are reported.
- Mixed quantitative signals: while log-likelihood favors the full model in most bootstrap samples, average rank correlation is slightly higher for the information-agnostic variant; this deserves explanation.
- EIG is computed under a specific hypothesis prior and noiseless answering; human answer costs/noise are only indirectly captured by complexity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide details on the semantic annotation process (guidelines, multiple annotators, inter-annotator agreement), and release code/grammar to ensure reproducibility.
- Report absolute LL gains and confidence intervals, not just bootstrap win rates; add alternative fit metrics less sensitive to sparsity (e.g., top-k recall of human questions).
- Analyze why the information-agnostic model slightly outperforms in rank correlation despite worse LL; e.g., sparsity effects, the role of answer-type priors, or feature interactions.
- Replace or augment the hand-coded relevance filter with learnable features or constrained generation (e.g., typed grammars, stronger typing to ensure board reference).
- Provide diagnostics for the importance sampler (ESS, variance), and consider stronger inference: MCMC over trees, annealed IS, or learning a neural proposal for the grammar.
- Explore alternative complexity measures (program tree size, MDL, learned rule priors) and test robustness.
- Add baselines: retrieval from nearest contexts, simple templates, or small neural sequence models (even with limited data) to establish difficulty.
- Move beyond Battleship: demonstrate portability by adapting the grammar to a second micro-domain, or show a natural language interface via a semantic parser (even partially).
- Incorporate answerer cost/noise into the utility (e.g., RSA-style cost term), and evaluate whether this better accounts for human preferences.

Decision
Weak reject. This is a thoughtful and well-executed conceptual contribution that advances the modeling of human question generation and points toward richer active learning. However, the empirical scope is narrow, several components are hand-engineered, and some evaluation signals are mixed. With additional experiments demonstrating generalization, clearer annotation and inference details, and stronger baselines, this work could merit acceptance.
