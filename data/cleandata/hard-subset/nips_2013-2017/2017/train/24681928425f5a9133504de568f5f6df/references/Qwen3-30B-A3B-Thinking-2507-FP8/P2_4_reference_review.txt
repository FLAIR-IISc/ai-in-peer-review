

# Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that can be executed on possible world states to produce answers. The authors argue that modeling questions as syntactically well-formed programs (rather than as simple label requests or natural language strings) captures the compositional and computable nature of human questioning. They develop a probabilistic model using a grammar-based program generation approach with features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like game, demonstrating superior predictive ability over variants that omit key components. The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding human question asking and provides a foundation for more human-like active learning systems.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with well-motivated theoretical foundations. The authors clearly establish their modeling approach through compositionality and computability, building on cognitive science and linguistics literature. The probabilistic model is rigorously developed with appropriate features, and the evaluation methodology is sound, using leave-one-out cross-validation with bootstrapping for confidence intervals. The model variants (lesioning key components) provide strong evidence for the importance of each feature, particularly highlighting that question complexity is as important as informativeness in predicting human behavior.

The paper acknowledges limitations thoughtfully, including the domain specificity of their grammar and the semantic-to-natural-language gap. The authors are careful to distinguish their approach from prior work on visual question generation and goal-directed dialog systems, providing a clear position in the literature. The technical details in the supplementary materials (grammar rules, feature descriptions) provide sufficient detail for reproduction.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and the authors' contribution. The related work section clearly differentiates this work from previous approaches to question asking. The methodology is presented logically, with clear explanations of the key concepts (compositionality, computability, program grammar). Figures 2 and 3 effectively illustrate the model's performance and novel question generation capabilities. The writing is accessible to the NIPS audience, and the supplementary materials provide additional technical depth without overwhelming the main text.

## Originality
This work is highly original in its approach to modeling question asking. While prior work has explored question generation (e.g., VQG datasets) or active learning (label requests), none have modeled questions as programs that can be executed on possible world states. The authors successfully bridge concepts from computational linguistics (compositionality), cognitive science (program-based concepts), and active learning (informativeness). The paper explicitly identifies the gap this work fills: "human question asking is robust to changes in task and goals, while state-of-the-art neural networks do not generalize flexibly in these ways." The grammar-based program generation approach is novel and provides a computational explanation for the productivity of human question asking (15% of human questions were novel across contexts).

## Significance
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a predictive model of open-ended human question asking, which has been largely unaddressed in the machine learning literature. The framework advances active learning beyond simple label requests toward more human-like information acquisition. The ability to generate novel, human-like questions (Figure 3) demonstrates the model's generative capabilities. While the domain is limited to the Battleship game, the authors convincingly argue that "many aspects of our grammar are domain general rather than domain specific, including very general functions and programming constructs."

## Strengths
1. Novel theoretical framework that models questions as executable programs
2. Clear demonstration of compositionality and productivity in human question asking
3. Strong empirical validation with comprehensive model variants
4. Demonstrated ability to generate novel, human-like questions
5. Thoughtful discussion of limitations and future work
6. Well-written with clear organization and sufficient technical detail

## Weaknesses
1. The domain specificity (Battleship game) limits immediate applicability to broader question-asking contexts
2. The semantic-to-natural-language gap (the model works with program representations, not natural language) requires additional engineering for practical applications
3. The human data is limited (605 questions from 40 players), though the authors acknowledge this
4. The model's scalability to more complex domains is not thoroughly explored

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of active learning and computational cognitive science. It addresses a meaningful gap in understanding human-like question asking and provides a theoretically grounded, empirically validated framework. While the domain is limited, the core approach is generalizable and provides a foundation for future work. The paper's strengths in quality, clarity, originality, and significance outweigh its limitations.

The paper clearly meets NIPS criteria: it's technically sound, well-written, original, and significant. It advances the state of the art by providing the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking, with implications for both cognitive science and AI. The model's ability to generate novel, human-like questions is particularly impressive and demonstrates the framework's potential.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS. This work provides a solid foundation for future research in human-like active learning and would be of interest to both cognitive science and machine learning communities. The authors have made a clear, significant contribution to the field.
