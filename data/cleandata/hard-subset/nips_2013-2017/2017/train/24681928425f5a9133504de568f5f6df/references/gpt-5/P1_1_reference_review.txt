Summary
This paper proposes a computational framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a simple LISP-like language; when executed on a hypothesized world state, a program returns an answer (Boolean, number, color, or location). A context-free grammar (with lambda, map, set operations) defines the compositional space of allowable programs. A log-linear model over program features assigns probabilities to questions, with features capturing expected information gain (EIG; context-dependent), complexity (−log probability under the PCFG), answer type, and a relevance filter. Parameters are learned to maximize the likelihood of human questions in a Battleship-like information search task [19]. Inference uses importance sampling from the PCFG to approximate expectations and the partition function. The model is evaluated via leave-one-context-out prediction of human question distributions (log-likelihood and rank correlations) and by sampling novel questions judged qualitatively.

Relation to prior work
The work extends active learning by moving beyond label queries to richer, compositional queries akin to partition semantics in linguistics (Groenendijk & Stokhof). It connects to program induction/program-execution approaches in cognitive science and visual reasoning (e.g., Lake et al., Johnson et al.) and contrasts with goal-oriented or data-driven question generation systems that lack explicit utility modeling. Compared to VQG and dialog systems, the contribution is goal-directed, context-sensitive question generation grounded in EIG and compositional semantics.

Strengths
- Clear, compelling formulation: modeling questions as executable programs captures compositionality and computability, aligning with linguistic and cognitive theories.
- Principled utility: the EIG feature formalizes informativeness via Bayesian belief updating over hypotheses.
- Human data grounding: evaluation on a dataset of 605 natural-language questions across 18 contexts; the grammar covers all questions after translation.
- Predictive performance: the full model outperforms ablations (information-, complexity-, and type-agnostic) on held-out contexts; complexity is especially critical. Average Spearman 0.64 between model energy and human frequencies is substantial given sparsity.
- Generativity: sampling produces novel, semantically rich and context-appropriate questions, demonstrating productivity beyond memorization.

Weaknesses and limitations
Quality/technical soundness
- Approximate inference details: The model relies on a fixed PCFG proposal to approximate both gradients and Z. It is unclear whether the 150k-sample bank is resampled per iteration or reused, and how variance affects stability. Using the same q for sampling and as the complexity feature may confound estimation and inductive bias.
- EIG computation cost: EIG requires summing over answers and hypotheses; contexts 1–2 and draw-questions were dropped due to computational burdens. More detail on hypothesis set sizes, pruning, and runtime would help assess scalability.
- Regularization and optimization: 100k gradient steps at lr=0.1 with no stated regularization may risk overfitting; feature counts are small, but reporting training vs held-out likelihood, and sensitivity to sample size, would help.
- Evaluation breadth: No external baselines beyond ablations. Simple heuristics (e.g., rank by EIG minus a length penalty; MDL-based complexity; or non-compositional retrieval/ranking baselines) would contextualize gains.

Clarity
- Overall clear exposition, with intuitive examples and sensible notation. However:
  - Some references are incomplete/garbled.
  - More explicit pseudocode for EIG computation and the Bayesian observer (prior over ship configurations) would aid reproducibility.
  - Details on mapping natural-language questions to programs are sparse (extent of manual effort, inter-annotator agreement).

Originality
- The central idea—treating question generation as program synthesis with utility-driven selection—is novel for active learning and dialog, and well integrated with LOT/program-induction perspectives. The combination with a learned log-linear energy over interpretable features is a clean, original contribution.

Significance
- Advances our understanding of human question asking and offers a path toward machine agents that ask informative, compositional questions. Within the constrained Battleship domain, results are promising. However, broader impact depends on portability to new domains and interfacing with natural language.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add external baselines: (i) EIG − λ·length without learning; (ii) bag-of-templates ranker; (iii) a neural sequence model trained to generate questions conditioned on contexts (even if data-limited) to show advantages of compositional utility.
- Report computational metrics: hypothesis space sizes per context, EIG runtime, total training time; clarify whether the proposal bank is resampled; include variance of importance estimates; consider control variates/stratified sampling.
- Alternative complexity priors: compare −log q to tree size, AST depth, or MDL; analyze sensitivity to the PCFG’s rule probabilities.
- Human evaluation of generated questions: ask independent raters to judge usefulness/answerability; or measure EIG of sampled questions vs human ones.
- Generalization: demonstrate on a second micro-domain with minimal new engineering; or quantify which grammar components are domain-general vs specific.
- Language interface: outline a path from surface text to programs (semantic parsing), or include a small semantic parsing experiment using existing tools.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually strong, bridges active learning, semantics, and program synthesis.
- Solid empirical evidence that complexity and EIG jointly explain human question choices; quantitative improvements over ablations.
- Demonstrates compositional productivity and generation of novel, clever questions.

Arguments against acceptance
- Narrow domain and reliance on hand-crafted grammar; unclear generalization.
- Evaluation lacks strong external baselines and human judgments of generated outputs.
- Approximate inference and optimization details leave questions about robustness and scalability; some data and rule exclusions reduce completeness.

Overall recommendation
A thoughtful and timely contribution with a clear problem formulation, principled modeling, and promising results on human data. While the scope is limited and evaluation could be strengthened, the paper offers novel ideas likely to influence research on active, goal-directed question generation. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying inference details and adding at least one strong baseline or additional evaluation.
