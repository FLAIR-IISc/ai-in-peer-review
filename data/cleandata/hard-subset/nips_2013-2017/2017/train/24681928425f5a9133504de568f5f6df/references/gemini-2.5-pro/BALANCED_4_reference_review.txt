### Paper Summary

This paper presents a novel computational framework for goal-directed, context-sensitive question asking, inspired by human cognition. The central idea is to model questions as programs in a compositional "language of thought." This recasts the problem of deciding what question to ask as a problem of probabilistic program synthesis. The authors define a grammar that generates a space of possible questions (programs). A log-linear model is then defined over this space to compute the probability of any given question. This model incorporates several key features that capture a question's utility, including its Expected Information Gain (EIG), its complexity (derived from its programmatic structure), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number), and its relevance to the task.

The framework is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game, where the goal is to ask questions to resolve ambiguity about hidden objects. The authors translate the human questions into their formal programmatic language and train their model to predict the frequency of these questions in held-out game contexts. The results demonstrate that the full model, which balances informativeness and complexity, provides a good fit to the human data and significantly outperforms ablated models that ignore complexity or informativeness. Qualitatively, the model is shown to be generative, capable of synthesizing novel, contextually relevant, and "human-like" questions that were not observed in the training data. This work connects active learning, which has traditionally focused on simple label queries, with cognitive science research on concept learning and program induction, proposing a new direction for building more sophisticated and human-like learning agents.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—framing goal-directed question asking as probabilistic program generation—is highly original and significant. It provides a formal, interpretable, and powerful new paradigm for active learning that moves beyond simple label requests. By connecting concepts from program synthesis, Bayesian inference (EIG), and cognitive science (compositionality), the paper opens up a promising new research avenue for building machines that can learn by asking richer, more structured questions. This could have a substantial impact on fields like AI, robotics, and computational cognitive science.

2.  **Technical Quality and Soundness:** The proposed model is technically sound and elegant. The use of a grammar to define a compositional hypothesis space of questions is well-motivated. The probabilistic model, which learns to balance multiple competing objectives (informativeness, simplicity, etc.), is a principled way to capture the constraints on human question asking. The experimental methodology is rigorous, using leave-one-out cross-validation to test the model's predictive power on unseen contexts. The ablation studies are particularly effective, clearly demonstrating that both informativeness (EIG) and complexity are critical components for explaining the data, a key claim of the paper.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the model is described in sufficient detail, and the connection between the theoretical framework and the experimental task is explicit. The authors do an excellent job of explaining complex ideas like compositionality and computability in an accessible manner. The qualitative examples of generated questions (Figure 3) are compelling and provide strong intuitive support for the model's capabilities.

4.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The model achieves a strong quantitative fit to the human data, with the full model outperforming several well-chosen baselines. The Spearman's rank correlation of 0.64 between model predictions and human question frequencies is impressive for such a complex, open-ended task. Critically, the qualitative results—the generation of novel, clever, and contextually appropriate questions—are very convincing and showcase the generative power of the framework in a way that aggregate statistics cannot.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

1.  **Scalability and Domain Generality:** The primary weakness of the current approach is its reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar. While the authors correctly point out that many components are domain-general (e.g., logical operators, set functions), the core predicates (e.g., `size`, `color`, references to ships) are tailored to the Battleship game. This raises questions about the scalability and generalizability of the framework. Porting this system to a new, more complex domain would require significant feature engineering to design a new grammar.

    *   **Suggestion:** For future work, it would be valuable to explore methods for learning the grammar's primitives from data, perhaps by grounding them in a perceptual system or learning them from a corpus of domain-specific text. This would be a crucial step toward creating more autonomous and scalable question-asking agents.

2.  **Inference Approximation:** The model requires approximating an intractable partition function (Z) and the expected feature values under the model distribution during gradient-based optimization. This is done via importance sampling from a proposal distribution defined by a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) with uniform weights. While a practical solution, the quality of this approximation can be sensitive to the choice of proposal distribution and the number of samples. A poor approximation could affect the reliability of the learned parameters and the log-likelihood estimates.

    *   **Suggestion:** The paper could be strengthened by a brief discussion of the potential limitations of this approximation. For instance, have the authors analyzed the variance of the importance weights? It might be useful to mention or explore alternative inference techniques, such as MCMC-based methods or variational approaches, which might provide more stable estimates, though likely at a higher computational cost.

3.  **Gap between Semantics and Natural Language:** The model operates entirely at the level of semantic programs (the "language of thought"), abstracting away the challenge of parsing natural language questions into these programs or generating natural language from them. The authors acknowledge this limitation. While this separation is methodologically sound for isolating and studying the question generation problem, it leaves a significant gap for practical applications.

    *   **Suggestion:** It would be interesting to see a discussion on how this system could be integrated with modern NLP tools. For instance, one could envision a two-stage system where this model proposes a semantic program, and a separate, pre-trained neural language model (e.g., a seq2seq model conditioned on the program structure) "translates" it into a fluent English question. This would bridge the gap to natural language and make the system's output more directly usable.

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and thought-provoking framework for a fundamental problem in AI and cognitive science. The central idea is elegant, the technical execution is solid, and the results are compelling. The work is significant in that it charts a new path for active learning that is more aligned with the richness of human inquiry. While the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar limits its immediate applicability, the conceptual contribution is substantial and likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up work. The paper is well-written, honest about its limitations, and represents a complete and impactful piece of research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
