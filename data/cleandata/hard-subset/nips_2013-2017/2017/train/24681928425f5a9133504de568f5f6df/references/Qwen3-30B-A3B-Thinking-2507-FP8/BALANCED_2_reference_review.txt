

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that can be executed on possible world states to produce answers. The authors argue that this program synthesis approach captures the compositionality and computability of human questions, which previous active learning methods (focused on label queries) and neural question generation systems (requiring large datasets) fail to address. They evaluate their model on a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship-like information-search game, using a probabilistic generative model that incorporates features of informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

## Strengths
The paper makes a significant contribution by reframing question asking as program synthesis, which provides a principled way to model compositionality and computability. This approach is distinct from both traditional active learning (which queries for labels) and neural question generation (which requires large paired datasets). The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation, connecting their work to cognitive science (compositionality, language of thought) and computational linguistics (program-based question meaning). Their evaluation is thorough, with both quantitative metrics (log-likelihood comparisons across model variants) and qualitative examples of novel question generation. The model's ability to generate genuinely novel, contextually appropriate questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") is particularly impressive and demonstrates the model's productivity.

## Weaknesses
The primary limitation is the narrow scope of the evaluation domain (Battleship game), which raises questions about generalizability. The paper acknowledges this, noting that "some aspects of our grammar are specific to the Battleship domain," yet fails to provide sufficient evidence that the approach can be extended to more open-ended, real-world scenarios. The model operates on semantic representations rather than natural language, which limits its direct applicability to practical dialogue systems. While the authors mention bridging this gap via "recent tools in computational linguistics," this remains a significant unresolved challenge.

The evaluation methodology also has limitations. The cross-validation approach (leaving out one context) doesn't adequately test the model's ability to generalize across different types of questions or domains. The paper doesn't address how the model would handle ambiguous or contextually inappropriate questions, nor does it compare against more recent neural question generation approaches that might better capture natural language patterns. Additionally, the paper doesn't sufficiently explore the trade-offs between the model's complexity and its performance gains.

## Overall Assessment
This is a technically sound paper with strong theoretical grounding that makes a meaningful contribution to understanding human question asking. The program synthesis approach is novel and well-motivated, and the evaluation demonstrates the model's ability to capture key aspects of human question generation. However, the narrow domain and lack of clear pathways to broader applicability limit the paper's significance for NeurIPS, which values work with broader impact on machine learning and AI.

## Recommendations
The paper is strong in its core idea and evaluation within the specific domain, but would benefit from:

1. Expanding the evaluation to at least one additional domain (e.g., a different game or simple real-world scenario) to demonstrate more general applicability.

2. Providing more concrete discussion of how to bridge the semantic representation to natural language generation, including potential integration with recent advances in program-to-text generation.

3. Comparing against more recent neural question generation approaches that might handle open-ended question asking more effectively.

4. Adding a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the model's complexity and its performance benefits.

While the paper makes a valuable contribution to cognitive science and computational linguistics, its narrow scope and limited generalizability to broader machine learning applications make it less compelling for NeurIPS. I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that with the suggested improvements, this could become a strong submission for future conferences. The core idea is promising and deserves further development with broader applicability.
