- Models human questions as executable programs in a small LISP-like language with a PCFG hypothesis space and a log-linear energy model over programs.
- Energy features include expected information gain (EIG), zero-EIG indicator, complexity penalty (−log PCFG prob), answer-type indicators, and a relevance filter.
- Trained with importance sampling to approximate gradients and the partition function, evaluated on 605 natural-language questions from a Battleship-like task.
- Evaluations: leave-one-context-out prediction of human question distributions and qualitative sampling of novel, human-like questions.
- Main claim: complexity (and to a lesser extent EIG and answer-type priors) improves fit to human question data.
- Strength: clear, principled formalization linking questions-as-partitions to executable programs and rigorous EIG calculation.
- Strength: compositional PCFG supports productivity and generalization beyond template or retrieval methods.
- Strength: cognitive plausibility in balancing informativeness and complexity, matching behavioral observations.
- Strength: empirical evaluation demonstrates both predictive and generative capabilities.
- Strength: paper transparently states assumptions, domain limits, and computational constraints.
- Weakness: small, synthetic dataset (605 questions across ~16–18 contexts) with many singleton counts limits statistical power.
- Weakness: importance sampling proposal (uniform PCFG) for a potentially peaked energy model risks high-variance estimates and biased learning.
- Weakness: missing IS diagnostics (ESS, Z variance, convergence, resampling strategy) and potential bias from fixed sample pool during many gradient steps.
- Weakness: ambiguous contribution of EIG—LL improvements versus slightly better correlation for the information-agnostic model need reconciliation.
- Weakness: complexity feature conflates cognitive simplicity with sampling/grammar bias since fcomp = −log q(x) uses the same PCFG as the proposal.
- Weakness: manual NL→program mapping lacks methodology, inter-annotator agreement, and error analysis, hurting reproducibility.
- Weakness: novelty/generation evaluation is qualitative without human judgments and the equivalence filtering method (MI thresholding) is under-specified.
- Weakness: limited baselines—missing retrieval/template baselines and simpler program-space alternatives to quantify the energy model's added value.
- Quality: methodology is sound and well-motivated, but IS-based training needs more diagnostics and robust estimators; EIG claims should be tempered.
- Clarity: generally clear writing, but crucial operational details (annotation protocol, grammar tables, IS diagnostics) are deferred and should be included.
- Originality: novel synthesis framing question asking as program generation bridging active learning, formal semantics, and program induction.
- Significance: promising cognitive-computational contribution with implications for interactive ML, but scope limited by domain, dataset size, and NL interface.
- Suggestion: provide IS diagnostics (ESS, variance of Z, resampling strategy) and consider AIS/SMC or MCMC alternatives.
- Suggestion: replace/augment fcomp with interpretable complexity proxies (AST size, length) and report robustness and correlations with behavior.
- Suggestion: clarify and release NL→program mapping with inter-annotator agreement and ambiguous example cases.
- Suggestion: quantify EIG’s incremental value with per-context improvements, CIs, and explain LL vs. correlation discrepancy.
- Suggestion: add external baselines (retrieval+EIG, template programs, length-only) and include a small human evaluation of generated questions.
- Questions for authors: Do you re-sample programs each gradient step and what are effective sample sizes across iterations?
- Questions for authors: How sensitive are results to the grammar prior and choice of complexity proxy?
- Questions for authors: How was equivalence via mutual information operationalized (thresholds, robustness)?
- Questions for authors: What was the protocol and reliability for mapping text questions to programs?
- Pros for acceptance: elegant formal framing, evidence of compositional generalization, and demonstration that complexity matters beyond informativeness.
- Cons for acceptance: small domain-specific dataset, qualitative generation eval, lacking IS diagnostics, and potential feature-sampling confounds.
- Overall: borderline—conceptually strong but requires stronger empirical validation, diagnostics, baselines, and NL mapping clarity to be compelling for NeurIPS.
