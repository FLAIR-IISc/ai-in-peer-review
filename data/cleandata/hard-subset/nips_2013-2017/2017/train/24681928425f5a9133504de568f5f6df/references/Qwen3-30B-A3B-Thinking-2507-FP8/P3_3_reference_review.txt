

# Peer Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that can be executed on possible world states to generate answers. The authors model questions as syntactically well-formed programs (in a simple programming language), capturing compositionality and computability. They develop a probabilistic generative model that predicts human question frequencies using features related to informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship-like game where participants asked questions to identify ship configurations. The authors demonstrate that their model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel human-like questions.

The work bridges cognitive science (compositionality in language), program synthesis, and active learning. Unlike previous work on question generation (e.g., VQG datasets) that focuses on general question production without goal-directedness, or goal-directed systems that use canned questions, this paper addresses context-sensitive, goal-directed question asking through a principled program generation approach.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant conceptual advance by framing questions as programs, providing a principled way to capture compositionality (the ability to combine concepts) and computability (questions as executable programs) in human question asking. This approach aligns with cognitive science literature on "language of thought" and computational models of concepts.

2. **Methodological Rigor:** The probabilistic model is well-constructed with clear feature design. The use of Expected Information Gain (EIG) to capture informativeness, combined with complexity features, addresses a key insight that human question asking isn't solely about maximizing information gain (which would favor overly complex questions). The model's ability to predict human question frequencies with a Spearman's ρ = 0.64 demonstrates its validity.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides strong evidence for the model's effectiveness through both quantitative (log-likelihood comparisons, correlation with human frequencies) and qualitative (novel question generation) evaluations. The ablation studies showing that removing complexity features significantly degrades performance is particularly compelling.

4. **Novelty:** The paper successfully models a previously unaddressed aspect of human cognition—how people construct semantically meaningful, context-sensitive questions. The program generation approach provides a new way to model question asking that goes beyond previous work on goal-directed dialog systems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Domain Specificity:** The evaluation is limited to a single domain (Battleship game). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't provide sufficient evidence for how the approach generalizes to new domains. The grammar and features are tightly coupled to the Battleship rules, making it unclear how much new knowledge engineering would be required for other domains.

2. **Natural Language Generation Gap:** The paper operates on semantic representations rather than natural language text. While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation, they don't sufficiently address how their framework could interface with natural language generation systems to produce human-readable questions. This is a significant practical limitation for real-world applications.

3. **Evaluation Scope:** The evaluation is limited to one dataset with a relatively small number of contexts (18 contexts for 40 participants). Additional datasets or human evaluations of the generated questions would strengthen the claims.

4. **Feature Design:** The "filter" feature (f∅(x)) to exclude questions that don't reference the game board is somewhat ad hoc. The paper doesn't adequately explain how this feature was determined or why it's necessary for the model's performance.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, technically sound contribution that makes a clear advancement in modeling human question asking. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a compelling case for the program generation framework. The methodology is rigorous, with appropriate ablation studies and careful evaluation.

The most significant limitation is the narrow domain scope, but this is acknowledged by the authors and is common in cognitive science work. The core contribution—the program generation framework for question asking—is generalizable in principle, and the authors provide a clear roadmap for future work in extending the approach to other domains.

From a NIPS perspective, this paper advances the state of the art in active learning by expanding beyond simple label queries to more complex, human-like question asking. It also contributes to computational cognitive science by providing the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

The paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses. The theoretical contribution is significant, the methodology is sound, and the results are well-supported. While the domain restriction is a limitation, the framework itself is generalizable, and the authors provide a clear path forward for extension to new domains. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, advancing both active learning and computational cognitive science.

The work is original, as it approaches question asking from a novel computational angle that hasn't been fully explored in previous NIPS papers. The evaluation is thorough within the scope of the problem, and the authors are appropriately careful in discussing limitations.

**Pro for Acceptance:** Strong theoretical foundation, clear methodology, novel contribution to modeling human question asking, well-supported empirical results.

**Con for Acceptance:** Limited to a single domain, requires substantial domain-specific knowledge engineering.

This paper represents a meaningful step toward enabling machines to ask rich, human-like questions, and the framework has significant potential for future development.
