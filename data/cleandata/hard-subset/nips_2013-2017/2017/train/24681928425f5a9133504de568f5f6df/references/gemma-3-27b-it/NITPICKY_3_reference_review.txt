This paper proposes a framework for modeling human question asking as program generation, leveraging compositionality and computability. The authors present a probabilistic model, evaluated on a dataset of questions from a Battleship-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions. While the core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet accumulating, flaws that ultimately detract from its overall quality and prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance.

The primary strength lies in the conceptual contribution – framing question asking as program synthesis offers a novel perspective. The attempt to bridge cognitive science and machine learning by modeling question generation with a formal grammar is commendable. The experimental evaluation on the collected dataset is also a positive aspect, demonstrating a degree of predictive power.

However, the weaknesses are significant. First, the paper is excessively verbose. The introduction, for instance, spends considerable space establishing the importance of active learning and human question asking, without directly motivating the proposed program synthesis framework early enough. The related work section, while comprehensive, feels repetitive and lacks critical analysis – it *lists* many relevant papers but doesn't adequately synthesize their limitations in relation to the authors’ approach.

Second, while the authors claim a focus on computational aspects, the technical details are often imprecise. The description of the “energy” function (Equation 1) is vague; the features themselves are only superficially described. What precisely constitutes a “relevant” question, beyond the filter feature, remains unclear. The optimization process is mentioned but lacks sufficient detail – the choice of learning rate (0.1) feels arbitrary without justification.  Furthermore, the statement regarding bootstrapping the normalizing constant doesn’t mention the specific bootstrap method used, raising concerns about the reliability of the results.

Third, the grammar itself, while presented in Tables SI-1 and SI-2 (in supplementary materials, which reviewers may not always prioritize), is not thoroughly discussed within the main text. The justification for specific rewrite rules is missing. Why these particular functions and operators?  The exclusion of questions involving “draw” (demonstrating colored tiles) due to computational complexity is a significant limitation that should be more prominently acknowledged; it suggests a fragility in the approach.

Fourth, the experimental results, while demonstrating some correlation, are not overwhelmingly convincing. The Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient of 0.64 is respectable, but the authors don’t adequately discuss the variance or statistical significance of this result. The comparison with the “information-agnostic” model is particularly troubling – achieving a similar result suggests that informativeness, as defined by the authors, isn't the dominant factor in human question asking, weakening the core premise.

Fifth, the formatting and presentation are riddled with minor inconsistencies. The citation style fluctuates. The references are not consistently formatted (e.g., some include pre-print labels, others don't). Table and figure captions are sometimes incomplete. There are instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors throughout the text.

Finally, the "novel" questions generated by the model (Figure 3) appear, upon closer inspection, to be rather simplistic and lack the nuanced reasoning one would expect from a human. The claim of "cleverness" feels overstated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel conceptual framework for modeling question asking.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict human question frequencies.
*   Potential to contribute to more human-like active learning systems.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Excessive verbosity and lack of conciseness.
*   Imprecise technical details and lack of justification for design choices.
*   Limited statistical significance of experimental results.
*   Numerous minor formatting and presentation inconsistencies.
*   Overstated claims regarding the “cleverness” of generated questions.
*   Fragility of the approach demonstrated by the exclusion of certain question types.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the interesting core idea, the paper is marred by a cumulative effect of minor flaws – insufficient technical detail, imprecise language, weak statistical evidence, and numerous presentation issues. While the authors demonstrate a degree of success, the limitations are substantial. I recommend **rejection**. The paper requires significant revision to address these issues before it would be suitable for NIPS. A more focused presentation, a more rigorous technical analysis, and more compelling experimental results are essential.
