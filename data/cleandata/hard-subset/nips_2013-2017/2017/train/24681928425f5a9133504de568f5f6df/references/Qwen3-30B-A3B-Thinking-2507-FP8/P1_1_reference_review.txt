

# NIPS Peer Review

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs. The authors argue that humans ask rich, context-sensitive questions that go beyond simple label queries in active learning, and they formalize this process as program synthesis. The key insight is that a question like "Do the purple and red ship touch?" can be represented as a program that, when executed on a game state, returns a truth value. The authors develop a probabilistic model that generates questions by combining features of informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain), complexity (favoring shorter programs), answer type (Boolean, Number, etc.), and relevance (ensuring questions refer to the game board).

The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like game where players needed to identify ship configurations. The authors show that their model can predict human question frequencies in held-out contexts and generate novel, human-like questions not present in the training data. This work represents a significant step toward understanding and enabling more human-like question asking in machines.

This work relates to previous NIPS contributions in active learning (e.g., [21]), program synthesis (e.g., [12, 13, 17]), and question answering (e.g., [16, 23, 24]). It differs from previous work by explicitly modeling question asking as program generation rather than simply generating questions from large datasets or using canned templates.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a technically sound approach that combines formal language theory, probabilistic modeling, and information theory in a coherent way.
- The experimental evaluation is thorough, with careful ablation studies showing the importance of each component (informativeness, complexity, etc.).
- The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of their model (Equations 1-4) and adequately justify their choices of features.
- The evaluation includes both quantitative (log-likelihood comparisons) and qualitative (novel question generation) assessments.
- The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., domain specificity, semantic representation vs. natural language) and discusses future directions thoughtfully.

**Weaknesses:**
- The model's evaluation is limited to a single domain (the Battleship game), which may not generalize to other question-asking contexts. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't provide evidence of broader applicability.
- The connection between the program representation and natural language generation is not fully addressed. The paper notes this limitation but doesn't provide a concrete path forward.
- The computational complexity of the model (with the need for importance sampling over a large grammar) is not thoroughly discussed, which may limit its practical applicability.
- The paper doesn't address how the model would handle ambiguous or poorly formed questions that humans sometimes ask.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and logically structured, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning.
- The use of concrete examples (e.g., the "size Blue" program for "How long is the blue ship?") effectively illustrates the key concepts.
- Figures 2 and 3 provide clear visualizations of model performance and novel question generation.
- The grammar and rewrite rules (Table SI-1) are well-described, and the supplementary materials provide additional detail.

**Weaknesses:**
- Some technical details (e.g., the optimization procedure in Section 4.4) could be clearer for readers unfamiliar with importance sampling and probabilistic grammar.
- The explanation of how the grammar handles set operations (e.g., the "Are all ships the same size?" example) could be more detailed.
- The connection between the program representation and natural language generation is somewhat underdeveloped, which might confuse readers about how this model could be integrated with real-world NLP systems.

## Originality

The core idea of modeling questions as programs is novel in the context of active learning and human question asking. Previous work on question asking (e.g., [16, 23, 24]) has focused on generating questions from large datasets without the compositional, programmatic approach the authors propose. The paper clearly distinguishes their work from previous approaches:

- Unlike goal-directed dialog systems ([25, 1]) that use canned questions
- Unlike neural VQG approaches ([16, 23]) that require large datasets
- Unlike open-ended dialog systems ([20]) that lack intentional question asking

The paper also makes a significant contribution by formalizing how humans balance informativeness and complexity when asking questions, which is not captured in previous models of question asking.

## Significance

The paper addresses a meaningful gap in active learning research by moving beyond simple label queries to model the rich, context-sensitive question asking observed in humans. The ability to generate novel, human-like questions (Figure 3) represents a significant step toward more human-like machine question asking. This work could potentially impact several areas:

- Active learning, by enabling richer information acquisition
- Human-computer interaction, by improving question-answering systems
- Cognitive science, by providing a computational model of human question asking

The significance is demonstrated by the model's ability to predict human question frequencies (Spearman correlation 0.64) and generate novel, useful questions not present in the training data. While the current implementation is domain-specific, the authors provide a clear path for future work to extend the approach to other domains.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a novel, well-motivated approach to modeling human question asking
- The technical contribution is sound and well-executed
- The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing
- The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions
- The work addresses an important gap in active learning research

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The evaluation is limited to a single domain (Battleship game), which may limit generalizability
- The connection to natural language generation is not fully developed
- The computational complexity of the model is not thoroughly addressed
- The paper could better distinguish its approach from related work on program synthesis for question answering

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work makes a significant contribution to understanding human question asking through a computational lens, and the proposed framework is both novel and well-executed. While the domain-specific nature of the current implementation is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and provide a clear path for future work to extend the approach to other domains. The paper's strengths in technical quality, clarity, and significance outweigh its weaknesses.

The paper would benefit from slightly more discussion of how the program representation could be connected to natural language generation (perhaps referencing recent advances in program-to-text generation [24]), and a clearer statement about the computational requirements of the model. However, these are minor points that do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

This work represents a meaningful step toward the long-term goal of enabling machines to learn by asking rich, human-like questions, and it aligns well with NIPS's focus on foundational advances in machine learning and cognitive science.
