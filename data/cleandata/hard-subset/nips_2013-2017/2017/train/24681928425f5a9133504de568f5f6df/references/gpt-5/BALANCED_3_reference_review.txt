Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a computational framework for question generation in which questions are represented as executable programs over possible world states. A typed, largely domain-general grammar (augmented with some Battleship-specific primitives) defines a space of well-formed question programs. A log-linear model over program features—expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, a complexity prior derived from a PCFG, answer-type indicators, and a relevance filter—induces a probability distribution over questions given a context. Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human questions from a prior study (Rothe et al., 2016) in a leave-one-context-out setup. The model predicts held-out human question distributions better than lesioned variants and can sample novel, plausible questions not observed in the data.

The work connects three strands: (i) active learning/EIG ideas (Settles, 2012), (ii) program-like representations for meaning and cognition (Goodman et al., 2008; Lake et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2015), and (iii) formal/pragmatic accounts of question semantics as partitions over worlds (Groenendijk & Stokhof, 1984; Roberts, 1996) and recent pragmatic models of question asking (Hawkins et al., 2015). Relative to prior NIPS/NeurIPS work on program induction and semantic parsing, the novelty here is to model the production of questions (not only answering) and to quantitatively fit distributions of human questions conditioned on context.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formalization: representing questions as programs evaluated over hypotheses integrates compositionality, computability, and informativeness in a single framework.
- Empirical evaluation on human data: the model predicts context-conditioned question distributions and can generate novel, human-like questions; ablations indicate the importance of complexity and answer-type priors alongside EIG.
- Conceptual contribution to active learning: demonstrates that maximizing EIG alone is insufficient to explain human production; a complexity/effort prior is necessary.
- Careful use of probabilistic semantics (partitions over hypotheses) and a transparent feature-based model that is interpretable.

Weaknesses
- Narrow domain and heavy knowledge engineering: performance hinges on a hand-crafted grammar tailored to Battleship. Generalization to new domains remains unclear.
- Approximate and partially ad hoc training/inference: importance sampling over a large, fixed proposal set and bootstrap estimates of Z introduce uncertainty. Sensitivity to the proposal q(x), grammar design, and sampling budget is not reported.
- Limited baselines and effect sizes: the main quantitative claims rely on likelihood differences and a Spearman correlation, but (a) EIG-agnostic correlates slightly higher than the full model, and (b) absolute effect sizes and per-context improvements are not reported. Comparisons to strong learned baselines (e.g., compositional/neural generative models over programs) are missing.
- No end-to-end linkage to natural language: the model operates on meaning representations and sidesteps the NL generation/interpretation problem, which limits NeurIPS significance for broader ML communities.
- Coverage gaps: the “draw” operator and two contexts were dropped for computational reasons; it is unclear how often such operators arise and how scalable the approach is when stochastic operators or larger hypothesis spaces are required.

Quality
- Generally technically sound with appropriate references and a coherent probabilistic formulation. However, several implementation choices deserve deeper analysis: reuse vs. resampling of the 150k proposal set across optimization steps; convergence diagnostics; variance of the Z estimator; and sensitivity of results to the PCFG probabilities and the length cap.
- The Bayesian ideal observer and EIG are appropriate, but human answer noise and answer cost are ignored; both likely interact with the strong role of complexity you find.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, and the formalism is well-motivated with examples. Some key details are deferred to the supplement (full grammar, hypothesis space sizes, EIG computation), and several references are incomplete. Clarify why the information-agnostic model yields slightly higher rank correlation than the full model and provide effect sizes for likelihood improvements.

Originality
- Representing question production as program generation and fitting a probabilistic model to human question distributions is a fresh angle relative to most prior work (which focuses on answering or on template-based question asking). The compositional, executable semantics are well aligned with recent program induction trends.

Significance
- For cognitive modeling, this is a meaningful step: it quantifies how informativeness trades off with complexity in human question asking. For NeurIPS, the broader ML impact is more limited: the approach is demonstrated in a single, constrained domain with hand-engineered semantics and without an NL interface or deployment on standard ML tasks. The ideas could influence future active learning and dialog systems, but this paper stops short of showing such impact.

Pros
- Elegant, compositional representation of questions as programs.
- Interpretable probabilistic model with informative ablations; complexity prior matters.
- Generates novel, context-sensitive questions that appear human-like.
- Bridges formal semantics, program induction, and active learning.

Cons
- Domain- and grammar-specific; unclear generalization or scalability.
- Limited baselines and ambiguous quantitative advantages (correlation vs. likelihood).
- Heavy approximations; sensitivity and robustness analyses missing.
- No natural language component; limited immediate applicability for ML systems.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger baselines: e.g., a learned neural generator over programs with type constraints; a retrieval + composition baseline; RSA-style pragmatic questioner models conditioned on an explicit answerer model.
- Report absolute and per-context likelihood gains, calibration (e.g., probability mass on human top-k), and sensitivity analyses (proposal size, grammar priors, length cap).
- Incorporate answer cost or cognitive effort explicitly (e.g., a penalty on program size or working-memory load) and explore noisy answerers.
- Analyze failure cases qualitatively and include human evaluation of generated questions’ usefulness and naturalness.
- Demonstrate portability: replicate in at least one additional domain with minimal new engineering, and/or add a lightweight NL interface via a semantic parser (e.g., overnight-style induction).
- Address stochastic operators and larger hypothesis spaces with more scalable inference (e.g., SMC/MCMC over programs, type-directed enumerative search).

Recommendation
Weak reject. This is a thought-provoking and well-executed cognitive modeling paper with an elegant formalism. However, for NeurIPS, the contribution is currently constrained by a single engineered domain, limited baselines, and a lack of end-to-end NL or ML-task impact. Addressing the above concerns—especially demonstrating portability, adding stronger baselines, and deepening the quantitative analysis—would substantially strengthen the case.
