Summary
The paper proposes modeling question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a small, hand-engineered, LISP-like language with a compositional grammar. A log-linear model over features (expected information gain, program complexity via a PCFG prior, answer type, and a relevance filter) defines a distribution over programs conditioned on context. The model is trained to match human question frequencies in a Battleship-like game dataset (605 questions across 18 contexts). Inference relies on importance sampling over questions sampled from the grammar. The paper evaluates (i) predictive likelihood on held-out contexts with ablations, (ii) rank correlations between predicted energies and human frequencies, and (iii) qualitative sampling of “novel” questions.

Relation to prior work
The formulation intersects several established threads: questions as partitions/programs in linguistics and QA; probabilistic program induction; active learning via EIG; and compositional grammars in cognitive modeling. The main novelty is combining these pieces to model human question generation in a specific domain and showing that a combination of informativeness and complexity features explains human choices better than ablations.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of a compositional, executable representation of questions and its connection to hypothesis-space reduction via EIG.
- Transparent generative model with interpretable features; ablations suggest complexity and type features matter in addition to informativeness.
- A small but carefully curated human dataset; leave-one-context-out setup.
- Qualitative examples of generated questions show some creativity beyond simple label queries.
- The paper is well written and situates itself in relevant literatures.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope and external validity. The dataset is tiny (605 questions), single-domain, with a hand-engineered grammar explicitly constructed to cover the dataset. Two contexts and 14 questions are removed for computational convenience, which undermines claims of generality.
- Heavy dependence on domain-specific knowledge. Although some constructs are general (sets, arithmetic, map), many primitives are Battleship-specific (colors, ship sizes, touching). The amount of engineering needed to port to another domain is unclear; no cross-domain evaluation is provided.
- Evaluation lacks strong baselines. All comparisons are within the proposed framework (ablations). Absent are standard alternatives such as:
  - Retrieval/template methods reweighted by context similarity.
  - Simple PCFG/LM over programs without EIG.
  - Neural sequence models (even small LSTMs/VAEs) conditioned on the context representation or on symbolic summaries.
  - Pure EIG maximization under a fixed question set.  
  Without such baselines, it is hard to assess whether the proposed machinery is necessary for the reported gains.
- Questionable statistical evaluation. Likelihoods are approximated with importance sampling over 150k questions and an estimated normalizer; variance and bias of these estimates are not rigorously characterized. Bootstrapping the estimate of Z does not fully address Monte Carlo error or proposal mismatch. Convergence diagnostics for the 100k gradient steps are absent.
- Mixed quantitative story. The full model’s average Spearman correlation (0.64) is comparable to, or slightly worse than, the information-agnostic variant (0.65), despite a claimed likelihood advantage. This raises concerns about the robustness and interpretability of the metrics, especially given sparsity (many singletons). The lack of any correlation between human frequency and EIG-alone replicated from [19] is informative but also suggests the model’s predictive power hinges critically on hand-chosen complexity/type features.
- Practicality and scalability. Computing EIG requires an ideal-observer over a large hypothesis space; two contexts were dropped because of tractability. The approach’s feasibility for richer domains or natural language inputs remains speculative. The relevance filter is an ad-hoc feature to exclude degenerate programs.
- Reproducibility gaps. Many crucial details are deferred to supplements (grammar tables, hypothesis enumeration, priors). It is unclear whether code for inference and optimization is available; only the dataset is mentioned.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the hand-engineered grammar? If you remove Battleship-specific primitives or lambda/map, how does performance change?
2. Can you report variance or effective sample sizes for the importance sampling estimates and show stability of likelihood rankings as the proposal size increases?
3. Why does the information-agnostic model achieve a slightly higher average rank correlation than the full model? Is the likelihood advantage significant after accounting for Monte Carlo uncertainty?
4. Can you provide comparisons to simple baselines (retrieval, PCFG-only, seq2seq on program strings) under the same leave-one-context-out protocol?
5. How would you translate this approach to a second domain with different ontologies? What is the engineering effort and which components transfer?
6. Did you consider human evaluation of generated questions for usefulness/clarity in context?

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, but the main paper depends on supplementary grammar and implementation details. Consider adding a concise grammar summary and a schematic of the EIG pipeline in the main text. Clarify which features are context-dependent vs. global.

Significance
Conceptually interesting, but the current empirical evidence is narrow and relies on substantial domain engineering and approximations. Without stronger baselines, broader datasets, or a demonstration of portability, the contribution feels more like a cognitive modeling case study than a scalable ML advance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, interpretable framework linking compositional programs and EIG.
- Insightful ablations highlighting the role of complexity constraints.
- Bridges cognitive science and ML perspectives on question asking.

Cons for acceptance
- Small, domain-specific dataset and grammar; limited generality.
- Missing comparisons to standard ML baselines.
- Approximate evaluation with limited statistical rigor; some inconsistencies across metrics.
- Scalability to realistic domains and natural language is unproven.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is intriguing and potentially impactful, but the current experimental validation and comparisons fall short of NeurIPS standards for significance and rigor. Strengthening with stronger baselines, more robust estimation, and at least one additional domain would substantially improve the case.

Confidence
High.
