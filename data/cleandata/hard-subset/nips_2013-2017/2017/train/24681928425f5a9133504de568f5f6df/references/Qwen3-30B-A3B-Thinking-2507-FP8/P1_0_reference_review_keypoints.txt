- Proposes a novel framework modeling human question asking as program generation using a context-free grammar and probabilistic weighting.  
- Models questions as executable programs evaluated over possible world states, scored by informativeness, complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated on a dataset of 605 human questions from a Battleship-like game, predicting held-out question frequencies and generating novel human-like questions.  
- Technically sound with clear formalization and a well-motivated probabilistic model.  
- Rigorous evaluation: cross-validation, ablation studies, and qualitative analyses.  
- Demonstrates that complexity, not just informativeness, is crucial to modeling human question preferences.  
- Successfully generates novel, plausible questions not seen in training data (e.g., global structural queries).  
- Exceptionally clear and well-organized presentation with helpful examples and figures.  
- Supplementary materials (grammar rules, examples) are well-structured and useful.  
- Original contribution: combining compositionality and computability by treating questions as programs.  
- Advances beyond prior active learning and VQG work by enabling rich, goal-directed question generation without massive paired datasets.  
- Potentially significant impact on cognitive science, NLP, and active learning by enabling more human-like querying.  
- Domain-general primitives (logical connectives, set ops, arithmetic) suggest extensibility to other domains.  
- Strength: comprehensive connection to cognitive science and computational linguistics literatures.  
- Weakness: grammar is somewhat domain-specific to the Battleship game, limiting immediate generality.  
- Weakness: model operates on semantic representations rather than raw natural language, creating a practical gap.  
- Weakness: relatively small dataset (605 questions) given model complexity.  
- Weakness: limited discussion/handling of more complex natural language question structures and broader answer types.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, predictive, well-evaluated, bridges fields, and enables richer active learning.  
- Arguments against acceptance: domain-specific grammar, lack of direct NLP integration, and small dataset size.  
- Recommendation: strongly recommend acceptance.  
- Suggested improvement: give a concrete example of extending the grammar to a new domain.  
- Suggested improvement: discuss interfacing program generation with modern natural language generation methods.  
- Suggested improvement: clarify "low correlation" contexts in Figure 2 to explain weaker matches.  
- Suggested improvement: explicitly connect to neural program synthesis work to contrast approaches.
