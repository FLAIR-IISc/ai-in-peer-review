**Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"**

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking. The central idea is to represent questions not as natural language strings, but as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. When executed on a given world state, these programs produce an answer. This reframes the problem of "what question to ask" as one of "what program to generate." The authors develop a probabilistic log-linear model over a space of programs defined by a context-free grammar. The model's energy function learns to balance multiple factors, including the Expected Information Gain (EIG) of a question, its syntactic complexity, and its answer type. The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions from a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The results demonstrate that this approach can accurately predict the frequency distribution of human questions in held-out contexts and, impressively, generate novel, contextually relevant, and human-like questions not seen in the training data. The work connects ideas from active learning, program synthesis, and computational cognitive science.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Core Idea:** The primary strength of this paper is its conceptual contribution. Framing question asking as program generation is a powerful and elegant idea. It moves beyond the standard active learning paradigm of selecting from a predefined set of queries (e.g., requesting labels) to a truly generative process. This provides a formal basis for understanding the compositionality and creativity inherent in human inquiry, a topic of great importance to AI that is often overlooked. This work provides a clear path forward for building more flexible and human-like active learning agents.

2.  **Technically Sound and Well-Motivated Model:** The proposed probabilistic model is well-designed and principled. The choice of a log-linear model to combine different features is standard and effective. The features themselves are highly intuitive and well-justified:
    *   **Informativeness (EIG):** Correctly grounds the model in the principles of Bayesian active learning.
    *   **Complexity:** Acknowledges the cognitive or communicative cost of overly complex queries, a crucial factor that pure information-theoretic models often miss. The ablation study strongly supports its importance.
    *   **Answer Type:** Captures human biases for certain types of questions (e.g., Boolean), adding another layer of psychological plausibility.
    The combination of these features provides a much richer account of human behavior than any single feature could alone.

3.  **Strong Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough and convincing. The leave-one-out cross-validation setup is appropriate for the dataset size. The ablation study, which systematically removes key components of the model, clearly demonstrates the contribution of each feature set (informativeness, complexity, and answer type). The qualitative results in Figure 3 are particularly compelling, showcasing the model's ability to generate non-trivial, clever, and useful questions that were not produced by humans in the dataset. This generative capability is a key claim of the paper, and this evidence strongly supports it.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation is established effectively in the introduction. The model is built up step-by-step, from the core idea of compositionality to the grammar and the final probabilistic model. The experimental results are presented clearly and directly support the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on a Hand-Crafted Grammar and Semantic Parser:** The most significant limitation is that the model operates on program representations, not on natural language directly. The entire framework relies on a pre-existing, domain-specific grammar and an implicit (or manual) translation from natural language questions to programs. The authors acknowledge this, but it substantially limits the practical applicability and scalability of the approach. Engineering such a grammar for a new, more complex domain would be a formidable task. This makes the system less of an end-to-end model of question asking and more a model of generating semantic intent.

2.  **Scalability of Inference:** The model relies on importance sampling over a very large set of generated programs (150,000 samples) to approximate the partition function and the gradients. While this is a valid technique, it raises questions about computational feasibility for more expressive grammars or larger state spaces, where the set of possible programs would explode. A discussion of the computational complexity and potential limitations of this inference method would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Limited Domain:** The experiments are conducted within a single, constrained, grid-world game. While this is an excellent testbed for isolating the core cognitive phenomena, it is unclear how the model's successes would transfer to more open-ended, real-world domains (e.g., visual question asking, dialogue systems). In such domains, defining the set of possible world states `H` and computing EIG over them would be significantly more challenging, if not intractable.

**Final Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that presents a significant and original contribution to the study of active learning and computational modeling of human intelligence. The core idea of modeling question asking as program generation is both insightful and technically well-executed. The work successfully demonstrates that human question asking is a trade-off between maximizing information and minimizing complexity, providing the first predictive, generative model of this behavior.

While the reliance on a hand-engineered grammar for a specific domain is a major limitation for immediate practical application, this does not diminish the paper's scientific contribution. It lays a strong conceptual and methodological foundation that future work can build upon, for instance, by integrating techniques for learning grammars or semantic parsers from data. The paper is well-written, the experiments are solid, and the ideas are likely to be influential for researchers in machine learning, cognitive science, and NLP. It is a perfect fit for NIPS and represents a clear advance in our understanding of intelligent inquiry.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
