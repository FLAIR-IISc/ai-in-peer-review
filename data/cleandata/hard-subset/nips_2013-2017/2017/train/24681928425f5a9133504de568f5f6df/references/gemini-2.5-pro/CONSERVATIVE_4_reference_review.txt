### **Review**

**Summary:**
This paper proposes a framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking by framing it as a program generation task. The core idea is that a question can be represented as a program in a compositional, LISP-like language, which, when executed on a world state, produces an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic log-linear model to score and generate these programs based on a weighted combination of features, including Expected Information Gain (EIG), program complexity, and answer type. The model is evaluated on a dataset of human questions collected in a constrained "Battleship"-like game, where the task is to identify hidden objects on a grid. The authors show that their model can predict the frequency of human questions in held-out contexts and generate novel, plausible questions. The work is positioned as a step towards building AI systems with more human-like active learning capabilities.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The paper introduces an interesting and elegant formalization of question asking. The idea of treating questions as programs is a powerful way to capture the compositional and computable nature of human inquiry. This provides a principled way to move beyond the simple label queries common in most active learning literature.

2.  **Technically Sound Model:** The probabilistic generative model is well-formulated. The choice of a log-linear model combining features like EIG (a standard in Bayesian experimental design) and complexity (a natural constraint on cognitive processes) is reasonable and principled. The use of a formal grammar to define the space of questions is a clear and interpretable approach.

3.  **Thoughtful Analysis:** The paper correctly identifies key aspects of human question asking, such as compositionality and the trade-off between informativeness and simplicity. The ablation study provides evidence that both informativeness and complexity are crucial components for modeling human behavior, a key insight that moves beyond a naive "maximize information" approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Competitive Baselines:** This is the most significant weakness of the paper. The experiments only compare the full model against lesioned versions of itself (e.g., "information-agnostic," "complexity-agnostic"). While this ablation study is useful for internal validation, it fails to demonstrate that the proposed program-generation approach is superior to other, potentially simpler or more established methods. Standard machine learning baselines are conspicuously absent. For instance:
    *   A **retrieval-based model** that, given a new context, re-ranks all questions seen during training based on their EIG in the current context.
    *   A **template-based model** where templates are extracted from the training data and slots are filled based on the current context.
    *   Given the small dataset size, a modern **sequence-to-sequence model** (e.g., a small transformer or LSTM) trained to map a representation of the game context to the program string might perform poorly, but its inclusion would be necessary to substantiate the authors' claims about the limitations of such models. Without these comparisons, the claim that this complex program synthesis machinery is necessary remains unproven.

2.  **Limited Generalizability and Scalability:** The entire framework relies on a hand-engineered grammar specific to the "Battleship" domain. While the authors claim some components are domain-general (e.g., arithmetic, logic), the core semantic primitives (`size`, `touch`, ship colors) are entirely domain-specific. The effort required to design such a grammar for any new, reasonably complex domain seems prohibitive. This raises serious questions about whether the approach can scale beyond toy problems. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but it significantly tempers the potential impact of the work for the broader machine learning community.

3.  **Ambiguous Empirical Results:** The quantitative evaluation is not as strong as it could be. While the full model achieves the best log-likelihood, the advantage over the information-agnostic and type-agnostic models is not overwhelmingly large (supported in 81% and 88% of bootstrap samples, respectively). Furthermore, the Spearman's rank correlation results are confusing: the information-agnostic model (0.65) slightly outperforms the full model (0.64), while the EIG alone has no correlation. This counter-intuitive result is not adequately explained and undermines the central role of EIG in the model. The qualitative evaluation, while interesting, is subjective and lacks formal human evaluation to confirm that the generated questions are indeed "human-like" or useful.

4.  **Scope and Contribution:** The work feels more aligned with computational cognitive science than with a mainstream machine learning conference like NIPS. The primary contribution is a model of human behavior in a specific task. While this is a valid scientific goal, the paper does not demonstrate a clear advance in the state-of-the-art of active learning algorithms or program synthesis techniques in a way that would be immediately useful for practitioners or other machine learning researchers.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and conceptually appealing idea of framing question asking as program synthesis.
*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in AI and active learning.
*   The model is well-specified, and the ablation study provides internal evidence for the model's design choices.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation is critically weak due to the complete absence of comparisons to established baseline methods.
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar severely limits the demonstrated generalizability and potential impact of the method.
*   The quantitative results are not uniformly strong and contain some unexplained inconsistencies.
*   The contribution is primarily a cognitive model, with limited demonstrated advancement for the field of machine learning itself.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a creative and thought-provoking idea, it falls short of the rigorous empirical standards expected for NIPS. The lack of any comparison to alternative, established machine learning baselines is a critical flaw that leaves the central claims about the necessity of this approach unsubstantiated. The heavy reliance on domain-specific engineering further limits its perceived significance. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The work shows promise but requires a much more thorough and comparative evaluation before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field.
