

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes modeling human question asking as program generation, where questions are represented as syntactically well-formed programs that, when executed on a possible world state, produce an answer. The authors argue this formalism offers a new approach to modeling human question asking and enables more human-like question asking in machines. They evaluate their model using a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship-like game, where players asked questions to identify ship configurations.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a compelling case for modeling questions as programs, which aligns with computational linguistics and cognitive science literature. The grammar-based approach to question generation is elegant and captures compositional aspects of human questioning. The evaluation on predicting human question frequencies (Spearman's 0.64 correlation) and generating novel questions demonstrates reasonable success. The ablation study showing that the full model outperforms variants (complexity-agnostic, information-agnostic, type-agnostic) is well-executed.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical missing technical details:** The paper references "Table SI-1" and "Table SI-2" in Sections 4.2 and 4.5, but these supplementary tables are not provided in the manuscript. Without seeing the grammar rules, it's impossible to verify whether their grammar can indeed represent all 605 human questions. The claim that "this set of rules is sufficient to represent all 605 questions in the human data set" is unverifiable.

2. **Methodological inconsistency in grammar design:** In Section 4.4, the authors state they had to remove "the rule L → (draw C) from the grammar and the corresponding 14 questions from the data set." However, they don't explain why this rule was problematic or how it affected the model's performance. This omission undermines confidence in the grammar's completeness.

3. **Inconsistent treatment of question features:** The paper defines fEIG=0(x) as "1 if EIG is zero and 0 otherwise," but then notes in a footnote that "Questions like (size Blue) do refer to the board but will have zero EIG if the size of the blue ship is already known." This creates confusion about when the feature should be applied and whether it's properly distinguishing between truly uninformative questions and questions that are informative in a specific context.

4. **Overstated claims about novelty:** The paper claims to provide "the first predictive model to date of open-ended human question asking," but this is questionable given prior work on question generation (e.g., [16, 23, 11] cited in the paper). The model is domain-specific (Battleship game) and doesn't generalize to more complex question-asking scenarios.

5. **Insufficient statistical validation:** The paper reports a Spearman's correlation of 0.64 between predicted and actual question frequencies but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for this correlation. The bootstrapping results (100% of samples for complexity-agnostic model) are mentioned but not contextualized with effect sizes or confidence intervals.

6. **Formatting and presentation issues:** The paper contains several formatting inconsistencies: "st Conference" in the header, inconsistent spacing around parentheses in program examples (e.g., "(size Blue)" vs. "(size Blue)"), and minor grammatical errors (e.g., "the subtle differences between contexts that determine if a question is potentially useful along with the open-ended nature of human question asking").

7. **Unjustified domain limitations:** The paper acknowledges that "some aspects of our grammar are specific to the Battleship domain" but doesn't provide a clear path to generalization. The claim that "many aspects of our grammar are domain general" is not substantiated with examples of how these general constructs would transfer to other domains.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents an interesting formalism for modeling human question asking, the technical flaws and unverified claims significantly undermine its contribution. The missing supplementary tables and inconsistent treatment of key methodological elements are particularly concerning for a NIPS submission, which should provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The paper's claims about novelty and significance are overstated relative to the limited scope of the evaluation (Battleship game only). 

The minor flaws identified above collectively represent a substantial issue for the paper's scientific rigor. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these technical shortcomings outweigh the paper's interesting premise and moderate empirical results. Therefore, I recommend rejection.

**Summary of Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Novel formalism for modeling questions as programs, elegant grammar-based approach, reasonable empirical results on a specific task.
*Con:* Missing critical technical details (supplementary tables), unverified claim about grammar completeness, overstated novelty claims, insufficient statistical validation, domain-specific limitations not properly addressed.
