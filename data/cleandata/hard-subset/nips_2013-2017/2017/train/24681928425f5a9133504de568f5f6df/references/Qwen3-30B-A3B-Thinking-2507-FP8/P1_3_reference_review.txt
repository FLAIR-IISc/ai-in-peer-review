

# Peer Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that, when executed on a world state, produce answers. The authors argue that questions are compositional (built from conceptual building blocks) and computable (evaluable on possible world states), framing question asking as program synthesis. They collect a dataset of 605 natural language questions from 40 human players in a Battleship-like information-search game, where participants asked questions to identify ship configurations. The authors develop a probabilistic model using features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (grammar-based length), answer type, and relevance to predict human question frequencies. The model outperforms ablated variants and generates novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

This work builds on prior research in active learning (which typically queries for labels), visual question generation (which requires large image-question pairs), and cognitive science (compositional semantics), while addressing the gap in modeling goal-directed, context-sensitive question asking.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a well-structured probabilistic model and appropriate evaluation methodology. The authors clearly define their approach to modeling questions as programs, develop a grammar for question generation, and implement a log-linear model with features that capture key aspects of human question asking. Their evaluation uses leave-one-out cross-validation across 16 contexts, with rigorous comparison of the full model against ablated versions (information-agnostic, complexity-agnostic, type-agnostic). The model's ability to predict human question frequencies (Spearman correlation of 0.64) and generate novel, meaningful questions demonstrates strong empirical support. The authors thoughtfully acknowledge limitations, including the domain-specific nature of their grammar and the need for future work to bridge the gap between program representations and natural language.

## Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The core concepts (compositionality, computability) are well-explained with concrete examples. The technical details of the grammar and probabilistic model are presented clearly, though some readers might benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the EIG calculation. The figures (e.g., Figure 2 showing correlation between predicted energy and human question frequencies) effectively illustrate key findings. The supplementary materials provide necessary details on grammar rules and features.

## Originality
This work makes a significant original contribution by modeling question asking as program generation. While prior work has explored question answering as program execution [24, 12] and visual question generation [16, 23], none have framed question *asking* as program synthesis with compositional, context-sensitive generation. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from goal-directed dialog systems (which use canned questions) and neural VQG systems (which require large datasets), showing that their model can generate novel questions without extensive training data. The specific application to the Battleship game dataset provides a controlled setting to test their hypothesis about human question asking, which is a novel contribution to computational cognitive science.

## Significance
The paper addresses a fundamental aspect of human cognition—question asking—that has been largely overlooked in machine learning. By modeling this as program generation, the work provides a framework for developing AI systems that can ask richer, more human-like questions. The ability to generate novel, contextually relevant questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") demonstrates the model's potential for more capable active learning systems. While the current work is domain-specific, the framework could be extended to other domains with appropriate grammar engineering. The paper advances the state of the art in modeling human cognition with computational approaches, and the results provide a foundation for future work on human-like question asking in AI.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical framework (questions as programs)
- Well-designed dataset and evaluation methodology
- Strong empirical results showing model effectiveness
- Demonstrated ability to generate novel human-like questions
- Clear comparison with ablated models to isolate key features

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited to a single domain (Battleship game), though the authors acknowledge this limitation
- Does not address the natural language generation aspect (program representations vs. text)
- The grammar contains domain-specific elements (e.g., ship colors, sizes)
- No discussion of computational complexity for larger domains

## Overall Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking. The framework is theoretically grounded, technically sound, and empirically validated. While the current implementation is domain-specific, the approach represents a promising direction for future work on human-like question asking in AI systems. The work is well-positioned to influence both cognitive science and machine learning research.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. The authors have made a strong contribution to the field that will likely inspire future work on question asking in AI. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation
- Strong empirical validation with human data
- Demonstrated ability to generate novel, human-like questions
- Clear theoretical grounding in compositionality and computability
- Rigorous comparison with ablated models

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Domain-specific implementation (though this is acknowledged as a limitation)
- Limited to program representations rather than natural language text
- No demonstration of scalability to more complex domains

The paper's contribution is substantial and well-executed, and the limitations are appropriately acknowledged. This work will be of interest to researchers in machine learning, cognitive science, and natural language processing.
