This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as programs that, when executed, yield answers. The core idea builds on the observation that human questions are often more informative than simple label requests in active learning and leverages compositionality and computability to represent questions. The authors evaluate their approach on a dataset of questions asked during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating the model’s ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper is strongly related to work in active learning (Settles, 2009), where the goal is to strategically query an oracle to improve learning efficiency. However, it departs from traditional approaches by focusing on the *richness* of questions, rather than just requesting labels. It also connects to research in dialog systems (Serban et al., 2017; Strub et al., 2017), particularly goal-directed dialog, but argues that existing systems lack the flexibility and creativity of human question asking.  The work draws inspiration from cognitive science (Fodor, 1975; Marcus, 1988; Piantadosi et al., 2016) in its emphasis on compositionality and a “language of thought.” Finally, the paper relates to work in visual question generation (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016) but highlights the limitations of data-hungry deep learning approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of modeling questions as programs is a significant departure from conventional active learning and dialog systems. It provides a principled framework for reasoning about the informativeness and complexity of questions.
* **Technical Soundness:** The probabilistic model appears well-defined, and the use of a grammar to constrain the space of possible questions is a clever way to address the combinatorial explosion of natural language. The optimization procedure, although complex, is clearly described.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results demonstrate that the model can reasonably predict human question frequencies and generate plausible, novel questions. The ablation studies, comparing the full model to variants without key features, provide valuable insights into the importance of different components.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core ideas are clearly explained, and the connection to related work is effectively established. The discussion of the limitations is also appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge the domain-specificity of some aspects of the grammar, it's unclear how easily this approach would generalize to other tasks. The Battleship game provides a constrained environment that simplifies the problem of question asking.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** The implementation appears quite complex, with numerous parameters and design choices. This could make it difficult for other researchers to reproduce and extend the work.
* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is primarily focused on a single dataset. Additional experiments on other tasks or with different human participants would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Feature Engineering:** The features used in the model (informativeness, complexity, answer type) rely on heuristics and assumptions that could be refined. A more principled approach to feature selection or learning would be beneficial.
* **Importance Sampling:** The reliance on importance sampling introduces approximation errors, and the sensitivity of the results to the quality of the proposal distribution is not thoroughly investigated.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to modeling human question asking.
* The technical contributions are solid, and the empirical results are promising.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the limitations of the work.
* The work opens up new avenues for research in active learning and dialog systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The domain-specificity of the approach raises concerns about its generalizability.
* The complexity of the implementation may limit its adoption by other researchers.
* The evaluation is somewhat limited, and further experiments are needed to confirm the robustness of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The authors have made a compelling case for their approach of modeling questions as programs. While there are some limitations, the novelty, technical soundness, and empirical validation make this a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the concerns raised above. Specifically, the authors should discuss the challenges of generalizing the approach to other domains and provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the importance sampling procedure.
