- Summary: Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs and a probabilistic generative model predicting question frequencies using EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance on a Battleship-like dataset.

- Strength: Theoretical contribution — reframes questions as programs, capturing compositionality and computability, linking to language-of-thought ideas.

- Strength: Methodological rigor — well-constructed probabilistic model with EIG and complexity features; strong correlation with human frequencies (Spearman ρ = 0.64).

- Strength: Empirical validation — quantitative and qualitative evaluations plus ablation studies showing complexity features are crucial.

- Strength: Novelty — addresses context-sensitive, goal-directed question asking via program generation beyond prior canned or VQG approaches.

- Weakness: Domain specificity — evaluation limited to a single Battleship domain, leaving generalization to other domains unclear.

- Weakness: Natural language gap — operates on semantic programs without adequately addressing interfacing to natural language generation.

- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope — single small dataset (18 contexts, 40 participants); more datasets or human evals needed.

- Weakness: Feature design concerns — use of an ad hoc filter feature (f∅) lacks sufficient justification.

- Overall assessment: Strong, technically sound contribution with clear advances in modeling human question asking despite narrow domain scope.

- Recommendation: Accept.

- Pro for acceptance: Strong theoretical foundation, clear methodology, novel contribution, and well-supported empirical results.

- Con for acceptance: Restricted to one domain and requires substantial domain-specific engineering.
