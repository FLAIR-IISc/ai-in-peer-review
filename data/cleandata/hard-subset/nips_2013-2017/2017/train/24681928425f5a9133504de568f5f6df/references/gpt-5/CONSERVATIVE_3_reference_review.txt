Summary
This paper proposes a computational framework for question asking that represents questions as programs in a small LISP-like language. A PCFG defines the space of well-typed programs, and a log-linear model over program features (expected information gain, complexity via PCFG-derived length, answer type, and a relevance filter) assigns probabilities to questions conditioned on context. The informativeness feature is computed using a Bayesian ideal observer over Battleship-like board configurations, and model parameters are learned by approximate maximum likelihood using importance sampling. Evaluation uses a dataset of 605 human-typed questions across 16 contexts (2 dropped due to computational issues) from a prior study. The model is compared to lesion ablations (removing informativeness, complexity, or type features) via leave-one-context-out likelihood and Spearman correlations; qualitative samples of “novel” questions are also provided.

Strengths
- Clear and interesting conceptual framing: questions-as-programs is a natural way to tie compositionality, computability, and answer types to a utility-based learner.
- The modeling choices connect to established ideas (EIG, PCFG complexity penalties, log-linear modeling), making the approach interpretable and analyzable.
- The work targets an underexplored aspect of active learning: rich query spaces beyond labels.
- Some predictive success: the full model outperforms its own ablations and achieves moderate rank correlations with human frequencies.
- The qualitative samples illustrate compositional generalization beyond the training set.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope and data scale: The entire evaluation is confined to a single, small, highly constrained domain (Battleship), with 605 questions, and two contexts dropped for computation. It is unclear how the approach would scale to realistic tasks or more open domains. The dataset is sparse (many singletons), limiting statistical power.
- Heavy domain engineering: The grammar, primitives, and semantics are tailored to the task, and the mapping from natural language to programs is not learned. This raises concerns about generality. The paper acknowledges this but does not demonstrate transfer to another domain.
- Baselines: Comparisons are only to ablations of the proposed model. There are no external baselines such as: (i) a grammar-only maximum entropy model without EIG; (ii) a rule-probability PCFG trained to fit human question distributions; (iii) RSA-style utility-cost models closer to Hawkins et al. (2015); (iv) simple heuristics (e.g., information gain under a constrained question template set) or neural sequence models trained over question templates. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to the core ideas rather than feature design.
- Approximation quality and reproducibility: The learning relies on importance sampling from the PCFG to approximate expectations and the partition function. There are no diagnostics (ESS, variance, bias analysis), and the proposal q is uniform over rules, which can mismatch the learned distribution. The paper reports 100k iterations and 150k samples but gives no runtime, convergence curves, or sensitivity to the sampler. Two contexts were excluded due to computational cost, highlighting scalability issues.
- Metric choice and statistical analysis: The main quantitative results are approximated log-likelihoods and Spearman correlations. Uncertainty is handled only via bootstrapping Z; there are no confidence intervals on correlations, and the absolute likelihood values are hard to interpret. The reported average Spearman 0.64 is modest and roughly matched by an information-agnostic model (0.65), which weakens the claim that informativeness is crucial.
- Complexity feature: Using −log q(x) under a uniform PCFG as a complexity proxy is ad hoc and may reflect grammar idiosyncrasies rather than human cognitive cost. No comparison to alternative cost measures (program length, number of functions, description length) is provided.
- Novelty analysis: The “novel” questions are evaluated qualitatively, with equivalence defined via MI over answer partitions and argument symmetry. The MI threshold and priors are not specified, and some equivalences (e.g., replacing Blue with Red) may not hold uniformly across contexts. No human evaluation of novelty/quality is reported.
- Polishing issues: References are incomplete or malformed in several places, and some details crucial to reproduction are deferred to supplementary material (e.g., full grammar, hypothesis space sizes).

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but the empirical validation is limited in scope, uses approximations without diagnostics, and lacks strong baselines. The claims about informativeness being important are not conclusively supported given the ablation correlations.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well written. However, key computational details (importance sampling diagnostics, hypothesis space sizes per context, runtime) are missing; the references need fixing; and claims such as “first predictive model of open-ended human question asking” should be tempered and better situated relative to prior work.

Originality
Representing questions as programs and learning a utility-weighted distribution over a PCFG is a sensible synthesis of known ideas. The conceptual framing is appealing, but much of the machinery (EIG, PCFG, log-linear features) is standard. The originality lies in applying this combination to question generation in a controlled domain.

Significance
Given the narrow domain, handcrafted grammar, and limited scale, the current significance for the NeurIPS audience is modest. The work is more a proof-of-concept than a general, scalable algorithm for question asking.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the results to the proposal q and sample size? Please report ESS, variance estimates, and convergence behavior.
- What is the size of the hypothesis space per context, and how does EIG computation scale? Can you process the two excluded contexts with more efficient inference (e.g., pruning, MCMC over hypotheses)?
- How do results change if complexity is measured by program length or number of function applications instead of −log q(x)?
- Can you include an external baseline (e.g., a learned PCFG without EIG, an RSA-style model, or a simple neural template model) to better contextualize gains?
- What MI threshold defines equivalence for “novel” questions? Does the Blue/Red substitution truly preserve partitions across contexts?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger baselines and uncertainty reporting; include at least one additional domain to assess generality.
- Provide diagnostics for the approximate learning procedure and sensitivity analyses for feature choices.
- Consider a small semantic parsing component to connect natural language to programs, even if weakly supervised.
- Fix and complete references; move key details from SI into the main text or ensure SI is thorough and accessible.

Pros
- Clear, principled framing with interpretable components.
- Demonstrates compositional generalization within a constrained domain.
- Some predictive gains over ablations; thoughtful discussion of limitations.

Cons
- Small, single-domain study with heavy hand engineering.
- Missing strong baselines and approximation diagnostics.
- Scalability and generality not demonstrated.
- Modest quantitative improvements; some claims not fully supported.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. Interesting idea with potential, but current empirical validation and generality fall short of NeurIPS standards. A stronger version with additional domains, baselines, and more rigorous inference analysis would be compelling.
