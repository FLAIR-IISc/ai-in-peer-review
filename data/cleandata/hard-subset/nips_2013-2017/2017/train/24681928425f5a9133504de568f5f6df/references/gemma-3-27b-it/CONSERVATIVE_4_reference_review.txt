This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea – representing questions as executable programs that, when evaluated against a world state, yield an answer – is an interesting attempt to formalize the often-complex cognitive process of inquiry. The authors connect this to active learning and, crucially, attempt validation against a dataset of human questions collected in a game context. While the idea has merit, the presentation suffers from a lack of rigorous evaluation and a reliance on a relatively narrow domain.

The strengths of the paper lie in its conceptual framing. The analogy to program synthesis is insightful, and the attempt to move beyond simple label-requesting active learning is commendable. The grammar developed to represent questions appears sufficient for the chosen domain, and the incorporation of features like informativeness and complexity is a step in the right direction. The demonstration that the model can predict frequencies of human questions and generate novel, plausible questions is encouraging.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the evaluation is not convincing. While the authors report improved log-likelihood compared to ablated models, the improvements are not dramatic and the reliance on leave-one-out cross-validation raises concerns about overfitting. The dataset itself, while valuable, is limited to a specific game and might not generalize to other question-asking scenarios. The comparison to existing active learning methods is superficial. Simply stating that they “lack the representational capacity” is insufficient; a quantitative comparison, even on this limited domain, is needed to demonstrate a clear advantage. The claim of generating "human-like" questions is subjective and not rigorously supported. The examples provided are plausible but lack a systematic evaluation of their quality beyond anecdotal observation.

A significant concern is the strong reliance on domain knowledge (the Battleship game rules) and the bespoke grammar. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't adequately address the scalability of their approach to more complex or open-ended domains. The "knowledge engineering" cost of creating a similar grammar for a different task is unclear. Furthermore, the probabilistic model, while theoretically sound, relies on several approximations (importance sampling) whose impact on the results is not thoroughly investigated. The description of the importance sampling procedure is brief and leaves open questions about its sensitivity to parameter choices. The connection to existing work on program synthesis, beyond a cursory mention, feels underdeveloped. The potential benefits of leveraging more sophisticated program synthesis techniques are not explored.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of question asking as program generation.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict human question frequencies (albeit modestly).
*   Initial evidence of generating plausible, novel questions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited domain and potential lack of generalizability.
*   Weak evaluation and susceptibility to overfitting.
*   Insufficient comparison to established active learning methods.
*   High reliance on domain knowledge and bespoke grammar.
*   Lack of rigorous analysis of approximation techniques used.
*   Superficial connection to relevant literature in program synthesis.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of demonstrating its practical significance or robustness. The evaluation is inadequate, and the reliance on a specific domain limits its broader impact. While the conceptual framing is promising, more rigorous experimentation and a more thorough comparison to existing methods are needed to justify acceptance into NIPS. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to expand the evaluation, address the limitations of the current approach, and explore its applicability to more challenging domains. The authors should focus on quantitative evaluations and comparisons to baselines, and provide a more compelling argument for the advantages of their program-generation framework.
