Summary
This paper proposes a computational framework for question asking that treats questions as executable programs over possible world states. A typed, LISP-like, lambda-calculus-based grammar defines a compositional space of question programs. A log-linear generative model over this space scores questions using features for informativeness (expected information gain under a Bayesian ideal observer), complexity (negative log PCFG probability), answer type, and task relevance. Parameters are fit by (approximate) maximum likelihood using importance sampling from the grammar as a proposal. Evaluation uses a dataset of 605 natural-language questions asked by humans in an information-search “Battleship” game across 18 contexts (16 used for modeling), with leave-one-context-out fits. The model predicts the distribution of human questions better than ablations that remove informativeness, complexity, or type features, and can sample novel, human-like questions.

Relation to prior work is well-drawn: active learning typically queries labels or simple interventions; dialog systems and VQG generate questions but lack goal-directed, compositional semantics; prior work models question meaning as partitions and uses programs for question answering/visual reasoning. The novelty here is to generate questions (not just answer them) by composing executable semantic programs and optimizing a utility over hypotheses.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling choices are sensible and grounded in prior cognitive/compositional modeling. The log-linear formulation with hand-engineered features is appropriate for small data; EIG is well-defined via Bayesian belief updating. However, several aspects would benefit from more rigor:
  - Approximate inference/learning: Both Z and expected feature values are estimated via importance sampling using a uniform PCFG proposal. It is unclear whether the 150k samples are drawn once or re-sampled each iteration; either way, proposal mismatch may yield high variance. Convergence diagnostics, sensitivity to sample size, and stability across random seeds are not reported.
  - Dataset preprocessing: Natural language questions are “translated into programs,” but the annotation process, inter-annotator agreement, and error rates are not described. This step is critical to the empirical claims.
  - Computational limits: Two contexts with large hypothesis spaces were dropped, and 14 questions using a probabilistic draw operator were removed. These omissions may bias evaluation and underscore scaling challenges.
  - Contribution of informativeness: Although the full model achieves higher (approximate) held-out log-likelihood than the information-agnostic variant in 81% of bootstrap samples, the rank-correlation with human frequencies is slightly worse on average (0.64 vs. 0.65). This weakens the claim that EIG, as implemented, is essential to predicting human preferences (complexity appears far more decisive; the complexity-agnostic model performs poorly).
- Experiments: The quantitative evaluation is limited to a single domain with a small, sparse dataset. Novel question generation is evaluated qualitatively without human judgments.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with good motivation and background. The description of the grammar and features is understandable, though it relies on supplementary tables for full specification.
- Missing details reduce reproducibility: the NL-to-program annotation protocol, exact sampling/training procedure, and code availability. It would help to report learned feature weights and their variability, and to show examples of semantically equivalent program forms and how equivalence classes are handled systematically.

Originality
- Modeling question asking as program generation, and learning a generative model over question programs guided by EIG, is a novel combination of ideas from LoT, active learning, and semantic parsing/program induction. While programs as question meaning are known, the focus on generating questions that align with human behavior is original and interesting.

Significance
- Conceptually, the work opens a path toward rich, goal-directed question generation beyond labels, with potential impact in active learning and human-AI interaction.
- Practically, significance is tempered by domain specificity (Battleship-specific predicates and structures), reliance on manual semantic representations, and computational scalability limits. Without demonstrations in additional domains or with an NL interface, broader utility remains speculative.
- Nevertheless, the ablation results convincingly show the importance of complexity and type, offering a useful insight for future question generation systems.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated formulation of questions as compositional programs.
- Integrates a rational utility (EIG) with a learned prior over programs, yielding human-like question distributions.
- Ablations isolate the roles of informativeness, complexity, and answer type; strong evidence that complexity constraints are crucial.
- Generates novel, sensible questions; dataset is public.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical scope: one small, domain-specific dataset; two contexts and some questions removed for tractability.
- Critical annotation step (NL-to-program) is under-specified; potential bias not quantified.
- Approximate learning/inference details are thin; no variance, sensitivity, or convergence analysis.
- Weak quantitative evidence that EIG improves rank ordering over a complexity- and type-driven prior.
- No human study assessing the quality/utility of sampled novel questions; few comparisons to alternative generative baselines (e.g., template induction, retrieval+composition).

Suggestions for improvement
- Describe and release the NL-to-program annotation protocol; report inter-annotator agreement.
- Provide robustness analyses: multiple random seeds, proposal variants, sample sizes, and convergence diagnostics; report feature weights with confidence intervals.
- Compare against stronger baselines (e.g., template extraction/composition from training questions, retrieval with minimal adaptation).
- Add human evaluations of generated questions’ usefulness and naturalness.
- Explore cost-sensitive utilities (explicit length/answerer cost) and/or amortized inference to improve scalability.
- Demonstrate transfer to at least one additional domain or include an end-to-end NL interface.

Overall assessment
A thoughtful and original contribution at the intersection of active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive modeling. The paper is clear and technically sound in core ideas, but empirical validation is narrow and some key methodological details are missing. I view this as a borderline accept: conceptually strong and potentially impactful, but strengthened by deeper evaluation and clearer methodology.
