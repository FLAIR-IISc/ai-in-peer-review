**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling goal-directed question asking by framing it as a program generation task. The central idea is that questions can be represented as executable programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. The authors develop a probabilistic model over this program space, defined by a log-linear model whose features include the program's expected information gain (EIG), its complexity (derived from a probabilistic context-free grammar), and its answer type. The model's parameters are fit to a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game. The authors evaluate their approach by its ability to predict the frequency distribution of human questions in held-out game contexts and by qualitatively assessing novel questions generated by the model. The work is positioned as an alternative to traditional active learning methods, which are limited to simple queries, and to large-scale neural language models, which often lack explicit goal-directedness and compositionality.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically interesting, but its claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous empirical validation against established methods.

*   **Strengths:** The proposed probabilistic model is well-defined. The use of features like EIG to capture informativeness and a PCFG-based prior to penalize complexity is a principled way to balance competing desiderata in question asking. The ablation study (Table 1) provides some evidence for the contribution of each feature set within their proposed framework.
*   **Weaknesses:** The methodological soundness is undermined by several key issues.
    1.  **Hand-Engineered Grammar:** The entire framework rests on a manually designed grammar specific to the Battleship domain. The expressiveness and success of the model are therefore heavily dependent on this significant feat of knowledge engineering. It is not clear how much of the performance is attributable to the probabilistic learning framework versus the carefully curated grammar that defines the search space. The authors' claim that many grammar components are "domain general" is not substantiated. This reliance on a bespoke grammar makes the approach appear more like a specialized expert system than a generalizable learning method.
    2.  **Lack of Baselines:** The paper critically lacks any comparison to established machine learning baselines. The "alternative models" presented are merely ablations of the proposed model. A thorough evaluation would require comparing against standard generative models, even if they require adaptation. For instance, a conditional language model (e.g., a fine-tuned GPT-2 or a smaller Transformer-based model) could be trained to generate question text conditioned on a serialized representation of the game context. While the authors argue such models are not goal-directed, they would provide a crucial performance baseline for the task of predicting human questions. Without such a comparison, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed complex machinery offers a tangible advantage over simpler, more established approaches.
    3.  **Limited Evaluation:** The model is evaluated on a single, small dataset (605 questions) from a highly constrained, symbolic, toy-world domain. The claim that this represents a step towards "more capable active learning machines" is a significant overstatement given the evidence. The evaluation metrics (log-likelihood and Spearman correlation on sparse data) are reasonable but not entirely convincing without baselines. The qualitative examples of generated questions are interesting but are likely cherry-picked and do not constitute rigorous evidence of the model's capabilities.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the core concepts are explained clearly. However, crucial details are omitted from the main text. The grammar, which is the cornerstone of the entire method, is relegated to supplementary material. Furthermore, the process of translating the raw natural language questions from the dataset into their programmatic representations for training is not described. This is a non-trivial step that likely involved significant manual annotation, and its omission hides a major practical limitation of the approach.

**Originality:** The core idea of modeling questions as programs is novel and provides an interesting, structured alternative to the dominant end-to-end neural paradigm. It elegantly integrates principles from programming languages, cognitive science, and Bayesian inference. This conceptual contribution is the paper's main strength.

**Significance:** The significance of the work for the NIPS community is questionable. While the problem is important, the proposed solution seems impractical and not scalable. The heavy reliance on a hand-crafted grammar and a fully specified symbolic world-state limits its applicability to real-world problems where such priors are unavailable. Most modern active learning research focuses on methods that can operate on high-dimensional, unstructured data (e.g., images, text) without extensive domain-specific engineering. This work moves in the opposite direction. Consequently, it is unlikely that practitioners or researchers outside the niche of computational cognitive modeling will build upon this specific method. The contribution feels more aligned with a cognitive science venue than a machine learning conference like NIPS, where generalizability, scalability, and performance against state-of-the-art baselines are paramount.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  Presents a novel and intellectually stimulating conceptual framework for question asking, combining program synthesis with probabilistic modeling.
2.  The model is interpretable and explicitly captures principles like compositionality and goal-directed information seeking.
3.  The approach successfully models human data in a constrained domain, providing a good proof of concept.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

1.  The method relies critically on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar, making it un-scalable and of limited practical use for general machine learning problems.
2.  The paper fails to compare its model against any established machine learning baselines, making it impossible to evaluate the method's effectiveness relative to prior art.
3.  The evaluation is conducted on a single, small, toy-world dataset, and the claims of advancing active learning are not well-supported by the limited experiments.
4.  The contribution appears more suited for a cognitive science audience, with limited demonstrated impact on advancing machine learning methodology in a generalizable way.

**Conclusion:**

While the paper presents an original and elegant idea, it feels more like a position paper or a proof-of-concept study than a complete piece of work ready for NIPS. The reliance on heavy knowledge engineering and the stark lack of baseline comparisons are significant weaknesses. The approach is not shown to be a viable alternative to established methods in any practical sense. Unless the authors can provide evidence of the method's utility beyond this single toy domain and rigorously compare it against standard generative baselines, the paper falls short of the standards for acceptance.
