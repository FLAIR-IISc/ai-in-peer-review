### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional language (akin to LISP). When executed on a possible world state, a program returns an answer. This recasts the problem of deciding what question to ask as a problem of program synthesis. The authors develop a probabilistic generative model over the space of possible programs, defined by a context-free grammar. The model's probability distribution is log-linear, with an energy function defined by a weighted sum of features, including the question's expected information gain (EIG), its complexity (length), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number), and its relevance to the task.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game, where the goal is to ask a question to resolve ambiguity about hidden objects on a grid. The authors demonstrate that their full model, which balances informativeness and complexity, successfully predicts the frequencies of human questions in held-out game contexts. Furthermore, the model is shown to be generative, capable of synthesizing novel, contextually relevant, and human-like questions that were not observed in the training data. This work positions itself as a significant step beyond traditional active learning, which typically uses simple label queries, and data-driven dialogue systems, which often lack goal-directed intentionality or require massive datasets. It draws connections to work in cognitive science on compositionality and "language of thought" models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed probabilistic model is well-founded, combining established concepts like program induction, log-linear models, and expected information gain in a coherent framework. The experimental design is sound, using leave-one-out cross-validation to test predictive performance. A key strength is the ablation study (lesioning different feature groups), which convincingly demonstrates that all components of the model—informativeness, complexity, and answer type—are necessary to explain the human data. The finding that the "complexity-agnostic" model performs very poorly is a particularly strong result, highlighting that humans do not simply maximize information gain but trade it off against the simplicity of the query. The authors are honest about the limitations of their work, particularly its reliance on a hand-engineered grammar for a specific domain. This is a complete and well-executed piece of research.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, situating the work within the broader context of active learning and AI. The model is described in a clear, step-by-step manner, from the grammar to the feature definitions and the optimization procedure. The use of concrete examples (e.g., `(size Blue)`) greatly aids understanding. The figures, particularly Figure 3 which shows generated novel questions, are highly effective at illustrating the model's capabilities. The paper provides enough detail that an expert could likely reproduce the core results, although the specifics of the grammar and the importance sampling setup would require the supplementary material.

**Originality:**
The central idea of framing question asking as probabilistic program generation is highly original and constitutes a significant conceptual advance. While individual components like grammars for concept representation (Piantadosi et al., 2012) and program synthesis for question answering (Liang et al.) have appeared in prior work, this paper is novel in applying these ideas to the *generation* of goal-directed questions in an active learning context. It successfully bridges the gap between the simple queries of classic active learning and the unconstrained nature of large language models, offering a structured, interpretable, and goal-oriented alternative. The synthesis of Bayesian inference (for EIG), program induction (for the question space), and probabilistic modeling (for fitting to human data) is a novel and powerful combination.

**Significance:**
The work is highly significant for several reasons.
1.  **For Active Learning:** It presents a compelling path forward for creating more sophisticated active learning agents that can ask richer, more efficient questions, moving beyond simple label requests.
2.  **For Human-AI Interaction:** It provides a computational basis for machines that can understand and generate questions in a flexible and intentional way, which is crucial for collaborative tasks.
3.  **For Cognitive Science:** It offers a strong, predictive computational model of a fundamental human cognitive capacity—inquiry. The finding that people balance informativeness and complexity provides a quantitative confirmation of an intuitive principle.
The ability of the model to generate creative and clever questions not seen in the data is particularly impressive and suggests that this framework captures some of the generative capacity of human thought. While the current implementation is domain-specific, the core framework is general and likely to inspire future research exploring how such grammars could be learned or adapted to new domains.

The main weakness, as acknowledged by the authors, is the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar for the Battleship domain. This limits the immediate applicability of the system to new problems and raises questions about scalability. It is unclear how much engineering effort would be required to port this framework to a substantially different or more open-ended domain. However, as a proof of concept and a new research direction, this limitation does not detract from the paper's core contribution.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** The core contribution—modeling question asking as program generation—is a powerful and original idea with high potential impact for both machine learning and cognitive science.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The probabilistic model is well-conceived, and the experimental evaluation, including the ablation studies, is rigorous and convincing.
*   **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex set of ideas accessible and compelling.
*   **Impressive Results:** The model not only predicts human question frequencies but also generates novel, meaningful questions, demonstrating true generative capability beyond simple imitation.
*   **Interdisciplinary Bridge:** The work effectively bridges concepts from active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive modeling, making it a perfect fit for the broad NIPS audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   **Domain Specificity:** The model's performance hinges on a hand-crafted grammar tailored to a single, constrained task. This raises valid concerns about the generalizability and scalability of the approach without significant domain engineering.
*   **Limited Quantitative Performance:** While the model outperforms the lesioned versions, the reported average Spearman correlation of 0.64 is solid but not overwhelmingly strong, suggesting room for improvement in the predictive model itself.

**Overall Recommendation:**
The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. The paper introduces a highly novel, significant, and elegant framework for a fundamental problem in intelligence. While the current implementation is domain-specific, this is acceptable for a paper that is establishing a new research direction. The quality of the work, the clarity of the presentation, and the impressiveness of the generative results make this a standout contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
