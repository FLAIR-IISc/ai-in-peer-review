

# Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that can be executed on possible world states to generate answers. The authors argue that previous work on active learning and AI question asking has focused on limited query types (e.g., label requests or canned questions), while humans ask rich, context-sensitive questions that are compositional and informative. They model questions as programs in a simple programming language (based on lambda calculus and LISP), where each program corresponds to a natural language question that evaluates to an answer (e.g., "How long is the blue ship?" becomes (size Blue)). Their probabilistic generative model uses features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (program length), answer type, and relevance to predict which questions humans will ask in a Battleship-style game. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete, well-executed study. The authors provide a detailed description of their grammar for generating questions as programs, the probabilistic model with well-defined features, and a thorough evaluation methodology. The experimental design includes cross-validation, ablation studies to isolate the importance of different features, and qualitative analysis of generated questions. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., domain-specific grammar, need for domain knowledge), demonstrating appropriate honesty about their work's scope.

The quantitative evaluation shows strong results: the full model with all features outperforms ablated versions in predicting human question frequencies (with a log-likelihood advantage over complexity-agnostic models in 100% of bootstrap samples). The qualitative generation of novel, contextually appropriate questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") demonstrates the model's ability to produce human-like questions.

One minor quality concern: The paper doesn't thoroughly address how well the model would generalize to domains beyond the Battleship game, though the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion. The grammar is specifically designed for this domain, and while they mention some domain-general constructs (logical connectives, set operations), it's unclear how much new knowledge engineering would be required to adapt to new domains.

## Clarity

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively establishes the problem and the authors' novel perspective. The related work section thoughtfully distinguishes their approach from previous work on active learning, AI dialog systems, and visual question generation. The technical description of the grammar and probabilistic model is precise, though some parts (like the grammar rewrite rules) might be dense for readers unfamiliar with program synthesis.

The paper uses good figures (e.g., Figure 2 showing correlation between model predictions and human question frequencies) to illustrate key points. The supplementary materials are referenced appropriately, though it's slightly inconvenient that key tables (SI-1, SI-2) are in supplementary materials rather than the main text.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution. The core idea of modeling questions as executable programs is novel in the context of active learning and question asking. Previous work has either focused on narrow query types (e.g., label requests), required large datasets (e.g., VQG datasets), or lacked the compositional structure that humans use. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work in Section 2 and provides a convincing argument for why program-based representations are necessary for modeling rich human question asking.

The authors also make a novel contribution to cognitive science by formalizing how people balance informativeness with complexity when generating questions. While there's prior work on program synthesis (e.g., Lake et al. 2015 on concept learning), this is the first predictive model of open-ended human question asking that captures compositionality.

## Significance

The significance of this work lies in its potential to bridge the gap between human-like question asking and machine learning. By modeling questions as programs, the authors provide a framework that could enable AI systems to ask richer, more informative questions to improve learning performance, rather than just requesting labels. This has potential applications in educational technology, human-AI collaboration, and more natural interactive learning systems.

The work also makes a meaningful contribution to computational cognitive science by providing a formal model of how humans balance the trade-offs between question informativeness and complexity. This is a fundamental cognitive process that's not well-modeled by existing approaches.

The significance is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of the evaluation (Battleship game), but the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest future work to extend to other domains. The paper's framework could be influential even if the specific implementation is domain-specific, as it provides a new conceptual lens for modeling question asking.

## Strengths
- Novel framework for modeling question asking as program generation
- Strong theoretical grounding in compositionality and computability
- Comprehensive evaluation with both quantitative and qualitative results
- Clear ablation studies demonstrating the importance of each feature
- Successful generation of novel, human-like questions
- Thoughtful discussion of limitations and future work

## Weaknesses
- Limited scope to a specific game domain; unclear how easily it would transfer to other domains
- Grammar is highly domain-specific, requiring substantial knowledge engineering
- Doesn't address how to automatically generate the grammar for new domains
- Evaluation focuses on predicting question frequencies rather than whether the questions actually help in learning
- The connection to broader active learning literature could be strengthened (though they do cite relevant work)

## Recommendation
I recommend accepting this paper for NeurIPS. The work presents a novel, technically sound framework with strong empirical validation. The paper makes a significant contribution to both machine learning (by expanding active learning to richer query types) and cognitive science (by formalizing human question asking behavior). While the domain-specific nature of the grammar is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and provide a clear path forward for generalization.

The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and addresses a meaningful problem. The results are significant and could inspire future work on more human-like question asking in AI systems. The limitations are clearly articulated, and the authors provide a thoughtful discussion of how to extend the approach to new domains.

This work would be valuable to the NeurIPS community as it bridges cognitive science, machine learning, and natural language processing, and provides a new conceptual framework that could influence multiple areas of research.

## Final Score
8/10 (Strong Accept)

## Justification for Score
This paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NeurIPS submission. It's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), makes a significant original contribution (Originality), and addresses an important problem with potential broad impact (Significance). The main limitation is the narrow scope of the evaluation, but this is acknowledged by the authors and doesn't undermine the core contribution. The paper provides a novel framework that could influence future work on active learning and human-AI interaction, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
