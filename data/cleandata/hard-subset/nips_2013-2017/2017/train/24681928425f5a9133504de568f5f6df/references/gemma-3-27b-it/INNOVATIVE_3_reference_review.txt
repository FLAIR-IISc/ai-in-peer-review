This paper presents a fascinating and bold approach to modeling human question asking by framing it as program synthesis. The core idea – representing questions as executable programs that query a world state – is genuinely novel and offers a compelling perspective on how humans might strategically seek information. The authors successfully demonstrate that this framework can capture human question-asking behavior in a “Battleship”-like game, predicting question frequencies and even generating plausible novel questions. This is a significant step beyond existing active learning methods that typically rely on simpler query strategies.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The conceptual leap from questions to programs is insightful, and the probabilistic model, while complex, appears well-motivated and reasonably implemented. The authors demonstrate a strong connection to cognitive science and linguistics, grounding their approach in theories of compositionality and the language of thought. The empirical results, while not exhaustive, are promising and provide initial validation of the framework. The generated questions (Fig. 3) are remarkably human-like, suggesting the model is capturing something essential about how people formulate inquiries. 

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the grammar is expressive enough to represent the observed questions, it’s unclear how easily it would scale to more complex domains. The reliance on a specific game context (Battleship) raises questions about generalizability. The optimization process, while described, appears computationally intensive and the importance sampling approximation might introduce biases. The ablation study, while informative, could be strengthened by a more granular analysis of feature contributions. Furthermore, the connection between the "relevance" feature and the underlying cognitive process is not fully elucidated. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of operating on semantic representations rather than natural language directly, this is a substantial one. Finally, the model still relies on substantial domain knowledge encoded in the grammar – a criticism the authors acknowledge, but the extent to which this limits its broader applicability remains an open question.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The program synthesis framing of question asking is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from traditional active learning approaches.
*   **Bold approach:** The paper tackles a challenging problem – modeling the complexities of human inquiry – with a creative and ambitious solution.
*   **Promising results:** The empirical results demonstrate that the model can predict and generate human-like questions, providing initial validation of the framework.
*   **Interdisciplinary connection:** The work bridges cognitive science, linguistics, and machine learning, offering a valuable perspective on the problem of intelligent question asking.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Domain specificity:** The reliance on a specific game context (Battleship) limits the generalizability of the findings.
*   **Computational complexity:** The optimization process is computationally intensive and relies on approximations.
*   **Limited scalability:** The grammar may not scale easily to more complex domains.
*   **Dependence on domain knowledge:** The model requires substantial domain knowledge encoded in the grammar.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The core idea is exceptionally innovative and the initial results are promising. The authors are tackling a difficult problem with a bold and well-motivated approach. While further research is needed to address the limitations, the potential impact of this work is substantial. I lean strongly towards acceptance, prioritizing the novelty and potential of this research over methodological perfection. The paper doesn't offer a *complete* solution, but it opens up a compelling new direction for research in active learning and cognitive modeling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity of the optimization procedure and further discuss the limitations of domain knowledge dependency.
