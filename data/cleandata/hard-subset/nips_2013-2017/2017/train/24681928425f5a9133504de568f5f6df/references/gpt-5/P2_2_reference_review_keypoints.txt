- Proposal: models human question asking by treating questions as executable programs generated from a context-free grammar and scored by a log-linear model with EIG, complexity, answer-type, and relevance features.
- Inference: uses importance sampling over programs from a uniform PCFG to approximate expectations and fits parameters by maximizing likelihood on human questions from a Battleship-style task.
- Evaluation: assessed with leave-one-context-out prediction, correlation with human question frequencies, and qualitative generation of novel questions.
- Relation to prior work: combines ideas from active learning, program induction, and probabilistic languages of thought, focusing on question generation rather than answering or large-scale supervised VQG.
- Technical soundness: formalization is coherent and importance sampling is a reasonable approximation for the large program space.
- Empirical support: full model outperforms ablations in held-out log-likelihood and shows moderate correlation with human frequencies (avg Spearman ~0.64); generates plausible novel questions.
- Informativeness concern: EIG's marginal contribution is modest—an information-agnostic variant achieves similar or better correlation—weakening the claim that EIG drives human question choice.
- Domain limitation: evaluation is confined to a single toy domain (one game), with two contexts and 14 questions dropped, narrowing empirical generality.
- Importance sampling issues: proposal q is a uniform PCFG which may yield high-variance importance weights; unclear whether candidate pools are fixed or resampled and how sensitive results are to these choices.
- Semantic redundancy: many semantically equivalent programs exist in the space and lack of canonicalization could bias learning and evaluation.
- Complexity feature caveat: the −log q(x) complexity penalty depends on grammar design and may conflate syntactic priors with cognitive costs.
- Missing baselines: no comparisons to stronger alternative generators (e.g., EIG-only with length penalty, PCFG-only ranker, neural sequence models, or RSA-style pragmatic objectives).
- Clarity: paper is generally clear and well organized, but important details (full grammar, hypothesis-space definitions, sampling diagnostics) are deferred to the supplement.
- Originality: novel combination of executable-question grammars with information-theoretic utility and complexity priors for predicting human question distributions.
- Significance: an intriguing proof-of-concept relevant to NeurIPS audiences, but impact limited by toy domain, hand-engineered grammar, and scaling questions.
- Pros: principled executable-program formulation, interpretable probabilistic model, prediction and generation of human-like questions, dataset release, frank discussion of limitations.
- Cons: narrow empirical scope, modest added value of informativeness feature in some metrics, domain-specific hand-engineering, underexplored inference details and semantic equivalence, missing stronger baselines.
- Suggestions: report sensitivity to proposal q and pool size (effective sample sizes/convergence), add EIG-only and PCFG-only baselines and RSA-style comparisons, canonicalize equivalent programs, report absolute LLs with uncertainty and reconcile conflicting metrics, and test generality on another domain or grammar reuse.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, clearly written, and creative with worthwhile contributions but limited by scope and evaluation; recommendation: weak accept.
