### Summary

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed, human-like question asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, return an answer. This approach leverages the principles of compositionality and computability to define a rich, generative space of possible questions using a formal grammar. The authors propose a probabilistic log-linear model to predict which questions humans are likely to ask in a given context. The model's energy function is a weighted combination of several key features: the question's expected information gain (EIG), its complexity (length), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, number), and its relevance to the task.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions collected during an information-seeking game (a variant of "Battleship"). Through leave-one-out cross-validation, the full model demonstrates a strong ability to predict the frequency of human-asked questions, outperforming several ablated versions. The results highlight that human question asking is not driven solely by maximizing information gain but is a trade-off between informativeness and complexity. Qualitatively, the model is also shown to be capable of generating novel, contextually relevant, and "clever" questions that were not present in the human-generated dataset. This work connects ideas from active learning, program synthesis, and computational cognitive science, presenting a significant step towards machines that can learn by asking more sophisticated questions.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The central idea of modeling questions as programs is highly original and powerful. It provides a principled way to move beyond the simple label queries common in active learning and the canned or stereotyped questions generated by many dialogue systems. This reframing as program synthesis opens up a rich, compositional, and interpretable space for question generation.

2.  **Strong Connection to Cognitive Science:** The paper is well-grounded in cognitive science principles like compositionality and the "language of thought" hypothesis. The model's success in predicting human behavior by balancing informativeness (EIG) with complexity provides a compelling computational account of a key aspect of human inquiry. The finding that complexity is a critical factor, and that pure EIG is a poor predictor, is an important insight.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model from multiple angles, strengthening their claims.
    *   The quantitative evaluation using log-likelihood on held-out data with proper cross-validation is sound.
    *   The lesion studies are particularly effective, clearly demonstrating that each component of their model (informativeness, complexity, answer type) contributes positively to performance. The dramatic drop in performance for the complexity-agnostic model is a striking result.
    *   The qualitative analysis, showing the generation of novel and intelligent questions, effectively demonstrates the model's generative power and goes beyond simple prediction.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the model is described in a logical, step-by-step manner, and the results are presented compellingly. The authors are also commendably transparent about the model's limitations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reliance on a Hand-Crafted Grammar:** The primary limitation of this work is its dependence on a domain-specific, hand-engineered grammar. The primitives of the language (e.g., `size`, `touch`, ship colors) are tailored specifically to the Battleship game. While the authors argue that some components (logic, arithmetic) are general, the core semantic building blocks are not. This raises significant questions about the scalability and generalizability of the approach to new, more complex, or open-ended domains. The amount of expert knowledge and engineering required to define such a grammar for a new domain could be substantial.

2.  **Disconnection from Natural Language:** The model operates entirely on the level of semantic program representations. It bypasses the difficult problems of both semantic parsing (natural language to program) and natural language generation (program to natural language). While this is a reasonable simplification for an initial investigation, it means the system as presented is not an end-to-end question-asking agent. The authors acknowledge this, but it remains a major gap between the model and a practical application.

3.  **Computational Scalability:** The inference method relies on sampling a large number of programs to approximate the partition function and gradients. Furthermore, calculating EIG requires iterating over a hypothesis space of possible world configurations. The authors note they had to exclude two contexts from their analysis due to the computational complexity of the large hypothesis spaces. This suggests that the approach may face scalability challenges in domains with larger state spaces or more complex grammars.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is an excellent paper presenting a highly original and significant contribution. It tackles a fundamental and challenging problem—how to generate rich, goal-directed questions—with a novel and well-motivated approach. The "question asking as program generation" framework is a powerful conceptual advance over prior work in active learning and dialogue systems. The experimental results are strong, and the analysis provides valuable insights into the cognitive constraints (informativeness vs. complexity) that shape human inquiry.

While the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar is a notable limitation for immediate practical application, this work should be viewed as a foundational step that opens up a very promising new research direction. The clarity, originality, and potential impact of these ideas are high. The paper is technically sound, well-executed, and will likely inspire a great deal of follow-up work at the intersection of machine learning, NLP, and cognitive science. It is an ideal fit for NIPS and represents a significant advance in our understanding of intelligent information seeking.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Originality/Significance:** Proposes a novel and elegant framework for a fundamental AI problem. High potential to influence future work in active learning and HCI.
*   **Quality:** The probabilistic model is principled, and the evaluation is rigorous and multi-faceted.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Insight:** Provides a strong computational model of human question-asking, revealing the crucial trade-off between informativeness and complexity.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   **Generalizability:** The reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar is a significant limitation that hinders immediate applicability in new domains.
*   **Scope:** The model does not handle the natural language interface, which is a core part of the overall question-asking problem.

**Final Recommendation:** Accept. The strengths, particularly the originality and significance of the core idea, far outweigh the limitations, which the authors openly acknowledge as avenues for future work.
