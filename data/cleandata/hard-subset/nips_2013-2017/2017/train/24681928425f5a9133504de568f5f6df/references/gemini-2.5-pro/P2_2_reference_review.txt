### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling context-sensitive, goal-directed human question asking. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. These programs, when executed on a possible world state (e.g., a specific game board configuration), return an answer. This reframes the problem of "what question to ask" as a problem of "what program to generate."

The authors develop a probabilistic generative model over the space of possible programs defined by a context-free grammar. The probability of a given question (program) is determined by a log-linear model, where the energy function is a weighted combination of several features:
1.  **Informativeness:** The Expected Information Gain (EIG) of the question, calculated in a Bayesian setting.
2.  **Complexity:** The length or structural complexity of the program, measured by its log probability under a uniform probabilistic grammar.
3.  **Answer Type:** Categorical features for whether the question expects a Boolean, Number, Color, or Location as an answer.
4.  **Relevance:** A feature to filter out programs that are syntactically valid but semantically irrelevant to the game.

The model's parameters are fit to a dataset of human-generated questions from a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The evaluation shows that the full model outperforms ablated versions (e.g., without informativeness or complexity features) in predicting the distribution of human questions in held-out contexts. Furthermore, the model is shown to be capable of generating novel, semantically meaningful, and contextually relevant questions that were not observed in the human data.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The central idea of treating questions as programs is highly original and powerful. It provides a formal, computable, and compositional framework for a problem that is often handled with less structured, end-to-end neural models. This approach elegantly connects active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science.

2.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The ablation study is a key strength. By systematically lesioning features (informativeness, complexity, answer type), the authors provide clear and convincing evidence for the contribution of each component. The finding that the complexity-agnostic model performs extremely poorly is particularly insightful, demonstrating that human question asking is not simply about maximizing information gain but involves a critical trade-off with cognitive effort or simplicity.

3.  **Generative Capability:** The model doesn't just score existing questions; it can generate new ones from scratch. The qualitative examples of novel, human-like questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") are compelling and effectively showcase the model's creative potential and its ability to capture the underlying structure of the task.

4.  **Cognitive Plausibility:** The framework is well-grounded in cognitive science literature, referencing concepts like compositionality and the "language of thought." It provides a compelling computational account of how humans might balance the competing demands of seeking information and formulating concise, understandable queries.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependency on a Pre-defined Semantic Representation:** The model operates on programs, not raw natural language. This sidesteps the challenging and critical step of semantic parsing (translating natural language like "How long is the blue ship?" into the program `(size Blue)`). While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it significantly narrows the immediate applicability of the work. As it stands, the system requires substantial manual effort to translate data and cannot be deployed in a setting with natural language input.

2.  **Domain Specificity of the Grammar:** The grammar, while containing some general-purpose logical and arithmetic operators, is heavily tailored to the "Battleship" domain with primitives like `size`, `Red`, `Blue`, etc. The paper does not provide a clear path for how this grammar could be learned or easily adapted to a new domain, suggesting that significant feature engineering and domain expertise would be required for each new application.

3.  **Scalability of Inference:** The model relies on importance sampling over a large set of generated programs to approximate the partition function and gradients. The authors note that they had to remove two contexts from their cross-validation due to computational complexity. This raises concerns about the scalability of the approach to more complex domains where the space of possible programs or the space of world hypotheses is significantly larger.

### Detailed Comments

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. However, a few points could be clarified:
*   The relationship between the complexity feature `f_comp(x) = -log q(x)` and the PCFG used for the proposal distribution `q(x)` could be made more explicit. Is the complexity penalty simply an inherent bias of the sampler, or is it a feature whose weight is learned independently? The text suggests the latter, which is the correct approach, but it could be stated more directly.
*   The mutual information criterion for determining question equivalence is clever but seems computationally expensive. A brief comment on the cost of this check would be useful context for the qualitative generation results.

### Assessment Against NIPS Criteria

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The probabilistic model is sound, the experimental design is rigorous, and the claims are well-supported by the results. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach (e.g., operating on semantic forms, domain specificity). It is a complete and well-executed piece of research within its stated scope.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and a pleasure to read. The core ideas are introduced intuitively and then formalized precisely. The figures and examples are effective at illustrating both the data and the model's capabilities.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While program synthesis and active learning are established fields, their combination to model rich, goal-directed question asking is a novel and significant contribution. The approach stands in stark contrast to the dominant paradigm of large-scale, end-to-end neural models for question generation, offering a structured, interpretable, and data-efficient alternative.

*   **Significance:** The paper's significance is substantial. It opens up a new and promising research direction for active learning, pushing beyond simple label queries towards more human-like information seeking. Researchers in AI, cognitive science, and computational linguistics are likely to be inspired by this work and build upon its core ideas. While the current implementation has limitations, the conceptual framework it proposes could have a lasting impact on how we design intelligent agents that can learn by asking questions.
