- Paper proposes modeling human question asking as executable program generation using a grammar-based, compositional representation.
- Authors collected 605 natural-language questions from a Battleship-like game across 18 contexts to train/evaluate the model.
- Probabilistic model combines compositional program generation with features for informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Model predicts human question frequencies with Spearman correlation 0.64 and generates novel, human-like questions not seen in training.
- Originality: reframes question asking as program synthesis, grounded in cognitive science and compositionality.
- Quality: technically sound with rigorous evaluation and ablation studies showing all features contribute.
- Clarity: well-written, well-organized, with clear grammar and feature descriptions and reproducible supplementary material.
- Significance: advances active learning and computational cognitive science by modeling rich, context-sensitive question asking.
- Weakness — Domain specificity: grammar is somewhat tailored to Battleship and generalizability to other domains is not demonstrated.
- Weakness — Natural language gap: model operates on semantic programs and does not directly generate fluent natural language outputs.
- Weakness — Dataset size: 605 questions is relatively small and many questions are sparse, limiting statistical power.
- Overall assessment: strong, significant contribution with solid execution and compelling results.
- Recommendation: accept for NIPS; minor limitations are acknowledged and appropriate directions for future work.
