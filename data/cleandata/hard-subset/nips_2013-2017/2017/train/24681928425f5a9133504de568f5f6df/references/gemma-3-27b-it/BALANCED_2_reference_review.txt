This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea – representing questions as executable programs that, when evaluated against a world state, yield an answer – is insightful and offers a compelling alternative to traditional approaches in active learning and dialog systems. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their model on a dataset of questions collected during a “Battleship”-like game, showing that it can predict human question frequencies and synthesize novel, human-like questions.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. First, the conceptual leap of framing questions as programs is genuinely original and provides a powerful lens for analyzing and modeling the underlying cognitive processes involved in question asking. This approach naturally incorporates compositionality and computability, key aspects of human intelligence often overlooked in existing models. Second, the authors provide a thorough and well-structured presentation, clearly articulating their motivations, methodology, and results. The connection to related work is well-established, and the paper effectively highlights the limitations of existing approaches. Third, the empirical evaluation, while conducted on a relatively specific task, is convincing. The model’s ability to predict human question distributions and generate plausible questions suggests that it captures meaningful aspects of human question asking behavior. The ablation study, comparing different model configurations, provides valuable insights into the importance of each component (informativeness, complexity, answer type). Fourth, the grammar designed to represent questions appears sufficiently expressive to capture the diversity of questions observed in the dataset. Finally, the authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and suggest promising directions for future research.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, the reliance on a single, somewhat artificial task (the Battleship game) limits the generalizability of the findings. While the game provides a controlled environment for studying question asking, it’s unclear how well the model would perform in more open-ended or complex scenarios. It is stated that the goal was to model context-sensitive, goal-directed question asking and that this falls outside the capabilities of existing systems, but the Battleship game, while a good starting point, is still a highly structured environment. Second, the feature engineering process, particularly the calculation of Expected Information Gain (EIG), appears complex and potentially sensitive to the assumptions made about the agent’s prior beliefs and the world state. The details of the Bayesian ideal observer are somewhat glossed over, and a more thorough discussion of the computational cost and potential biases associated with EIG would be beneficial. Third, the qualitative evaluation of the generated questions is limited. While the examples provided are plausible, a more systematic evaluation, perhaps involving human judges, would provide stronger evidence for the quality and relevance of the generated questions. Fourth, the importance sampling approximation for the normalizing constant, while necessary for computational tractability, introduces a source of potential error that is not fully addressed. The sensitivity of the results to the choice of importance distribution should be investigated. Fifth, the paper acknowledges that the grammar requires substantial domain knowledge, which somewhat undermines the claim of generality. Future work is needed to explore how the grammar could be adapted to new domains with minimal manual effort.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and insightful framework for modeling question asking as program generation.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the model’s ability to predict human behavior and generate plausible questions.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and thoroughly grounded in related work.
*   The ablation study provides valuable insights into the importance of different model components.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The reliance on a single task limits the generalizability of the findings.
*   The EIG calculation is complex and potentially sensitive to assumptions.
*   The qualitative evaluation of generated questions is limited.
*   The feature engineering and grammar require substantial domain knowledge.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of active learning and cognitive science. The proposed framework offers a novel and compelling perspective on question asking, and the empirical results are encouraging. However, the limitations related to task specificity, computational complexity, and domain knowledge warrant careful consideration. While not without its flaws, the originality and potential impact of this work are substantial.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should address the limitations outlined above by: (1) exploring the applicability of their model to other tasks or domains; (2) providing a more detailed discussion of the EIG calculation and its potential biases; (3) conducting a more systematic evaluation of the generated questions; and (4) investigating methods for automating the grammar construction process. Addressing these points will strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
