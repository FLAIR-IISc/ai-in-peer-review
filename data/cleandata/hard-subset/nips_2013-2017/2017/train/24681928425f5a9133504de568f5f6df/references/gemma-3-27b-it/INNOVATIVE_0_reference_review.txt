This paper presents a compelling and novel framework for understanding question asking as program generation. The core idea – modeling questions as executable programs that map world states to answers – is a significant departure from traditional approaches in active learning and dialog systems, and I find it genuinely exciting. The authors convincingly argue that this perspective offers a more nuanced and human-like approach to information seeking, allowing for the generation of richer and more targeted queries.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of current active learning methods and establishing a strong connection to cognitive science research on human question asking. The authors’ use of a formal grammar to define the space of possible questions is particularly insightful, allowing for both prediction and generation of questions. The experimental evaluation on the “Battleship” dataset demonstrates that the model can effectively capture human question-asking behavior and even synthesize novel, plausible queries. The results showing the importance of balancing informativeness *and* complexity are particularly interesting.

However, the work isn’t without limitations. While the probabilistic model and feature engineering are reasonably well-described, some aspects feel somewhat ad-hoc. The choice of features, particularly those related to informativeness (EIG), feels computationally expensive and potentially sensitive to the specific implementation details. The scaling of the importance sampling method could be a bottleneck for more complex domains. While the authors acknowledge the domain-specificity of some grammar components, the extent to which this framework can generalize to other tasks remains an open question. The paper focuses heavily on *syntactic* correctness and plausibility, but doesn’t delve deeply into the *semantic* grounding of the generated questions – ensuring they are truly relevant and useful beyond simply being well-formed programs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The program generation framework is a genuinely new and promising approach to question asking.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The connection to formal linguistics, cognitive science, and program synthesis provides a strong theoretical foundation.
*   **Empirical validation:** The experiments on the Battleship dataset demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the approach.
*   **Insightful analysis:** The findings regarding the balance between informativeness and complexity are valuable.
*   **Generative capability:** The model can synthesize novel questions, showcasing its potential for creative exploration.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational complexity:** The EIG calculation and importance sampling could be computationally expensive.
*   **Domain specificity:** The extent of generalization to other domains is unclear.
*   **Semantic grounding:** The paper doesn't fully address how to ensure the semantic relevance of generated questions.
*   **Feature engineering:** Some feature choices feel ad-hoc and require further justification.
*   **Limited scope of evaluation:** While effective, the Battleship game is a relatively constrained environment.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a fundamentally new and potentially transformative approach to question asking.
*   The theoretical grounding and empirical validation are strong.
*   The results are encouraging and suggest the potential for significant future work.
*   The work challenges existing paradigms in active learning and dialog systems.
*   Even with its limitations, the paper represents a significant step forward in our understanding of human-like question asking.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity could limit scalability.
*   The domain specificity raises concerns about generalizability.
*   The reliance on feature engineering introduces some arbitrariness.
*   The evaluation is limited to a single, relatively simple domain.

**Overall:**

I strongly believe that this paper warrants acceptance to NIPS. While the work is not perfect, its novelty and potential impact are substantial. The authors have identified a promising research direction and presented a compelling first step towards realizing it. The minor flaws and limitations can be addressed in future work, but the core idea is sufficiently innovative and well-supported to justify acceptance despite these shortcomings. I’m particularly enthusiastic about the potential for this framework to inspire new approaches to active learning and artificial intelligence more broadly. I would encourage the authors to address the scalability and semantic grounding concerns in future investigations.
