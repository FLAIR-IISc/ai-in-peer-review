This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea is compelling: representing questions as programs that, when executed on a possible world state, yield an answer. This elegantly recasts question asking as a program synthesis problem, allowing for a more structured and potentially more powerful approach than traditional methods relying on simple label requests or canned responses. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions asked during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating that it can predict human question frequencies and even synthesize novel, human-like questions.

The strengths of this work are substantial. First, the central premise – framing questions as programs – is a genuinely original and insightful contribution. It provides a theoretically grounded way to think about the compositional and computational aspects of human questioning. Second, the authors ground their approach in empirical data, using a well-defined game context and a dataset of human-generated questions. This is a significant improvement over purely theoretical proposals. Third, the evaluation is thorough, comparing the full model against several ablated versions to identify the importance of different features. The results clearly demonstrate the value of considering informativeness, complexity, and answer type. Finally, the ability to *generate* novel, plausible questions is a notable achievement, demonstrating the generative power of the proposed framework. The link to existing work, especially in cognitive science (Fodor, Goodman, Lake) and computational linguistics, is well-articulated.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The primary concern is the reliance on a fairly specific, pre-defined grammar. While the authors argue for its domain-general aspects, the grammar is clearly tailored to the Battleship game and its associated concepts (size, color, position). Extending this framework to more complex and diverse domains will likely require significant engineering effort and may reveal limitations in the current grammatical structure. Furthermore, the "language of thought" analogy, while appealing, feels somewhat under-justified. It’s not clear *why* this particular programming language is a good model for human cognition, beyond its ability to represent the questions in the dataset.

Another weakness lies in the computational tractability issues highlighted in the optimization section. The use of importance sampling to approximate the gradient is a practical necessity, but it introduces an additional layer of approximation and potential error. The authors acknowledge this, but the impact on the results is not fully explored. Additionally, the supplementary material is crucial for understanding the details of the grammar and the feature engineering, which feels somewhat buried.  The exclusion of contexts 1 and 2 during evaluation due to computational complexity is also concerning. While understandable, it limits the generalizability of the findings. The model's performance while good, is presented with somewhat loose language (e.g. “strong agreement”) and more precise quantitative reporting (e.g., confidence intervals) would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and insightful framing of question asking as program generation.
*   Empirical validation on a human-generated dataset.
*   Demonstrated ability to generate novel, plausible questions.
*   Thorough ablation study to assess the importance of different features.
*   Strong connections to relevant work in cognitive science and computational linguistics.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Reliance on a domain-specific grammar that may not generalize easily.
*   Computational tractability issues and reliance on approximation techniques.
*   Limited justification for the chosen "language of thought."
*   Minor issues with clarity and precision in reporting results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the core idea is exciting and the results are promising, the limitations related to the grammar, computational tractability, and lack of broader justification outweigh the strengths. The paper presents a valuable starting point for future research, but requires significant further development before it can be considered a major contribution to the field. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Investigate methods for learning the grammar from data, rather than relying on a hand-crafted structure.
*   Explore alternative programming languages or formalisms that might be more expressive or more closely aligned with human cognition.
*   Address the computational tractability issues by developing more efficient inference algorithms or exploring alternative approximation techniques.
*   Provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the programming language and its connection to human cognitive processes.
*   Report more precise quantitative results and confidence intervals.
*   Include contexts 1 and 2 in the final evaluation, even if it requires simplifying the model or using a different evaluation metric.
*   Expand the discussion of limitations and potential future directions.
