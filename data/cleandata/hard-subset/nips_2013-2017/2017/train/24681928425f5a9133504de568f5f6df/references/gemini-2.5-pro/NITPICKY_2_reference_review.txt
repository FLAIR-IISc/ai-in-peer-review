### Summary

This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional language which, when executed, yield an answer. The authors define a grammar that generates a space of possible questions and use a log-linear model to predict which questions humans are likely to ask in a given context. The model's energy function incorporates features for informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity (related to program length), answer type, and relevance. The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game, where it is shown to predict human question frequencies reasonably well and to generate novel, plausible questions.

The work relates to active learning by proposing a way to generate much richer queries than typical label requests. It also connects to cognitive science by formalizing question asking through a "language of thought" and to AI/NLP by offering an interpretable, goal-driven alternative to large-scale neural models for question generation.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—modeling question asking as probabilistic program synthesis—is elegant, novel, and highly significant. It provides a formal, interpretable framework for a complex cognitive ability and opens a promising avenue for developing more sophisticated active learning agents that can ask human-like questions. This is a clear departure from and improvement upon existing approaches that rely on "canned" questions or require massive datasets for training opaque neural models.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The probabilistic modeling approach is principled. The choice of features, particularly the trade-off between informativeness (EIG) and complexity, is well-motivated and provides valuable insight into the cognitive constraints on human inquiry. The use of an ablation study to demonstrate the importance of each feature set strengthens the paper's claims considerably.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the core concepts are explained clearly. The use of a constrained game environment as a case study makes the abstract ideas of program generation and EIG calculation concrete and understandable.

### Weaknesses

Despite the compelling core idea, the paper suffers from a significant number of minor flaws, a lack of methodological transparency in key areas, and an unresolved contradiction in the results, which collectively undermine confidence in the work's current state.

1.  **Lack of Polish and Formatting Issues:** The manuscript exhibits a lack of careful preparation.
    *   There is an unusual and unprofessional footnote on the first page identifying the conference ("1st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...").
    *   The reference list is riddled with errors. For example, [1], [2], and [3] end with stray commas. References [12] and [22] begin with single-letter fragments ("L,", "d,") and are improperly formatted. Reference [1] is missing a year and title. Such errors suggest a rushed submission process.

2.  **Opaque Methodological Details:** Key components of the methodology are either not fully explained or have their limitations relegated to footnotes, obscuring their impact.
    *   **Grammar Engineering:** The grammar is the foundation of the entire model, yet its construction is described as something the authors "designed." This hand-engineering is a critical step. How much of it is truly domain-general versus tailored specifically to the quirks of this single dataset? The paper's claims of generality are not substantiated, and the reliance on bespoke engineering is a major weakness for scalability.
    *   **Data Exclusion:** Footnotes 2, 4, and 7 reveal that a non-trivial amount of data was excluded from the analysis for "computational reasons" or ambiguity. Specifically, 14 questions requiring a certain grammar rule were removed, and 2 of the 18 contexts (over 10%) were dropped entirely. These are significant limitations that should be discussed transparently in the main body of the paper, not hidden in footnotes. The precise criteria for these exclusions are also insufficiently detailed.

3.  **Inconsistencies and Gaps in Evaluation:** The evaluation, while generally sound, contains a major unresolved issue and relies on potentially biased presentation.
    *   **Contradictory Correlation Results:** The paper reports that the full model achieves a Spearman's rank correlation of 0.64, while the information-agnostic model achieves 0.65. It is a major oversight to not address or even acknowledge that the model variant *without* the central feature of informativeness (EIG) scores slightly *higher* on this metric. This directly contradicts the narrative built around the log-likelihood results and casts doubt on the role of EIG as modeled. While correlation is not the only metric, this discrepancy demands an explanation.
    *   **Approximation Quality:** The model relies on importance sampling to approximate the partition function and the gradient. The proposal distribution is a simple uniform PCFG. There is no analysis of the quality of this approximation. If the learned model distribution is peaked far from the proposal, the variance of the estimates could be extremely high, making the learned parameters and log-likelihood values unreliable.
    *   **Qualitative Results:** The generated novel questions in Figure 3 are interesting, but the paper states that the "four contexts were selected to illustrate the creative range of the model." This is a textbook case of potential cherry-picking. A more systematic qualitative analysis is needed. What percentage of generated samples are coherent and useful? How do they compare to human questions on average?

### Arguments for/against acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Presents a highly original and compelling formalization of question asking as program synthesis.
*   The work bridges cognitive science and machine learning in a meaningful way.
*   The model is interpretable and the feature ablation study provides clear insights.

**Con:**
*   The evaluation contains a significant, unexplained contradiction regarding the Spearman correlation of the information-agnostic model.
*   Crucial methodological details (grammar engineering, data exclusion) are opaque and their limitations are downplayed.
*   The quality of the crucial approximation via importance sampling is not analyzed.
*   The manuscript suffers from numerous formatting errors and a general lack of polish, suggesting it is not ready for publication.

### Recommendation

**Reject.**

While the paper is built on a strong, creative, and potentially impactful idea, the execution and presentation are not yet at the level required for NIPS. The unexplained contradiction in the evaluation results is a serious flaw that undermines the paper's central claims about the role of informativeness. Furthermore, the lack of transparency regarding the hand-engineered grammar and the various data exclusions, combined with numerous minor errors, gives the impression of a preliminary work. I strongly encourage the authors to revise the paper to address these significant issues, as the core contribution is promising and deserves to be presented in a more rigorous and polished manner.
