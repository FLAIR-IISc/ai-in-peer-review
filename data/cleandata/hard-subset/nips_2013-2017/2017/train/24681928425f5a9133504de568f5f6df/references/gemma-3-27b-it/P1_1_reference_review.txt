## NIPS 2017 Review: Question Asking as Program Generation

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel framework for understanding human question asking by modeling questions as programs. The core idea is that a question, when "executed" on a possible world state, yields an answer. This recasts question asking as a program synthesis problem, leveraging compositionality and computability. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of natural language questions collected during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the fields of active learning, AI dialog systems, and computational linguistics. It distinguishes its approach from traditional active learning’s limited query types (e.g., label requests) and goal-oriented dialog systems’ reliance on pre-defined question sets. It acknowledges the progress in visual question generation and open-ended dialog but highlights the limitations of these approaches – their dependence on large datasets and lack of flexibility in novel scenarios. The work builds on ideas from formal linguistics (compositionality), cognitive science (language of thought), and program synthesis, appropriately citing relevant literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel and Intriguing Framework:** The central idea of modeling questions as programs is insightful and provides a powerful new lens for studying human question asking and potentially building more intelligent AI systems.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The authors ground their approach in established concepts like compositionality, computability, and Bayesian belief updating, lending theoretical weight to their model.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on the Battleship dataset are compelling. The model's ability to predict human question frequencies and generate plausible questions suggests it captures meaningful aspects of human questioning behavior.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the complex concepts and methodology. The use of examples (e.g., representing questions as programs) significantly aids understanding.
* **Thorough Related Work:**  The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of relevant literature and effectively position their work within the existing landscape.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge limitations, the current grammar and feature engineering are heavily tailored to the Battleship domain.  The extent to which this framework generalizes to other domains remains unclear and requires further investigation. The exclusion of questions involving drawing colored tiles highlights this constraint.
* **Computational Complexity:** The importance sampling method used to approximate the normalization constant is computationally expensive, potentially limiting the scalability of the model.  This is briefly mentioned, but a more detailed discussion of computational trade-offs would be valuable.
* **Feature Engineering:** The model relies on carefully engineered features. While justified, the reliance on these features raises concerns about the model’s robustness and ability to learn directly from data.  Could some of these features be learned automatically?
* **Limited Qualitative Analysis:**  While the authors show some generated questions, a more in-depth qualitative analysis, perhaps involving human evaluation of question quality and relevance, would strengthen the claims.
* **Dataset Limitations:** The dataset, while valuable, is relatively small (605 question-context pairs).  Larger and more diverse datasets would be needed to fully assess the generalization ability of the model.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  The technical approach is sound, and the experimental results are well-supported. The use of importance sampling is a pragmatic solution to a challenging computational problem.
* **Clarity:** The paper is mostly clear, but the mathematical notation could be slightly simplified.  More intuitive explanations of the energy function and feature weights would be helpful.
* **Originality:** The framework of modeling questions as programs is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field.
* **Significance:** This work has the potential to significantly impact our understanding of human cognition and pave the way for more intelligent and human-like AI systems. The ability to generate meaningful questions is crucial for active learning, knowledge discovery, and human-computer interaction.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel and theoretically grounded framework for understanding human question asking.
* Demonstrates promising empirical results on a challenging dataset.
* Addresses a significant problem with potential for broad impact.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The model's current implementation is heavily domain-specific.
* Computational complexity could be a limiting factor.
* Reliance on hand-engineered features.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and innovative paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. While the domain specificity and computational complexity are legitimate concerns, the novelty of the approach, the solid theoretical foundation, and the promising empirical results outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity of mathematical notation and expanding the discussion on computational trade-offs. The authors should also briefly discuss potential avenues for generalizing the model to new domains.
