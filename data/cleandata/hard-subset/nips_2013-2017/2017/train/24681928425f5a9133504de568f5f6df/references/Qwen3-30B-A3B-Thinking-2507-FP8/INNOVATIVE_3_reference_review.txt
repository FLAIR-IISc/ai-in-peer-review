

# Review for "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes a fundamentally novel approach to modeling human question asking by framing questions as executable programs that, when run on a possible world state, produce answers. The authors argue that this computational framework captures the compositionality and computability of human questions, which previous work has largely overlooked. Their key insight is that questions can be represented as programs (e.g., "(size Blue)" for "How long is the blue ship?") that can be combined through a grammar to form more complex questions. The authors develop a probabilistic model that samples from this program space, using features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance to predict human question patterns in a Battleship-like game.

The paper's novelty is substantial and bold. While there is work on question generation (e.g., [16, 23, 11]) and active learning [21], these approaches typically rely on neural networks trained on large datasets or focus only on label requests. This paper fundamentally shifts the paradigm by modeling questions as programs with compositionality, a concept rooted in cognitive science and formal semantics. The authors clearly distinguish their work from previous methods by demonstrating that their approach can generate novel, human-like questions without requiring large datasets of paired questions and contexts—a capability that current neural approaches lack.

The evaluation is appropriate for the task: the model successfully predicts human question frequencies in held-out contexts (with Spearman correlation of 0.64) and generates novel questions not present in the training data (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?"). The ablation studies convincingly show that all key features (informativeness, complexity, answer type) contribute to the model's performance, with the complexity-agnostic model performing particularly poorly (Spearman correlation of -0.36).

While the paper has limitations—most notably the domain-specific grammar and the need for substantial domain knowledge—the conceptual contribution outweighs these. The authors acknowledge these limitations and provide a clear path forward for extending the approach to other domains. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find these limitations to be minor compared to the paper's significant conceptual contribution.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The theoretical grounding in cognitive science and formal semantics is strong, and the probabilistic model is well-motivated. The authors effectively position their work within the broader literature on question asking, active learning, and cognitive science.

This work represents a significant step toward modeling human-like question asking in machines, offering a new framework that could inspire future research in active learning, cognitive modeling, and human-AI interaction. The ability to generate novel questions that were not present in the training data demonstrates the model's creative potential, which is particularly impressive.

**Strengths:**
- Conceptually bold and novel approach to question asking as program generation
- Captures compositionality and computability of human questions
- Successfully generates novel human-like questions without requiring large datasets
- Strong theoretical grounding in cognitive science and formal semantics
- Clear ablation studies demonstrating the importance of each key feature

**Weaknesses:**
- Domain-specific grammar (though authors acknowledge this and discuss generalizability)
- Requires substantial domain knowledge to implement
- Limited to a single game context for evaluation

**Recommendation: Accept.** This paper introduces a significant new concept that opens a new direction for research in active learning and cognitive modeling. While the current implementation is domain-specific, the core idea—that questions can be modeled as programs—has broad implications for how we think about and implement question asking in AI systems. The evaluation is appropriate for the task, and the paper provides a solid foundation for future work. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution that NIPS values. The paper's conceptual contribution far outweighs its methodological limitations, making it an ideal candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
