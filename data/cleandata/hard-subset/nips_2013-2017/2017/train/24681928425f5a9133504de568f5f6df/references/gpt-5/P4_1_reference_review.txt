Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes modeling question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a simple, LISP-like lambda calculus that, when run on a hypothesized world state, return an answer (Boolean, number, color, or location). A probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) defines the compositional space of programs; a log-linear energy model assigns probabilities to programs using features for expected information gain (EIG), complexity (−log prior under the PCFG), answer type, and a relevance indicator. The model samples programs from the PCFG, estimates a normalizing constant via importance sampling, and fits feature weights by maximizing the likelihood of human questions.

Evaluation uses a dataset of 605 natural-language questions from 40 participants in a Battleship-like information search game, mapped to programs. Leave-one-context-out experiments compare the full model to ablations removing informativeness, complexity, or type features in terms of held-out log-likelihood and rank correlation between model energy and human question frequencies. The full model outperforms ablations in log-likelihood (with bootstrap support), achieves average Spearman ρ ≈ 0.64, and can synthesize novel, context-appropriate questions not present in the training data. The work connects to active learning (beyond label queries), question semantics as partitions over possible worlds, program-based QA, and cognitive models using languages of thought. It complements prior work (e.g., Hawkins et al.) by focusing on generation over a compositional program space, rather than only evaluating utility of fixed templates.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of questions as executable programs, leveraging compositionality and computability to explain productivity in human question asking.
- Well-motivated feature set combining EIG (informativeness), complexity, type preferences, and relevance; ablations substantiate the importance of complexity and, to a lesser extent, informativeness and type.
- Probabilistic generative framing with likelihood-based evaluation on human data; leave-one-context-out procedure mitigates overfitting to specific contexts.
- Demonstration of generating novel, semantically meaningful questions using the same machinery used for prediction, with a sensible equivalence check via mutual information over answer distributions.
- Conceptually bridges active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive modeling; relevant to NeurIPS themes on structured representations and learning.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality
- The complexity feature is tied to −log q(x), where q is the uniform PCFG proposal used for importance sampling. This couples the feature to an arbitrary proposal and risks confounding “complexity” with sampling design. Learning PCFG rule probabilities or using a length-based complexity measure would reduce dependence on q.
- Importance sampling over a vast program space using a uniform PCFG may suffer from high variance. Details about whether the 150k proposals are resampled across iterations, effective sample sizes, and convergence diagnostics are missing. The robustness of Z estimates and gradient estimates is unclear.
- EIG computation requires enumerating the hypothesis space and all possible answers. The need to drop two contexts and exclude the stochastic draw operator underscores scalability limits. Quantitative runtime/complexity and any approximations (e.g., hypothesis pruning) are not reported.
- The full model’s average rank correlation (0.64) is slightly below the information-agnostic model (0.65), despite a log-likelihood advantage. This tension warrants discussion (e.g., likelihood sensitivity to tail probabilities versus rank).
- Baselines are limited to ablations. No comparison against alternative scoring functions (e.g., value of information with answer costs, heuristic utility), nearest-neighbor/template transfer across contexts, or neural text models conditioned on context (acknowledging data size constraints) is provided.
- The dataset and grammar mapping are essential to reproduction; core grammar rules and mapping procedures are in the supplement, and some questions were excluded. Public code or more detail on the mapping would improve reproducibility.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. However, evaluation details (absolute LL values, variance across folds, confidence intervals for correlations) are sparse.
- The interaction between the relevance feature and the grammar (i.e., the “b marker”) should be specified more precisely.
- Some references appear incomplete or malformed; ensure correct citation metadata.

Originality
- Modeling question generation as program synthesis with a PCFG over executable question semantics is novel relative to prior active learning and dialog systems, and distinct from program-based QA which interprets rather than generates questions. The work also complements prior pragmatic/RSA-style models by supplying a compositional generator integrated with EIG.

Significance
- The contribution is primarily conceptual and methodological within a constrained domain. It advances understanding of human question generation and suggests a path toward richer machine queries. However, scalability beyond small, fully specified hypothesis spaces and adaptation to raw natural language remain open. As such, near-term impact on large-scale ML systems is moderate, with higher significance for cognitive modeling and structured active learning.

Pros
- Principled compositional representation enabling generalization and novelty.
- Solid probabilistic formulation with interpretable features; informative ablations.
- Empirical alignment with human question distributions and qualitative generation examples.
- Bridges literatures on active learning, program induction, and cognitive science.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on domain-specific semantics and hypothesis enumeration; limited scalability.
- Complexity feature entangled with proposal distribution; potential identifiability concerns.
- Limited baseline comparisons; some evaluation metrics suggest mixed benefits of EIG.
- Exclusions of contexts/operators reduce completeness; limited reporting on estimation stability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Learn PCFG rule probabilities and/or include explicit program length/depth penalties, decoupling complexity from the proposal. Report sensitivity to the proposal distribution.
- Provide diagnostics for importance sampling (effective sample size, resampling strategy) and convergence, plus runtime and scaling characteristics.
- Reconcile rank-correlation and likelihood results; add confidence intervals and per-context analyses. Consider additional utility features (e.g., answerer cost) or pragmatic models.
- Add baselines: nearest-neighbor/template transfer across contexts; simple heuristic utilities; where feasible, a lightweight neural generator conditioned on abstracted context features.
- Extend to a second domain with different primitives to test portability, and/or integrate a semantic parsing layer to map natural language to programs for end-to-end evaluation.
- Release code and full grammar/mapping to enhance reproducibility.

Overall assessment
A technically sound and clearly presented approach that is original in framing question asking as program generation and demonstrates predictive and generative alignment with human data in a controlled domain. While scalability and baseline breadth limit immediate ML impact, the work offers a meaningful step toward richer, goal-directed question generation and merits consideration.
