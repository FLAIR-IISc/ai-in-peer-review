This paper presents a novel and compelling framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking as a form of probabilistic program generation. The central idea is to represent questions not as natural language strings, but as formal programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. These programs, when executed on a possible world state (in this case, a "Battleship" game board configuration), return an answer. The problem of asking a good question is thus reframed as synthesizing an optimal program. The authors propose a log-linear model to define a probability distribution over the space of possible programs, defined by a hand-crafted grammar. The model scores programs based on a weighted combination of features, most notably Expected Information Gain (EIG) to capture informativeness and a complexity penalty derived from the grammar. The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a Battleship-like game, demonstrating that it can predict the frequency of human questions in held-out contexts and generate novel, meaningful questions.

This work connects several important lines of research, including active learning, program synthesis, and computational cognitive science. It moves beyond the simple label queries of traditional active learning and the less-structured, data-intensive approaches of neural question generation models (e.g., for VQG). The explicit modeling of a trade-off between informativeness and complexity is a key contribution, providing a strong cognitive hypothesis for how humans select questions.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The core idea of treating question asking as program synthesis is highly original and powerful. It provides a formal, interpretable, and generative account of a complex cognitive ability. This is a significant conceptual advance over existing paradigms in active learning and question generation.

2.  **Strong Cognitive Plausibility:** The model elegantly captures the balance between seeking maximal information (EIG) and adhering to cognitive constraints on complexity (favoring shorter, simpler programs). The ablation study, which shows a dramatic drop in performance for the complexity-agnostic model, provides strong evidence for this central claim. This aligns well with established principles in cognitive science.

3.  **Compositionality and Productivity:** The grammar-based approach naturally accounts for the compositional nature of questions and the human ability to generate novel queries tailored to a specific context. The fact that the model can generate new, sensible questions not seen in the training data is a powerful demonstration of its generative capabilities, a key weakness of many existing systems.

4.  **Rigorous Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is sound. The authors use leave-one-out cross-validation, perform principled lesion studies to test the contribution of different model components (informativeness, complexity, type), and report both quantitative (log-likelihood, correlation) and qualitative (generation of novel questions) results.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

1.  **Domain Specificity and Scalability:** The primary weakness is the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar and a highly structured, toy-like domain (Battleship). The authors acknowledge this limitation, but its severity cannot be overstated. It is unclear how this approach would scale to more complex, open-ended domains where the space of possible world states is vast and the relevant concepts are not as cleanly defined. The calculation of EIG, which requires iterating over a hypothesis space, would become intractable in richer environments.

2.  **Lack of Language Grounding:** The model operates entirely at the semantic level of a "language of thought," bypassing the difficult problems of parsing natural language into programs and generating natural language from them. While the authors suggest interfacing with existing NLP tools, this is a non-trivial challenge that represents a major hurdle for practical application. As it stands, the system cannot take natural language as input or produce it as output.

3.  **Inference Approximation:** The model relies on importance sampling to approximate the intractable normalizing constant and the gradient expectation. While a standard technique, the quality of the model's fit is dependent on the quality of this approximation. The use of a simple probabilistic context-free grammar with uniform probabilities as the proposal distribution may not be efficient for exploring the space of high-utility programs, potentially affecting the accuracy of the learned weights and likelihood estimates.

**Detailed Comments:**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-specified, and the optimization via gradient ascent with importance sampling is a valid approach. The experimental design is solid, particularly the inclusion of ablation studies. The authors are honest about the limitations of their work, such as the domain specificity.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and clear. The motivation is compelling, the model is described in a logical progression, and the figures and tables are informative. The authors do an excellent job of explaining a complex idea in an accessible manner, making the paper a pleasure to read.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The synthesis of ideas from program induction, Bayesian inference, and active learning to model question asking is novel and thought-provoking. It carves out a unique niche distinct from both traditional active learning and large-scale neural language models for question generation.

**Significance:** The paper has high potential significance. For the active learning community, it proposes a concrete path toward richer and more human-like information acquisition. For cognitive science, it offers the first predictive, computational model of open-ended, goal-directed question asking. While the immediate practical applications are limited by the scalability and language grounding issues, the conceptual contribution is likely to inspire significant follow-up research. It advances the state of the art by demonstrating how a structured, symbolic approach can capture nuances of human cognition that are difficult to address with purely data-driven methods.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that introduces a creative and significant new direction for research in machine learning and cognitive science. The conceptual framework is elegant and the empirical validation is convincing within the chosen domain. While significant challenges related to scalability and language grounding remain, they do not diminish the importance of the foundational contribution made here. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to be influential.
