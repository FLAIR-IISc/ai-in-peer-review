This paper introduces a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea – representing questions as programs that, when executed on a world state, yield an answer – is insightful and potentially powerful. The authors connect this idea to concepts from linguistics (compositionality), cognitive science (language of thought), and AI (program synthesis), and demonstrate its applicability to a dataset of questions collected during an information-search game. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a compelling argument for its approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Insight:** The framing of question asking as program generation is a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach. It moves beyond viewing questions as simple label requests and allows for a more nuanced, compositional representation.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper grounds its approach in established concepts from linguistics, cognitive science, and AI, providing a solid theoretical foundation. The connections to compositionality and the language of thought are particularly well-articulated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors evaluate their model on a real-world dataset of human questions, demonstrating its ability to predict human question-asking behavior and even generate novel, plausible questions.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ reasoning and understand their methodology.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive, covering relevant areas like active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation. It appropriately positions the current work within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity:** The model relies on a carefully crafted grammar specific to the Battleship domain. While the authors acknowledge this, it raises concerns about the generalizability of their approach to other, more complex domains. The extent to which the grammar can be readily adapted to new scenarios is unclear.
* **Complexity of the Model:**  The model incorporates several features (informativeness, complexity, answer type, relevance) and involves intricate calculations (e.g., Expected Information Gain). This complexity could make it difficult to scale the approach to larger datasets or more sophisticated domains.
* **Limited Qualitative Analysis of Generated Questions:** While the authors provide a few examples of generated questions, a more systematic and in-depth qualitative analysis would be beneficial. It's not entirely clear how “human-like” the generated questions truly are beyond the initial impression.  Are they genuinely insightful, or simply plausible based on the grammar?
* **Importance Sampling Reliance:** The optimization process relies heavily on importance sampling to approximate intractable expectations. The accuracy and robustness of this approximation could be a concern, and further sensitivity analysis or exploration of alternative approximation techniques would be valuable.
* **Dataset Limitations:** The dataset, while valuable, is relatively small (605 question-context pairs). A larger and more diverse dataset could help to further validate the model and improve its performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and insightful approach to modeling human question asking.
* The empirical results demonstrate the feasibility and effectiveness of the proposed framework.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and thoroughly grounded in existing literature.
* The work has the potential to contribute to both AI (active learning, dialog systems) and cognitive science (understanding human reasoning).

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The domain specificity of the model raises concerns about its generalizability.
* The complexity of the model could limit its scalability.
* The qualitative evaluation of generated questions is limited.
* The reliance on importance sampling introduces potential approximation errors.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a compelling idea and promising results. While the domain specificity and model complexity are legitimate concerns, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh these drawbacks. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, but with the caveat that the authors should address the limitations mentioned above in future work. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Generalization Exploration:**  Investigate methods for adapting the grammar and model to new domains with minimal manual effort. Explore the possibility of learning the grammar directly from data.
* **Model Simplification:**  Explore ways to simplify the model without sacrificing performance. Consider feature selection or dimensionality reduction techniques.
* **Enhanced Qualitative Analysis:** Conduct a more rigorous qualitative analysis of the generated questions, potentially involving human evaluations or comparisons to questions asked by humans in similar scenarios.
* **Alternative Optimization Methods:** Explore alternative optimization methods that do not rely on importance sampling, or investigate ways to improve the accuracy and robustness of the importance sampling approximation.
* **Larger Dataset:**  Consider collecting or utilizing a larger and more diverse dataset to further validate the model and improve its performance.
