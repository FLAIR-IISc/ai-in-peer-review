

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes modeling human question asking as program generation, where questions are represented as executable programs that return answers based on a world state. The authors develop a probabilistic model using a grammar-based approach to generate questions, evaluated on a dataset of human questions from a Battleship-like game. While the formalism of questions as programs is conceptually interesting and builds on established work in computational linguistics and cognitive science, the paper suffers from significant limitations in scope, validation, and generalizability that undermine its contribution to the field.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a clear and technically sound framework for modeling question generation as program synthesis. The grammar-based approach effectively captures compositional aspects of human questions, and the probabilistic model with features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance is well-structured. The evaluation on the human question dataset demonstrates reasonable predictive performance (Spearman's correlation of 0.64), and the ability to generate novel questions that weren't in the training set is an interesting demonstration of the model's productivity.

**Weaknesses:** The primary weakness is the extremely narrow scope of evaluation. The model is tested exclusively on a single, highly constrained Battleship game domain with a specific grammar and domain knowledge built into the model. The authors acknowledge this limitation ("some aspects of our grammar are specific to the Battleship domain"), but fail to demonstrate how this approach could be generalized to more practical or diverse question-asking scenarios. The paper makes unsubstantiated claims about "enabling more human-like question asking in machines" without evidence beyond this narrow domain.

More critically, the paper lacks rigorous comparison with established baselines. The evaluation compares variants of their model (e.g., "information-agnostic" vs. "full model") but does not compare against existing question-answering or question-generation systems that might handle richer contexts. The comparison to "canned questions" in goal-directed dialog systems (Section 2) is superficial, as the paper doesn't demonstrate how their approach would outperform such systems on relevant benchmarks.

The paper also lacks evidence of scalability or practical utility. The grammar requires substantial domain-specific knowledge engineering (e.g., ship sizes, colors, positions), and the authors acknowledge that "it is unclear exactly how much new knowledge engineering will be needed" to extend to other domains. This is a significant limitation for a paper claiming to advance active learning.

The evaluation metrics are also problematic. The log-likelihood comparison (Table 1) is approximate and bootstrapped, with the authors acknowledging that "the human data is sparse" and correlations "are limited as a measure of fit." The paper claims "strong agreement" with human question frequencies (Spearman's 0.64), but this correlation is modest at best and appears to be driven primarily by the model's ability to capture basic question types rather than sophisticated question generation.

The paper's claim of "novel human-like questions" (Figure 3) is overstated. The generated questions remain tightly constrained by the grammar and domain knowledge, and the novelty is limited to the specific context of the Battleship game. For example, "What is the column of the top left of the tiles that have the color of the bottom right corner of the board?" is novel within the game context but would be meaningless in a real-world scenario.

**Comparison to Related Work:** The paper builds on existing work in program synthesis for question answering (citing [12, 24]), but fails to demonstrate how it advances beyond these approaches. The Visual Question Generation (VQG) work [16] is mentioned but not properly contrasted, despite the fact that VQG systems operate on more diverse, real-world contexts. The authors acknowledge the need for large datasets in VQG approaches but don't show how their method overcomes this limitation.

**Overall Assessment:** While the paper presents a technically sound formalism for a very specific task, it does not sufficiently demonstrate the significance of the approach beyond the narrow domain it was tested on. The lack of rigorous validation against meaningful baselines, the absence of evidence for generalizability, and the overstatement of potential impact make this work more of a domain-specific contribution than a significant advancement to the field.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Testing on a more diverse set of domains
2. Comparing against established question-generation systems
3. Providing evidence of scalability to more complex contexts
4. Discussing concrete pathways for generalization beyond the Battleship domain

As a reviewer who prioritizes methodological rigor and evidence of broad applicability, I find the current evidence insufficient to support the paper's claims. While the formalism is interesting, the lack of thorough validation against established methods and the narrow scope of evaluation prevent this work from making a compelling contribution to NIPS. The paper represents a incremental step in a specific direction rather than a significant advance to the field.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's contribution is limited to a narrow domain with insufficient evidence of generalizability or practical impact. The authors would need to substantially extend the evaluation to more diverse domains and demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches to justify publication at NIPS.
