### Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for modeling goal-directed question asking by framing it as a problem of program generation. The authors argue that existing active learning and dialog systems are limited to simple queries, whereas humans ask rich, compositional questions. Their core idea is to represent questions as programs in a LISP-like language that, when executed on a world state, return an answer. This recasts question asking as synthesizing the most useful program in a given context.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like game, where the goal is to ask a question to resolve ambiguity about hidden objects. The authors define a grammar that generates a space of possible questions (programs). They then propose a probabilistic, energy-based model to score these programs based on a weighted combination of features, including Expected Information Gain (EIG), program complexity, and answer type. The model's parameters are fit to human data using leave-one-out cross-validation.

The results demonstrate that this model can successfully predict the frequency of human questions. A key finding is that a combination of informativeness and complexity is crucial; models that ignore complexity perform very poorly, suggesting humans balance the desire for information with a preference for simplicity. The model is also capable of generating novel, semantically meaningful questions that were not observed in the human data, highlighting its generative power. This work connects ideas from active learning, program synthesis, and computational cognitive science to provide a compelling model of a sophisticated human ability.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of question asking as probabilistic program generation is elegant and well-motivated. The use of a log-linear (energy-based) model is a standard and appropriate choice for combining different features into a coherent probabilistic framework. The experimental methodology is rigorous, employing leave-one-out cross-validation and, crucially, performing lesion studies to ablate different components of the model. These studies provide strong evidence for the central claims of the paper, particularly the trade-off between informativeness (EIG) and complexity. The authors are also commendably honest about the limitations of their approach, such as the reliance on a hand-engineered grammar and the abstraction away from natural language.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is established effectively in the introduction, contrasting the proposed approach with prior work. The model is described in a logical progression from the core concepts (compositionality, computability) to the grammar and the probabilistic model. The inclusion of concrete examples of questions and their corresponding programs (e.g., "How long is the blue ship?" -> `(size Blue)`) is extremely helpful for building intuition. The results are presented clearly, and the qualitative examples of generated novel questions in Figure 3 are particularly compelling.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of this paper is its originality. While program induction has been used to model concept learning (e.g., Lake et al., 2015) and program synthesis is a field in itself, framing *goal-directed question asking* this way is a novel and powerful idea. It provides a significant conceptual advance over both traditional active learning (which typically queries labels) and data-driven VQA/dialog systems (which often lack deep semantic understanding or require massive datasets). The work carves out a unique and interesting space at the intersection of machine learning, cognitive science, and computational linguistics. The finding that humans do not simply maximize EIG, but are strongly constrained by complexity, is an important scientific contribution in its own right.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant for several reasons.
1.  For the active learning community, it opens up a new avenue for research into much richer and more human-like forms of information acquisition. This could eventually lead to more efficient and intuitive interactive machine learning systems.
2.  For computational cognitive science, it provides the first quantitative, predictive model of open-ended, goal-directed question asking, a fundamental aspect of human intelligence.
3.  The framework itself—defining a compositional hypothesis space of queries and scoring them based on utility and cost—is generalizable and could inspire similar approaches in other domains of planning and reasoning.

The main weakness, as the authors acknowledge, is the reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar. This raises questions about the scalability and generalizability of the approach. Creating such a grammar for a new, more complex domain would be a significant undertaking, limiting the model's immediate practical application. Furthermore, the model operates purely on the level of semantic representations (programs), bypassing the difficult problem of parsing natural language questions into these forms or generating natural language from them. While this is a reasonable simplification for a foundational paper, it represents a major gap that must be bridged for this work to be integrated into a real-world system.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The core idea of "question asking as program generation" is highly original and represents a significant conceptual shift from prior work in active learning and dialogue systems.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The model provides a good quantitative fit to human data, and the lesion studies convincingly demonstrate the importance of balancing informativeness with complexity.
*   **Generative Power:** The ability to generate novel, sensible, and useful questions that were not in the training set is a powerful demonstration of the model's capabilities.
*   **Interdisciplinary Significance:** The paper makes a valuable contribution to both machine learning (specifically active learning) and computational cognitive science, which is a hallmark of strong NIPS papers.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   **Scalability/Generalizability:** The dependence on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar is a major limitation. It is unclear how this approach would scale to more open and complex domains without substantial manual engineering.
*   **Disconnection from Natural Language:** The model completely abstracts away the language component, dealing only with pre-translated programs. This sidesteps a central challenge in building a true question-asking agent.
*   **Limited Dataset:** The model is evaluated on a relatively small dataset (605 questions) from a single, constrained game environment.

### Conclusion

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The novel conceptual framework, strong empirical validation, and significant potential impact on both machine learning and cognitive science make it an excellent contribution. While the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar is a valid concern for practical scalability, it is an acceptable simplification for a paper that is introducing a fundamentally new way of thinking about a complex problem. This work is likely to be influential and inspire a great deal of follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
