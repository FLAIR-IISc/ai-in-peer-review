Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework that treats question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a simple, typed, lambda-calculus/LISP-like language; executing a question program on a possible world yields an answer. A probabilistic generative model over this compositional program space predicts which questions humans ask in context, balancing informativeness (expected information gain), complexity (via a PCFG prior), answer type preferences, and a relevance filter. The model is trained with a log-linear energy over program features, with expectations approximated via importance sampling. Evaluation is on a Battleship-style dataset of 605 natural-language questions asked by humans across 18 contexts; human questions are translated to programs. Leave-one-context-out experiments show the full model improves held-out log-likelihood over ablations and produces novel, human-like questions not in the dataset.

Relation to prior work
The work connects several strands: active learning (mostly limited to label queries), formal semantics of questions as partitions, program induction and semantic parsing for question answering, and neural question generation/dialog systems. Unlike VQG or goal-directed dialog systems, this model targets goal-directed, context-sensitive question generation with explicit utility and compositional semantics, and unlike pragmatic models of question asking, it operationalizes a generative, productive space of executable questions with a grammar and a learned utility trade-off.

Strengths
- Bold, original framing: modeling question asking as program synthesis over a compositional, typed space is conceptually powerful and bridges cognitive science and ML.
- Generativity: the grammar supports recombination beyond the training set; the model can synthesize novel, useful questions.
- Principled utility: integrates Bayesian belief updating and EIG with a cognitive prior on complexity and type preferences; the ablation on complexity is especially revealing.
- Empirical evidence: leave-one-out predictions on human data with both quantitative (LL, correlations) and qualitative (novel samples) assessments.
- Clarity of the core idea: the representation and modeling choices are motivated and accessible.

Weaknesses
- Domain specificity and scale: the grammar is heavily engineered for Battleship; evaluation is on a small dataset (605 questions, one domain). Generalization beyond this setting is untested.
- Natural language gap: the system operates on program semantics rather than generating/understanding NL; mapping to/from language is deferred.
- Mixed evidence for informativeness: reported correlations show information-agnostic slightly exceeding the full model (0.65 vs. 0.64), undermining the claimed contribution of EIG in regression-to-frequency; the LL advantage mitigates this but should be analyzed.
- Approximate inference and computational load: importance sampling over 150k programs and 100k iterations; convergence/variance diagnostics and sensitivity to proposal q(x) are not detailed.
- Handcrafted relevance feature and filter: a manual marker for board-referential questions may bake in domain knowledge; it would be better to learn this or constrain the grammar.
- Limited baselines: no comparison to pragmatic/RSA-style questioner models, neural sequence models over programs, or simple template libraries tuned by utility.

Quality
Technically sound overall with a reasonable probabilistic formulation and ablations. The EIG computation and Bayesian update machinery are standard; the complexity prior via PCFG probability is sensible. However, approximation details (variance of Z estimates; robustness to different proposal grammars; sensitivity to sample size; stopping criteria) are sparse. The dataset and evaluation are honest about limitations and include ablations, but broader baselines are missing.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The formalization of the language, typing, and examples are clear. Some crucial pieces are in the supplement (grammar tables, exact hypothesis priors), and the optimization section would benefit from pseudocode and diagnostics. The discrepancy between correlation and LL results for EIG merits clearer discussion.

Originality
High. Treating question asking as program generation with a learned utility-complexity trade-off is a fresh angle relative to active learning, dialog, and question generation literatures. The combination of a productive grammar, EIG-based utility, and a predictive model of human question frequencies is novel.

Significance
Promising. If extended beyond a toy domain and connected to NL interfaces, this could influence interactive ML/AL, robotics, and cognitive modeling of inquiry. The demonstrated ability to synthesize human-like, context-sensitive questions points toward more capable query agents. Current impact is limited by domain scope, but the conceptual contribution is substantial.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide diagnostics for importance sampling (ESS, variance of Z, convergence) and sensitivity to grammar proposal distributions and sample sizes.
- Reconcile the correlation vs LL results for EIG; consider alternative utility features (e.g., MI, value-of-information under different priors) and report effect sizes.
- Add baselines: (i) RSA-style question selection with a small template set, (ii) neural program generators trained on program strings, (iii) kNN/template reuse with paraphrase normalization.
- Move toward NL by adding a semantic parser (e.g., Overnight-style weak supervision) to map text to programs and evaluate end-to-end.
- Test a second domain to probe portability of the grammar (e.g., simple block worlds, card games).
- Replace the manual relevance marker with a learned constraint or typed grammar that prevents non-referential programs by construction.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and bold
- Solid probabilistic formulation and insightful ablations (complexity matters)
- Demonstrated generativity and human-like question synthesis
- Bridges ML and cognitive science in a meaningful way

Cons for acceptance
- Single, small, domain-specific dataset; heavy knowledge engineering
- Limited baselines and partial evidence for informativeness
- Missing NL interface; practical applicability not yet shown
- Approximation details and robustness underreported

Recommendation: Weak Accept (for its originality and promising direction; I’m willing to overlook current scope/engineering limitations if the paper more clearly analyzes EIG’s contribution and adds minimal diagnostics/baselines).

Confidence: Medium.
