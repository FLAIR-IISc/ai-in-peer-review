### NIPS Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel and exciting framework for modeling goal-directed question asking, recasting the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The central thesis is that rich, human-like questions can be formally represented as programs that are executed over possible world states to yield an answer. The authors develop a probabilistic model over a compositional grammar (a "language of thought") that can generate such programs. The probability of a given question/program is determined by a log-linear model that weighs features such as its expected information gain (EIG), its complexity, and its answer type. The model is evaluated on a dataset of human-generated questions in a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The results show that the model can quantitatively predict the frequency of human questions in unseen contexts and, perhaps more impressively, generate novel and contextually appropriate questions that were not observed in the human data. This work represents a significant conceptual departure from traditional active learning, which typically focuses on simple label queries, and data-driven dialogue systems, which often lack goal-directed intentionality.

**Strengths**

1.  **Exceptional Originality and Significance:** The core contribution of this paper—framing goal-directed question asking as program generation—is a powerful and deeply original idea. It provides a formal, interpretable, and generative solution to a problem of fundamental importance to artificial intelligence. Instead of offering an incremental improvement on existing methods, this work proposes a new paradigm for active learning and intelligent information gathering. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-risk, high-reward research that can open up entirely new avenues of inquiry. The potential impact is significant, suggesting a path toward machines that can learn by asking the same kinds of rich, compositional questions that humans do.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Framework:** The choice to model questions as programs in a compositional language is elegant and well-motivated. It naturally captures the structured and generative nature of human inquiry, where a finite set of conceptual primitives can be combined to form a seemingly infinite variety of questions. The integration of Bayesian information gain with a complexity penalty (derived from the grammar) provides a compelling explanation for how humans balance the drive for information with a preference for simplicity, a finding supported by the ablation studies.

3.  **Compelling Qualitative Results:** While the quantitative correlations with human question frequencies are solid, the qualitative results are truly striking. The model's ability to generate novel, clever, and contextually useful questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?", "What is the top left of all the ship tiles?") that were not in the training data is a powerful demonstration of its generative capabilities. This goes far beyond simply memorizing or recombining seen examples; it shows a genuine capacity for creative, goal-directed synthesis within the problem space.

4.  **Strong Connection to Cognitive Science:** The work builds a valuable bridge between machine learning and cognitive science, grounding the computational model in theories of human cognition like the "language of thought." This interdisciplinary perspective enriches the paper and increases its relevance to the broader NIPS community interested in building machines that learn and think more like people.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on a Hand-Crafted Grammar:** The model's impressive performance relies on a carefully designed, domain-specific grammar. While this is a necessary and understandable simplification for a foundational paper exploring a new idea, it leaves open the question of scalability. It is unclear how much expert knowledge and engineering would be required to apply this framework to new, more complex domains. The authors are transparent about this limitation, but it remains the most significant hurdle for broader application.

2.  **Abstraction from Natural Language:** The model operates on semantic representations (programs) and does not engage with the challenge of parsing natural language into these forms or generating natural language from them. This sidesteps a major part of the overall problem of human-machine interaction. However, I view this not as a flaw, but as a deliberate and effective research strategy to isolate the core problem of *what* to ask, which is arguably the more fundamental cognitive question.

3.  **Idealized Bayesian Observer:** The calculation of EIG assumes an ideal observer that can enumerate and reason about the entire hypothesis space of possible game boards. This is tractable in the constrained Battleship domain but would not be in more realistic, open-ended scenarios. Future work will need to explore approximations for this component.

**Recommendation**

I am strongly in favor of accepting this paper. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations of scope that are entirely reasonable for a paper introducing a paradigm-shifting idea. The core contribution—re-conceptualizing question asking as program synthesis—is exceptionally novel, insightful, and significant. It tackles a grand challenge in AI with a bold and creative approach. While the methodology is not yet a perfectly polished, off-the-shelf solution, the conceptual framework it introduces is likely to be highly influential and inspire a great deal of follow-up work in active learning, program synthesis, and human-computer interaction. This paper prioritizes a powerful new idea over incremental methodological refinement, and for that, it should be celebrated and shared with the NIPS community.
