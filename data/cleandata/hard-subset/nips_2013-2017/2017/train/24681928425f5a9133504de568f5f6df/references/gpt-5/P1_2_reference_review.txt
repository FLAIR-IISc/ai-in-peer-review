Summary
This paper proposes a computational framework for human question asking by treating questions as executable programs in a typed, LISP-like language. A probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) defines a compositional hypothesis space of question-programs. A log-linear model assigns probabilities to questions based on features: expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, program complexity (PCFG-derived description length), answer type priors, and a relevance filter. Parameters are learned via maximum likelihood using importance sampling to approximate the partition function and gradients. Evaluation is on a Battleship-like dataset of 605 natural-language questions asked by humans across 18 contexts (two contexts and 14 “draw” questions are excluded for tractability). The model is assessed by leave-one-context-out prediction of human question distributions (log-likelihood and rank correlation) and qualitative generation of novel, context-sensitive questions. Ablations show that removing complexity hurts performance most; the full model generally outperforms variants without EIG or type priors.

Relation to prior work
The work connects several strands: active learning (EIG as utility), formal semantics of questions as partitions over worlds, the language-of-thought/program-induction perspective on compositionality, and program-based semantic parsing/QA. Compared to task-oriented dialog and visual question generation, which are largely template- or data-driven, this contribution emphasizes generative, goal-directed question synthesis with explicit semantics and utility. It advances prior cognitive models of questioning by generating questions from scratch, rather than evaluating a fixed set.

Quality
- Modeling: The formulation is sound: a typed, compositional grammar supports executability and utility computation; EIG is computed over a hypothesis space of board configurations with Bayesian updating; complexity is tied to PCFG probabilities; type priors capture base-rate preferences. The equivalence filtering via mutual information over answer distributions is a nice touch for evaluating novelty.
- Inference/optimization: Importance sampling over a large program set is a reasonable approximation, though the quality of Z and gradient estimates depends on the proposal q. The authors report 150k samples and 100k gradient steps; more diagnostics on variance and convergence would help.
- Empirical support: LOOCV on 16 contexts shows the full model has the best test log-likelihood; ablations support the claim that complexity and EIG both matter. Reported average Spearman ρ of 0.64 between energy and human frequencies indicates moderate alignment.
- Caveats: Two contexts and 14 questions were dropped due to tractability, which raises concerns about scalability of EIG and the generality of conclusions. The surprising result that the information-agnostic variant slightly outperforms the full model on rank correlation (0.65 vs. 0.64) deserves discussion (the authors rely on LL for the main claim, but this discordance should be reconciled). Baselines are limited to ablations; comparisons to simpler heuristics (e.g., PCFG-only prior, EIG-only over an enumerated subset, or template libraries) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The core ideas (questions as programs, grammar design, EIG computation) are explained with concrete examples. Useful implementation details are given (proposal distribution, number of samples, optimizer). However:
- Move key grammar fragments from the supplement into the main text or at least provide a summary table of types and key productions.
- Provide more detail on the hypothesis space sizes per context and the computational cost of EIG (and why contexts 1–2 are especially hard).
- Clarify the discrepancy between LL and rank correlation across ablations.

Originality
Modeling question asking as program generation and learning a utility-weighted prior over questions to predict and generate human queries is novel relative to prior work that either evaluates given questions or uses templates/neural language models without explicit semantics/utility. The integration of EIG with a compositional generative question prior is a fresh synthesis.

Significance
The work addresses an important and under-explored problem—goal-directed, compositional question generation—and proposes a framework that could influence interactive learning, dialog systems, and cognitive modeling. That said, evidence is confined to a small, domain-specific dataset with hand-engineered grammar and semantic translation; generalization to new domains and to natural language remains future work. Within NeurIPS, this is a solid cognitive-computational contribution with potential to inspire follow-up in program synthesis for interactive ML.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation marrying compositional semantics with active learning utility.
- Executable questions enable precise utility and equivalence calculations.
- Convincing ablation showing the importance of complexity priors beyond informativeness.
- Qualitative generation of non-trivial, human-like questions illustrating creativity.
- Careful discussion of limitations.

Weaknesses
- Scalability: EIG over large hypothesis spaces is computationally heavy; contexts and rule types were dropped.
- Limited baselines beyond ablations; no comparison to simple heuristics or template libraries.
- Dataset/domain specificity; natural language-to-program mapping is manual; unclear portability.
- Approximate inference may bias results; no variance/error bars for LL estimates, and convergence diagnostics are absent.
- Mixed metrics: information-agnostic variant slightly better rank correlation; needs interpretation.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide variance estimates for LL via multiple sampling seeds and discuss convergence?
- How large are the hypothesis spaces per context, and what is the runtime per EIG computation?
- How sensitive are results to the PCFG prior (e.g., different rule probabilities) and to the complexity feature definition?
- Could you report performance of a PCFG-only prior and an EIG-only model over a bounded enumerated set?

Suggestions
- Add simpler baselines and report both LL and rank correlation with confidence intervals.
- Explore alternative utility functions (e.g., cost-sensitive EIG, answerer cognitive cost) and richer complexity priors (e.g., MDL).
- Provide code and grammar definitions for reproducibility.
- Discuss strategies to scale EIG (e.g., amortized inference, pruning, Monte Carlo over hypotheses).

Recommendation
Borderline accept. Pros: principled, original framework; solid modeling; informative ablations; clear potential impact. Cons: limited scale and baselines; domain specificity; some evaluation inconsistencies and approximations. On balance, it is a valuable step toward program-based, goal-directed question generation and should interest NeurIPS audiences at the intersection of program synthesis, active learning, and cognitive modeling.
