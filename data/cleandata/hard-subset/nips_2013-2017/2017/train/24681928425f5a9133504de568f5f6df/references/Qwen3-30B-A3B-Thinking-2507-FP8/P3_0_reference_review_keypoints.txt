- Proposes modeling human question asking as executable programs evaluated on possible world states, applied to a Battleship-like game.  
- Collected 605 natural language questions from 40 participants to train and evaluate the model.  
- Uses a probabilistic (log-linear) model combining Expected Information Gain (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance to generate questions.  
- Model predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions not seen in training.  
- Strength: Technically sound approach with principled EIG-based informativeness and multi-feature integration.  
- Strength: Thorough evaluation with quantitative log-likelihood comparisons and qualitative novel-question examples.  
- Strength: Clear writing, helpful examples, and supplementary materials for reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Grammar and features are domain-specific to the Battleship game, raising generalization concerns.  
- Weakness: No systematic analysis of how much domain knowledge or re-engineering is needed for new domains.  
- Weakness: Reliance on EIG requires a well-defined Bayesian model of the world, which may not be feasible in complex real-world domains.  
- Weakness: Claim that people can ask intelligent questions in novel scenarios is not well-supported given participants received extensive instructions and practice.  
- Clarity issue: Grammar section is dense and the program-to-natural-language mapping is underexplained for NLP integration.  
- Clarity issue: EIG calculation lacks precision—hypothesis space and prior are not clearly defined.  
- Originality: Representing questions as executable, compositional programs is a novel and compelling idea.  
- Originality caveat: The probabilistic modeling framework is standard; novelty mainly in feature choice and domain adaptation.  
- Significance: Opens avenues for human-like question generation useful for education and human-AI interaction but limited by narrow dataset and domain specificity.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions focusing on domain generalization, NLP interfacing, and clearer EIG/hypothesis-space description.  
- Suggested revisions: (1) Expand discussion on cross-domain generalization, (2) Clarify program-to-language interface in main text, (3) Detail the Bayesian hypothesis space and prior for EIG.  
- Pro-accept arguments: novel conceptual framework, strong empirical validation, connection to cognitive science, and creative question generation.  
- Con-reject arguments: domain-specific grammar, need for substantial domain knowledge, single-game dataset, and limited novelty in modeling framework.
