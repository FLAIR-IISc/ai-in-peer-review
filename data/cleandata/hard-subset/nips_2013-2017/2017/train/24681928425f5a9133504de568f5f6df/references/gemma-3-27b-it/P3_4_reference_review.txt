## NIPS 2017 Review: Question Asking as Program Generation

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for modeling human question asking as program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs that, when executed on a world state, yield an answer. This recasts question asking as a program synthesis problem, allowing for a computationally tractable approach to understanding the structure and generation of human queries. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions asked during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating that it can predict human question frequencies and synthesize novel, human-like questions.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper appropriately positions itself within the broader context of active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation. It effectively highlights the limitations of existing active learning methods that primarily focus on label requests and the canned-response nature of many dialog systems.  It distinguishes itself from VQG by focusing on *generating* questions in novel situations with limited training, rather than relying on large image-question pairs. The authors rightly point out the lack of goal-directedness in many open-ended question generation systems.  The connection to linguistic theories of compositionality and formal approaches to question meaning (e.g., partition over possible worlds) is also well-established and adds theoretical depth.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel and compelling framework:**  The program synthesis view of question asking is a significant conceptual leap. It provides a powerful and flexible way to represent questions and reason about their informativeness.
* **Strong grounding in cognitive science:** The connection to theories of compositionality and the "language of thought" provides a theoretically motivated approach, grounding the work in cognitive principles.
* **Empirical validation:**  The evaluation on the human question dataset is crucial. The model's ability to predict question frequencies and generate novel questions demonstrates its capacity to capture aspects of human question asking behavior.
* **Detailed model specification:** The authors provide a clear and comprehensive description of their probabilistic model, including the grammar, features, and optimization procedure.
* **Careful analysis and ablation studies:** The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of different features (informativeness, complexity, answer type) in the model's performance.
* **Clear writing and organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity of the Grammar:** While the authors acknowledge this, the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar tailored to the Battleship game is a significant limitation.  The grammar, while powerful enough to represent the dataset, lacks the generality to easily transfer to other domains without substantial engineering.  The removal of the tile drawing rule due to computational complexity further highlights this issue.
* **Computational Complexity:**  The importance sampling method used for approximation introduces computational challenges.  The need to sample a large number of questions (150,000) to approximate the gradient is computationally expensive, potentially limiting scalability.
* **Informativeness Metric (EIG):** While conceptually sound, the EIG calculation relies on the assumption of a rational Bayesian agent with a uniform prior.  Human question asking is often guided by biases, heuristics, and incomplete knowledge, which are not fully captured by this metric.  The correlation with human question frequencies, while positive, isn't overwhelmingly strong, suggesting other factors influence human behavior.
* **Limited Qualitative Analysis of Generated Questions:** While a few generated questions are shown, a more systematic and in-depth qualitative analysis would be beneficial.  It’s hard to gauge the full quality and naturalness of the generated questions based on the limited examples provided.
* **Dataset Size:** The dataset of 605 question-context pairs, while valuable, is relatively small.  A larger and more diverse dataset would provide stronger evidence for the generalizability of the model.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and insightful framework for modeling question asking.
* The empirical results provide strong evidence that the model can capture aspects of human question asking behavior.
* The theoretical grounding in cognitive science and linguistics adds significant value to the work.
* The detailed model specification and ablation studies demonstrate a thorough and rigorous approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The domain specificity of the grammar and the computational complexity of the model may limit its practical applicability.
* The reliance on a rational Bayesian agent for the informativeness metric may not fully capture the nuances of human question asking.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and innovative paper that makes a significant contribution to the understanding of human question asking.  The program synthesis framework is a compelling and potentially transformative idea. While the domain specificity and computational complexity are legitimate concerns, they do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work.  The strengths of the paper – its novelty, theoretical grounding, and empirical validation – outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:

*   Discuss the limitations of the grammar's domain specificity more explicitly and outline potential avenues for generalization.
*   Provide a more detailed qualitative analysis of the generated questions, including examples of failure cases.
*   Consider exploring alternative methods for approximating the normalizing constant in the probabilistic model to improve computational efficiency.
*   Acknowledge the limitations of the rational Bayesian agent assumption and explore ways to incorporate more realistic cognitive biases into the informativeness metric.
