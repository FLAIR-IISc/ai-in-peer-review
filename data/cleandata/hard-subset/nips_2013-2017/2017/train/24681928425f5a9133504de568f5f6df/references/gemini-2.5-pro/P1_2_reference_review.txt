**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed, open-ended question asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that human questions can be represented as programs in a compositional "language of thought." When executed on a possible world state, these programs return an answer. The problem of asking a good question is thus transformed into one of synthesizing a useful program.

The authors develop a probabilistic log-linear model defined over a space of programs generated by a formal grammar. This model learns to select questions by balancing several key factors: informativeness (modeled as Expected Information Gain, EIG), complexity (a penalty on longer, more complex programs), and answer type preferences (e.g., a bias for Boolean questions).

This framework is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The model successfully predicts the distribution of human questions in held-out contexts and, impressively, can generate novel, contextually relevant, and human-like questions that were not observed in the training data. The work contrasts with standard active learning (which uses simpler queries), dialogue systems (which often rely on canned questions), and large-scale neural question generation models (which are less data-efficient and compositional). It aligns more closely with work in cognitive science on probabilistic models of concept learning.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed model is principled, combining a generative grammar with a well-defined probabilistic objective function. The use of a log-linear model to weigh features like EIG, complexity, and answer type is a sound approach. The experimental methodology is strong, particularly the use of leave-one-out cross-validation and lesion studies. The ablation analysis convincingly demonstrates that all components of the model are necessary for good performance. The finding that complexity is a crucial constraint—even more so than informativeness in some comparisons—is a key insight. The authors are also honest about the limitations, such as the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar and the circumvention of natural language parsing.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the model is explained in a step-by-step manner, and the experimental results are presented logically and effectively. The examples of programs corresponding to natural language questions are very helpful for building intuition. The figures, particularly the examples of generated novel questions (Figure 3), provide a compelling qualitative demonstration of the model's capabilities. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the core mechanics of the approach.

**Originality:** The paper's main strength lies in its originality. The reframing of question asking as program synthesis is a highly novel and powerful idea for the machine learning community. While program induction has been explored at NIPS and elsewhere (e.g., Lake et al.), its application to the generation of *questions* for active learning is new and insightful. This work provides a significant conceptual leap beyond the standard active learning paradigm of querying labels. It clearly distinguishes its contributions from related work in dialogue systems and visual question generation, highlighting its unique focus on compositional, goal-directed inquiry in a data-efficient manner.

**Significance:** The potential significance of this work is high. It opens up a new and promising research direction for active learning, aiming to equip agents with a much richer, more human-like mechanism for acquiring information. The core insight—that agents should balance informativeness with the complexity of the query—is a general principle that could be broadly applicable. Researchers in active learning, probabilistic programming, and human-computer interaction are likely to build on these ideas. The qualitative results, showing the model generating clever and non-obvious questions, suggest that this approach is not just fitting noise but capturing something fundamental about effective inquiry.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Domain Specificity and Scalability:** The most significant weakness is the reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar. While the authors acknowledge this, it remains a major barrier to applying this method to new, less structured domains. The amount of expert knowledge and engineering required to design a suitable grammar for a more complex task (e.g., scientific discovery, debugging code, or visual understanding) is substantial. This currently limits the model's practical, out-of-the-box applicability.
2.  **Bypassing Natural Language:** The model operates on semantic program representations, not raw text. Human questions from the dataset had to be manually translated into the formal language. This sidesteps the challenging but crucial problem of natural language understanding. While this is a reasonable simplification for an initial investigation, a complete system would need to bridge this gap, perhaps by integrating a semantic parser.
3.  **Dataset Scope:** The evaluation is conducted on a single, relatively constrained domain ("Battleship"). While the dataset is unique and valuable, its limited scope makes it difficult to assess how well the learned feature weights and the overall approach would generalize. For instance, the strong preference for simple questions may be an artifact of this specific game.
4.  **Computational Cost:** The inference method, which relies on importance sampling over a large set of 150,000 programs at each gradient step, appears computationally intensive. It is unclear how this would scale to richer grammars that define a combinatorially larger space of possible questions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Highly Novel Idea:** The core concept of question asking as probabilistic program generation is a significant and original contribution to the active learning literature.
*   **Strong Conceptual Framework:** The paper provides a principled and elegant framework for thinking about rich, goal-directed inquiry, balancing information gain and complexity.
*   **Convincing Empirical Results:** The lesion studies clearly demonstrate the importance of each model component, and the qualitative examples of generated questions are compelling.
*   **High Potential Impact:** The work opens up a new avenue of research and is likely to inspire follow-up work in making machine learning agents more effective and human-like learners.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Limited Practicality:** The dependence on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar significantly limits the model's immediate applicability to new problems.
*   **Disconnect from Natural Language:** The model does not handle raw text, which sidesteps a major part of the overall challenge of human-machine interaction.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a thought-provoking and high-quality paper that introduces a novel and significant conceptual framework. Despite the practical limitations regarding scalability and the lack of natural language processing, the originality of the core idea and the strength of the empirical validation make it an excellent contribution. It addresses a fundamental problem in AI and offers a compelling new direction for research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
