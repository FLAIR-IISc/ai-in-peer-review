This paper introduces a novel and exciting computational framework for modeling rich, goal-directed human question asking. The central idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. This recasts the problem of question asking as one of program generation. The authors propose a probabilistic model that defines a distribution over the space of possible programs (questions). The probability of a question is determined by a log-linear model combining several well-motivated features, most notably the Expected Information Gain (EIG) of the question and its structural complexity. This framework is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. The model is shown to successfully predict the distribution of human questions in held-out scenarios and, impressively, to generate novel, plausible, and contextually relevant questions. This work connects ideas from active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science, moving beyond the simple label queries of typical active learning systems to a much richer and more human-like form of inquiry.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main strength is its highly original and significant contribution. The "question asking as program generation" framing is a powerful and elegant idea that opens up a new research direction for active learning and AI systems. It provides a formal basis for moving beyond simple queries towards the kind of flexible, compositional, and goal-directed inquiry that is a hallmark of human intelligence. The potential impact is substantial, with clear relevance to more sophisticated active learning agents, more flexible dialogue systems, and computational cognitive science.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed model is technically sound and well-executed. The choice of a probabilistic, energy-based model over a grammar-defined space is appropriate for the task. The features used in the model—balancing informativeness (EIG) with a complexity penalty, alongside preferences for answer types—are thoughtfully chosen and well-justified. The experimental validation is thorough, including leave-one-out cross-validation, ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the importance of each model component (especially complexity), and both quantitative and qualitative analyses. The qualitative results, showcasing the generation of clever, novel questions, are particularly compelling evidence for the model's capabilities.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly, the model is developed in a logical, step-by-step manner, and the experimental setup and results are easy to understand. The use of concrete examples of questions and their corresponding program representations greatly aids comprehension. The authors are also commendably clear about the limitations of their work.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, there are several points that could be addressed to strengthen it further.

1.  **Generalizability and Domain Specificity:** The most significant weakness is the reliance on a hand-engineered grammar specific to the Battleship domain. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but its implications for the broader applicability of the framework deserve more discussion. It is unclear how much effort would be required to adapt this system to a new domain. Future work should explore methods for learning parts of the grammar from data or defining a more universal set of primitives. The paper could be improved by a more detailed breakdown of the current grammar, distinguishing between truly domain-general components (e.g., logical operators, set functions, lambda calculus) and domain-specific ones (e.g., `size(S)`, predicates referring to the board). This would give readers a better sense of the framework's portability.

2.  **Scalability of Inference:** The model relies on importance sampling over a very large set of generated programs (150,000) to approximate the partition function and the gradients for learning. The authors note that they had to exclude two game contexts with particularly large hypothesis spaces due to computational constraints. This raises concerns about the scalability of the approach to more complex domains where the space of possible questions and/or world states might be orders of magnitude larger. It would be beneficial to discuss potential avenues for more efficient inference. For example, could a search-based method (like MCMC over program structures) or techniques from amortized inference (e.g., training a neural network to propose promising programs) be more scalable than rejection sampling from a simple PCFG?

3.  **Natural Language Interface:** The model operates on semantic program representations, bypassing the difficult problems of semantic parsing (text-to-program) and natural language generation (program-to-text). While this is a reasonable simplification for a foundational paper, it is a critical gap for building a complete question-asking system. The authors might consider adding a brief discussion on the challenges involved. For instance, the mapping from a single program to natural language is one-to-many (e.g., `(> (size Red) (size Blue))` could be "Is the red ship bigger than the blue ship?" or "Does the red ship have more tiles than the blue one?"). Accounting for this variability is a non-trivial challenge for both parsing and generation.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Pro:** Proposes a highly novel, elegant, and significant framework for goal-directed question asking.
*   **Pro:** The model is technically sound, well-motivated by cognitive principles (balancing information and complexity), and thoroughly evaluated.
*   **Pro:** The empirical results are strong, demonstrating both predictive accuracy on human data and the ability to generate creative, novel questions.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. It has the potential to be highly influential and to inspire a new line of research in active learning and AI.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   **Con:** The approach relies on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar, which raises questions about its generalizability.
*   **Con:** The current inference method has potential scalability issues that may limit its application to more complex problems.
*   **Con:** It sidesteps the challenge of grounding the program representations in natural language.

### Decision

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a groundbreaking idea with a strong technical execution and compelling results. The limitations, while important, are acceptable for a foundational piece of work in a new research area and are clearly acknowledged by the authors. This paper is an excellent fit for NeurIPS and is likely to have a significant impact on the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
