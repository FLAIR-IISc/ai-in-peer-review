Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for question asking: questions are represented as executable programs over possible world states, generated via a compositional, LISP-like grammar. A log-linear probabilistic model over programs balances informativeness (Expected Information Gain under a Bayesian ideal observer), complexity (via a PCFG prior), and answer type. The model is trained to predict which questions humans ask in an ambiguous information-search game (Battleship-like), and it can also synthesize novel, human-like questions. Evaluation uses leave-one-context-out prediction of human question distributions, ablations of feature groups, and qualitative sampling of new questions.

Relation to prior work
The work bridges active learning (beyond label queries), formal semantics of questions as partitions, program induction, and question generation. Unlike goal-directed dialog with fixed slots and VQG models trained on large corpora, this approach targets open-ended, goal-directed question asking with explicit utility. It differs from neural VQG or 20-questions systems by grounding the space of questions in a compositional program language and a Bayesian utility model.

Strengths
- Clear, bold idea: casting question asking as program synthesis and scoring with EIG is an elegant, general formulation that could influence both ML and cognitive science.
- Compositionality and computability are operationalized via a grammar that can represent the entire human dataset and generate novel queries.
- The probabilistic model integrates human-relevant trade-offs: informativeness vs. complexity vs. type preferences.
- Quantitative evidence: full model improves held-out log-likelihood vs. ablations; complexity is shown to be critical (complexity-agnostic performs poorly).
- Qualitative samples are indeed clever and diverse; the equivalence filtering via answer-partition mutual information is a nice touch.
- Methodologically transparent about approximations and limitations; dataset link provided.

Weaknesses
- Domain-specific grammar and semantics: while many primitives are general, a non-trivial amount of engineering targets Battleship; it’s unclear how much carries over to other domains without new design.
- Natural language is not modeled end-to-end; mapping from NL to programs is left for future work. This limits practical applicability in current form.
- Importance sampling with a uniform PCFG proposal to approximate Z and expectations may suffer from mismatch as θ evolves; variance and convergence diagnostics are not provided. It’s unclear whether 150k samples are sufficient across the large space (up to 100 function applications).
- In Results, the info-agnostic model attains slightly higher rank correlation (0.65) than the full model (0.64), while the full model has better log-likelihood. This tension suggests EIG contributes but sparsity and model misspecification limit its predictive power for frequencies; the paper could analyze this discrepancy more deeply.
- The setup is single-turn; no demonstration of closed-loop interaction where answers update beliefs and subsequent questions are produced, which is central to active learning utility.
- Two hardest contexts and 14 “draw” questions were dropped for computational reasons; this narrows the evaluation and may bias conclusions about generality.

Quality
Overall technically sound and carefully presented. The Bayesian EIG formalization is standard; the novelty lies in weaving it into a program space with a trainable energy-based model. Approximations (IS, bootstrapping Z) are reasonable, but more diagnostics would strengthen claims (ESS, ablation of sample size, stability across random seeds).

Clarity
Well written and organized. The grammar and features are clearly described. It would help to include more concrete program examples and a compact table of primitives in the main paper (not just the supplement), plus a brief complexity analysis of the hypothesis space per context.

Originality
High. Framing question asking as program generation, and learning to predict human question frequencies in a compositional space with explicit informativeness and complexity, is a fresh, ambitious direction compared to prior active learning and question generation work.

Significance
Potentially high. This formalism could inform future ML systems that ask rich, goal-directed questions, and offers a testbed for human-like inquiry. Even if near-term performance is domain-bound, the conceptual step is meaningful and may spur cross-fertilization with program synthesis, semantic parsing, and interactive learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide variance/diagnostics for importance sampling and sensitivity to the proposal (e.g., guided proposals using current θ, annealing, or MCMC in program space).
- Analyze the correlation vs. log-likelihood discrepancy for the info-agnostic variant; report per-context differences and sparsity effects.
- Include a baseline using a neural sequence model over the program language (trained on programs) to test whether compositional generalization depends on the designed grammar vs. learned syntax.
- Demonstrate a short multi-turn interaction (simulate answer, update belief, propose next question) to showcase the utility loop.
- Report an out-of-domain pilot (e.g., a small gridworld or shapes domain) with minimal new primitives to test portability.
- Release code for the grammar, sampler, and evaluation to facilitate reuse.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually bold and novel formulation with clear potential beyond the toy domain.
- Demonstrated ability to generate human-like, compositional, utility-aware questions.
- Careful ablation showing the necessity of complexity control.

Cons against acceptance
- Domain-specific engineering; limited evidence of generalization.
- Approximate inference details lack robustness checks.
- Single-turn setting and removal of harder contexts/questions reduce ecological validity.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline to clear). The paper advances a compelling new direction—questions as programs with principled utilities—that outweighs the methodological imperfections. It is the kind of bold idea NeurIPS should encourage.

Confidence
3.5/5 (familiar with active learning, program induction, and semantics; not an expert on all psycholinguistic nuances of question semantics).
