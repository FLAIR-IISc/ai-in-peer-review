

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes modeling human question asking as program generation, where questions are represented as programs in a simple programming language. The authors develop a probabilistic model that predicts which questions humans will ask in a Battleship-style game, based on features like informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance to the game state. They evaluate their model on a dataset of 605 natural language questions from 40 human players in a game context, claiming their model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel human-like questions.

The paper's main strengths include a clear formalization of questions as programs, a well-structured probabilistic model, and a thoughtful evaluation framework. The authors demonstrate that their model outperforms variants lacking key components (informativeness, complexity, or answer type features) in predicting human question distributions. The grammar-based approach provides a principled way to handle question compositionality, and the generated questions (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") demonstrate some creativity within the game context.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's contribution and methodological rigor. The work is highly domain-specific to a Battleship game with limited generalizability. The grammar and features are designed specifically for this narrow context, with the authors acknowledging "some aspects of our grammar are specific to the Battleship domain." The model requires substantial domain knowledge engineering to define the grammar rules and features, which undermines the claimed generality of the approach. The authors note that "to extend this approach to new domains, it is unclear exactly how much new knowledge engineering will be needed," which is a major limitation for a paper claiming to advance active learning.

The evaluation is limited to a single game context with a small dataset (605 questions total), which is insufficient to demonstrate meaningful progress. The authors don't compare against established active learning baselines or question generation methods. The model's performance metrics (log-likelihood, Spearman correlation) are weak indicators of practical utility, and the "novel" questions generated are constrained by the narrow game context.

The paper's novelty is overstated. While modeling questions as programs has been explored in computational linguistics and cognitive science (cited in the paper), this work doesn't convincingly demonstrate how it advances beyond these prior approaches. The connection to broader active learning literature is superficial - they cite [21] on active learning but don't engage with how their approach differs from or improves upon standard active learning methods.

The paper's claims about "more human-like question asking in machines" are premature given the constrained domain and lack of evaluation in more natural settings. The authors acknowledge that their system operates on semantic representations rather than natural language directly, which is a significant limitation for practical applications.

In terms of the NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The technical approach is sound but the evaluation is inadequate. The paper fails to adequately address limitations and doesn't compare against strong baselines.
- Clarity: Well-written and clearly organized, but the narrow scope limits the broader relevance.
- Originality: The core idea is not substantially novel, and the contribution is incremental within a very narrow domain.
- Significance: The work addresses an interesting problem but doesn't demonstrate meaningful advancement or broader applicability.

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to modeling question asking, it lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of advancing active learning or enabling more human-like question asking in machines. The domain-specific nature of the approach, limited dataset, and lack of comparison to established methods significantly undermine the paper's contribution.

I recommend rejection because the work is too narrowly focused to be of broad interest to the NIPS community, and the evidence provided doesn't justify the claims made about the generality and significance of the approach. The paper would benefit from a more extensive evaluation across multiple domains, stronger comparison to established baselines, and a clearer demonstration of how this approach advances beyond the state of the art in active learning.
