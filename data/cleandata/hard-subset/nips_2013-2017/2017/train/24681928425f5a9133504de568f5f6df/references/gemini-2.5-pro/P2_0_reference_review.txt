### **Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for modeling goal-directed, open-ended question asking, framing it as a problem of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is that human questions can be represented as programs in a compositional language (a "language of thought") that, when executed on a possible world state, return an answer. The authors develop a system comprising a grammar that defines the space of possible questions (programs) and a probabilistic log-linear model that scores these questions based on a set of features. These features include the question's expected information gain (EIG), its complexity (derived from the grammar), its answer type (e.g., Boolean, number), and its relevance to the game. The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game designed to resolve ambiguity. The results demonstrate that the model can predict the frequency distribution of human questions in held-out contexts and can also generate novel, syntactically valid, and semantically meaningful questions.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and well-executed piece of work.

**Strengths:**
*   **Methodology:** The choice of a probabilistic generative model over a space of programs defined by a grammar is well-suited for the problem. The log-linear model is a standard and appropriate way to combine different features influencing question preference.
*   **Evaluation:** The experimental design is strong. The leave-one-out cross-validation provides a robust estimate of the model's predictive power. The ablation studies are particularly insightful, clearly demonstrating the necessity of each feature set. The finding that the `complexity-agnostic` model performs extremely poorly (Spearman's ρ = -0.36) is a powerful result, highlighting that humans do not simply maximize information gain but balance it against conciseness.
*   **Honesty:** The authors are transparent about the model's limitations, such as the computational complexity forcing them to exclude two contexts and the reliance on pre-defined semantic representations instead of raw text.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Computational Scalability:** The optimization process, requiring importance sampling over 150,000 questions and extensive gradient ascent, appears computationally expensive. The fact that the two most complex contexts had to be removed for computational reasons suggests that the approach may not scale well to domains with significantly larger hypothesis spaces. This is a practical limitation for future applications.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

**Strengths:**
*   **Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the gap in existing active learning and dialogue systems and motivates the need for a framework that can handle richer, more compositional queries.
*   **Model Description:** The model is explained in a clear, step-by-step manner. The use of simple examples like `(size Blue)` and `(> (size Red) (size Blue))` makes the program representation intuitive. The breakdown of the model into the grammar, the probabilistic scoring function, the features, and the optimization procedure is logical and easy to follow.
*   **Figures and Tables:** The figures are illustrative and effectively convey the core results. Figure 3, showing novel generated questions alongside their context and EIG, is particularly compelling evidence for the model's generative capabilities.

#### **Originality**

The work is highly original and proposes a significant conceptual shift.

**Strengths:**
*   **Core Idea:** The central idea of modeling question asking as program generation is a novel and powerful synthesis of concepts from active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science. While prior work has modeled *concepts* as programs or used programs for question *answering*, applying this framework to generative, goal-directed question *asking* is a significant new contribution.
*   **Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a problem—open-ended, context-sensitive question asking—that has been largely overlooked by mainstream machine learning, which has focused on simpler queries. This work carves out a new and interesting research direction.
*   **Relation to Prior Work:** The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to existing approaches in active learning, dialog systems, and VQG, clearly articulating how their method overcomes key limitations of each.

#### **Significance**

The paper has high potential for significant impact on multiple fields.

**Strengths:**
*   **Advancing Active Learning:** This work represents a major step towards making active learning agents more human-like. It provides a concrete path to move beyond simple label requests to a much richer and more efficient space of queries, which could dramatically accelerate learning in complex domains.
*   **Cognitive Science Contribution:** The model serves as the first predictive computational model of open-ended human question asking. The finding that humans balance informativeness (EIG) with complexity provides a quantitative and testable theory of this cognitive process.
*   **Inspiring Future Work:** The framework is likely to inspire follow-up research. Researchers in HCI might explore building more natural interactive systems, while NLP researchers could work on bridging the gap between natural language and this programmatic representation.

**Weaknesses (related to broader impact):**
*   **Domain Specificity:** The most significant limitation affecting broader significance is the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar for the Battleship domain. While the authors argue that many components (logics, set operators) are domain-general, the core predicates (`size`, `color`, etc.) are domain-specific. The effort required to engineer such a grammar for new, more complex domains is substantial and represents a major barrier to widespread adoption. This tempers the immediate practical applicability of the work.
*   **Lack of Language Grounding:** The model operates in a "language of thought," sidestepping the challenge of parsing natural language into these programmatic forms. While this is a valid simplification for a research paper focused on the generation mechanism, it means the system is not an end-to-end solution for a practical AI agent.

### **Final Assessment and Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a highly original and thought-provoking framework for question asking. The core contribution—recasting question asking as probabilistic program generation—is elegant and powerful. The technical execution is sound, the experimental results are strong and well-analyzed, and the paper is written with outstanding clarity. The ablation studies provide compelling evidence for the model's components, and the qualitative examples of novel generated questions are impressive.

The main weakness lies in the reliance on a domain-specific, hand-engineered grammar, which raises questions about the approach's generalizability and scalability to new domains. However, for a NIPS paper, the conceptual and theoretical contribution far outweighs this practical limitation. This work opens up a new and exciting research direction at the intersection of active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science. It is precisely the kind of foundational, high-impact research that NIPS should showcase.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept.
