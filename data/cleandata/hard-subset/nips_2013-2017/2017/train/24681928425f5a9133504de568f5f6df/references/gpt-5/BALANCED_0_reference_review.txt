Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a small, typed, LISP-like language defined by a context-free grammar. A log-linear model over program “features” assigns probabilities to questions conditioned on a game context (a Battleship-like task). Key features include expected information gain (EIG) computed via Bayesian belief updating over possible boards, a complexity prior derived from the probabilistic grammar, answer-type indicators, and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit by maximizing likelihood of human-asked questions (translated to programs), with importance sampling approximations for gradients and normalization. The model is evaluated by leave-one-context-out prediction of human question distributions and by sampling novel, human-like questions.

The work connects to: (i) active learning (most prior work queries labels or interventions, e.g., Settles 2010); (ii) question semantics as partitions over worlds (Groenendijk & Stokhof; Roberts); (iii) language-of-thought/program induction approaches (Goodman & Tenenbaum; Lake et al.); (iv) program-based question answering/semantic parsing (Wang et al.; Johnson et al.). It differs from visual question generation and dialogue systems that rely on large supervised corpora and typically lack explicit informational utility optimization. It also extends prior cognitive models of question asking (e.g., Hawkins et al., pragmatic models over restricted hypothesis spaces) by generating questions compositionally from a grammar rather than choosing from a fixed set.

Strengths
- Clear, principled computational framing of questions as executable programs, enabling precise semantics and EIG-based utility.
- Elegant use of a PCFG both as a generative prior (complexity) and as a proposal for importance sampling.
- Empirical evidence that combining informativeness with a complexity prior and answer-type preferences better matches human behavior than lesioned variants (full model best log-likelihood; complexity feature especially important).
- Demonstrates compositional productivity: grammar can generate and the model can score novel, context-sensitive questions not in the data.
- Thoughtful discussion of domain specificity vs. generality and limitations.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment

Quality
- Inference approximations: Z and feature expectations are estimated from a large but finite proposal (150k samples) using a uniform PCFG; variance/consistency diagnostics are limited. AIS or SMC could provide stronger evidence of robustness. Sensitivity to the proposal and sample size is not analyzed.
- Evaluation scope is narrow: small dataset (605 questions over 16 contexts after exclusions), domain-specific DSL, and removal of two hardest contexts and a rule (draw) for tractability. This weakens claims about generality.
- Quantitative results are mixed across metrics: full model has best held-out log-likelihood, but the reported average rank correlation with human frequencies is slightly higher for the information-agnostic model (0.65 vs. 0.64), suggesting the correlation metric may not consistently favor the full model.
- No comparisons to alternative families (e.g., retrieval + reweighting over past questions, neural seq2seq conditioned on context representations, or RL-based question selection over a candidate set).
- Novel questions are evaluated qualitatively only; no human judgments of naturalness/utility.

Clarity
- Overall well written and organized; the exposition of the grammar and features is clear. Some details are relegated to the supplement (grammar tables, full EIG computation). A compact schematic of the inference pipeline would help.
- The relationship between energy, probability, and the correlation metric (using energies vs. probabilities) should be clarified, as should why the information-agnostic model attains higher rank correlation while underperforming in likelihood.

Originality
- Representing questions as programs and optimizing EIG is a compelling synthesis, but the ingredients (partition semantics, program induction, PCFG complexity priors, information-theoretic utilities) are known. The contribution is the integration and the empirical demonstration on open-ended human question generation.
- Claim of “first predictive model of open-ended human question asking” is plausible within this compositional/program synthesis framing, though prior pragmatic models have optimized utility over predefined question spaces.

Significance
- Provides a promising path toward richer, goal-directed question generation that goes beyond label queries and canned templates. However, the impact for the broader NeurIPS audience is tempered by the narrow domain, small scale, and lack of comparisons to strong contemporary baselines.
- If extended to other domains and coupled to natural language interfaces, this paradigm could be influential in active learning and interactive ML.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide stronger inference validation: sensitivity to proposal size, alternative proposals, and variance estimates; consider AIS/PMC/SMC for Z and expectations.
- Add baselines: (i) retrieval/reweighting over observed questions; (ii) neural question generators conditioned on board encodings; (iii) restricted candidate sets with RL selection. Even if these do not share the same DSL, they would contextualize performance.
- Human evaluation of sampled novel questions for usefulness, naturalness, and answerability.
- Analyze learned feature weights, per-answer-type preferences, and error cases where the model predicts implausible questions.
- Explore noise/cost: incorporate answerer noise and explicit cognitive cost beyond length/PCFG probability; test whether such costs improve fit.
- Demonstrate transfer: reuse the domain-general part of the grammar in a different structured domain to assess how much new engineering is required.
- Clarify the correlation vs. likelihood discrepancy and report uncertainty on correlations.

Reasons to accept
- Principled, compositional formalism for question generation with interpretable utilities and priors.
- Evidence that complexity and informativeness jointly explain human question choices.
- Generates novel, meaningful questions, showcasing the benefits of a programmatic representation.

Reasons to reject
- Limited empirical scope and scalability; reliance on domain-specific DSL and heavy approximations.
- Lack of comparisons to alternative modeling paradigms and absence of quantitative human evaluation of generated questions.
- Mixed signals across evaluation metrics; some claims (generality) outpace the evidence.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The paper is interesting and well executed for a cognitive modeling venue, but for NeurIPS its empirical scope and comparative evaluation are currently too limited. With stronger baselines, broader validation, and some transfer or natural language grounding, I would be inclined to accept.

Confidence: 3/5
