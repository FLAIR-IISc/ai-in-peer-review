- Proposes modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs in a compositional grammar (lambda-calculus-like).
- Uses a probabilistic generative model that balances informativeness (EIG), program complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Evaluated on 605 human questions from a Battleship-like game; model predicts question frequencies with Spearman 0.64 and generates novel human-like questions.
- Frames contribution at the intersection of cognitive science (language of thought), compositional semantics, and active learning.
- Novel approach: models questions as programs rather than raw natural language, capturing compositionality and computability.
- Strong theoretical foundation tying compositionality and information-theoretic measures into the probabilistic model.
- Rigorous evaluation with ablation comparisons, leave-one-out cross-validation, and qualitative novel-question generation.
- Clearly differentiates from prior work in active learning, visual question generation, and dialog systems.
- Balanced presentation combining theoretical development and empirical validation on human data.
- Main limitation: grammar and model are highly domain-specific to the Battleship game.
- Significant gap between semantic program representations and natural language; no explored method to bridge to NL generation.
- Evaluation is limited to a single domain, reducing evidence of broader applicability.
- Some important technical details (grammar rules, feature sets) are only in supplementary materials rather than the main text.
- Lacks comparison to recent neural question-generation approaches that produce diverse questions.
- Recommendation to expand discussion on domain generalization and what parts of the grammar are domain-general vs. domain-specific.
- Suggests integrating semantic representations with natural language generation or neural paraphrasing methods.
- Recommends adding at least one more domain to strengthen claims of generality.
- Advises moving key technical details into the main paper for accessibility.
- Calls for brief comparisons to recent neural question-generation work to better position contributions.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-written, original, significant; recommended as Strong Accept (8/10) despite noted limitations.
