**Summary**

This paper proposes a computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking, framing the problem as one of program generation. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a LISP-like language that, when executed, return an answer. The authors define a probabilistic log-linear model over the space of programs generated by a hand-crafted grammar. The model's energy function is a weighted sum of features including informativeness (Expected Information Gain), complexity (related to program length), answer type, and relevance to the game domain. The model is trained and evaluated on a dataset of human questions asked in a "Battleship"-like game, where it is tasked with predicting the distribution of questions for a given game context. The results show that the full model provides a better fit to the human data than several ablated versions and can generate novel, plausible questions.

**Strengths**

*   **Novel Framing:** The central idea of treating question asking as probabilistic program generation is novel, ambitious, and elegant. It provides a powerful compositional framework that moves beyond the simple queries typical in active learning and the canned responses of many dialogue systems.
*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** The paper tackles the challenging and important problem of generating creative, context-sensitive, and goal-directed questions, which is a key aspect of human intelligence and a significant goal for AI.
*   **Principled Model:** The use of a probabilistic model with interpretable features (informativeness, complexity) allows for a clear analysis of the factors driving human question asking. The ablation study, in particular, offers insights into the trade-offs people make, especially the strong influence of question complexity.
*   **Generativity:** The model demonstrates the ability to generate novel, syntactically correct, and semantically meaningful questions that were not observed in the training data, highlighting the productive nature of the programmatic representation.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is compelling, the paper suffers from a significant number of minor-to-moderate flaws in its methodology, evaluation, and presentation that collectively undermine the contribution's solidity.

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   **Omission of Critical Experimental Details:** Several crucial details are relegated to footnotes, obscuring significant limitations. Footnote 4 reveals that 14 questions and a grammar rule were removed due to computational complexity. Footnote 7 states that two of the 18 contexts were dropped entirely, also for computational reasons. These exclusions are not trivial and should be transparently addressed and justified in the main experimental setup section.
    *   **Manual Preprocessing:** The entire framework relies on translating natural language questions into the formal programming language. This crucial step is not described at all. Was this done manually? How was consistency ensured? Without this information, the work is not reproducible, and it's impossible to assess how much of the "intelligence" is in the (unstated) translation process versus the model itself.
    *   **Inconsistent and Flawed Referencing:** The bibliography is unacceptably sloppy for a top-tier conference submission. Reference [12] begins "L, J. Johnson..." and [22] begins "d, F. Strub...", both appearing to be copy-paste errors. Citation styles are inconsistent throughout. This lack of care in presentation raises concerns about the care taken in the research itself.
    *   **Optimization Details:** The description of the optimization procedure is opaque. The formula for the approximation of the normalizing constant `Z` in Section 4.4, `Z ≈ Ex∼q[p(x;θ)q(x)]`, appears to be incorrect. The standard importance sampling estimator for `Z = ∑ exp(-E(x))` would be `Ex∼q[exp(-E(x))/q(x)]`. This potential error in a core mathematical component is a serious concern.

2.  **Methodological Concerns:**
    *   **Hand-Engineered Grammar and Features:** The model's performance relies heavily on a manually designed grammar and features. The paper states the grammar was designed to be "sufficient to represent all 605 questions," which suggests it was tailored post-hoc to the dataset, limiting claims of generalizability. Furthermore, features like the relevance filter `f∅(x)` feel like ad-hoc patches to compensate for a grammar that overgenerates irrelevant programs.
    *   **Computational Soundness:** The approximation of the partition function `Z` and the model expectations via importance sampling from a uniform PCFG proposal is a potential weak point. This is a vast, combinatorial space, and sampling from a simple proposal distribution is likely to have extremely high variance, potentially making the gradient estimates and the reported log-likelihoods unreliable. The choice of 150,000 samples is not justified.

3.  **Evaluation Issues:**
    *   **Contradictory Results:** There is a glaring contradiction in the results presented in Section 5. While the full model achieves the best log-likelihood, the reported Spearman's rank correlation for the full model (0.64) is slightly *worse* than that of the information-agnostic model (0.65). This directly undermines the paper's narrative about the importance of information gain as a key component for matching human behavior. This discrepancy is not acknowledged or explained.
    *   **Limited Dataset and Qualitative Analysis:** The model is evaluated on a small dataset (591 questions after exclusions, across 16 contexts). The qualitative results showing novel generated questions are interesting but are explicitly "selected to illustrate the creative range of the model." This is anecdotal. A more systematic evaluation, including uncurated samples (both good and bad), is needed to properly assess the model's generative capabilities.

**Recommendation**

I recommend **rejection**.

The paper introduces a highly promising and thought-provoking research direction. However, the execution and presentation fall short of the standards expected for NIPS. The work is undermined by a lack of transparency regarding data exclusions, a critical gap in describing the natural-language-to-program translation process, apparent errors in core mathematical formulas, and a significant contradiction in the reported evaluation metrics. While many of these are minor points individually, their sheer number suggests a lack of thoroughness that prevents me from confidently accepting the paper's claims. The core ideas are strong, and I would encourage the authors to revise the paper to address these substantial issues for a future submission.
