Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a computational framework for question asking in which questions are programs executed over possible world states. A PCFG/LISP-like grammar defines a compositional question space; a log-linear model assigns probabilities to programs via features capturing informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (−log probability under the grammar), answer type, and a relevance filter. Parameters are learned by maximizing the likelihood of human-asked questions using importance sampling over programs. Evaluation is on a Battleship-style dataset of 605 natural-language questions collected across 18 ambiguous contexts; the model predicts held-out context question distributions (leave-one-out) and samples novel, human-like questions.

This work connects active learning (typically limited to label queries) with formal semantics (questions as partitions over worlds) and probabilistic program induction. It contrasts with goal-directed dialog agents and visual question generation systems that rely on canned templates or large paired datasets, and with prior cognitive models focusing on EIG without a generative account of open-ended question production.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent: questions as typed programs, a PCFG to define the hypothesis space of programs, a log-linear model over features, and Bayesian EIG computed over a hypothesis space of board configurations. The training objective and gradient are standard for log-linear models; importance sampling approximates partition functions and feature expectations.

- Empirical support: The model achieves higher (approximate) held-out log-likelihood than ablations in most bootstrap samples and produces plausible novel questions. Crucially, the complexity prior is shown to be essential (complexity-agnostic performs poorly). However, the contribution of the informativeness feature is less clear: the full model’s rank correlation with human frequencies (0.64) is similar to, and slightly below, the information-agnostic variant (0.65), and the paper notes that EIG alone does not correlate with human frequencies. This weakens the empirical claim that informativeness is a dominant driver in this dataset (though the LL comparisons favor it).

- Methodological limitations and missing analyses:
  - The grammar and world model are hand-engineered to the Battleship domain; two contexts and 14 questions had to be dropped for computational reasons. This limits generality and completeness.
  - Approximation details are underspecified: whether the 150k program sample is fixed across optimization, variance of importance weights, and sensitivity to the proposal q. No diagnostics for estimation error of Z/gradients are reported.
  - No comparisons to simple data-driven baselines (e.g., template reuse across contexts, a sequence model over program strings, or a nearest-neighbor retriever) that would help contextualize the gains.
  - The novelty of sampled questions is evaluated qualitatively; no human judgments or task-based utility evaluation are provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well organized. The mapping from natural language to programs is explained with examples; the features and learning objective are explicit. Some crucial details are deferred to the supplement (full grammar, hypothesis space sizes). It would help to clarify: computational cost of EIG per program/context, whether program samples are resampled across iterations, and robustness to the choice of q. Reporting SEs/variance for LL and correlations would increase transparency.

Originality
Modeling question asking as program generation with a compositional grammar and optimizing a utility/complexity tradeoff is novel in this domain. While programs-as-meaning and EIG-based utility have precedents in formal semantics, program execution for QA, and cognitive modeling, bringing these together to generate open-ended, goal-directed questions is a noteworthy contribution.

Significance
The work advances the discussion on how to endow agents with richer query capabilities than label requests. It offers a concrete method that predicts human choices and can synthesize new questions. That said, the empirical scope is narrow (one domain, small dataset, hand-specified grammar), and the informativeness signal’s mixed role in the results suggests more work is needed to establish generalizable principles and practical utility. The impact is thus moderate: conceptually interesting, likely to inspire follow-up work on compositional query spaces and active learning, but not yet a drop-in method for broader settings.

Pros
- Clear, principled formalization connecting active learning, formal semantics, and program synthesis.
- Demonstrates that complexity priors are critical in modeling human question preferences.
- Generates semantically novel questions using a meaningful equivalence test (partition/MI-based).
- Provides a predictive, generative account of open-ended question asking beyond label queries.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar and world model; limited generalization.
- Informativeness feature yields mixed evidence (LL gains but no stronger rank correlation than info-agnostic).
- Evaluation lacks strong baselines and human judgments of generated questions; some data/contexts removed.
- Approximation/inference details and stability not fully quantified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: (i) template reuse across contexts, (ii) a neural sequence model over program strings, (iii) a random-PCFG or length/answer-type-only model. Report LL and rank metrics.
- Quantify importance sampling variance and sensitivity to q; clarify whether samples are fixed vs resampled.
- Include human evaluation of sampled questions’ usefulness and clarity in held-out contexts.
- Test portability: reuse the domain-general subset of the grammar in a different small domain to assess transfer.
- Explore alternative complexity priors (e.g., program length, MDL) and report their effect.
- Discuss/experiment with an end-to-end semantic parsing front-end to reduce manual engineering.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a compelling conceptual framework with a reasonably careful implementation and some predictive success. Despite empirical limitations and domain specificity, it is a stimulating contribution likely to interest NeurIPS audiences working on program induction, active learning, and language/meaning. Confidence: medium.
