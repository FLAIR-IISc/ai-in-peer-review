## NIPS 2017 Review: Question Asking as Program Generation

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for understanding human question asking by modeling questions as programs. The core idea is compelling: rather than treating questions as simple requests for labels, the authors propose that they can be represented as executable programs that, when applied to a possible world state, yield an answer. This reframes the problem as one of program synthesis. The authors demonstrate this approach on a dataset of questions collected during a “Battleship”-like game, showing that their model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions. 

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper effectively positions itself within the landscape of active learning, highlighting the limitations of current approaches that primarily focus on simple label requests. It contrasts its work with dialog systems, noting their reliance on pre-defined question sets or lack of goal-directedness. The connection to Visual Question Generation (VQG) is well-articulated, with the authors pointing out that their approach requires less training data and exhibits greater flexibility than existing VQG systems. The paper also appropriately cites relevant work in linguistics, cognitive science (compositionality, language of thought), and probabilistic programming.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel and Intriguing Approach:** Modeling questions as programs is a genuinely novel and insightful idea. It provides a formal framework for analyzing and potentially generating intelligent questions.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The link to program synthesis and the use of lambda calculus and LISP provide a robust theoretical underpinning. The concepts of compositionality and computability are well-motivated and contribute to the model's expressiveness.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on the Battleship dataset are encouraging. The ability to predict human question frequencies and generate plausible novel questions demonstrates the practical viability of the approach.
* **Detailed Model Description:** The paper provides a thorough explanation of the probabilistic model, including the grammar, features, and optimization procedure. The inclusion of supplementary material further enhances clarity.
* **Thoughtful Analysis of Related Work:** The authors accurately position their work within the broader fields of active learning, dialog systems, and cognitive science.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Domain Specificity:** The reliance on a specific game domain (Battleship) raises concerns about the generalizability of the approach. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it’s a significant one. The grammar, although aiming for generality, still appears tailored to the game's structure.
* **Complexity of the Model:** The model is relatively complex, involving a probabilistic grammar, feature engineering, and importance sampling for optimization.  This complexity makes it potentially difficult to scale to larger and more diverse domains.
* **Limited Quantitative Evaluation:** While the log-likelihood results are positive, the reliance on a single dataset and the potential for data sparsity (mentioned by the authors) limit the strength of the quantitative evaluation. A more rigorous comparison against simpler baselines would be valuable.
* **Lack of Human Evaluation of Generated Questions:** While the authors show that the model can *generate* questions, there's no formal human evaluation of the quality, relevance, or usefulness of these generated questions beyond observing their similarity to human questions.
* **The "Filter" Feature:** The introduction of the f∅(x) “filter” feature feels like a workaround for limitations in the grammar rather than a core component of the model's conceptualization of human question asking.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and insightful approach to modeling human question asking.
* The theoretical foundation is strong, drawing upon concepts from program synthesis and cognitive science.
* The empirical results are promising, demonstrating the feasibility of the approach on a challenging dataset.
* The paper is well-written and provides a clear and detailed description of the methodology.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The domain specificity of the model raises concerns about its generalizability.
* The complexity of the model could hinder its scalability to larger domains.
* The quantitative evaluation is limited and could benefit from a more thorough comparison against baselines.
* The lack of a direct human evaluation of generated questions leaves open the question of their true quality.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach. While the domain specificity and complexity are legitimate concerns, the strengths of the work – particularly its theoretical grounding and initial empirical success – outweigh these weaknesses. The paper represents a significant step towards developing more human-like question asking capabilities in machines. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Address Generalizability:**  Discuss potential strategies for adapting the grammar and model to different domains. Perhaps a section outlining the core domain-independent components and the domain-specific adaptations would be beneficial.
* **Strengthen Quantitative Evaluation:** Include a comparison against simpler baseline models (e.g., a unigram model of questions).
* **Conduct Human Evaluation:** Perform a human evaluation of the generated questions, asking participants to assess their relevance, clarity, and usefulness in the context of the Battleship game.
* **Clarify the Role of f∅(x):** Provide a more compelling justification for the “filter” feature or explore alternative ways to address the issue of irrelevant programs.
