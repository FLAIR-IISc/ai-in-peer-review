- Overall: Novel, compelling framework recasting question asking as program generation; recommend acceptance with minor revisions due to limited empirical evaluation.

- Main idea: Model questions as executable programs and frame question generation as program synthesis using a probabilistic grammar evaluated on a Battleship-like game.

- Relation to prior work: Extends active learning and compositional semantics, differs from VQG and goal-directed dialogue by enabling rich, compositional queries learned from limited data.

- Strength — Novelty: Framing question asking as program generation is original and insightful.

- Strength — Theoretical foundation: Strong grounding in formal language theory, cognitive science, and information gain theory.

- Strength — Compositionality: Exploits compositional structure to generate novel, meaningful queries.

- Strength — Empirical validation: Model predicts human question choices and can generate plausible human-like questions.

- Strength — Clarity: Well-written and explains complex concepts clearly.

- Strength — Dataset: Uses a well-defined existing dataset (Gureckis et al., 2017) as a solid evaluation base.

- Weakness — Limited evaluation: Experiments confined to a single, constrained domain, limiting claims about generality.

- Weakness — Dataset size: Only 605 questions, which may be too small for robust learning.

- Weakness — Optimization complexity: Importance sampling approximation may introduce biases; sensitivity analysis lacking.

- Weakness — Grammar specificity: Some grammar rules are domain-specific; adaptation to other domains is underexplored.

- Weakness — Feature engineering: Features appear ad-hoc; learned representations might perform better.

- Qualitative — Technical soundness: Probabilistic modeling, program synthesis, and EIG use are well-justified; ablations support component importance.

- Qualitative — Clarity issues: Overall clear, but Section 4.4 needs more detail on the optimization process.

- Qualitative — Originality: Approach is highly original and departs meaningfully from traditional methods.

- Qualitative — Significance: Potentially high impact for interactive learning and human-computer collaboration.

- Pro for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound framework with empirical feasibility and strong potential impact.

- Con for acceptance: Limited domain evaluation, small dataset, and reliance on approximate optimization.

- Suggestion — Generalization: Discuss adapting the grammar to other domains and key challenges for broader application.

- Suggestion — Scaling: Analyze computational complexity as domain size and question length grow.

- Suggestion — Alternative optimizations: Consider methods like variational inference to address importance sampling limits.

- Suggestion — Human evaluation: Conduct evaluations where humans rate informativeness/usefulness of generated questions.

- Suggestion — Feature representation: Explore learned feature representations instead of hand-engineered features.
