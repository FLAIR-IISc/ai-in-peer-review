Summary and relation to prior work
The paper models human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a small, typed, lambda-calculus/LISP-like language. Executing a program on a hypothesized world state returns an answer (Boolean, Number, Color, or Location). A context-free grammar (with some extensions: lambdas, map, set operations) defines the compositional question space. A probabilistic model assigns a distribution over programs via a log-linear energy with features: expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, an offset for zero-EIG, a complexity penalty derived from the probabilistic grammar, answer-type indicators, and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit by maximizing the likelihood of human questions using importance sampling over programs. Evaluation uses a Battleship-style dataset of 605 natural-language questions asked by 40 participants across 18 contexts, with leave-one-context-out training/testing. The model predicts human question distributions (log-likelihood, rank correlations) and samples novel, context-appropriate questions not present in the data.

This builds on partition semantics of questions and pragmatic modeling from linguistics and cognitive science, notably work that scores questions by information-theoretic utility and cost (e.g., Hawkins et al., 2015). It also connects to program-based representations in cognitive science (e.g., probabilistic LoT) and to program-executing question answering (e.g., semantic parsing, neural module networks). Relative to active learning (Settles), goal-oriented dialogue (Young; Bordes & Weston), and VQG, the contribution is a generative, compositional mechanism for synthesizing goal-directed questions in novel contexts, without relying on large paired corpora.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling choices are coherent: a typed grammar for computability, a log-linear energy for flexibility, and EIG for informativeness. Importance sampling is used to approximate intractable partition functions and feature expectations, with q given by the grammar’s PCFG. However, several aspects merit clarification: (i) variance and bias of the Z and gradient estimates given reuse of a fixed 150k-sample proposal across 100k updates; (ii) sensitivity to sample size and truncation (questions ≤100 functions); (iii) computational tractability of EIG (hypothesis space pruning, exact vs approximate enumeration). The reported ablations are informative: removing complexity severely harms fit, indicating necessity of a concise-question prior; removing information and type also reduces fit in likelihood terms. Yet rank correlations show the information-agnostic model slightly higher (0.65) than the full model (0.64), suggesting the contribution of EIG to rank ordering is modest, even if it helps likelihood. The paper also removes a stochastic “draw” operator and two largest contexts, which limits completeness.
- Empirics: LOOCV across 16 contexts is reasonable given data size. Reporting of likelihood comparisons includes bootstrap of Z, but more uncertainty quantification (e.g., CIs for correlations, per-context metrics) would strengthen the claims. Qualitative samples appear plausible and diverse; equivalence filtering via MI over answer distributions is a sound idea, though thresholds and details are deferred to SI.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The core model is described clearly; examples bridge formalism and the task. Some key technical pieces are in the supplement (full grammar, EIG details), which is acceptable but limits standalone reproducibility. Several references have formatting issues or missing years/authors; these should be corrected. It would help to more explicitly define the hypothesis space size per context and the EIG computation pipeline.

Originality
The main novelty lies in framing question production as probabilistic program synthesis with a compositional grammar, enabling generation of unseen questions, and combining this with EIG and a learned complexity prior. Prior pragmatic models typically select among predefined questions; this work synthesizes them. The components themselves (EIG, log-linear production, program execution) are known, but their combination for open-ended question generation is a meaningful extension.

Significance
Within computational cognitive science and goal-directed dialogue, the work is a useful step: it provides a predictive, generative account of human-like, context-sensitive question asking and illustrates a path to richer active querying. However, empirical scope is limited to a single constrained domain with a hand-engineered grammar, and the strongest driver of performance appears to be the complexity prior rather than informativeness. For broader NeurIPS impact, demonstrating portability to a second domain or a clearer path to natural language interfaces would strengthen significance.

Pros
- Clear formulation of questions-as-programs with a compositional, typed grammar.
- Principled utility (EIG) coupled with a learned complexity prior captures trade-offs humans make.
- Generates novel, interpretable, context-relevant questions without large paired corpora.
- Ablation study supports the importance of complexity, informativeness, and type features.
- Dataset link and many implementation details provided.

Cons
- Evaluation confined to a single, domain-specific setup; two contexts and one operator dropped for tractability.
- Heavy reliance on importance sampling with limited discussion of estimator variance, convergence, and sensitivity.
- EIG’s additive value appears modest in rank correlation; complexity dominates.
- No comparison to alternative generative baselines (e.g., grammar-only prior, retrieval+EIG, neural semantic parsers conditioned on context).
- Reference list contains multiple formatting errors; some details deferred to SI impede reproducibility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide variance/sensitivity analyses: effect of sample size, proposal distribution, and truncation on likelihood and correlations; report CIs.
- Add stronger baselines: (i) grammar prior only; (ii) retrieval/template methods; (iii) a simple sequence model over programs or a neural semantic parser, if feasible.
- Report per-context results and error analyses to identify where EIG helps most.
- Clarify EIG computation details (approximation, hypothesis space sizes) and generalization to larger spaces.
- Demonstrate portability by applying the framework to at least one additional domain with minimal grammar changes, or include a preliminary NL mapping layer.
- Fix and complete the citations.

Overall assessment
A solid, well-presented contribution that advances modeling of open-ended question asking via program synthesis, with promising qualitative generation and reasonable quantitative fit. The empirical scope and some methodological details limit impact; nonetheless, the framework is conceptually appealing and relevant to NeurIPS topics in active learning, semantic parsing, and cognitive modeling. Borderline to weak accept, contingent on improved evaluation and clarity.
