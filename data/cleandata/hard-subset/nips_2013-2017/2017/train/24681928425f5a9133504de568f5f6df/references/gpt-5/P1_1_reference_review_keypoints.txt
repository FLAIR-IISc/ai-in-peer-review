- Proposes modeling human question asking as generation of executable programs in a LISP-like language, evaluated by running on hypothesized world states.
- Uses a context-free grammar (with lambda, map, set ops) to define compositional question programs and a log-linear model over program features to assign probabilities.
- Key features: expected information gain (EIG), complexity (−log PCFG probability), answer type, and a relevance filter; parameters learned from human data.
- Inference approximates expectations and the partition function using importance sampling from the PCFG proposal.
- Evaluated on a Battleship-like information-search dataset (605 natural-language questions across 18 contexts) via leave-one-context-out prediction and qualitative sampling.
- Positions itself relative to active learning, linguistics (partition semantics), and program-induction approaches, contrasting with data-driven VQG/dialog systems.
- Strength: clear, compelling formulation that captures compositionality and computability of questions and aligns with cognitive/linguistic theory.
- Strength: principled utility modeling through Bayesian EIG formalization.
- Strength: grounded in human data with the grammar covering all translated questions.
- Strength: predictive performance beats ablations (complexity feature especially important) with respectable Spearman correlation to human frequencies.
- Strength: generative sampling yields novel, semantically rich, context-appropriate questions demonstrating productivity.
- Weakness: approximate inference is under-specified—use of a fixed PCFG proposal, reuse/resampling of 150k samples, and variance/stability concerns are unclear.
- Weakness: EIG computation is costly, leading to dropped contexts/questions; missing details on hypothesis set sizes, pruning, and runtimes.
- Weakness: optimization/regularization concerns (100k steps at lr=0.1, no regularization reported); lacks training vs held-out diagnostics and sensitivity analyses.
- Weakness: evaluation lacks external baselines beyond ablations (e.g., simple EIG−length heuristics, MDL, or neural baselines).
- Clarity issues: some garbled references, sparse pseudocode for EIG/Bayesian observer, and insufficient detail on mapping natural-language to programs and annotation effort.
- Originality: novel integration of program synthesis and utility-driven question selection, well situated in LOT/program-induction literature.
- Significance: advances modeling of human question asking and suggests paths for agents that ask compositional, informative questions, though portability beyond Battleship is uncertain.
- Suggested improvements: add external baselines (EIG−length, template ranker, neural seq2seq), report computational metrics and importance-sampling variance, and compare alternative complexity priors.
- Suggested improvements: conduct human evaluations of generated questions, test generalization to another micro-domain, and present a pathway or small experiment for mapping surface text to programs.
- Arguments for acceptance: conceptually strong, bridges multiple fields, and provides empirical evidence that EIG and complexity explain human question choices.
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow domain, hand-crafted grammar, missing baselines/human judgments, and under-specified inference/optimization raising robustness concerns.
- Overall recommendation: lean toward acceptance conditioned on clarifying inference details and adding at least one strong baseline or additional evaluation.
