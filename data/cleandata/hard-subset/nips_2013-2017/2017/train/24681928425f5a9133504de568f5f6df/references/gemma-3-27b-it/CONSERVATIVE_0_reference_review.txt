This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The core idea – representing questions computationally and leveraging program synthesis – is interesting and connects to work in linguistics, cognitive science, and AI. The authors demonstrate this approach on a dataset of questions asked during a “Battleship”-like game, showing their model can predict human question frequencies and generate plausible, novel questions.

While the premise is intriguing, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing as a substantial contribution to NIPS. My primary concern revolves around the limited novelty and empirical rigor. The idea of representing questions computationally isn't new (see references [12, 24] cited within the paper), and the connection to program synthesis, while a useful framing, feels more like a re-interpretation than a breakthrough. More critically, the evaluation lacks the robustness expected at NIPS. 

The primary evaluation involves predicting question frequencies in a held-out set of game contexts. While the authors report good correlations, the sparse nature of the human data (many questions asked only once) limits the reliability of these measures. The comparison to ablated models is helpful, demonstrating the importance of complexity, but the gains over simpler baselines (e.g., a language model predicting question sequences) are not adequately established. A more convincing evaluation would include comparison to established active learning methods (even those limited to simple label queries, to demonstrate an advantage beyond representational capacity) and a more thorough analysis of the generated questions. Simply stating that generated questions are “clever” (as in the conclusion) is insufficient. A quantitative evaluation of question “usefulness” – perhaps measured by information gain in reducing uncertainty about the game state – would be highly valuable.

The grammatical framework, while seemingly powerful, appears tailored to the specific game domain. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but the extent to which the grammar generalizes to other tasks remains unclear. The fact that domain knowledge was *required* to construct the grammar raises doubts about its broader applicability. The restriction to questions with 100 or fewer functions, and the removal of questions involving tile demonstrations due to computational complexity, are also concerning. These limitations suggest that the method may not scale well to more complex scenarios.

The probabilistic model itself is relatively standard (log-linear with feature engineering), and the importance sampling optimization appears computationally intensive. The details of feature engineering, particularly concerning the Bayesian belief updating and EIG calculation, are complex and could benefit from further clarification. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel framing of question asking as program generation.
*   The model demonstrates some ability to predict human question behavior.
*   The authors provide a dataset and implementation details, fostering reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The core idea lacks substantial novelty, building on existing concepts in computational linguistics and AI.
*   The evaluation is limited and lacks comparison to established baselines.
*   The method appears domain-specific and may not generalize well.
*   The computational complexity and feature engineering are substantial.
*   The conclusions are overstated, particularly regarding the “human-like” quality of generated questions.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the idea has merit and the work is technically sound, it falls short of the novelty, rigor, and significance expected at NIPS. The authors have identified an interesting problem, but the proposed solution feels more like a proof-of-concept than a substantial advance. Significant revisions addressing the concerns outlined above—particularly a more comprehensive evaluation against relevant baselines and a demonstration of broader generalizability—would be needed for reconsideration. The paper, as currently presented, does not represent a significant contribution to the field.
