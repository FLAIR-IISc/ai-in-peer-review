

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes modeling human question asking as program generation, where questions are represented as executable programs over a possible world state. The authors develop a grammar-based probabilistic model that generates questions by balancing informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance. They evaluate their model on a dataset of 605 natural language questions from a Battleship-like game, showing it can predict human question frequencies and generate novel human-like questions.

## Strengths
The paper presents a theoretically grounded approach to modeling question asking, with a clear connection to compositionality and computability in cognitive science. The grammar-based approach elegantly captures the compositional nature of human questions, and the probabilistic model provides a principled way to balance multiple question properties. The evaluation demonstrates the model's ability to predict human question frequencies with a Spearman correlation of 0.64 and generate novel, human-like questions.

## Weaknesses

**Quality Issues:**
1. The paper claims to use "leave one out cross-validation" but states they "had to drop contexts 1 and 2" due to "especially large hypothesis spaces" (Section 5). This undermines the cross-validation claim and introduces potential bias. The paper should clarify how these dropped contexts affect the evaluation and whether they were selected based on model performance.

2. In Section 4.4, the paper mentions removing the rule L → (draw C) and 14 questions due to "exponentially complex computations," but doesn't explain how this affects the model's generality or whether these questions would have been generated by the full model. This is a significant omission given the paper's claim about the grammar's ability to represent all human questions.

3. The "Relevance" feature (f∅(x)) is poorly explained (Section 4.5). The paper states it "marks questions that refer to the Battleship game board with a value of 1" but doesn't clarify how this feature is computed or how it differs from the other features. The footnote also reveals that f∅(x) and fEIG=0(x) are not identical, but this distinction isn't explained in the main text.

4. The paper states "We define X to be the set of questions with 100 or fewer functions" (Section 4.3), but provides no justification for this arbitrary limit. How was this value determined? What is the impact on the model if this limit is too low or too high?

**Clarity Issues:**
1. The paper uses "EIG" without spelling out the acronym on first use (Section 4.5), which is a minor but unnecessary confusion for readers.

2. The paper references "Table SI-1" and "Table SI-2" but these tables aren't included in the provided text, making it impossible to verify the grammar details.

3. In Section 4.1, the paper claims their approach is "closely related to formalizing question meaning as a partition over possible worlds [6]" but doesn't explain how their specific implementation differs from this prior work.

**Originality Concerns:**
The paper states "An important contribution of our work here is that it tackles question asking and provides a method for generating meaningful questions/programs from scratch" (Section 4.1), but doesn't sufficiently distinguish this from prior work in program synthesis. The reference to "Building a Semantic Parser Overnight" [24] suggests similar approaches exist, but the paper doesn't adequately explain how their question-generation approach differs from these semantic parsing works.

**Significance Limitations:**
The paper's focus on a narrow Battleship domain limits its broader significance. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't sufficiently address how their approach could generalize to more open-ended domains or practical applications. The claim that "many aspects of our grammar are domain general" (Section 6) is not substantiated with evidence of successful transfer to new domains.

## Technical Nuances
The paper's claim that "there is, surprisingly, no correlation at all between question generation frequency and EIG alone" (Section 5) is concerning. If human question frequency doesn't correlate with informativeness, the entire premise of their model (which heavily weights EIG) is questionable. The paper doesn't provide sufficient explanation for this surprising finding, nor does it explore whether this lack of correlation is specific to their domain.

## Formatting Inconsistencies
1. The paper contains a typo in the conference reference: "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems" should be "The Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems."

2. In Section 4.2, the paper uses inconsistent spacing around parentheses: "(size Blue)" versus " (size Blue)" in the same paragraph.

3. The paper states "the only rules given to players about questions was that they must be answerable using one word" but later describes questions with multiple-word answers (e.g., "What is the color of tile A1?"), creating confusion about the actual constraints.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting theoretical approach to modeling question asking, the methodological inconsistencies, unclear explanations of key components, and limited scope undermine its scientific contribution. The paper would benefit from a more thorough evaluation of the cross-validation procedure, clearer explanation of the relevance feature, and better justification for the arbitrary 100-function limit. The significant limitations in domain generality and the questionable correlation between EIG and question frequency further reduce the paper's significance.

The paper's technical approach is sound, but the execution contains several unresolved issues that prevent it from being a compelling contribution to NIPS. I recommend rejection due to the substantial methodological inconsistencies and lack of clear evidence for the model's broader applicability.
