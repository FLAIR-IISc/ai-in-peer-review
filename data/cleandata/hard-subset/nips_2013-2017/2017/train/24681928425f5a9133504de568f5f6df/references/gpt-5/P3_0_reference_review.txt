Summary
This paper models human question asking as program generation: questions are typed, executable programs in a small LISP-like language that return answers when run on candidate world states. A PCFG defines the compositional hypothesis space; a log-linear energy model over programs assigns probabilities conditioned on context via features for expected information gain (EIG), a zero-EIG indicator, a complexity penalty (−log PCFG probability), answer-type indicators, and a relevance filter. The model is trained with importance sampling to approximate gradients and the partition function. Using a dataset of 605 natural-language questions from a Battleship-like information search task, the model (i) predicts the distribution of human questions across held-out contexts (leave-one-context-out) and (ii) samples novel, human-like questions. Ablations suggest complexity and, to a lesser extent, EIG and answer-type priors contribute to fit.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formalization: Mapping questions to executable programs connects to well-established notions of questions as partitions over worlds and permits rigorous utility calculations (EIG).
- Compositional hypothesis space: The PCFG supports productivity beyond memorization, addressing a core limitation of prior template-based or retrieval-based approaches.
- Cognitive plausibility: The trade-off between informativeness and complexity aligns with behavioral observations that EIG alone poorly predicts human question frequency.
- Empirical evaluation: Leave-one-context-out prediction and qualitative sampling of novel questions demonstrate both predictive and generative capacity.
- Transparent assumptions and limitations: The paper is forthright about domain specificity, the use of semantic representations rather than surface language, and computational constraints.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited data and domain: 605 questions across 16–18 contexts (with two dropped for tractability) in a synthetic domain is small for NeurIPS standards, and some conclusions hinge on sparse counts (many singletons), limiting statistical power.
- Inference and estimation details: Importance sampling from a uniform PCFG as proposal for an energy model that may be highly peaked risks high-variance estimates. The paper lacks diagnostics (effective sample size, variance of Z, convergence behavior) and clarity on whether proposals are re-sampled each iteration. Using a fixed pool of 150k samples for 100k gradient steps may bias learning if support is insufficient.
- Ambiguity about EIG’s contribution: While the full model outperforms the information-agnostic model in log-likelihood in 81% of bootstrap samples, the reported correlation with human frequencies is slightly higher for the information-agnostic model (0.65 vs. 0.64). This weakens the claim that EIG substantively improves predictive fit; the narrative should reconcile these metrics.
- Complexity feature conflation: fcomp = −log q(x) uses the same PCFG that acts as the proposal, conflating cognitive simplicity with sampling bias and grammar idiosyncrasies. A simpler and more interpretable proxy (e.g., AST size, length, number of compositional operators) or human-rated complexity would clarify the effect.
- Natural language mapping: The paper relies on manual translation of natural-language questions to programs but provides no methodology, inter-annotator agreement, or error analysis. This is crucial for reproducibility.
- Evaluation of novelty: The “human-like” generation is evaluated qualitatively on selected contexts without human judgments or blinded evaluation, and the equivalence filtering via answer distribution mutual information is under-specified (thresholds, sensitivity).
- Baselines: Comparisons are restricted to ablations of the authors’ features. Missing are non-program baselines (e.g., retrieval from similar contexts, template banks with EIG ranking), or program-space alternatives (e.g., EIG-only with length penalty; length-only; uniform PCFG). These would help quantify how much the learned energy adds beyond simple heuristics.

Quality
The core methodology is sound and well-motivated. However, the importance sampling–based training requires more diagnostics and ablations to be fully convincing. The EIG computation relies on a hand-crafted prior over hypotheses and drops challenging contexts/operators, which may bias the evaluation. Claims about EIG’s importance should be tempered given the correlation results.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-written, with helpful examples. Important operational details are deferred to the supplement or prior work (the natural language-to-program mapping; grammar tables; IS diagnostics), limiting reproducibility. Adding a concise description of the annotation protocol and IS diagnostics would improve clarity.

Originality
Framing question asking as program generation is a novel synthesis that bridges active learning, formal semantics (questions as partitions), and program induction. While program-based QA and concept learning exist, using programs to generate questions and predict human question distributions is original and interesting.

Significance
As a cognitive-computational contribution, this is a notable step toward modeling open-ended, goal-directed question asking, with implications for interactive ML. For NeurIPS, significance is tempered by the narrow domain, small dataset, and limited generalization/NL interface. Still, the formulation could inspire follow-up work in interactive systems and active learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide IS diagnostics (ESS, variance of Z, sensitivity to proposal; whether proposals are refreshed; runtime) and consider stronger estimators (AIS, SMC) or MCMC in program space.
- Replace or augment fcomp with length/AST node count and show robustness; report correlations between complexity measures and human behavior.
- Clarify the NL-to-program mapping process and release mappings; report inter-annotator agreement and examples of ambiguous cases.
- Quantify EIG’s incremental value: report per-context improvements, confidence intervals; explain the discrepancy between LL and rank correlations.
- Add baselines beyond ablations (e.g., retrieval + EIG re-ranking; template program library; length-only).
- Include a small human evaluation of sampled questions’ usefulness and naturalness.
- Discuss portability: demonstrate on one additional micro-domain or show how much of the grammar is reused.

Questions for the authors
- Do you re-sample programs each gradient step, and what is the effective sample size across iterations?
- How sensitive are results to the grammar prior and to the choice of complexity proxy?
- How was equivalence via mutual information operationalized (thresholds, robustness)?
- Can you report the mapping protocol from text to programs and its reliability?

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant framing with clear links to active learning and semantics.
- Demonstrates compositional generalization in a way retrieval methods cannot.
- Empirical evidence that complexity matters substantially beyond informativeness.

Cons for acceptance
- Small, domain-specific dataset; qualitative generation evaluation.
- Inference/training methodology lacks diagnostics; some features conflate modeling choices with sampling artifacts.
- Limited evidence that EIG materially improves predictive power over simpler baselines.

Overall
Borderline. Strong conceptual contribution and promising direction, but empirical validation and methodological rigor need strengthening for NeurIPS. With added diagnostics, stronger baselines, and clearer NL mapping, the paper would be much more compelling.
