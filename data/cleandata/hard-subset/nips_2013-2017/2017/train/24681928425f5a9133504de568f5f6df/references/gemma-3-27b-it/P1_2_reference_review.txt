## Peer Review for "Question Asking as Program Generation"

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and compelling framework for understanding human question asking by modeling questions as programs. The core idea – recasting question generation as program synthesis – is insightful and offers a promising avenue for bridging the gap between human and machine intelligence in interactive learning scenarios. The authors demonstrate the feasibility of their approach by applying it to a well-defined information-seeking game and showing its ability to predict and generate human-like questions. While the technical details are reasonably thorough, the empirical evaluation, while promising, feels somewhat limited and leaves room for further investigation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary of Main Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper argues that humans learn effectively by asking rich, informative questions, going beyond the simple label requests typical of active learning. It proposes modeling these questions as executable programs that, when evaluated on a hypothetical world state, yield an answer. This shifts the problem to one of program synthesis, leveraging tools from formal languages and computational linguistics. The authors then build a probabilistic model to generate questions based on a defined grammar and evaluate it on a dataset of human questions from a “Battleship”-like game.

This work builds upon and distinguishes itself from several existing areas. It addresses the limitations of traditional active learning (Settles, 2018) by considering richer queries than simple labels. It moves beyond goal-directed dialogue systems (Serban et al., 2017) with their limited question sets and visual question answering (Jain et al., 2016) which often generates stereotypical questions. Unlike VQG (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016) which requires large datasets, this approach aims to learn from limited data by leveraging the compositional structure of questions. The connection to formal linguistics (Groenendijk & Stokhof, Thesis) and cognitive science (Fodor, 1987; Piantadosi et al., 2016) grounding questions in compositional semantics and a "language of thought" is particularly well-articulated.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The framing of question asking as program generation is a genuinely novel and insightful contribution.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the approach in formal language theory, cognitive science, and information theory (EIG) provides a strong theoretical foundation.
* **Compositionality:**  The recognition and exploitation of the compositional structure of questions is a key strength, enabling the generation of new, meaningful queries.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that the model can predict human question choices with reasonable accuracy and, crucially, *generate* plausible questions.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the complex concepts involved.
* **Well-defined dataset:** The use of the existing dataset from Gureckis et al. (2017) provides a solid base for evaluation.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is focused on a single, relatively constrained domain (the Battleship game). While valuable as a proof-of-concept, the generalizability of the approach to more complex scenarios remains unclear.
* **Dataset Size:** The dataset of 605 questions, while well-defined, is relatively small, potentially limiting the model's ability to learn robust representations.
* **Complexity of Optimization:** The importance sampling method used for optimization, while practical, is an approximation and may introduce biases. More details on the sensitivity of the results to the sampling parameters would be beneficial.
* **Grammar Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge the domain-specificity of some grammar rules, more discussion on how to adapt or generalize the grammar to other domains would be valuable.
* **Feature Engineering:** The choice of features feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic exploration of relevant features or the use of learned feature representations might improve performance.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The technical aspects of the model are sound. The use of probabilistic modeling, program synthesis, and information gain theory are all well-justified and implemented. The ablation studies strengthen the claims by demonstrating the importance of different components.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally very clear. However, Section 4.4 could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the optimization process.
* **Originality:** The approach is highly original and represents a significant departure from traditional active learning methods.
* **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. A machine capable of asking intelligent, informative questions could revolutionize interactive learning and human-computer collaboration.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound framework for modeling human question asking.
* The empirical results demonstrate the feasibility of the approach and its ability to generate plausible questions.
* The work has the potential to significantly advance the field of active learning and human-computer interaction.

**Con:**

* The evaluation is limited to a single domain.
* The dataset size is relatively small.
* The optimization process relies on approximations.




**5. Specific Suggestions & Questions:**

* **Generalization:** Can the model be applied to other domains without significant modifications to the grammar? What are the key challenges in adapting the approach to more complex scenarios?
* **Scaling:** How does the computational complexity of the model scale with the size of the domain and the length of the questions?
* **Alternative Optimizations:** Have you considered alternative optimization methods, such as variational inference, to address the limitations of importance sampling?
* **Human Evaluation:**  A more rigorous human evaluation of the generated questions (e.g., asking participants to rate their informativeness or usefulness) would strengthen the claims.
* **Feature Representation:** Explore learned feature representations instead of hand-engineered features.




**Overall, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the suggestions above. This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of active learning and has the potential to inspire further research in this exciting area.**
