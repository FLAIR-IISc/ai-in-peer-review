Summary
This paper proposes a computational model of human question asking that represents questions as executable programs and treats question generation as probabilistic program synthesis. A context-free grammar (with lambda abstraction, map, and set constructs) defines the question space; a log-linear model over program features assigns probabilities to questions, with features capturing expected information gain (EIG) under a Bayesian ideal observer, a complexity penalty derived from the PCFG prior, answer-type preferences, and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit by maximizing the likelihood of human questions in a Battleship-style information search task (605 questions over 18 contexts; two contexts later dropped for computational reasons). Approximate inference relies on importance sampling over programs drawn from the grammar. The model (and ablated variants) is evaluated via leave-one-context-out prediction of human question distributions, correlation with human frequencies, and qualitative generation of novel, human-like questions.

Relation to prior work
The work situates itself at the intersection of active learning, program induction, and cognitive modeling. It differs from most active learning (which queries labels/interventions) by modeling rich, compositional queries. It connects to semantic parsing/QA-as-program-execution and to probabilistic languages of thought but focuses on question generation rather than answering. It also contrasts with neural visual question generation and dialog systems by targeting goal-directed, compositional questions in novel contexts without large-scale supervised training.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formalization is coherent: a grammar-defined program space, a log-linear energy over features, and a Bayesian ideal observer to compute EIG. Importance sampling is a reasonable choice for approximating expectations over a large hypothesis space of programs.
- Empirical support: The full model outperforms ablations in held-out log-likelihood; correlations between model energies and human frequencies are moderate (average Spearman ~0.64). The model produces plausible novel questions.
- Limitations and approximations:
  - Informativeness appears to have a modest marginal contribution: the information-agnostic variant attains similar correlation (0.65 vs. 0.64) even if LL favors the full model (81% bootstrap). This weakens a key claim that EIG is a major driver of human question choice (also consistent with prior reports that EIG alone does not correlate with frequency).
  - The evaluation relies on a single, small domain (one game), with two contexts and 14 questions removed for computational reasons. This narrows the empirical basis.
  - The proposal distribution q is a uniform PCFG; importance weights may have high variance. It is unclear whether a fixed candidate pool (150k) is reused across optimization steps or resampled, and how sensitive results are to this choice.
  - The space contains many semantically equivalent programs; without canonicalization, learning may be distorted by multiple surface forms per meaning.
  - The complexity feature −log q(x) depends on grammarian choices and may conflate syntactic priors with cognitive costs.
  - No comparisons to stronger alternative generators (e.g., EIG-only with length penalty, PCFG-only ranker, neural sequence models over the program language with a copy/compositional bias, or pragmatic RSA-style objectives) beyond ablations.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The core idea and modeling choices are explained accessibly, with motivating examples. Some important details are deferred to the supplement (full grammar; exact hypothesis-space definitions), and a few computational choices would benefit from clarification (importance sampling procedure; convergence diagnostics; number/diversity of unique sampled programs; handling of semantic equivalence). The discussion is balanced and acknowledges limitations.

Originality
Modeling question asking as program generation with a grammar over executable semantics, combined with an information-theoretic utility and complexity prior, is a novel formulation relative to most active learning and VQG/dialog work. While it builds on known ingredients (Bayesian ideal observer, program induction, PCFG priors), the combination for predicting human question distributions and generating novel questions is original and timely.

Significance
As a proof of concept, the work is intriguing and relevant to NeurIPS audiences interested in active learning, program synthesis, and cognitively inspired models. However, its current impact is limited by the single toy domain, hand-engineered grammar, and heavy reliance on approximate sampling that may not scale. There is no demonstration of transfer to new domains or to natural language realization. Nonetheless, the framing could catalyze follow-up work on richer query spaces in active learning and on neurally-guided program generation for questions.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation of questions as executable programs; elegant use of compositional semantics.
- Probabilistic model with interpretable features balancing informativeness and complexity.
- Demonstrates prediction of human question frequencies and generation of novel, plausible questions.
- Dataset release and explicit acknowledgment of limitations.

Cons
- Empirical scope is narrow (one domain; some data dropped), and scalability is unclear.
- Informativeness feature’s added value is modest in correlation metrics; claims about optimizing for EIG need stronger evidence.
- Hand-engineered grammar and answer-type features are partly domain-specific; complexity prior depends on grammar design.
- Approximate inference details (importance sampling variance, convergence) and effects of semantic equivalence are underexplored.
- Missing comparisons to stronger baselines beyond ablations.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Quantify sensitivity to the proposal q and candidate-pool size; report effective sample sizes and convergence diagnostics.
- Provide results for an EIG-only + length penalty baseline and a PCFG-only baseline; consider RSA-style objectives with explicit cost.
- Canonicalize or merge semantically equivalent programs during training/evaluation to avoid duplication bias.
- Report absolute LLs with uncertainty; reconcile why the information-agnostic model correlates as well or better while LL favors the full model.
- Explore a second domain or demonstrate partial grammar reuse to substantiate generality.
- Discuss or prototype a mapping from programs to natural language to close the loop.

Overall assessment
The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and offers a creative, compositional approach to question generation that advances understanding of human question asking and suggests new directions for active learning. Despite limitations in scope and evaluation, I find it a worthwhile contribution. Recommendation: weak accept.
