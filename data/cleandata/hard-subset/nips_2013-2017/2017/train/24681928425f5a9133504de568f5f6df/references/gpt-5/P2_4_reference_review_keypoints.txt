- Proposes modeling question asking as program generation: executable, typed programs in a compositional language with a log-linear model over programs using features for informativeness (EIG), complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Trained to match human question frequencies in a Battleship-like information-search task (605 questions across 18 contexts; 16 modeled), and can predict held-out distributions and sample novel human-like questions.  
- Novelty: combines program-based question representation with Bayesian experimental design (EIG) to predict human question choices, distinct from neural VQG and dialog systems.  
- Technical soundness: coherent formulation (PCFG proposal, log-linear reweighting, EIG over hypothesis space), but relies heavily on importance sampling from fixed q and 150k samples without diagnostics.  
- Concern: using −log q(x) as the complexity feature ties the learned prior to the proposal q, potentially biasing results and increasing importance-sampling variance.  
- Scalability issue: EIG computation is expensive, causing two contexts and 14 human questions to be dropped, raising concerns about completeness and generality.  
- Evaluation shortcomings: log-likelihood comparisons are approximate; confidence intervals, sensitivity analyses (hyperparameters, grammar design), and sampling diagnostics are missing.  
- Ambiguity about informativeness: full model has better LL but Spearman ρ is slightly worse than the information-agnostic model, requiring clarification and per-context analysis.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and motivated with helpful examples, but some references are garbled and key implementation details should be moved from supplement to main text.  
- Originality: moderately high—representing questions as programs and combining with EIG is a distinctive angle despite existing work in semantic parsing and experimental design.  
- Significance: conceptually important for linking active learning, program induction, and cognitive models, but practical impact limited by single synthetic domain, small dataset, and hand-crafted grammar.  
- Strengths: principled program-based formulation, integration of EIG with complexity prior, ability to predict and generate human-like questions without large corpora, and use of semantic equivalence to ensure novelty.  
- Weaknesses: heavy reliance on hand-engineered domain-specific grammar, scalability and inference limitations, potential bias/variance from importance sampling, mixed evidence for informativeness, no NL grounding, and no human judgments of generated questions.  
- Suggested improvements: provide importance-sampling diagnostics (ESS, variance), use robust Z estimators (AIS/bridge sampling), test sensitivity to grammar/prior choices, clarify informativeness discrepancy, try a second domain, and add even a heuristic NL mapping or human evaluations.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject for NeurIPS main track—elegant and thought-provoking but currently limited by scope, scalability, inference approximations, and unclear ML impact; resubmission encouraged after addressing these issues.
