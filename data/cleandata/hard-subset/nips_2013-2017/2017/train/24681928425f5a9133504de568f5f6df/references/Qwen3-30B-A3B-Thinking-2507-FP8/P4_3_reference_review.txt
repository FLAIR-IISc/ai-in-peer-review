

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a computational framework modeling human question asking as program generation. The authors argue that questions can be represented as syntactically well-formed programs that, when executed on a possible world state, output an answer. They evaluate their model on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like information search game. The model uses a probabilistic generative approach with features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (via grammar log-probability), answer type, and relevance to the domain. The authors demonstrate that their full model significantly outperforms variants that omit key features in predicting human question frequencies and can generate novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

The work builds on and extends several prior areas: (1) active learning literature that has largely focused on simple label queries [21]; (2) computational linguistics work on modeling questions as programs [24, 12]; (3) cognitive science research on compositional language [10, 4, 15, 14]; and (4) recent work on visual question generation [16, 23, 11]. The paper distinguishes itself by modeling open-ended, goal-directed question asking rather than canned questions or large-scale VQG systems.

## Strengths
**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-justified. The authors provide a thorough evaluation using both quantitative metrics (log-likelihood comparisons, Spearman's correlation) and qualitative analysis of generated questions. Their model's ability to generate novel human-like questions demonstrates the model's capacity to capture human question-asking patterns. The paper appropriately addresses limitations, including the domain-specific nature of their grammar and the need for substantial domain knowledge.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors effectively explain the key concepts of compositionality and computability, and the grammar for question generation is well-described with concrete examples. The model description (Sections 4.1-4.6) is sufficiently detailed for experts to reproduce the work. The figures (Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate key concepts and results.

**Originality:** The core insight—modeling questions as executable programs—is novel and represents a significant shift from previous approaches to question asking. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work on active learning (which focuses on simple label queries), goal-directed dialog systems (which use canned questions), and VQG systems (which require large datasets). The use of program synthesis to model human question asking is a fresh perspective that advances the field.

**Significance:** The work addresses an important gap in active learning research, moving beyond simple label queries to rich, human-like questioning. The ability to generate novel, contextually appropriate questions has significant implications for developing more human-like AI systems. The model provides the first predictive account of open-ended human question asking, with potential applications in cognitive science and AI.

## Weaknesses
**Domain specificity:** While the authors acknowledge that some aspects of their grammar are domain-specific, they don't sufficiently address how much domain knowledge would be required to adapt the approach to new domains. The paper would benefit from more concrete discussion of transferability to other domains.

**Evaluation scope:** The evaluation is limited to a single domain (the Battleship game). While this is understandable given the data, it would strengthen the paper to discuss how the approach might scale to more complex domains or how the grammar could be extended.

**Feature engineering:** The paper relies on hand-crafted features (informativeness, complexity, etc.) rather than learning them from data. While this approach has merits for interpretability, it would be interesting to see how an end-to-end learning approach would perform.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Novel approach to modeling question asking as program generation
- Comprehensive evaluation demonstrating superior performance over model variants
- Ability to generate novel, human-like questions not present in training data
- Clear connection to cognitive science and computational linguistics literature
- Well-structured and technically sound methodology

## Arguments against Acceptance
- Limited evaluation to a single domain (Battleship game)
- Some domain-specific assumptions in the grammar that may limit broader applicability
- Feature engineering rather than end-to-end learning

## Overall Assessment
The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by introducing a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking. The approach is technically sound, well-evaluated, and represents a significant step toward understanding how humans generate rich, goal-directed questions. While the domain-specific nature of the grammar is a limitation, the authors appropriately acknowledge this and provide a clear path forward for extension to other domains. The work is highly relevant to NIPS as it bridges cognitive science and machine learning, addressing an important gap in active learning research. The paper meets all four criteria for quality at NIPS: it is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant.

The paper should be accepted for publication at NIPS. It represents a meaningful contribution to the field that advances our understanding of human question asking and provides a new direction for more human-like AI systems.
