- Models question asking as lambda/LISP-style programs sampled from a PCFG and scored by a log-linear model using EIG, complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Parameters learned to maximize likelihood of 591 human-authored questions from a Battleship-like domain using importance sampling and ablation tests.  
- Connects to active learning, formal semantics, program induction, and cognitive models; contrasts with data-heavy VQG/dialog work.  
- Formalization is coherent and interpretable; ablations show complexity control is important.  
- Inference relies on heavy approximations: importance sampling from a fixed 150k program pool risks bias and high variance without reported diagnostics.  
- No ESS/variance/sensitivity analyses or details on whether the sampler is resampled per gradient step.  
- Dataset is small, single-domain, and handcrafted (grammar/primitives), with contexts and operators dropped due to computation.  
- Evaluation is narrow: held-out contexts from the same domain, qualitative novel-question samples without human judgment, and lack of strong baseline comparisons.  
- Reported correlation results (full model 0.64, info-agnostic 0.65) undercut claims about informativeness; LL improvements lack absolute magnitudes and clarity.  
- EIG depends on a manually specified hypothesis space and exact program execution; some questions were removed for computational complexity.  
- Relevance feature requires manual annotation of board-referencing constructs.  
- Paper is clear and well organized, but omits important implementation details (hypothesis space sizes, sampler behavior, estimator formula and typo, computational cost).  
- The central idea is novel for the NeurIPS community and well situated in prior literature.  
- Potential significance for cognitive/computational accounts of questioning, but limited ML impact due to domain specificity and engineering needs.  
- Pros: principled executable-program formulation, interpretable features with informative ablations, public dataset, and creative novel-question samples.  
- Cons: heavy reliance on handcrafted grammar and annotations, small single-domain data, dropped operators/contexts, thin inference diagnostics, and mixed evaluation signals.  
- Requested clarifications: sampler resampling policy, ESS/variance/sensitivity diagnostics, absolute LL gains with CIs, hypothesis space sizes and runtimes, handling of “draw” operator, and stronger baselines/human evaluations.  
- Recommendation: Borderline reject pending stronger empirical validation (additional domains, better baselines, human judgments, clearer inference diagnostics).
