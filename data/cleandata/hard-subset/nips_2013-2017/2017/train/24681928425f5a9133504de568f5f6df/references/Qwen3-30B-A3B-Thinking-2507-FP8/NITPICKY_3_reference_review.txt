

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes modeling natural language questions as executable programs that can be evaluated on possible world states, with the goal of predicting and generating human-like questions. The authors present a grammar-based program synthesis approach and evaluate it on a dataset of questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like game.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**
The paper builds on the idea that questions can be viewed as programs that compute answers from world states, connecting to previous work on program synthesis for question answering (e.g., [24, 12]) and compositional language models (e.g., [10]). It differs from prior work by focusing on human question generation rather than question answering, and by incorporating information-theoretic features to model question usefulness.

**Major Technical Flaws**

1. **Data Set Discrepancy**: The paper states it uses 605 question-context pairs across 18 contexts with 26-39 questions per context. However, 18 contexts × 26 questions = 468, while 18 × 39 = 702, neither matching 605. This inconsistency undermines confidence in the dataset description and analysis.

2. **Cross-Validation Procedure**: The authors claim "leave one out cross-validation" but state they "fit to 15 contexts and asked to predict the remaining one" (16 contexts total), contradicting the data set description of 18 contexts. They also omit contexts 1 and 2 due to "large hypothesis spaces" without explaining why these contexts were excluded from the cross-validation procedure. This creates ambiguity about the evaluation methodology.

3. **Grammar and Novelty Claims**: The paper claims "the grammar can represent all 605 questions in the human data set" but provides no evidence for this beyond a reference to supplementary materials. The grammar's design lacks justification for why specific rules were chosen, making it unclear how generalizable the approach is. The claim of being "the first predictive model to date of open-ended human question asking" is overstated given prior work on VQG [16, 23, 11] and dialog systems [25, 1].

4. **Filter Feature Implementation**: The "filter" feature f∅(x) is described as marking questions that "refer to the Battleship game board" but the implementation details are missing. How exactly is this feature calculated? How many questions were filtered out? Without this information, it's impossible to assess its impact on the model.

**Clarity and Presentation Issues**

1. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper uses "program" and "question" interchangeably without always clarifying whether it refers to the formal representation or the natural language expression.

2. **Missing Figures and Tables**: The paper references "Figure 3" and "Tables SI-1 and SI-2" but these are not included in the provided text, making it difficult to verify claims about the model's performance and grammar.

3. **Grammatical Errors**: Multiple grammatical errors appear throughout the paper, including "the the subtle differences" on page 4 and inconsistent use of punctuation in technical descriptions.

4. **Evaluation Ambiguity**: The paper states "the full model's log-likelihood advantage over the complexity-agnostic model held in 100% of the bootstrap samples" but doesn't provide the actual log-likelihood values or their magnitudes, making it impossible to assess the practical significance of the results.

**Minor but Significant Issues**

1. The paper claims "the grammar can produce syntactically coherent programs that have no reference to the game board" but then states they removed 14 questions due to a rule "L → (draw C)" without explaining how this rule was related to the "no reference" issue.

2. The model's claim of generating "novel" questions is problematic since the authors reject samples equivalent to human questions based on "mutual information of their answer distributions" rather than linguistic novelty, which may not align with the human definition of "novel."

3. The paper mentions "the only rules given to players about questions was that they must be answerable using one word" but provides examples of questions that would require more than one word to answer (e.g., "What is the size of the red ship?").

**Overall Assessment**

While the paper presents an interesting formalization of question asking as program generation, the technical flaws in the methodology, unclear implementation details, and significant presentation issues outweigh the novelty of the approach. The data set discrepancy, cross-validation confusion, and lack of sufficient justification for key claims undermine the paper's scientific rigor. The paper is not sufficiently detailed to allow replication, which is a critical requirement for NIPS.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**

*Pro*: The formalization of questions as programs is conceptually interesting and connects to several important lines of work in computational linguistics and cognitive science.

*Con*: The methodological flaws, unclear evaluation procedures, and presentation issues significantly weaken the contribution. The paper would benefit from a complete reworking of the methodology section with clear implementation details, corrected data set descriptions, and more precise claims.

The paper has potential but currently lacks the technical rigor and clarity expected for NIPS. I recommend rejection.
