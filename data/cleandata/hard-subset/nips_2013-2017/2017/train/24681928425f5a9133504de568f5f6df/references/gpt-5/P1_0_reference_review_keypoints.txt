- Proposes a probabilistic model of human question generation representing questions as executable programs in a typed, LISP-like language with a CFG hypothesis space.
- Uses a log-linear model with features: expected information gain (EIG), program complexity (−log grammar probability), answer-type priors, and a relevance flag.
- Learns parameters by maximizing human question likelihood via importance sampling and evaluates on 605 questions from a Battleship-like game with leave-one-context-out prediction and sampling.
- Connects active learning, formal semantics of questions, languages-of-thought probabilistic program induction, and program-based QA literature.
- Strength: clear, principled framing of questions as executable programs enabling computation of informativeness and compositional reuse.
- Strength: generative probabilistic model that predicts human question frequencies and can synthesize novel, context-sensitive questions; features and ablations are transparent.
- Strength: empirical findings show complexity strongly influences questions, EIG alone is insufficient, and answer-type preferences matter.
- Strength: methodology grounded in Bayesian belief updating and information-theoretic objectives, linking cognitive plausibility and ML.
- Strength: grammar covers all dataset questions and novelty filtering via answer-distribution equivalence is thoughtful.
- Weakness: narrow domain and small dataset with many hand-engineered predicates/constants limit generality and scalability.
- Weakness: evaluation depends on approximate log-likelihood with importance sampling and sparse human frequencies; some significance claims are modest.
- Weakness: full model’s energy correlates only marginally better than an information-agnostic variant, complicating claims about informativeness improving fit.
- Weakness: importance sampling from a uniform PCFG may have high variance/bias against rare-but-good questions with no diagnostics provided.
- Weakness: complexity proxy (−log q(x) under uniform PCFG) may not capture cognitive/computational complexity; alternative priors not explored.
- Weakness: limited baselines—no simple heuristics, neural program-sequence models, or pragmatic/RSA-style comparisons.
- Weakness: generated questions assessed qualitatively only; no human evaluation of usefulness/naturalness or quantitative metrics beyond novelty.
- Quality: technically sound and principled, but key computational details are deferred to supplements and diagnostics (proposal distribution, length cap, optimization) are missing.
- Clarity: generally well written, though precise grammar rules, hypothesis space enumeration, runtime, and NL→program mapping are under-detailed or in supplement.
- Originality: novel methodological/conceptual contribution modeling question asking as program generation combining EIG, complexity, and type features.
- Significance: promising path toward executable, goal-directed query generation, but current impact limited by domain specificity and scale.
- Suggestion: provide importance-sampling diagnostics (ESS, variance, sensitivity to samples/length cap).
- Suggestion: explore alternative complexity priors (MDL/operator costs, depth constraints) and report effects.
- Suggestion: add baselines (complexity+type-only, neural program models, template/n-gram), and run a small human study for generated question quality.
- Suggestion: report Bayesian ideal observer details (hypothesis space sizes, pruning, runtime) and discuss generalization to other domains.
- Pros for acceptance: conceptually novel, transparent/interpretable model, and empirical evidence that complexity/type shape human question choice.
- Cons for acceptance: limited scale/domain, modest quantitative gains over ablations, insufficient diagnostics and ML baselines.
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept (weak accept) — compelling and relevant but needs stronger evaluation and baselines.
