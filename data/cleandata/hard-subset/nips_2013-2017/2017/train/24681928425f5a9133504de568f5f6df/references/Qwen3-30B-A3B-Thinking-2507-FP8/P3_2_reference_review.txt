

# Peer Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as programs that can be executed on possible world states to produce answers. The authors argue that human questions exhibit compositionality (building complex questions from simpler conceptual components) and computability (questions as programs that evaluate to answers), and they formalize this as a probabilistic program generation model. The model incorporates features for informativeness (using Expected Information Gain), complexity (favoring shorter programs), answer type (Boolean, Number, Color, Location), and relevance (ensuring questions reference the game board). They evaluate the model on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like information-search game, demonstrating that the model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper builds on prior work in cognitive science (e.g., Fodor's Language of Thought), program induction (e.g., Lake et al., 2015), and active learning, but makes a significant contribution by applying these concepts specifically to modeling human question asking as a program synthesis problem rather than treating questions as natural language sequences.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper makes a compelling case for modeling questions as programs, drawing on established concepts in cognitive science (compositionality) and computational linguistics (program semantics). The formalism is well-motivated and provides a clear computational framework for understanding question asking.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation using both quantitative (log-likelihood comparisons across model variants) and qualitative (novel question generation) assessments. The ablation studies (lesioning key components) provide strong evidence for the importance of each feature (informativeness, complexity, etc.) in human question generation.

3. **Novelty of Approach**: The paper makes a clear distinction between previous work on question generation (which typically treats questions as natural language sequences) and their program-based approach. The demonstration of novel question generation that humans would actually ask (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?") is particularly impressive.

4. **Rigorous Modeling**: The probabilistic model is well-defined with clear mathematical formalism. The use of Bayesian belief updating to compute Expected Information Gain (EIG) is particularly strong, providing a principled way to measure question informativeness.

5. **Insightful Cognitive Contribution**: The paper provides important insights about how humans balance informativeness with complexity when generating questions, which is a significant contribution to computational cognitive science.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The evaluation is restricted to a single domain (Battleship game) with a relatively small dataset (605 question-context pairs). This limits the generalizability of the findings and makes it difficult to assess how well the approach would transfer to more open-ended domains. The paper doesn't provide comparisons to other question generation models (e.g., neural sequence-to-sequence models) on this dataset, which would strengthen the evaluation.

2. **Lack of Natural Language Generation Component**: The paper models questions as programs but doesn't address how these programs would be converted to natural language. While they mention this could be bridged with "recent tools in computational linguistics," this is a significant gap in the proposed framework that would need to be addressed for practical applications.

3. **Domain-Specific Knowledge Requirements**: The authors acknowledge that their grammar is domain-specific, which is a limitation. While they argue that some aspects are domain-general (logical connectives, set operations), the paper doesn't sufficiently address how much domain knowledge would be required to apply this approach to new domains. This is a significant hurdle for real-world deployment.

4. **Evaluation of Novel Questions**: The method for determining whether a generated question is "novel" (based on mutual information of answer distributions) is somewhat ad hoc and not fully justified. The paper doesn't provide a clear explanation of why this measure is appropriate for assessing novelty in question generation.

5. **Comparison to Related Work**: While the paper cites relevant work, it could better distinguish itself from recent advances in neural question generation. For example, it doesn't compare against approaches like Jain et al. (2016) for diverse question generation or the more recent work on program synthesis for visual question answering (e.g., Johnson et al., 2017).

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Expand Evaluation to Additional Domains**: If possible, the authors should demonstrate their approach on a second domain to better establish its generalizability. Even a small-scale extension to a different game or scenario would strengthen the paper.

2. **Address Natural Language Generation**: The paper should include a brief discussion of how the program representation could be mapped to natural language, perhaps referencing existing work on program-to-text generation (e.g., Wang et al., 2015).

3. **Clarify Domain Knowledge Requirements**: The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of how much domain knowledge would be needed to apply this approach to new domains, and whether the domain-specific components could be learned rather than engineered.

4. **Improve Novelty Assessment**: The paper should provide a more rigorous justification for their novelty metric and potentially compare against other metrics for question novelty.

5. **Add Comparison to Neural Baselines**: Including a comparison to neural sequence-to-sequence models (e.g., using the same dataset) would strengthen the evaluation by showing the relative advantages of the program-based approach.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to understanding human question asking and modeling it computationally. The theoretical foundation is strong, the methodology is well-executed, and the results are convincing within the domain of evaluation. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in computational models of question asking.

However, the limited scope of the evaluation and the lack of discussion about the natural language generation component are significant limitations that prevent the paper from making a broader impact. The approach is promising but would need to demonstrate applicability beyond the specific Battleship domain to be truly groundbreaking.

## Recommendation

**Accept with Minor Revisions**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, particularly in bridging cognitive science and machine learning approaches to question asking. With the suggested improvements (particularly expanding the evaluation scope and addressing the natural language generation gap), it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The current version is strong but would benefit from the additional clarifications and comparisons mentioned above.

## Strengths vs. Weaknesses Summary

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical foundation in compositionality and computability
- Comprehensive evaluation with ablation studies
- Novel program-based approach to question generation
- Demonstrated ability to generate novel, human-like questions
- Important insights about human question generation

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited evaluation to a single domain
- Lack of natural language generation component
- Unclear domain knowledge requirements
- Ad hoc novelty assessment
- Limited comparison to neural question generation baselines

The paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and the proposed approach has clear potential for future work in human-AI interaction and active learning. With the suggested revisions, this would be an outstanding contribution to the field.
