This paper proposes a framework for modeling human question asking as program generation, leveraging compositionality and computability. The core idea – representing questions as programs that, when executed on a world state, yield an answer – is intriguing and attempts to bridge the gap between traditional active learning and the richness of human inquiry. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions asked during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating its ability to predict human question frequencies and synthesize novel, human-like questions.

While the premise is novel and potentially impactful, the execution and presentation fall short of NIPS standards due to a multitude of issues, ranging from minor inconsistencies to substantial methodological concerns. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Framing:** The perspective of framing question asking as program generation is a valuable contribution, potentially opening new avenues for research in active learning and human-computer interaction.
*   **Dataset Utilization:** The authors effectively utilize the existing dataset [19] to validate their model.
*   **Generative Capability:** The model demonstrates a capacity to generate novel questions, which is a promising step towards more human-like AI systems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Grammar Specification & Scalability:** The grammar presented (Table SI-1 in the supplementary material) feels *ad hoc* and lacks a strong theoretical justification. The limitation to questions with 100 or fewer functions (footnote 3) is a concerning practical constraint, raising questions about the generalizability of the approach. The removal of questions involving tile demonstration further underscores this limitation.
*   **Informativeness Metric (EIG):** The reliance on Expected Information Gain (EIG) as the primary measure of informativeness appears overly simplistic. While EIG is a reasonable starting point, the authors fail to adequately address its known limitations (e.g., sensitivity to prior distributions, computational complexity). The Bayesian ideal observer component, while mentioned, lacks sufficient detail to assess its robustness.
*   **Feature Engineering:** The feature set, while attempting to capture various aspects of question quality, feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks a clear motivation. The inclusion of a “filter” feature (f∅(x)) to identify questions related to the game board suggests the grammar is capable of generating irrelevant questions, a design flaw that should have been addressed through a more constrained grammar.
*   **Optimization Details:** The optimization procedure (gradient ascent with importance sampling) lacks sufficient detail. The choice of learning rate (0.1) and number of iterations (100,000) are presented without justification. A more thorough ablation study on hyperparameters is needed.
*   **Evaluation Limitations:** The leave-one-out cross-validation, while standard, provides a limited assessment of the model's generalizability. The reliance on Spearman’s rank correlation as the primary evaluation metric is insufficient. A more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to baseline methods (e.g., random question generation, rule-based systems), is necessary.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from several instances of imprecise language and unclear explanations. For example, the connection between the lambda expressions and the “language of thought” ([3]) feels tenuous and underdeveloped. The discussion of the model's limitations is superficial.
*    **Minor Errors & Inconsistencies:** Several minor issues detract from the paper’s professionalism: the inclusion of URLs instead of formal citations (e.g., the GitHub link), the lack of consistent formatting in the supplementary material, and a few instances of grammatical errors.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The technical details are insufficiently robust and lack theoretical grounding.
*   The evaluation is limited and fails to demonstrate significant improvement over plausible baselines.
*   The presented grammar lacks scalability and practical applicability.
*   The paper's presentation is marred by clarity issues and minor inconsistencies.

**For:**

*   The core idea of framing question asking as program generation is novel and potentially impactful.
*   The model demonstrates a limited ability to generate human-like questions.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the intriguing premise, this paper is not ready for acceptance at NIPS. The methodological weaknesses, evaluation limitations, and presentation issues outweigh the potential novelty of the approach. The authors need to address the concerns regarding the grammar, informativeness metric, feature engineering, optimization procedure, and evaluation methodology. A more rigorous and comprehensive analysis is required to demonstrate the viability of this approach and its potential to advance the field of active learning. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should consider revising and resubmitting to a venue with less stringent standards.
