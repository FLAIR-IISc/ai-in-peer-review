### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The central idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. When executed on a possible world state, these programs return an answer. This approach recasts the challenge of generating a good question into synthesizing an optimal program.

The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like information-seeking game. They define a context-free grammar that specifies the space of possible questions (programs). To predict which questions humans are likely to ask in a given game context, they propose a log-linear probabilistic model. The probability of a question is determined by its "energy," a weighted sum of several features: informativeness (modeled as Expected Information Gain, EIG), complexity (derived from the program's structure), answer type (e.g., Boolean, Number), and relevance to the game. The model's parameters are fit to the human data using gradient ascent with importance sampling to handle the intractable normalization constant.

This work connects to several research areas. It extends traditional active learning, which typically involves simple label queries, to a much richer, compositional query space. It contrasts with contemporary dialogue systems and visual question generation models, which are often less flexible, less goal-directed, or require massive datasets. The framework builds upon foundational ideas in cognitive science, such as the "language of thought" and representing concepts as programs, and applies them to the novel problem of question *generation*, whereas prior work often focused on question *answering*.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-formulated, and the choice of a log-linear model with interpretable features is appropriate. The use of Expected Information Gain (EIG) as a measure of informativeness is principled and grounded in Bayesian reasoning. The authors are careful in their methodology, employing importance sampling to approximate the intractable partition function and using leave-one-out cross-validation for evaluation. The ablation studies, which systematically remove feature groups (informativeness, complexity, answer type), provide clear evidence for the contribution of each component to the model's performance. The authors are also transparent about the model's limitations, such as the need to remove certain computationally complex questions from the dataset.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, and the core idea of "questions as programs" is explained with illustrative examples. The sections detailing the grammar, the probabilistic model, the features, and the optimization procedure are presented logically and are easy to follow. The inclusion of figures showing example contexts, human questions, and model-generated questions greatly enhances the reader's understanding. The writing is precise and provides sufficient detail for an expert to grasp the technical approach.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of this work is its novel conceptualization of question asking as program synthesis. While the component ideas (probabilistic grammars, log-linear models, EIG) are established, their combination to create a generative, goal-directed model of rich, open-ended questions is highly original. This provides a new and powerful formalism for active learning and computational cognitive science. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior work by tackling the generation of questions from scratch in a context-sensitive manner, rather than selecting from a predefined set or learning statistical patterns from large, non-goal-directed corpora.

**Significance:** The work represents a significant step forward in modeling a complex and fundamental aspect of human intelligence. The results are important for both AI and cognitive science. For AI, it suggests a path toward more human-like active learning agents that can probe their environment in flexible and efficient ways. For cognitive science, it provides the first quantitative, predictive model of open-ended human question asking, offering insights into how people balance informativeness and complexity. The demonstration that the model can generate novel, sensible questions not seen in the training data is particularly compelling and highlights the framework's generative power. Other researchers are likely to build upon this programmatic approach to inquiry.

Despite these strengths, the work has limitations:

1.  **Reliance on a Hand-Crafted Grammar:** The model's capabilities are fundamentally constrained by the predefined grammar. This grammar is tailored to the "Battleship" domain, and designing such a grammar for a new, more complex domain would require significant expert knowledge and engineering effort. This raises questions about the scalability and generalizability of the approach. The paper acknowledges this but does not offer a clear path toward learning this structure automatically.
2.  **Abstraction from Natural Language:** The model operates entirely on the semantic level of programs, bypassing the difficult problems of natural language understanding (parsing questions into programs) and generation (translating programs into fluent questions). While this is a reasonable simplification for a foundational study, it means the system is not an end-to-end model of question asking.
3.  **Domain Specificity:** The evaluation is confined to a single, relatively constrained game environment. While the game is well-suited for studying goal-directed inquiry, the model's success in this domain does not guarantee its applicability to more open-ended, real-world scenarios where the space of possible world states and relevant predicates is vastly larger and less well-defined.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly original and elegant framework for modeling a complex cognitive ability.
*   The technical execution is sound, and the experimental evaluation is rigorous and well-supported by ablation studies.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and impactful.
*   The ability to generate novel, contextually relevant questions is a strong demonstration of the model's generative power.
*   The work has high significance for both active learning and computational cognitive science.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The reliance on a hand-engineered, domain-specific grammar limits the model's scalability and generality.
*   The model sidesteps the challenge of interfacing with natural language text.
*   The evaluation is performed on a single, controlled task, and its performance in more complex domains is an open question.

In summary, the conceptual novelty and significance of modeling question asking as probabilistic program generation are substantial. The paper presents this idea clearly and supports it with a sound technical implementation and a solid empirical evaluation within its chosen domain. While the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar is a notable limitation, it is an acceptable simplification for a paper introducing a new paradigm. The strengths of this work far outweigh its weaknesses, making it a strong contribution to the field.
