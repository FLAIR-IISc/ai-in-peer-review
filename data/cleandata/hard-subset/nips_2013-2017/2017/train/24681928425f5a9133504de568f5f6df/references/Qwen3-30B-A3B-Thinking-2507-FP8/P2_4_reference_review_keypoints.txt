- Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs generated by a grammar to capture compositionality and computability.
- Develops a probabilistic grammar-based program generation model with features for expected information gain, complexity, answer type, and relevance.
- Evaluates on 605 human questions from a Battleship-like game, showing better predictive performance than lesioned variants.
- Uses rigorous evaluation (leave-one-out cross-validation and bootstrapped confidence intervals).
- Finds question complexity is as important as informativeness for predicting human question asking.
- Demonstrates the model can generate novel, human-like questions (15% novelty across contexts).
- Grounds the approach in cognitive science and computational linguistics, bridging multiple literatures.
- Paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and reproducible with detailed supplementary materials.
- Exceptionally well-written and organized, accessible to the target audience.
- Highly original: first to treat questions as executable programs rather than labels or strings.
- Significant contribution to active learning and computational cognitive science, advancing beyond simple label requests.
- Limitations include domain specificity (Battleship), a semantic-to-natural-language gap, limited human data, and untested scalability to complex domains.
- Strengths summarized: novel framework, empirical validation, compositionality, generative ability, and thoughtful discussion of limitations.
- Recommendation: accept — the paper is original, significant, clear, and technically sound.
