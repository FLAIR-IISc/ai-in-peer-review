Summary
This paper models question asking as program generation within a compositional, LISP-like language. Questions are programs executed on hypothesized world states (Boards in a Battleship-like game), yielding answers. The model defines a log-linear distribution over programs with features for expected information gain (EIG), complexity (via PCFG prior), answer type, and a relevance filter. Parameters are learned to fit human question distributions collected in an information-seeking task. Optimization uses importance sampling over programs from a uniform PCFG proposal; evaluation uses leave-one-context-out cross-validation to compare full and lesioned models (without EIG, without complexity, without type features). The full model yields higher approximate log-likelihoods and moderate rank correlation (avg. Spearman 0.64) with human frequencies; the model can also sample novel, context-sensitive questions.

Relation to prior work: Connects to active learning (Settles), inquisitive semantics (Groenendijk & Stokhof; Roberts), program induction/LOT (Goodman et al.; Lake et al.), and semantic parsing/program execution for QA (Wang et al.; Johnson et al.). Compared to VQG and goal-oriented dialog, it aims at goal-directed, compositional question generation with Bayesian utility.

Strengths
- Clear, interesting framing: questions as executable programs supports principled utility (EIG) and compositional generalization.
- Sensible feature set (informativeness, complexity, type), with lesion studies showing the importance of complexity and (to a lesser extent) informativeness/type.
- Human data in a controlled domain; public dataset link provided.
- Qualitative samples demonstrate creative, context-appropriate questions beyond the training set.
- The approach is compatible with cognitive theories (LOT, compositionality) and provides a predictive model of human question frequencies.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Apparent sign error in the gradient (Eq. 4). For p(x) ∝ exp(−E(x)), ∂ log L/∂θk = −N E_D[fk] + N E_P[fk], but the paper states N E_D[fk] − N E_P[fk]. This is the negation of the standard result for log-linear models with positive energy. Please clarify whether your energy is defined with an extra minus sign or correct the derivation.
- Importance sampling for Z is misstated. You write “Z ≈ E_q[p(x;θ)/q(x)]”, but p depends on Z, leading to circularity. The standard IS estimator should use the unnormalized density: Z = ∑ exp(−E(x)) ≈ (1/M)∑_m exp(−E(x_m))/q(x_m).
- High-variance estimation likely: q(x) is a uniform PCFG, whereas pθ may concentrate on a very different subset; no diagnostics (ESS, variance) are reported. It is unclear whether you resample the 150k proposals each optimization step. If fixed, IS bias/variance may be severe as θ moves; if resampled, the computational cost seems prohibitive. Please report ESS per iteration, sensitivity to sample size, and whether self-normalized IS or control variates were used.
- Context dependence omitted in sampling details: EIG depends on context, but the proposal q(x) is context-agnostic. You do not specify whether you recompute EIG for each sampled program per training context (implying 150k × 15 expensive evaluations). Provide computational budget, caching strategy, and hypothesis space sizes per context.
- Dropped content undermines completeness: contexts 1–2 are removed; 14 questions are excluded due to the probabilistic draw operator; questions limited to ≤100 functions. These exclusions call into question the “complete” modeling of the dataset and the generality of the grammar/semantics (particularly the inability to handle stochastic primitives).
- Mapping from natural language to programs is manual but unspecified. Who annotated, with what guidelines, and what inter-annotator agreement? How are paraphrases/ambiguities handled? This is central to claims about modeling human questions, but the translation pipeline is not documented.
- Evaluation scope and metrics: Only a single toy domain with small data; no tests of domain transfer or robustness to grammar modifications. The correlation results are mixed: the information-agnostic model slightly outperforms the full model on Spearman (0.65 vs 0.64), weakening claims about informativeness (despite log-likelihood advantages). No human evaluation of the “novel” questions is reported (beyond the model’s EIG).
- Equivalence/rejection criterion for novelty uses mutual information over answer partitions but details are missing: how estimated, thresholding, and whether it is symmetric and robust to sampling noise in H.
- Answer type handling and “Orientation as Boolean” is ad hoc; conflating semantic types with Boolean may distort type priors and learning. The type features seem to be used only as frequency offsets; no analysis of learned weights is provided.
- PCFG complexity as −log q(x) is reasonable, but there is no sensitivity analysis to grammar rule probabilities. Uniform rewrite choices bias “complexity” in subtle, arbitrary ways.
- Inconsistencies/clarity issues: The paper states “there is, surprisingly, no correlation at all between question generation frequency and EIG alone,” yet shows sizable improvements from EIG in log-likelihood; provide scatter plots and per-context analyses to reconcile this.
- Reproducibility: Code for the model/optimization is not linked. Given the approximate inference and many implementation choices (proposal, IS, caching), a code release is essential.

Presentation and formatting issues
- References are inconsistent/incomplete (many missing years/venues; malformed author lists: “L, J. Johnson…”, “d, F. Strub…”; repeated commas; “arXiv preprint, ,”). Please fix all entries.
- Numerous typographical/formatting glitches: “within in a particular domain,” “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems…,” broken footnotes (“4We had to…”), cross-references to missing figures/tables (Figure 1B/C, Table SI-1/SI-2), and undefined notation (“A→N” without full grammar). Ensure all figures/tables are present and legible.
- Ambiguity about the prior over hypotheses H and the exact number per context; report counts and constraints (ship placements, pruning).
- Notation: Use consistent symbols for distributions p(d;x) vs p(d|x); define Ax clearly; specify when probabilities are normalized vs unnormalized.

Pros
- Novel, principled framing of question generation as program synthesis with Bayesian utility.
- Demonstrates compositional generalization and can produce interesting, context-sensitive queries.
- Lesion study indicates the importance of complexity and type features.
- Public dataset.

Cons
- Critical technical issues in the optimization and partition-function estimation as written.
- Heavy reliance on engineered, domain-specific grammar; limited generality.
- Small, single-domain evaluation; removal of contexts/primitives; manual NL→program mapping unspecified.
- Approximate inference lacks diagnostics; computational details missing.
- Numerous presentation and reference problems.

Recommendation
Reject. The conceptual idea is compelling and suitable for NeurIPS, but the current manuscript has substantial technical ambiguities (including a likely gradient/sign error and mis-specified IS estimator), limited and fragile evaluation, and significant clarity/formatting issues. With corrected math, transparent and validated inference, clearer annotation/evaluation procedures, and stronger experiments (robustness analyses, broader domains, human evaluation of generated questions), this work could become a strong contribution.
