This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking. The central idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. This recasts the problem of question asking as one of probabilistic program generation. The authors develop a log-linear model that defines a probability distribution over the space of possible questions, which are generated by a context-free grammar. The model scores questions based on a weighted combination of features, including their expected information gain (EIG), complexity (program length), answer type (e.g., Boolean, number), and relevance to the game context.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game, where the goal is to resolve ambiguity about hidden objects on a grid. The authors demonstrate that their full model, with learned weights for all features, can accurately predict the frequencies of human questions in held-out contexts. Furthermore, the model is capable of generating novel, contextually appropriate, and "human-like" questions that were not observed in the training data.

This work connects several lines of research. It extends the paradigm of active learning, which has traditionally focused on simple label queries, to a much richer and more expressive space of information requests. It also relates to work in AI dialog systems and visual question generation, but distinguishes itself by focusing on goal-directed, compositional question asking in novel scenarios without requiring massive datasets. The approach is grounded in cognitive science literature on compositionality and the "language of thought," framing question asking as a form of probabilistic inference over structured representations.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality:** The core contribution—formulating goal-directed question asking as probabilistic program generation—is highly original and provides an elegant formalization of a complex problem. This approach effectively unifies the principles of compositionality (questions built from smaller parts), computability (questions can be executed to yield answers), and goal-directedness (questions are chosen to be informative). This is a significant conceptual advance over existing approaches in active learning and question generation.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the choice of features (EIG, complexity, answer type) is well-motivated by both rational analysis and cognitive science principles. The use of a log-linear model is appropriate, and the method for handling the intractable partition function via importance sampling is a standard and valid technique. The ablation studies, which systematically remove key components of the model, provide strong evidence for the necessity of each component. For instance, the dramatic performance drop of the "complexity-agnostic" model convincingly demonstrates that humans balance informativeness with simplicity, a key claim of the paper.

*   **Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. The work provides a clear path forward for building more intelligent active learning agents that can ask sophisticated, human-like questions. This has potential applications in areas like interactive machine learning, intelligent tutoring systems, and scientific discovery. The demonstration that the model can generate creative and useful questions not seen in the data is particularly compelling and showcases the generative power of the framework. This paper advances the state of the art by providing the first predictive, computational model of open-ended, goal-directed human question asking.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established, the model is described with sufficient detail for understanding, and the experiments are presented logically. The authors do an excellent job of connecting their computational model to broader concepts in AI and cognitive science, making the paper accessible and impactful.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Generalizability and Domain Specificity:** The primary limitation is the reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar. While the authors rightly point out that many components of the language (e.g., logical operators, set functions) are domain-general, significant knowledge engineering was still required to define the primitives and rules for the Battleship domain. It is unclear how this approach would scale or generalize to new, more complex domains, and how much manual effort would be needed to construct a suitable grammar for each. This currently limits the framework's practical applicability as a general-purpose question-asking system.

*   **Abstraction from Natural Language:** The model operates on semantic program representations and completely bypasses the natural language understanding and generation aspects of the task. The human questions in the dataset were manually translated into the formal language. While this is a reasonable simplification to isolate and study the core problem of semantic question generation, it is a major idealization. Bridging this "semantic gap" by integrating the model with modern semantic parsing and NLG techniques is a non-trivial challenge for future work.

*   **Computational Scalability:** The inference procedure relies on sampling a very large number of programs (150,000) to approximate the partition function and gradients. The authors also note that they had to exclude two experimental contexts because their hypothesis spaces were too large for computation. This suggests that the current approach may face scalability challenges when applied to domains with larger state spaces or more expressive grammars.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and powerful formalization for a fundamental aspect of intelligence.
*   Technically strong execution with rigorous experimental validation and ablation studies.
*   Demonstrates impressive generative capabilities, producing novel and insightful questions.
*   Opens a promising new research direction at the intersection of active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar raises questions about generalizability.
*   The model abstracts away from the challenging natural language processing components of the problem.
*   Potential computational scalability issues may limit application to more complex domains.

In summary, this is a high-quality, original, and significant paper that makes a foundational contribution to the computational study of question asking. While the limitations regarding domain specificity and the abstraction from natural language are real, the conceptual framework and the strong empirical results are a major step forward. The strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses, and it is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research.
