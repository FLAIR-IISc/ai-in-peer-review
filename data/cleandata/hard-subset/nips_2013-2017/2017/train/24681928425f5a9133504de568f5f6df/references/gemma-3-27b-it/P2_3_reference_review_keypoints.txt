- Proposes a novel framework modeling questions as executable programs, reframing question asking as program synthesis.  
- Demonstrated on a Battleship-like game: predicts human question frequencies and generates plausible novel questions.  
- Positions itself against active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation, arguing for a compositional, computable, context-sensitive approach.  
- Novelty & originality: the program-as-question idea is genuinely new and conceptually strong.  
- Strong theoretical grounding in cognitive science (compositionality, language of thought, Bayesian belief updating).  
- Promising empirical results showing advantage over simpler models despite limited data.  
- Paper is clearly written with well-presented probabilistic model and feature engineering.  
- Related work coverage is comprehensive and well-integrated.  
- Evaluation is limited to a single game context, raising concerns about generalizability.  
- Heavy domain dependence: grammar and features are Battleship-specific, implying manual effort for new domains and scalability issues.  
- Model complexity: many features and components without sufficient justification or ablation to show relative contributions.  
- Reliance on importance sampling introduces potential bias and instability; more robust approximations or sensitivity analyses are needed.  
- Quality: technically sound but weakened by approximation dependence and narrow evaluation.  
- Originality and significance: highly original with potentially significant impact if generalizable.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions, asking authors to discuss grammar adaptation, perform feature/ablation analyses, and address importance sampling limitations.
