- Formalizes questions as executable programs combining compositionality and computability to model human question asking.  
- Collected a dataset of 605 natural-language questions from a Battleship-like game.  
- Builds a probabilistic model using a context-free grammar with features for Expected Information Gain, complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Model predicts human question frequencies (Spearman’s ρ = 0.64) and generates novel, human-like questions not in training data.  
- Ablation studies show informativeness, complexity, and answer type all matter; complexity-agnostic model performs very poorly (ρ = -0.36).  
- Strong experimental design: leave-one-out cross-validation across 16 contexts with 40 participants each; supplementary materials enable reproducibility.  
- Major limitation: grammar and features are domain-specific; unclear how much engineering is needed to generalize to other domains.  
- Evaluation limited to a single controlled domain; unknown performance on complex, real-world, noisy, or incomplete world representations.  
- Computational scalability concerns: grammar had to be constrained (removed “draw” rule) and training/runtime for larger grammars is not analyzed.  
- Practical deployment issues: unclear how generated programs would be converted to natural language or integrated into real AI systems; NLG challenges not addressed.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and outline future work; framework bridges cognitive science and machine learning.  
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, original, and significant for active learning and modeling human question asking.  
- Suggested improvement: provide more discussion or examples on how the grammar could be generalized to other domains.
