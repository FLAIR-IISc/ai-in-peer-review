

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper presents a novel conceptual framework for modeling human question asking by formalizing questions as executable programs. The authors propose that questions can be represented as syntactically well-formed programs in a simple programming language (based on lambda calculus and LISP) that, when executed on a possible world state, output an answer. This approach recasts question asking as program synthesis, offering a computational model that captures the compositional nature of human questions and their relationship to the underlying world state.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a conceptual bridge between cognitive science (human question asking) and computational modeling (program synthesis), drawing on compositionality principles from linguistics and cognitive science. Second, it develops a probabilistic generative model that uses a context-free grammar to generate questions, incorporating key features such as informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (favoring shorter questions), answer type preferences, and relevance to the game board. Third, the authors evaluate their model on a novel dataset of human questions from a Battleship-like game, demonstrating that their full model outperforms variants that ignore key properties (informativeness, complexity, answer type), and that the model can generate novel, human-like questions not present in the training data.

The paper's novelty is its core contribution: treating questions as programs rather than natural language strings. This represents a significant shift from previous approaches that either used canned questions (goal-directed dialog systems) or required large datasets of paired images and questions (VQG). The authors convincingly argue that their approach captures the compositional and goal-directed nature of human question asking, which previous methods fail to address.

The paper's significance lies in its potential to advance active learning beyond simple label queries toward more human-like information acquisition. The authors demonstrate that their model can predict human question frequencies (with Spearman's correlation of 0.64) and generate novel, contextually appropriate questions, showing the framework's potential to enable more natural machine question asking.

While the paper has some limitations (it's domain-specific to a Battleship game, uses semantic representations rather than natural language directly, and has a limited evaluation scope), these are outweighed by the paper's conceptual novelty. The authors acknowledge these limitations and discuss how the framework might be extended to other domains.

In terms of the NIPS criteria:
- **Originality**: The paper introduces a fundamentally novel perspective on question asking as program generation. It clearly differentiates itself from related work in active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation, making a strong case for its novelty.
- **Significance**: The framework opens up new directions for active learning and human-AI interaction, potentially enabling machines to ask richer, more goal-directed questions. The ability to generate novel, human-like questions represents a significant advance beyond current methods.
- **Quality**: The probabilistic model is well-constructed, with a clear theoretical foundation in Bayesian inference and compositional semantics. The evaluation demonstrates the model's effectiveness in predicting human question patterns and generating novel questions.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the key concepts and a logical flow from problem statement to model to evaluation.

This paper represents a bold, novel approach to a significant problem in active learning and human-AI interaction. While the evaluation is limited to a specific game domain, the conceptual framework is generalizable and opens up new research directions. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper's conceptual contribution to be sufficiently significant to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

The paper's main strength is its novel framing of question asking as program generation, which provides a theoretically grounded and computationally tractable approach to modeling a rich aspect of human cognition that has been largely overlooked in machine learning. This work has the potential to influence future research in active learning, cognitive science, and natural language processing.

I recommend acceptance for its significant conceptual novelty and potential to open new research directions in active learning and human-like question asking. The methodological limitations are acceptable given the paper's strong theoretical contribution and the fact that it establishes a new framework rather than merely improving existing methods.
