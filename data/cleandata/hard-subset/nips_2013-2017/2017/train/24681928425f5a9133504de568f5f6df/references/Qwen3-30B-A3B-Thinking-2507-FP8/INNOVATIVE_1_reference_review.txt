

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as executable programs. The authors argue that human questions can be formalized as syntactically well-formed programs that, when executed on a possible world state, produce answers. This approach recasts question asking as program synthesis, with the key insight that compositionality and computability are central to human question generation. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-style information-search game, demonstrating that their grammar-based probabilistic model can predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.

The paper makes a compelling case for the novelty of its approach. While previous work in active learning has focused on simple label queries and question-answering systems have used neural networks on large datasets, this work offers a fundamentally different perspective by modeling questions as programs. The authors effectively distinguish their work from related approaches: goal-directed dialog systems use canned questions, visual "20 questions" systems produce stereotyped questions, and VQG models require large image-question datasets. The paper's core contribution—treating questions as programs with compositional structure—is genuinely fresh and has the potential to reshape how we think about question asking in AI.

The paper's strengths are significant. The model successfully predicts human question frequencies (Spearman correlation of 0.64) and generates novel questions that weren't in the training data, demonstrating the model's creativity. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, connecting it to cognitive science (language of thought) and computational linguistics. The probabilistic model, which balances informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (grammar-based length), and answer type preferences, offers a principled way to model human question selection.

The work also shows strong theoretical grounding, with the grammar-based approach enabling compositionality and productivity—key features of human language. The authors demonstrate that their model can account for 15% of human questions that appeared in only one context, suggesting the model captures the productive aspect of human question asking that simpler pattern-matching approaches cannot.

While the paper has strong theoretical foundations, it has some limitations. The evaluation is confined to a single domain (Battleship game), and the grammar is somewhat domain-specific, requiring knowledge engineering for new domains. The model operates on semantic representations rather than natural language directly, which limits its immediate applicability to real-world NLP systems. The paper also doesn't fully address how to scale this approach to more complex domains with richer semantics.

However, these limitations don't undermine the paper's core contribution. The authors acknowledge these constraints and provide a clear path forward for future work. The paper's value lies in its conceptual breakthrough rather than its immediate practical applications. The approach represents a significant shift from current paradigms in active learning and question generation, moving from simple label queries to rich, compositional question asking.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for its novelty. The authors effectively situate their work within cognitive science and machine learning literature, demonstrating awareness of related work while clearly differentiating their contribution.

In terms of the NeurIPS review criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound, with a clear theoretical foundation and reasonable experimental validation. The model is well-specified, and the evaluation methods are appropriate for the task.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to solution to evaluation.
- Originality: This is the paper's strongest aspect. The core idea of modeling questions as programs represents a significant conceptual advance in active learning and question generation.
- Significance: The work opens a new direction for active learning that could lead to more human-like AI systems. While the immediate application is limited to a single domain, the framework has broad potential implications for how we design AI systems that interact with humans through questioning.

The paper's novelty and bold conceptual framework outweigh its minor methodological limitations. It's not just an incremental improvement to existing question generation systems—it's a new way of thinking about question asking that could inspire future research across cognitive science, NLP, and active learning.

I recommend acceptance for its significant conceptual contribution, even if the immediate applications are limited to a specific domain. This paper represents a valuable step toward more human-like AI question asking, and its core insight has the potential to influence the field for years to come. The authors have provided a compelling new framework that should stimulate further research in active learning and human-AI interaction.
