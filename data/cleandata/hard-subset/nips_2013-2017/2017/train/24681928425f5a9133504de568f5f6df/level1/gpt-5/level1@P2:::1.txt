Summary and relation to prior work
The paper models human question asking as program generation: questions are lambda-calculus/LISP-like programs that, when executed on a hypothesized world state, return an answer of a typed sort (Boolean/Number/Color/Location). A PCFG defines the compositional hypothesis space; a log-linear model over program features (informativeness via Expected Information Gain, an offset for zero-EIG, complexity via −log prior under the grammar, answer-type indicators, and a relevance filter) induces a probability distribution over questions conditioned on a context. Parameters are fit by maximizing the likelihood of human-produced questions in a Battleship-style game (605 questions across 18 contexts; two contexts and 14 “draw” questions removed for computational reasons) using importance sampling to approximate the partition function and feature expectations. The model predicts human question distributions in held-out contexts and generates novel, human-like questions.

This work connects to active learning (beyond label queries), formal semantics of questions (partitions over worlds), program-induction concept learning, and semantic parsing as program execution. Relative to dialog systems and visual question generation, it emphasizes goal-directed information-seeking and compositionality, with a cognitive modeling angle. Treating questions as programs is known in QA, but using this representation to predict and generate human questions with EIG/complexity trade-offs is novel.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The probabilistic formulation is coherent, and the feature design aligns with the hypothesized trade-off between informativeness and cognitive cost. The Bayesian ideal observer and EIG computation are appropriate for the Battleship domain, though they presuppose a fairly strong world model. Importance sampling with a PCFG proposal for both inference and the complexity feature is reasonable, but the dual use warrants discussion of potential coupling/bias.
- Empirical support: Leave-one-context-out fits show the full model has the highest (approximate) held-out log-likelihood; ablations (no EIG, no complexity, no type) underperform, with the complexity-agnostic variant particularly weak. Correlations (Spearman) between model energy and human frequencies average 0.64 for the full model; interestingly, the information-agnostic model achieves a similar 0.65, consistent with prior observations that raw EIG alone correlates poorly with human question frequency. Qualitative samples demonstrate the ability to generate plausible, novel questions. The evaluation is careful about equivalence classes of questions (by answer partitions).
- Limitations: (i) Heavy reliance on hand-engineered grammar and features; (ii) computational constraints (dropping contexts, pruning probabilistic “draw” operator) limit completeness; (iii) the mapping from natural language to program is assumed given (semantic representations are taken as ground truth), so end-to-end question generation from text is not addressed; (iv) the training/test contexts are from a single, fixed domain with shared priors; (v) the strong ideal-observer assumption for EIG and exact world simulation may not scale or generalize.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The formalism is explained with intuitive examples, and the training/inference procedures are described sufficiently to support reproduction, pending access to the supplementary grammar tables and code for importance sampling. Some citations appear incomplete in the references list; polishing would help. It would also help to report actual learned weights and give concrete numbers for log-likelihood differences, not only bootstrap dominance rates.

Originality
Modeling question asking as program generation and learning a distribution that trades off EIG with program complexity is a fresh contribution at the intersection of cognitive modeling, active learning, and program induction. While semantic parsing/execution and EIG are established, their integration to predict human question distributions in open-ended settings is a novel framing.

Significance
The work advances our understanding of human question formation and suggests a path toward richer, goal-directed querying by machines. For NeurIPS, the main significance is conceptual and methodological rather than immediately scalable performance: it demonstrates how to operationalize compositional, typed question spaces with an information-theoretic objective and cognitive constraints. Broader impact will depend on extending beyond a bespoke domain and bridging to natural language and perception.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formalization of questions as executable programs with types.
- Probabilistic model that captures an informativeness–complexity trade-off aligned with human behavior.
- Compositional grammar supports productivity and generation of novel questions.
- Thoughtful ablations showing importance of complexity and type priors; qualitative samples are compelling.

Weaknesses
- Narrow domain with substantial hand-crafted knowledge; unclear how much transfers with minimal engineering.
- EIG’s contribution to predictive correlation appears modest; the information-agnostic variant matches or slightly exceeds correlation, suggesting complexity/type priors and grammar drive most of the fit.
- Computational approximations are heavy and require dropping contexts/operators; scalability is uncertain.
- No comparison to alternative generative baselines (e.g., PCFG prior only; neural program generators; supervised paraphrase models) or to planning-based active learning agents in the same task.
- Not end-to-end: assumes ground-truth semantic forms of human questions.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Report effect sizes: absolute/relative log-likelihoods and their variance, and include a PCFG-only baseline and an EIG-only baseline to isolate contributions.
- Analyze learned weights and provide qualitative failure cases; where does EIG help most?
- Evaluate generalization to a new but related grid size or altered ship priors to test robustness.
- Add a simple neural baseline (e.g., sequence model over program tokens) trained on contexts and semantic forms.
- Explore mapping from text to programs (even a small semantic parser) to close the loop toward end-to-end systems.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled formulation with clear cognitive motivation.
- Solid empirical evidence that compositionality and complexity priors are crucial for human-like questions.
- Generates creative, valid questions beyond training set; productive modeling of open-ended querying.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited domain and engineered grammar; uncertain scalability and generality.
- Ambiguity about the practical contribution of EIG to quantitative prediction given the reported correlations.
- Heavy approximations and pruning raise questions about completeness and reproducibility at scale.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper offers a well-motivated and technically sound framework that is likely to interest both cognitive modeling and ML communities, despite limited scope and scalability in this first instantiation.
