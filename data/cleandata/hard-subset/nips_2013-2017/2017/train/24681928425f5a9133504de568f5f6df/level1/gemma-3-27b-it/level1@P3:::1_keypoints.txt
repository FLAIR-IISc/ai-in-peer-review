- Models questions as executable programs, connecting program synthesis, cognitive science, and active learning.
- Strong theoretical grounding in cognitive science and linguistic compositionality (“language of thought”).
- Empirical validation on a Battleship-like dataset shows the model predicts human question choices and generates plausible novel questions.
- Uses leave-one-out cross-validation and ablation studies to show informativeness and complexity are important for human-like questions.
- Paper is generally clear, well-written, and well-organized.
- Grammar used to represent questions is complex and may not scale or generalize without substantial domain knowledge.
- Importance sampling optimization is computationally expensive, limiting scalability to larger grammars and datasets.
- Evaluation is confined to a specific game context; generalization to open-ended scenarios is unclear.
- Relies on manually engineered features (e.g., 'relevance' marker) rather than automated feature learning.
- Expected Information Gain (EIG) calculation involves assumptions/simplifications that need more justification.
- Technically sound with experimentally supported claims; limitations are acknowledged by the authors.
- Some technical sections (optimization details, grammar tables) are dense and would benefit from clearer examples or visuals.
- The approach is highly original and departs from traditional question generation and active learning work.
- Results are significant, offering a new framework for understanding human question asking and generating human-like questions.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, promising results, nuanced analysis, contribution to cognitive AI.
- Arguments against acceptance: computational cost/scalability concerns, limited evaluation domain, manual feature engineering.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions; clarify optimization, justify EIG choices, and discuss scalability/generalization strategies.
