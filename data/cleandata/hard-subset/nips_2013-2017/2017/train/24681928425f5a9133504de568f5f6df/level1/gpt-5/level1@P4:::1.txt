Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper models question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as executable programs in a small, typed, LISP-like language (lambda calculus with mapping and set operators). A probabilistic grammar defines a large compositional hypothesis space of programs. A log-linear model over program-level features induces a distribution over questions conditioned on context. Key features include informativeness via expected information gain (EIG) computed under Bayesian belief updating over possible world states (Battleship boards), a complexity prior (−log probability under a probabilistic context-free grammar), answer-type indicators (Boolean, Number, Color, Location), and a relevance filter. Parameters are fit by maximum likelihood to human question data (605 questions across 18 contexts) using importance sampling to approximate the partition function and expected feature values.

Evaluation uses leave-one-context-out: train on questions from 15 contexts and predict distributions in a held-out context. The full model outperforms lesion variants that remove informativeness, complexity, or type features in terms of held-out log-likelihood. Correlations between model energies and human question frequencies average 0.64 (Spearman). The model also samples novel, contextually appropriate questions not seen in training, using mutual information over answer distributions to enforce semantic novelty.

This work builds on and connects: (i) active learning and value of information, extending beyond label queries; (ii) program induction and language-of-thought models in cognitive science; (iii) formal semantics of questions as partitions over possible worlds; and (iv) semantic parsing / program-based question answering. Relative to prior VQG and dialog systems, the novelty is in goal-directed question generation with explicit informativeness and compositional semantics, and in predicting human question distributions rather than only producing fluent natural language.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled formulation of questions as executable programs, enabling explicit computation of answer distributions and EIG.
- Compositional grammar covers all observed human questions and permits generativity beyond the dataset; the lambda/mapping constructs are expressive yet interpretable.
- Probabilistic model integrates informativeness with a complexity prior; the empirical finding that complexity is crucial (lesion yields large degradation) is informative for cognitive modeling.
- Sound training objective (MLE) and reasonably justified approximations (importance sampling over the grammar) with bootstrapped uncertainty analyses on model comparisons.
- Demonstrates both predictive power (log-likelihood, rank correlations) and constructive capability (novel question generation with semantic equivalence checking).
- Relates well to literature in cognitive science and ML; motivates how this approach could extend active learning beyond labels.

Weaknesses:
- Limited domain and small dataset (605 questions, single game). Generalization to other tasks is not demonstrated; some grammar components are domain-specific. This limits broader ML significance.
- Baselines are mainly lesion studies. There is no comparison to non-grammatical parametric generative baselines (e.g., n-gram over programs, neural sequence models constrained by types), or to alternative complexity priors (e.g., program length, MDL). It is unclear whether the observed gains are specific to the chosen q(x)-based complexity measure.
- The complexity feature equals −log q(x) under the same PCFG used as the proposal distribution. This coupling may introduce biases; analysis of sensitivity to grammar rule probabilities or to alternative priors would strengthen claims.
- Informativeness relies on full hypothesis enumeration and exact EIG for most contexts; two contexts and a stochastic operator had to be dropped for tractability. The impact of these exclusions and the scalability of EIG computation to richer domains remain unclear.
- Evaluation metrics and reporting could be more detailed: the “no correlation with EIG alone” claim references prior work without quantitative values here; absolute LL improvements and confidence intervals per context are only partially addressed (bootstrap over Z but not over the whole training pipeline).
- Clarity depends on supplementary materials: core grammar tables and some implementation details are deferred. Some references are incomplete or malformed.

Quality:
- Technically sound overall. The probabilistic model, inference approximations, and EIG computations are standard and appropriate for the setting. Assumptions are clearly stated, and limitations (domain specificity, NL interface) are acknowledged.
- The analysis supports the main claims: compositionality is needed for productivity; both informativeness and complexity contribute to human-like question distributions.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized. The modeling pipeline is explained clearly with concrete examples. Suggestions:
  - Bring essential grammar rules and typing into the main text.
  - Provide more detail on hypothesis space sizes per context, computational cost, and caching strategies for EIG.
  - Clarify the semantic equivalence criterion and thresholds for mutual information.
  - Fix broken references and ensure consistent citation formatting.

Originality:
- Moderate to good. Representing questions as programs is known in semantic parsing and formal semantics; however, learning a probabilistic generator that predicts human question distributions and integrates EIG with a grammar-based complexity prior is novel and interesting.

Significance:
- For cognitive modeling of question asking and for conceptual advances in active learning, the contribution is meaningful. For broad NIPS impact in ML, significance is tempered by the single-domain evaluation, engineered grammar, and lack of comparison to stronger ML baselines. Nonetheless, the approach suggests a pathway to richer, goal-directed querying in interactive systems.

Pros:
- Principled, interpretable model unifying informativeness and complexity.
- Strong evidence that complexity constraints matter for human-like question generation.
- Demonstrated compositional generativity and semantic novelty.
- Clear connections to active learning and LoT.

Cons:
- Single domain; limited dataset.
- No comparisons to alternative priors or modern generative baselines.
- Scalability of EIG and reliance on domain-engineered grammar not fully addressed.
- Some methodological details deferred to supplement; minor reference issues.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Evaluate on an additional domain with a new grammar, or demonstrate transfer of core operators.
- Compare to: (a) program-length or MDL-based complexity priors; (b) neural generative models over the DSL with type constraints; (c) RSA-style pragmatic questioner models if applicable.
- Report full quantitative metrics per context, including uncertainty over the entire training/evaluation pipeline.
- Explore computational shortcuts for EIG (e.g., sampling hypotheses, amortized estimators) and analyze scalability.
- Provide a minimal NL interface or mapping to show end-to-end feasibility.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a clear, technically sound, and relatively novel framework with solid evidence in a controlled domain, providing useful insights into question generation and potential avenues for richer active learning.
