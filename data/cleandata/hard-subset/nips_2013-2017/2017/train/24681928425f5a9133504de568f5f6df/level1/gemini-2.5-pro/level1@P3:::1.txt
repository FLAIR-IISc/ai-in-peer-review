### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed human question asking, treating it as a problem of program generation. The core idea is that questions can be represented as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. These programs, when executed on a world state, yield an answer. The authors propose a probabilistic model over this space of programs, defined by a formal grammar. The probability of asking a particular question is determined by a log-linear model that balances the question's informativeness (measured by Expected Information Gain, EIG) and its complexity (related to the program's length/structure). The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game. The results demonstrate that the proposed model can predict the frequency of human questions in unseen contexts and, impressively, generate novel, contextually relevant questions that were not observed in the training data. An ablation study confirms that both informativeness and complexity are critical components for modeling human-like question generation.

This work connects ideas from active learning, computational cognitive science, and program synthesis. While active learning has traditionally focused on simple label queries, this paper extends the concept to a much richer, compositional space of queries. It builds on the "language of thought" hypothesis from cognitive science by providing a concrete, computable model and differs from contemporary data-driven approaches to question generation (e.g., from VQA or dialogue systems) by being explicitly goal-directed and generative from a compact, structured representation rather than requiring massive datasets.

### Strengths

**Originality & Significance:** The central contribution—framing question asking as probabilistic program generation—is highly original and significant. It provides a powerful formalism for a fundamental aspect of intelligence that has been difficult to capture computationally. By moving beyond simple label requests, this work opens a new and promising direction for active learning and, more broadly, for building more inquisitive and interactive AI agents. The implications for cognitive science are also substantial, as it offers the first quantitative, predictive model of open-ended human question asking, formalizing the trade-off between gaining information and cognitive effort (complexity).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The choice of a log-linear model over a grammar-defined space is well-motivated and technically appropriate. The use of Expected Information Gain is a standard and fitting choice for quantifying informativeness. The experimental evaluation is strong. The quantitative analysis, including the leave-one-out cross-validation and the ablation study, convincingly demonstrates the necessity of the model's core components. In particular, the finding that the "complexity-agnostic" model fails dramatically underscores that human inquiry is not simply about maximizing information gain. The qualitative results, showcasing the model's ability to generate creative and sensible new questions, are particularly compelling and highlight the generative power of the framework.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, explaining their formal approach, and presenting the results. The use of concrete examples to illustrate how natural language questions map to programs (e.g., "How long is the blue ship?" -> `(size Blue)`) is very effective. The structure is logical, guiding the reader smoothly from the high-level concept to the technical details and empirical validation.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

**Scalability and Domain Specificity:** The primary weakness of the current work lies in its reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar. The authors acknowledge this, but its implications are significant. The grammar contains primitives like `size(S)` and knowledge about ships, colors, and the grid, which were engineered for the Battleship task. It is unclear how much effort would be required to adapt this framework to a new domain. Future work should explore methods for learning these grammatical primitives, perhaps from data or interaction, to make the approach more scalable and reduce the manual engineering burden. This would be a crucial step toward creating truly general-purpose question-asking agents.

**Lack of Natural Language Interface:** The model operates on the semantic representation of questions (programs), not on the raw natural language text. The human-generated questions in the dataset had to be manually translated into the formal language. This is a non-trivial "semantic parsing" step that is not addressed by the model. While the authors rightly point to other work that could bridge this gap, the absence of this component means the system is not end-to-end. Integrating a semantic parser would be a valuable and necessary extension for practical applications.

**Approximation in Inference:** The model relies on importance sampling to approximate the intractable partition function and the gradient during optimization. While this is a standard technique, the paper provides limited details on the stability of this approximation. The performance of importance sampling depends heavily on the similarity between the proposal distribution (here, a uniform PCFG) and the target distribution (the learned model). A large discrepancy can lead to high-variance estimates. Including a brief analysis of the variance of the log-likelihood estimates or the stability of the learned weights would strengthen the quantitative results.

**Cognitive Plausibility of Complexity:** The complexity feature `f_comp(x) = -log q(x)` is derived from a uniform probabilistic context-free grammar, effectively penalizing longer derivations. While this is a reasonable proxy for complexity, it may not fully capture the nuances of human cognitive effort. For example, are deeply nested structures truly harder for humans to formulate than long, flat ones, even if they have similar derivation lengths? Exploring alternative, more psychologically grounded complexity metrics could be a fruitful direction for future research.

### Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Highly original and significant conceptual contribution that opens a new research direction.
*   Strong technical execution with a well-designed probabilistic model.
*   Convincing empirical results, including a rigorous ablation study and compelling qualitative examples of generative capabilities.
*   Clearly written and accessible, with high potential impact on both machine learning and cognitive science.

**Con:**
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar limits immediate generalizability.
*   The system is not end-to-end, as it requires manual translation from natural language to the program representation.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel and thought-provoking framework. The strengths, particularly the originality of the core idea and the quality of the execution, far outweigh the weaknesses, which primarily relate to future challenges of scalability and integration. This work is a significant step towards a computational understanding of inquiry and is a perfect fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
