- Introduces a novel framework treating questions as executable programs that map world states to answers.  
- Builds a probabilistic model that generates questions in a "Battleship"-like game, predicting human question choices and creating plausible novel questions.  
- Situates work within active learning but extends beyond simple label requests to richer, informative queries.  
- Integrates cognitive science perspectives with formal methods (lambda calculus, program synthesis) to model question meaning.  
- Addresses limitations of dialog systems that rely on predefined question sets and contrasts with deep learning approaches lacking intentionality.  
- Probabilistic model is technically sound; importance sampling is an appropriate optimization given intractable exact inference.  
- Grammar and program semantics connection is well-established; ablation studies show informativeness, complexity, and answer type matter.  
- Experimental evaluation is convincing within the single game domain but limited in scope.  
- Paper lacks detailed discussion of importance sampling limitations and potential bias from the approximation.  
- Needs a more thorough error analysis of generated questions beyond qualitative examples.  
- Writing is clear and organized; examples effectively illustrate program representations.  
- Mathematical notation has inconsistencies (e.g., varying meaning of 'x') and feature descriptions could be more intuitively explained.  
- A visual representation of the grammar rules would aid comprehension.  
- Core idea is original and significant, combining compositional semantics, probabilistic modeling, and grammar to advance question-asking research.  
- Recommendation: accept — novelty, soundness, and potential impact outweigh limitations.
