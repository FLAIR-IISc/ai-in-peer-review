## Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper presents a novel framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as formal programs that, when executed, yield an answer. The core idea – treating questions as executable code – is a compelling approach to capturing the intentionality and informativeness inherent in human queries, moving beyond simpler active learning paradigms focused solely on label requests. The authors ground this framework in a specific game context (Battleship) and demonstrate that their model can predict human question frequencies and even generate novel, plausible questions. This work builds upon existing research in active learning (Settles, 2009) and dialog systems (Serban et al., 2017), but distinguishes itself by focusing on the *generation* of rich, semantically meaningful questions rather than simply selecting from a predefined set. It also connects to work in cognitive science exploring the “language of thought” (Fodor, 1975) and probabilistic program induction (Lake et al., 2015).

**Strengths:** The paper’s primary strength lies in its innovative approach. Modeling questions as programs provides a powerful formalism for reasoning about informativeness and complexity. The experimental evaluation, while limited to a single game, is well-executed and demonstrates the model’s ability to capture human behavior. The ablation studies (comparing the full model to variants lacking key features) are particularly insightful, highlighting the importance of both informativeness and complexity in generating human-like questions. The generated examples are also quite impressive, showcasing the model’s creative potential. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and discuss future directions.

**Weaknesses:** The reliance on a single, relatively constrained game environment is a significant limitation. While the game provides a controlled setting for evaluation, it’s unclear how well the model would generalize to more open-ended domains. The grammar, while sufficient for the task, appears somewhat hand-engineered and might not scale easily to more complex scenarios. The optimization process, relying on importance sampling, could be computationally expensive and may not guarantee convergence to a global optimum. The comparison to existing dialog systems is somewhat superficial; a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art neural question generation models (e.g., Jain et al., 2017) would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to modeling question asking.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating the model’s ability to predict and generate human-like questions.
*   Insightful ablation studies revealing the importance of key features.
*   Clear writing and well-defined research question.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited generalizability due to reliance on a single game environment.
*   Potential scalability issues with the grammar and optimization process.
*   Lack of a comprehensive comparison to existing question generation models.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a compelling idea and solid execution. While the limited scope is a concern, the novelty and potential impact of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with a request for the authors to address the generalizability concerns in future work.
