

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by representing questions as formal programs that, when executed on a world state, output an answer. The authors propose that questions can be treated as compositional programs, with the model specifying a probability distribution over a space of programs that favors concise, informative questions relevant to the current context. The approach is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-style information-search game, where participants sought to identify ship configurations in ambiguous game states.

The work builds on several lines of prior research. It extends active learning beyond simple label requests (Settles, 2011) to address richer question types. It differs from goal-directed dialog systems (e.g., [25, 1]) that use canned questions and from visual question generation approaches (VQG [16, 23, 11]) that require large datasets and lack flexibility. The paper also relates to computational cognitive science work modeling concepts as programs [17, 5, 13], but makes a significant contribution by applying this approach specifically to question asking.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-structured probabilistic model using a log-linear framework with features for informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (via grammar log probability), answer type, and relevance. The evaluation methodology is rigorous, employing leave-one-out cross-validation with bootstrapping to compare model variants. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the importance of each component (informativeness, complexity, answer type), showing that the full model outperforms variants by 100% (vs complexity-agnostic), 81% (vs information-agnostic), and 88% (vs type-agnostic) in bootstrap samples.

2. **Originality:** The core insight of modeling questions as executable programs that capture compositionality and computability represents a significant departure from prior work. The paper clearly articulates how this approach differs from previous methods, particularly in its ability to generate novel questions not present in training data (e.g., "Are all the ships horizontal?"). The demonstration of compositionality as an essential ingredient for modeling human question productivity (15% of human questions appeared in no other contexts) is particularly compelling.

3. **Significance:** The work addresses an important gap in active learning literature by modeling the rich, open-ended question asking that humans employ. The model's ability to predict human question frequencies (Spearman correlation of 0.64) and generate novel, contextually appropriate questions demonstrates tangible progress toward more human-like question asking in machines. The approach has clear potential for applications in educational technology, AI assistants, and human-AI collaboration systems.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with thorough explanations of the grammar, probabilistic model, and evaluation methodology. The examples of program representations (e.g., "(> (size Red) (size Blue))" for "Is the red ship larger than the blue ship?") effectively illustrate the approach. The supplementary materials provide additional detail on the grammar and features.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the current implementation is tightly coupled to the Battleship domain. The grammar and features are tailored to this specific game, and it's unclear how much domain-specific knowledge would be required to adapt the approach to other domains. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence of domain generalizability beyond the current application.

2. **Natural Language Interface:** The model operates on semantic representations rather than natural language text directly. The authors note that bridging this gap would require interface with computational linguistics tools, but they don't explore this in depth or provide evidence that such an interface would be feasible.

3. **Comparison with Recent Neural Methods:** While the paper contrasts with VQG approaches, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison with more recent neural methods for question generation (e.g., Jain et al., 2017; Vijayakumar et al., 2017). Given the prevalence of neural approaches in NLP, this would strengthen the paper's claim of novelty.

4. **Evaluation Limitations:** The human data is sparse (many questions were asked only once), which limits the strength of correlation-based evaluation metrics. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't explore alternative evaluation metrics that might be more appropriate for sparse data.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant contribution to computational cognitive science and active learning by introducing a novel program synthesis approach to modeling human question asking. The technical approach is sound, the evaluation is thorough, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over alternative model variants. The work successfully bridges cognitive science and machine learning by providing a computational model that captures key aspects of human question asking, including compositionality, informativeness, and complexity.

While the current implementation is domain-specific, the authors provide a clear path forward for extending the approach to other domains. The paper's main limitation is the lack of detailed discussion about how to bridge the semantic representation to natural language, but this is acknowledged as a future direction rather than a fundamental flaw.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS as it advances our understanding of cognitive processes through computational modeling and offers a new approach to active learning that could improve human-AI interaction. The model's ability to generate novel, contextually appropriate questions represents a meaningful step toward more human-like question asking in machines.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality: it's technically sound with well-supported claims; clear in its exposition; original in its approach to modeling question asking; and significant for advancing both cognitive science and active learning. I recommend acceptance.
