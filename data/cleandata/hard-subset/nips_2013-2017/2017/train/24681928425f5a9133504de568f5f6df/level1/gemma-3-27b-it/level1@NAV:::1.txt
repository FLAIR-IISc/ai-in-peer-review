## Review of “Question Asking as Program Generation”

This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as formal programs that, when executed, yield answers. The core idea builds on the notion of compositionality and computability, leveraging a grammar-based approach to generate questions and assessing their quality based on informativeness and complexity. The authors evaluate this model against a dataset of questions posed by humans during a game involving ship placement, demonstrating the ability to predict human question choices and generate novel, human-like queries.

The work is situated well within the active learning and dialog systems literature. It acknowledges the limitations of existing active learning methods focused on simple label requests and contrasts itself with goal-driven dialog systems relying on predefined question sets. The paper also distinguishes itself from recent deep learning approaches to question generation, which require large datasets and lack the ability to generalize in novel scenarios. The connection to formal linguistics and cognitive science through references to theories of compositionality and “language of thought” is also clearly established.

**Strengths:** The primary strength lies in the novel approach of modeling questions as programs. This provides a theoretically grounded and flexible framework for capturing the semantic meaning and generative potential of human queries. The experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage of incorporating both informativeness and complexity into the model, and the qualitative examples of generated questions are compelling. The rigorous methodology, including ablation studies and a clear explanation of the optimization process, adds to the paper’s credibility. The clear link to existing cognitive science theories strengthens the contribution.

**Weaknesses:** While the grammar-based approach is powerful, it requires careful design and is inherently domain-specific. The paper acknowledges this, but further discussion of the scalability and generalizability to other domains would be beneficial. The reliance on a simplified “Battleship” game context might limit the external validity of the findings. The importance sampling approximation used for optimization adds complexity and may introduce potential biases. Though mentioned, a more thorough discussion of the limitations in dealing with natural language directly (instead of pre-defined programs) would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Novel and theoretically sound framework for modeling question asking.
* Strong experimental results demonstrating predictive power and generative capabilities.
* Rigorous methodology and ablation studies.
* Clear connection to relevant literature in AI, linguistics, and cognitive science.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* Domain-specificity of the grammar-based approach.
* Limited generalizability due to the simplified game context.
* Complexity of the optimization process.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of active learning and cognitive modeling. The novel framework for representing questions as programs is well-motivated, implemented, and evaluated. Despite some limitations, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points regarding generalizability and optimization details.
