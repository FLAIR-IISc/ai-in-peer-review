- Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs that yield answers, a novel and compelling framing.
- Demonstrates approach on a “Battleship”-like game dataset, showing the model predicts human question choices and can generate plausible novel questions.
- Representation of questions as programs is well-motivated and provides a formal basis for measuring informativeness.
- Probabilistic model (log-linear features) and use of importance sampling to handle the normalizing constant are technically sound.
- Experimental evaluation is thorough, with comparisons across model variants and strong ablation studies showing informativeness and complexity matter.
- Connection to the “language of thought” framing feels tenuous; choice of programming formalism isn’t fully justified relative to alternatives.
- Bayesian calculation of informativeness is clearly explained, but implementation details (e.g., priors) are insufficiently explicit.
- Reliance on a relatively small dataset (605 questions) is a notable limitation acknowledged by the authors.
- Claim of producing “clever” questions is subjective and relies on examples; needs more quantitative assessment of novelty and usefulness.
- Paper is generally well-written and organized; notation and equations are clear.
- Grammar and lambda-calculus-based descriptions can be dense for some readers; supplementary material is crucial.
- Suggestion: include an intuitive walkthrough showing how a specific human question maps to a program.
- Originality is strong—the combination of programmatic questions with a probabilistic generative model and human data appears novel.
- Potential significance: could impact active learning and human-computer interaction, but scalability to complex real-world domains is unclear.
- Acceptance arguments: novel idea, solid technical foundation, thorough experiments, and potential impact.
- Rejection risks: small dataset, subjective evaluation of generated questions, forced “language of thought” framing, and unclear scalability.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address dataset size concerns and strengthen evaluation/implementation details.
