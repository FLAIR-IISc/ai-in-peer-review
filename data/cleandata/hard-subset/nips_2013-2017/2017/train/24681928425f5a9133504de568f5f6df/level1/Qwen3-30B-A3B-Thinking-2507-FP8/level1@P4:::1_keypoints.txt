- Introduces modeling questions as executable, compositional programs that map world states to answers, evaluated in a Battleship-style information-search game.  
- Uses a dataset of 605 human questions collected in ambiguous game states.  
- Presents a probabilistic log-linear model with features for Expected Information Gain, grammar-based complexity, answer type, and relevance.  
- Rigorous evaluation using leave-one-out cross-validation, bootstrapping, and ablation studies showing the full model outperforms variants (≈100% vs complexity-agnostic, 81% vs information-agnostic, 88% vs type-agnostic).  
- Core originality: framing questions as programs enables compositionality and generation of novel questions not seen in training.  
- Evidence that compositionality is crucial: 15% of human questions were unique to their contexts.  
- Demonstrates significance: predicts human question frequencies (Spearman ρ = 0.64) and has clear application potential (education, assistants, human-AI collaboration).  
- Paper is clear and well-organized with illustrative program examples and detailed supplementary materials.  
- Main weakness: current implementation is tightly coupled to the Battleship domain, raising generalizability concerns.  
- Operates on semantic representations rather than natural language; bridging to NL is acknowledged but not explored or demonstrated.  
- Lacks detailed comparison to recent neural question-generation methods, which would strengthen novelty claims.  
- Evaluation limited by sparse human data (many one-off questions), constraining the robustness of correlation-based metrics.  
- Overall: technically sound, original, and significant contribution to cognitive modeling and active learning; recommended for acceptance.
