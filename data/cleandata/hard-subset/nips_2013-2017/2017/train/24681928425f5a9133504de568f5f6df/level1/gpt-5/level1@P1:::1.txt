Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models human question asking as program generation. Questions are represented as typed, executable programs in a small lambda-calculus/LISP-like language; executing a question-program on a hypothesized world yields an answer. A PCFG defines a compositional space of questions (including lambda, map, sets), enabling productivity beyond a fixed set. A log-linear model over program features (informativeness via Expected Information Gain over a Bayesian hypothesis space, complexity via −log probability under the PCFG, answer type priors, and a relevance filter) defines a distribution over questions conditioned on context. Parameters are fit by maximizing likelihood of human questions in a Battleship-like game dataset (605 questions across 18 contexts), with importance sampling used to approximate intractable expectations and normalizers. The full model better predicts human question distributions (leave-one-context-out), correlates with human frequencies (avg Spearman 0.64), and samples novel, human-like questions not seen in training. Ablations indicate both informativeness and complexity are necessary; removing complexity hurts most.

This connects to active learning (Settles, 2010), formal semantics of questions as partitions (Groenendijk & Stokhof), and probabilistic languages of thought/program induction (Piantadosi et al., Goodman et al., Lake et al.). It complements work in dialog systems and VQG (Mostafazadeh et al.), and program-based QA/visual reasoning (e.g., Wang et al.; Johnson et al.). Unlike sequence models that need large paired data, the approach aims for zero/low-shot generalization via compositional generation and utility optimization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent: questions as programs; a Bayesian hypothesis space for contexts; EIG as utility; and a log-linear prior capturing length/type preferences. The training objective and gradient are standard; importance sampling is reasonable, and bootstrapping Z acknowledges approximation noise.
- Assumptions/approximations: The EIG computation requires enumerating hypotheses; the authors drop two contexts and one stochastic grammar rule (draw), and cap program length—reasonable but they reduce empirical coverage. p(d|h;x) is a deterministic indicator, which ignores answer uncertainty/noise. The complexity feature relies on −log q(x), where q is also used as the proposal for importance sampling; this coupling may conflate “human complexity” with “what the sampler generates easily.” The relevance feature f∅ is a hand-engineered filter to counter grammar overgeneration.
- Empirics: Quantitative comparisons (LL under LOOCV, rank correlations) support claims that both EIG and complexity matter; the strong negative result for a complexity-agnostic variant is informative. However, key numbers (e.g., per-context LLs, learned weights and their uncertainty) are not fully reported in the excerpt. Novel question samples are sensible, but human evaluation is qualitative.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with intuitive examples translating between natural language and programs. The hypothesis space, EIG definition, and grammar are explained at a level sufficient to follow the approach; some crucial details are deferred to the supplement (full grammar tables, hypothesis space sizes), which should be made accessible. Reporting more implementation details (runtime, number of hypotheses per context, convergence) would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Modeling question asking (not answering) as program generation with a compositional grammar and explicit utility over possible worlds is novel and timely. Prior cognitive/pragmatic models of questions (e.g., Hawkins et al., 2015) reason about informativeness but do not synthesize a broad space of executable question forms or learn a distribution over them to match human data. The synthesis of LOT-style priors with EIG-based utilities for generation is a distinctive contribution.

Significance
For cognitive science, this is a substantive step: a predictive, generative model that accounts for open-ended human question asking and highlights the trade-off between informativeness and complexity. For ML, it suggests a path toward richer active learning and dialog via compositional query spaces and explicit utilities. Practical impact is limited in this paper to a single synthetic domain with manual program annotations; bridging to natural language and diverse tasks remains future work.

Strengths
- Clear, compelling formalization of questions as executable programs.
- Compositional grammar that covers the dataset and enables creative generation.
- Principled utility (EIG) and a learned trade-off with complexity and type preferences.
- Empirical validation with held-out contexts; ablations implicate key ingredients.
- Qualitative generation of novel, context-sensitive questions.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Domain and annotation specificity: All human questions are manually mapped to programs; discuss annotation protocol and reliability, and test transfer to another domain (e.g., a different structured game) to assess generality.
- Coupling of complexity prior and sampler: Using the same q(x) for both proposal and the complexity feature risks biasing toward what is easy to sample. Consider (i) using an independent complexity metric (e.g., description length/number of nodes) and (ii) a different or adaptive proposal for importance sampling; report sensitivity to the choice of q.
- Limited hypothesis coverage: Two contexts and a stochastic rule were dropped; quantify the effect on predictive performance and whether the model scales with approximate EIG (e.g., MCMC over hypotheses, amortized estimators).
- Utility realism: Human costs likely vary by answer type and compositional structure beyond length; explore richer cost models (e.g., cognitive cost proxies, answerer’s effort) and compare EIG with alternative utilities (e.g., value of information with misspecification, task loss).
- Evaluation of novelty: Augment qualitative examples with human judgments comparing sampled vs. human questions on helpfulness and naturalness.
- Bridge to language: Even a small semantic parser or template realizer (e.g., Wang et al.’s overnight approach) would strengthen claims about practical deployment.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually elegant and original formulation with solid empirical support.
- Addresses an underexplored but important capability (asking, not just answering).
- Advances compositional program-based modeling in a new direction.
- Clear ablation evidence that complexity matters alongside informativeness.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical scope is narrow (single domain, manual annotations, reduced contexts).
- Potential methodological confound in using q both as proposal and complexity prior.
- Lack of quantitative human evaluation of generated questions and limited reporting of learned parameters and computational efficiency.
- No integration with natural language, limiting immediate ML impact.

Overall: A thoughtful and technically sound contribution at the intersection of ML and cognitive modeling, with clear novelty and solid evidence within its scope. Strengthening the evaluation and decoupling the complexity prior from the sampler would further enhance the paper.
