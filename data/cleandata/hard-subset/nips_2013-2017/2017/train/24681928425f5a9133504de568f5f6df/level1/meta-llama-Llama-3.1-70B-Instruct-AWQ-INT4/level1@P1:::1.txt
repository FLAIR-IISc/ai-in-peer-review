The paper "Question Asking as Program Generation" presents a novel approach to modeling human question asking abilities in computational terms. The authors propose a probabilistic model that treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. This approach recasts the problem of question asking as one of program synthesis, offering a new perspective on human question asking.

The paper relates to previous work on active learning, AI dialog systems, and question generation. However, the authors argue that their approach is distinct from existing methods, which often rely on canned questions or require large datasets of images paired with questions. In contrast, their model can generate novel, human-like questions that are useful in a given context.

The authors evaluate their model using a data set of natural language questions asked by human participants in an information-search game. The results show that the model can predict the frequencies of human questions given a game context and can also synthesize novel human-like questions that were not present in the training set.

The strengths of the paper include:

* A novel and well-motivated approach to modeling human question asking abilities
* A clear and well-organized presentation of the model and its components
* Strong empirical results that demonstrate the model's ability to predict human question asking behavior
* A thorough discussion of the limitations of the current approach and potential avenues for future work

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The model relies on a specific grammar and set of features, which may not generalize to other domains or tasks
* The authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the model's parameters are learned or optimized
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison to existing methods and a discussion of the implications of the results for our understanding of human question asking abilities

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of artificial intelligence and cognitive science. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the model's limitations and potential avenues for future work.
