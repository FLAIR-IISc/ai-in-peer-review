- Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs generated by a grammar to capture compositionality and computability.  
- Uses a grammar-based framework that scores questions by informativeness and complexity.  
- Evaluates the model on a human question dataset from a Battleship-like game, showing it predicts human choices and generates novel, human-like queries.  
- Positions the work within active learning, dialog systems, and contrasts with goal-driven and deep learning question generation methods.  
- Connects to formal linguistics and cognitive science (compositionality, “language of thought”).  
- Strength: novel, theoretically grounded program-based representation of questions.  
- Strength: empirical advantage from combining informativeness and complexity metrics.  
- Strength: compelling qualitative examples of generated questions.  
- Strength: rigorous methodology with ablation studies and clear optimization description.  
- Strength: clear linkage to cognitive science theories strengthens contribution.  
- Weakness: grammar-based approach is domain-specific and requires careful design.  
- Weakness: limited external validity due to simplified Battleship game context.  
- Weakness: importance sampling approximation adds optimization complexity and potential bias.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of scaling to other domains and handling natural language directly.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound framework; strong predictive and generative results; rigorous experiments; solid interdisciplinary grounding.  
- Arguments against acceptance: domain-specific grammar, limited generalizability from the toy task, and complex optimization.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper; accept with minor revisions to address generalizability and optimization details.
