

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as formal programs that, when executed on a world state, output an answer. The authors introduce a probabilistic generative model that predicts human question patterns in a Battleship-like game domain, where players must identify ship configurations through strategic questioning. The model's key innovation lies in representing questions as programs in a simple language based on lambda calculus, allowing for compositional question generation while balancing informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain), complexity, answer type, and relevance.

The paper makes significant contributions to the field, building on recent work in computational cognitive science (e.g., Lake et al., 2015; Goodman et al., 2016) while addressing limitations of previous approaches to question generation (e.g., VQG datasets, goal-directed dialog systems). It successfully demonstrates that human-like question patterns can be predicted by combining program compositionality with probabilistic modeling of question features. The evaluation is thorough, with the full model outperforming variants that omit key features (informativeness, complexity, answer type) in predicting human question frequencies across contexts, and demonstrating the ability to generate novel, contextually appropriate questions not present in the training data.

Strengths include the theoretically grounded approach, clear connection to cognitive science principles, and strong empirical evaluation. The paper is well-written, with a logical flow from problem statement through model development to evaluation. The model's ability to generate creative questions that humans would ask represents a meaningful advance over existing neural approaches to question generation.

Weaknesses include the domain-specific nature of the grammar (though the authors acknowledge this limitation), the lack of direct integration with natural language processing systems, and the narrow scope of evaluation limited to a single game domain. While the authors acknowledge these limitations, future work extending the approach to broader domains would strengthen the paper's significance.

Pro: Novel theoretical framework bridging cognitive science and AI, strong empirical validation, clear model interpretability. Con: Limited domain scope, no direct application to real-world NLP systems, some domain-specific knowledge required for grammar design.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to understanding human question asking as a cognitive process. The work advances the state of the art in modeling goal-directed question generation and provides a promising foundation for future AI systems capable of rich, human-like questioning. I recommend acceptance.
