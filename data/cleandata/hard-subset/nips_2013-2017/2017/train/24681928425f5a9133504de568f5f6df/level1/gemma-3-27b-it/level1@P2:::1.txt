This paper proposes a novel framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as programs that, when executed, yield an answer. This is a compelling idea, grounded in the observation that human questions are often more targeted and informative than simple label requests in active learning. The authors demonstrate this with a dataset collected from a “Battleship”-like game, showing their model can predict human question choices and generate novel, plausible questions.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The idea of representing questions as programs is well-motivated and provides a formal basis for analyzing question informativeness. The probabilistic model, based on log-linear weighting of features, is a reasonable approach, and the use of importance sampling to address the intractable normalizing constant is appropriate. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing different model variants and demonstrating the importance of both informativeness and complexity. The ablation studies are a strong point. However, the connection between the “language of thought” framing and the actual programming language used feels somewhat tenuous. While the grammar captures the observed questions, it is not entirely clear why this particular formalism is superior to others. The Bayesian calculation of informativeness is clearly explained and justified, but its implementation details (e.g., handling of prior distributions) could be more explicit.  The reliance on a relatively small dataset (605 questions) is a potential limitation, although the authors acknowledge this. The claim of generating “clever” questions is subjective and relies heavily on the examples provided. A more quantitative assessment of the novelty and usefulness of generated questions would be valuable.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the proposed approach. The description of the grammar and probabilistic model is detailed, though potentially dense for readers unfamiliar with lambda calculus. The use of examples is helpful, particularly in Section 3 describing the game and question data. The notation is consistent and the equations are clearly presented. The supplementary material is crucial for understanding the full details of the grammar and optimization process.  A minor suggestion would be to provide a more intuitive explanation of the grammar rules – perhaps a walkthrough of how a specific human question is translated into a program.

**Originality:** The core idea of representing questions as programs is novel, although it builds on related work in program synthesis, active learning, and cognitive science. The specific combination of this idea with a probabilistic generative model and evaluation on a human question-asking dataset appears to be new. The paper clearly distinguishes its work from existing approaches in active learning, dialog systems, and visual question generation, highlighting the limitations of those approaches in capturing the richness and creativity of human questions. The authors adequately cite relevant work.  The biggest risk of overlap is with the broader area of program induction, but the focus on *question* generation, and the specific application to a human game-playing context, differentiate this work.

**Significance:** This work has the potential to be significant.  A better understanding of human question asking could lead to more intelligent and effective active learning algorithms, as well as more natural and engaging human-computer interfaces. The demonstration that a computational model can predict and generate human-like questions is a significant step forward. The finding that both informativeness and complexity are important for human question asking provides valuable insights for designing such algorithms. While the current application is limited to a specific game, the underlying framework is potentially generalizable to other domains. The paper addresses a challenging problem in a novel and promising way, and the results suggest that this approach could advance the state of the art.  However, the practical impact will depend on the ability to scale this approach to more complex domains and real-world applications.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to modeling question asking.
*   Strong technical foundation and thorough experimental evaluation.
*   Clear demonstration of the importance of both informativeness and complexity.
*   Potential for significant impact on active learning and human-computer interaction.
*   Ablation studies demonstrating the impact of different features.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on a relatively small dataset.
*   Subjectivity in evaluating the quality of generated questions.
*   The “language of thought” framing feels somewhat forced.
*   Limited scalability to more complex domains is unclear.



**Overall assessment:**  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem, with promising results and potential for significant impact. Addressing the concerns regarding dataset size and the evaluation of generated questions would further strengthen the paper.
