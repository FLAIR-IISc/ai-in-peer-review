- Paper models human question asking as generating typed, executable programs in a small lambda/LISP-like language evaluated against hypothesized worlds.  
- A PCFG defines a compositional space of questions enabling productivity beyond a fixed set.  
- A log-linear model combines Expected Information Gain (EIG), PCFG-based complexity (−log q(x)), answer-type priors, and a relevance filter to score questions.  
- Parameters are fit by maximizing likelihood on a Battleship-like dataset (605 questions, 18 contexts) using importance sampling to approximate intractable expectations and normalizers.  
- The full model outperforms alternatives in leave-one-context-out prediction, correlates with human frequencies (avg Spearman 0.64), and generates sensible novel questions.  
- Ablations show both informativeness (EIG) and complexity are necessary; removing complexity harms performance most.  
- Technical formulation is coherent: program representation, Bayesian hypothesis space, EIG utility, and log-linear prior are sound, with standard training and importance sampling.  
- Approximations include enumerating hypotheses, dropping two contexts and a stochastic grammar rule, capping program length, and treating p(d|h;x) as deterministic, which reduce coverage and ignore answer noise.  
- Complexity feature uses the proposal q(x) as −log q(x), coupling the sampler with the learned prior and risking bias toward easily sampled programs.  
- A hand-engineered relevance filter f∅ is used to counter grammar overgeneration.  
- Quantitative metrics (LL, rank correlations) support claims, but some key numbers (per-context LLs, parameter uncertainties) and broader human evaluations are missing.  
- Paper is generally clear with intuitive examples; full grammar, hypothesis sizes, runtime, and convergence details are deferred to the supplement and should be made accessible.  
- The idea of synthesizing LOT-style priors with EIG-based utilities for compositional program generation of questions is novel.  
- Significance: meaningful contribution to cognitive modeling and a promising direction for ML active learning/dialog, but practical impact is limited by single synthetic domain and manual annotations.  
- Strengths: elegant executable-question formalization, compositional grammar enabling creativity, principled EIG utility, learned complexity trade-off, held-out validation and qualitative novel samples.  
- Weaknesses/suggestions: manual program annotations and domain specificity need discussion and transfer tests; decouple complexity metric from proposal q(x) and test alternative proposals.  
- Further suggestions: quantify effects of dropped contexts/rules, explore scalable/approximate EIG methods, model richer cost/cognitive effort, and add human judgments for novelty/naturalness.  
- To strengthen claims, add a small semantic parser/realizer to connect to natural language and report learned weights, uncertainties, hypothesis counts, and runtime.  
- Arguments for acceptance: original, conceptually elegant, solid empirical support, and addresses underexplored capability (asking).  
- Arguments against acceptance: narrow empirical scope, methodological confound (q used both as proposal and complexity prior), limited quantitative human evaluation, and no language integration.
