### **Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel computational framework for modeling human goal-directed question asking. The central idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional, LISP-like language. This recasts the problem of question asking as one of program synthesis. The authors propose a probabilistic generative model that defines a distribution over the space of possible questions (programs). The probability of a given question is determined by a log-linear model, where the energy of a question is a weighted sum of several features. The key features are a measure of informativeness, based on Expected Information Gain (EIG) from a Bayesian ideal observer perspective, and a measure of complexity, derived from the program's length/structure in a probabilistic context-free grammar.

The model's parameters are fit to a dataset of natural language questions asked by humans in a "Battleship"-like game, where players ask questions to resolve ambiguity about a hidden board configuration. The paper evaluates the model by its ability to predict the distribution of human questions in held-out contexts and by its capacity to generate novel, meaningful questions not seen in the training data. The results show that a model incorporating both informativeness and complexity provides the best fit to human data, outperforming ablated models that lack one of these components.

This work connects several lines of research. It extends traditional active learning, which typically uses simple label queries, to a much richer, compositional space of information requests. It also draws heavily on ideas from computational cognitive science, particularly the "language of thought" hypothesis and models of concept learning as program induction (e.g., Lake et al., 2015; Goodman et al., 2008), applying these concepts to the generation of questions rather than the representation of concepts. Finally, it contrasts with data-hungry deep learning approaches to question generation by providing a framework that generalizes from very limited data.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of framing question asking as probabilistic program generation is highly original and significant. It provides an elegant and powerful formalism for understanding how humans construct creative, goal-directed queries. This work represents a substantial conceptual advance over existing models of active learning and question generation, offering a path toward machines that can learn more flexibly and intelligently. The idea of balancing informativeness with complexity is intuitive and provides a compelling explanation for human behavior.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed model is technically sound and well-executed. The use of a log-linear model over programs generated by a grammar is a principled way to explore the trade-off between different desiderata (informativeness, simplicity). The experimental methodology, including leave-one-out cross-validation and importance sampling for intractable computations, is appropriate. The ablation studies provide strong evidence for the necessity of both the information-gain and complexity components of the model.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly motivate the problem, explain the model components in a step-by-step manner, and present the results and their implications effectively. The use of concrete examples of questions and their program representations greatly aids understanding.

4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The model is validated on a challenging dataset of open-ended human questions. The quantitative results, showing that the full model provides the best predictive log-likelihood, are convincing. More impressively, the qualitative results in Figure 3 demonstrate the model's generative power. Its ability to produce novel, clever, and contextually relevant questions that were not produced by humans is a strong testament to the framework's capabilities.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Domain Specificity and Scalability:** The primary limitation is the reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar. While the authors note that some components are domain-general (e.g., logic, arithmetic), the core predicates are tailored to the Battleship game. It is unclear how this approach would scale to more open-ended, real-world domains where enumerating a complete and correct grammar would be a monumental task. This reliance on significant prior knowledge engineering is a major hurdle for broader application.

2.  **Disconnection from Natural Language:** The model operates entirely at the semantic level of programs, bypassing the difficult problem of translating between natural language and these formal representations. While this is a reasonable simplification for a foundational paper, it leaves a critical gap for building a complete question-asking system. The authors acknowledge this, but the challenge of grounding these programs in language remains substantial.

3.  **Computational Intractability:** The model relies on an ideal Bayesian observer to compute EIG, which requires iterating over a space of possible world hypotheses. This, along with the intractable partition function for the probability model, limits the approach to small, well-defined problem spaces. The authors had to omit two contexts from their analysis due to the computational cost, highlighting these scalability concerns.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   The comparison of Spearman's correlation across models is slightly confusing. The full model has a correlation of 0.64, while the information-agnostic model has a slightly higher correlation of 0.65, yet the latter has a much worse log-likelihood. While log-likelihood is arguably the more principled evaluation metric for a probabilistic model, this discrepancy deserves a brief comment. It likely reflects the noisiness of frequency counts in sparse data, where log-likelihood is more sensitive to penalizing the model for assigning low probability to observed data.

*   The bootstrap results comparing the full model to the information-agnostic (81%) and type-agnostic (88%) models are supportive but not overwhelming. Could you comment on the statistical power or potential reasons why the advantage isn't more decisive in the bootstrap analysis?

*   Regarding future work on scalability, have the authors considered methods for learning the grammar (or parts of it) from data? This could be a promising direction to reduce the reliance on manual engineering for new domains.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the fields of machine learning and computational cognitive science. It introduces a compelling new paradigm for thinking about active learning and inquiry. The theoretical framework is elegant, the implementation is technically sound, and the results are strong. While the approach has limitations in terms of scalability and its reliance on a pre-defined semantic representation, these are acceptable for a paper introducing such a novel research direction. The work is thought-provoking and is very likely to inspire follow-up research. It is a perfect fit for NIPS and I strongly recommend acceptance.
