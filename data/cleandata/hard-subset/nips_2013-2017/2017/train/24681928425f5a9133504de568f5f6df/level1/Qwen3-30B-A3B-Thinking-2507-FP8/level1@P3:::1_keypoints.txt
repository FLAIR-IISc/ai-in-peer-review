- Proposes modeling questions as executable programs combining Expected Information Gain, grammar-based complexity, answer type, and relevance to predict human questions in a Battleship-like game.
- Presents a probabilistic model that accurately predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, human-like questions not in the training set.
- Builds on literature in compositionality, program synthesis, and active learning; differs from neural question generation and dialog systems by focusing on compositional program generation.
- Technically sound with strong theoretical grounding and well-motivated feature choices.
- Includes lesion analyses to test contributions of model components.
- Experimental design is thorough, using leave-one-out cross-validation and bootstrapping for robust evaluation.
- Authors acknowledge limitations such as domain-specific grammar and the need for semantic representations rather than direct natural language processing.
- Novel question generation evaluation is compelling and shows creative, context-sensitive outputs.
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well organized, with precise grammar description and accessible mathematical presentation.
- Some grammar implementation details (e.g., handling set operators) could be more detailed.
- Original contribution: reframes question asking as program generation and treats compositionality as a linguistic property.
- Significant implications for active learning and interactive systems that ask human-like questions.
- Strength: conceptual novelty linking cognitive science and machine learning with rigorous evaluation.
- Strength: clearly demonstrates how humans balance informativeness and complexity in question asking.
- Weakness: single-domain (Battleship) evaluation limits claims of generalizability; adaptation to other domains needs discussion.
- Weakness: natural language interface gap is acknowledged but not practically addressed.
- Weakness: novel-question evaluation is qualitative rather than quantitatively assessed.
- Weakness: lacks discussion of handling ambiguous or poorly formed human questions.
- Weakness: computational complexity and scalability (importance sampling over large program spaces) not fully addressed.
- Recommendation: strong accept — meets NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
