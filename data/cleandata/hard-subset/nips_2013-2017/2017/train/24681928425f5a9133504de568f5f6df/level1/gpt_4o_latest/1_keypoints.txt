- Introduces a novel framework treating questions as programs that operate on world state, using a probabilistic generative model balancing informativeness and complexity.  
- Uses a compositional grammar and Bayesian belief updating to synthesize semantically meaningful, contextually relevant questions.  
- Evaluated on a 605-question dataset from an information-search game, showing ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel human-like questions.  
- Novelty: reframes question generation as program synthesis, bridging active learning, cognitive science, and AI.  
- Technical soundness: well-formulated probabilistic model with compositional grammar, EIG, complexity features, and Bayesian inference.  
- Empirical validation: real-world dataset evaluation with leave-one-out cross-validation and strong quantitative and qualitative results.  
- Significance: addresses underexplored problem of human-like question asking with implications for active learning, dialogue, and cognitive modeling.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and uses examples to aid comprehension.  
- Domain specificity: model depends on domain-specific knowledge (e.g., Battleship), limiting generalizability to other domains.  
- Natural language gap: operates on semantic representations rather than raw language, reducing real-world applicability.  
- Sparse human data: relatively small dataset and sparsity of human questions limit robustness and alignment in some contexts.  
- Computational complexity: importance sampling and large hypothesis space make the approach computationally expensive and potentially hard to scale.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a novel, important problem with innovative, sound methods and compelling results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited generalizability due to domain-specific grammar, small/context-specific dataset, and high computational overhead.  
- Recommendation: accept—contributions are significant and innovative, with strengths outweighing limitations.
