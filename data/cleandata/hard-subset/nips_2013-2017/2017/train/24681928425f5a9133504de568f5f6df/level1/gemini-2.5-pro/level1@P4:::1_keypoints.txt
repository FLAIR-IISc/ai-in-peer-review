- Proposes modeling goal-directed, open-ended question asking as probabilistic program generation in a compositional LISP-like language.
- Represents questions as programs executed on world states to produce answers, generated by a context-free grammar.
- Defines a log-linear generative model over programs with feature-based energy weighting.
- Key features: Expected Information Gain (informativeness), complexity penalty from grammar probability, answer-type preferences, and relevance filtering.
- Uses importance sampling to approximate the intractable normalizing constant during optimization.
- Evaluated on a Battleship-like task predicting human questions from partially revealed boards with leave-one-context-out cross-validation.
- Ablation study shows balancing informativeness and complexity is important; complexity feature is particularly influential.
- Method is more compositional, data-efficient, and goal-directed than typical deep-learning question generators.
- Paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and methodologically rigorous.
- Exceptionally clear and well-organized writing with effective figures and explanations.
- Highly original framing that applies program induction ideas to open-ended question generation.
- Significant potential impact for AI (interactive learning, dialogue) and cognitive science (quantitative model of human inquiry).
- Main weakness: heavy reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar limits scalability and generality.
- Lacks an end-to-end natural language interface; assumes lossless translation between language and programs.
- Evaluation is limited to a single, constrained domain with a modest dataset (605 questions).
- Bootstrap analysis shows EIG’s advantage is positive but not overwhelmingly robust across samples.
- Pro-acceptance: novel, principled framework with strong empirical and generative results and cross-disciplinary significance.
- Con-acceptance: concerns about scalability, absence of NL grounding, and limited evaluation domain.
