- Introduces a novel framework that models questions as compositional programs (lambda-calculus) and frames question asking as probabilistic program generation.  
- Uses a log-linear model over programs defined by a hand-crafted grammar that trades off informativeness and simplicity.  
- Quantifies informativeness with Expected Information Gain (EIG) and complexity via program length/grammar-based probability.  
- Evaluated on a human-collected "Battleship"-like question dataset and predicts human question frequency in held-out contexts.  
- Model is generative and can produce novel, contextually useful questions not seen in the data.  
- Ablation study shows both EIG and complexity penalties are critical; removing complexity breaks performance.  
- Theoretical framework is technically sound and empirically supported by leave-one-out CV, ablations, correlation analysis, and qualitative examples.  
- Authors transparently discuss limitations, notably dependence on a hand-engineered grammar and scaling challenges.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and uses examples and figures effectively.  
- Highly original: applies program synthesis and probabilistic reasoning to goal-directed question generation rather than answering.  
- Bridges active learning, cognitive science (language of thought), and program synthesis, differentiating from traditional active learning and VQG.  
- High potential impact for active learning, human-AI interaction, and computational cognitive science by modeling curiosity-like inquiry.  
- Main weaknesses: reliance on hand-crafted grammar and evaluation limited to a single, constrained game domain.  
- Recommendation: clear accept — significant conceptual advance, well-executed experiments, and strong potential to inspire future work.
