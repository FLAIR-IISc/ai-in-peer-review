- Core idea: model human question asking as probabilistic program generation in a compositional LISP-like language.  
- Model: log-linear distribution over program-questions with features for Expected Information Gain (informativeness) and program complexity.  
- Data & task: fitted to human questions in a "Battleship"-like game and evaluated on held-out contexts.  
- Evaluation: model predicts human question distributions and can generate novel, meaningful questions; full model (informativeness + complexity) fits best.  
- Connection to prior work: extends active learning to compositional queries and builds on program-induction cognitive models, offering data-efficient generalization.  
- Strength: originality — reframing question asking as program synthesis is a significant, novel contribution.  
- Strength: technical quality — principled log-linear formulation, appropriate inference (leave-one-out, importance sampling), and ablation studies.  
- Strength: clarity — well-written, motivated, with helpful examples linking programs to questions.  
- Strength: strong empirical results — convincing quantitative fit and qualitative generative examples of novel questions.  
- Weakness: domain specificity — relies on a hand-crafted, Battleship-specific grammar limiting generalization to open-ended domains.  
- Weakness: disconnect from natural language — operates at semantic-program level without addressing NL ↔ program translation.  
- Weakness: computational intractability — ideal Bayesian EIG and partition function computations limit scalability; some contexts omitted for cost.  
- Comment: reconcile discrepancy where Spearman correlation favors an ablated model (0.65) but log-likelihood favors the full model (0.64 vs lower LL), likely due to sparse-frequency noise.  
- Comment: bootstrap advantages (81% vs 88%) are supportive but modest; discuss statistical power and reasons for limited decisiveness.  
- Suggestion / future work: explore learning parts of the grammar from data to reduce manual engineering and improve scalability.  
- Overall conclusion: excellent, significant, and well-executed paper with acceptable limitations for a foundational contribution; strong recommendation for acceptance.
