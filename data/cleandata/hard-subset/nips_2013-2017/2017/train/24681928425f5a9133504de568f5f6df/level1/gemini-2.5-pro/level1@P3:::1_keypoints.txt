- Models goal-directed human question asking as probabilistic program generation in a compositional LISP-like language.
- Uses a grammar-defined program space and a log-linear model that trades off Expected Information Gain (informativeness) against program complexity.
- Evaluated on a Battleship-like dataset; predicts human question frequencies and generates novel, contextually appropriate questions.
- Ablation study shows both informativeness and complexity are critical; complexity-agnostic models fail.
- Original and significant contribution that opens new directions for active learning and computational cognitive science.
- Technically sound: well-motivated log-linear model, appropriate use of EIG, and convincing quantitative and qualitative results.
- Exceptionally clear writing and effective use of examples mapping natural language to programs.
- Primary weakness: reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar limits scalability and generalizability.
- Lacks an end-to-end natural language interface; human questions were manually translated into programs (semantic parsing not addressed).
- Uses importance sampling to approximate the partition function and gradients, but provides limited analysis of estimator stability and variance.
- The complexity feature (-log q(x) from a uniform PCFG) may not fully capture human cognitive effort; alternative psychological metrics could be explored.
- Pro acceptance: highly original, strong execution, rigorous ablation, and high potential impact across ML and cognitive science.
- Con acceptance: grammar hand-engineering and absence of semantic parsing limit immediate applicability.
- Overall recommendation: strong accept; strengths outweigh weaknesses and paper is a significant contribution.
