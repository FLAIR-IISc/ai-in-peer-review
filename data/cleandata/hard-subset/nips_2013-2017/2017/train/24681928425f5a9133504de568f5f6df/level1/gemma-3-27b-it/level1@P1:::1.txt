This paper introduces a novel framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as programs. The core idea is compelling: questions are formalized as executable programs that, when applied to a world state, yield an answer. The authors demonstrate this by building a probabilistic model that generates questions within a "Battleship"-like game context, demonstrating the ability to both predict human question choices and creatively produce novel, yet plausible, questions. This work is situated within the broader context of active learning, but distinguishes itself by moving beyond simple label requests to richer, more informative queries. It builds on previous work in cognitive science ([8], framing question asking as a cognitive tool) and links it to formal approaches like lambda calculus and program synthesis. The approach directly addresses limitations of existing AI dialog systems ([25, 1]) which often rely on pre-defined question sets, and contrasts with recent deep learning approaches ([22, 16]) that, while capable of generating questions, often lack the intentionality and flexibility of human inquiry.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is clearly defined, and the optimization procedure (importance sampling) is appropriate given the intractability of exact inference. The connection between the grammar and the program semantics is well-established. While the experimental setup is limited to a single game, the authors convincingly demonstrate the model's ability to predict and generate questions within that domain. The ablation studies (Table 1) effectively demonstrate the importance of each feature (informativeness, complexity, answer type). However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the importance sampling approximation and its potential impact on the results. A more thorough error analysis of the generated questions beyond the qualitative examples would also strengthen the claims.  It's a complete piece of work, providing a clear formulation, model description, experimental evaluation, and discussion of limitations. The authors are careful to highlight both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the core contribution. The grammar is presented clearly, and the probabilistic model is explained in a reasonably accessible manner. The use of examples is helpful, particularly in illustrating the program representation of questions. However, the mathematical notation could be slightly more consistent (e.g., the meaning of 'x' changes across different equations). The description of the features, while detailed, could be further improved with a more intuitive explanation of how each feature contributes to the overall model.  A visual representation of the grammar rules might also aid comprehension.

**Originality:** The core idea of modeling questions as programs is novel and represents a significant departure from traditional approaches in active learning and dialog systems. The combination of compositional semantics, probabilistic modeling, and a formal grammar is also noteworthy. The paper builds upon existing work in cognitive science and program synthesis, but it synthesizes these ideas in a new and compelling way. The exploration of informativeness *and* complexity as key factors in human question asking is a valuable contribution.  The authors acknowledge related work appropriately and position their contribution effectively.

**Significance:** The results are important because they offer a new perspective on human question asking and provide a promising framework for building more intelligent agents. The ability to generate novel, human-like questions is particularly significant.  If successful beyond the current domain, this approach could have a substantial impact on areas such as active learning, education, and human-computer interaction. The work addresses a difficult problem in a demonstrable way. The unique dataset collected by the authors [19] provides a valuable resource for future research.  The theoretical implications – formalizing question meaning as program evaluation – are potentially broad.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to modeling question asking.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict and generate human-like questions.
*   Clear presentation and well-supported experimental results.
*   Addresses a challenging problem with potential for significant impact.
*   Rigorous ablation studies demonstrate the importance of key features.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Evaluation is limited to a single game domain.
*   Importance sampling approximation could introduce bias.
*   Lack of detailed error analysis of generated questions.
*   Mathematical notation could be improved for clarity.
*   The connection to existing work in computational linguistics (e.g. semantic parsing) could be more explicit.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. Despite the limitations mentioned above, the novelty, technical soundness, and potential significance of this work outweigh its weaknesses. The authors have made a compelling case for their approach and provided a strong empirical demonstration of its effectiveness. The work offers a valuable contribution to the field and has the potential to inspire further research in active learning and intelligent agents.
