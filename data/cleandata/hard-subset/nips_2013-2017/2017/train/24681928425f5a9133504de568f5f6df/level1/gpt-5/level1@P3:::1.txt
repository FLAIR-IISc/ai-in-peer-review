Summary and relation to prior work
This paper models question asking as program generation: questions are lambda-calculus/LISP-like programs that, when executed on hypothesized world states, return answers. A PCFG defines a compositional space of question programs; a log-linear model over features (informativeness via Expected Information Gain, a zero-EIG indicator, complexity via −log PCFG probability, answer type indicators, and a relevance filter) induces a probability distribution over programs conditioned on context. Parameters are learned by maximum likelihood of human questions in an information-search game (Battleship-like) using importance sampling over 150k programs and gradient ascent. The model predicts human question distributions across contexts and produces novel, human-like questions; ablations indicate that both informativeness and complexity are critical.

This work draws on formal semantics of questions as partitions over worlds (Groenendijk & Stokhof), pragmatic modeling in cognitive science (Roberts; Hawkins et al.), probabilistic languages of thought and program induction (Goodman et al.; Lake et al.), semantic parsing to programs (Wang et al.), and active learning/optimal querying (Settles). It complements neural question-generation (e.g., VQG; Jain et al.) and dialogue systems, arguing for goal-directed, compositional question synthesis rather than pattern-based generation.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formalization of questions as executable programs linking meaning to EIG under a Bayesian hypothesis space; this unifies compositional semantics with active learning.
- A compact feature-based model that captures a core trade-off between informativeness and complexity, consistent with cognitive constraints on question production.
- Demonstrated productivity: the grammar covers all human questions (after modest exclusions) and enables generation of novel, context-relevant questions absent from training.
- Quantitative evaluation with leave-one-context-out, plus ablation studies that isolate the role of EIG and complexity; complexity emerges as especially important.
- Careful treatment of program equivalence for novelty (mutual information over answer partitions), which is a nice semantic criterion.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Heavy domain engineering: the grammar includes domain-specific predicates (ships, colors, size), a hand-crafted relevance filter, and a PCFG prior that doubles as a complexity prior. It is unclear how much transfers to new domains without substantial manual effort.
- The complexity feature is −log q(x) under a uniform PCFG; this conflates linguistic/program length with grammar idiosyncrasies. Sensitivity to grammar design and rule probabilities is not explored; an MDL/code-length or simple program-length prior might be more robust/comparable.
- Learning and inference rely on a fixed importance sampler (uniform PCFG) and reuse of 150k samples with 100k iterations. As θ changes, importance weights may degenerate, risking bias/variance issues and unstable normalization estimates. No diagnostics (ESS, variance of Ẑ) are reported.
- Dataset limitations and exclusions: contexts 1–2 and “draw” questions are dropped for computational reasons; some questions were omitted as ambiguous. This may bias evaluation and reduces the claim of broad coverage.
- The reported average Spearman correlation is slightly higher for the information-agnostic model (0.65) than for the full model (0.64), while LL favors the full model in most bootstrap samples. This discrepancy merits analysis (e.g., sensitivity to sparse counts, the impact of the relevance/type features, or the rank-based vs likelihood metrics).
- No mapping from natural language to programs at test time; the model explains semantic production but not end-to-end question asking. The program translation step is manual and may hide ambiguity/underspecification issues that arise in natural language.
- EIG depends on a specific prior over hypotheses (uniform sizes/placements) and assumes deterministic answers; human priors and pragmatic answerer noise are plausible and could alter predictions. The finding of weak correlation between question frequency and EIG alone underscores this gap.

Quality
Technically sound in its formulation of the hypothesis space, EIG, and log-linear modeling. However, the approximate inference/learning procedure needs more justification and diagnostics. Claims are supported by LL/correlation and qualitative samples, but the small, domain-specific dataset and exclusions limit generality. Ablations are appropriate and informative.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized; the formalism is accessible with examples. Critical implementation details (full grammar, proposal distribution, IS diagnostics, hyperparameters) are deferred to the supplement or not reported; adding more detail would aid reproducibility. The evaluation metrics and the correlation discrepancy should be clarified.

Originality
Modeling question generation as program synthesis with an EIG/complexity trade-off is a novel and timely synthesis across semantics, active learning, and cognitive modeling. Prior work modeled answering (semantic parsing to programs) and neural QG, but endogenously generating goal-driven questions in a compositional program space is new.

Significance
Conceptually significant for cognitive modeling of curiosity and active querying; provides a potentially general recipe for question-asking agents. Practical impact for NeurIPS audiences would increase with demonstrations beyond a single small domain and with an end-to-end NL interface. As-is, significance is moderate.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report importance sampling diagnostics (ESS, variance of Ẑ) and assess sensitivity to proposal distributions and sample size; consider resampling during training or using AIS/SMC.
- Compare complexity priors (program length, MDL, description length of AST) and analyze sensitivity to grammar design; learn rule probabilities from data if possible.
- Incorporate pragmatic costs (answerer/load), and/or an RSA-style utility that better matches human preferences beyond EIG.
- Provide a semantic parser or a mapping layer to NL to demonstrate end-to-end generation/interpretation; even a small mapping would strengthen applicability claims.
- Evaluate on an additional micro-domain with minimal new engineering to test transfer.
- Reconcile the correlation vs likelihood results; include human evaluation of generated questions’ usefulness/naturalness.

Arguments for acceptance
- Elegant, principled framework connecting program semantics and active information gathering.
- Clear empirical evidence that complexity and informativeness jointly explain human question choices.
- Demonstrated ability to generate novel, context-appropriate questions.
- Cross-disciplinary contribution likely to interest NeurIPS communities in cognitive modeling, program induction, and interactive learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong domain dependence and manual grammar engineering; unclear generality.
- Approximate inference/training lacks robustness analysis; potential biases.
- Small, filtered dataset with limited external validity; no end-to-end NL handling.
- Mixed quantitative signals (correlation vs likelihood) and limited baseline comparisons beyond ablations.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and well-motivated paper with solid ideas; with stronger inference diagnostics, complexity prior analysis, and some step toward NL grounding or cross-domain generalization, it would be compelling.
