- Treats questions as executable programs evaluated against world states, offering a formal modeling framework.  
- Introduces a probabilistic, compositional grammar-based model favoring concise, informative queries.  
- Uses importance sampling to handle the intractable normalization constant.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship-like dataset of human questions and contexts.  
- Model predicts human question choices and generates novel, human-like questions; full model outperforms ablations (Table 1).  
- Positions work relative to active learning, dialog systems, visual question generation, formal semantics, and language-of-thought literature.  
- Novel contribution: framing question asking as program generation.  
- Technically sound: information-gain principles and compositional grammar are well-motivated.  
- Empirical validation is convincing but limited by dataset size.  
- Paper is clearly written with helpful examples and organization.  
- Weakness: domain specificity (Battleship grammar may not generalize without major changes).  
- Weakness: grammar complexity could hinder scalability and NLP integration.  
- Weakness: relatively small dataset (605 pairs) risks limited statistical power/overfitting.  
- Weakness: reliance on hand-engineered features rather than learned representations.  
- Weakness: qualitative evaluation of generated questions is limited; human-judged evaluation would strengthen claims.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, impactful approach with solid technical and empirical support.  
- Rejection concerns: limited generalizability, grammar complexity, and small dataset.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address domain generalization and more rigorous qualitative evaluation.
