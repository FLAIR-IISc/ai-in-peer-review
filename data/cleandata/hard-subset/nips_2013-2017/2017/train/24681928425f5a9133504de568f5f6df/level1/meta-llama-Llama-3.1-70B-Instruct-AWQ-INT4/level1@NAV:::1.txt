This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling human question asking as a program generation task. The authors introduce a cognitive model that treats questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The model specifies a probability distribution over a complex, compositional space of programs, favoring concise programs that help the agent learn in the current context.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The use of a probabilistic generative model to predict which questions people will ask and which not is a significant contribution to the field of active learning. The evaluation of the model using a data set of natural language questions asked by human participants in an information-search game is also a strong aspect of the paper.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to capture the compositionality and computability of human questions. The authors show that their model can predict the frequencies of human questions given a game context and can also synthesize novel human-like questions that were not present in the training set. The results demonstrate that the model is capable of asking novel and clever human-like questions that are useful in their respective contexts.

The paper also provides a thorough discussion of related work, highlighting the limitations of previous approaches to active learning and question asking. The authors demonstrate that their approach is more flexible and generalizable than previous methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main limitations is that the model operates on semantic representations rather than on natural language text directly. This may limit the applicability of the model to real-world scenarios where natural language text is the primary input. Additionally, some aspects of the grammar are specific to the Battleship domain, which may require significant modifications to apply the model to other domains.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a significant contribution to the field of active learning and question asking. The model's ability to capture the compositionality and computability of human questions is a major strength, and the results demonstrate its potential for generating novel and clever human-like questions.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling human question asking as a program generation task.
* The model is capable of predicting the frequencies of human questions given a game context and synthesizing novel human-like questions that were not present in the training set.
* The results demonstrate that the model is capable of asking novel and clever human-like questions that are useful in their respective contexts.
* The paper provides a thorough discussion of related work, highlighting the limitations of previous approaches to active learning and question asking.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The model operates on semantic representations rather than on natural language text directly, which may limit its applicability to real-world scenarios.
* Some aspects of the grammar are specific to the Battleship domain, which may require significant modifications to apply the model to other domains.
* The paper could benefit from more discussion on the potential applications and limitations of the model in real-world scenarios.

Rating: 8/10
