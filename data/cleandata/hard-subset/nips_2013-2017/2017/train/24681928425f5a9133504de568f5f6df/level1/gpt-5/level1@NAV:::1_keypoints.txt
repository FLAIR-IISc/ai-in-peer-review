- Proposes a probabilistic, compositional model of human question asking: questions as executable programs, with a log-linear distribution trading off expected information gain (EIG), PCFG-derived complexity prior, answer type, and relevance.  
- Evaluated on a Battleship-style information-search game (605 human questions, 18 contexts); model predicts held-out-context question distributions and synthesizes novel context-appropriate questions.  
- Ablations indicate both informativeness and complexity matter; full model gives strong log-likelihoods vs variants and Spearman ≈ 0.64 between model energy and human frequencies.  
- Technical formulation is sound and carefully implemented, using importance sampling to approximate the intractable partition function and expectations over programs.  
- Ablations and leave-one-context-out evaluation are appropriate methodological choices.  
- Caveat: Full model’s rank correlation is comparable to or slightly below an information-agnostic variant, so EIG alone doesn’t fully explain human question frequency.  
- Caveat: Excluding some contexts and the “draw” operator reduces coverage and may bias estimates of model performance.  
- Further diagnostics needed on sampler bias, sensitivity to the grammar prior, and assumptions about participants’ priors to strengthen claims.  
- Clarity: Paper is well written with clear motivation and intuitive examples; some essential grammar and computational-budget details are relegated to the supplement and should be summarized in the main text.  
- Originality: While questions-as-programs appear in prior QA work, learning a distribution over question-programs that balances EIG and complexity to predict human question asking is a novel contribution.  
- Significance: Promising step toward agents that ask rich, goal-directed questions with potential impact on dialog systems and interactive learning, though currently limited to one domain and semantic (not natural language) representations.  
- Pros: Clear, principled formulation; strong ablations and predictive results; integrates compositional program representations with Bayesian utility; generates novel, human-like questions.  
- Cons: Domain- and grammar-specific engineering with limited generalization evidence; mixed evidence for EIG’s incremental contribution; approximation choices (importance proposal, context exclusions) require deeper analysis.
