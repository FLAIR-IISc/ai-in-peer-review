

# Peer Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as formal programs that, when executed on the state of the world, output an answer. The authors propose a probabilistic model that generates questions by sampling from a compositional program space, balancing informativeness (measured via Expected Information Gain), complexity (favoring shorter programs), answer type, and relevance. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans in a Battleship-like game, where players sought to identify ship configurations through question asking.

This work builds on NIPS contributions in probabilistic program induction (Lake et al., NIPS 2015), cognitive science approaches to compositional semantics (Fodor, Marcus), and active learning frameworks. It differs from previous work by explicitly modeling the compositional structure of questions rather than treating them as simple input-output pairs. Unlike recent neural approaches to question generation (e.g., Jain et al., 2017), which require large datasets and produce stereotyped questions, this model captures the intentional, goal-directed nature of human question asking with a relatively small training set.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with rigorous evaluation. The probabilistic model is well-motivated, and the authors carefully address limitations (e.g., domain-specific grammar elements). The experimental design (leave-one-out cross-validation) is appropriate for the task, and the quantitative results (log-likelihood comparisons) provide strong evidence for the model's effectiveness. The model's ability to generate novel human-like questions demonstrates its creative capacity beyond simple pattern matching.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical concepts (compositionality, computability, grammar) are explained clearly with helpful examples. The grammar description (Section 4.2) is sufficiently detailed for experts to reproduce the results, and the supplementary materials provide additional implementation details. The figures effectively illustrate key concepts (e.g., program representation and novel question generation).

**Originality:** This is a highly original contribution that reframes question asking as program synthesis. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work on question generation (e.g., VQG, neural "20 questions" systems) by capturing both the compositional structure of questions and the goal-directed nature of human question asking. The authors successfully connect their work to foundational cognitive science concepts (e.g., language of thought, compositional semantics) while providing a computationally implementable framework.

**Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental aspect of human cognition that has been underexplored in machine learning. The framework provides a new direction for developing AI systems that can learn through human-like questioning. The ability to generate novel, contextually appropriate questions demonstrates significant potential for applications in human-AI interaction, educational technology, and cognitive modeling.

## Weaknesses

**Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the grammar is heavily tailored to the Battleship domain. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how much domain-specific knowledge would be required to adapt the framework to new domains, which could limit its broader applicability.

**Natural Language Generation:** The model operates on semantic representations rather than generating natural language directly. While the authors mention bridging this gap with computational linguistics tools, this represents a significant gap between the model's output and a practical AI system that would need to produce natural language questions.

**Question Complexity:** The paper doesn't adequately address the potential for the model to generate questions that are too complex for human comprehension. While complexity is modeled as a feature, the authors don't provide evidence that the generated questions remain within the cognitive processing limits of humans.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Highly original contribution that reframes question asking as program synthesis
- Rigorous evaluation demonstrating the model's ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel questions
- Strong connection to foundational cognitive science concepts
- Well-written and technically sound with appropriate statistical grounding
- Clear distinction from previous work on question generation

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Domain-specific grammar may limit broader applicability without significant adaptation
- Limited discussion of how to bridge the semantic representation to natural language generation
- Insufficient exploration of the cognitive limits of the generated questions
- The model's performance is evaluated only on a single domain (Battleship game)

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a significant contribution to computational cognitive science and AI. The framework of modeling questions as programs is novel, well-executed, and demonstrates clear advantages over previous approaches to question generation. The model's ability to generate novel, human-like questions that were not present in the training data is particularly impressive.

The paper is technically strong, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. While the domain-specific nature of the grammar is a limitation, the authors appropriately acknowledge this and the framework's potential for extension to other domains. The work represents an important step toward AI systems that can learn through human-like questioning.

I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS 2017. This paper advances the state of the art in modeling human question asking, provides a new framework for active learning, and opens up promising directions for future research in human-AI interaction. The significance of the work for computational cognitive science and AI is substantial, and the paper is well-positioned to influence future research in these areas.
