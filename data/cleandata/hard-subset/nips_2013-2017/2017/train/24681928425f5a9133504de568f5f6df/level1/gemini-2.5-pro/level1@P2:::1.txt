### Paper Review

**Summary:**
This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling goal-directed, open-ended human question asking. The central and elegant idea is to represent questions as formal programs in a compositional language based on lambda calculus. This recasts the problem of question asking as one of probabilistic program generation. The authors propose a log-linear model that defines a probability distribution over the space of possible programs, which is itself defined by a hand-crafted grammar. This model favors questions that are both informative and simple. Informativeness is quantified by the Expected Information Gain (EIG) over a distribution of possible world states, while simplicity (or complexity) is captured by the program's length/structure under a probabilistic context-free grammar.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions collected from humans playing a "Battleship"-like game, where their goal is to ask questions to resolve ambiguity about the hidden game board configuration. The authors demonstrate that their model can predict the frequency distribution of human questions in held-out game contexts. An ablation study compellingly shows that both informativeness and complexity are critical features for explaining human behavior; a model without a complexity penalty performs very poorly. Furthermore, the model is shown to be generative, capable of producing novel and contextually useful questions that were not observed in the human data.

This work connects ideas from active learning (EIG), cognitive science (compositionality, "language of thought"), and program synthesis. It distinguishes itself from traditional active learning, which typically uses simple label queries, and from recent deep learning approaches to question generation (VQG), which are often less goal-directed and require much larger datasets.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:** The paper presents a high-quality, complete piece of research.
*   The theoretical framework is technically sound. Modeling questions as programs is a powerful formalism, and the probabilistic model built upon it is well-defined.
*   The claims are strongly supported by a combination of quantitative and qualitative evidence. The leave-one-out cross-validation and ablation studies provide rigorous quantitative support for the model's components. The correlation analysis (Figure 2) and, particularly, the examples of novel generated questions (Figure 3) provide compelling qualitative evidence for the model's capabilities.
*   The authors are careful and honest about the limitations of their work, explicitly discussing the reliance on a hand-engineered grammar and the challenge of scaling to new domains. This adds to the credibility of the research.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and a pleasure to read.
*   The motivation for the problem is well-articulated, and the core idea is introduced intuitively before the formal details are presented.
*   The structure is logical, moving from the dataset to the model's components (grammar, features, optimization) and finally to the results.
*   The use of examples, such as the different ways to ask about ship size, effectively illustrates the principle of compositionality and motivates the program synthesis approach. The figures are informative and support the main text well. The writing provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the methodology.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original.
*   While its constituent parts (program synthesis, EIG, log-linear models) are known, their application to the problem of *generating* goal-directed, open-ended questions is novel and represents a significant conceptual contribution. Previous work in program induction has focused more on concept learning or question *answering*. This paper pioneers its use for question *asking*.
*   The work carves out a new direction that sits between classic active learning and data-driven dialogue systems. It clearly differentiates its contributions from related work, showing how it addresses the limitations of both (i.e., the representational poverty of active learning queries and the lack of flexible goal-directedness in many neural models).

**Significance:** The paper has high potential for significant impact.
*   It addresses a fundamental and challenging problem in AI: how to build machines that can ask intelligent, creative questions to learn about their environment. This is a key aspect of curiosity and a hallmark of human intelligence that is largely absent in current systems.
*   The framework is likely to inspire new research in several areas. For practitioners in active learning, it suggests a path toward richer and more efficient querying mechanisms. For researchers in human-AI interaction and cognitive science, it provides a powerful, testable model of human inquiry.
*   By demonstrating a successful synthesis of symbolic (program generation) and probabilistic (Bayesian inference) reasoning, the paper contributes to the broader discussion on building more robust and human-like AI systems. While the immediate applicability is constrained by the domain-specificity of the grammar, the conceptual advance is substantial and provides a strong foundation for others to build upon.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a highly original and elegant framework for a fundamental AI problem.
*   **Pro:** The model successfully combines principles from machine learning and cognitive science in a novel way.
*   **Pro:** The experimental results are strong, with thorough ablations and compelling qualitative examples of generative creativity.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clear.
*   **Pro:** The work has high potential to influence future research in active learning, dialogue systems, and computational cognitive science.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   **Con:** The primary limitation is the reliance on a hand-crafted grammar, which raises questions about scalability and the amount of domain engineering required to apply the method elsewhere.
*   **Con:** The evaluation is conducted within a single, relatively constrained game-like environment.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that presents a significant conceptual advance. The authors tackle a difficult and important problem with an original and well-executed approach. The weaknesses regarding scalability are acknowledged by the authors and are typical for foundational work of this nature. The strengths in originality, quality, clarity, and potential significance far outweigh these limitations. This paper is a clear accept and represents the kind of creative, thought-provoking research that NIPS should showcase.
