This paper presents a novel framework for modeling human question asking, framing questions as programs that, when executed, produce an answer. This is a compelling idea, drawing connections between program synthesis, cognitive science, and active learning. The authors evaluate their model on a dataset of questions asked during a “Battleship”-like game, demonstrating that it can predict human question choices and even generate novel, plausible questions. 

The work builds effectively on prior research in active learning (e.g., Settles, 2008), which largely focuses on simple label requests, and contrasts this with the richness of human queries. It also positions itself well against work in dialog systems (Serban et al., 2017) and visual question generation (Mostafazadeh et al., 2016), arguing that those approaches lack the intentional, goal-directed nature of the questions studied here. The connection to linguistic theories of compositionality (e.g., Fodor, 1987) and the “language of thought” (ibid) is thoughtfully made and provides a strong theoretical foundation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of modeling questions as programs is genuinely innovative and offers a powerful new lens for understanding human inquiry.
* **Strong Theoretical Grounding:**  The work is well-connected to relevant research in cognitive science and linguistics, providing a solid theoretical basis for the proposed model.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results are promising, showing that the model can predict human question choices and generate plausible new questions.  The use of leave-one-out cross-validation provides a reasonable evaluation strategy.
* **Careful Analysis:** The ablation studies (comparing the full model to variants) demonstrate the importance of both informativeness and complexity in generating human-like questions. This nuanced analysis is a major strength.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally clear and well-structured, making it relatively easy to follow the complex ideas presented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Grammar Complexity & Scalability:** While the grammar provides a useful abstraction, it seems quite complex (Tables SI-1 and SI-2). The authors acknowledge limitations related to scaling to more complex domains and the need for domain knowledge in constructing the grammar.  It’s unclear how easily this approach would generalize without significant manual effort.
* **Computational Cost:** The importance sampling method used for optimization is computationally expensive, as noted by the authors. This limits the scalability of the model and makes it difficult to experiment with larger grammars or datasets.
* **Limited Generalization Beyond the Game:** The evaluation is confined to a specific game context. While insightful, it’s unclear how well the model would perform in more open-ended or diverse scenarios.  The authors acknowledge this as a limitation.
* **Feature Engineering:** The features used in the model, while justified, rely on some degree of manual engineering (e.g., defining "relevance" through the 'b' marker).  Exploring more automated feature learning approaches could be beneficial.
* **EIG Calculation:** The calculation of Expected Information Gain (EIG) is a significant component of the model. The assumptions and simplifications made in this calculation (as hinted at in the paper) could potentially impact the accuracy and robustness of the model. More detailed justification of these choices would be valuable.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are reasonably well-supported by the experimental results. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their work. It is a complete piece of work for a conference paper, although further development and generalization would be needed for a more mature system.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, but some sections (e.g., the details of the optimization procedure and the grammar itself) are dense and require careful reading. Providing more illustrative examples and visual aids could improve clarity.

**Originality:** The idea of modeling questions as programs is highly original and represents a significant departure from traditional approaches to question generation and active learning. 

**Significance:** The results are important because they provide a new framework for understanding human question asking and potentially for building more intelligent machines. The ability to generate novel, plausible questions is a significant step towards more human-like AI. The work addresses a difficult problem in a novel and potentially impactful way.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to modeling human question asking.
* Demonstrates promising empirical results on a challenging dataset.
* Offers a nuanced analysis of the factors that contribute to human-like question generation.
* Contributes to the growing field of cognitive AI and active learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The approach is computationally expensive and may not scale well to more complex domains.
* The evaluation is limited to a specific game context.
* The model relies on some degree of manual feature engineering.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. This is a strong paper that presents a novel and significant contribution to the field. While the limitations are acknowledged and are not fatal, addressing them in future work would further strengthen the impact of this research. I suggest the authors address the clarity of the optimization procedure and provide more justification for the EIG calculation.  Additionally, a brief discussion of potential approaches for improving scalability and generalization would be beneficial.
