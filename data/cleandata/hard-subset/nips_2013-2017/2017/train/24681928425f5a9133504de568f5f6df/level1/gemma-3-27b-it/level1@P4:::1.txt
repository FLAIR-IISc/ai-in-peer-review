This paper presents a novel framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as programs. The core idea is compelling: questions can be formalized as executable programs that, when evaluated against a world state, yield an answer. The authors introduce a probabilistic model over this space of programs, favoring concise and informative queries, and evaluate it against a dataset of human questions collected in a Battleship-like game. The results demonstrate that the model can predict human question choices and generate novel, human-like questions.

The work builds upon existing research in active learning (21), which traditionally focuses on simple label requests, and attempts to bridge the gap towards more human-like, rich questioning strategies. It also relates to work in dialog systems (25, 1) but argues that those systems lack the flexibility and creativity present in human questioning. The authors appropriately highlight the difference between their approach and visual question generation (16) which relies on large paired image-question datasets, whereas their model learns from a more limited, context-driven environment. The connection to formal semantics (6, 18, 9) and the “language of thought” (3) are also well-placed.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The framing of question asking as program generation is a significant contribution. It provides a formal and potentially powerful approach to modeling this complex cognitive ability.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The probabilistic model, based on a compositional grammar and informed by information gain principles, appears well-motivated and theoretically sound. The use of importance sampling to address the intractability of the normalization constant is a sensible approach.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The model’s ability to predict human question choices and generate novel, plausible questions provides strong evidence of its validity. The quantitative results (Table 1) demonstrate a clear advantage of the full model over its ablated variants.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors provide clear explanations of their approach and connect it to relevant prior work. The inclusion of examples (e.g., Figure 3) helps to illustrate the model's capabilities.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Domain Specificity:** While the authors acknowledge the domain specificity of their grammar, the reliance on the Battleship game may limit the generalizability of their findings.  The extent to which the proposed framework can be applied to other domains without significant modifications remains unclear.
*   **Complexity of the Grammar:** The grammar, while expressive, appears complex (Tables SI-1 and SI-2). This complexity might make it challenging to scale the approach to more complex domains or to integrate it with natural language processing techniques.
*   **Limited Dataset Size:** The dataset of 605 question-context pairs, while valuable, is relatively small. This could limit the statistical power of the evaluation and potentially lead to overfitting.
*   **Feature Engineering:** The reliance on hand-engineered features (informativeness, complexity, answer type) introduces a degree of subjectivity. Exploring automated feature learning techniques could be a valuable direction for future work.
*   **Qualitative Evaluation:** The qualitative evaluation of the generated questions (Figure 3) is somewhat limited. A more systematic evaluation, perhaps involving human judges, would strengthen the claims about the quality and human-likeness of the generated questions.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to modeling human question asking.
*   The technical framework is sound and the empirical results are compelling.
*   The work addresses a significant challenge in AI and cognitive science.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains its contributions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The domain specificity of the approach may limit its broader applicability.
*   The complexity of the grammar could hinder scalability.
*   The limited dataset size raises concerns about statistical power.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of AI and cognitive science. The novel framing of question asking as program generation is particularly noteworthy. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding domain specificity and the need for a more rigorous qualitative evaluation of the generated questions.  The authors should also clearly discuss how the grammar could be generalized or adapted to other domains.
