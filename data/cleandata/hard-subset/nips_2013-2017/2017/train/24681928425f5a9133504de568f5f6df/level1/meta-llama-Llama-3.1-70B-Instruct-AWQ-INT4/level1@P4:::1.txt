**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper introduces a cognitive model for constructing human-like questions, treating questions as formal programs that output an answer when executed on the state of the world. The model specifies a probability distribution over a complex, compositional space of programs, favoring concise programs that help the agent learn in the current context. The authors evaluate their approach using a data set of natural language questions asked by humans in an information-search game, finding that their model predicts human question frequencies and can generate novel, human-like questions.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds on previous work in active machine learning, cognitive science, and linguistics. The authors draw inspiration from compositionality and computability, concepts that have been influential in cognitive science and linguistics. They also relate their work to formalizing question meaning as a partition over possible worlds, a notion used in previous studies in linguistics and psychology.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The paper proposes a new computational framework for modeling question asking, treating questions as programs that output an answer when executed on the state of the world.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate their approach using a large data set of natural language questions asked by humans in an information-search game, demonstrating the model's ability to predict human question frequencies and generate novel, human-like questions.
3. **Interdisciplinary connections**: The paper draws on concepts and ideas from cognitive science, linguistics, and computer science, demonstrating the potential for interdisciplinary collaboration and knowledge transfer.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Domain specificity**: The model is currently specific to the Battleship domain, and it is unclear how much new knowledge engineering will be needed to extend the approach to new domains.
2. **Semantic representations**: The system operates on semantic representations rather than on natural language text directly, which may limit its applicability to real-world scenarios.
3. **Computational complexity**: The model's computational complexity may be a limitation, particularly for large hypothesis spaces.

**Originality**

The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling question asking, treating questions as programs that output an answer when executed on the state of the world. The authors also introduce a new probabilistic generative model for sampling context-sensitive, relevant programs from this space.

**Significance**

The paper's results have significant implications for our understanding of human question asking and the development of more capable active learning machines. The authors demonstrate how people balance informativeness and complexity when producing semantically coherent questions, providing the first predictive model to date of open-ended human question asking.
