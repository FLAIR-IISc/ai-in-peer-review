

# Peer Review: "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper introduces a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as formal programs that execute on possible world states to produce answers. The authors propose a probabilistic model that combines question informativeness (via Expected Information Gain), complexity (via grammar-based log probability), answer type, and relevance features to predict human question frequencies in a Battleship-like game. The model demonstrates strong predictive power on human question data and generates novel, human-like questions not present in the training set.

The work builds on significant previous contributions, particularly in cognitive science (compositionality as language of thought [3, 4, 10]), program synthesis [12, 17, 24], and active learning [21]. It differs from recent neural approaches to question generation [11, 16, 20, 23] by focusing on compositional program generation rather than statistical language modeling, and from goal-directed dialog systems [1, 25] by capturing open-ended, context-sensitive question asking rather than constrained canned questions.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with rigorous theoretical grounding. The probabilistic model is well-motivated, with careful consideration of question features and model variants (lesioning each key component). The experimental design is thorough, employing leave-one-out cross-validation and bootstrapping to robustly evaluate model performance. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, including the domain-specific nature of their grammar and the need for semantic representation rather than direct natural language processing. The evaluation of novel question generation is particularly compelling, with the model producing creative questions that demonstrate genuine understanding of the game context.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The key concepts (compositionality, computability, program representation) are clearly explained with helpful examples. The grammar description is precise, and the probabilistic model is presented with mathematical rigor yet remains accessible. Figures effectively illustrate the model's predictions and novel question generation. The paper provides sufficient detail for replication, though some aspects of the grammar implementation (e.g., handling of set operators) could be slightly more detailed.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant conceptual contribution by reframing question asking as program generation, which is distinct from previous approaches. The compositionality of questions is modeled as a linguistic property (not just a statistical pattern), and the use of program execution to evaluate question informativeness represents a novel perspective. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly in its focus on human-like open-ended question asking rather than stereotyped or constrained questions. The authors have thoroughly reviewed the relevant literature, including recent NIPS papers on program induction [13] and question generation [11, 16, 23].

**Significance:** The work has substantial significance for advancing active learning systems toward human-like question asking capabilities. By modeling questions as programs, the authors provide a framework that could enable machines to ask rich, context-sensitive questions to learn about the world. The ability to generate novel, useful questions (as demonstrated in Figure 3) suggests potential for applications in interactive learning systems. The paper makes a strong case for the importance of both informativeness and complexity in human question asking, which has implications for cognitive science.

**Strengths:**
- Conceptual novelty in modeling questions as executable programs
- Strong theoretical foundation connecting cognitive science and machine learning
- Rigorous evaluation showing predictive power and creative question generation
- Clear demonstration of how human question asking balances multiple factors (informativeness, complexity, answer type)
- Appropriate handling of model variants and statistical validation

**Weaknesses:**
- The single-domain evaluation (Battleship game) limits generalizability claims; the paper would benefit from discussion of how the grammar would need to be adapted for other domains
- The natural language interface gap (semantic representation vs. natural language) is acknowledged but not adequately addressed as a practical challenge
- The evaluation of novel question generation is qualitative rather than quantitative, making it difficult to assess the model's true creative capacity
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the model would handle ambiguous or poorly formed questions that humans sometimes ask
- The computational complexity of the approach (requiring importance sampling over a large program space) isn't fully discussed in terms of scalability

**Recommendation:** This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of active learning and computational cognitive science. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and represents a meaningful advancement beyond previous approaches to question asking. While the single-domain evaluation is a limitation, the conceptual framework is robust and generalizable. The paper's main weakness is the limited discussion of practical implementation challenges for natural language interfaces, but this is a common challenge in this line of work.

The paper meets all the NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel approach to question generation (Originality), and addresses a significant problem in AI (Significance). I recommend acceptance.

**Pro/Con Summary:**
Pro: Novel program-based framework for question generation, strong theoretical foundation, excellent evaluation of human question prediction and novel question generation, clear demonstration of the importance of complexity alongside informativeness.
Con: Limited domain scope, insufficient discussion of natural language interface challenges, qualitative rather than quantitative evaluation of novel questions.
