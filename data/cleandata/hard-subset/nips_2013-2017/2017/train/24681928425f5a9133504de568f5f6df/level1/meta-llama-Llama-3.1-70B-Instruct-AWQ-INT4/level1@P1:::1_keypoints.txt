- Paper frames question asking as program synthesis via a probabilistic model that treats questions as executable programs.
- Approach is distinct from prior methods (canned questions, large image-question datasets) and can generate novel, contextually useful human-like questions.
- Evaluated on human question data from an information-search game; model predicts human question frequencies and synthesizes novel human-like questions not in training.
- Strength: novel and well-motivated modeling of human question asking.
- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation of the model and components.
- Strength: strong empirical results demonstrating prediction of human question behavior.
- Strength: thorough discussion of limitations and future work.
- Weakness: relies on a specific grammar and feature set that may not generalize to other domains or tasks.
- Weakness: unclear description of how model parameters are learned or optimized.
- Weakness: limited comparison to existing methods and insufficient discussion of implications for understanding human question asking.
- Overall: significant contribution to AI and cognitive science with strong empirical support.
- Quality: 8/10 — technically sound but needs clearer parameter-learning details.
- Clarity: 9/10 — well-written though some technical details may be hard for non-experts.
- Originality: 9/10 — presents a novel approach.
- Significance: 9/10 — potential to advance understanding of human cognition.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, strong empirical results, and thoughtful limitations discussion.
- Arguments against acceptance: potential lack of generalizability, unclear parameter learning, and inadequate comparisons/implications discussion.
