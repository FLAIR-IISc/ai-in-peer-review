This paper proposes a probabilistic, compositional account of human question asking by representing questions as executable programs over possible world states, and learning a log-linear distribution over such programs that trades off informativeness (expected information gain, EIG), complexity (via a PCFG-derived prior), answer type, and relevance. Evaluated on a Battleship-style information-search game with 605 human questions across 18 contexts, the model predicts the distribution of human questions in held-out contexts and can synthesize novel, contextually appropriate questions. Ablations indicate both informativeness and complexity are important; the full model achieves strong log-likelihoods relative to information-, complexity-, and type-agnostic variants and Spearman correlations around 0.64 between model energy and human frequencies. This work connects to active learning and Bayesian experimental design, linguistic theories of questions as partitions, probabilistic languages of thought, and program-based QA/semantic parsing; it complements NeurIPS threads on program induction and compositional models for reasoning.

Quality: The technical formulation is sound and carefully implemented, with importance sampling used to approximate the intractable partition function and expectations over programs. The ablations and leave-one-context-out evaluation are appropriate. Two caveats: (i) the reported rank correlation of the full model is comparable to or slightly below the information-agnostic variant, suggesting EIG alone does not explain frequency; and (ii) removal of some contexts and the “draw” operator reduces coverage and may bias estimates. More diagnostics on sampler bias, sensitivity to the grammar prior, and participants’ priors would strengthen claims.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized, with clear motivation and intuitive examples. Some essential grammar details and computational budgets relegated to the supplement could be summarized in the main text.

Originality: Modeling questions as programs has appeared in QA, but learning a distribution over question-programs that balances EIG and complexity to predict human question asking is a novel and timely contribution bridging cognitive modeling and active learning.

Significance: The approach is a compelling step toward agents that ask rich, goal-directed questions, with potential impact on dialog systems and interactive learning. Current scope is limited to a single domain and semantic representations rather than natural language, but the framework is generalizable.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation; strong ablations and predictive results
- Integrates compositional program representations with Bayesian utility
- Generates novel, human-like questions

Cons against acceptance:
- Domain- and grammar-specific engineering; limited generalization evidence
- EIG’s incremental contribution to frequency prediction is mixed
- Approximation choices (importance proposal, context exclusions) need deeper analysis
