### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a computational model for goal-directed, open-ended question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is to represent questions as programs in a compositional language (LISP-like lambda calculus). These programs, when executed on a world state, produce an answer. The authors define a context-free grammar that generates a space of possible question-programs.

To model which questions humans are likely to ask in a given context, the paper introduces a probabilistic generative model over this program space. The probability of a question is defined by a log-linear model, where the energy of a program is a weighted sum of several features. These features include: 1) Informativeness, quantified by the Expected Information Gain (EIG) over a distribution of possible world states, which captures the goal-directed nature of inquiry; 2) Complexity, a penalty for longer or more complex programs, derived from the program's probability under a simple probabilistic context-free grammar; 3) Answer Type, features that capture base-rate preferences for different types of answers (e.g., Boolean, Number); and 4) Relevance, a filter for programs that are semantically related to the task domain.

The model is evaluated on a dataset of natural language questions collected from human participants playing a "Battleship"-like game. Participants were shown a partially revealed game board and asked to formulate a question to help them identify the hidden configuration of ships. The proposed model is tasked with predicting the distribution of human questions in held-out game contexts. The work relates to prior research in active learning, but generalizes the query space from simple label requests to a rich, compositional set of questions. It also differs from contemporary deep learning approaches to question generation by being more data-efficient, compositional, and explicitly goal-directed, aligning it with "language of thought" models in cognitive science.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic framework is well-defined and appropriate for the task. The choice of a log-linear model to combine multiple, well-motivated features (informativeness, complexity, etc.) is a standard and principled approach. The use of importance sampling to approximate the intractable normalizing constant during optimization is a valid technique. The ablation study, which compares the full model to variants with key features removed, provides strong support for the authors' claims about the importance of balancing informativeness and complexity. The experimental design, using leave-one-context-out cross-validation, is methodologically sound for evaluating the model's predictive power on novel scenarios.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, and the core concept of "questions as programs" is explained intuitively before the technical details are presented. The different components of the model—the grammar, the probabilistic framework, and the feature set—are described systematically and with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the implementation. Figures are used effectively to illustrate the game task, present quantitative results (correlations), and showcase the model's qualitative, generative capabilities.

**Originality:** The primary strength of this work lies in its originality. The framing of question asking as program generation is a highly novel approach that elegantly captures the compositional, computable, and goal-directed nature of human inquiry. This represents a significant conceptual advance over existing methods in active learning, which are typically restricted to simple queries, and data-driven neural models for question generation, which often lack explicit representations of semantics, goals, or compositionality. While the use of program induction has appeared in other cognitive science domains (e.g., concept learning), its application to generating open-ended questions is new and insightful. The work effectively synthesizes ideas from active learning, program synthesis, and cognitive science into a coherent and powerful new framework.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is significant for both artificial intelligence and computational cognitive science. For AI, it provides a concrete step towards building agents that can learn more efficiently and naturally by asking rich, creative questions. This has potential applications in areas like dialogue systems, interactive machine learning, and scientific discovery. For cognitive science, it offers the first predictive, computational model of how humans generate open-ended questions, providing quantitative evidence for the cognitive trade-off between maximizing information gain and minimizing complexity. The qualitative results, where the model generates novel and clever questions not seen in the training data, compellingly demonstrate the generative power of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Domain Specificity and Scalability:** A significant limitation is the reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar. The grammar for the Battleship game encodes substantial prior knowledge about the objects (ships), properties (size, color, orientation), and relations (touching, distance) in the domain. While the authors argue that some components are domain-general (e.g., logical operators, set functions), engineering such a grammar for a new, more complex domain would be a non-trivial undertaking. This raises questions about the scalability and generalizability of the approach beyond well-structured, toy-like worlds.

2.  **Lack of Natural Language Interface:** The model operates entirely on the level of semantic representations (programs), bypassing the challenges of natural language understanding and generation. It assumes that human questions can be losslessly translated into the formal language and does not model how a program would be verbalized as a natural language question. While the authors suggest this could be coupled with existing semantic parsers, this is a non-trivial gap that limits its immediate application as an end-to-end question-asking system.

3.  **Evaluation Dataset:** The model is evaluated on a single, relatively constrained task with a moderately sized dataset (605 questions). While the task is well-suited for demonstrating the core ideas, the model's performance on more diverse or complex domains remains an open question. Furthermore, the bootstrap analysis shows the full model's log-likelihood advantage over the information-agnostic model is significant in only 81% of samples, suggesting the evidence for the contribution of the EIG feature, while positive, is not overwhelmingly strong compared to the evidence for the complexity feature.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and conceptually elegant framework for question asking as program generation.
*   The approach is technically sound, combining a principled probabilistic model with well-motivated features.
*   Strong empirical results, including a thorough ablation study, demonstrate the model's ability to predict human question-asking behavior.
*   The model exhibits impressive generative capabilities, creating novel, contextually relevant, and human-like questions.
*   High potential for impact on both AI (active learning, dialogue) and cognitive science.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   The reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar limits scalability and generalizability.
*   The model does not address the natural language-to-program interface, which is a critical component for a complete system.
*   Evaluation is confined to a single, relatively simple domain.
