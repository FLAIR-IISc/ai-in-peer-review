This paper presents a novel computational model of goal-directed human question asking, framing the problem as one of probabilistic program generation. The core idea is to represent questions as formal programs within a compositional "language of thought" which, when executed on a world state, produce an answer. The model defines a probability distribution over this space of programs, favoring those that are both informative (maximizing Expected Information Gain) and concise. This approach extends prior work in active learning, which typically handles simple label queries, by enabling a far richer, more structured space of inquiry. It also connects to work on program induction in cognitive science (e.g., Lake et al., 2015) by applying these ideas to the generative problem of question asking, rather than concept learning or question answering.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality & Significance:** The central contribution—modeling question asking as probabilistic program synthesis—is highly original and significant. It offers a principled, interpretable framework for a complex cognitive capacity that has been difficult to capture with existing AI paradigms. The work represents a substantial step toward more human-like active learning agents and could inspire new research at the intersection of machine learning, cognitive science, and NLP.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The probabilistic model is well-defined, and the claims are strongly supported by a thorough experimental evaluation on a human dataset. The inclusion of model ablations compellingly demonstrates that both informativeness and complexity are essential components for predicting human behavior. The qualitative results, showcasing the model's ability to generate creative and contextually relevant new questions, are particularly impressive.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly motivate the problem, explain their complex model with precision, and present their results effectively, making the work accessible despite its technical depth.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Generalizability:** The primary limitation is the reliance on a hand-crafted, domain-specific grammar for the "Battleship" game. While the authors acknowledge this, it raises valid questions about the framework's scalability and the engineering effort required to apply it to new, more complex domains.
*   **Language Grounding:** The model operates on semantic program representations, bypassing the difficult challenge of parsing natural language into programs. This makes it more of a cognitive model than a ready-to-deploy AI system, though it provides a crucial foundation for one.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a powerful and elegant new framework for a fundamental problem in AI.
*   The work is technically solid, rigorously evaluated, and opens many avenues for future research.
*   It is likely to be highly influential and of broad interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The reliance on a domain-specific grammar may limit its perceived immediate impact.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This is a high-quality, thought-provoking paper that makes a significant and original contribution.
