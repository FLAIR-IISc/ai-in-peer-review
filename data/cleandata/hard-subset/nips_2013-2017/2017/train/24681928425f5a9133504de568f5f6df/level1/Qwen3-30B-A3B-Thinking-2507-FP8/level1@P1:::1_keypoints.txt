- Proposes modeling human questions as executable programs and a probabilistic generator balancing Expected Information Gain, complexity, answer type, and relevance; evaluated on 605 Battleship-game questions.  
- Technically sound with rigorous evaluation (leave-one-out CV, log-likelihood comparisons) and demonstrated ability to generate novel human-like questions.  
- Exceptionally clear and well-organized writing, with detailed grammar and supplementary materials enabling reproducibility and useful illustrative figures.  
- Highly original reframing of question asking as program synthesis that connects to cognitive science (language of thought, compositional semantics) and differs from neural QG approaches.  
- Significant contribution addressing an underexplored cognitive ability with potential applications in human-AI interaction, education, and cognitive modeling.  
- Major weakness: grammar and implementation are heavily domain-specific (Battleship), raising questions about scalability and adaptation to new domains.  
- Major weakness: model outputs semantic representations rather than natural language; paper insufficiently addresses bridging to practical NLG systems.  
- Major weakness: potential for producing questions too complex for human comprehension is not empirically addressed despite complexity being modeled.  
- For acceptance: originality, rigorous evaluation, strong cognitive grounding, and clear writing argue strongly in favor.  
- Against acceptance: domain-specific grammar, semantic-to-language gap, lack of cognitive-limit analysis, and evaluation confined to a single domain temper enthusiasm.  
- Overall: significant, well-executed, and influential contribution to computational cognitive science and AI; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
