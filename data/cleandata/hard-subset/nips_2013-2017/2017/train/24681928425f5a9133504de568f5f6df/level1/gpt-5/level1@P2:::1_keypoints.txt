- The paper models human questions as typed lambda/LISP-like programs and uses a PCFG plus a log-linear model (EIG, complexity, type, relevance) to predict question distributions.  
- Parameters are fit by maximizing likelihood on 605 human questions from a Battleship-like game, using importance sampling to approximate partition functions.  
- The probabilistic formulation is coherent and aligns with an informativeness–cognitive-cost trade-off; EIG and complexity features are appropriate for the domain.  
- Importance sampling with the PCFG proposal is reasonable but the dual use may introduce coupling/bias that needs discussion.  
- Leave-one-context-out evaluation shows the full model has highest approximate held-out log-likelihood; ablations (no EIG, no complexity, no type) perform worse.  
- Spearman correlations between model energy and human frequencies average 0.64 for the full model; surprisingly the information-agnostic model achieves ~0.65.  
- The model generates novel, plausible human-like questions and handles equivalence classes by answer partitions.  
- Heavy reliance on hand-engineered grammar and features limits generality and scalability.  
- Computational constraints required dropping contexts and pruning operators, reducing completeness and raising scalability concerns.  
- The work assumes ground-truth semantic forms (no NL→program mapping), so it is not end-to-end.  
- Training and test contexts come from a single domain with shared priors, limiting claims about generalization.  
- The ideal-observer EIG assumption and exact world simulation may not scale or reflect human bounded rationality.  
- The paper is clearly written and reproducible in principle, though some citations are incomplete and supplementary grammar/code access is needed.  
- The approach is original in integrating program-based question representations with EIG/complexity trade-offs to predict human questions.  
- Significance is conceptual and methodological for cognitive modeling and goal-directed querying, but immediate scalability is limited.  
- Strengths: principled executable-program formalization, compositional grammar enabling novel questions, and thoughtful ablations.  
- Weaknesses: narrow, engineered domain; modest apparent contribution of EIG to predictive accuracy; heavy approximations; no comparisons to simple or neural baselines; not end-to-end.  
- Suggestions: report absolute/relative log-likelihoods and learned weights, add PCFG-only and EIG-only baselines, analyze failure cases, test generalization (e.g., different grid/prior), and add a simple neural program generator or NL→program parser.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept — novel and well-motivated with solid empirical signals, but limited scope and scalability warrant caution.
