

# Review of "Question Asking as Program Generation"

This paper proposes a novel computational framework for modeling human question asking by treating questions as programs that, when executed on a world state, produce answers. The authors develop a probabilistic model that samples from a compositional space of programs, favoring concise and informative questions relevant to the current context. The model is evaluated on a dataset of 605 natural language questions asked by humans playing a Battleship-style game, where players sought to identify ship configurations in an ambiguous board state.

The work builds on several strands of previous research. It extends active learning literature (e.g., Settles' work on active learning) by moving beyond simple label queries to model rich, open-ended questions. The approach connects to computational linguistics (e.g., compositional semantics, language of thought), cognitive science (e.g., Fodor's work on the language of thought), and program induction (e.g., Lake et al.'s work on probabilistic program induction). Unlike neural approaches to question generation (e.g., Mostafazadeh et al.'s VQG dataset work), this model requires no large datasets of paired questions and images, and unlike goal-directed dialog systems (e.g., Serban et al.), it produces genuinely creative questions rather than canned responses.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The paper presents a technically sound model with a solid theoretical foundation. The authors clearly articulate the energy-based probabilistic model (Equations 1-2), carefully define question features (informativeness via EIG, complexity, answer type), and provide thorough evaluation through model variants (lesioning key components). The log-likelihood comparisons across models are statistically rigorous (with bootstrapping), and the qualitative generation of novel questions demonstrates the model's creative capabilities. The authors are honest about limitations (domain-specific grammar, need for some domain knowledge).

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The key insights (compositionality, computability) are clearly explained with concrete examples. The grammar description (Section 4.2) and program examples make the technical approach accessible. The evaluation setup (Battleship game, context-dependent question generation) is well-motivated and clearly described. Supplementary materials appear to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

*Originality:* This work makes a significant original contribution by modeling questions as executable programs rather than natural language strings. The paper clearly differentiates from related work: it's not just a neural question generator (which requires large datasets), nor a canned question system (which lacks creativity), and it provides a computational account of how humans balance informativeness and complexity in question asking. The connection between program synthesis and human question asking is novel and well-justified.

*Significance:* The approach represents a meaningful step toward more human-like AI systems that can learn by asking questions. The model's ability to generate novel, contextually appropriate questions (Figure 3) demonstrates its potential for creative question asking beyond simple pattern matching. This work advances the state of the art in modeling human-like question asking, which has broader implications for human-computer interaction and AI learning systems. The paper clearly articulates how it advances the field beyond previous work.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary limitation is the domain-specific grammar, which requires substantial knowledge engineering for each new domain. While the authors note that some elements (logical connectives, set operations) are domain general, the extent to which this approach can be generalized without significant re-engineering remains unclear. The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how much domain knowledge would be required for other applications.

Another minor weakness is the limited scope of the evaluation - the model was tested only on the Battleship game. While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation, the paper doesn't discuss how the model would scale to more complex domains with richer semantics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

- Novel computational framework that models questions as programs
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear probabilistic model
- Rigorous evaluation with both quantitative (log-likelihood) and qualitative (novel question generation) results
- Clear demonstration of the importance of both informativeness and complexity
- Well-written, clear, and logically organized
- Significant contribution to active learning and computational cognitive science

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

- Domain-specific grammar requires substantial knowledge engineering
- Evaluation limited to a single game domain
- Limited discussion of how this approach might generalize to other domains

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of active learning and computational cognitive science. The modeling approach is novel, technically sound, and well-evaluated. While the domain-specific nature of the grammar is a limitation, the authors appropriately acknowledge this and provide a clear path for future work. The paper advances the state of the art in modeling human-like question asking by providing the first predictive model for open-ended question generation that accounts for both informativeness and complexity.

The paper meets all NIPS reviewing criteria exceptionally well. It is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), makes a genuine contribution beyond previous work (Originality), and addresses an important problem with significant potential impact (Significance). The authors have carefully situated their work in the literature and provided thorough evaluation that demonstrates the model's advantages over alternatives.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This work represents a significant step toward more human-like AI systems that can learn by asking rich, creative questions, and it provides a compelling new framework for understanding human question asking from a computational perspective.
