

This paper addresses the challenge of extreme multi-class classification with very large numbers of classes (10,000-100,000), proposing an aggressive double sampling strategy for a multi-class to binary reduction approach. The authors build upon Joshi et al. (2015) by introducing two sampling mechanisms: first, per-class sampling to rebalance long-tailed class distributions (oversampling small classes, subsampling large classes), and second, random sampling of comparison classes to reduce the size of the transformed binary dataset. The theoretical contribution lies in deriving generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity that account for both the sampling-induced distribution shift and the inter-dependency between dyadic examples.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets, showing significant improvements in training time, prediction time, and memory consumption compared to state-of-the-art approaches (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse), while maintaining competitive predictive performance measured by accuracy and macro F1. The authors show that their sampling strategy effectively addresses the two main limitations of existing approaches: computational intractability with large K and class imbalance issues.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical analysis extends previous work by Joshi et al. (2015) with new generalization bounds that account for the sampling procedures. The experimental design is thorough, with careful comparisons across multiple datasets and metrics, and the implementation details are well-specified. The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach, particularly noting that the sampling strategy shifts the class distribution, which they address through their theoretical analysis.

Clarity is generally good, with clear explanations of the double sampling procedure and the theoretical framework. The algorithm descriptions are precise, and the experimental setup is well-documented. However, the prediction algorithm in Section 2.3 (candidate selection using class centroids) lacks theoretical justification, which limits the ability to compare the sampling strategy with other approaches that might use different prediction mechanisms. The paper could benefit from more detailed discussion of how the sampling affects the prediction algorithm's performance.

Originality is moderate. While the core reduction strategy follows Joshi et al. (2015), the aggressive sampling technique represents a meaningful extension that addresses a significant practical challenge in extreme classification. The paper does a good job distinguishing its approach from tree-based methods (which suffer from error propagation) and label embedding approaches (which rely on problematic low-rank assumptions). However, the paper could better articulate how the sampling strategy differs from other recent approaches to class imbalance in multi-class problems.

Significance is high. Extreme classification is a growing problem in text classification and other domains with massive label spaces. The proposed method provides a practical solution that significantly reduces computational requirements without sacrificing predictive performance, making it highly relevant for real-world applications. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over strong baselines, particularly for very large K, where standard approaches become computationally intractable.

Key strengths: The paper provides a theoretically grounded solution to a practical problem in extreme classification, with strong empirical validation. The double sampling strategy effectively addresses long-tailed class distributions, a common challenge in large-scale multi-class problems. The generalization bounds are well-motivated and extend the theoretical foundation of the field.

Key weaknesses: The theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm (Section 2.3) is incomplete, as the authors do not analyze how sampling affects the candidate selection process. The paper lacks a comparison of sample complexity and generalization bounds to standard multi-class formulations, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. The novelty of the approach is somewhat limited to the sampling strategy, with the reduction framework building directly on Joshi et al. (2015).

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of extreme classification, with practical implications for large-scale text classification problems. While the theoretical novelty is somewhat limited, the practical improvements in computational efficiency and performance on large label spaces are significant. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, particularly for scenarios with extremely large numbers of classes where existing methods become computationally prohibitive.

Pro: Strong empirical results demonstrating practical advantages for large-scale problems; Theoretical justification for sampling strategy using fractional Rademacher complexity; Addresses a significant practical challenge (long-tailed class distributions) in extreme classification.

Con: Limited theoretical novelty beyond sampling strategy; Missing analysis of prediction algorithm; Incomplete comparison of generalization bounds to standard multi-class approaches; Core reduction framework largely follows Joshi et al. (2015).

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that addresses an important problem with practical relevance. The authors provide a theoretically sound approach with strong empirical validation, making it a valuable contribution to the field. The paper would benefit from more thorough theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm and a clearer articulation of how the sampling strategy differs from previous work addressing class imbalance. Despite these limitations, the work represents a significant step forward in making extreme classification feasible for very large label spaces.
