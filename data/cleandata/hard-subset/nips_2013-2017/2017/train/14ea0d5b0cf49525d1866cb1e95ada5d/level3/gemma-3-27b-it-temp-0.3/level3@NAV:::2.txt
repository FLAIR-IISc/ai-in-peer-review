## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of large-scale multi-class classification, specifically focusing on the computational and statistical hurdles arising from an extremely large number of classes (K). The authors propose a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction, aiming to balance class distributions and reduce the size of the transformed dataset. They provide theoretical analysis, extending Joshi et al. (2015) with improved generalization bounds based on fractional Rademacher complexity, and demonstrate empirical competitiveness on text classification datasets with up to 100,000 classes.

The core idea of reducing the multi-class problem to binary via pairwise comparisons is well-established (following Joshi et al., 2015), but the novelty lies in the aggressive sampling procedure. This sampling, consisting of per-class sampling to address imbalance and random sampling to reduce dataset size, is a significant contribution. The theoretical justification for maintaining generalization guarantees despite this sampling is a key strength. The paper clearly articulates the challenges posed by correlated transformed datasets and provides a rigorous analysis to address them. The empirical results on DMOZ and Wikipedia collections are convincing, demonstrating both the efficiency (in terms of time and memory) and the effectiveness of the proposed method compared to several strong baselines. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The prediction algorithm described in Section 2.3, which limits comparisons to the k nearest class centroids, lacks a corresponding theoretical analysis. This omission hinders a complete understanding of the method’s performance characteristics. Furthermore, a crucial comparison is missing: a discussion of the sample complexity and generalization bounds achieved by this transformed binary classification approach *relative* to those achievable by non-transformed multi-class formulations. This would provide a clearer picture of the overall benefits of the proposed reduction strategy. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the feature mapping (φ) being application-dependent, a more in-depth discussion of alternative mappings or their impact on performance would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practically important problem (extreme multi-class classification).
*   Novel sampling strategy with strong theoretical justification.
*   Empirically competitive performance with significant gains in efficiency.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of theoretical analysis for the k-nearest centroid prediction.
*   Missing comparison of generalization bounds to non-transformed formulations.
*   Limited discussion of alternative feature mappings.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution with a clear focus and strong technical foundation. While the missing analysis and comparison are notable weaknesses, the novelty of the sampling strategy and the positive empirical results make this paper worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the identified weaknesses.
