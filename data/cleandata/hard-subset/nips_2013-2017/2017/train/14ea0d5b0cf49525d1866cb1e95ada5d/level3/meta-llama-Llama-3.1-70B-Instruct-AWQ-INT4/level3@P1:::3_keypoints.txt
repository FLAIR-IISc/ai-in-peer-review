- Proposes a novel extreme multi-class method: multi-class-to-binary reduction plus a double sampling strategy that forms binary pairs of examples.  
- Designed to handle long-tailed class distributions and reduce the number of pairwise examples in the expanded dataset.  
- Claims tree-based methods suffer cascading errors and label-embedding fails when label matrices are not low-rank; method aims to avoid those pitfalls.  
- Offers theoretical consistency guarantees relative to multi-class ERM.  
- Introduces "Aggressive Double Sampling" with two control parameters: class sampling inversely proportional to empirical frequency and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples.  
- Experiments on five text datasets (including >30,000 classes) show superior accuracy and F1, faster training, and much lower memory usage.  
- Prediction is slower than competing methods; authors propose a centroid-based candidate selection heuristic to speed inference.  
- Theoretical analysis is complicated by dependent dyadic examples and distribution shift from oversampling.  
- Proof approach splits dependent sums by induced graph structure and uses Janson's concentration inequalities for partial dependencies.  
- Finite-sample risk bound includes bias from true vs. oversampled class probability ratios, but bias decreases roughly linearly with sample/resample sizes.  
- Reviewer finds the analysis plausible, the algorithm theoretically sound, and practically useful on large datasets.  
- Strength: scalable approach with theoretical consistency guarantees relative to multi-class ERM.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance (accuracy/F1), faster training, and much lower memory on very large problems.  
- Strength: novel "Aggressive Double Sampling" strategy as a new contribution.  
- Weakness: slower prediction time compared to other methods.  
- Weakness: theoretical analysis is complex and may be hard to follow.  
- Weakness: finite-sample risk bound carries bias due to oversampling class probability shifts.  
- Originality: novel combination of multi-class-to-binary reduction and double sampling, including the aggressive sampling variant.  
- Significance: provides a scalable, low-memory solution for extreme classification with applicability to text, recommendation, and ranking.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, explanations and experiments are clear and easy to follow.  
- Quality: technically sound, thorough analysis, and convincing experimental validation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant contribution, strong empirical results on large-scale datasets, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments for rejection: slower inference, complex theoretical analysis, and bias in finite-sample risk bound.
