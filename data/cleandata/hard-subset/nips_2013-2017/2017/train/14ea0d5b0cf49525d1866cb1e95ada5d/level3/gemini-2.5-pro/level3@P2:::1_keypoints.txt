- Problem: Tackles extreme multi-class classification with very large, long-tailed label spaces.
- Method: Proposes a multi-class-to-binary reduction with an "aggressive double sampling" scheme (resample to balance classes, then sample κ negative classes per example).
- Theoretical: Derives generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity to argue consistency of empirical risk minimization under sampling.
- Empirical evaluation: Extensive experiments on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia) up to 100k classes, showing strong training time, memory, and accuracy results.
- Quality: Technically sound algorithm with strong empirical support and clear experimental comparisons.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and motivated; theoretical results lack intuitive interpretation and comparison to prior bounds.
- Originality: Simple and effective combination of known ideas; incremental extension of prior work [16] rather than a radically new approach.
- Significance: Practically important trade-off between training efficiency, memory, and performance; scales to 100k classes.
- Prediction-time drawback: Prediction is considerably slower than tree-based methods; candidate-selection heuristic’s speed/accuracy trade-off is underexplored.
- Memory-comparison concern: Mixing batch and online methods in memory comparisons may be unfair and needs clarification.
- Theoretical-importance concern: Practical implications and advantages of the new bounds over previous work are not well explained.
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses a hard problem, proposes a simple effective method, shows comprehensive empirical gains, and provides theoretical backing.
- Arguments against acceptance: Incremental novelty, major practical drawback in prediction latency, potentially uneven experimental comparisons, and unclear practical value of theory.
- Overall verdict: Valuable and practically useful contribution with strong empirical results, tempered by limited novelty and prediction-time limitations.
