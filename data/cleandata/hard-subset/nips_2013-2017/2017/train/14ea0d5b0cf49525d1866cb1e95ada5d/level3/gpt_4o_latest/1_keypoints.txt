- Introduces an aggressive double sampling strategy for multi-class-to-binary reduction targeting extreme classification with many classes and long-tailed distributions.  
- Significance: Tackles a critical, real-world problem and scales to datasets with up to ~100,000 classes.  
- Empirical validation: Comprehensive experiments across datasets show competitive accuracy, macro F1, and resource efficiency versus baselines.  
- Theoretical contributions: Provides generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexities, adding theoretical rigor.  
- Efficiency: Double sampling significantly reduces training time and memory, making it viable where OVA and M-SVM are infeasible.  
- Limited novelty: Builds heavily on prior work with mainly incremental contributions (double sampling + analysis).  
- Prediction time: Slower inference than alternatives, limiting use in latency-sensitive settings; candidate selection only partially mitigates this.  
- Clarity of comparisons: Interpretation and comparison of the proposed generalization bounds with existing bounds are unclear and need more explicit discussion and numerical insight.  
- Experimental design: Mixing batch and online algorithms yields differing memory usage and complicates direct comparisons; a more uniform setup is needed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses an important problem, shows strong large-scale empirical results, and includes sound theoretical analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited novelty and practical issues (longer prediction time, unclear theoretical comparisons) weaken the contribution.  
- Recommendation: Overall a meaningful contribution warranting acceptance if clarity issues and prediction-time trade-offs are addressed.
