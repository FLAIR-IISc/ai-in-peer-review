- Paper proposes reducing extreme multiclass (10K–100K) to binary dyadic (example,class) pairs with a (π,κ) double sampling: oversample rare classes and subsample κ negative “adversaries” per example.  
- Theoretical contribution: analysis of dependencies and sampling-induced distribution shift using graph coverings, fractional/local Rademacher complexity, and Janson’s concentration, yielding generalization bounds with α,β terms and ERM consistency.  
- Practical contribution: candidate selection at test time via nearest-centroid classes to accelerate prediction.  
- Positions against tree-based (cascading errors), label-embedding (low-rank failure), and full dyadic-pair methods (computationally prohibitive); builds on prior dyadic reductions and dependent-data generalization.  
- Strength: conceptually simple, directly addresses class imbalance and pair explosion, and is practically effective.  
- Strength: theoretical analysis explicitly handles dyadic dependencies and sampling bias, with finite-sample rates improving with M and κ.  
- Strength: strong empirical results on large text datasets (up to 100K classes) with competitive/superior accuracy/MaF1, lower memory, and faster training than several baselines; code released.  
- Strength: clear practical engineering (simple text φ, candidate selection) and reproducible experiments.  
- Weakness: generality beyond text is under-explored; φ uses 10 hand-crafted BOW features, unclear portability to other modalities without learned embeddings.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of candidate selection — no theory or empirical recall vs q, or sensitivity analysis for q, critical for very large K.  
- Weakness: multi-label datasets converted to multi-class by duplicating instances, which may change distributions and bias comparisons; needs discussion/controls.  
- Weakness: thin hyperparameter guidance — no ablation isolating effects of class rebalancing vs negative subsampling vs candidate selection, and no rules for κ and π.  
- Weakness: bounds given for 0/1 loss without explicit link to surrogate losses actually optimized; practical implications of α,β not illustrated.  
- Quality: methods and analysis are technically sound and appropriate; experiments are thorough on large text datasets with strong baselines.  
- Clarity: generally well written and organized, but notation-heavy; needs more intuition for α,β and a summary of bound dependence on (M,κ,K) and practical rules for κ and q.  
- Originality: novel pragmatic double-sampling refinement and an original combination of analytical tools for dependent dyadic data.  
- Significance: impactful for extreme classification due to memory and training savings at 50K–100K classes, though breadth limited by lack of non-text evidence and candidate-selection analysis.  
- Empirical suggestions: report sensitivity to κ and π, ablate rebalancing vs negative subsampling, provide candidate-selection recall vs q and accuracy/runtime trade-offs, and show per-dataset complexity and memory breakdowns.  
- Empirical suggestion: include a learned joint-embedding φ (e.g., WSABIE-style) baseline to assess portability beyond hand-crafted features.  
- Questions for authors: how are κ and π set in practice; any adaptive/data-driven rules?  
- Question: does oversampling cause overfitting of rare classes and is any class-prior correction applied?  
- Question: what is true-class recall in candidate sets as a function of q and how often does pruning cause errors?  
- Question: can the analysis be extended to the surrogate losses actually optimized (hinge/logistic)?  
- Question: have you tested learned φ (e.g., linear models over x and class features) to assess generality?  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses important problem with simple scalable method, nontrivial matching theory for dependent dyadic data, strong empirical gains, and reproducibility.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on task-specific φ, lack of candidate-selection guarantees and characterization, missing ablations and hyperparameter guidance, and potential confound from multi-label-to-multi-class conversion.  
- Overall recommendation: generally positive and leans toward acceptance, conditional on clarifying candidate selection, adding ablations, and discussing φ generality.
