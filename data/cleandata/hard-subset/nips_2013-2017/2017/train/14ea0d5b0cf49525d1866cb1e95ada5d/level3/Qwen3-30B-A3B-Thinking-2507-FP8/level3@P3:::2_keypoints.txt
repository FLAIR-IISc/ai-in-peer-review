- Addresses extreme multi-class text classification with very large K (10k–100k), building on Joshi et al. (2015) multi-class→binary reduction.  
- Proposes a double sampling ((π, κ)-DS): per-class rebalancing (inverse-frequency sampling) and sampling κ comparison classes per example to reduce dyadic pairs.  
- Provides theoretical generalization guarantees (Theorem 2) using fractional Rademacher complexity that account for sampling and dependencies in transformed data.  
- Shows empirical competitiveness on DMOZ and Wikipedia (up to 100k classes), improving training time, prediction time, and memory while maintaining accuracy and macro F1.  
- Strength: Practical, well-motivated solution addressing class imbalance and computational bottlenecks in extreme classification.  
- Strength: Sound theoretical analysis extending prior work to incorporate second-order information and sampling effects.  
- Strength: Comprehensive experimental setup and convincing empirical gains over OVA, M-SVM, and other baselines.  
- Weakness: Core reduction framework largely follows Joshi et al. (2015); main novelty is the sampling strategy.  
- Weakness: No theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm (k nearest class centroids), so unclear if training guarantees extend to prediction.  
- Weakness: Lacks comparison of sample complexity and generalization bounds to non-transformed multi-class formulations.  
- Clarity: Generally well-written; theoretical section could use more intuition on fractional Rademacher arguments.  
- Originality: Double sampling is novel and practically useful, though reduction itself is not new.  
- Significance: Addresses an important practical problem with meaningful computational efficiency gains for very large K.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to provide theoretical justification for the prediction algorithm and comparisons to non-transformed multi-class sample complexity.
