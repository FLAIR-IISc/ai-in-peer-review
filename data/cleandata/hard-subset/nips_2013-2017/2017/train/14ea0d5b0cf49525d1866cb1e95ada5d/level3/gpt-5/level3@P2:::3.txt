Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses extreme multiclass classification (10K–100K classes) by reducing the problem to a binary classification task over dyadic (example, class) pairs. The core contribution is an aggressive double sampling scheme that (i) rebalances long-tailed class distributions by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones, and (ii) limits the number of negative classes per example to κ uniformly sampled “adversaries.” The authors analyze the resulting dependence structure among dyadic examples and the distribution shift introduced by sampling. Using graph coverings, fractional/local Rademacher complexity, and concentration for partially dependent variables (Janson), they prove generalization bounds and consistency of ERM under this reduction. At test time, they accelerate prediction via candidate selection: a small set of nearest-centroid classes per test point is scored.

The paper positions itself against: (a) tree-based methods (e.g., RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML), which achieve log-time inference but suffer cascading errors; (b) label-embedding methods (e.g., LEML, SLEEC), which can be unstable when low-rank assumptions fail in extreme regimes; and (c) dyadic reductions with full pair construction, which are computationally prohibitive. The work builds on dyadic reductions and dependent-data generalization (e.g., Usunier & Amini; Ralaivola & Amini) and complements recent extreme classification systems (e.g., PD-Sparse).

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and practically effective reduction that directly targets two core bottlenecks: class imbalance and pair explosion.
- Theoretical analysis acknowledges and handles dependencies induced by dyadic expansion and the distribution shift from sampling; bounds incorporate α,β terms that capture the sampling-induced bias and show consistency of ERM with finite-sample rates improving with M and κ.
- Strong empirical results on LSHTC/DMOZ/Wikipedia with up to 100K classes: competitive or superior accuracy/MaF1 when K is large, markedly lower memory, and faster training than OVA/M-SVM and PD-Sparse; competitive overall time vs many tree-based methods.
- Clear practical engineering: simple φ mapping for text, candidate selection for inference, and public code.

Weaknesses
- Generality of the approach beyond text is under-explored; the feature map φ uses 10 hand-crafted ranking-inspired features tailored to bag-of-words. It is unclear how performance translates to other modalities without domain-specific φ or learned joint embeddings.
- Limited analysis of prediction-time candidate selection: no theoretical or empirical study of recall of the true class in candidate sets, or sensitivity to the number of candidates q; this step is critical for scalability when K is very large.
- Empirical setup converts multi-label datasets to multi-class by duplicating instances; this can change the effective distribution and may favor some methods. A discussion of potential biases and controls (e.g., per-class stratification) would help.
- Hyperparameter guidance is thin: how to set κ and π across datasets? There is no ablation isolating the impact of class rebalancing vs negative subsampling vs candidate selection.
- The bounds are presented for 0/1 loss; the link to the trained surrogate (e.g., hinge/logistic) is not explicitly stated, and practical implications of α,β are not illustrated (e.g., bound behavior vs class imbalance).

Quality
The methods are technically sound and the analysis is plausible. The handling of dependence via fractional/local Rademacher complexity and exact proper fractional covers is appropriate. I did not fully verify the proofs, but the approach aligns with prior art. Experiments are thorough on large text datasets with strong baselines (OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML). Reporting includes accuracy, macro-F1, time, and memory; code and hyperparameters are provided (supplement).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The dyadic reduction and the (π,κ)-DS algorithm are clearly stated. Some sections are notation heavy; it would help to give more intuition for α and β, to summarize the bound’s dependence on (M,κ,K), and to provide practical rules of thumb for choosing κ and q. A brief complexity analysis (train/predict) in big-O with constants tied to φ would aid readers.

Originality
The double sampling scheme is a novel and pragmatic refinement of dyadic reduction tailored to extreme classification. The generalization analysis that combines fractional/local Rademacher tools with sampling-induced distribution shift appears original and useful. While each ingredient has antecedents, their combination here is nontrivial.

Significance
Given the growth of extreme classification, a reduction that brings memory savings and fast training while preserving accuracy at 50K–100K classes is impactful. The approach is likely to be adopted or extended, particularly with learned φ in other domains. The lack of analysis for candidate selection and limited non-text evidence somewhat limit breadth, but the contribution remains significant.

Empirical evaluation comments
- Please report sensitivity to κ and π (e.g., κ ∈ {1,5,10,50}) and ablate class rebalancing vs negative subsampling.
- Provide candidate selection recall of the true label vs q, and the accuracy/runtime trade-off curves.
- Include per-dataset training/prediction complexities and memory footprints attributable to φ, dyadic construction, and classifier storage.
- Consider adding a learned joint embedding φ baseline (e.g., WSABIE-style) to illustrate portability beyond hand-crafted features.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you set κ and π in practice? Any adaptive or data-driven rules?  
2) What is the effect of oversampling on overfitting rare classes, and do you apply any class-prior correction at training or prediction?  
3) What is the recall of the true class in candidate sets as a function of q, and how often does candidate pruning cause errors?  
4) Can your analysis be extended to cover the surrogate loss actually optimized?  
5) Have you tested learned φ (e.g., linear models over concatenated x and class features) to assess generality?

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and challenging problem with a simple, scalable method.  
- Nontrivial theory that matches the practical setting of dependent dyadic data and sampling.  
- Strong empirical results at extreme scale with substantial memory and time savings.  
- Reproducible and likely to influence practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on task-specific φ; limited evidence beyond text.  
- Candidate selection lacks theoretical guarantees and detailed empirical characterization.  
- Missing ablations and guidance on key hyperparameters.  
- Use of multi-label-to-multi-class conversion may confound comparisons.

Overall recommendation
A solid and timely contribution to extreme multiclass classification. Despite some missing analyses and limited generality beyond text, the proposed reduction and its theory are compelling, and the empirical gains at large K are persuasive. I lean toward acceptance, conditional on clarifying candidate selection, providing ablations, and discussing generality of φ.
