This paper addresses the problem of extreme multi-class classification, where the number of classes can be very large. The authors propose a double sampling strategy to improve the scalability of a prior multi-class to binary reduction method [16]. This method transforms the problem into a binary classification task over pairs of examples. The proposed sampling has two goals: first, to balance long-tailed class distributions by over-sampling rare classes and under-sampling frequent ones; second, to reduce the number of pairs in the expanded dataset, thereby decreasing computational cost. The paper supports this approach with both a theoretical analysis, providing a new generalization bound using local Rademacher complexity, and an extensive empirical evaluation on large-scale text classification datasets.

The paper has several notable strengths. It tackles a significant and practical problem in machine learning. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, demonstrating that the proposed method scales effectively to problems with up to 100,000 classes. The results show a compelling combination of high predictive performance (in terms of Accuracy and Macro-F1) and substantially reduced training time and memory usage compared to several state-of-the-art baselines, including OVA, M-SVM, and PD-Sparse. The double sampling strategy is well-motivated and appears effective at handling the challenges of extreme classification.

However, the work also has some weaknesses that temper its contribution. The primary concern is its limited novelty, as the paper is largely an incremental improvement upon the method presented in [16]. The main new contributions are the sampling scheme and the associated generalization bound. From a practical standpoint, while the method achieves faster training times, its prediction time remains long compared to tree-based methods like FastXML. In many applications, low-latency inference is more critical than training efficiency, a trade-off that is not sufficiently addressed. Furthermore, the theoretical contribution could be strengthened; the paper does not clearly explain the interpretation or comparative advantage of its generalization bound over existing ones. Finally, the experimental comparison of memory usage may be misleading, as it mixes batch methods with online baselines, which have fundamentally different memory consumption profiles, making a direct comparison of total memory usage potentially uneven.

In summary, the paper presents a well-executed empirical study of a practical technique for extreme classification. While the novelty is modest and some practical and methodological concerns remain, the strong performance and efficiency gains are significant.
