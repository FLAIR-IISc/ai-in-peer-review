

This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification with very large numbers of classes (up to 100,000), which is common in text classification tasks like Wikipedia categorization. The authors propose a novel reduction approach that transforms the multi-class problem into a binary classification problem through a dyadic representation, while introducing an aggressive double sampling strategy to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions. The approach first samples the original training set to balance class representation (oversampling small classes and subsampling large classes), then constructs dyadic examples from the sampled set with a parameter controlling the number of adversarial classes per example.

The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing limitations of two dominant approaches in extreme classification: tree-based methods (which suffer from error propagation due to cascading decisions) and label embedding methods (which fail when the label matrix isn't low-rank). The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing that their sampling strategy preserves the consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle despite the induced dependencies between dyadic examples and the shift in class distribution. Their theoretical analysis, while complex, leverages graph covering and fractional Rademacher complexity to derive generalization bounds that account for the dependent nature of the dyadic examples.

The experimental evaluation on DMOZ and Wikipedia collections with 10,000-100,000 classes demonstrates clear advantages over state-of-the-art methods. The proposed approach (π, κ)-DS achieves superior predictive performance (accuracy and macro F1) compared to OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse on the largest datasets. Crucially, it dramatically reduces memory consumption (avoiding terabyte-scale requirements of OVA/M-SVM on 100k-class problems) and training time while maintaining competitive prediction speed through a candidate selection strategy using class centroids. The results are particularly compelling for problems with >30k classes where the method outperforms all competitors.

The paper excels in quality through its sound theoretical foundation and comprehensive experimental validation. The theoretical analysis, though technically complex, provides a plausible justification for the sampling strategy's consistency, and the experiments are well-designed with appropriate baselines and metrics (including macro F1 to address class imbalance). The clarity of the paper is generally good, with clear problem formulation and algorithm description, though the theoretical section could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the fractional Rademacher complexity analysis. The originality is strong, as the double sampling strategy represents a novel approach to extreme classification that differs substantially from existing work, and the authors properly contextualize their contribution against relevant NIPS papers (e.g., [1, 16, 28] on dyadic representations, [9, 21] on tree-based methods).

The significance of this work is high for the field of extreme classification, which has become increasingly important with the growth of large-scale text repositories. The method provides a practical solution to a major bottleneck in applying machine learning to large-scale text classification tasks, with clear advantages in memory efficiency and scalability. The theoretical guarantees further strengthen the contribution by providing a principled foundation for the empirical success.

Weaknesses include the relatively slower prediction time compared to tree-based methods (though the candidate selection strategy mitigates this), and the sampling strategy's potential specificity to text classification (though the authors note the mapping could be adapted for other domains). The theoretical analysis, while sound, is quite technical and might benefit from additional intuitive explanations to make it more accessible.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical contribution with novel sampling strategy; clear experimental validation demonstrating superior performance on the largest datasets; significant practical impact for extreme classification problems; addresses a real-world challenge with compelling results.

Arguments against acceptance: Theoretical analysis is complex and may be challenging for some readers; prediction time is slower than tree-based methods (though the authors provide a mitigation strategy).

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of extreme classification. The authors successfully bridge theory and practice, providing both a principled sampling strategy with theoretical guarantees and a method that demonstrates clear practical advantages on large-scale text classification problems. The work is well-positioned to influence both theoretical and applied research in machine learning for large-scale classification tasks. The paper meets all four evaluation criteria effectively and represents a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS 2017.
