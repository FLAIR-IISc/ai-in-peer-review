- Problem & approach: Reduces extreme multiclass to binary dyadic pairs using φ(x,y) and scorer f, with ranking loss; addresses dependent pairs via local (variance-sensitive) fractional Rademacher complexity for sharper generalization bounds.  
- Sampling & scalability: Proposes double sampling—per-class π-sampling to rebalance and κ-sampling of comparison classes—to reduce dyadic pairs while bounding excess risk (depends on α, β and scales like 1/√(Mκ)).  
- Prediction speedup: Uses 10 lightweight text features and q‑nearest-centroid candidate selection at prediction to reduce latency.  
- Empirical results: Competitive or superior accuracy/MaF1 on 10k–100k class datasets with much lower memory/runtime than OVA/M‑SVM and competitive speed with tree methods.  
- Theory quality: Extension to local fractional Rademacher complexity is technically sound and addresses dependence; suggest numeric illustration of α,β under inverse-frequency π to quantify claims.  
- Algorithmic concern: Double sampling is clear and effective, but candidate selection lacks theoretical analysis and may cause train–test mismatch; recommend recall@q or approximation bounds.  
- Empirics concern: Evaluation is thorough but should discuss bias from multilabel→multiclass conversion, and include ablations on π, κ and q to isolate contributions to accuracy, memory, and time.  
- Clarity suggestions: Specify learning loss/regularizer/model class for f, define π normalization and α,β intuition, summarize precise improvement over Joshi et al., and restate dependence graph/fractional cover.  
- Originality: Main novelty is aggressive double sampling and local fractional analysis; core reduction and dependency tools largely follow prior work.  
- Significance: Practically relevant with strong empirical gains and useful theoretical tools for dependent reductions, but missing candidate-selection analysis and comparison to non-transformed multiclass bounds limits broader impact.  
- Pros: New local fractional bounds; simple theoretically-justified double sampling with big efficiency gains; strong large‑K experiments; public code and clear φ for text.  
- Cons: Sampling is the main algorithmic novelty; no theory for candidate selection; limited comparison to non‑transformed bounds; φ is handcrafted for text so generality is unclear.  
- Requests for authors: Provide recall@q or bounds for candidate selection; compare sample complexity vs OVA/multinomial logistic; report typical α,β under π∝1/η on datasets; add ablations on κ and π (including uniform π); specify training loss/regularizer/dimensionality and feature normalization; clarify centroid computation space and potential learned embeddings.  
- Minor comments: Define symbols in Theorem 2 where first used and fix a few typos.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — practically relevant and theoretically grounded, but needs candidate-selection analysis and comparison to non-transformed bounds.
