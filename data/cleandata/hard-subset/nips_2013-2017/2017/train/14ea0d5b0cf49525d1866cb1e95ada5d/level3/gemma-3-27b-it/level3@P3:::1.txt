This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, proposing a double sampling strategy to improve the multi-class to binary reduction method of Joshi et al. (2017) [16]. The core idea is to balance class distributions and reduce the number of dyadic examples, thereby improving both training time and scalability. The authors provide theoretical analysis in the form of generalization bounds and demonstrate empirical improvements on large-scale text datasets like DMOZ and Wikipedia.

The work builds directly on the foundation laid by [16], and related works like those employing one-vs-all (OVA) [18], tree-based methods [4, 6, 9], and label embedding approaches [11, 19]. It differentiates itself by focusing on a specific sampling technique designed to address the long-tailed class distributions common in extreme classification, coupled with novel generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity. The paper is well-motivated, tackling a practical and important problem in machine learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a relevant problem:** Extreme multi-class classification is a critical area with applications in various domains, including text categorization, recommendation systems, and image recognition.
* **Scalability:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method scales effectively to datasets with a very large number of classes (up to 100,000), where many other approaches struggle.
* **Performance:** The method achieves competitive or superior performance in terms of accuracy and macro F1-score compared to several state-of-the-art baselines on the tested datasets.
* **Reduced Training Time & Memory Usage:** The paper shows compelling results in terms of reduced training time and memory consumption, which are crucial for handling extremely large datasets.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The inclusion of generalization bounds, while complex, attempts to provide a theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the proposed sampling strategy.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The primary contribution lies in applying double sampling to an existing framework [16]. While the technique demonstrably improves performance, the fundamental approach remains largely incremental. The theoretical contribution of the generalization bounds, while mathematically sound, feels somewhat detached from the practical gains observed.
* **Prediction Time:** Despite faster training, the paper acknowledges that prediction time remains a bottleneck. In many real-world applications, prediction latency is arguably more important than training time. This limitation should be emphasized more strongly.
* **Generalization Bound Interpretation:** The practical significance and comparative advantage of the presented generalization bound (Theorem 2) are unclear. How does this bound compare to existing bounds in the literature, and what insights does it offer beyond simply confirming consistency? Further elaboration would be beneficial.
* **Uneven Comparison:** Mixing batch and online baselines (like RecallTree) in the memory usage comparisons is problematic. Online methods inherently have lower peak memory requirements than batch methods. This makes a direct comparison misleading and potentially exaggerates the memory benefits of the proposed method. A fairer comparison would involve either converting all methods to batch mode or explicitly acknowledging and accounting for the different memory access patterns.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the method to the choice of the (π, κ) hyperparameters is not thoroughly discussed. While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, a more detailed analysis of how these parameters impact performance would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem with a practical and effective solution.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear advantages in scalability and training efficiency.
* The theoretical analysis, while complex, provides some justification for the observed performance.
* The writing is generally clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the approach is relatively limited.
* The prediction time remains a significant drawback.
* The generalization bound is not convincingly justified as a meaningful contribution.
* The memory usage comparison is potentially misleading.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. The proposed double sampling strategy is a practical and effective technique for improving the scalability and efficiency of existing methods. However, the limited novelty and the unresolved issues regarding prediction time and the generalization bound prevent it from being a truly outstanding submission. While it doesn’t fundamentally alter the landscape of extreme classification, it represents a valuable engineering improvement.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  More thoroughly discuss the limitations related to prediction time and potential strategies to address this issue.
2.  Provide a clearer interpretation of the generalization bound and its relevance to the practical results.
3.  Revise the memory usage comparison to ensure a fair comparison between batch and online methods, or clearly acknowledge the limitations of the current comparison.
4.  Include a more detailed discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the hyperparameters (π, κ).
