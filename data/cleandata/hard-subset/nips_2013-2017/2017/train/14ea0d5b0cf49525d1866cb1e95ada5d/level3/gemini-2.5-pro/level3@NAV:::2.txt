This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, where the number of classes (K) is very large and often follows a long-tailed distribution. The core approach is a reduction from multi-class to binary classification, which learns a scoring function over (input, class) pairs. This reduction strategy builds directly on previous work, most notably Joshi et al. (2015), which also addressed the issue of dependent data in the transformed dataset. The main contribution of this work is the introduction of an aggressive "double sampling" strategy. This consists of two steps: (1) per-class sampling to rebalance the training data by over-sampling rare classes and under-sampling frequent ones, and (2) sampling a small subset of negative classes for each example to drastically reduce the size of the final binary training set. The authors provide a novel generalization analysis using fractional Rademacher complexity to show that their sampling procedures do not fundamentally compromise the consistency of the empirical risk minimizer.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance & Quality:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem in machine learning. The proposed sampling strategy is a practical and well-motivated solution to the computational and class imbalance issues inherent in extreme classification.
*   **Technical Soundness:** A major strength is the theoretical analysis. The authors provide improved generalization bounds that account for the data dependency introduced by the reduction and, crucially, extend this analysis to their sampling scheme. Showing that the sampling strategy is theoretically grounded (Theorem 2) adds significant weight to the contribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on large-scale text classification benchmarks are convincing. The proposed method demonstrates a highly competitive trade-off between predictive accuracy, memory usage, and computational time when compared against a suite of strong baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Originality:** While the sampling technique and its analysis are novel, the underlying multi-class to binary reduction framework is not. The paper could be clearer in positioning its contribution as a significant, but specific, improvement upon the framework of Joshi et al.
*   **Incomplete Analysis:** A key component of the method's practical utility is the fast prediction algorithm (Sec. 2.3), which uses an approximate search over the k-nearest class centroids. This is presented as a heuristic, but its impact on the generalization bounds derived in the main theorems is not analyzed, leaving a gap between the theory and the implemented algorithm.
*   **Missing Context:** The paper would be strengthened by a discussion comparing its theoretical bounds to those of non-reduction-based multi-class methods (e.g., one-vs-all). This would help readers understand the theoretical price paid, in terms of sample complexity, for the computational benefits of this reduction approach.
