## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, proposing a double sampling strategy to improve upon the multi-class to binary reduction method introduced in [16]. The core idea is to balance class distributions and reduce the number of pairwise examples constructed during the reduction process, aiming for faster training and improved performance. The authors support their approach with theoretical analysis, providing generalization bounds, and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia).

The paper builds logically on prior work in extreme classification, clearly situating itself within the landscape of tree-based and embedding-based methods, as well as binary reduction techniques. The motivation for addressing the class imbalance and computational cost associated with these methods is well-articulated. The theoretical contribution, extending the Rademacher complexity analysis from [16], is a valuable addition, though its practical implications and the interpretation of the presented bound remain somewhat unclear. 

The experimental results are a significant strength. The authors demonstrate that their method scales effectively to datasets with tens of thousands of classes, achieving competitive performance in terms of accuracy and macro F1-score, while also reducing training time and memory consumption compared to several baselines. The comprehensive comparison against methods like OVA, RecallTree, and PD-Sparse is commendable. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While faster training is reported, the authors acknowledge that prediction time remains a bottleneck. In many real-world applications, prediction latency is critical, and this limitation should be given more emphasis. Furthermore, the comparison between batch and online baselines regarding memory usage feels uneven, potentially skewing the results. Mixing these paradigms makes a direct comparison difficult. Finally, the novelty is incremental; the core contribution lies in the application of double sampling and the associated theoretical analysis to an existing framework.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and practical problem in extreme classification.
* Presents a theoretically sound approach with generalization bounds.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance on large-scale datasets.
* Offers improvements in training time and memory usage.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited novelty – largely builds on existing work.
* Prediction time remains a significant issue.
* Uneven comparison of batch and online baselines.
* Unclear interpretation of the generalization bound.



**Overall:** This paper represents a solid contribution to the field of extreme classification. While the novelty is limited, the practical improvements and theoretical grounding are valuable. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding prediction time and the comparison of baselines in a revised version.
