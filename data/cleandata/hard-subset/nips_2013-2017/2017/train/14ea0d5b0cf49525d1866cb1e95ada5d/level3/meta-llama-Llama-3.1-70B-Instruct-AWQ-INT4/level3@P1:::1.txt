This paper proposes a novel approach to tackle the problem of extreme multi-class classification, where the number of classes is very large. The authors build upon their previous work [16] and introduce a double sampling strategy to improve the scalability of the method. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, including new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexity, and presents comprehensive experimental results on several text classification datasets.

The main contribution of this paper is the proposal of a double sampling strategy, which aims to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions and reduce the number of pairs of examples in the expanded data. The authors demonstrate that this strategy does not alter the consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle defined over the double sample reduction. The experimental results show that the proposed approach scales well to multi-class problems with many classes and achieves high performance and shorter training times compared to several state-of-the-art methods.

The strengths of this paper include:

* The proposal of a novel double sampling strategy to improve the scalability of the method, which is a significant contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification.
* The provision of thorough theoretical analysis, including new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexity, which provides a solid foundation for the proposed approach.
* The presentation of comprehensive experimental results on several text classification datasets, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

However, there are also some limitations and potential drawbacks:

* The novelty of the paper is limited, as it largely builds upon the authors' previous work [16] and adds double sampling and local Rademacher complexity bounds.
* The prediction time of the proposed approach remains long compared to other methods, which may be more important in practice despite faster training times.
* The interpretation and comparative advantage of the presented generalization bound are unclear, and further explanation is needed to fully understand its significance.
* The mixing of batch and online baselines makes memory-usage comparisons uneven and potentially misleading, and a more careful comparison is needed to fully evaluate the proposed approach.

In terms of significance, this paper addresses the important and practical area of extreme classification, which is a challenging problem in machine learning. The proposed approach has the potential to be applied to a wide range of applications, including text classification, recommendation, and ranking. However, further research is needed to fully explore the potential of this approach and to address the limitations and potential drawbacks mentioned above.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the limitations and potential drawbacks mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should:

* Provide further explanation and clarification on the interpretation and comparative advantage of the presented generalization bound.
* Conduct a more careful comparison of the proposed approach with other methods, including a more detailed analysis of prediction times and memory usage.
* Explore the potential of the proposed approach in other applications beyond text classification.

With these revisions, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification and to be of interest to a wide range of researchers and practitioners in the field.
