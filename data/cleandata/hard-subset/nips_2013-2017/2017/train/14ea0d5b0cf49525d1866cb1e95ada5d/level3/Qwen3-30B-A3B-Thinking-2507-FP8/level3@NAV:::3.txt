

This paper proposes a novel learning reduction for extreme classification (up to ~100k classes) by transforming the problem into pairwise binary classification through "Aggressive Double Sampling." The authors address two key limitations of existing approaches: tree-based methods suffer from cascading errors, while label-embedding methods fail when label matrices aren't low-rank. Their solution samples classes inversely proportional to empirical frequency and uses adversarial examples to construct a dyadic classifier, with theoretical guarantees showing consistency under empirical risk minimization.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on five text datasets, showing superior accuracy and F1 scores for problems with >30k classes compared to state-of-the-art methods (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, etc.). It achieves faster training and significantly lower memory usage while maintaining competitive prediction times. The theoretical analysis is sophisticated, using Janson's concentration inequalities to handle dependent dyadic examples and oversampling effects, with a finite-sample risk bound that shows bias decreases linearly with sample size.

Quality is excellent: the theoretical analysis is technically sound (though complex), the experiments are thorough and well-designed, and the authors acknowledge limitations (slower prediction). Clarity is good, with clear explanations of the sampling procedure and theoretical contributions, though the notation could be slightly simplified for broader accessibility. Originality is strong—the double sampling strategy represents a meaningful advancement over existing reduction techniques, clearly differentiating from prior work in NIPS proceedings (e.g., [16, 28] on dyadic reductions). Significance is high: the method effectively addresses the scalability challenges of extreme classification, providing a practical solution for large-scale text classification with minimal memory overhead.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, offering both theoretical rigor and practical utility. While the slower prediction time is a limitation, the trade-off is justified for large-scale applications where training efficiency and memory usage are critical constraints. The work is well-positioned to influence future research in extreme classification. Overall, this is a high-quality, significant contribution that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. I recommend acceptance.
