Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses extreme multi-class classification (very large K) by reducing to a binary problem over dyadic (example, class) pairs. The core reduction follows prior work (e.g., Joshi et al., 2015; Usunier et al., 2005/2006; Weston & Watkins), where a scoring function g(x, y) = f(φ(x, y)) is learned so that correct classes outrank incorrect ones. A well-known issue with such reductions is statistical dependence among the K−1 dyads per example, which complicates generalization analysis.

The main contributions are:
- A double sampling scheme: (i) class-rebalancing to counter long-tailed label distributions (oversampling rare classes, subsampling frequent ones), and (ii) for each sampled training point, sampling only κ “adversarial” classes (κ ≪ K) to form dyads, dramatically shrinking the expanded dataset.
- New generalization bounds for dependent dyads that (a) build on fractional covers/fractional Rademacher complexity (Ralaivola & Amini, 2015; Janson, 2004), and (b) incorporate local Rademacher complexities to obtain faster rates under variance control. Theorems are presented for the full dyadic expansion and for the double-sampled regime, with explicit dependence on κ, K, and the rebalancing probabilities π.
- An efficient text-classification instantiation with a simple 10-feature φ inspired by learning-to-rank, plus a candidate-selection prediction heuristic that evaluates f only on q nearest centroid classes. Empirically, on LSHTC/Wikipedia/DMOZ-scale datasets (up to 100K classes), the method is competitive in accuracy and macro-F1 while offering substantial gains in training/prediction time and memory versus OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, FastXML, PfastReXML, and RecallTree.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and timely problem in extreme classification with a simple, practical pipeline that is easy to implement.
- Sound and relevant theory for the dyadic reduction with dependent examples; the move to local fractional Rademacher complexity is a meaningful extension beyond Joshi et al. (2015).
- The double sampling is well-motivated by long-tail label distributions and leads to real efficiency gains without degrading consistency; the bound in Theorem 2 makes dependence on κ, K, and class imbalance explicit.
- Strong empirical results on large, standard benchmarks with multiple baselines; the method achieves a favorable performance–efficiency trade-off. Macro-F1 improvements are particularly relevant under class imbalance.
- Code and datasets are provided, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- The novelty is incremental: the reduction and φ-based scoring paradigm are standard; the main novelty lies in sampling/rebalancing and in tightening the bounds.
- The candidate-selection step at prediction time (nearest centroids, top-q) is not analyzed theoretically; its impact on consistency and error is unclear and could bias comparisons when q ≪ K.
- Limited discussion contrasting the obtained sample complexity with known bounds for non-reduction multiclass formulations (e.g., Crammer-Singer, Weston-Watkins), or with ECOC/log-time methods; this would better situate the contribution.
- Empirical evaluation focuses on text with a hand-crafted 10-D φ; generality to other domains is asserted but not demonstrated.
- Details of the sampling scheme could be clearer (with/without replacement, target sample size M, sensitivity to π and κ).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The dependence-aware generalization analysis appears correct and extends prior tools appropriately. Theorems clearly exhibit how α and β (functions of π and class proportions η) and κ control the bounds. However, the practical setting of π ∝ 1/η deserves a more explicit discussion of how α = max η/π behaves and whether constants can be large under severe imbalance.
- Empirics: Comparisons are thorough for the 2016–2017 landscape (OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree). Results convincingly support efficiency/accuracy claims. Ablations isolating the effects of (i) class rebalancing, (ii) κ-sub-sampling, and (iii) candidate selection would strengthen causal claims.
- A missing piece is a theoretical or empirical characterization of the prediction-time candidate selection (e.g., recall@q of the true class vs q; its effect on the final generalization bound).

Clarity
- Generally well-organized. The reduction and bounds are presented cleanly, with appropriate prior references. Some notation is dense; define all symbols where first used (e.g., distinguish μk and ηk; both are “proportions”).
- The sampling algorithm could specify whether Sπ is formed with/without replacement and how M is controlled. Section 2.3 should define q and its relation to κ, and discuss choosing q.
- Minor language issues/typos (see below).

Originality
- Moderate. Dyadic reductions and fractional complexity analyses are known; this paper integrates local complexity into the dependent-data setting and introduces a pragmatic double sampling for extreme K. The theoretical handling of the rebalanced, sub-sampled dyadic set is a useful addition.

Significance
- High practical significance for extreme text classification with long-tailed labels: the approach is simple, fast, memory-efficient, and accurate. The theory clarifies when such aggressive sampling preserves consistency. Likely to be adopted or extended, especially as a strong baseline.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, scalable method with open-source code; strong results on large benchmarks.
- Meaningful theoretical extension (local fractional Rademacher bounds in the dependent dyadic setting) supporting the double sampling.
- Addresses a ubiquitous pain point (long tails) with a simple solution.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to prior dyadic reductions; limited theoretical treatment of the candidate-selection step.
- Empirical scope limited to text with a specific φ; no ablations to isolate contributions; limited comparison to multiclass methods beyond reductions/trees.

Questions for authors
- Provide a sensitivity study for π and κ, and an ablation showing the effect of each sampling step.
- Analyze candidate selection: what q is needed to maintain high recall of the true class? Can the bound incorporate a candidate-recall term?
- Clarify whether Sπ is sampled with replacement and how M is chosen. What is the expected α under π ∝ 1/η?
- Discuss how the bounds compare (qualitatively) to known multiclass (non-reduction) sample complexities.
- How robust is the method to different φ choices? Any evidence beyond text?

Minor issues and typos
- Several hyphenation/grammar issues (e.g., “Even-though,” “most small size classes,” “MaF1thus”).
- “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems…” stray text.
- Define all symbols consistently (μk vs ηk).

Reproducibility
- Positive: code and datasets are linked; hyperparameters reportedly listed in the appendix. Please ensure scripts reproduce the main tables and include default π, κ, q settings.

Recommendation
- Weak accept. Solid empirical and theoretical contribution for an important problem; some aspects (prediction heuristic theory, ablations) could be improved but overall the paper offers a useful, scalable approach with supporting analysis. Confidence: medium.
