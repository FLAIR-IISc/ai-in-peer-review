Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing the problem to binary classification over dyadic (input, class) pairs. Each training example produces K−1 pairwise comparisons via a mapping φ: X×Y → Rp and a scorer f, yielding g(x, y) = f(φ(x, y)), and a ranking-style loss that counts how many non-true classes outrank the true class. The key technical challenge is that the transformed examples are dependent (each original example spawns K−1 pairs), invalidating standard bounds. Building on Joshi et al. (2015), which also used fractional Rademacher complexity to handle dependence, the paper derives improved generalization bounds using local (variance-sensitive) fractional Rademacher complexity, providing sharper rates.

The main algorithmic novelty is a “double sampling” strategy to make the reduction scalable and robust to long-tailed label distributions: (i) per-class sampling with replacement to rebalance head/tail classes (π-sampling across original classes), and (ii) random sampling of κ comparison classes per example to reduce the number of dyadic pairs. The authors prove that these two sampling steps do not substantially worsen generalization bounds: the excess risk scales with α and β terms that depend on the sampling probabilities and with 1/√(Mκ), where M is the number of retained examples and κ the number of sampled comparisons. For text, φ is instantiated with 10 lightweight similarity features (including distance to class centroids), and a candidate selection scheme at prediction restricts scoring to q nearest class centroids to reduce latency.

Experiments on LSHTC/DMOZ and Wikipedia subsets with 10k–100k classes show competitive or superior accuracy/MaF1 compared to strong baselines (OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML), with substantially better memory/runtime than OVA/M-SVM and competitive speed with tree-based methods.

Quality
- Theory: The extension from fractional to local fractional Rademacher complexity for the dependent dyadic setting is technically sound and well-motivated, and it directly addresses the correlated pairs issue that undermines classical i.i.d. bounds. The bounds for double sampling (Theorem 2) are useful, showing consistency and explicit dependence on π and κ. It would help to interpret the magnitude of α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy under the recommended inverse-frequency sampling (πy ∝ 1/ηy). As written, α could be large in head classes unless normalization effects keep it moderate; a short numerical illustration on real class histograms would make the “does not significantly worsen” claim more concrete.
- Algorithms: The double sampling procedure is clear and practically impactful. However, the candidate selection used at prediction (Section 2.3) lacks theoretical analysis. There is no guarantee that the true class lies among the q centroids selected, and training does not incorporate this selection, potentially causing train–test mismatch. Even a simple recall@q analysis (probability the true class is in Yx) or a bound on induced approximation error as a function of q and centroid separation would strengthen the story.
- Empirics: The evaluation is thorough for large K, with strong baselines and reporting of time and memory. The proposed method is competitive in accuracy/MaF1 and notably efficient. Two issues to consider: (i) since LSHTC datasets are multilabel converted to multiclass by replication, please discuss any bias this introduces for macro-F1 and whether stratified splits preserve label frequencies; (ii) an ablation on π (class rebalancing) and κ (comparison subsampling) would clarify their respective contributions to accuracy, memory, and time. Also report the impact of q (candidate set size) on speed/accuracy.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written, with a clear reduction, sampling procedure, and high-level proof ideas. Suggestions:
- Clearly specify the learning algorithm for f (loss, regularizer, linear/nonlinear class). The current text reads as if 0–1 loss is analyzed but a convex surrogate must be used in practice—please state which and whether the bounds extend.
- Define normalization for π explicitly and provide intuition for α, β under inverse-frequency sampling (e.g., typical values on WIKI-50K).
- Briefly summarize the improvement over Joshi et al. (2015) in rate or constants, and cite the precise differences (local vs global fractional complexities).
- In Section 2.1, restate the dependence graph and fractional cover used to compute the complexity to aid readers unfamiliar with Janson (2004).

Originality
The reduction framework and handling of dependence via fractional Rademacher complexity are not entirely new (Joshi et al., Usunier & Amini, Ralaivola & Amini). The main novelty lies in the aggressive double sampling (rebalancing + κ comparisons) and the local fractional Rademacher analysis that yields improved, variance-aware bounds and shows the sampling does not degrade consistency. The prediction-time candidate selection is a practical trick rather than a theoretical contribution.

Significance
Extreme multiclass is important, and the proposed method is attractive in practice: a single binary learner with small dyadic features, strong accuracy/MaF1, and much better memory/runtime than OVA/M-SVM on 50k–100k classes. The theoretical developments are relevant to others working with reductions that induce dependent samples. The lack of analysis for the candidate selection step and missing comparison to sample complexity of non-transformed multiclass formulations (e.g., multinomial logistic/OVA bounds scaling with K) reduce the theoretical contribution’s broader impact; adding such a comparison would sharpen the significance.

Pros
- Addresses dependence in dyadic reductions with new local fractional Rademacher bounds; clear consistency result.
- Double sampling is simple, effective, and theoretically justified; substantial savings in memory/time with negligible loss in generalization.
- Strong empirical results on large K with competitive or better accuracy/MaF1 and excellent efficiency.
- Public code and clear description of φ for text.

Cons
- Main algorithmic novelty is sampling/rebalancing; the core reduction and dependency handling largely follow prior work.
- No theoretical analysis of prediction-time candidate selection; potential train–test mismatch.
- Limited discussion comparing the bounds to those for non-transformed multiclass methods; no quantification of improvement over Joshi et al. in practice.
- φ is handcrafted for text; unclear generality to other modalities without learning φ.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
1) Provide a bound or at least empirical recall@q curves for the candidate selection step, and discuss how to pick q to control approximation error vs speed.
2) Include a short comparison of sample complexity: how do your bounds scale with K, M, κ vs OVA/multinomial logistic (e.g., margin-based Rademacher bounds)? Even a qualitative table would help.
3) Clarify the typical magnitude of α, β with πy ∝ 1/ηy on your datasets; show that they remain modest in long-tailed regimes.
4) Add ablations on κ and π (including using uniform π) to show the contribution of each sampling step to accuracy and efficiency.
5) Specify the training loss, regularization, and model class for f; if linear, state dimensionality p and whether features were standardized.
6) Consider reporting whether centroids used in prediction are computed in the dyadic feature space or original space, and whether using learned class embeddings could further help.

Minor comments
- Define all symbols in Theorem 2 in the main text (α, β, ηy, πy) where they first appear.
- Typos: “Even-though” → “Even though”, “most small size classes” → “the smallest classes”, “MaF1thus” → “MaF1 thus”.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a practically relevant and theoretically grounded approach for extreme multiclass problems, with strong empirical performance. Addressing the missing analysis for the prediction algorithm and adding a brief comparison to non-transformed bounds would solidify the contribution.
