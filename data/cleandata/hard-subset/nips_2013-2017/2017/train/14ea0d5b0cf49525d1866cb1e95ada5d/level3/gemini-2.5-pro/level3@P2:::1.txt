### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of extreme multi-class classification, where the number of classes is very large and typically exhibits a long-tailed distribution. The authors propose a method that builds on a multi-class to binary reduction strategy [16], which reformulates the original problem as a binary classification task over pairs of (example, class) representations. The core contribution is an "aggressive double sampling" scheme designed to improve the scalability and performance of this reduction. This sampling has two stages: 1) The original training set is re-sampled to create a more balanced class distribution, mitigating the effect of the long tail. 2) For each example in the new set, only a small subset of `κ` negative classes are sampled to form the negative pairs for the binary problem, drastically reducing the size of the expanded dataset. The paper provides theoretical justification for this approach with generalization bounds derived using local Rademacher complexity, arguing for the consistency of the empirical risk minimization. The method is evaluated extensively on large-scale text classification datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia) with up to 100,000 classes, demonstrating impressive results in terms of training time, memory usage, and predictive accuracy against a comprehensive set of baselines.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a well-defined algorithm backed by both theoretical analysis and strong empirical results. The experiments are thorough, using relevant large-scale datasets and comparing against a wide range of state-of-the-art methods for extreme classification. The reported gains in training time and memory efficiency, while maintaining competitive or superior accuracy and Macro-F1 scores, are significant and well-supported by the evidence in Table 3. This represents a solid piece of work.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the double sampling approach—to tackle both class imbalance and the computational burden of the binary reduction—is clearly articulated. The algorithm is presented logically. However, the clarity of the theoretical contribution could be improved. While Theorems 1 and 2 provide generalization bounds, their interpretation and comparative advantage are not discussed in detail. It is unclear what new insights these bounds provide over previous ones (e.g., in [16]), or what the practical implications of the different terms in the bounds are. A more intuitive explanation would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

**Originality:** The work addresses the important and challenging problem of extreme classification. The proposed double sampling strategy is a simple, elegant, and effective solution to the scalability issues of the reduction framework in [16]. However, the novelty is somewhat limited, as the paper is a direct and incremental extension of [16]. The core ideas—multi-class to binary reduction and sampling to handle class imbalance—are established techniques. The main contribution lies in the effective combination and application of these ideas to the extreme classification setting, supported by a refined theoretical analysis.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are of high practical importance. The method offers an excellent trade-off between training efficiency, memory usage, and predictive performance, making it a very attractive option for practitioners facing extreme classification problems. The demonstrated ability to scale to 100,000 classes is a notable achievement. However, a significant concern that tempers the practical impact is the prediction time. The results in Table 3 show that the proposed method, while fast to train, is considerably slower at prediction time than tree-based methods like FastXML and PfastReXML. In many real-world applications (e.g., real-time tagging or recommendation), prediction latency is a more critical bottleneck than training time. The paper proposes a candidate selection heuristic to speed up prediction, but the trade-off between speed and accuracy for this heuristic is not fully explored.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  The paper tackles a highly relevant and difficult problem in machine learning: extreme multi-class classification.
2.  The proposed double sampling method is simple, well-motivated, and demonstrably effective, leading to significant reductions in training time and memory usage.
3.  The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and shows that the method is highly competitive with, and often superior to, several state-of-the-art baselines on large-scale benchmark datasets.
4.  The work is supported by a theoretical analysis that shows the consistency of the learning procedure despite the sampling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The novelty is incremental, as the work is a direct extension of the framework proposed in [16].
2.  The high prediction time relative to other state-of-the-art methods is a major practical drawback that is not sufficiently addressed.
3.  The experimental comparison of memory usage could be seen as uneven, as it mixes batch methods with online methods (e.g., from Vowpal Wabbit) without discussing the inherent differences in their memory models. This could be clarified to provide a fairer comparison.
4.  The practical significance of the theoretical bounds is not well explained.

Overall, despite some limitations in novelty and concerns about prediction time, the strong empirical results and the practical utility of the proposed method for scaling up training for extreme classification problems make this a valuable contribution to the field.
