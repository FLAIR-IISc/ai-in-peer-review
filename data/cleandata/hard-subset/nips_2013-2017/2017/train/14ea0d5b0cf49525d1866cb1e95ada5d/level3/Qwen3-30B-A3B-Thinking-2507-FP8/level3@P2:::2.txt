

# Review for NIPS 2017

The paper proposes an aggressive double sampling strategy for multi-class to binary reduction in large-scale classification problems with extremely large numbers of classes (10,000-100,000). The authors build upon the binary reduction approach of Joshi et al. (2015) by introducing a novel double sampling procedure to address two key challenges: (1) long-tailed class distributions common in large-scale multi-class problems, and (2) the computational burden of constructing the dyadic training set with O(mK) examples. The proposed (π, κ)-DS algorithm first oversamples small classes and subsamples large classes (using class size inversely proportional to sampling probability) and then randomly samples κ comparison classes per example to reduce the transformed dataset size. The authors provide theoretical analysis showing that this double sampling preserves consistency of empirical risk minimization through new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity.

This work relates directly to several NIPS papers on extreme classification. It extends the binary reduction approach of Joshi et al. (2015) which the authors cite as foundational. The paper also builds upon the multi-class to binary reduction framework of [1, 28, 16] while addressing their computational limitations. The authors' approach differs from tree-based methods [4, 3, 6, 7, 9, 21, 5, 15] by avoiding error propagation from hierarchical decisions, and from label embedding approaches [11, 5, 19] by not requiring the low-rank label matrix assumption.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper addresses a significant practical challenge in large-scale multi-class classification - the computational intractability of standard approaches when K is extremely large. The double sampling strategy effectively tackles the curse of long-tailed class distributions, which is a well-known issue in real-world text classification problems.

2. The theoretical contribution provides valuable generalization bounds for the sampling strategy. The authors extend Joshi et al. (2015) by using local fractional Rademacher complexity to derive tighter bounds that account for the inter-dependency between dyadic examples. This analysis is rigorous and addresses an important gap in the theoretical understanding of such reduction approaches.

3. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple large-scale text classification datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia) with up to 100,000 classes. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed method achieves better trade-offs between training/prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art approaches.

4. The paper is well-written with clear descriptions of both the theoretical and algorithmic contributions. The experimental setup is thorough, with careful comparison to relevant baselines including OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The novelty of the paper is somewhat limited. The core reduction strategy (mapping (x,y) to φ(x,y)) and the general binary reduction framework largely follow Joshi et al. (2015). The primary novelty is the sampling technique, which while valuable, is not as substantial as a novel reduction strategy.

2. The paper lacks a theoretical comparison of the sample complexity and generalization bounds to standard multi-class formulations (without transformation). It would be valuable to understand how the sampling strategy affects the fundamental sample complexity compared to directly learning a multi-class classifier.

3. The prediction algorithm in Section 2.3 (candidate selection using nearest centroids) is not theoretically analyzed. The authors do not discuss how the choice of q (number of candidate classes) affects the generalization error or how this compares to the standard approach of evaluating all classes.

4. The paper does not adequately address how the sampling strategy interacts with different feature representations. The text classification experiments use a specific feature mapping, but it's unclear how sensitive the approach is to the choice of φ.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper addresses a significant practical problem in large-scale multi-class classification with a well-motivated solution.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable generalization bounds for the proposed sampling strategy.
- The empirical results are compelling, showing clear advantages in scalability and performance on large datasets.
- The paper builds upon and extends previous work in a meaningful way, with clear theoretical contributions.

*Con:*
- The core reduction idea is not novel and largely follows prior work.
- The novelty of the sampling technique is somewhat limited given the substantial technical contributions required to demonstrate its effectiveness.
- The theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm is missing, which is a significant omission.
- The comparison to standard multi-class formulations is incomplete.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale multi-class classification by providing an effective sampling strategy that addresses practical challenges of long-tailed distributions and computational complexity. While the core reduction strategy follows prior work, the authors provide meaningful extensions through their sampling procedure and theoretical analysis. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate clear advantages over state-of-the-art approaches.

However, the paper's novelty is somewhat limited, and the theoretical analysis is incomplete (particularly regarding the prediction algorithm). The authors should address these gaps in the final version. Given the practical significance of the problem and the strong empirical results, I recommend acceptance, though I would suggest the authors strengthen their theoretical discussion and clarify the novelty relative to Joshi et al. (2015).

Quality: 8/10 (technically sound, well-supported, but some theoretical gaps)
Clarity: 9/10 (well-written, organized, but some theoretical sections could be clearer)
Originality: 7/10 (builds on prior work with a meaningful extension, but core idea is not novel)
Significance: 8/10 (addresses a practically important problem with clear benefits for large-scale applications)
