- Tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing to binary classification and learning a scoring function over (input, class) pairs.  
- Builds on prior reductions (notably Joshi et al. 2015) that handle dependent data in the transformed dataset.  
- Main contribution: an aggressive "double sampling" strategy—(1) per-class sampling to rebalance long-tailed data and (2) sampling a small subset of negative classes per example to shrink training size.  
- Provides novel generalization analysis using fractional Rademacher complexity showing sampling preserves consistency (Theorem 2).  
- Strength: addresses a significant practical problem with a well-motivated, computationally efficient sampling solution.  
- Strength: strong theoretical grounding with improved generalization bounds that account for data dependence and sampling.  
- Strength: convincing empirical results on large-scale text benchmarks showing a good trade-off among accuracy, memory, and time versus strong baselines.  
- Weakness: underlying reduction framework is not novel; clarity needed on how this work advances Joshi et al. specifically.  
- Weakness: fast prediction heuristic (approximate k-NN over class centroids) is used in practice but its effect on the theoretical bounds is unanalyzed, creating a theory–implementation gap.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion comparing theoretical bounds to non-reduction multi-class methods (e.g., one-vs-all), so the sample-complexity cost of the reduction is unclear.
