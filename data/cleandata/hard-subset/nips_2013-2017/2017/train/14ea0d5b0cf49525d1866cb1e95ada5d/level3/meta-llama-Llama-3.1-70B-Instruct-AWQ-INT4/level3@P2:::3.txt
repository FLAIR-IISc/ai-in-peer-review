This paper proposes a novel approach to extreme multi-class classification problems by reducing them to pairwise binary classification. The authors argue that existing tree-based methods suffer from cascading errors and label-embedding methods fail when label matrices are not low-rank. To address these limitations, they introduce a scalable, sampling-based reduction with theoretical consistency guarantees relative to multi-class empirical risk minimization (ERM).

The proposed method, called "Aggressive Double Sampling" (ADS), is controlled by two parameters: class sampling frequency inversely proportional to empirical frequency, and the number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples. The training process repeats the sampling procedure to construct the final dyadic (pairwise) classifier. To reduce prediction time, the authors use a heuristic candidate selection via class centroids to avoid enumerating all class pairwise features.

The theoretical analysis of the proposed method is complicated by dependent dyadic examples and oversampling that shifts the empirical distribution. However, the authors provide a finite-sample risk bound that carries bias from true vs. oversampled class probability ratios. This bias decreases linearly with sample/resample sizes.

The experimental evaluation of the proposed method on five text datasets shows superior accuracy and F1 scores for problems with more than 30,000 classes, faster training, and much lower memory use. However, the prediction time is slower compared to other methods.

The strengths of this paper include:

* A novel and scalable approach to extreme multi-class classification problems
* Theoretical consistency guarantees relative to multi-class ERM
* Superior performance on large-scale datasets
* Low memory and computation requirements

The weaknesses of this paper include:

* The analysis is complicated and relies on several assumptions
* The prediction time is slower compared to other methods
* The choice of hyperparameters requires careful tuning

Overall, this paper presents a strong method for extreme classification with low memory/computation and superior performance on the largest datasets. The theoretical analysis is plausible, and the algorithm is theoretically sound and practically useful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The proposed method is novel and scalable, making it a valuable contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification.
* The theoretical analysis provides consistency guarantees relative to multi-class ERM, which is a desirable property for any classification method.
* The experimental evaluation shows superior performance on large-scale datasets, which is a key challenge in many real-world applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The analysis is complicated and relies on several assumptions, which may not hold in all scenarios.
* The prediction time is slower compared to other methods, which may be a limitation in certain applications.
* The choice of hyperparameters requires careful tuning, which can be time-consuming and may not always lead to optimal results.

**Confidence:** 8/10

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The authors should consider addressing the limitations of their method, such as the slower prediction time and the need for careful tuning of hyperparameters. Additionally, they should provide more details on the implementation of their method and the experimental setup.
