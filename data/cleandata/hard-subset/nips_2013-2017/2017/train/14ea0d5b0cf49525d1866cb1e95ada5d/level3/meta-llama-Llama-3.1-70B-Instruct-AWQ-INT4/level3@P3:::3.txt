This paper proposes a novel method for extreme classification, where the number of classes is very large (up to 100,000). The authors introduce a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction strategy, which transforms the original multi-class problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples. This approach aims to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions and reduce the number of pairs of examples in the expanded data.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed solution. They also provide a thorough analysis of the related work, highlighting the limitations of existing tree-based and label-embedding methods. The proposed method is scalable, and the authors provide theoretical consistency guarantees relative to multiclass ERM.

The experimental results are impressive, showing superior accuracy and F1 scores for problems with more than 30,000 classes, faster training, and much lower memory use. The authors also introduce a heuristic prediction-time candidate selection via class centroids to avoid enumerating all class pairwise features.

The theoretical analysis is sound, and the authors provide a proof of the generalization bound using local fractional Rademacher complexity. The proof is complicated by dependent dyadic examples and oversampling that shifts the empirical distribution. However, the authors provide a clear explanation of the proof and the assumptions made.

Overall, this paper presents a strong method for extreme classification with low memory and computation requirements and superior performance on the largest datasets. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments on five text datasets.

Strengths:

Weaknesses:
