## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper tackles the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, focusing on improving the scalability and performance of the multi-class to binary reduction approach. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling strategy – (π, κ)-DS – to address class imbalance and reduce the computational burden associated with expanding the dataset into pairwise comparisons. They build upon prior work by Joshi et al. (2015) by deriving improved generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity. 

The core idea of reducing multi-class classification to a binary problem (as initially explored in Joshi et al.) is well-established. However, the authors correctly identify the practical limitations stemming from the resulting dataset size and class imbalance. Their sampling procedure, combining class rebalancing (π) and pairwise example reduction (κ), is a logical and potentially effective way to mitigate these issues. The theoretical analysis, extending existing bounds, is a key contribution, demonstrating the consistency of empirical risk minimization with their sampling strategy. The experimental validation on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia) is strong, showcasing competitive performance against several strong baselines, particularly in terms of runtime and memory usage. 

A significant strength of the paper is the clear articulation of the problem and the proposed solution. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of related work and position their contribution effectively within the existing literature. The empirical results provide compelling evidence of the benefits of their approach, especially in scenarios with extremely large numbers of classes. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The prediction algorithm utilizing k-nearest class centroids (Sec. 2.3) lacks a dedicated theoretical analysis. A deeper dive into its impact on generalization error would strengthen the work. More importantly, the authors don't sufficiently compare the sample complexity/generalization bounds of their transformed binary classification formulation with those achievable in non-transformed multi-class settings. Understanding this trade-off is crucial for assessing the true value of the reduction approach. Furthermore, while the ablation study is present in the appendix, a more detailed analysis of the impact of parameters π and κ would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a practically important problem (extreme multi-class classification).
* Proposes a novel and well-motivated sampling technique to improve scalability and performance.
* Provides theoretical justification for the sampling strategy with improved generalization bounds.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on large-scale datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Lack of theoretical analysis for the prediction algorithm utilizing k-nearest class centroids.
* Insufficient comparison of sample complexity to non-transformed multi-class formulations.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. While there are some areas for improvement, the strong theoretical grounding, practical relevance, and promising experimental results make it a worthy addition to the conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the concerns raised above.
