

This paper proposes a double sampling strategy to improve a multi-class to binary reduction approach for extreme classification, building upon the work in [16]. The authors introduce a (π, κ)-DS algorithm that oversamples small classes and subsamples large classes to address long-tailed distributions, followed by a dyadic transformation of the training data. Theoretical analysis provides new generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity, while experiments demonstrate scalability on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets with up to 100,000 classes.

The paper's strengths include a well-executed empirical evaluation showing significant improvements in training time and memory usage compared to baselines like OVA and M-SVM, which become intractable for large K. The method's practical relevance to extreme classification is commendable, and the theoretical contribution extends the analysis of [16] with local Rademacher complexity bounds. The experiments are comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and performance metrics including macro F1 and prediction time.

However, the novelty is limited as the approach largely extends [16] with double sampling, without fundamentally new conceptual insights. The prediction time remains substantial compared to tree-based methods, which is a critical practical concern despite faster training. The generalization bound interpretation is unclear, making it difficult to assess the theoretical contribution's significance. Additionally, mixing batch and online baselines creates uneven memory usage comparisons, potentially misleading readers about the method's practical advantages.

While the paper makes a solid contribution to extreme classification with demonstrated scalability and efficiency, its incremental nature and unresolved prediction time issue temper its significance. The work is technically sound with appropriate theoretical and empirical validation, but the limited novelty and practical limitations prevent it from advancing the state-of-the-art substantially. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, but the weak theoretical interpretation and practical constraints warrant careful consideration for acceptance at NIPS.
