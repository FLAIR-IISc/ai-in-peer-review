- Proposes (π, κ)-double sampling for multi-class-to-binary dyadic reduction that oversamples small classes, subsamples large ones, and reduces dyadic examples.  
- Builds on dyadic representation [16] and introduces novel generalization bounds via local fractional Rademacher complexity.  
- Empirical results show improved training time, memory usage, and predictive performance on DMOZ and Wikipedia, scaling to ~100K classes.  
- Demonstrates best trade-off among training time, memory, accuracy, and macro F1 compared to several baselines (OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse).  
- Strong practical contribution to extreme classification with thorough experimental validation and well-organized results.  
- Theoretical novelty is limited—mainly extends [16] with sampling techniques rather than a fundamentally new framework.  
- Interpretation and practical significance of the local Rademacher complexity bounds are unclear; link between theory and empirical gains is weak.  
- Theorem 2 is technically correct but lacks intuitive explanation and context about the sampling strategy’s optimization impact.  
- Prediction/inference time is relatively slow compared to tree-based methods with logarithmic prediction complexity, limiting real-world utility.  
- Memory comparisons between batch and online baselines are potentially misleading due to differing implementation strategies.  
- Insufficient theoretical justification for why the sampling strategy yields the observed empirical advantages.  
- Clarity is generally good for algorithms and experiments, but the theoretical section needs more intuitive exposition.  
- Significance is meaningful due to scalability to 100K classes and real-world relevance, though the contribution is somewhat incremental.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify theoretical benefits and address prediction-time limitations.
