## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, proposing a double sampling strategy built upon a multi-class to binary reduction technique [16]. The core idea is to mitigate the impact of long-tailed class distributions and reduce computational overhead by strategically sampling both instances and pairs during the binary conversion process. The authors provide theoretical justification via generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity and demonstrate promising empirical results on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia).

The paper is well-motivated, tackling a significant problem in machine learning with practical relevance. The proposed double sampling approach seems intuitively sound and the theoretical analysis, while building upon existing work, adds a generalization bound tailored to this specific sampling scheme. The experimental validation is comprehensive, showcasing performance gains in terms of training time, memory consumption, and predictive accuracy compared to several state-of-the-art methods. The scalability demonstrated on datasets with up to 100,000 classes is particularly noteworthy.

However, the novelty is somewhat limited. The work largely extends [16] by adding the double sampling and associated theoretical analysis. While valuable, it doesn't present a fundamentally new paradigm. A significant weakness is the relatively long prediction time reported, which the authors acknowledge. Despite faster training, slow prediction can be a critical drawback in many real-world applications. The interpretation and comparative advantage of the presented generalization bound are also unclear; its practical benefits beyond the theoretical contribution need further elaboration. 

Furthermore, the comparison with baselines is slightly skewed. Mixing batch and online baselines potentially makes memory usage comparisons uneven, as online methods inherently require less memory. This could be misleading when assessing the memory efficiency of the proposed method. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and practical problem (extreme classification).
*   Provides a sound theoretical analysis with generalization bounds.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on large-scale datasets.
*   Offers improvements in training time and memory usage.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – primarily an extension of existing work [16].
*   Slow prediction time compared to other methods.
*   Unclear interpretation of the generalization bound’s advantage.
*   Potentially misleading memory usage comparisons.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution that improves upon existing methods for extreme classification. However, the limitations regarding prediction time and the incremental nature of the novelty may warrant considering it as a strong but not groundbreaking paper. I would lean towards acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity of the generalization bound and the fairness of the memory usage comparisons in a revised version.
