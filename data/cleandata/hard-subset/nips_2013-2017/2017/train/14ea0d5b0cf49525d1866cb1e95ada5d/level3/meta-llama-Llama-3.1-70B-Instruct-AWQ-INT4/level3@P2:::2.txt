This paper addresses the problem of multi-class classification in the case where the number of classes is very large. The authors propose a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction strategy, which transforms the original multi-class problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples. The aim of the sampling strategy is to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions exhibited in majority of large-scale multi-class classification problems and to reduce the number of pairs of examples in the expanded data.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem it addresses is relevant to the NIPS community. The authors provide a clear and concise overview of the related work in the field, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches. The proposed method is novel and has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of large-scale multi-class classification.

The technical contribution of the paper is sound. The authors provide a new generalization bound using local fractional Rademacher complexity, which is an improvement over existing bounds. The sampling strategy is also well-motivated, and the authors provide a theoretical analysis of its effect on the generalization bounds.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed. The authors compare their method to several strong baselines and demonstrate its competitive performance. The results are presented in a clear and concise manner, making it easy to understand the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.

However, there are a few limitations to the paper. The core reduction idea largely follows Joshi et al. (2015), and the main novelty of the paper is the sampling/rebalancing technique. While this is a significant contribution, it would be helpful to discuss how the sample complexity/generalization bounds of the proposed method compare to those for non-transformed multi-class formulations.

Additionally, the paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm in Sec. 2.3 that limits comparisons to the k nearest class centroids. This is an important aspect of the proposed method, and a theoretical analysis would provide a more complete understanding of its strengths and limitations.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors have made a significant contribution to the field of large-scale multi-class classification. With some minor revisions to address the limitations mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
