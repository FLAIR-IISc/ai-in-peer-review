- Paper tackles extreme multi-class classification (up to 100k classes) via a double sampling strategy applied to the multi-class-to-binary reduction of Joshi et al. (2016).

- Proposed method: first rebalances class distribution by oversampling small classes and subsampling large ones, then reduces dyadic examples by sampling only κ classes per example.

- Theoretical contribution: consistency and generalization bounds derived using local fractional Rademacher complexity (Theorems 1–2).

- Empirical evaluation: comprehensive experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia (WIKI-Small, WIKI-50K, WIKI-100K) comparing training time, prediction time, memory, accuracy, and macro F1.

- Experimental results show clear advantages in training scalability and performance on very large datasets where OVA and M-SVM are intractable.

- Strength: addresses a highly relevant practical problem in text classification, recommendation, and ranking.

- Strength: thorough empirical validation across multiple large datasets and baselines (tree-based methods, OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse).

- Strength: theoretical analysis adds insight into consistency under the proposed sampling, especially regarding class imbalance and dyadic dependency.

- Weakness: novelty is limited—mostly an extension of Joshi et al. (2016) with sampling and incremental theoretical tightening.

- Weakness: Theorem 2 and its bound terms (α, β, r, etc.) lack clear interpretation and practical significance.

- Weakness: prediction time is slower than tree-based methods, undermining deployment practicality where inference speed matters most.

- Weakness: memory comparisons are misleading because experiments mix batch and online methods; tree-based methods' lower memory often stems from online/hierarchical design.

- Weakness: the paper does not sufficiently discuss how sampling affects learned representation quality or interpretability.

- Quality: technically sound with appropriate experiments, but theoretical novelty is limited.

- Clarity: well-organized and clearly written; pseudocode and experimental setup are detailed, but theoretical parameters need clearer explanation.

- Originality: incremental contribution—sampling to address imbalance in multi-class-to-binary reduction is not highly novel relative to prior work.

- Significance: practically useful for scaling training, but diminished by slower prediction and limited theoretical impact.

- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, strong empirical validation, clear computational training advantages, well-motivated sampling strategy.

- Arguments against acceptance: limited theoretical novelty, slower prediction than tree methods, unclear practical interpretation of bounds, and misleading memory comparisons.

- Overall: practical contribution with solid experiments but incremental novelty and unresolved prediction-time trade-offs make the paper less compelling for acceptance.
