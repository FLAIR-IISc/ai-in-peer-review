This paper addresses the challenging problem of large-scale multi-class classification, a common scenario in text repositories like Wikipedia and DMOZ. The authors propose a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction approach, aiming to mitigate the curse of dimensionality and class imbalance often encountered in such problems. The core idea is to reduce the number of pairs of examples used in the expansion step of the binary reduction, while also balancing the class distribution through oversampling of minority classes and subsampling of majority classes.  A theoretical analysis provides generalization bounds based on local fractional Rademacher complexity, demonstrating consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle despite the introduced sampling. Extensive experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets, with up to 100,000 classes, showcase the efficiency of the approach in terms of training/prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance, outperforming several state-of-the-art methods.

This work builds directly upon existing literature in extreme classification. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of the one-versus-all (OVA) approach [18] and reviews two main existing strategies: tree-based methods [4, 3, 6, 7, 9, 21, 5, 15] and label embedding approaches [11, 5, 19].  It also acknowledges binary reduction methods [1, 28, 16] as a foundational step for its proposed approach. The citation of relevant work in these areas is generally good, demonstrating an understanding of the current landscape.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a relevant and important problem:** Extreme multi-class classification is a significant challenge with numerous practical applications.
*   **Clear problem formulation and approach:** The paper clearly articulates the problem, outlines the proposed solution, and provides a well-defined theoretical justification.
*   **Strong empirical validation:** The experiments on large-scale datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in terms of both performance and efficiency. The authors show improvements in training and prediction time, as well as memory consumption, compared to several competitive baselines.
*   **Theoretical contribution:** The derivation of generalization bounds, even if not entirely novel, strengthens the theoretical foundation of the work and provides insights into the behavior of the algorithm.
*   **Comprehensive comparison:** The authors compare their approach to a good selection of recent and relevant baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited novelty:** The core idea builds heavily on the binary reduction approach presented in [16]. The double sampling strategy, while effective, represents an incremental improvement rather than a fundamentally new approach.
*   **Prediction time concerns:** While training time is improved, the paper acknowledges that prediction time remains a potential bottleneck.  In many real-world scenarios, prediction latency is critical, and the method’s performance in this area requires further scrutiny.  The relatively high prediction time compared to simpler methods could limit its practical applicability.
*   **Generalization bound interpretation:** The benefits and practical implications of the derived generalization bounds are not entirely clear. A more intuitive explanation of their significance would be helpful.  Specifically, how does this bound translate to concrete advantages in terms of sample complexity or robustness?
*   **Uneven baseline comparison:** Mixing batch-based (e.g., OVA, M-SVM) and online learning baselines (e.g., RecallTree) can lead to an unfair comparison of memory usage. Batch methods require storing the entire dataset in memory, while online methods typically process data sequentially, making the comparison less meaningful.
*   **Parameter sensitivity:** The performance of the method likely depends on careful tuning of the sampling parameters (π, κ). The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity and guidelines for choosing appropriate values.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in machine learning.
*   The proposed method demonstrably improves upon existing techniques in terms of training efficiency and, in many cases, predictive performance.
*   The theoretical analysis, while not groundbreaking, adds to the understanding of the algorithm's behavior.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is limited.
*   The prediction time performance requires further investigation.
*   The interpretation and practical value of the generalization bounds are unclear.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. While the novelty is incremental, the proposed double sampling strategy is effective in improving the efficiency and performance of existing methods. The comprehensive experimental evaluation and theoretical analysis provide strong support for the claims made by the authors. I recommend **acceptance**, but suggest the authors address the concerns regarding prediction time, generalization bound interpretation, and the fairness of the baseline comparison in a revised version.  A clearer discussion of parameter tuning would also be beneficial.
