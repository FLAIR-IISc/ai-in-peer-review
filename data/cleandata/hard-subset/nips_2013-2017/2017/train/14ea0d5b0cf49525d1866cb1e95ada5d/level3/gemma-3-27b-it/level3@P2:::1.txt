This paper addresses the problem of large-scale multi-class classification, a challenging area with growing importance in applications like text categorization. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction technique (building on prior work, particularly [16]), aiming to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions and reduce computational cost. They accompany this with theoretical analysis, providing generalization bounds, and demonstrate empirical effectiveness on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets.

The core idea – strategically sampling both instances and classes to balance the dataset and reduce the size of the binary problem – is intuitively appealing and directly tackles a key limitation of many existing extreme classification methods. The connection to prior work in multi-class to binary reduction ([1, 28, 16]) is clearly established, and the authors appropriately position their contribution as an improvement to existing techniques. The theoretical analysis, specifically the generalization bounds based on local fractional Rademacher complexity, is a valuable addition, although its practical implications and advantages over existing bounds aren’t fully elucidated. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear problem definition, well-motivated approach, and comprehensive experimental evaluation. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive performance in terms of accuracy and macro F1-measure, while also offering significant improvements in training and prediction time, and reduced memory consumption, especially when dealing with datasets containing tens of thousands of classes. The comparison against a range of established baselines (OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PD-Sparse) is thorough and provides convincing evidence of the method’s efficacy. The practical consideration of candidate selection during prediction to further reduce computational cost is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the novelty is incremental, the contribution feels largely like an engineering optimization of [16] rather than a fundamentally new approach. The benefits of the proposed generalization bound – using local Rademacher complexity – are not clearly articulated in terms of practical advantages. What specific insights does this bound provide that were previously inaccessible? A more detailed discussion comparing this bound to others in the field would be helpful. 

A key concern arises from the comparison of runtimes. Mixing batch-based methods (like M-SVM) with online methods (like RecallTree) makes direct comparisons of memory usage potentially misleading. Batch methods require storing the entire dataset, inherently using more memory. The comparison would be fairer if memory usage was evaluated in the context of per-example memory requirements. Furthermore, while the training time improvements are significant, the prediction time, though improved, remains a potential bottleneck, particularly for real-time applications. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and discuss potential strategies for further optimizing prediction speed.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some sections – particularly those detailing the theoretical analysis – are dense and may require significant effort from readers unfamiliar with Rademacher complexity. More intuitive explanations and illustrative examples would improve accessibility. The organization is logical, following a standard structure for machine learning papers.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and important problem in machine learning – extreme multi-class classification.
* Presents a practical and effective method for improving the scalability and performance of existing techniques.
* Includes a rigorous theoretical analysis, providing generalization bounds.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on challenging datasets.
* Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Incremental novelty – primarily an optimization of existing work.
* Limited discussion of the practical benefits of the derived generalization bound.
* Potential for unfair comparisons in runtime and memory usage due to the mixed nature of baselines.
* Prediction time could be a limitation in certain applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of extreme classification. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the proposed method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The practical improvements in scalability and performance are significant, and the paper addresses a challenging and important problem. I would recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised regarding the clarity of the theoretical analysis, a more nuanced discussion of runtime comparisons, and acknowledging the potential limitations of prediction time. Specifically, I suggest the authors add a discussion about the computational cost of the feature mapping and how it impacts the overall prediction time.
