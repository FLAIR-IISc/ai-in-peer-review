- Addresses extreme multi-class classification (up to 100k classes) via a novel multi-class to pairwise binary reduction with double sampling.  
- Proposes "Aggressive Double Sampling" (ADS): inverse-frequency class sampling plus drawing κ uniform adversarial examples per instance.  
- ADS dramatically reduces the number of dyadic training examples while rebalancing long-tailed class distributions.  
- Provides theoretical consistency guarantees and finite-sample risk bounds using fractional Rademacher complexity.  
- Carefully handles dependent dyadic examples and shifted empirical distribution from oversampling, using Janson's concentration inequalities.  
- Shows bias converges linearly with sample size in the provided theoretical bounds.  
- Evaluated on 5 text datasets (including DMOZ and Wikipedia) with up to 100k classes, demonstrating superior accuracy and F1 for >30k classes.  
- Achieves significant reductions in training time and memory consumption on large-scale problems.  
- Introduces an efficient prediction-time candidate selection using class centroids to avoid full pairwise enumeration.  
- Strength: clear motivation and practical focus on a significant extreme-classification problem.  
- Strength: strong theoretical foundation with novel generalization bounds for dependent dyadic data.  
- Strength: comprehensive experiments and practical implementation details supporting reproducibility.  
- Weakness: theoretical analysis is complex and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with Rademacher complexity.  
- Weakness: prediction time remains slower than tree-based methods, though mitigated by candidate selection.  
- Weakness: relationship to other sampling-based approaches could be clarified further.  
- Novelty: introduces a distinctive double sampling strategy that meaningfully advances prior multi-class-to-binary reductions.  
- Significance: practical and scalable solution for real-world extreme classification tasks; recommended for acceptance.
