This paper proposes a scalable reduction for extreme multiclass classification (up to ~100k classes) by converting the task to pairwise binary classification in a dyadic feature space and introducing an “Aggressive Double Sampling” scheme. The method oversamples rare classes with probability inversely proportional to empirical frequency and, for each retained example, uniformly samples κ adversarial classes to form pairs. Training repeats the sampling procedure to construct the final classifier. At prediction time, the authors avoid enumerating all class pairs by a heuristic candidate selection via class centroids. The work positions itself against tree-based methods (cascading errors) and label-embedding methods (break down when label matrices are not low-rank), and relates to prior dyadic reductions and ranking-style multiclass losses (e.g., AdaBoost.MR, multiclass SVM), as well as generalization bounds for interdependent data (fractional covers, Janson-type inequalities).

Quality: The theoretical contribution addresses two central difficulties: dependence among dyadic examples and the distribution shift induced by class reweighting. The proof strategy—splitting sums according to an induced dependency graph and invoking Janson’s inequalities, coupled with local/fractional Rademacher complexity—appears plausible and yields finite-sample bounds with a bias term governed by true vs. oversampled class probabilities that vanishes linearly with sample/resample sizes. Empirically, on five text datasets, the method attains superior accuracy and macro-F1 beyond ~30k classes, with markedly faster training and lower memory than strong baselines (OVA, PD-Sparse, tree methods), albeit with slower prediction.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, but notation is heavy and the dependence structure could be explained more intuitively. It would help to summarize the main assumptions for the bounds, explicitly connect the 0/1 analyses to the surrogate used in practice, and provide complexity formulas for training/inference.

Originality: While dyadic reductions are known, the specific double-sampling scheme with consistency guarantees under dependence and oversampling bias is novel and well-motivated. The use of Janson’s inequalities in this context is a nice touch.

Significance: The memory/runtime gains and state-of-the-art performance on the largest benchmarks make this practically relevant. The approach should be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on extreme text classification.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong empirical results at scale with large memory and training-time savings.
- Sound and plausible theory addressing dependence and distribution shift.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with code provided.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited ablation/sensitivity on π, κ and candidate-set size q; unclear trade-offs.
- Prediction slower; end-to-end top-k and wall-clock inference analysis would help.
- Comparisons to embedding baselines are argued away; a small-scale sanity check would strengthen claims.
- Method depends on text-specific dyadic features; generality beyond text is not demonstrated.

Suggestions: Add ablations for π, κ, q; report top-k accuracy and candidate coverage; clarify surrogate-loss training and its link to the bounds; and include a brief comparison to at least one embedding method on a smaller dataset.
