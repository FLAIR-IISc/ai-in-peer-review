This paper proposes a novel approach to reduce a multi-class classification problem to a binary classification problem, specifically designed for scenarios where the number of classes is very large. The authors introduce a double sampling strategy to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions and reduce the number of pairs of examples in the expanded data. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the proposed method, including generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity.

The main contributions of this paper are the introduction of a double sampling strategy to rebalance classes and reduce the transformed dataset size, and the provision of improved generalization analysis using new bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity. The authors also demonstrate the empirical competitiveness of their approach against several strong baselines.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their proposed method. The use of mathematical notation is consistent throughout the paper, making it easy to follow for readers familiar with the subject matter. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the authors provide a detailed description of the datasets, baselines, and evaluation measures used.

One of the strengths of this paper is its ability to address correlated transformed datasets, which is a common issue in multi-class classification problems. The authors' extension of Joshi et al. (2015) provides improved generalization analysis, making their approach more robust and reliable.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates the competitiveness of the proposed approach against several strong baselines. The authors show that their method is highly competitive in terms of time, memory, and performance measures, making it a suitable choice for large-scale multi-class classification problems.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, the authors do not provide a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm in Sec. 2.3, which limits comparisons to the k nearest class centroids. This is an important aspect of the paper, and providing a theoretical analysis would strengthen the authors' claims.

Secondly, the paper lacks a discussion comparing the sample complexity and generalization bounds to those for non-transformed multi-class formulations. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method and its advantages over other approaches.

In terms of originality, the paper's main novelty is the sampling/rebalancing technique. While the core reduction idea largely follows Joshi et al. (2015), the authors' extension and improvement of the generalization analysis make their approach distinct.

Overall, this paper provides a significant contribution to the field of multi-class classification, particularly in scenarios where the number of classes is very large. The authors' proposed method is well-analyzed, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates its competitiveness against several strong baselines.
