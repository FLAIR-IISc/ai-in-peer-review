Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles extreme multi-class classification (tens to ~100k classes) via a learning reduction that maps the problem to pairwise binary classification in a dyadic feature space. The core contribution is an aggressive double sampling (ADS) scheme that (i) rebalances classes by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones, and (ii) samples only κ adversarial labels per example to construct the dyadic pairs, thereby reducing both imbalance and data expansion. The authors provide finite-sample, distribution-shift-aware generalization guarantees showing that ERM on the doubly sampled dyadic data is consistent relative to multiclass ERM, with explicit bias terms induced by oversampling that vanish with sample size.

The paper argues persuasively that popular tree-based methods (e.g., FastXML, RecallTree, PfastReXML) suffer from cascading errors in top-down decisions, and that label-embedding methods (e.g., compressed sensing, WSABIE/LEML/SLEEC) degrade when the label matrix is not low-rank—both common in text with long-tailed class distributions. Instead, the proposed reduction learns a single dyadic classifier, trained via repeated resampling to construct the final pairwise dataset, and uses a heuristic candidate selection at prediction time based on nearest class centroids to avoid enumerating all K class pairs.

Technically, the analysis contends with two issues: (a) dyadic pairs are dependent (many pairs share the same example), and (b) oversampling alters the empirical class distribution. The proof splits sums according to the dependency graph and uses Janson-style concentration bounds for partially dependent variables, together with fractional graph covers and local Rademacher complexity, to obtain risk bounds. The bounds include bias factors α, β reflecting true-vs-oversampled class ratios; this bias decreases linearly with sample/resample sizes M and κ. Overall, the analysis appears plausible and aligns with prior dependent-data generalization work.

Experiments on five text datasets (DMOZ/Wikipedia, up to 100k classes) demonstrate superior accuracy and macro-F1 once K ≥ 30k, with faster training and substantially lower memory than strong baselines (OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, RecallTree/FastXML/PfastReXML). The main trade-off is slower prediction because dyadic scoring is costlier; a centroid-based candidate shortlist mitigates but does not eliminate this gap.

Quality
- Theory: The reduction’s consistency relative to multiclass ERM is clearly stated. Handling dependence via fractional covers and Janson inequalities is well-motivated; the use of local Rademacher complexity is appropriate for faster rates. The appearance of α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y transparently captures oversampling bias. While I did not fully verify every step, the approach is standard and the results look sound.
- Method: Aggressive Double Sampling is simple, scalable, and controlled by two interpretable parameters: π (class sampling frequencies, set inversely to empirical frequencies) and κ (number of uniformly drawn adversarial labels). Training repeats the sampling to build the final dyadic classifier, a practical way to cover negative classes without enumerating all K − 1 pairs.
- Empirics: Strong comparisons on large text benchmarks; using accuracy and macro-F1 is appropriate for long-tailed distributions. The method wins on the largest regimes, with meaningful runtime and memory advantages versus non-tree methods. The slower prediction is candidly acknowledged and partially addressed via centroid shortlisting.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The reduction, the ADS algorithm, and prediction via candidate selection are clearly presented, with helpful pseudocode. A few suggestions:
- Explicitly state the number of resampling rounds and show sensitivity to κ and π (and q, the number of candidates at test time).
- Clarify how φ’s 10 features generalize beyond text; a brief ablation or alternative φ would help.
- Summarize constants and key dependencies in the main risk bound to improve readability (e.g., highlight Mκ vs. m, α, β, K terms).

Originality
Combining an extreme-class multiclass-to-binary reduction with a principled double sampling scheme and a dependent-data generalization analysis appears novel. The reliance on local Rademacher complexity plus Janson’s inequality for the induced dependency graph is a thoughtful contribution. Empirically scaling dyadic reductions to ~100k classes is also notable.

Significance
Extreme classification is a central problem in text, tagging, and recommendation. The proposed approach offers an attractive memory/runtime profile during training and improved accuracy/macro-F1 at very large K, where many competitors struggle. Although prediction can be slower, the centroid shortlist is a practical compromise, and learned shortlist mechanisms could further reduce the gap. I expect practitioners and researchers to adopt or build on this reduction.

Pros
- Scalable, simple reduction with clear hyperparameters (π, κ); strong accuracy and macro-F1 for K ≥ 30k.
- Substantial reductions in training time and memory versus strong baselines; practical for 50k–100k classes.
- Theoretical guarantees that explicitly account for dependence and distribution shift; consistency relative to multiclass ERM.
- Sensible candidate selection to limit prediction cost; release of code and datasets.

Cons
- Prediction remains slower than tree-based methods; reliance on a heuristic centroid shortlist may limit recall in some domains.
- Results focus on text with a specific φ; generality to other modalities is not empirically demonstrated.
- Conversion of multilabel datasets to multiclass by replication may alter label frequencies and evaluation dynamics.
- Limited ablations: the paper would benefit from sensitivity to π, κ, and q; and from analysis of how many resampling rounds are needed.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide ablations varying κ, π, and the number of resampling rounds, plus wall-clock vs. performance trade-offs.
- Report sensitivity to q (candidate set size) and possibly compare centroid shortlisting to learned shortlist models or ANN.
- Include top-k accuracy and micro-F1 to complement macro-F1, especially for long-tailed regimes.
- Explore alternative φ or learned joint representations to show robustness beyond text.
- Discuss how the bias factors α, β behave under extreme imbalance and offer practical guidance for setting π.

Overall assessment
A strong and thoughtfully executed reduction for extreme classification, with a plausible and relevant theoretical analysis and compelling empirical benefits at very large K. The method is theoretically sound and practically useful, with low memory and training computation and superior performance on the largest datasets; the main trade-off is slower prediction. I recommend acceptance.
