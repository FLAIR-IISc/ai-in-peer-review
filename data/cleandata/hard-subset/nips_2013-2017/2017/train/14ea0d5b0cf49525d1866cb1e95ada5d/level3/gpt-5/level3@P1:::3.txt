Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme multiclass classification by reducing to binary classification over dyadic pairs (x, y) vs. (x, y′), optimizing a ranking-style surrogate of the multiclass loss. Building on earlier dyadic reductions (e.g., Weston & Watkins, AdaBoost.MR, Usunier et al.), the novelty is an aggressive double sampling scheme that (i) balances long-tailed label distributions by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones, and (ii) reduces the O(mK) blow-up by sampling only κ adversarial classes per example. The analysis addresses two complications relative to classic reductions: interdependence among dyadic pairs derived from the same base example, and the distribution shift induced by class-dependent sampling. Using graph-cover arguments, fractional/local Rademacher complexities, and Janson-style concentration for partly dependent variables, the authors derive finite-sample generalization bounds (Theorems 1–2) that include terms reflecting the sampling bias (α, β) and show ERM consistency under the double-sampled reduction. For prediction, the paper proposes candidate selection via nearest class centroids in the dyadic feature space to avoid scoring all K classes. Experiments on LSHTC DMOZ and Wikipedia (up to 100K classes) compare against OVA, M-SVM, tree-based (RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML), and PD-Sparse, reporting accuracy/Macro-F1, runtime, and memory. The proposed method is highly competitive in accuracy/MaF1 on the largest datasets while being markedly more memory- and time-efficient during training; prediction is slower unless candidate selection is used.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus on extreme K with heavy-tailed label distributions; the sampling design directly addresses both class imbalance and quadratic pair growth.
- Theory extends prior work on dyadic reductions with dependent examples, yielding bounds that plausibly capture variance/local complexity effects and the sampling-induced bias; ERM consistency under double sampling is valuable.
- Practical design choices (κ negative sampling; centroid-based candidate selection) produce substantial reductions in memory and training time without sacrificing accuracy at large K.
- Extensive experiments on recognized extreme text benchmarks with strong baselines; code and datasets are provided.
- Good use of Macro-F1, which is appropriate under imbalance.

Weaknesses
- The projection φ uses a small set of hand-crafted, IR-style features tailored to text. Generality to non-text domains (images, recommendation) is discussed but not empirically validated; learning φ (e.g., representation learning) is not explored.
- The theoretical bounds depend on α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy, which can be large under aggressive oversampling; practical guidance to control these constants and the resulting estimation bias is limited.
- Sensitivity analyses for κ (negatives per example), q (candidate set size), and the π schedule are not deeply explored. It would help to quantify the accuracy/runtime trade-offs and robustness.
- Prediction-time complexity remains higher than tree-based methods unless candidate selection is used; the candidate selection heuristic may miss true labels for rare classes, but this risk is not ablated.
- The multi-label-to-multiclass conversion by duplicating instances can skew distributional properties; discussion of its impact on Macro-F1 and fairness of comparisons is brief.
- Some related lines (e.g., tournaments/ECOC, importance-weighted reductions, NCE-style negative sampling) could be discussed more explicitly to situate the approach.

Quality
The algorithmic contribution is sound and well-motivated. The analysis appears technically careful (I did not verify all proofs line-by-line) and appropriately accounts for dependence and sampling shift, with reasonable finite-sample forms. Empirical evaluation is thorough on text; baselines are strong and hyperparameters tuned on held-out sets. A few missing ablations (for κ, q, π; and feature choices for φ) would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear but dense. The dyadic construction and the sampling steps are well specified, though the notation around graph covers and local fractional Rademacher complexities will be heavy for non-specialists; an intuition box for α/β and a brief complexity table (examples, pairs, memory) would help. More details on φ in the main text (beyond Table 1) and explicit guidance for setting κ, π, and q would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The combination of double sampling (class- and pair-level), the specific treatment of dependence via fractional/local Rademacher complexities, and the candidate selection heuristic is novel within the dyadic-reduction family. While negative sampling is common in ranking/contrastive learning, its application with accompanying consistency guarantees in extreme multiclass is a useful contribution.

Significance
High for extreme classification on text with very large K. The demonstrated memory and runtime gains over OVA/M-SVM, and the competitive accuracy over trees and PD-Sparse on 50K–100K classes, suggest practical impact. Extending to learned φ and non-text domains could broaden significance further.

Pros
- Scalable reduction with strong empirical wins at large K.
- Addresses imbalance and pair explosion jointly.
- Theoretical guarantees under dependence and sampling shift.
- Public code and datasets; clear baselines.

Cons
- Heavily text-specific feature mapping; limited validation beyond text.
- Limited guidance on tuning κ, π, and q; missing sensitivity plots.
- Prediction still slower than trees without candidate selection; candidate selection risks under-coverage.
- Bounds involve potentially large α/β; practical control not fully discussed.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you report accuracy/MaF1 vs. κ and training time/memory vs. κ to show the trade-off? Similarly for q in candidate selection vs. prediction time.
2) Did you try importance weighting by 1/πy during training to correct the sampling bias and potentially reduce α in practice?
3) How often is the true class absent from the candidate set Yx? Any recall@q measurements and their effect on final accuracy?
4) How does the method perform with a learned φ (e.g., linear joint embedding) vs. the 10 handcrafted features?
5) Can you include non-text datasets or at least synthetic experiments to isolate the effect of long-tail imbalance?

Minor comments and suggestions
- Clarify whether Sπ is drawn once or via repeated resampling; if repeated, averaging across resamples or ensembling could be discussed.
- Provide an explicit complexity summary: training pairs O(κ|Sπ|), memory footprint, and prediction cost with/without candidate selection.
- A brief discussion of error-correcting tournaments and importance-weighted reductions would better position the contribution.

Overall assessment
A strong, well-motivated method with solid theory and compelling empirical results in the extreme-K regime. With additional analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and broader validation of φ, this would be an easy accept; as is, I lean toward acceptance given the practical impact and theoretical care.
