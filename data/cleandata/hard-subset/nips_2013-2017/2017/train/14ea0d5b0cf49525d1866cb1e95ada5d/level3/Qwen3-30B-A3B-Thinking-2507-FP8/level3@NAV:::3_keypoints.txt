- Proposes "Aggressive Double Sampling," a learning reduction for extreme classification (up to ~100k classes) that converts the task into pairwise binary (dyadic) classification.  
- Addresses two main limits of prior work: tree-based methods' cascading errors and label-embedding methods' failure when label matrices aren't low-rank.  
- Samples classes inversely proportional to empirical frequency to handle class imbalance.  
- Constructs a dyadic classifier using adversarial examples to improve pairwise discrimination.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees, proving consistency under empirical risk minimization.  
- Uses Janson's concentration inequalities to manage dependent dyadic examples and oversampling in the analysis.  
- Derives a finite-sample risk bound showing bias decreases linearly with sample size.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on five text datasets, outperforming OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, etc., for problems with >30k classes.  
- Achieves faster training and substantially lower memory usage while keeping prediction time competitive.  
- Notes slower prediction as a limitation, but argues the trade-off is acceptable for large-scale applications.  
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound but complex.  
- Experiments are thorough and well-designed, supporting claimed benefits.  
- Clarity is good with clear explanations of sampling and theory, though notation could be simplified for broader accessibility.  
- Originality and significance are strong: the double sampling strategy is a meaningful advance and likely to influence future extreme classification research.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution with practical utility and theoretical rigor; recommendation for acceptance.
