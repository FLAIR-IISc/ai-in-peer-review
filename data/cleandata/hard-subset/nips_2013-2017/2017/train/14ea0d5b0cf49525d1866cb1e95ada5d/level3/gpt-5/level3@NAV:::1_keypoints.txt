- Core idea: double sampling—class-balanced resampling and κ negative-class sampling—to reduce dyadic expansion for extreme multiclass problems.  
- Theoretical claim: derives generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexities with fractional covers to handle dependent pairs and distribution shift.  
- Empirical setting: evaluated on LSHTC DMOZ/Wikipedia text datasets up to 100K classes.  
- Empirical results: competitive accuracy/MaF1 while substantially reducing training time and memory versus OVA, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding baselines.  
- Prediction: uses nearest-centroid candidate selection, but prediction latency remains higher than logarithmic-time tree methods.  
- Practical benefit: useful where training cost and memory are bottlenecks for large-text extreme classification.  
- Theoretical clarity issue: role of α and β (functions of π and class proportions) and conditions for faster rates are hard to interpret in practice.  
- Missing theoretical comparison: no contrast of local-complexity bound against the [16] bound to demonstrate practical advantage.  
- Missing quantitative guidance: no clear trade-off analysis showing how κ and π affect risk versus computation.  
- Experimental comparison issue: mixes batch and online implementations, potentially biasing memory and latency comparisons.  
- Clarity gaps: insufficient explanation of how π is set beyond “inverse class frequency,” and how q (candidate size) and κ are tuned.  
- Computational cost question: unclear cost of computing and maintaining centroids used for candidate selection.  
- Reproducibility suggestion: an ablation study on π, κ, and q is needed for insight and reproducibility.  
- Originality and significance: incremental extension of [16] with a sensible, practically useful sampling scheme; impact limited by slower prediction and theoretical opacity.
