This paper proposes a novel approach to multi-class classification problems with a large number of classes. The authors introduce a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction strategy, which transforms the original multi-class problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples. The sampling strategy aims to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions and reduce the number of pairs of examples in the expanded data.

The main contributions of this paper are the introduction of a new sampling strategy, which is theoretically grounded and empirically evaluated. The authors provide new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity, which shows that the empirical risk minimization principle over the transformation of the sampled training set remains consistent. The experiments on large-scale text classification datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in terms of training and prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance.

Strengths of the paper:

* The authors propose a novel approach to multi-class classification problems with a large number of classes, which is theoretically grounded and empirically evaluated.
* The double sampling strategy is effective in reducing the curse of long-tailed class distributions and improving the predictive performance.
* The experiments on large-scale text classification datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Weaknesses of the paper:

* The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm in Section 2.3 that limits comparisons to the k nearest class centroids.
* The paper does not provide a discussion comparing the sample complexity/generalization bounds to those for non-transformed multi-class formulations.
