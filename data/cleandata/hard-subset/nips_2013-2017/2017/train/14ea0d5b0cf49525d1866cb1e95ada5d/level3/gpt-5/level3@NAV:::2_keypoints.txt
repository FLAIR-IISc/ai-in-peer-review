- Reduction of extreme multiclass to binary dyadic (input, class) pairs via φ and learning f∘φ, tackling dependencies from K−1 comparisons.  
- Improved generalization analysis for dependent dyadic data using fractional Rademacher complexity and fractional covers.  
- Aggressive double sampling: per-class rebalancing to address long-tailed labels and random sampling of comparison classes to shrink transformed dataset.  
- The sampling scheme is shown not to significantly degrade theoretical bounds.  
- Empirical results on DMOZ/Wikipedia (10k–100k labels) show competitive accuracy/MaF1 with substantial time and memory improvements.  
- Candidate-selection prediction: restrict scoring to q nearest class centroids to speed inference.  
- Theorems 1–2 appear technically sound and meaningfully extend prior dependent-data analyses.  
- No theoretical treatment of the q-nearest-centroid approximation; could alter decision rules and consistency.  
- Dependence of bounds on α, β, K, κ, and class imbalance is not transparently discussed and lacks practical guidance.  
- Experiments are appropriate but would be strengthened by ablations/sensitivity analyses for π, κ, and q.  
- Clarity is good overall, but notation around fractional covers and local complexities is dense and needs more intuition or a concrete example.  
- Original contribution is mainly the double sampling/rebalancing and refined bounds under dependence; the reduction framework builds on prior work.  
- Missing comparison of sample complexity to native multiclass formulations (e.g., OVA/multiclass SVM).  
- Significance: addresses an important extreme-K regime with demonstrable efficiency gains; broader applicability depends on choice of φ.  
- Pros: solid dependent-data theory, practical sampling for scalability, strong empirical efficiency with competitive accuracy.  
- Cons: no theory for the approximate prediction step, limited bound comparisons with native multiclass methods, and missing ablations/sensitivity analyses.
