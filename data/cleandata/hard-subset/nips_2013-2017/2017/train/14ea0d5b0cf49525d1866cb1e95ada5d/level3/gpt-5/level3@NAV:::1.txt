This paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by building on the multiclass-to-binary dyadic reduction of [16]. The core idea is a double sampling scheme: (i) class-balanced resampling of the training set to mitigate long-tailed label distributions, and (ii) κ negative-class sampling per example to shrink the dyadic expansion. The authors derive generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexities under dependent pairs via fractional covers, arguing ERM consistency despite distribution shift and dependence. Empirically, on LSHTC DMOZ/Wikipedia text datasets with up to 100K classes, the method shows competitive accuracy/MaF1, reduced training time and memory relative to OVA, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding baselines; prediction uses nearest-centroid candidate selection.

Quality: The empirical study is thorough on text datasets and demonstrates strong training-time and memory savings with solid predictive performance. However, the theoretical contribution is hard to interpret in practice: the role of α and β (functions of π and class proportions) and the conditions under which local complexity yields faster rates are not contrasted against the [16] bound, nor is there guidance on how κ and π quantitatively trade off risk vs. computation. Prediction latency appears to remain comparatively high—even with candidate selection—relative to logarithmic-time tree methods, which matters in many extreme classification applications. The comparison mixes batch and online implementations, making memory usage comparisons potentially uneven.

Clarity: Generally well organized, but some choices need clearer exposition: how π is set beyond “inverse class frequency,” how q (candidate size) and κ are tuned, and the computational cost of computing/maintaining centroids. An ablation on π, κ, and q would aid reproducibility and insight.

Originality: Incremental. The work largely extends [16] with double sampling and a local Rademacher bound. The sampling idea is sensible and practically useful, but the methodological novelty is limited.

Significance: Extreme classification is an important NIPS topic. The approach appears practically valuable for large text problems where training cost and memory are bottlenecks, though its slower prediction may limit adoption in latency-sensitive settings.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, practical setting (extreme classification).
- Clear, effective sampling strategy with empirical gains in training time and memory.
- Scales to 100K classes with strong accuracy/MaF1.
- Public code and thorough comparisons.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited novelty beyond [16]; unclear practical advantage of the new bound.
- Prediction time remains relatively long vs. tree methods.
- Mixed batch/online baselines complicate memory/latency comparisons.
- Missing ablations and clearer guidance on π, κ, q and bound interpretation.
