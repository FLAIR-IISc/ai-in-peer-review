This paper addresses the challenging problem of multi-class classification with a very large number of classes, a scenario increasingly common in applications like text categorization. The authors propose a double sampling strategy applied on top of a multi-class to binary reduction technique. This aims to mitigate the class imbalance often seen in such datasets and reduce the computational burden of creating all possible pairwise comparisons. The core contribution lies in demonstrating, theoretically, that this aggressive sampling does not compromise the consistency of empirical risk minimization, supported by experimental results on large-scale datasets.

The work builds upon previous approaches to extreme multi-class classification. The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature, contrasting its approach with tree-based methods [4, 3, 6, 7, 9, 21, 5, 15] and label embedding techniques [11, 5, 19]. It correctly identifies the limitations of these methods, particularly the challenges of balancing tree structures or the potential for low-rank assumptions to fail. The authors also acknowledge the related work on binary reduction via dyadic representations [1, 28, 16], and importantly, highlight the computational cost of constructing these representations. The proposed method directly addresses this cost. The connection to Joshi et al. (2015) [16] is also clearly stated; this work extends that prior work with improved generalization analysis.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The derivation of generalization bounds, using local fractional Rademacher complexity, is a significant contribution and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying statistical learning principles. The authors clearly articulate how their analysis accounts for the dependencies introduced by the transformation and the sampling procedure.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant, and the experimental evaluation on large-scale datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia) demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the approach. The reported improvements in training and prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance are compelling.
* **Novel Sampling Strategy:** The (π, κ)-double sampling is a clever idea that directly tackles the issues of class imbalance and computational cost. The combination of per-class sampling to rebalance the data and random sampling of comparison classes to reduce dataset size is well-motivated and effective.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear and consistent, and the explanations of the theoretical concepts are accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Novelty:** While the sampling strategy and generalization bounds are valuable contributions, the core idea of reducing multi-class classification to binary classification via pairwise comparisons isn't entirely novel, building directly on prior work like Joshi et al. (2015). The authors acknowledge this, but it’s important to keep in mind when assessing the overall impact.
* **Missing Theoretical Analysis of Candidate Selection:** The prediction algorithm described in Section 2.3, which utilizes candidate selection based on nearest centroids, lacks a corresponding theoretical analysis.  The impact of this approximation on the overall generalization performance isn't discussed. This omission weakens the overall theoretical completeness of the work.
* **Lack of Sample Complexity Comparison:** The paper would benefit from a discussion comparing the sample complexity requirements of the proposed method to those of standard multi-class classification formulations *without* the dyadic transformation.  Understanding how the sampling affects the overall data efficiency would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors tune hyperparameters, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of π and κ would be helpful.  



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in machine learning.
* It presents a novel and effective sampling strategy that improves both the computational efficiency and predictive performance of multi-class classification.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a strong justification for the proposed approach.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the method.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The core idea of binary reduction is not entirely novel.
* The theoretical analysis of the candidate selection step is missing.
* The comparison to standard multi-class sample complexity is absent.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. The proposed sampling strategy is a practical and effective way to address the challenges of class imbalance and computational cost. The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the method, and the experimental evaluation demonstrates its effectiveness. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the combination of theoretical rigor, practical relevance, and clear presentation makes this a worthy addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the minor weaknesses identified above, particularly providing some insight into the theoretical justification (or empirical sensitivity analysis) of the candidate selection approach.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
