This paper tackles extreme multiclass classification by reducing to a binary dyadic learning problem coupled with an aggressive double sampling scheme. The core idea is to avoid constructing all O(mK) dyadic pairs by (i) resampling the training set with class-dependent probabilities πk that oversample rare classes and subsample frequent ones, and (ii) for each retained example, sampling only κ “adversarial” negative classes. Learning is then performed in a joint example–class feature space φ(x, y) with a single binary classifier predicting whether y is preferred over a sampled y′. The paper provides generalization bounds for dependent dyadic examples using fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities, arguing ERM consistency despite the induced dependencies and class-distribution shift. For prediction, a candidate selection step restricts evaluation to q nearest-centroid classes. Experiments on LSHTC DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets (up to 100K classes) show strong accuracy/Macro-F1 with markedly reduced training time and memory compared to OVA/M-SVM and competitive performance relative to PD-Sparse and tree/embedding baselines.

Relation to prior work. The reduction to dyadic binary classification follows prior work (e.g., [16], [28]), and the ranking-style multiclass loss is standard (e.g., multiclass SVM [29], AdaBoost.MR [24]). The sampling design addresses the long-tailed label distributions emphasized in extreme classification (e.g., [2]), and the candidate selection at prediction time echoes nearest-centroid heuristics often used to curb inference cost. On the theory side, the use of fractional covers for dependency ([14]) and local Rademacher complexities ([22]) extends the analysis of [16], which used fractional Rademacher complexity ([25]). Comparisons include contemporary tree-based and embedding baselines (e.g., FastXML [21], RecallTree [9]) and PD-Sparse [30].

Quality. Methodologically, the (π, κ)-DS strategy is simple and effective: πk ∝ 1/µk addresses class imbalance, while κ controls dyadic expansion. The theoretical analysis is technically sound in spirit: Theorem 1 provides a dependency-aware bound for the full dyadic expansion, and Theorem 2 establishes a bound under double sampling with explicit dependence on α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy. However:
- The interpretation of the bounds is not fully clarified. When πk heavily rebalances rare classes, α and β may be large, making the constants potentially loose; guidance on choosing π, κ to trade off variance and bias is missing. The “faster rates” suggested by local Rademacher analysis are not connected to measurable improvements (no empirical study of convergence vs sample size).
- The link between the optimized ranking loss and top-1 multiclass error is indirect; a calibration or regret transfer discussion would strengthen claims about multiclass accuracy.
- Ablations isolating the contribution of oversampling vs κ-negative subsampling vs candidate selection (q) are absent; this makes it hard to attribute gains.
- The evaluation mixes batch (OVA, M-SVM) and online/tree methods (RecallTree, FastXML), which complicates runtime/memory comparisons; peak vs model memory and precomputation costs are not separated. The conversion of multi-label LSHTC2 to multiclass by instance replication may also distort class skew and should be discussed.

Clarity. The paper is generally well written, with clear problem setup, algorithms, and a useful description of φ for text (10 features inspired by learning-to-rank). Suggestions for improved clarity:
- Explicitly state whether oversampling is with or without replacement and how duplicates are handled during dyadic construction.
- Provide more intuition for α, β in Theorem 2 and a paragraph on practical selection of π, κ, q with sensitivity plots.
- Summarize key steps of the proofs in the main text to convey why local complexities help under dependency.
- Clarify computational costs of candidate selection (distance to centroids vs full φ computation).

Originality. The work is an incremental but meaningful extension of [16]: the double sampling scheme is practically motivated and the theoretical treatment leverages known tools (fractional covers, local Rademacher) in a new combination for this setting. The representation and candidate selection are pragmatic rather than novel.

Significance. Extreme classification is important, and the reported results on up to 100K classes are compelling. The approach offers a useful accuracy–training-time–memory trade-off and is attractive when OVA/M-SVM are infeasible. However, prediction remains linear in q and is generally slower than logarithmic-time tree methods, which can be a limiting factor in latency-sensitive applications. The focus on text with a handcrafted φ restricts the immediate generality; demonstrating portability to other domains (e.g., images, recommender tags) would broaden impact.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses a practically important setting (long-tailed extreme classification) with a simple, scalable reduction.
- Strong empirical results on large benchmarks, with favorable training time and memory.
- Dependency-aware generalization bounds under double sampling; clear ERM consistency message.
- Candidate selection reduces inference cost without heavy engineering.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited novelty relative to [16]; main contributions are sampling heuristics and an adaptation of known complexity tools.
- Bounds have opaque constants (α, β) and limited practical guidance; no empirical/theoretical link to improved rates.
- Prediction time is still relatively high compared to tree-based methods; evaluation under strict latency constraints is missing.
- Comparison protocol mixes heterogeneous training regimes; memory/runtime comparisons may not be apples-to-apples.
- No ablations to disentangle the roles of π, κ, and q.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add ablation studies over π (rebalancing strength), κ (negatives per example), and q (candidate size), showing accuracy–time–memory trade-offs and sensitivity to class skew.
- Report peak vs model memory, preprocessing time, and training wall-clock with standardized stopping criteria; include throughput/latency for prediction.
- Provide regret-transfer or calibration results from pairwise ranking loss to multiclass top-1 error.
- Evaluate on a non-text extreme classification task with an alternative φ (or learned φ) to test generality.
- Compare to additional sampled-negative approaches and ECOC baselines; discuss connections to sampled softmax in large-output models.

Overall: A solid, well-executed piece with practical value in extreme multiclass text classification. The contributions are incremental but useful; with stronger ablations and clearer theoretical guidance, this would be a clear accept. As is, I lean toward borderline accept for its strong empirical showing and sensible methodology.
