- Reduction: tackles extreme multiclass by reducing to binary dyadic pairs with g(x,y)=f(φ(x,y)), addressing dependence among K−1 dyads per example.  
- Main contributions: double sampling (class rebalancing + κ adversarial-class subsampling), new dependence-aware generalization bounds using fractional/local Rademacher complexity, and an efficient text-classification instantiation with centroid candidate selection.  
- Double sampling: oversamples rare classes and subsamples frequent ones, and samples only κ ≪ K negative classes per example to shrink training data.  
- Theory: new generalization bounds for dependent dyads building on fractional covers and local Rademacher complexity, with explicit dependence on κ, K, and rebalancing probabilities π.  
- Empirics: competitive accuracy and macro-F1 on large benchmarks (up to 100K classes) with substantial training/prediction time and memory gains vs. OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree.  
- Practicality: simple, fast, memory-efficient pipeline that is easy to implement and likely to be adopted as a baseline.  
- Theoretical novelty: meaningful extension by incorporating local fractional Rademacher complexity in the dependent dyadic setting.  
- Sampling motivation: well-motivated for long-tail label distributions and shown to preserve consistency in bounds (Theorem 2).  
- Code/datasets: provided, aiding reproducibility.  
- Incremental novelty: core dyadic reduction and φ-based scoring are standard; main novelty is in sampling/rebalancing and tightened bounds.  
- Candidate selection: nearest-centroid, top-q prediction heuristic lacks theoretical analysis and may bias comparisons when q ≪ K.  
- Comparative theory gap: limited discussion contrasting sample complexity with non-reduction multiclass methods (Crammer–Singer, Weston–Watkins) or ECOC/log-time methods.  
- Domain generality: empirical evaluation limited to text with a hand-crafted 10-D φ; no demonstration of generality to other domains.  
- Sampling details unclear: unclear whether Sπ is sampled with/without replacement, how target sample size M is set, and sensitivity to π and κ.  
- Theoretical quality: dependence-aware analysis appears correct; need clearer discussion of α = max η/π behavior when π ∝ 1/η and possible large constants under severe imbalance.  
- Empirical quality: thorough comparisons to contemporaneous baselines, but lacking ablations isolating effects of (i) class rebalancing, (ii) κ-subsampling, and (iii) candidate selection.  
- Missing evaluation: no characterization of candidate-selection recall@q or its effect on final generalization bound.  
- Clarity: generally well-organized and clean presentation, but notation is dense and some symbols (μk vs ηk) need clearer definitions.  
- Algorithm clarity: Section 2.3 should state whether sampling is with/without replacement, define M, define q, and discuss choosing q relative to κ.  
- Originality: moderate—combines known tools (dyadic reductions, fractional complexity) but contributes useful integration of local complexity and pragmatic double sampling.  
- Significance: high practical impact for extreme text classification due to efficiency and accuracy trade-offs; theory helps explain when aggressive sampling is safe.  
- Acceptance arguments: scalable practical method with open-source code, strong empirical results, and meaningful theoretical support for double sampling.  
- Rejection arguments: incremental relative to prior dyadic work, inadequate theory for candidate-selection, limited empirical scope and missing ablations.  
- Key questions for authors: provide sensitivity studies for π and κ and ablation showing each sampling step's effect.  
- Key questions: analyze candidate selection—what q ensures high recall of the true class and can bounds incorporate candidate-recall?  
- Key questions: clarify Sπ sampling protocol, how M is chosen, and expected α under π ∝ 1/η.  
- Key questions: compare qualitatively to non-reduction multiclass sample complexities and report robustness to different φ choices.  
- Minor issues: several typos/hyphenation errors, stray text fragments, and inconsistent symbol definitions.  
- Reproducibility note: positive due to code/datasets and appendix hyperparameters; request that scripts reproduce main tables and include default π, κ, q settings.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — solid empirical and theoretical contribution with medium confidence, but needs stronger treatment of prediction heuristic theory and ablations.
