This paper tackles extreme multiclass classification by reducing the task to binary classification over dyadic (input, class) pairs via a mapping φ and learning a score function f∘φ. It addresses a key obstacle in such reductions: the K−1 comparisons per example create strong dependencies among transformed samples, invalidating i.i.d.-based bounds. Building on Joshi et al. (2015), the authors develop improved generalization analysis using fractional Rademacher complexity and local complexity tools for dependent data (via fractional covers), and propose an aggressive double sampling scheme: (i) per-class sampling to rebalance long-tailed label distributions, and (ii) random sampling of comparison classes to shrink the transformed dataset. They show the sampling does not significantly degrade bounds and provide empirical evidence on DMOZ/Wikipedia (10k–100k labels) that the approach is competitive in accuracy/MaF1 while substantially improving time and memory. A candidate-selection prediction procedure further restricts scoring to the q nearest class centroids.

Quality: The theoretical development for the dependent setting is careful and extends prior analyses in a meaningful way; Theorems 1–2 are plausible and technically sound at a high level. However, the paper lacks a theoretical treatment of the approximate prediction scheme (Sec. 2.3): limiting to q nearest centroids could alter the decision rule and consistency. The dependence of bounds on α, β, K, κ, and class imbalance could be discussed more transparently, with practical guidance. Experiments are solid and fairly chosen, though more ablations (varying π, κ, q) would strengthen claims.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Some notational density around fractional covers and local complexities could be lightened with intuition and a concrete dependency graph example. Minor editorial issues aside, the paper is reproducible.

Originality: The reduction framework follows prior work; the main novelty lies in the double sampling/rebalancing and the refined generalization bounds under dependence. Positioning relative to non-transformed multiclass formulations (e.g., OVA/multiclass SVM) in terms of sample complexity is missing.

Significance: Addresses an important practical regime (extreme K) with demonstrable gains in efficiency and competitive accuracy on standard text benchmarks. Broader applicability beyond text depends on φ; a brief discussion would help.

Pros for acceptance:
- Sound and nontrivial generalization analysis for dependent dyadic data.
- Practical double sampling that improves scalability without harming bounds.
- Strong empirical efficiency with competitive accuracy on large K.

Cons for acceptance:
- No theory for the q-nearest-centroid prediction step.
- Limited discussion comparing bounds to native multiclass formulations.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses for π, κ, and q.
