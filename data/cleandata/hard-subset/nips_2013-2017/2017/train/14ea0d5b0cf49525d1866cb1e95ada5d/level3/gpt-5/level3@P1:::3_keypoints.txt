- Tackles extreme multiclass classification via dyadic reductions and introduces a double sampling scheme to handle class imbalance and reduce pair explosion.  
- Novelty: aggressive double sampling that oversamples rare classes, subsamples frequent ones, and samples only κ negative classes per example.  
- Theoretical contribution: finite-sample generalization bounds accounting for dependent dyadic pairs and sampling-induced distribution shift, using graph covers and fractional/local Rademacher complexities; ERM consistency shown.  
- Prediction strategy: candidate selection via nearest class centroids in dyadic feature space to avoid scoring all K classes.  
- Empirically competitive in accuracy/Macro-F1 on large text benchmarks (up to 100K classes) while being much more memory- and time-efficient during training.  
- Prediction can be slower than tree-based methods unless candidate selection is used.  
- Clear problem focus and sampling design directly address heavy-tailed label distributions and quadratic pair growth.  
- Theory plausibly captures variance/local complexity and sampling bias; technical analysis appears careful.  
- Practical design choices (κ negatives, centroid candidate selection) yield substantial memory and training time savings at large K.  
- Extensive experiments with strong baselines and provided code/datasets; appropriate use of Macro-F1 for imbalance.  
- Feature mapping φ is hand-crafted, IR-style, and text-specific; generality to non-text domains is not empirically validated.  
- Theoretical bounds depend on α and β (sampling bias factors) which can be large under aggressive oversampling; limited practical guidance to control them.  
- Sensitivity analyses for κ, q (candidate size), and π schedule are limited, reducing insight into accuracy/runtime trade-offs and robustness.  
- Candidate selection heuristic may miss true labels for rare classes; no ablation on recall@q or its effect on final accuracy.  
- Multi-label-to-multiclass conversion by duplicating instances could skew distributions; discussion of its impact on Macro-F1 and fairness of comparisons is brief.  
- Related methods (tournaments/ECOC, importance-weighted reductions, NCE negative sampling) are not thoroughly discussed.  
- Quality: algorithm and analysis are sound; some missing ablations (κ, q, π, and φ features) would strengthen empirical claims.  
- Clarity: generally clear but dense; heavy notation around graph covers and complexities may hinder non-specialists; more intuition and complexity tables would help.  
- Originality: combination of double sampling, dependence-aware theory, and candidate selection is novel within dyadic-reduction literature.  
- Significance: high for extreme text classification at very large K; practical impact shown, broader impact depends on learned φ and non-text validation.  
- Pros summarized: scalable reduction, joint handling of imbalance and pair explosion, theoretical guarantees under dependence, public code/datasets.  
- Cons summarized: text-specific φ, limited tuning guidance and sensitivity analysis, prediction slower without candidate selection, bounds involve large α/β.  
- Requested experiments/questions: report accuracy/MaF1 vs. κ and training time/memory vs. κ; report prediction time vs. q; try importance weighting by 1/πy; measure how often true class is absent from candidate set (recall@q); compare learned φ vs. handcrafted features; include non-text or synthetic imbalance experiments.  
- Minor suggestions: clarify whether Sπ is drawn once or repeatedly; provide explicit complexity summary (training pairs, memory, prediction cost); discuss error-correcting tournaments and importance-weighted reductions.  
- Overall assessment: strong, well-motivated method with solid theory and compelling empirical results for extreme-K text; acceptance recommended if additional hyperparameter sensitivity and broader φ validation are added.
