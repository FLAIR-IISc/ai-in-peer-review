This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, proposing a novel approach based on aggressive double sampling applied to a multi-class to binary reduction strategy. The core idea is to mitigate the issues of imbalanced class distributions and computational cost associated with creating all pairwise comparisons, common in existing methods. The authors argue convincingly that tree-based methods suffer from error propagation and label embedding techniques struggle with non-low-rank label matrices, establishing a clear motivation for their work.

The proposed (π, κ)-DS sampling method is well-motivated and aims to balance class representation and reduce the number of dyadic examples. A significant strength is the theoretical analysis, attempting to provide generalization bounds despite the inherent challenges posed by dependent examples and the distributional shift introduced by oversampling. The use of local Rademacher complexity and graph covering concepts, while complex, demonstrates a rigorous attempt to ground the method in sound theoretical principles. The experimental evaluation on five text datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia collections) is thorough, showcasing significant improvements in accuracy, F1-score, training time, and memory usage, particularly for problems with >30k classes. The identified tradeoff of slower prediction time is honestly acknowledged.

The paper's originality lies in the combination of aggressive sampling with the dyadic reduction framework and the accompanying theoretical analysis. While multi-class to binary reductions are not new [1, 16, 28], the specific sampling strategy, its theoretical justification, and empirical validation on large-scale datasets represent a valuable contribution. The algorithm’s parameters – class sampling frequency and the number of adversarial examples – provide a degree of control over the bias-variance tradeoff. The introduction of a candidate selection heuristic for prediction time optimization is a practical addition.

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-written, though the theoretical sections (particularly around Theorem 1 & 2) are dense and require careful reading. Providing more intuition behind the mathematical derivations, potentially through intermediate steps or simpler examples, could enhance understanding. The notation is consistent, and the organization is logical. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented effectively in both tables and figures.

Regarding significance, this work addresses a practical and increasingly important problem in machine learning – extreme classification. The demonstrated improvements in performance and scalability, especially concerning memory usage, are substantial and could facilitate applications that were previously infeasible. The method’s ability to handle imbalanced datasets effectively is a notable benefit. It’s likely that other researchers and practitioners will find these ideas valuable and build upon them.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong empirical results:** Consistently outperforms baselines on large datasets, demonstrating practical value.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** Attempts a rigorous theoretical analysis with generalization bounds, even with the inherent complexities.
*   **Addresses a significant problem:** Tackles the challenges of extreme classification with a novel and effective approach.
*   **Scalability:** Demonstrates substantial improvements in memory usage and training time, enabling applications on larger datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical complexity:** The theoretical analysis is challenging to follow and requires further scrutiny (although the reviewer found it plausible).
*   **Slower prediction time:** Acknowledged limitation that may be a concern for some applications.
*   **Dependency on heuristics:** The candidate selection heuristic, while practical, is not theoretically grounded.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a technically sound and practically useful method for extreme classification. While the theoretical analysis is complex, the authors make a compelling case for the consistency of their approach. The empirical results are impressive, and the method’s scalability is a significant advantage. The slower prediction time is a drawback, but the candidate selection heuristic provides a reasonable mitigation strategy. I recommend acceptance.
