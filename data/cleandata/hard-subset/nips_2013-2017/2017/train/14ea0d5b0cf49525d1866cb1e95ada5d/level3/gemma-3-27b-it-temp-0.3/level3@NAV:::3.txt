## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, proposing a novel sampling-based reduction technique to mitigate issues with existing approaches. The core idea is to reduce the multi-class problem to pairwise binary classification, coupled with an “Aggressive Double Sampling” strategy to balance class distributions and reduce the number of pairwise examples. The authors argue convincingly that tree-based methods suffer from error propagation and label embedding methods struggle with non-low-rank label matrices, motivating their approach. 

The proposed method is well-motivated and theoretically grounded. The authors provide a non-trivial generalization error bound, accounting for the induced dependencies and the oversampling inherent in their approach, leveraging recent advances in Rademacher complexity and graph covering techniques. While a full verification of the theoretical analysis is beyond the scope of a review, the arguments presented appear plausible and the connection to existing theoretical work is clearly established. 

The experimental evaluation on five text datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly for problems with a large number of classes ( >30k). The results show superior accuracy and F1 scores compared to several baselines, alongside significant improvements in training time and memory usage. The reported slowdown in prediction time is a noted drawback, but the authors address this with a heuristic candidate selection strategy based on class centroids, which appears to offer a reasonable trade-off. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The aggressive double sampling strategy is a creative approach to address the challenges of extreme classification.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with generalization bounds, demonstrating a strong understanding of the underlying principles.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling, showing significant improvements in accuracy, speed, and memory usage on large-scale datasets.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its theoretical foundations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Prediction Time:** While addressed with a heuristic, the slower prediction time remains a limitation. Further investigation into optimizing this aspect could be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance likely depends on the careful tuning of the two sampling parameters (class sampling frequency and adversarial example count). A more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity would be valuable.
*   **Complexity of Analysis:** The theoretical analysis, while impressive, is quite complex and may be difficult for some readers to fully grasp.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective method for extreme multi-class classification. The combination of theoretical rigor and compelling experimental results makes a significant contribution to the field. Despite the limitations regarding prediction time and parameter sensitivity, the benefits in terms of accuracy, speed, and memory usage, particularly for very large datasets, are substantial. I recommend **acceptance** to NIPS.
