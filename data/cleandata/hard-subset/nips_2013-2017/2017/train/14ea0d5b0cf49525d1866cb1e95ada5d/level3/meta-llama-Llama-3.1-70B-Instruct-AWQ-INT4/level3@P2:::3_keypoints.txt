- Proposes a novel reduction of extreme multi-class classification to pairwise binary classification called Aggressive Double Sampling (ADS).
- Motivates ADS by limitations of tree-based methods (cascading errors) and label-embedding methods (fail when label matrices are not low-rank).
- ADS is controlled by two parameters: class sampling frequency (inverse to empirical frequency) and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples, with repeated sampling to build the dyadic classifier.
- Uses a heuristic candidate selection via class centroids to reduce prediction time and avoid enumerating all class pairwise features.
- Theoretical contribution: provides a finite-sample risk bound accounting for dependent dyadic examples and oversampling bias, which decreases linearly with sample/resample sizes.
- Analysis is complicated and relies on several assumptions that may not hold universally.
- Empirical results: superior accuracy and F1 on datasets with >30,000 classes, faster training, and much lower memory usage.
- Prediction time is slower compared to other methods.
- Hyperparameter choices require careful tuning.
- Strengths: novel, scalable approach with theoretical consistency guarantees and strong large-scale performance with low memory/computation.
- Weaknesses: complex analysis, slower inference, and sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; authors should address slower prediction, hyperparameter tuning, and add more implementation/experimental details.
- Reviewer rating: 8/10, confidence: 8/10.
