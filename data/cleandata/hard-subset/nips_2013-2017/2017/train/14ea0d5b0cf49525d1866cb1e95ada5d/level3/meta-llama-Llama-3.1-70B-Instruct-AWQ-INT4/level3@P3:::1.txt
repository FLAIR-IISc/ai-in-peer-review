This paper proposes a double sampling strategy to improve the multiclass method in [16] by transforming the original multiclass problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples. The authors provide both theoretical and empirical analysis of the proposed approach. The main idea is to address the problem of extreme classification, where the number of classes is very large, by reducing the number of pairs of examples in the expanded data.

The authors start by introducing the problem of large-scale multiclass classification and discussing the limitations of existing approaches, such as one-versus-all (OVA) and tree-based methods. They then propose a double sampling strategy, referred to as (π, κ)-DS, which aims to balance the presence of classes by oversampling the most small size classes and subsampling the few big size classes. The second goal of the strategy is to reduce the number of constructed dyadic examples.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the proposed approach by deriving new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity. They show that even with a shift in the original class distribution and inter-dependency between the pairs of examples, the empirical risk minimization principle over the transformation of the sampled training set remains consistent.

The authors conduct experiments on several datasets, including DMOZ and Wikipedia collections with up to 100,000 classes. The results show that the proposed approach scales to multiclass problems with many classes and outperforms state-of-the-art approaches in terms of training and prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance.

The strengths of the paper include the proposal of a novel double sampling strategy, comprehensive experimental comparisons, and addressing the important and practical area of extreme classification. However, the novelty of the paper is limited, as it largely builds on [16], adding double sampling and local Rademacher complexity bounds. Additionally, the prediction time remains long compared to other methods, which may be more important in practice despite faster training. Furthermore, the interpretation and comparative advantage of the presented generalization bound are unclear.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the authors could have been more careful in evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. However, some sections, such as the theoretical analysis, may be challenging for non-experts to follow.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a new combination of familiar techniques, but the novelty is limited, as mentioned earlier. The related work is adequately referenced, but the authors could have discussed the differences between their approach and existing methods in more detail.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The results are important, and other researchers or practitioners are likely to use these ideas or build on them. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides unique data, conclusions on existing data, or a unique theoretical or pragmatic approach.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel double sampling strategy for extreme classification.
* The authors provide comprehensive experimental comparisons and show that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods.
* The paper addresses the important and practical area of extreme classification.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The novelty of the paper is limited, as it largely builds on [16].
* The prediction time remains long compared to other methods.
* The interpretation and comparative advantage of the presented generalization bound are unclear.
