This paper proposes a scalable reduction for extreme multi-class classification, targeting problems with tens of thousands of classes. The core idea is to reduce the problem to pairwise binary classification via a novel, aggressive double-sampling scheme. The authors position their work against established methods, arguing that tree-based approaches are prone to cascading errors and label-embedding techniques falter when the label matrix is not low-rank, a common scenario in extreme classification. This reductionist approach builds upon prior work but distinguishes itself through a sampling strategy designed to be computationally efficient and robust to long-tailed class distributions.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. **(Significance)** The primary contribution is a practical and effective algorithm for a very challenging problem. The empirical results on large-scale text datasets are compelling, showing superior accuracy and F1 scores on problems with over 30,000 classes, alongside drastically reduced training time and memory usage. This makes the method highly valuable for practitioners. **(Quality)** The approach is supported by a solid, albeit complex, theoretical analysis that establishes consistency guarantees. The authors commendably address the technical challenges introduced by their sampling scheme—namely, the inter-dependency of dyadic examples and the distribution shift from oversampling—by leveraging advanced tools like Janson’s concentration inequalities. **(Originality)** The "Aggressive Double Sampling" strategy, which combines class-frequency-based sampling with adversarial example selection, is a novel and well-motivated technique.

The main weakness is the method's slower prediction time, a direct consequence of the pairwise framework. While the authors introduce a reasonable heuristic using class centroids for candidate selection to mitigate this, the trade-off between prediction speed and accuracy could be further analyzed. Furthermore, while the theoretical analysis is a strength, its density may limit its accessibility; a more intuitive discussion of the resulting risk bound and its bias term would improve the paper's **clarity**.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a difficult, high-impact problem in machine learning.
*   Demonstrates state-of-the-art empirical performance on massive datasets, significantly advancing the field.
*   Provides sound theoretical justification for its novel sampling approach.
*   Offers a practical solution with low memory and training time requirements.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Prediction speed is slower than some competing methods, which could be a critical limitation in certain applications.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a well-motivated, theoretically-grounded, and empirically-validated method. The substantial performance gains on large-scale problems represent a significant contribution that is likely to influence future work. The strengths decisively outweigh the weaknesses, and I strongly recommend acceptance.
