- Proposes a double sampling strategy for extreme multi-class classification by converting to binary dyadic examples (input-class pairs) and applying per-class oversampling and random comparison-class sampling.  
- Extends Joshi et al. (2015) primarily by introducing aggressive sampling techniques to handle long-tailed distributions and large K.  
- Theoretical contribution: provides improved generalization bounds using fractional Rademacher complexity to account for dependency in transformed data.  
- Empirical results on DMOZ and Wikipedia (10k–100k classes) show competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods while significantly reducing training time and memory.  
- Sampling procedures are argued to be theoretically justified and consistent despite class distribution shifts and dependent examples.  
- Strength: well-motivated approach addressing a significant practical problem in large-scale classification.  
- Strength: solid theoretical analysis beyond prior work and compelling empirical advantages in efficiency.  
- Weakness: core reduction largely follows Joshi et al.; main novelty is limited to the sampling strategy.  
- Weakness: Section 2.3 lacks proper theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm (k nearest class centroids), critical for practical use.  
- Weakness: no comparison of sample complexity or generalization bounds to non-transformed (standard) multi-class formulations, limiting assessment of relative advantage.  
- Overall: technically sound with strong experiments but incomplete theoretical foundation and limited novelty; reviewer recommends rejection due to insufficient theoretical contribution.
