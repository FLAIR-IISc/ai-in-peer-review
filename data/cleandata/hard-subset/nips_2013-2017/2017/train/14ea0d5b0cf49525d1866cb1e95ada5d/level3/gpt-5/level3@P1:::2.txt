This paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem over dyadic (example, class) pairs and introduces a double sampling scheme to address both long-tailed class distributions and the combinatorial blow-up in the number of pairs. The method first rebalances the training set by class-conditional sampling (π), then limits the number of negative comparisons per example to κ sampled classes, and learns a scoring function f∘φ over a joint example–class representation φ. The authors derive generalization bounds for dependent dyadic samples using local fractional Rademacher complexity, showing that ERM remains consistent under the induced dependence and distribution shift. For prediction, they further speed up inference via candidate selection: restricting scoring to the q nearest class centroids in the dyadic space. Experiments on LSHTC DMOZ and Wikipedia subsets (10k–100k classes) show competitive macro-F1 and accuracy with substantial gains in training/inference time and memory versus OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree-based extreme classifiers.

Relation to prior work: The dyadic reduction and ranking-style loss connect to established multi-class-to-binary reductions and label ranking (e.g., Weston & Watkins, AdaBoost.MR), and to prior work handling dependence in pairwise constructions via fractional Rademacher complexity (Usunier et al.; Janson; Ralaivola & Amini). The paper extends Joshi et al. (2015) by (i) adding an aggressive two-stage sampling strategy tailored to extreme class imbalance and (ii) strengthening the analysis with local fractional Rademacher bounds that incorporate variance terms for faster rates. Compared to label-embedding and tree-based methods (e.g., Bengio et al.; FastXML/RecallTree), the proposal avoids the low-rank assumption and the error-propagation of deep trees, while still achieving near-logarithmic-time prediction when combined with candidate selection.

Strengths:
- Practicality and scalability: The double sampling sharply reduces training pairs and memory while counteracting long-tail imbalance. The empirical speed/memory improvements at 50k–100k classes are compelling, and code is provided.
- Theoretical contribution: Clean generalization bounds for dependent dyadic samples with local complexities, and an explicit treatment of the distribution shift introduced by π,κ via α,β constants.
- Solid empirical evaluation on standard large-scale text benchmarks with appropriate metrics (macro-F1), showing that the proposed approach is competitive with strong baselines (OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse) and often better than tree-based methods in accuracy while matching their efficiency.
- Simple, domain-aligned dyadic features for text; the centroid-based feature doubles as a natural vehicle for candidate selection at test time.

Weaknesses:
- Incremental novelty: The core reduction is known; the main novelty is in sampling/rebalancing and tightening analysis. While valuable, it reads as an engineering and analysis refinement rather than a fundamentally new formulation.
- Missing theory for candidate selection (Sec. 2.3): Restricting to q nearest centroids changes the decision rule and could exclude the true class; no recall or regret guarantees (e.g., probability true label appears in the candidate set) are provided, and no end-to-end bound integrates this step.
- Limited comparative theory: No discussion of how the derived bounds compare in rate or constants to standard multiclass formulations (e.g., Crammer–Singer, OVA) or to pairwise ranking approaches; the role and magnitude of α=max_y η_y/π_y and β=1/π_y in practice is underexplored. In extreme rebalancing, α can be large.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Limited analysis of how κ, π, and q affect accuracy, macro-F1, time, and memory; no comparison between exhaustive vs candidate-restricted prediction; no study of learned vs fixed φ.
- Scope: All results are on text with a hand-crafted φ; claims of broader applicability (e.g., images, recommendation) are not empirically substantiated.
- Experimental protocol: Converting multilabel LSHTC to multiclass by instance replication may distort class priors and instance difficulty; some discussion of the impact would help. Baselines omit other dyadic/ranking-based methods (e.g., WSABIE/WARP), which would be natural comparators.

Quality:
- The theory is technically sound given the dependencies; results are well-stated, and assumptions are clear. However, the lack of analysis for the candidate selection step leaves a gap between training-time guarantees and test-time practice.
- Empirical comparisons are thorough among popular extreme classifiers; hyperparameter tuning and hardware are reported. Additional sensitivity/ablation studies would strengthen the empirical claims.

Clarity:
- Generally well-organized and readable. The definitions of α,β and the sampling procedure could be clarified (e.g., precise sampling with/without replacement, expected sample sizes). Explicit complexity statements for training and prediction in terms of m,K,κ,q would aid readers.

Originality:
- Moderate. The sampling scheme is thoughtfully designed for extreme class imbalance and pairwise blow-up, and the learning guarantees use local fractional Rademacher complexity, but the overall framework builds on established dyadic reductions.

Significance:
- High practical significance for extreme classification in text. The method is simple, scalable, and effective, with available code. The theoretical treatment of dependence under double sampling is of interest to the community.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong scalability and memory/time reductions with competitive accuracy/macro-F1 at 50k–100k classes.
- Provides new generalization bounds for a widely used but dependent dyadic construction under sampling.
- Simple, easy-to-implement approach with open-source code; likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Main novelty centers on sampling and analysis; core reduction is known.
- No theoretical treatment of the candidate selection step used in practice.
- Limited ablations and missing comparisons to other dyadic/ranking methods; only text domain is evaluated.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide guarantees for candidate selection (e.g., conditions under which the true class is in the q-nearest set with high probability; integrate into a full risk bound).
- Add ablations on κ, π, and q; report top-k accuracy and candidate recall; compare exhaustive vs candidate-restricted prediction.
- Compare to additional dyadic/ranking baselines (e.g., WSABIE/WARP) and discuss bounds vs standard multiclass formulations.
- Clarify the sampling algorithm details and present explicit complexity analysis; discuss the practical range of α,β under class rebalancing.
