This paper proposes an aggressive double sampling strategy to improve the multi-class to binary reduction method introduced in [16]. The authors provide both theoretical and empirical analysis of their approach, demonstrating its ability to scale to multi-class problems with a large number of classes.

The paper begins by summarizing the limitations of existing multi-class classification methods, particularly in the context of extreme classification where the number of classes is very large. The authors then introduce their proposed method, which involves a double sampling strategy to balance the presence of classes and reduce the number of dyadic examples. This approach is based on a multi-class to binary reduction strategy, which transforms the original multi-class problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples.

The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of their approach, including a new generalization bound based on local fractional Rademacher complexity. This bound implies faster convergence rates and provides a theoretical justification for the proposed method. The authors also present an algorithm for the double sampling strategy, which involves oversampling small classes and subsampling large classes.

The paper includes comprehensive experimental comparisons with several state-of-the-art methods on various text datasets, including the Large Scale Hierarchical Text Classification challenge (LSHTC) datasets. The results demonstrate the high performance and shorter training time of the proposed method on several datasets.

The strengths of the paper include its ability to address the important and practical area of extreme classification, its comprehensive experimental comparisons, and its novel theoretical analysis. However, the novelty of the paper is limited, as it largely builds on the work in [16] and adds double sampling and local Rademacher complexity bounds. Additionally, the prediction time of the proposed method remains long compared to other methods, which may be more important in practice despite faster training.

Furthermore, the interpretation and comparative advantage of the presented generalization bound are unclear. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of this bound and how it compares to existing bounds in the literature.

Finally, the mixing of batch and online baselines in the experimental comparisons makes memory-usage comparisons uneven and potentially misleading. The authors should consider separating these comparisons or providing a more detailed discussion of the memory usage of each method.
