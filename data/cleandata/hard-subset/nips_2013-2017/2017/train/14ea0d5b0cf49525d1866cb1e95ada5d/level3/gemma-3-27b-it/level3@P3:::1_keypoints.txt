- Proposes a double sampling strategy to balance class distributions and reduce dyadic examples, improving Joshi et al.'s multi-class-to-binary reduction.  
- Provides theoretical generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity.  
- Demonstrates empirical improvements on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia) and scalability to ~100k classes.  
- Addresses a relevant, practical problem in extreme multi-class classification.  
- Shows strong scalability and competitive/superior accuracy and macro F1 compared to baselines.  
- Achieves reduced training time and memory usage in experiments.  
- Includes theoretical analysis to justify the sampling strategy.  
- Main novelty is incremental—applying double sampling to an existing framework rather than a fundamentally new approach.  
- Prediction time remains a bottleneck and is not adequately resolved.  
- The practical significance and comparative advantage of the generalization bound (Theorem 2) are unclear.  
- Memory comparisons mix batch and online baselines, making results potentially misleading.  
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters (π, κ) is insufficiently analyzed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: practical, effective solution with strong empirical results and some theory; well-written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, unresolved prediction latency, unconvincing theoretical contribution, and potentially unfair memory comparisons.  
- Overall assessment: solid engineering contribution that improves scalability and efficiency but lacks groundbreaking novelty.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: more discussion of prediction-time limitations and mitigation; clearer interpretation of the generalization bound; fairer memory comparisons or explicit caveats; detailed hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
