- Proposes a scalable dyadic reduction ("Aggressive Double Sampling"): oversample rare classes inversely to frequency, sample κ adversarial classes per example, train on repeated sampled pairs, and use centroid-based candidate selection at prediction.
- Theoretically addresses dependence among dyadic examples and distribution shift from reweighting via dependency-graph splitting, Janson-type inequalities, and local/fractional Rademacher complexity, giving finite-sample bounds with a bias term that vanishes with sample/resample size.
- Empirically achieves superior accuracy and macro-F1 on five text datasets for ~30k+ classes, with much faster training and lower memory than strong baselines, though prediction is slower.
- Notation is heavy; the dependency structure and main assumptions for the bounds need clearer, more intuitive explanation.
- The connection between the 0/1 loss analysis and the surrogate loss used in practice should be made explicit.
- Complexity formulas for training and inference (including wall-clock/top-k inference) are missing and should be provided.
- Novelty: the specific double-sampling scheme with consistency guarantees under dependence and oversampling bias is new and well-motivated; applying Janson’s inequalities here is a nice contribution.
- Significance: practical memory/runtime gains and state-of-the-art performance on large benchmarks make the approach relevant for extreme text classification.
- Pros: strong large-scale empirical results, sound and plausible theory, simple implementable algorithm with provided code.
- Con: limited ablation/sensitivity analyses for π, κ, and candidate-set size q; trade-offs are unclear.
- Con: prediction is slower and lacks end-to-end top-k and wall-clock inference evaluation.
- Con: comparisons to embedding baselines are dismissed without a small-scale sanity check.
- Con: relies on text-specific dyadic features; generality beyond text is not demonstrated.
- Suggestions: add ablations for π, κ, q; report top-k accuracy and candidate coverage; clarify surrogate-loss training link to theoretical bounds; include a brief comparison to at least one embedding method on a smaller dataset.
