This paper addresses the challenging problem of multi-class classification with an extremely large number of classes, a common scenario in applications like text categorization of large document repositories. The authors propose a double sampling strategy applied on top of a multi-class to binary reduction technique, aiming to mitigate the issues of class imbalance and computational cost inherent in these extreme classification settings. The core idea builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015) by transforming the problem into binary classification over pairs of examples and classes, but introduces a novel sampling scheme to improve scalability and generalization performance.

The authors identify the key limitations of existing approaches: the computational intractability of one-vs-all (OVA) with a large number of classes, and the challenges of tree-based and embedding-based methods.  They correctly highlight the dependence created in the data when transforming to a binary problem, which invalidates the application of standard generalization bounds.  To address this, the paper leverages and extends local fractional Rademacher complexity from recent work (Ralaivola et al., 2015) to provide new theoretical guarantees for their sampled reduction strategy.  Specifically, they introduce a (π, κ)-double sampling scheme:  first, a per-class sampling strategy (π) rebalances the training distribution by oversampling minority classes; second, a random sampling step (κ) reduces the number of binary comparisons made for each example, reducing the size of the transformed dataset.  A key theoretical contribution is demonstrating that these sampling procedures preserve the consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle, despite the altered class distribution and data dependencies.

The strengths of this paper are severalfold. The theoretical analysis is a significant contribution, offering a rigorous justification for the proposed sampling strategy and demonstrating its robustness to data dependencies. The problem setting – extreme multi-class classification – is important and practically relevant. The experimental results on the DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets are compelling, showing that the proposed approach is competitive with, and often outperforms, state-of-the-art methods in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency (training/prediction time, memory consumption). The paper is well-structured and the presentation is clear.  The connection to previous work, especially Joshi et al. (2015), is well-established.

However, there are also some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis provides generalization bounds, it doesn't fully explore the implications of the sampling rate parameters (π and κ) on the sample complexity. A more detailed analysis of how these parameters influence the number of samples needed to achieve a target level of performance would be valuable. The prediction algorithm described in Section 2.3, involving candidate selection based on nearest class centroids, lacks a theoretical justification. The impact of this approximation on the overall performance and generalization error is not analyzed. Furthermore, the paper does not compare the sample complexity/generalization bounds derived for their transformed binary problem to those for standard multi-class formulations. This would provide a clearer understanding of the trade-offs involved in the reduction process. The parameter tuning details mentioned in the experimental setup section would benefit from being more thoroughly described and perhaps placed in an appendix.  

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper offers a rigorous theoretical analysis with novel generalization bounds, addressing a crucial issue in extreme multi-class classification.
*   **Empirically Validated:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on large-scale datasets, outperforming several strong baselines.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is important and the proposed solution offers a scalable and efficient approach for extreme multi-class classification.
*   **Well-Written and Structured:** The paper is clear, concise, and well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ contributions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Analysis of Sampling Parameters:**  A more thorough exploration of the impact of π and κ on sample complexity is needed.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Prediction Algorithm:** The candidate selection step lacks theoretical support.
*   **Missing Comparison to Standard Multi-Class Bounds:**  A comparison to the sample complexity of standard multi-class formulations would provide valuable insights.



Overall, this is a solid contribution with a strong theoretical foundation and compelling experimental results. While the weaknesses mentioned above could be addressed with further analysis, the paper presents a significant advancement in the field of extreme multi-class classification. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should include a discussion on the implications of the sampling parameters, a justification for the candidate selection strategy, and a comparison to existing multi-class generalization bounds.
