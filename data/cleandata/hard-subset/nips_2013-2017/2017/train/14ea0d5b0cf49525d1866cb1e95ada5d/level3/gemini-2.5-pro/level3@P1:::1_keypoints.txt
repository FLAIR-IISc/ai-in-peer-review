- Problem & method: Tackles extreme multi-class classification via a multi-class-to-binary dyadic reduction and introduces a (π, κ)-double sampling scheme to rebalance long-tailed classes and limit negative pairs.

- Significance & practicality: Addresses a important, real-world extreme classification setting (long-tailed distributions) relevant to text categorization and recommendations.

- Strong empirical results: Achieves state-of-the-art Accuracy and Macro-F1 on large datasets (WIKI-50K/100K) while greatly reducing training time and memory versus OVA, M-SVM, and PD-Sparse.

- Scalability: Double sampling effectively handles class imbalance and the quadratic negative-pair complexity, enabling training on 100k-class problems on a single machine.

- Theoretical justification: Provides non-trivial generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexities to argue consistency under the sampling and dependence introduced by the reduction.

- Limited originality: Core dyadic reduction idea is inherited from prior work (e.g., [16]); the main novelty is the sampling scheme and refined theoretical analysis.

- High prediction time: Inference is orders of magnitude slower than tree-based methods (FastXML, PfastReXML), making it less suitable for latency-sensitive applications despite candidate selection heuristics.

- Unclear theoretical impact: Theorem 2 is complex and its practical advantages over prior bounds are not clearly explained, making the theoretical contribution feel like a sanity check.

- Potentially misleading memory comparisons: Directly comparing batch and online/streaming methods’ memory footprints is inequitable and not sufficiently discussed.

- Overall recommendation: Well-executed, practically valuable work with strong empirical gains; somewhat incremental with notable prediction-time limitations — recommend acceptance.
