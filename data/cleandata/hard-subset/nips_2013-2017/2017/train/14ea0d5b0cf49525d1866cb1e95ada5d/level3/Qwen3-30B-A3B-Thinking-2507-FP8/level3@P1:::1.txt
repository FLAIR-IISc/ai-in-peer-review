

This paper proposes a double sampling strategy for multi-class to binary reduction in extreme classification problems with very large numbers of classes (up to 100,000). The authors build upon the dyadic representation approach of [16] by introducing (π, κ)-DS sampling that oversamples small classes and subsamples large classes to address long-tailed distributions, while simultaneously reducing the number of dyadic examples. The theoretical contribution includes novel generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity, and the empirical evaluation demonstrates improved training time, memory usage, and predictive performance on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets compared to state-of-the-art approaches.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the important area of extreme classification, which has been a recurring theme at NIPS (e.g., [5, 16, 21]). While the problem is well-motivated, the theoretical novelty is limited as the authors primarily extend [16] with sampling techniques and refine the generalization analysis. The local Rademacher complexity bounds are technically sound but their interpretation remains unclear - it's not evident how these bounds provide meaningful advantages over existing results in the literature, particularly regarding the practical implications of the "shift in class distribution" mentioned in the paper. The connection between the theoretical results and the empirical improvements observed in experiments could be strengthened.

The experimental section is comprehensive, with careful comparisons against multiple baselines including OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse. The results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves the best balance among training time, memory consumption, and predictive performance (accuracy and macro F1) for large-scale problems. The authors rightly note that standard OVA and M-SVM become intractable for 50K+ classes due to linear memory complexity with respect to the number of classes. The method's ability to handle extreme classification scenarios with 100K classes while maintaining competitive accuracy is a significant practical contribution.

However, the paper has several important weaknesses. First, the prediction time remains relatively long compared to tree-based approaches (FastXML, PfastReXML) which have logarithmic prediction complexity. Given that prediction speed is often more critical than training speed in real-world applications, this limitation significantly affects the practical utility of the approach. Second, the comparison between batch and online methods (like FastXML) is problematic - the memory usage comparisons are potentially misleading because the online methods use different implementation strategies that inherently require less memory. This makes the memory usage claims difficult to interpret fairly.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with clear algorithm descriptions and sufficient experimental details. However, the theoretical section could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the sampling strategy's impact on the underlying optimization problem. The presentation of the generalization bound in Theorem 2 is technically correct but lacks sufficient context to understand its practical significance.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses a crucial problem in machine learning with real-world applications in text classification, recommendation systems, and tagging. The demonstrated scalability to 100K classes represents a meaningful advancement over previous methods. However, the incremental nature of the contribution (primarily building on [16]) slightly diminishes the significance relative to more novel approaches that have appeared at NIPS in recent years.

Strengths:
- Strong empirical validation on large-scale text classification datasets
- Demonstrated scalability to 100K classes
- Good balance of training time, memory, and predictive performance
- Addresses an important practical problem in machine learning
- Well-organized presentation of experimental results

Weaknesses:
- Limited theoretical novelty (primarily extends [16])
- Unclear interpretation of the generalization bound
- Prediction time remains high compared to tree-based methods
- Problematic comparison between batch and online baselines
- Insufficient justification for the sampling strategy's theoretical advantages

The paper makes a valuable practical contribution to extreme classification, particularly for problems where training time and memory are critical constraints. While the theoretical novelty is modest, the empirical results are convincing and the approach is well-suited for real-world applications with large numbers of classes. The main drawback is the relatively slow prediction time compared to tree-based methods, which may limit its adoption in production systems where inference speed is critical. The authors should address this limitation in future work by exploring more efficient prediction strategies.

Overall, this is a solid technical contribution that advances the state of the art in scalable multi-class classification. The paper is well-executed with strong empirical results, though the incremental nature of the theoretical contribution and the prediction speed limitation slightly reduce its significance. The authors have made a clear contribution to a problem of increasing importance in machine learning applications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the theoretical advantages and better address the prediction time limitations.
