- Problem: Tackles extreme multi-class classification with very large label sets (10k–100k) and long-tailed class distributions.  
- Method: Proposes an aggressive double sampling strategy for a multi-class-to-binary reduction: per-class rebalancing (over/undersampling) plus random sampling of comparison classes.  
- Prior work: Builds on Joshi et al. (2015) reduction framework while focusing on aggressive sampling to address scalability and imbalance.  
- Theory: Derives generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity that account for sampling-induced distribution shift and dyadic-example dependencies.  
- Empirics: Strong results on DMOZ and Wikipedia showing large reductions in training time, prediction time, and memory while keeping competitive accuracy and macro F1.  
- Practical impact: Effectively addresses computational intractability for very large K and mitigates long-tailed class imbalance.  
- Experimental quality: Thorough experimental design, multiple datasets/metrics, and well-specified implementation details.  
- Clarity: Generally clear exposition and precise algorithm descriptions; double sampling and theoretical framework well explained.  
- Prediction step issue: Candidate selection using class centroids (Section 2.3) lacks theoretical justification and analysis.  
- Theoretical gaps: No analysis of how sampling affects the prediction algorithm and missing comparison of sample complexity/generalization bounds to standard multi-class formulations.  
- Originality: Moderate—sampling strategy is a meaningful extension but core reduction follows Joshi et al. (2015).  
- Comparison to other approaches: Well-differentiated from tree-based and embedding methods, but could better contrast with other imbalance-handling techniques.  
- Strengths: Theoretically grounded, practically effective double sampling approach with strong empirical validation on large label spaces.  
- Weaknesses: Limited theoretical novelty beyond sampling, incomplete analysis of prediction component, and lacking comparisons of theoretical guarantees to standard formulations.
