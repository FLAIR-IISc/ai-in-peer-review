- Addresses large-scale (extreme) multi-class classification with long-tailed label distributions.  
- Proposes aggressive double sampling atop multi-class-to-binary reduction (building on [16]) to balance data and reduce problem size.  
- Aims to reduce computational cost and mitigate the curse of long-tailed classes.  
- Provides theoretical analysis: generalization bounds based on local fractional Rademacher complexity.  
- Empirically evaluated on DMOZ and Wikipedia, showing competitive accuracy and macro F1.  
- Demonstrates significant improvements in training time, prediction time, and memory, especially for tens of thousands of classes.  
- Thorough baseline comparisons (OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PD-Sparse) support empirical claims.  
- Practical candidate selection at prediction time further reduces computational cost.  
- Clear connection to prior multi-class-to-binary reduction work; positions contribution as an enhancement.  
- Novelty is incremental; contribution largely appears as an engineering optimization of [16].  
- Practical benefits and implications of the derived generalization bound are not clearly articulated.  
- Runtime/memory comparisons may be misleading by mixing batch and online baselines; per-example memory comparisons suggested.  
- Prediction time, while improved, may remain a bottleneck for real-time applications and needs acknowledgment.  
- Theoretical sections are dense and would benefit from more intuitive explanations and examples.  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-motivated, theoretically backed, and empirically validated—recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: clarify theoretical advantages vs. existing bounds, provide nuanced runtime/memory comparisons, acknowledge prediction-time limits, and discuss feature-mapping computational cost.
