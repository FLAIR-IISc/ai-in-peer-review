This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, where the number of classes can be very large (up to 100,000). The authors propose a novel approach based on a double sampling strategy applied to a multi-class to binary reduction technique. This aims to overcome the limitations of existing methods, namely the computational intractability of one-vs-all (OVA) and the inaccuracies of tree-based and label embedding approaches when dealing with long-tailed class distributions common in these scenarios. The core idea is to aggressively sample both the original examples and the resulting pairwise examples to reduce computational cost and improve generalization performance.

The paper builds upon the existing literature on extreme classification. OVA methods [18] are a common baseline but become impractical with a large number of classes. Tree-based methods [4, 3, 6, 7, 9, 21, 5, 15] offer logarithmic complexity but struggle with unbalanced trees and error propagation. Label embedding approaches [11, 5, 19] rely on low-rank assumptions that often do not hold in extreme settings. Previous binary reduction techniques [1, 28, 16] suffer from high computational cost during the construction of the dyadic representation.  This work distinguishes itself by explicitly addressing the class imbalance problem via intelligent sampling and providing theoretical guarantees for this sampling process. 

The strengths of this paper are significant. The proposed (π, κ)-DS sampling strategy appears to be a practical and effective way to address the computational challenges of extreme multi-class classification. The theoretical analysis, while complex, provides a valuable foundation for understanding the method’s behavior and guarantees its consistency. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets, consistently outperforming existing state-of-the-art methods in terms of accuracy, F1-score, training time, and memory consumption for problems with a large number of classes ( >30k ).  The introduction of a candidate selection method during prediction helps mitigate the potential slowdown associated with pairwise comparisons. The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental results. The authors thoughtfully acknowledge the limitations of other approaches and position their work as a strong alternative.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The theoretical analysis, while impressive, is quite dense and may be difficult for some readers to follow in detail. While the authors cite relevant work on concentration inequalities [14, 22], a more detailed discussion of the assumptions underlying these inequalities and their applicability to the specific problem setting could be beneficial. The practical impact of the parameters α and β in Theorem 2 is not thoroughly discussed, and it is unclear how sensitive the performance is to their values. The performance gains on the smaller datasets are less pronounced, and it would be helpful to explore the conditions under which the proposed method is most effective. The prediction time, while improved over some baselines, remains a potential bottleneck, particularly for real-time applications. The candidate selection strategy, while effective, adds another layer of complexity to the system and could benefit from further investigation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The proposed method addresses a challenging and important problem in machine learning.
*   The approach is novel and well-motivated, building upon and overcoming limitations of existing techniques.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a strong foundation for the method and guarantees its consistency.
*   The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating superior performance on large-scale datasets.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis is complex and may be inaccessible to some readers.
*   The performance gains are less significant on smaller datasets.
*   Prediction time remains a potential concern for certain applications.
*   The sensitivity to the parameters in the theoretical bounds is not fully explored.



**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. The proposed method is both theoretically sound and practically effective, offering a compelling solution to a challenging problem. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh them.  I recommend **acceptance** with a confidence of 8/10. The authors have presented a valuable new tool for researchers and practitioners working with large-scale classification problems.
