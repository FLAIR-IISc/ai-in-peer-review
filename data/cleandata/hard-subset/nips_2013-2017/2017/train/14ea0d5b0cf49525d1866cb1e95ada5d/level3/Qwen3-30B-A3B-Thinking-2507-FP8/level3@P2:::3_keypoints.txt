- Proposes a novel double sampling strategy for extreme multi-class classification that balances classes and samples adversarial classes per example.  
- Targets limitations of tree-based methods (error propagation) and label-embedding methods (fail on non-low-rank label matrices).  
- Provides theoretical guarantees showing consistency under distribution shift and dependent dyadic examples.  
- Derives novel generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity and Janson’s concentration inequalities.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing to strong baselines (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse).  
- Experiments cover multiple datasets up to 100k classes and show advantages in memory, training time, accuracy, and macro F1.  
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes precise pseudocode for the double sampling procedure.  
- The double sampling idea is original within multi-class-to-binary reduction and well situated relative to recent extreme-classification work.  
- Demonstrates significant practical impact on large datasets (WIKI-50K, WIKI-100K) where other methods are memory-infeasible.  
- Theoretical contributions are technically sound but could better explain practical implications of bias terms (α, β) in risk bounds.  
- Prediction time is slower than tree-based methods; proposed centroid candidate selection needs more analysis of time–accuracy tradeoffs.  
- Exclusion of recent methods (SLEEC, LEML) from experiments lacks sufficient justification or discussion.  
- For acceptance: method is theoretically rigorous, practically useful, and shows strong empirical results for extreme classification.  
- Against acceptance: needs more analysis of prediction-time tradeoffs, clearer rationale for excluded baselines, and denser theory may hinder accessibility.  
- Overall: strong contribution combining theory and practice for extreme classification; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.
