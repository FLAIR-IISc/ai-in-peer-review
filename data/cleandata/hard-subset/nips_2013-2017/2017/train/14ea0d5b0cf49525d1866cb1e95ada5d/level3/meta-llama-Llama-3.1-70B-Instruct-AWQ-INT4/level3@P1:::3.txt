This paper proposes a novel approach to extreme multi-class classification problems, where the number of classes is very large. The authors introduce a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction strategy, which transforms the original multi-class problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples. The proposed approach is designed to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions and reduce the number of pairs of examples in the expanded data.

The authors argue that existing tree-based methods suffer from cascading errors and label-embedding methods fail when label matrices are not low-rank. In contrast, their proposed approach is scalable and has theoretical consistency guarantees relative to multi-class empirical risk minimization (ERM). The authors also introduce "Aggressive Double Sampling" controlled by two parameters: class sampling frequency inversely proportional to empirical frequency, and the number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples.

The experimental results on five text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 for problems with more than 30,000 classes, faster training, and much lower memory use, but slower prediction. The authors also propose a heuristic prediction-time candidate selection via class centroids to avoid enumerating all class pairwise features.

The theoretical analysis is complicated by dependent dyadic examples and by oversampling that shifts the empirical distribution. The proof splits dependent sums according to the induced graph structure and applies Janson's concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables. The finite-sample risk bound carries bias from true vs. oversampled class probability ratios, but this bias decreases linearly with sample/resample sizes.

Overall, the reviewer finds the analysis plausible (not fully verified) and the algorithm theoretically sound and practically useful. The proposed approach is a strong method for extreme classification with low memory/computation and superior performance on the largest datasets.

Strengths:

* The proposed approach is scalable and has theoretical consistency guarantees relative to multi-class ERM.
* The experimental results show superior accuracy and F1 for problems with more than 30,000 classes, faster training, and much lower memory use.
* The authors introduce a novel "Aggressive Double Sampling" strategy that is controlled by two parameters.

Weaknesses:

* The prediction time is slower compared to other methods.
* The theoretical analysis is complicated by dependent dyadic examples and by oversampling that shifts the empirical distribution.
* The finite-sample risk bound carries bias from true vs. oversampled class probability ratios.

Originality:

* The proposed approach is a novel combination of multi-class to binary reduction strategy and double sampling strategy.
* The "Aggressive Double Sampling" strategy is a new contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification.

* The approach has the potential to be applied to a wide range of applications, including text classification, recommendation, and ranking.
