

This paper proposes a novel double sampling strategy for large-scale multi-class classification, extending Joshi et al. (2015) by addressing the challenges of long-tailed class distributions and large K. The authors transform the multi-class problem into a binary classification task over dyadic examples (input-class pairs), then introduce aggressive sampling techniques: per-class oversampling of rare classes and random sampling of comparison classes to reduce dataset size. The theoretical contribution lies in providing improved generalization bounds using fractional Rademacher complexity that account for the inter-dependency in the transformed dataset.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets with 10,000-100,000 classes, showing competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods while significantly reducing training time and memory consumption. The sampling procedures are theoretically justified through generalization bounds that demonstrate consistency despite class distribution shifts and dependent examples.

Strengths include a well-motivated approach to a significant problem in large-scale classification, solid theoretical analysis (beyond Joshi et al.), and compelling empirical results showing practical advantages over competing methods. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on extreme multi-class classification.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The core reduction idea largely follows Joshi et al., with the main novelty being the sampling technique. The theoretical analysis is incomplete: Section 2.3 lacks proper analysis of the prediction algorithm that limits comparisons to k nearest class centroids, which is critical for practical implementation. Additionally, the paper fails to compare the sample complexity and generalization bounds to non-transformed multi-class formulations, making it difficult to assess the relative advantages of the proposed approach.

The paper is technically sound with well-executed experiments, but the theoretical gaps and insufficient comparison to standard multi-class formulations weaken the contribution. While the empirical results are promising, the lack of theoretical justification for the prediction algorithm and comparison to alternative approaches limits the paper's significance.

In summary, this paper makes a practical contribution to large-scale multi-class classification with a well-motivated sampling technique, but the theoretical foundation is incomplete and the novelty is somewhat limited compared to the existing literature. I recommend rejection due to insufficient theoretical contribution and the absence of critical theoretical analysis.
