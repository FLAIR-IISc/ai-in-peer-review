Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing the problem to binary classification over dyadic example–class pairs. Building on prior dyadic reductions (notably [16]) and generalization analysis for interdependent examples using fractional Rademacher complexity ([25], [22]), the authors propose: (i) an aggressive double sampling scheme that first rebalances long-tailed class distributions via class-dependent sampling (π) and then reduces dyadic complexity via sampling κ adversarial classes per example; and (ii) new generalization bounds (Theorems 1–2) combining fractional and local Rademacher complexity to handle dependence and variance-sensitive rates under distribution shift induced by sampling. For efficient prediction, the paper augments standard argmax over dyadic scores with a candidate selection step using nearest class centroids.

Empirically, on LSHTC/DMOZ and Wikipedia subsets with 10K–100K classes, the method is compared to OVA/M-SVM (LibLinear), PD-Sparse [30], and tree/embedding baselines (FastXML [21], PfastReXML [13], RecallTree [9]). The proposed method often matches or surpasses strong baselines in macro-F1 and accuracy while using substantially less training time and memory than OVA/M-SVM and PD-Sparse, and achieving competitive runtime relative to tree-based methods but with better accuracy.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important and active area (extreme classification) with a method that is simple, scalable, and easy to implement on top of dyadic reductions.
- Clear focus on long-tailed label distributions; the class-wise sampling step (π) directly targets this challenge and is well-motivated.
- Theoretical analysis extends fractional Rademacher tools to a local complexity setting under class-rebalancing and dyadic dependence; Theorem 2 explicitly shows how sampling affects bounds via α, β.
- Strong empirical results on large text corpora (up to 100K classes), with public code and datasets.
- Sensible candidate selection at prediction time; acknowledges accuracy–speed trade-offs.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: the core reduction is inherited from [16]; the main additions are the double sampling heuristic and a refined (local) complexity analysis.
- Practical benefit of the new bounds is unclear. The α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy factors can be large under aggressive oversampling/undersampling, potentially rendering bounds loose; guidance on tuning π, κ to trade off bias/variance and keep bounds meaningful is limited.
- Prediction remains comparatively expensive versus tree/embedding methods; the candidate filtering helps, but the paper provides limited analysis of q (candidate set size) and its impact on accuracy/speed. End-to-end prediction latency comparisons are under-discussed.
- Comparisons conflate batch and online/streaming implementations (e.g., Vowpal Wabbit for RecallTree). Memory usage and wall-clock comparisons can be skewed by implementation choices and feature representations.
- Experimental design choices may bias results: (a) converting multilabel to multiclass by replication changes class frequencies and may advantage rebalancing; (b) the dyadic feature map uses 10 LTR-style features, while baselines likely operate on high-dimensional sparse text features; fairness and the effect of feature engineering are not fully controlled.
- Limited ablations: lack of analyses isolating the impact of π vs κ, the candidate selection, and the particular dyadic featurization.

Quality
- Theory: Sound high-level approach using fractional/local Rademacher complexities; proofs (in supplement) appear to follow standard dependent-process decompositions via graph covers. However, the main bound’s constants (α, β) and the use of 0–1 loss raise questions about tightness and applicability to the actual surrogate trained in practice (e.g., hinge/logistic). It would help to (i) extend to surrogate losses; (ii) provide data-dependent estimates or empirical checks of α, β; (iii) discuss consistency under the practical training objective.
- Empirics: Strong results and scale, but clearer, controlled comparisons are needed. Please report: identical feature representations across baselines where possible; ablations of π, κ, and q; sensitivity curves for speed/accuracy; and end-to-end prediction latency. Also, clarify how κ and π were tuned and whether class prior shift is corrected at test time.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized, with helpful algorithms and problem setup. Some notation could be streamlined, and editorial issues (typos, “Even-though”, etc.) remain. The role and computation of class centroids and the dyadic feature map should be described more concretely; explicitly state the classifier (e.g., linear SVM/logistic) used on dyadic features and its hyperparameters in the main text.

Originality
- Moderate. The sampling strategy is natural (akin to negative sampling/importance sampling used in ranking and language models), and the analytical extension is incremental but nontrivial. Positioning relative to sampled softmax/importance-weighted ERM and NCE literature would strengthen the novelty discussion.

Significance
- Practical significance is good for text extreme classification; improvements in training scalability with competitive accuracy are valuable. Broader impact depends on prediction-time efficiency and generality beyond text (the dyadic map is text-specific). Stronger analysis of candidate selection and more balanced implementation comparisons would enhance confidence.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide surrogate-loss generalization guarantees (or a calibration argument linking surrogate to 0–1 ranking loss) and discuss how π, κ influence excess risk.
- Offer guidance or a heuristic for setting π to avoid exploding α, β; e.g., capped reweighting or importance weighting at training/test to correct prior shift.
- Add ablations: π-only vs κ-only vs both; effect of q on accuracy/latency; effect of dyadic feature choice; and comparison to training on all dyads with importance weights instead of subsampling.
- Ensure fair memory/time comparisons by standardizing feature representations and reporting per-example throughput and model size on disk.
- Discuss connections/differences to sampled softmax/negative sampling and pairwise ranking (e.g., WARP), and clarify why your sampling is preferable here.
- Clarify fairness of converting multilabel to multiclass by replication; report label cardinality and its effect on class priors.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a simple, scalable method that performs well at large K.
- Solid, relevant theoretical treatment of dependence and sampling.
- Strong empirical results on realistic datasets with open code.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental novelty over [16]; theoretical contribution’s practical implications are unclear.
- Prediction-time efficiency and candidate selection are under-analyzed.
- Comparisons and reporting (memory/time, features) could be more controlled and fair; limited ablations.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is promising and practically relevant, but would benefit from clearer theoretical-to-practice connections, more rigorous and fair empirical comparisons, and deeper analysis of prediction-time trade-offs and sampling choices. With these improvements, it could be a strong submission.
