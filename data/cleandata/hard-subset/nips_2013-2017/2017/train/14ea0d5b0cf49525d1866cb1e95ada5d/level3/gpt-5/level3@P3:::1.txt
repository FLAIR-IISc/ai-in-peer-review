Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by revisiting the multiclass-to-binary dyadic reduction idea of [16]. The key contribution is a double sampling scheme: (i) class-rebalancing on the original training set via per-class sampling probabilities πk (oversampling rare classes, subsampling frequent ones), and (ii) for each retained example, sampling only κ “adversarial” (negative) classes to form dyadic pairs. The authors analyze learning with dependent dyadic pairs using fractional/local Rademacher complexities and prove consistency-type generalization bounds under the double sampling-induced distribution shift. To reduce prediction cost, they propose a candidate selection heuristic that considers only q nearest-centroid classes per test example in the dyadic space. Empirically, on large text corpora from LSHTC (DMOZ, Wikipedia, up to 100K classes), the method shows strong macro-F1 and accuracy with substantially reduced training time and memory vs. OVA/M-SVM, and competitive performance vs. PD-Sparse and tree-based methods (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree).

This work is closest to [16] (dyadic reduction and analysis for dependent pairs). Compared to tree-based [3,4,6,7,9,15,21] and embedding-based [5,11,19] extreme classification, the proposed approach preserves flat multiclass decision-making while focusing computation via sampling. The theoretical analysis adapts fractional Rademacher tools [25] and local complexities [22] to this setting.

Strengths
- Addresses an important, practical problem: extreme classification with long-tailed label distributions.
- Simple, effective double sampling that directly targets both imbalance and dyadic expansion cost; easy to implement and tune.
- Theoretical treatment acknowledges pairwise dependence and class distribution shift; consistency-style results are provided.
- Strong empirical results on text datasets with up to 100K classes: competitive or superior macro-F1/accuracy; much faster training and lower memory than OVA/M-SVM; competitive with PD-Sparse; better accuracy than several tree/embedding baselines.
- Sensible candidate selection for prediction; code and datasets are shared.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to [16]: the core reduction and analytical framework are inherited; the main additions are the double sampling scheme and bounds using local complexities.
- Prediction time remains comparatively high. Even with candidate selection, scoring q classes with dyadic features can be slower than logarithmic-time trees; in many applications, prediction latency dominates.
- The practical meaning and comparative advantage of the new bounds are unclear. The bounds contain α, β terms that depend inversely on πk; these can be large under aggressive rebalancing, potentially making bounds loose. There is no empirical study connecting κ, π to generalization behavior or comparing to [16]’s bounds.
- Comparisons mix batch methods (e.g., LIBLINEAR, PD-Sparse) with online/streaming/tree baselines (Vowpal Wabbit RecallTree), making peak-memory and wall-clock comparisons uneven; the paper does not standardize memory reporting (model vs. training-time working set, I/O, hashing).
- The approach relies on hand-crafted dyadic features specialized to text. Generality beyond text is not demonstrated; the candidate selection via centroids depends on this design.
- Missing or limited ablations: no separation of the effects of (i) class rebalancing vs. (ii) dyadic negative subsampling vs. (iii) candidate selection; limited sensitivity analysis over κ, π, and q.

Quality
- Technical development is sound in spirit: the dependence structure is handled via fractional covers; local complexity yields variance-aware terms. However, the presentation does not make clear when the new bounds are tighter than existing ones, nor how α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy scale under the recommended π choices. Without empirical calibration, the bounds’ usefulness for setting κ, π is unclear.
- Experiments are substantial on standard LSHTC text datasets. Training-time and memory gains are convincing. However, prediction time is still high relative to trees; the candidate selection trade-off (q vs. accuracy/latency) is not fully explored at fixed accuracy targets.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized. The sampling algorithm is clear. The theoretical section is dense; more intuition about α, β, and dependence on K, κ, and class imbalance would help. The description of candidate selection’s complexity and its interaction with dyadic feature computation could be clearer. Memory/time reporting methodology should be standardized and stated explicitly.

Originality
- Incremental. The double sampling strategy is a practical heuristic (akin to negative sampling and class rebalancing) applied to the dyadic reduction of [16], and the analysis extends existing fractional/local Rademacher tools to the sampled setting.

Significance
- Moderate. The method is practical and seems useful for large-K text classification, especially when training time and memory are primary constraints. Impact is limited by prediction-time latency and by reliance on text-specific feature engineering. The theoretical results will be more impactful if supported by guidance on setting κ, π and by comparisons to prior bounds.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations: only π-rebalancing; only κ negative sampling; both; and with/without candidate selection. Report accuracy/MaF1, training/prediction time, and memory.
- Calibrate the generalization bounds: plot empirical error vs. bound terms across κ, π; show typical α, β under your π schedule; compare to [16]’s bounds. Discuss when local complexity brings tangible tightening.
- Standardize and clarify resource reporting: peak RSS and model size separately; include preprocessing and dyadic feature construction times; for online baselines, include buffer/hashing memory.
- Expand prediction-time analysis: report accuracy vs. latency curves by varying q; include top-k accuracy; compare to trees at matched accuracy levels.
- Discuss/implement importance weighting to correct sampling bias (e.g., 1/πy and (K−1)/κ) and evaluate its effect.
- Evaluate on at least one non-text dataset to demonstrate generality, or clarify scope to text classification.
- Compare against additional strong XMC baselines where possible (e.g., more recent log-time methods) and justify exclusions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, practically important problem (extreme classification with long tails).
- Simple, scalable method with strong empirical performance and substantial training-time/memory gains.
- Theoretical analysis acknowledges dependence and sampling-induced shift.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond [16]; double sampling and local-complexity bounds are incremental.
- Prediction latency remains high; in many applications, this is the primary constraint.
- Bounds’ interpretation and practical guidance are unclear; no empirical validation of theoretical predictions.
- Some experimental comparisons (especially memory) are not fully fair across batch vs. online methods; limited ablations.

Overall evaluation: borderline accept if the authors can strengthen the empirical fairness/ablations and clarify the theoretical contribution; otherwise borderline reject on limited novelty and unclear advantage of the analysis.
