- Proposes a learning reduction for extreme classification (up to ~100k classes) by reducing to pairwise binary classification.  
- Argues existing tree-based methods suffer cascading errors and label-embedding methods fail when label matrices aren’t low-rank.  
- Introduces a scalable, sampling-based reduction with theoretical consistency guarantees relative to multiclass ERM.  
- Experiments on 5 text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 for problems with >30k classes, faster training, and much lower memory use, but slower prediction.  
- Introduces “Aggressive Double Sampling” controlled by two parameters: class sampling frequency inversely proportional to empirical frequency, and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples.  
- Training repeats the sampling procedure to construct the final dyadic (pairwise) classifier.  
- Uses a heuristic prediction-time candidate selection via class centroids to avoid enumerating all class pairwise features.  
- Theoretical analysis is complicated by dependent dyadic examples and by oversampling that shifts the empirical distribution.  
- Proof splits dependent sums according to the induced graph structure and applies Janson’s concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables.  
- Finite-sample risk bound carries bias from true vs. oversampled class probability ratios, but this bias decreases linearly with sample/resample sizes.  
- Reviewer finds the analysis plausible (not fully verified) and the algorithm theoretically sound and practically useful.  
- Overall assessment: strong method for extreme classification with low memory/computation and superior performance on the largest datasets.
