- Proposes a double sampling strategy to handle extremely large multi-class problems (10k–100k classes) by reducing to dyadic binary classification.  
- Introduces two-stage sampling: oversample small classes/subsample large classes to balance, then reduce dyadic pairs via parameter κ for complexity control.  
- Uses aggressive sampling to mitigate long-tailed class distributions and lower computational cost.  
- Provides theoretical generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity that account for sampling and dyadic dependencies.  
- Builds on recent Rademacher complexity results for dependent data, offering meaningful guarantees for the sampling scheme.  
- Validated on DMOZ and Wikipedia; shows improvements in training time, prediction time, memory, accuracy, and macro F1 versus multiple baselines.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree, and PD-Sparse across datasets.  
- Well-organized and clearly written with sufficient experimental detail for reproducibility.  
- Maintains a reasonable connection between theory and implementation, including candidate selection for prediction.  
- Method is well-justified for text classification via an appropriate feature mapping.  
- Novelty is clear: distinguishes from tree-based, label-embedding, and naive multi-class-to-binary approaches; double sampling is a novel contribution.  
- Minor weakness: limited discussion and guidance on hyperparameter choices (π and κ) and sensitivity to these settings.  
- Minor weakness: could better connect theoretical guarantees to observed empirical gains and clarify how sampling affects assumptions.  
- Suggests need for more guidance on selecting κ and more comparisons on smaller-scale problems to contextualize applicability.  
- Overall: technically sound, practically effective, significant contribution for scalable, long-tailed multi-class classification.
