- Addresses extreme multi-class classification using a double sampling strategy on a multi-class-to-binary reduction to combat dimensionality and class imbalance.  
- Provides theoretical generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexity.  
- Demonstrates empirical effectiveness on DMOZ and Wikipedia, improving training/prediction time, memory, and accuracy.  
- Aggressive sampling plus theoretical analysis is a practically relevant and compelling solution.  
- Experimental evaluation includes a detailed ablation study and comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.  
- Theoretical presentation is dense and heavily reliant on prior literature, making accessibility an issue.  
- Heavy, complex notation hampers readability of theorem derivations.  
- Algorithm description, especially the prediction stage and candidate selection (choice of q nearest-centroid classes), lacks sufficient detail and pseudocode.  
- Insufficient analysis of why the method performs well on long-tailed distributions.  
- Feature mapping (φ) is only briefly described; its effect and sensitivity to the number of features p are unclear.  
- Hyperparameter tuning process is underspecified; parameter ranges explored should be clarified in the appendix.  
- Limited discussion and explicit comparison with recent extreme-classification advances (e.g., neural embeddings, specialized data structures).  
- Overall assessment: significant, theoretically sound, and empirically validated contribution; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: add intuitive explanations for theoretical results, clarify notation, provide pseudocode for prediction, elaborate feature mapping, and detail hyperparameter tuning and broader comparisons.
