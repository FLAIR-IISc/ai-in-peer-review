

This paper proposes a novel double sampling strategy for multi-class to binary reduction in large-scale classification problems with extremely large numbers of classes (up to 100,000). The authors address the limitations of existing approaches (OVA, tree-based methods, and label embedding techniques) by introducing a two-step sampling mechanism: first, balancing the class distribution through oversampling small classes and subsampling large classes; second, reducing the number of dyadic examples through a parameter κ. The theoretical contribution includes new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity that demonstrate the consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle under the proposed sampling strategy.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the NIPS literature on extreme classification, building on prior work in multi-class to binary reduction [1, 16, 28] while addressing the critical issue of long-tailed class distributions that plague large-scale classification. The authors' theoretical analysis is rigorous, with Theorems 1 and 2 extending previous results by accounting for the inter-dependency between dyadic examples introduced by the sampling strategy.

The experimental evaluation on DMOZ and Wikipedia collections with 10,000-100,000 classes demonstrates the practical advantages of the proposed method. The authors show significant improvements in training time, prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance (measured by accuracy and macro F1) compared to state-of-the-art approaches, particularly on large-scale datasets where OVA and M-SVM become computationally intractable. The comparison with six baselines (OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse) is comprehensive and well-motivated.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. A well-motivated approach to a significant problem in large-scale machine learning
2. Rigorous theoretical analysis that addresses a key limitation in prior work
3. Comprehensive experimental evaluation on challenging datasets
4. Clear presentation of both theoretical and empirical contributions
5. Practical implementation details that make the method accessible

The main weaknesses are:
1. The paper would benefit from more detailed explanation of why the double sampling strategy works well for long-tailed distributions. A concrete example illustrating the imbalance in the original data versus the balanced data after sampling would strengthen the argument.
2. The theoretical analysis relies on the fractional Rademacher complexity, which may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with this concept. A brief intuitive explanation of how the local fractional Rademacher complexity leads to improved bounds would be helpful.
3. The paper mentions that the class centroids are computed during the preliminary projection, but doesn't clarify how this affects the prediction time complexity in practice. A more detailed discussion of the prediction time complexity would be valuable.
4. The choice of the specific features for text classification (Table 1) is somewhat arbitrary without justification for why these particular features are optimal for the sampling strategy.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical notation and sufficient experimental details to reproduce the results. The authors have addressed related work thoroughly, and the comparison with recent approaches (including those from NIPS 2016-2017) demonstrates the novelty of their contribution.

For future work, it would be interesting to explore how the double sampling strategy performs on non-text classification problems (e.g., image classification with large numbers of categories) to demonstrate broader applicability.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of large-scale classification. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental results are compelling, and the practical benefits are significant for real-world applications with extreme numbers of classes. The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the NIPS community, and I believe it would be valuable for researchers working on large-scale machine learning problems.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound with well-supported theoretical and empirical results
- Clarity: Well-organized and clearly written
- Originality: Novel double sampling strategy with theoretical justification
- Significance: Addresses an important problem with practical implications for large-scale classification

I recommend acceptance. The paper has a clear contribution to the field, the results are compelling, and the methodology is sound. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed in the final version but do not detract from the paper's overall quality and contribution.
