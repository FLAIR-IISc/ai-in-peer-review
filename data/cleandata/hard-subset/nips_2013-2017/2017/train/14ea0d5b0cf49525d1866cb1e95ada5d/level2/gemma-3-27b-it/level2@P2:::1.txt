This paper addresses the problem of large-scale multi-class classification, a crucial area for applications like text categorization with massive numbers of classes. The authors propose a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction technique, aiming to mitigate the curse of dimensionality and class imbalance commonly found in such scenarios. They provide theoretical guarantees for their approach in terms of generalization bounds and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets.

The key strength of this paper lies in its practical relevance. Extreme multi-class classification is a challenging problem, and the proposed approach directly tackles the computational and memory bottlenecks. The aggressive sampling strategy, combined with the theoretical analysis, offers a compelling solution. The generalization bounds derived using local Rademacher complexity are a valuable contribution, providing a theoretical foundation for the method's consistency. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed approach in terms of training/prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods. The detailed ablation study and comparison with existing techniques are well-presented.

However, there are several weaknesses. The presentation of the theoretical results is dense and relies heavily on existing literature (e.g., graph covering, Rademacher complexity). While the citations are present, a more intuitive explanation of the underlying concepts would benefit readers less familiar with these advanced techniques. The notation is also quite heavy, making it challenging to follow the derivation of the theorems. The algorithm description (particularly the prediction stage with candidate selection) lacks detail; a pseudocode clarifying the choice of 'q' nearest-centroid classes would be useful. While the authors acknowledge limitations of other methods, a more thorough discussion on why *their* method performs particularly well in certain scenarios (e.g., long-tailed distributions) would strengthen the analysis. The feature mapping (φ) is briefly described but could benefit from further elaboration, especially regarding its impact on performance. It's unclear how sensitive the performance is to the choice of the 'p' features. The hyperparameter tuning process is mentioned but the specific parameter ranges explored could be clarified in the appendix. Also, while the authors mention prior work, they could more explicitly contrast their approach with recent developments in extreme classification (beyond the listed baselines). For example, methods leveraging neural embeddings or specialized data structures might be worth discussing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant problem in machine learning with practical relevance.
*   Proposes a novel and effective approach with strong empirical results.
*   Provides theoretical guarantees for the proposed method.
*   Clearly demonstrates the efficiency gains in terms of time, memory, and accuracy.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Theoretical presentation is dense and potentially inaccessible to a broader audience.
*   Algorithm descriptions lack sufficient detail.
*   Analysis of the advantages of the proposed method over alternatives could be deeper.
*   Limited discussion of recent advances in extreme classification.

**Overall assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale multi-class classification. The proposed approach is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The benefits in terms of efficiency and performance are substantial. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and provide more detailed explanations of the theoretical concepts and algorithm implementation. Specifically, adding more intuition to the theoretical derivations, elaborating on the feature mapping, and providing a more complete description of the hyperparameter tuning process would significantly enhance the paper's impact.
