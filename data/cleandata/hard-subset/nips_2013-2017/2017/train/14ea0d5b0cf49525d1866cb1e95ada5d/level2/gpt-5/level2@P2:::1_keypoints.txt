- Proposes dyadic reduction for extreme multiclass with aggressive double sampling: per-class rebalancing (π) and κ negative-class subsampling.  
- Derives dependency-aware generalization bounds using fractional graph covers and local Rademacher complexities.  
- Uses nearest-centroid candidate selection at prediction time to accelerate inference.  
- Empirical results on datasets up to 100K classes show strong training/prediction time and memory, with competitive or improved accuracy and Macro-F1; code released.  
- Builds on multiclass-to-binary reductions, dyadic/label-embedding work, and dependent-sample generalization literature; novelty is combining double sampling with dependency-aware analysis.  
- Theoretical contribution: variance-sensitive rates via local Rademacher complexities on fractional covers, with bounds depending on α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y.  
- Practical implications of α and β are under-discussed; aggressive rebalancing can inflate constants and the √(K−1)/(Mκ) factor worsens if κ is small.  
- Method strengths: simple, sensible double sampling for long tails; clear dyadic feature map for text; practical centroid-based preselection.  
- Empirics are compelling on scale and Macro-F1, but important ablations (π, κ, q) and comparisons to uniform sampling/class-weighted losses are missing.  
- The multilabel-to-multiclass replication strategy needs discussion for its effect on class priors and long-tail statistics.  
- Paper is generally clear and organized, though some notation (dyadic transform, k<y convention) and the theoretical intuition for covers/local complexity could be streamlined.  
- Originality: methodological novelty in double sampling + candidate selection; theoretical novelty in applying local Rademacher complexities to fractional covers for this reduction.  
- Significance: practical and potentially useful for extreme multiclass, but broader datasets (beyond text) and stronger ablations would increase impact.  
- Pros: clear motivation, dependency-aware bounds, strong large-scale empirical performance, and open-source implementation.  
- Cons: limited sensitivity analysis of π, κ, q; thin interpretation of bounds and potentially large constants; fairness concerns in baseline comparisons due to feature/candidate differences; missing comparisons to hard-negative/one-against-some baselines.  
- Suggested improvements: ablation sweeps for κ, π, q (including no candidate selection); report magnitudes of α, β and guidance for π; add class-reweighting/no-subsampling baselines and comparisons to hard-negative/one-against-some methods; clarify multilabel conversion and report multilabel metrics.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
