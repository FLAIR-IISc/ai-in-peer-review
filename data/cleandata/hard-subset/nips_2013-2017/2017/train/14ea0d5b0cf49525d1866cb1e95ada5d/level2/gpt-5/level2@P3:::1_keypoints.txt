- Proposes a (π, κ)-double sampling reduction for extreme multi-class: class-aware resampling (π) plus limiting dyadic pairs per example to κ negatives.  
- Learns by minimizing empirical pairwise ranking error in dyadic space and predicts via candidate selection over q nearest class centroids.  
- Theoretically derives high-probability generalization bounds for dependent dyadic data using local fractional Rademacher complexities, exposing dependence on κ and sampling via α and β.  
- Shows consistency of ERM on the sampled reduction despite distribution shift and pair dependencies.  
- Empirically competitive accuracy/MaF1 on LSHTC-derived datasets with 10k–100k classes and substantially reduced training/prediction time and memory versus OVA, M-SVM, tree/embedding methods, and PD-Sparse.  
- Builds on prior dyadic reductions and fractional-cover analyses but integrates double sampling and dependency-aware theory in a novel way.  
- Theory caveat: training uses unweighted ERM while bounds scale with α; importance-weighted ERM could better match target risk and tighten constants.  
- Method is simple, scalable, and implementable; uniform negative sampling is reasonable but hard-negative mining could improve efficiency.  
- Prediction candidate-selection is a practical heuristic leveraging precomputed centroids to cut evaluation cost.  
- Experiments lack ablations on π, κ, and q, and do not report per-dataset candidate-set recall, statistical significance, or variance across runs.  
- Clarity is generally good; some details (dyadic feature list, complexity pseudocode, sampling-with/without-replacement, π normalization) should be moved/clarified in main text.  
- Originality lies in combining aggressive class-aware subsampling, dyadic centroid candidate selection, and dependency-aware generalization bounds.  
- Significance: offers a practical computational–statistical trade-off for extreme K and theoretical interest for interdependent-example learning under biased sampling.  
- Pros: principled scalable reduction with empirical resource gains, dependency-aware theory, simple candidate pruning, and open-source code/standard benchmarks.  
- Cons: missing sensitivity analyses for π/κ/q, unweighted ERM issues, potential unfairness in feature representation comparisons, and no candidate-set recall or run variance reported.  
- Essential improvements requested: add κ/ q/ π ablations (accuracy, time, memory, candidate recall), test/justify importance weighting, and report per-dataset resources and run variances.  
- Optional improvements: compare hard-negative sampling, provide contraction link to surrogates, and discuss/apply to non-text domains or show learned dyadic features.  
- Overall: a solid, timely contribution combining practical scaling and nontrivial theory; stronger with added ablations and clarification on weighting and candidate recall.
