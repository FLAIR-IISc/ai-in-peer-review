## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of large-scale multi-class classification, specifically focusing on the computational and memory limitations arising from long-tailed class distributions. The authors propose a double sampling strategy applied *on top* of a multi-class to binary reduction technique, aiming to reduce the number of pairwise examples and maintain consistency of empirical risk minimization. The core idea – aggressive sampling to balance class representation and reduce the dyadic expansion – is a reasonable approach to tackle the scalability issues inherent in this problem setting.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing approaches like one-vs-all and label embedding methods. The theoretical contribution, establishing generalization bounds for the proposed method using local Rademacher complexity, is a significant strength. The authors correctly identify the challenges posed by the sampling process and attempt to address them through rigorous analysis. The experimental evaluation on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets demonstrates the efficiency of the proposed approach in terms of training/prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance, showing competitive results against state-of-the-art methods.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is present, the connection between the theoretical bounds and the practical performance gains isn't explicitly highlighted. The description of the feature mapping (φ) is somewhat brief; more detail on its implementation and impact on performance would be beneficial. The parameter tuning process is mentioned but lacks specifics – a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter selection would strengthen the reproducibility of the results. Additionally, the presentation could be improved by including a more intuitive explanation of the fractional Rademacher complexity and its relevance to the proposed method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in large-scale classification.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated sampling strategy.
*   Provides theoretical justification for the approach with generalization bounds.
*   Demonstrates competitive empirical performance on benchmark datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Connection between theory and practice could be strengthened.
*   Feature mapping description is brief.
*   Parameter tuning details are lacking.
*   Theoretical concepts could be explained more intuitively.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale multi-class classification. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical analysis, and the promising experimental results suggest that this work has the potential to significantly impact the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding clarity, detail, and the connection between theory and practice. The authors should be encouraged to further explore the practical implications of their theoretical findings and provide more detailed insights into the implementation aspects of their method.
