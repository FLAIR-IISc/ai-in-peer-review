- Proposal: (π, κ)-double sampling combines class-rebalancing (π) and negative subsampling (κ) with a multiclass-to-binary dyadic reduction for extreme multiclass problems.
- Theory: New generalization bounds for ERM over interdependent dyadic pairs using local fractional Rademacher complexity, showing consistency under sampling-induced distribution shift and dependencies.
- Prediction engineering: Nearest-centroid candidate pruning to reduce scoring cost at inference.
- Empirics: Competitive accuracy and macro-F1 with favorable training/prediction time and memory on LSHTC/DMOZ and Wikipedia subsets up to 100K classes.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on dyadic reductions and dependent-sample analysis, avoids tree error propagation and brittle low-rank embeddings; novelty in imbalance-aware π and negative subsampling κ plus local complexity bounds.
- Strength — scalability: (π, κ)-DS substantially reduces pairs and mitigates class imbalance, enabling feasible training at 50K–100K classes where OVA/M-SVM fail.
- Strength — theoretical contribution: Careful extension to local fractional Rademacher complexity that accounts for reweighting and pair dependencies.
- Strength — problem framing: Proper focus on long-tailed label distributions relevant to extreme classification.
- Strength — prediction-time simplicity: Candidate pruning is simple, cheap, and integrates cleanly with dyadic scoring.
- Strength — reproducibility: Code and datasets provided; baselines are recent and appropriate.
- Weakness — ablations missing: No sensitivity analyses disentangling effects of π, κ, and candidate size q.
- Weakness — bound looseness: Dependence on α = max ηy/πy and β = max 1/πy may make bounds vacuous under aggressive sampling; no empirical diagnostics provided.
- Weakness — feature mapping φ: Uses 10 hand-crafted text-specific features, limiting generality; no learned φ or non-text experiments.
- Weakness — candidate pruning risk: No reported recall of true label in candidate sets or accuracy vs q curves.
- Weakness — baseline coverage: Missing dyadic baseline without double sampling and hard-negative mining comparisons.
- Weakness — classifier details: Learning algorithm for dyadic classifier f (loss, regularization, optimizer) is not clearly specified.
- Quality: Analysis is technically sound but some constants and K/κ/π dependencies reduce interpretability; simplified corollaries or intuition would help.
- Empirical rigor: Datasets and metrics are appropriate, but lack of ablations limits insight into when the method works best.
- Clarity: Well organized overall, but notation is dense in places and proof sketches/complexity statements are sparse.
- Originality: Moderately strong — novel combination of imbalance-aware sampling with dyadic reduction and local fractional Rademacher analysis.
- Significance: High practical significance for extreme, long-tailed multiclass problems; offers a useful resource-accuracy trade-off.
- Suggestions: Add κ/π/q ablations, report candidate recall and accuracy vs q, compare to dyadic/no-DS and hard-negative sampling baselines, clarify classifier f and complexity, provide empirical bound diagnostics, and include a non-text dataset or learned φ.
- For acceptance: Addresses an important problem with a simple, scalable method, nontrivial theory, and reproducible results.
- Against acceptance: Insufficient ablations/sensitivity analysis, limited generality due to text-specific φ, potentially loose bounds, and incomplete evaluation of candidate pruning.
- Recommendation: Weak accept (reviewer confidence: medium).
