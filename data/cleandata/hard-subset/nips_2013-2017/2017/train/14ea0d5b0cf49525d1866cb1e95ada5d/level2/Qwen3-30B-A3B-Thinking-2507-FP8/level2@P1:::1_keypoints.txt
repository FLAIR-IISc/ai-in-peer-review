- Proposes a novel double sampling strategy for multi-class to binary reduction targeting extreme classification with up to 100,000 classes.  
- Two-step sampling: balance class distribution by oversampling small and subsampling large classes, then reduce dyadic examples via a parameter κ.  
- Theoretical contribution: new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity showing ERM consistency under the sampling scheme.  
- Rigorous analysis (Theorems 1–2) that accounts for inter-dependencies between dyadic examples introduced by sampling.  
- Experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia (10k–100k classes) show substantial gains in training time, prediction time, memory, accuracy, and macro F1.  
- Comprehensive empirical comparison against six baselines (OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse).  
- Strength: well-motivated approach addressing long-tailed class distributions in large-scale ML.  
- Strength: solid and rigorous theoretical analysis extending prior work.  
- Strength: thorough experimental evaluation on challenging datasets.  
- Strength: clear presentation and accessible practical implementation details.  
- Weakness: needs clearer, concrete explanation/examples of why double sampling helps long-tailed distributions.  
- Weakness: fractional Rademacher complexity may be hard to follow; add an intuitive explanation of how it improves bounds.  
- Weakness: unclear how computing class centroids during projection affects practical prediction-time complexity—needs more detail.  
- Weakness: feature choice for text classification (Table 1) lacks justification for optimality with the sampling strategy.  
- Paper is well-written, reproducible, novel, significant, and meets NIPS criteria; recommended for acceptance.  
- Suggestion for future work: evaluate the double sampling approach on non-text tasks (e.g., large-scale image classification) to show broader applicability.
