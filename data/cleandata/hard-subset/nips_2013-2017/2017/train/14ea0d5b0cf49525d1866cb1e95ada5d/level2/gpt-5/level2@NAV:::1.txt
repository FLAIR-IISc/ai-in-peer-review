Summary and relation to prior work: The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to binary classification over dyadic example–class pairs, and proposes an aggressive double sampling scheme: (i) class-balanced sampling of training examples (π) to mitigate long-tailed label distributions, and (ii) sampling κ adversarial classes per example to shrink the dyadic expansion. The authors provide generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity that account for distribution shift and dependency among dyadic pairs, and introduce a centroid-based candidate selection for faster prediction. Empirically, on LSHTC-derived DMOZ and Wikipedia subsets with 10K–100K classes, the method improves memory/runtime while maintaining or improving accuracy and macro-F1 versus OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding baselines. The work builds on dyadic reduction (e.g., Usunier & Amini; Joshi et al.) and complements tree and label-embedding methods popular at NIPS for extreme classification.

Quality: The theoretical development appears sound and addresses dependence induced by dyadic construction; however, the bounds depend on α and β (functions of π and class frequencies) that may be large in practice, and the practical loss/optimizer (beyond 0/1 in statements) is not clearly specified. The experimental evaluation is convincing at scale, with appropriate metrics (macro-F1) and strong baselines, though the conversion from multilabel to multiclass by replication could bias results.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized, but key implementation details are underspecified: exact classifier f in F, choice of surrogate loss, regularization, and optimization. The φ mapping is described briefly (10 hand-crafted features), and candidate selection needs clearer discussion of trade-offs.

Originality: The double sampling design and the adaptation of local fractional Rademacher bounds to this reduction are novel and well-motivated. The sampling idea relates to negative sampling/sampled softmax, but that connection is not discussed.

Significance: The method is practically useful for extreme classification: simple to implement, memory- and time-efficient, and competitive in accuracy. The released code strengthens impact.

Pros:
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck with a simple, effective design.
- Provides theory under dependence and distribution shift.
- Strong empirical results at 50K–100K classes; code available.

Cons:
- Missing ablations on π, κ, and candidate set size; unclear sensitivity.
- Under-specified training details; reliance on ad hoc φ limits generality.
- Bounds may have loose constants; limited discussion of their magnitude.
- Multilabel-to-multiclass conversion may distort class priors.

Suggestions: Add ablations varying π, κ, and q (candidate size) with head/tail breakdown; compare to random-negative dyadic baselines; isolate the effect of candidate selection; report top-k metrics and misrouting rates; clarify f, loss, and optimization; discuss α, β scaling; and evaluate with learned φ to demonstrate portability. Overall: a solid contribution; I lean toward acceptance pending strengthened empirical analysis.
