- Addresses extreme multi-class classification by reducing to binary tasks and introduces a novel (π, κ)-double sampling to handle scalability and long-tailed classes.  
- π-sampling re-balances training by over-sampling rare classes and under-sampling frequent ones.  
- κ-sampling pairs each instance with only a small random subset of negative classes, drastically reducing dyadic examples.  
- Provides theoretical generalization bounds (Theorems 1 and 2) showing consistency despite distribution shift and dependencies from sampling.  
- Empirical evaluation on large text benchmarks (up to 100k classes) shows competitive accuracy with much lower training time and memory than strong baselines.  
- Technical quality is high: method is well-motivated, practical, and supported by rigorous theory.  
- Experimental methodology is rigorous, using standard large-scale datasets and diverse strong baselines.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized; algorithm presentation is clear.  
- Clarity issues: joint feature mapping φ needs more detailed explanation and motivation for reproducibility.  
- Clarity issues: the prediction-time candidate count q used in experiments is not reported and its sensitivity is not analyzed.  
- Clarity issues: Theorem 2 would benefit from an intuitive explanation of α and β measuring π-induced distribution shift.  
- Originality: combination of re-sampling with dyadic reduction and theoretical analysis is novel and non-trivial.  
- Significance: method is practically important and impactful, offering a favorable accuracy/time/memory trade-off for extreme classification.  
- Suggested improvements: report q and analyze its effect on accuracy/time; include sensitivity analysis for κ; expand φ details (possibly in supplement).  
- Acceptance arguments: tackles an important problem, proposes a simple effective method with sound theory and strong empirical results.  
- Rejection argument: minor reproducibility gaps (missing q and κ sensitivity) that are easily fixable.
