Summary
The paper tackles extreme multiclass classification by reducing it to a binary problem over pairs (example, class) via a dyadic representation, and introduces an aggressive double sampling strategy: (i) instance-level class-rebalancing via per-class inclusion probabilities π to mitigate long-tail distributions, and (ii) negative class subsampling with κ adversarial classes per instance to reduce pair explosion. The authors derive generalization bounds for the dependent dyadic samples using fractional graph covers and local Rademacher complexities, arguing that ERM on the doubly sampled reduction remains consistent despite distribution shift and dependencies. A candidate selection strategy at prediction time further speeds inference by scoring only a small set of nearest-centroid classes. Experiments on LSHTC/DMOZ and Wikipedia subsets with up to 100K classes show favorable training/prediction time, memory, and competitive or improved accuracy/Macro-F1 versus OVA, multi-class SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on multiclass-to-binary reductions and dyadic/label-embedding approaches (e.g., WSABIE, AdaBoost.MR, multiclass SVMs), and on dependent-sample generalization analysis via fractional Rademacher complexity. It contrasts with tree-based and embedding methods aimed at logarithmic inference and with sparse OVA/PD-Sparse methods. The paper cites relevant NeurIPS/ICML lines (e.g., Usunier et al. on interdependent data, Ralaivola & Amini for dependent concentration, SLEEC), as well as recent extreme classification systems (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree, PD-Sparse). The main differentiator is the combination of double sampling for scalability with a dependency-aware generalization analysis using local complexities.

Quality
- Theory: The use of local Rademacher complexities on fractional covers to obtain variance-sensitive rates for dyadically dependent samples is interesting. The bounds explicitly account for κ and π through α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y. However, the practical implications of these constants are under-discussed: for aggressive rebalancing (π ∝ 1/η), α can become large for head classes; the bound also carries a √(K−1)/(Mκ) factor that worsens if κ is small. The paper claims consistency, but a more explicit discussion of conditions on π, κ, and M under which the bounds remain non-vacuous would strengthen the argument.
- Method: The double sampling strategy is simple and sensible for long-tailed label distributions; the candidate class preselection via centroids is a practical inference accelerator. The dyadic feature map for text (10 IR-inspired features) is clearly described.
- Empirics: Results on large K are compelling in runtime/memory; accuracy and especially Macro-F1 benefit from rebalancing. That said, important ablations are missing: impact of π (degree of rebalancing), κ (negative sampling), and q (candidate set size) on accuracy/time; and comparison to uniform sampling or class-weighted losses without subsampling. The conversion from multilabel to multiclass by replication should be discussed for its effect on class priors and long-tail statistics.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The dyadic transformation T(S) and notational choices (the k<y convention) could be streamlined. The algorithms are clear. Some grammatical issues exist but do not impede understanding. The theoretical section would benefit from a short, self-contained intuition for the cover construction and local complexity terms, and a paragraph interpreting α, β, and the K, κ, M dependencies.

Originality
- Methodological novelty lies in the specific “double sampling” tailored to dyadic reductions and in the candidate selection scheme coupled with a lightweight joint feature map.
- Theoretical novelty is in adapting local Rademacher complexities to the fractional cover setting for this reduction; prior work treated fractional Rademacher complexity but not with local/variance-sensitive rates in this context. This appears new and relevant.

Significance
Extreme multiclass remains an active area; practical, simple methods that deliver good performance with strong resource savings are valuable. The empirical gains at K up to 100K, combined with theoretical support, make the contribution potentially useful for practitioners. The significance would be further bolstered by stronger ablations and broader datasets (beyond text), since the feature map is text-specific.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear motivation and practical algorithm addressing both long tails and pair explosion.
- Dependency-aware generalization bounds with local Rademacher terms; plausible theoretical contribution.
- Strong empirical results on large K: improved time/memory, competitive accuracy and notably higher Macro-F1.
- Open-source implementation; straightforward to adopt.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited ablation and sensitivity analysis of π, κ, and q; unclear robustness and optimal settings.
- Bound interpretation is thin; constants (α, β) may be large under aggressive rebalancing, and the K, κ dependence may be loose.
- Potential fairness issues in comparisons: the proposed method uses a compact dyadic feature map and candidate selection, while some baselines operate in the original high-dimensional space. It is hard to disentangle the benefits of sampling vs. feature engineering and candidate pruning.
- No comparison to closely related sampling baselines (e.g., one-against-some, hard negative mining) or to recent label-tree methods using better balancing heuristics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations: (i) sweep κ and report accuracy/Macro-F1 vs time/memory; (ii) vary π from uniform to inverse-frequency; (iii) sweep q for candidate selection and plot accuracy-time trade-offs. Include a “no candidate selection” variant.
- Discuss and, if possible, empirically examine the magnitude of α and β under your π choices; provide guidance on setting π to keep bounds and practice aligned.
- Add a baseline with class reweighting (without subsampling) and/or negative sampling within existing OVA/PD-Sparse frameworks to isolate the effect of the double sampling.
- Compare against “one-against-some” and/or error-correcting tournaments where feasible.
- Clarify the multilabel-to-multiclass conversion and its impact on long-tail distribution; consider reporting multilabel metrics on at least one dataset.
- Expand related work to situate against hard-negative sampling and retrieval-based candidate generation used in extreme classification and retrieval.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper proposes a simple, scalable approach with a meaningful theoretical angle and promising large-scale results. Strengthening ablations and clarifying theoretical constants and fairness of comparisons would substantially improve the work.
