

This paper proposes a novel double sampling strategy ("(π, κ)-DS") to address class imbalance and computational inefficiency in large-scale multi-class classification, particularly for text datasets with 10,000–100,000 classes. The core claim is that aggressive sampling of both classes (oversampling small classes, undersampling large classes) and dyadic examples (reducing the number of pairs per instance) maintains consistency of empirical risk minimization while significantly improving scalability. The authors extend prior work on multi-class-to-binary reduction [16] by introducing theoretical guarantees via fractional Rademacher complexity (Theorems 1–2) and demonstrate empirical superiority over state-of-the-art methods (OVA, FastXML, PD-Sparse) on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets.

Strengths: The experimental results are compelling and well-justified. The paper clearly shows dramatic reductions in training time (up to 10× faster than OVA/M-SVM), memory usage (avoiding terabyte-scale requirements), and comparable or better predictive performance (accuracy, macro-F1) on large datasets (WIKI-50K/WIKI-100K). The candidate selection strategy for prediction is practical and effectively mitigates the cost of large class spaces. The theoretical analysis, while relying on supplementary material, correctly contextualizes the consistency argument within the dependent dyadic data structure.

Weaknesses: The lack of statistical significance tests for performance metrics (e.g., p-values for F1 differences) weakens the empirical claims. The hyperparameter selection (π, κ) is under-justified—while π is set inversely proportional to class size, the paper does not analyze how sensitivity to κ affects results or propose a principled method for choosing it. The discussion of related work omits recent advances in extreme classification (e.g., [30] PD-Sparse is cited but not contrasted with the authors' approach beyond results), reducing the novelty context. The theoretical bounds (Theorem 2) assume specific conditions (e.g., bounded variance) that are not empirically validated.

Overall, this is a strong, practical contribution to large-scale classification. The empirical results are robust and address a genuine pain point in the field (class imbalance + scalability). While the theoretical depth could be strengthened, the paper’s clear problem statement, sound experiments, and tangible efficiency gains make it a valuable addition to NIPS. The main gap is the insufficient discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and statistical rigor in evaluation. With minor revisions to address these points, this work would be highly suitable for acceptance.
