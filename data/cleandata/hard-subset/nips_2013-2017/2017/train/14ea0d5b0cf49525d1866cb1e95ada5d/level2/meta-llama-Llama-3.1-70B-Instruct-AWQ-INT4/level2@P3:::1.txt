This paper proposes a novel approach to multi-class classification problems by transforming the original problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples. The authors introduce a double sampling strategy to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions and reduce the number of pairs of examples in the expanded data. The proposed method is evaluated on several large-scale text classification datasets and compared with state-of-the-art approaches.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed method. The theoretical results, including the generalization bounds, are well-established and provide a solid foundation for the approach. The experimental evaluation is also comprehensive, covering various datasets and comparing the proposed method with several state-of-the-art approaches.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method, including the theoretical background and the experimental setup. The notation is consistent, and the figures and tables are well-organized and easy to understand.

**Originality:** The proposed approach is novel and addresses a significant problem in multi-class classification. The double sampling strategy is an innovative solution to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions, and the theoretical analysis provides new insights into the problem.

**Significance:** The paper has significant implications for large-scale multi-class classification problems, particularly in text classification. The proposed method is shown to be competitive with state-of-the-art approaches in terms of predictive performance, while also providing significant improvements in terms of memory usage and computational complexity.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

Pro:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to multi-class classification problems, which is technically sound and well-evaluated.
* The double sampling strategy is an innovative solution to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions.
* The theoretical analysis provides new insights into the problem and establishes a solid foundation for the approach.
* The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering various datasets and comparing the proposed method with several state-of-the-art approaches.

Con:

* The paper assumes a specific form of the loss function, which might not be applicable to all multi-class classification problems.
* The proposed method requires careful tuning of hyperparameters, which can be time-consuming and may not be feasible for very large datasets.
* The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which might be a concern for very large datasets.

**Recommendations for Revision:**

* Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method.
* Discuss the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future research.
* Consider providing additional experimental results on other datasets or problem domains to further demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

Overall, this paper is a strong contribution to the field of multi-class classification, and with some revisions, it has the potential to be a top-tier publication.
