Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem via dyadic example–class pairs, and then aggressively sub-sampling to address both computational cost and long-tailed class imbalance. The proposed (π, κ)-double sampling first class-balances the training set by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones (π), then limits the number of dyadic pairs per example to κ adversarial classes. The learning minimizes an empirical pairwise ranking error in the dyadic space; prediction uses a candidate-selection heuristic that restricts scoring to q nearest class centroids. Theoretical contributions derive generalization bounds for dependent dyadic data using local fractional Rademacher complexities, showing that ERM on the sampled reduction remains consistent despite distribution shift and pair dependencies. Experiments on LSHTC-derived DMOZ/Wikipedia datasets with 10k–100k classes demonstrate competitive or better predictive performance with substantially reduced training/prediction time and memory compared to OVA, M-SVM, tree/embedding methods (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree), and PD-Sparse.

The work builds on multi-class-to-binary dyadic reductions (e.g., [16]), interdependent-data generalization analysis via fractional covers [25] and local Rademacher complexities [22], and contrasts with tree-based [4, 9, 21, 15] and label-embedding approaches [11, 19]. The combination of a principled double sampling scheme with theory that accounts for both dependency and induced class-distribution shift appears novel.

Quality
- Theory: Theorems 1–2 provide high-probability generalization bounds for the dependent dyadic setting. The use of local fractional Rademacher complexity is well motivated, and the bounds expose key dependencies on κ and the sampling distribution via α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy. This clarifies the bias–variance trade-offs induced by aggressive sampling. One caveat is that the training objective is the unweighted empirical loss on the sampled set, while the bound scales it by α; importance-weighted ERM would more closely align optimization with the target risk and could tighten constants. The connection to standard surrogates (hinge/logistic) is implicit; an explicit contraction argument would improve completeness.
- Method: The (π, κ)-DS procedure is simple, scalable, and easy to implement. Uniform negative sampling is a reasonable default; hard-negative mining could further improve sample efficiency. The candidate-selection prediction step is a practical heuristic that leverages precomputed centroids in the dyadic space.
- Empirics: Results on multiple LSHTC datasets up to 100k classes show strong runtime/memory advantages over OVA/M-SVM and competitive accuracy/MaF1 against PD-Sparse and substantially better than tree methods. Code and data are provided. However, ablations on π, κ, and q are missing; per-dataset candidate-set recall (probability the true label is in the q candidates) would help quantify the impact of candidate pruning. Statistical significance and variance across runs are not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The reduction, sampling procedure, and prediction algorithm are presented concretely, with helpful intuition about class imbalance and dependency. Some details are deferred to the supplement (proofs; hyperparameters). Minor improvements: explicitly list or summarize the 10 dyadic features in the main text; provide pseudocode complexity annotations; clarify whether sampling is with or without replacement and whether class-proportional π is normalized.

Originality
The approach is a novel combination of:
- aggressive, class-aware subsampling for dyadic reductions,
- candidate selection at prediction time using dyadic centroids, and
- generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity under both dependency and shifted class distributions.
While dyadic reductions and fractional-cover analyses exist ([16], [25], [22]), their integration with a double sampling scheme and the concrete large-scale text instantiation appears original.

Significance
Extreme classification requires strong computational–statistical trade-offs. The proposed method offers a practical path to scale with large K while maintaining accuracy, making it useful to practitioners working with long-tailed label distributions. The theoretical analysis may interest researchers studying learning with interdependent examples and biased sampling. Generality beyond text rests on availability or learnability of effective dyadic features; the paper argues this is feasible in other domains.

Pros
- Clear, principled, and scalable reduction with substantial empirical gains in time and memory at 50k–100k classes.
- Theoretical bounds that explicitly capture dependency and sampling-induced shift, supporting consistency claims.
- Simple prediction-time candidate selection that further reduces cost.
- Open-source implementation and use of standard benchmarks.

Cons
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses for π (class-sampling), κ (negative sampling), and q (candidate size).
- Unweighted ERM under class-skewed sampling leads to an α factor in the bounds; importance weighting is neither analyzed nor tried.
- Comparisons involve a 10-dimensional dyadic feature map versus high-dimensional BoW features for some baselines; a discussion of fairness and representational capacity would strengthen the claims.
- No report of candidate-set recall, statistical significance, or variance across runs.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential (feasible within rebuttal/revision):
- Add ablation studies: vary κ and report accuracy/MaF1, training time, and memory; vary q and report candidate-set recall and accuracy to quantify the effect of candidate pruning; show sensitivity to π (e.g., inversely proportional vs. tempered).
- Report whether importance weighting by 1/πy was tried; if not, add a comparison or justify unweighted ERM empirically.
- Include per-dataset resource usage and top-line numbers in the main text (currently summarized in figures/tables), and report standard deviations over multiple runs or folds.

Optional (nice-to-have):
- Compare to alternative negative sampling (hard negatives via nearest impostor classes) to assess potential gains at fixed κ.
- Provide a brief contraction argument linking the 0/1 loss bounds to the surrogate used in training.
- Discuss generalization to non-text domains with examples of φ learned or constructed features; include a small-scale non-text dataset if available.

Overall assessment
A solid and timely contribution to extreme multi-class classification that combines a practical reduction/sampling strategy with nontrivial theory and convincing large-scale experiments. With added ablations and clarification on weighting and candidate recall, the paper would be stronger and its empirical claims more robust.
