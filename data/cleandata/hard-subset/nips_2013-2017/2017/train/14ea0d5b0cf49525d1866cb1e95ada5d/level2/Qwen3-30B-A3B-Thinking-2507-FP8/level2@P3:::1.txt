

The paper proposes a double sampling strategy to address large-scale multi-class classification with very large numbers of classes (10,000-100,000). It builds upon multi-class to binary reduction approaches by introducing aggressive sampling to overcome long-tailed class distributions and reduce computational complexity. The authors transform the multi-class problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples, then apply two-stage sampling: first oversampling small classes and subsampling large classes to balance the distribution, and second reducing the number of dyadic examples through a parameter κ. The theoretical contribution includes new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity that account for the sampling strategy and inter-dependency between dyadic examples. The approach is validated on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets, showing improvements in computational efficiency and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper makes several strong contributions. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with careful derivation of generalization bounds that account for the specific sampling strategy and dependencies introduced in the dyadic representation. The bounds build upon recent advances in Rademacher complexity for dependent data, providing meaningful theoretical guarantees for the proposed approach. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple relevant baselines (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree, PD-Sparse) across multiple datasets with varying class sizes. The results demonstrate significant improvements in training time, prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance (accuracy and macro F1) for large-scale problems, particularly highlighting the method's resilience to long-tailed class distributions.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail to reproduce the experiments. The motivation for the double sampling strategy is compelling, addressing a real challenge in large-scale classification where class imbalance severely impacts performance. The connection between theoretical analysis and practical implementation is reasonably well-maintained, particularly in the candidate selection strategy for prediction. The method's applicability to text classification is well-justified through the specific feature mapping for text data.

A minor weakness is the limited discussion of the practical implications of the hyperparameters π and κ. While the paper mentions setting π proportional to 1/μk (where μk is the class proportion), it does not explore how sensitive the results are to this specific choice or provide guidance for tuning these parameters in different domains. The connection between the theoretical guarantees and the observed performance gains could also be strengthened with more explicit discussion of how the sampling strategy affects the key assumptions in the generalization bounds.

The paper's significance is clear: large-scale multi-class classification is a critical problem in applications like text tagging and recommendation systems, where class counts can reach hundreds of thousands. The proposed method offers a practical solution that scales well to these extreme settings while maintaining good predictive performance. The approach represents a meaningful advance over existing methods, particularly in handling long-tailed distributions, which is a common challenge in real-world classification problems.

The paper's novelty is well-established through a thorough review of related work, distinguishing the approach from tree-based methods (which suffer from error propagation), label embedding methods (which rely on questionable low-rank assumptions), and standard multi-class to binary reduction (which becomes computationally intractable for large K). The double sampling strategy is a novel contribution to this space, with the theoretical guarantees providing a solid foundation for the approach.

The paper is technically sound, with appropriate experimental validation against multiple baselines across multiple datasets. The results consistently demonstrate the proposed method's advantages in computational efficiency while maintaining competitive predictive performance. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and addresses the specific challenges introduced by the sampling strategy, providing meaningful generalization bounds that support the empirical findings.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between the sampling parameters (π and κ) and their impact on performance. While the paper mentions that κ controls the number of dyadic examples, it does not provide guidance on how to select this parameter for different problem settings. Additionally, a more thorough comparison of the method's performance on smaller-scale problems (where traditional approaches might still be feasible) would help contextualize the method's applicability.

Overall, this paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and practically effective approach to large-scale multi-class classification. The contributions to both theory and practice are significant, and the method demonstrates clear advantages over state-of-the-art approaches in the challenging regime of extremely large numbers of classes. The paper is well-written and makes a strong contribution to the field of large-scale classification.
