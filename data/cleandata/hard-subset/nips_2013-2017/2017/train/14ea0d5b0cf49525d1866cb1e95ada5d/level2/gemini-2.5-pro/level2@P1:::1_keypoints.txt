- Tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing K-class problems to binary pairs (instance, class) but notes naive reduction yields O(mK) examples, which is infeasible.  
- Proposes (π, κ)-DS "aggressive double sampling": class-aware sampling π to rebalance classes and κ adversarial negatives per example to avoid pairing with all K classes.  
- Double sampling dramatically reduces training set size, enabling scalability to tens of thousands of classes.  
- Provides theoretical analysis (Theorems 1 and 2) using local Rademacher complexities showing consistency despite π-induced distribution shift and dependency from the reduction.  
- Empirical evaluation on large-scale text benchmarks (DMOZ, WIKI up to 100k classes) shows state-of-the-art accuracy while remaining computationally feasible.  
- Significant practical impact: balances predictive accuracy, training/prediction time, and memory for extreme classification.  
- Methodologically solid: well-motivated algorithm, thorough empirical comparisons to strong baselines, and non-trivial theoretical justification.  
- Novelty lies in the aggressive double sampling scheme and a generalization analysis that accounts for sampling shift and dependencies.  
- Main weakness: method relies on a hand-crafted joint feature mapping φ (10-dim IR/LTR features) whose design and transferability to other domains (images, recommender systems) is unclear.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity not fully explored: sampling probabilities π, negative count κ, and prediction candidate size q need more analysis/trade-off plots.  
- Presentation of theoretical results could use more intuition (e.g., roles of α and β in Theorem 2) to clarify implications for practice.  
- Overall judgment: high-quality, practical, and theoretically backed contribution with minor concerns about generality and hyperparameter guidance; recommended for acceptance.
