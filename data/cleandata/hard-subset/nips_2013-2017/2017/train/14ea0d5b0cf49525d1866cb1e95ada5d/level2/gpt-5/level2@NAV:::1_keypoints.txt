- Paper reduces extreme multiclass to binary dyadic pairs and proposes double sampling: class-balanced example sampling (π) and κ adversarial classes per example.
- Provides generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity accounting for distribution shift and pair dependence.
- Introduces centroid-based candidate selection to speed prediction.
- Empirically improves memory/runtime and maintains or improves accuracy/macro-F1 on 10K–100K-class datasets versus strong baselines.
- Builds on prior dyadic reductions and complements tree/embedding approaches in extreme classification.
- Theoretical development is sound but bounds depend on α and β (functions of π and class frequencies) that may be large in practice.
- Practical loss and optimizer used in experiments are not clearly specified beyond 0/1 statements.
- Experimental evaluation is convincing at scale with proper metrics, though multilabel-to-multiclass conversion by replication could bias results.
- Presentation is generally clear and well-organized.
- Key implementation details are underspecified: exact classifier f, surrogate loss, regularization, and optimization choices.
- The φ mapping is brief and ad hoc (10 hand-crafted features), limiting generality.
- Candidate selection method needs clearer discussion of trade-offs (accuracy vs. speed).
- Double sampling design and adapting local fractional Rademacher bounds to this reduction are novel and well-motivated.
- The connection to negative sampling/sampled softmax is not discussed.
- Method is practically useful: simple, memory- and time-efficient, and competitive; released code increases impact.
- Missing ablations on π, κ, and candidate size; sensitivity to these hyperparameters is unclear.
- Bounds may have loose constants; paper lacks discussion of their magnitude and practical implications.
- Multilabel-to-multiclass conversion may distort class priors and affect comparisons.
- Suggested additions: ablations over π/κ/q with head/tail breakdown, compare to random-negative dyadic baselines, isolate candidate selection effect, report top-k and misrouting rates, clarify f/loss/optimization, discuss α/β scaling, and evaluate learned φ for portability.
