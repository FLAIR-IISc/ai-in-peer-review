

This paper proposes a "double sampling" strategy to address large-scale multi-class classification with extremely large numbers of classes (10,000-100,000). The authors transform the multi-class problem into a binary classification problem using dyadic representations, then apply aggressive sampling to balance class distributions and reduce the number of dyadic examples. The main novelty is the (π, κ)-DS algorithm that oversamples small classes and undersamples large classes while reducing the number of constructed dyadic examples. The authors provide theoretical guarantees showing the consistency of empirical risk minimization under their sampling strategy, and demonstrate empirical improvements in training time, memory consumption, and predictive performance on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning with clear practical relevance to real-world applications like text classification.
2. The theoretical analysis extends previous work on fractional Rademacher complexity with Theorem 1 and 2, providing meaningful generalization bounds that account for the sampling strategy and inter-dependency between dyadic examples.
3. The experimental evaluation on large-scale datasets (up to 100,000 classes) is comprehensive, with careful comparison to multiple state-of-the-art baselines including tree-based methods, embedding approaches, and recent extreme classification techniques.
4. The results demonstrate clear practical benefits: the proposed method achieves competitive predictive performance while significantly reducing memory usage and training time compared to standard approaches like OVA and M-SVM.

Weaknesses:
1. The theoretical analysis, while technically sound, doesn't fully connect to the practical benefits. The paper claims the sampling strategy "does not alter the consistency," but the proof (Theorem 2) shows a bound with additional terms (α, β) that depend on the sampling strategy. The authors don't quantify how these terms affect the actual generalization error in practice, making it difficult to assess the practical significance of the theoretical guarantee.
2. The comparison with state-of-the-art methods is incomplete. The authors state they excluded SLEEC and LEML because they're "consistently outperformed," but don't provide evidence for this claim. The paper should directly compare with these recent methods to establish true novelty.
3. The experimental setup lacks important details. For example, the paper mentions PD-Sparse uses hashing to reduce memory but doesn't clarify if other methods used similar optimizations. The parameter tuning process isn't sufficiently detailed, making it hard to reproduce results.
4. The paper doesn't sufficiently address why the dyadic representation with sampling is better than simpler approaches like class-balanced sampling without the dyadic transformation. A comparison with this baseline would strengthen the case for the proposed method.

Recommendations:
1. The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of how the theoretical bounds translate to practical performance. Specifically, they should quantify the relationship between the sampling parameters (π, κ) and the generalization error terms in Theorem 2.
2. Include comparisons with SLEEC and LEML to better establish the novelty of their approach. If these methods were indeed outperformed, the authors should provide evidence from their experiments.
3. Clarify the experimental setup details, particularly regarding parameter tuning and whether other methods used similar memory optimization techniques as PD-Sparse.
4. Add a comparison with class-balanced sampling without the dyadic transformation to demonstrate the specific benefits of their approach.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that addresses an important problem with a theoretically grounded approach. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate clear practical benefits for large-scale multi-class classification. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened by better connecting the bounds to practical performance, and the comparison with prior work could be more comprehensive, the paper makes a solid contribution to the field. The method is both theoretically sound and practically useful for extreme classification problems.

The paper meets the quality criteria with technically sound analysis and comprehensive experiments. It's clear and well-organized, though some details could be improved. The novelty is in the specific double sampling strategy applied to the multi-class to binary reduction, which is sufficiently different from prior work. The significance is high given the growing importance of large-scale classification tasks in real-world applications. I recommend acceptance.
