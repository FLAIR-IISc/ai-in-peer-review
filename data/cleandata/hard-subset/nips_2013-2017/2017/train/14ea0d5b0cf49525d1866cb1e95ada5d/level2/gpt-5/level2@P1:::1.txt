Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses extreme multiclass classification (tens to hundreds of thousands of classes) by combining a multiclass-to-binary dyadic reduction with an aggressive “double sampling” scheme. The method first rebalances class frequencies via per-class sampling probabilities π (oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones), then reduces dyadic explosion by sampling κ negative classes per example. The authors derive generalization bounds for the empirical risk minimization over interdependent dyadic pairs using local fractional Rademacher complexity, showing consistency despite distribution shift from the π-sampling and dependency induced by pair construction. They also propose a practical candidate-selection strategy at prediction time using nearest class centroids to reduce scoring cost. Experiments on LSHTC/DMOZ and Wikipedia subsets (up to 100K classes) suggest favorable trade-offs in training/prediction time and memory, with competitive accuracy and macro-F1 compared to OVA/M-SVM/PD-Sparse and tree/embedding baselines (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree).

The work builds on prior dyadic reductions and dependent-sample analysis (e.g., fractional Rademacher complexity and chromatic covers), improves upon tree-based methods that suffer from error propagation, and avoids low-rank label embedding assumptions known to be brittle in extreme settings. Relative to earlier dyadic approaches, the paper adds an explicitly class-imbalance-aware sampling (π) and negative subsampling (κ), together with local complexity bounds that incorporate variance information.

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability: The (π, κ)-DS scheme reduces both the number of pairs and class-imbalance effects, yielding notable gains in runtime and memory, especially at 50K–100K classes where OVA/M-SVM become infeasible.
- Theoretical contribution: New generalization bounds for ERM over interdependent dyadic pairs using local fractional Rademacher complexity. The analysis explicitly accounts for class-reweighting (π) and pairwise dependencies. The consistency result is reassuring given the distribution shift introduced by sampling.
- Sound problem framing: The focus on long-tailed label distributions is well motivated and highly relevant in extreme classification.
- Prediction-time engineering: The nearest-centroid candidate pruning is simple, cheap to maintain, and integrates cleanly with the dyadic scoring function.
- Reproducibility: Code and datasets are provided; baselines are recent and appropriate for NIPS 2017.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Sensitivity and ablations: The method’s performance likely hinges on π (oversampling policy), κ (number of negatives), and q (candidate set size). The paper describes defaults (π ∝ 1/µk) but does not report sensitivity analyses or ablations disentangling the contributions of (i) class rebalancing vs (ii) negative subsampling vs (iii) candidate pruning.
- Bound tightness and practical relevance: The bounds depend on α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy. For aggressive subsampling/oversampling, these can be large, risking vacuity. A discussion of typical magnitudes under the recommended π (and any empirical proxy such as bound terms vs error) would strengthen the theoretical story.
- Generality of φ: The dyadic mapping uses 10 hand-crafted ranking-inspired features tailored to text. While effective, this limits generality. It would be useful to show either learned φ or domain-agnostic features and at least one non-text domain (even small-scale) to demonstrate portability.
- Candidate selection risks: Pruning to q nearest centroids introduces a potential recall loss. The paper suggests tuning q but does not report the recall of the true label in the candidate set or accuracy vs q trade-offs.
- Comparison coverage: While the set of baselines is strong for 2017, the empirical section could benefit from including a dyadic baseline without double sampling (to quantify the gain from (π, κ)-DS itself) and/or hard-negative mining strategies as alternatives to uniform negative sampling.
- Details of the classifier f: The main text does not clearly specify the learning algorithm used for f in the dyadic space (loss, regularization, optimization), which affects both runtime and accuracy.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The analysis is careful and grounded in established tools for dependent data. The extension to local complexities is interesting. Some constants and dependence on K, κ, π reduce interpretability; adding intuition or simplified corollaries would help.
- Empirical rigor: Datasets and baselines are appropriate; metrics include macro-F1, which is robust to imbalance. However, the lack of ablations and q/κ/π studies limits insight into when/why the method works best.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, with clear statements of the reduction, algorithms, and theorems. Some notation is dense (e.g., Eq. (3) indexing), and a high-level sketch of the proof ideas (beyond referencing supplements) would improve readability. Explicitly state the learning algorithm for f and the complexity of training/inference as functions of M, κ, p, and q.

Originality
- Moderate to strong. The combination of imbalance-aware sampling with dyadic reduction at extreme scales, plus local fractional Rademacher analysis, appears novel. The practical candidate selection is sensible though standard in spirit.

Significance
- High practical significance for extreme classification with long-tailed labels, common in text and tagging. The approach offers a useful operating point between accuracy and resource usage. The theoretical guarantees add confidence in the sampling heuristics.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: vary κ (incl. κ=K−1), π policies (e.g., square-root inverse frequency, capped oversampling), and candidate set size q; report accuracy/MaF1, runtime, memory, and candidate recall.
- Report accuracy vs q curves and the fraction of times the true class is filtered out.
- Compare to a dyadic baseline without (π, κ)-DS and to a hard-negative sampling variant (e.g., centroid-similarity-biased negatives).
- Clarify the classifier f (loss, regularization, optimizer) and provide complexity estimates; discuss how choices affect the bounds and practice.
- Discuss expected scales of α and β under your π choice; if possible, provide empirical bound diagnostics.
- Consider an additional non-text dataset or a learned φ to support generality claims.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical problem (extreme multiclass with long tails).
- Simple, scalable method with strong empirical trade-offs on large benchmarks.
- Nontrivial theoretical contribution ensuring consistency under double sampling and dependency.
- Reproducible and clearly positioned relative to prior work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited ablation and sensitivity analysis; unclear robustness to π/κ/q choices.
- Application-specific φ; limited evidence of generality beyond text.
- Bounds may be loose in practice due to α, β dependence; limited interpretability without empirical diagnostics.
- Inference candidate pruning not thoroughly evaluated w.r.t. recall and accuracy trade-offs.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept
