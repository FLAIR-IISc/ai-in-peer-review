## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of large-scale multi-class classification, specifically focusing on improving the efficiency of multi-class to binary reduction strategies. The authors propose a double sampling approach – first balancing class distributions and then reducing the number of dyadic examples – aiming to mitigate the curse of long-tailed distributions and reduce computational cost. They also provide theoretical justification through generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity.

The core idea of aggressive sampling is compelling. The motivation for balancing the class distribution and reducing the number of dyadic pairs is well-articulated, and the proposed method directly tackles the bottlenecks of existing approaches. The theoretical analysis establishing consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle despite the sampling and induced dependencies is a significant contribution, building on existing work in Rademacher complexity and graph covering. 

The experimental validation on DMOZ and Wikipedia collections with up to 100,000 classes demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The results show that the (π, κ)-DS approach achieves competitive performance while substantially reducing training and prediction time and memory consumption compared to state-of-the-art methods like OVA, M-SVM, and tree-based approaches.  The clear presentation of results, including both numerical data and visualizations, strengthens the paper’s claims.

However, the paper could be improved with a more detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity. While hyperparameters are mentioned, a more thorough analysis of how the choice of π and κ impacts performance would be beneficial. The feature mapping (φ) described in Section 3.1 seems somewhat simplistic, and its potential limitations in different text classification scenarios are not discussed. Also, while acknowledging related work, a more in-depth comparison of the proposed method with recent embedding-based techniques (beyond just citing them) would add value.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to address a critical challenge in large-scale multi-class classification.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with generalization bounds.
*   Empirically validated effectiveness on large datasets, demonstrating improvements in both efficiency and accuracy.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited analysis of parameter sensitivity.
*   Simplistic feature mapping could be a limitation.
*   Could benefit from a more detailed comparison with recent embedding-based methods.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale multi-class classification. While there are areas for improvement, the novel approach, strong theoretical grounding, and compelling experimental results make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above. Encouraging the authors to explore the robustness of their approach to different feature representations and provide a more in-depth analysis of hyperparameter tuning would further strengthen the work.
