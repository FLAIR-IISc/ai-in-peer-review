This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, characterized by a very large number of classes and long-tailed label distributions. The authors build upon the multi-class to binary reduction framework, which transforms the problem into classifying dyadic pairs of examples and classes. The key contribution is a novel and aggressive double-sampling strategy, termed (π, κ)-DS, designed to make this reduction scheme scalable. The first sampling step (π) rebalances the training data to counteract the long-tailed distribution, while the second step (κ) reduces the computational burden by sampling a small subset of negative classes for each example. The work relates to existing binary reduction techniques but significantly advances their practicality, offering a strong alternative to dominant tree-based and embedding methods.

The paper’s primary strength is its elegant and effective solution to a highly significant problem. The claims are exceptionally well-supported through both extensive empirical evaluation and sound theoretical analysis. The authors provide generalization error bounds (Theorems 1 and 2) that formally justify the consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle under their sampling scheme, which is a non-trivial contribution. The experiments on large-scale datasets with up to 100,000 classes are thorough and convincing. The proposed (π, κ)-DS method is shown to be highly competitive with state-of-the-art methods in terms of predictive performance (Accuracy and Macro F1) while dramatically outperforming them in training time and memory efficiency. This makes the work of high practical significance. The paper is also very clear, well-written, and easy to follow.

One minor weakness is the limited discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity. While the results are excellent, a more detailed analysis of how performance trades off with the key parameters `κ` (number of sampled classes) and `q` (number of prediction candidates) would offer valuable practical insights for implementers. Furthermore, the feature mapping function φ used for the dyadic representation is hand-crafted for text classification. While effective, a brief discussion on the potential challenges or strategies for adapting this component to other domains (e.g., image classification) could further strengthen the paper's claim of generality.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and thoroughly validated contribution to extreme classification. Its strengths in originality, technical quality, and significance far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and is likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
