- Proposes reducing extreme multiclass classification to binary classification over dyadic (example, class) pairs with a double sampling scheme to handle class imbalance and dyadic explosion.
- Double sampling: reweight classes via π (oversample rare, subsample frequent) and sample κ adversarial negative classes per example instead of all K−1.
- Provides generalization bounds for dependent dyadic data using fractional graph covers and local Rademacher complexities, showing consistency under the sampling scheme.
- Introduces a prediction-time candidate selection heuristic that restricts scoring to q nearest-centroid classes to reduce latency.
- Empirical results on LSHTC/DMOZ and Wikipedia (up to 100K classes) show competitive accuracy and macro-F1 with much lower training time and memory than OVA, M-SVM, and PD-Sparse, and improved accuracy over tree-based methods.
- Dyadic reduction aligns with prior ranking-style and dyadic multiclass-to-binary approaches but distinguishes itself with the explicit double sampling and dependency-aware analysis.
- Theoretical contribution is plausible: Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 credibly capture how sampling mismatch (α, β), M, κ, K affect bounds, though constants may be loose.
- Analysis does not cover the theoretical impact of the candidate selection (q-nearest centroids) on risk.
- The (π, κ)-DS algorithm is straightforward and implementable, but notation in equations and pseudocode needs clarity (e.g., φ(x,k) vs φ(x,y), sign conventions).
- Experiments demonstrate significant speed/memory gains and competitive performance; hyperparameters and hardware are documented and code is released.
- Missing ablations: sensitivity to π, κ, and q and trade-offs between accuracy and runtime are not reported.
- Converting multilabel data to multiclass by duplicating instances may change task characteristics and affect fairness of comparisons with multilabel baselines.
- Metrics are limited to accuracy and macro-F1; common extreme classification ranking metrics (e.g., P@k) are absent.
- Paper is generally well organized, but dyadic notation and pseudocode should be unified and “adversarial classes” explicitly defined; proof sketches would aid readability.
- The double sampling plus dependency-aware theory is a simple but nontrivial and likely novel contribution; candidate selection is an engineering heuristic.
- The approach addresses a key practical problem (scaling to extreme K with long-tailed labels) and provides reassuring theoretical guarantees for sampling heuristics.
- Reliance on hand-crafted text features limits demonstrated generality; results on learned φ or non-text domains would strengthen claims.
- Complexity analysis could be clearer: expected number of pairs and training/prediction complexity in terms of m, K, κ, q, and feature dimension p should be provided.
- Strengths: tackles important problem, simple scalable algorithm with two knobs, nontrivial theory for dependent samples, strong practical gains and released code.
- Weaknesses: lacks ablations and theoretical coverage of key engineering choices, has notation/clarity issues, and evaluation scope is confined to text with limited metrics.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, sound theoretical treatment of practical sampling reduction and demonstrated scalability with competitive accuracy on large benchmarks.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient experimental ablations and theoretical analysis of prediction heuristic, clarity problems, and limited demonstration of generality beyond text.
