This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, proposing an aggressive double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction approach. The core idea is to mitigate the class imbalance and reduce the computational burden associated with expanding the data into pairs. The paper provides theoretical justification for the consistency of empirical risk minimization with the proposed sampling and demonstrates strong empirical performance on large-scale text classification datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia).

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing approaches like OVA and label embedding methods. The proposed method builds upon existing work in dyadic representations [1, 16, 28] and tackles the computational cost with a novel sampling scheme. The theoretical contribution – demonstrating the consistency of ERM under the proposed sampling – is valuable and strengthens the paper. The experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia collections, with up to 100,000 classes, provide a compelling demonstration of the effectiveness of the method, particularly in terms of scalability. The comparison against strong baselines, including FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse, highlights the advantages of the proposed approach. The inclusion of runtime and memory usage as evaluation metrics is crucial given the target problem.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is a significant contribution, it relies heavily on local Rademacher complexity and graph covering arguments, making it difficult for readers unfamiliar with those areas to fully grasp the details. The explanation of Theorem 1 and Theorem 2, while present, feels dense and lacks intuitive explanation. A more accessible discussion of the key ideas and assumptions behind the bounds would be beneficial.

The experimental section, while extensive, lacks detail in hyperparameter tuning. The statement "For each methods, we tuned the hyperparameters over a heldout set and used the combination which gave best predictive performance" is insufficient. Providing the specific parameter ranges explored and the chosen values for each dataset would enhance reproducibility and allow for a better understanding of the sensitivity of the results. The supplementary material is mentioned, but further details *within* the main paper would be valuable. 

The description of the feature mapping φ is rather concise. While the citation to [17] is helpful, a more detailed explanation of the features (beyond listing them in Table 1) would improve understanding.  The claim that this mapping can be "learned" for other applications isn’t substantiated – how would this be achieved?

The presentation could be improved. The equations are numerous and dense, and could benefit from more descriptive annotations.  While clarity is generally good, some sentences are verbose and could be streamlined. The phrase "aggressive double sampling" feels somewhat marketing-oriented and doesn’t add significant information.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed aggressive sampling strategy is a novel approach to address the computational challenges of extreme multi-class classification.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides theoretical guarantees for the consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The method demonstrates strong performance on large-scale datasets, outperforming several state-of-the-art baselines in terms of both accuracy and efficiency.
*   **Scalability:** The method’s ability to handle datasets with 100,000 classes is a significant contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Complexity:** The theoretical analysis is difficult to follow for readers unfamiliar with advanced learning theory concepts.
*   **Lack of Experimental Detail:** Insufficient detail is provided regarding hyperparameter tuning.
*   **Concise Feature Mapping Description:**  The explanation of the feature representation is somewhat lacking.
*   **Presentation:** Dense equations and verbose sentences hinder readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. The proposed method addresses a critical challenge – scalability – with a novel and theoretically grounded approach. While the theoretical analysis could be more accessible and the experimental section more detailed, the strong empirical results and the novelty of the approach outweigh these weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity of the theoretical explanations and the experimental details. Specifically, authors should expand on hyperparameter tuning, elaborate on the feature representation, and streamline some of the equations and descriptions for improved readability.
