- Addresses extreme multi-class classification by proposing an aggressive double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class-to-binary reduction to mitigate class imbalance and computational cost.  
- Provides theoretical justification showing consistency of empirical risk minimization under the proposed sampling.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance and scalability on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia) with up to 100k classes.  
- Compares favorably to strong baselines (FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse) and reports runtime and memory usage.  
- Well-motivated critique of OVA and label-embedding methods; builds on prior dyadic representation work.  
- Novel sampling scheme that meaningfully reduces training cost for dyadic expansions.  
- Theoretical analysis is valuable but relies heavily on local Rademacher complexity and graph covering, making it hard to follow for non-experts.  
- Explanations of Theorem 1 and 2 are dense and lack intuitive discussion of key ideas and assumptions.  
- Experimental section omits sufficient hyperparameter tuning details (ranges and chosen values), harming reproducibility.  
- Feature mapping φ is described too concisely; citations help but the features and claim that φ can be “learned” are not well substantiated.  
- Presentation suffers from numerous dense equations, verbose sentences, and occasionally marketing-like phrasing (“aggressive double sampling”).  
- Strengths: novelty, theoretical soundness, empirical performance, and demonstrated scalability.  
- Weaknesses: theoretical complexity, lack of experimental detail, insufficient feature mapping description, and readability issues.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify theoretical intuition, expand hyperparameter details, elaborate feature representation, and streamline presentation.
