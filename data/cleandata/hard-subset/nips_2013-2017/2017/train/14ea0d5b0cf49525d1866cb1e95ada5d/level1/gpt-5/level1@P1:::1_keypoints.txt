- Tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing to binary dyadic pairs and using a double sampling scheme to handle long-tailed classes and quadratic pair blow-up.  
- Rebalances training by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones with probabilities πk, then samples κ adversarial classes per example to form dyads.  
- Provides generalization bounds for ERM under the two-stage sampling despite pair dependence, using fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities.  
- Uses candidate class selection at prediction via nearest class centroids in dyadic feature space.  
- Empirical evaluation on LSHTC DMOZ/Wikipedia (10K–100K classes) shows competitive accuracy and macro-F1 with reduced training/prediction time and memory versus OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding baselines.  
- Builds on prior dyadic reductions and dependent-sample analyses (Usunier, Amini, Janson, Ralaivola & Amini) and contrasts with label-tree and embedding methods.  
- Novel use of local Rademacher complexities to get variance-sensitive bounds for dependent dyadic samples.  
- Addresses a practical scaling problem for tens/hundreds of thousands of classes and long-tailed distributions.  
- Presents a simple, implementation-friendly pipeline: dyadic reduction + double sampling + candidate selection.  
- Theoretical contribution: bounds under class-distribution shift and pair dependence with explicit dependence on π, κ, K, and class proportions.  
- Empirical scope and results are strong, and code/datasets are provided; macro-F1 is appropriately reported for imbalance.  
- Main methodological details are incomplete: base learner f (loss, regularization), optimization, and surrogate loss specification are unclear.  
- Dyadic feature mapping φ is sparsely described (10 hand-crafted features), limiting perceived generality and potential performance.  
- Theoretical bounds depend on α = max ηy/πy and β = max 1/πy, but guidance on choosing π to control these constants is limited and lacks analysis of optimality or importance weighting.  
- No ablation studies to isolate effects of class rebalancing, κ subsampling, and candidate selection; sensitivity to κ, π, and candidate size q is not reported.  
- Candidate pruning at prediction can miss true labels; impact on accuracy vs. speed is not systematically analyzed (e.g., accuracy as a function of q).  
- Time/memory comparisons may be confounded by candidate pruning since baselines likely score all classes at test time.  
- Multi-label-to-multiclass conversion by replicating instances may bias frequencies and evaluations; macro-F1 implications need discussion.  
- Applicability beyond text is claimed but untested; feature mapping is text-tailored and not demonstrated on other modalities.  
- Approach is technically sound overall; derivations correctly adapt local Rademacher tools via fractional covers, and Theorem 2 formalizes consistency under double sampling.  
- Practicality of theoretical bounds (magnitudes of α, β) and hyperparameter scaling guidance are not elucidated.  
- Paper is generally well written, but some notation (Eq. 3), description of f, feature list, and choice of q/distance metric need clarification.  
- Originality is moderate: double sampling tailored to long tails plus local Rademacher/fractional-cover analysis is novel; candidate prediction and hand-crafted dyadic features are not.  
- Significance is high for extreme text classification if resource savings generalize; theoretical results aid understanding of ERM under dependent/doubly sampled dyadic data.  
- Pros for acceptance: timely scalable solution, simple and scalable method with strong empirical trade-offs, theoretical guarantees under dependence, public code and datasets.  
- Cons against acceptance: lacks ablations/sensitivity analyses, incomplete methodological/training details, potential unfairness in time comparisons, and limited empirical scope beyond text.  
- Requests for authors: specify base learner, loss, regularization, solver, and any importance weighting; provide ablations over κ, π, q and report tail/rare-class performance.  
- Additional requests: clarify centroid/distance computation and feature list, analyze α/β under πk ∝ 1/µk with practical guidance for π and κ scaling, and include reproducibility details (seeds, splits, hyperparameters).
