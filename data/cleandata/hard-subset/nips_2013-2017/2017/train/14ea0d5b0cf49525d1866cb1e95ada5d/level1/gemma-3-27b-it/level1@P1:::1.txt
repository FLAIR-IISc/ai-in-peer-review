This paper addresses the problem of large-scale multi-class classification, a common scenario in text categorization with a vast number of classes. The authors propose a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction technique, aiming to mitigate the curse of long-tailed class distributions and reduce computational costs. The core idea is to aggressively sample the training data to balance class representation and reduce the number of pairwise examples generated during the binary reduction process, while theoretically guaranteeing consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle. The approach is evaluated on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets with up to 100,000 classes, demonstrating improved efficiency and performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in machine learning. The problem of extreme multi-class classification is clearly defined, and the authors effectively position their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant approaches such as one-vs-all, tree-based methods, label embedding approaches, and existing binary reduction strategies.  The proposed double sampling strategy feels like a natural and logical extension to the standard binary reduction approach, specifically targeting the issues of class imbalance and computational complexity. The theoretical analysis, specifically Theorems 1 and 2, attempting to establish generalization bounds under the proposed sampling scheme, is a strong point and distinguishes this work from purely empirical contributions. The experimental evaluation on large-scale datasets demonstrates the practical benefits of the approach in terms of training/prediction time, memory consumption, and accuracy. The results showing the competitive performance and superior efficiency compared to baselines like OVA, M-SVM, and tree-based methods are compelling.

However, several weaknesses need addressing. First, the presentation of the mathematical derivations can be improved for clarity. While the authors state that the proofs are in the supplementary material, key steps within the main text could benefit from more detailed explanation. For example, the connection between the fractional Rademacher complexity and the proposed sampling strategy isn't fully elucidated. The reliance on Definition 1 (exact proper fractional cover of G) feels somewhat abrupt and requires more contextualization, particularly for readers unfamiliar with this concept.

Second, while the experiments demonstrate strong performance, the hyperparameter tuning process is briefly mentioned but lacks sufficient detail. The specific values used for parameters like π and κ, and the methodology for selecting these values (e.g., grid search, cross-validation) should be clearly documented. Providing a more in-depth ablation study varying these parameters would strengthen the empirical analysis.  The choice of features (Table 1) also feels somewhat arbitrary. While the authors mention it’s inspired by learning to rank, a justification for selecting these specific features over others in the context of extreme multi-class text classification would be beneficial. 

Third, the comparison with existing methods isn't fully comprehensive. While the authors justify excluding some recent approaches like SLEEC and LEML based on prior work, a brief discussion of their limitations in relation to the proposed method would add value.  Additionally, the comparison focuses largely on runtime and memory usage; a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between these metrics and predictive performance would be helpful. For instance, is the improved efficiency achieved at the cost of significant accuracy loss in certain scenarios?

Finally, while the paper claims consistency of the ERM principle, the theoretical bounds (Theorems 1 & 2) contain terms like 'α' and 'β' dependent on the class proportions, which might suggest performance is still susceptible to imbalances despite sampling. A more thorough discussion of the influence of class distribution on the bounds and the practical implications would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The aggressive double sampling strategy is a novel approach to addressing the challenges of large-scale multi-class classification.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis with generalization bounds, which supports the validity of the proposed method.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Experimental results on large-scale datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach in terms of efficiency and performance.
*   **Significance:** The proposed method offers a practical solution for handling extreme multi-class classification problems, which have numerous real-world applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity of Mathematical Derivations:** The presentation of the theoretical analysis could be improved for better understanding.
*   **Lack of Hyperparameter Tuning Detail:**  Insufficient details regarding the hyperparameter selection process.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:**  A more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of different parameter settings.
*   **Incomplete Comparison:** The comparison with existing methods could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of large-scale multi-class classification. The proposed method is novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. While there are some areas for improvement in terms of clarity and experimental detail, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should provide more details on hyperparameter tuning, elaborate on the theoretical derivations, and perform a more comprehensive comparison with relevant baseline methods.
