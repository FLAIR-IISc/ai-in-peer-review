## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of large-scale multi-class classification, specifically focusing on the computational and memory bottlenecks that arise with a large number of classes. The authors propose a double sampling strategy applied *after* a multi-class to binary reduction, aiming to balance class distributions and reduce the number of pairwise examples. They theoretically ground their approach by demonstrating consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle under their sampling scheme, using local fractional Rademacher complexity. The method is evaluated on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets with up to 100,000 classes, demonstrating improvements in training/prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance compared to several state-of-the-art methods.

The paper builds upon existing work in extreme classification, specifically referencing the one-vs-all approach [18], tree-based methods [4, 6, 9], label embedding approaches [11, 19], and previous dyadic expansion techniques [1, 16, 28]. The contribution lies in the *combination* of aggressive sampling *with* the dyadic reduction, and the theoretical analysis showing consistency despite the sampling-induced distribution shift and dependency between pairs. The use of local Rademacher complexity [22] to derive generalization bounds is a strong point, building on previous work [14, 25].

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an important problem:** Large-scale multi-class classification is a significant challenge, and the proposed approach directly tackles the scalability issues.
*   **Sound theoretical foundation:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis justifying the proposed sampling strategy, demonstrating consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The experiments on large datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in terms of both performance and efficiency.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the proposed method and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The performance seems dependent on the choice of parameters (π, κ), and the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how to select these parameters in practice.
*   **Limited comparison:** While several baselines are included, the comparison could be broadened to include more recent and specialized extreme classification methods (e.g., SLEEC [5]).
*   **Feature mapping details:** The description of the feature mapping φ is somewhat brief. More details about its implementation and potential impact on performance would be helpful.
*   **Practical considerations:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical guarantees and scalability. A discussion of practical implementation challenges and potential optimizations would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in machine learning.
*   The proposed method is novel and offers significant improvements in scalability and performance.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a strong justification for the approach.
*   The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The hyperparameter sensitivity and limited comparison raise some concerns about the robustness of the results.
*   The lack of detailed discussion on practical implementation challenges may limit the immediate applicability of the method.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of extreme classification. The combination of theoretical analysis and strong empirical results makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance**, with a request for the authors to address the points raised regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and comparison to more recent methods in a revised version.
