This paper tackles extreme multiclass classification by combining a dyadic multi-class-to-binary reduction with an aggressive double sampling scheme: (i) rebalancing the long-tailed label distribution via class-dependent subsampling/oversampling (π), and (ii) limiting per-example negatives to κ sampled impostor classes. The authors derive generalization bounds for the dependent dyadic examples using fractional graph covers and local Rademacher complexity, arguing that ERM on the doubly sampled reduction remains consistent. Experiments on LSHTC/DMOZ and Wikipedia subsets with up to 100K classes show favorable trade-offs in training/prediction time, memory, and accuracy/MaF1 versus OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding baselines (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree). The approach relates to prior pairwise reductions and interdependent-data analyses (e.g., Usunier & Amini, NIPS; Janson; Ralaivola & Amini, ICML) and complements label-embedding and tree-based speedups.

Quality: The theoretical development is technically sound at a high level: using fractional covers to handle dependence in dyadic expansions is appropriate, and the introduction of local complexity captures variance for faster rates. However, the bounds introduce problem-dependent constants (α, β) that can blow up under aggressive reweighting (small πk), and practical guidance on choosing π, κ to control these constants is limited. Empirically, the gains are convincing on text benchmarks, but there are no confidence intervals/significance tests, and sensitivity analyses for π, κ and the candidate-selection parameter q are missing.

Clarity: Overall well-organized, though some notation (Eq. 3 indexing) and assumptions could be streamlined. Proof sketches in the main text clarifying how local fractional complexities differ from prior bounds would aid readability. Please cite foundational LRC works (e.g., Bartlett & Mendelson, 2005) alongside [22].

Originality: The double sampling within dyadic reduction and the accompanying local fractional Rademacher analysis appear novel. The practical candidate-selection step using class centroids is simple and effective.

Significance: For extreme text classification, the method offers a strong speed–memory–accuracy compromise and is likely to be adopted. Generality beyond text depends on constructing suitable φ; discussion of non-text domains would help.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled, novel sampling-reduction with new generalization bounds for dependent pairs
- Strong empirical efficiency and competitive accuracy/MaF1 at 50–100K classes
- Simple, reproducible engineering (public code)

Cons against acceptance:
- Bounds may be loose with aggressive π; limited guidance/sensitivity analysis
- Evaluation lacks error bars and broader extreme baselines/metrics (e.g., P@k/propensity scores)
- Applicability beyond text not demonstrated; fixed 10-D φ may limit generality
