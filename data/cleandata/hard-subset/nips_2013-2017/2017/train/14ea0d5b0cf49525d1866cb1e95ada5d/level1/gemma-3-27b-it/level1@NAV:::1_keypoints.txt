- Addresses extreme multi-class text classification via a multi-class-to-binary reduction with an “aggressive double sampling” scheme.  
- Aggressive double sampling oversamples minority classes and subsamples majority classes before dyadic transformation to reduce imbalance and cost.  
- Main contribution: theoretical generalization bounds proving ERM consistency despite induced distribution shift and pairwise dependencies.  
- Theoretical analysis leverages local Rademacher complexity and graph covering, providing a strong theoretical foundation.  
- Builds on dyadic expansion [16,28] and addresses its computational bottleneck in a practical way.  
- Experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia show improved runtime, memory usage, and predictive performance versus several baselines.  
- Paper is well-motivated and clearly positions limitations of one-vs-all and tree-based methods.  
- Weakness: connection between theoretical bounds and practical parameter tuning (π, κ) is not sufficiently clarified.  
- Weakness: description of the feature mapping φ is brief and lacks discussion of its performance impact.  
- Weakness: experimental results lack statistical significance analysis.  
- Weakness: evaluation could be strengthened by comparing to more recent extreme classification methods.  
- Overall recommendation: valuable contribution with minor revisions suggested (clarify tuning, feature mapping, and significance/comparisons).
