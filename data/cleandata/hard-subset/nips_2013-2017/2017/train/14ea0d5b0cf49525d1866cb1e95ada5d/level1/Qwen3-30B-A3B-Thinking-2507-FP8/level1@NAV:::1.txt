

This paper proposes an aggressive double sampling strategy for multi-class to binary reduction in extreme classification scenarios with very large numbers of classes (10,000-100,000). The authors address two key challenges in this domain: the curse of long-tailed class distributions and the computational burden of constructing dyadic examples in binary reduction approaches. Their (π, κ)-DS algorithm first balances class representation by oversampling small classes and subsampling large classes, then reduces the number of dyadic examples by sampling κ adversarial classes per example. The paper provides theoretical guarantees using local fractional Rademacher complexity showing that the empirical risk minimization principle remains consistent under their sampling strategy. Experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia collections demonstrate significant improvements in training/prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of extreme classification, building on previous work in binary reduction [1,16,28] while addressing their computational limitations. The theoretical analysis is sound and well-motivated, with Theorems 1 and 2 providing meaningful generalization bounds. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple relevant baselines (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse) on challenging datasets with up to 100,000 classes.

Strengths include the clear problem statement, well-structured theoretical analysis, and convincing empirical results showing the method's scalability and effectiveness. The paper appropriately acknowledges related work and distinguishes its approach from tree-based and embedding-based methods. The candidate selection strategy for prediction is particularly practical for large-scale applications.

The main weaknesses are the somewhat limited discussion of the hyperparameter sensitivity (π and κ) and the lack of comparison with the most recent methods like SLEEC [5] and LEML [31] (though the authors justify omitting them based on prior evidence of their inferior performance). The theoretical analysis, while technically sound, could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the local Rademacher complexity bounds.

The paper meets all four quality criteria: it's technically sound with strong theoretical and empirical validation, clearly written with good organization, makes a novel contribution to a significant problem in extreme classification, and demonstrates clear practical value for real-world applications. The proposed method represents a meaningful advance in scalable multi-class classification that would be valuable to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
