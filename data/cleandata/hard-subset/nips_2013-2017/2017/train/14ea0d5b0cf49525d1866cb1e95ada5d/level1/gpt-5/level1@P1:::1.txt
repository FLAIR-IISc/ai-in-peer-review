Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing the problem to binary classification over dyadic pairs (example, class) and introducing an aggressive double sampling scheme to address (i) the long-tailed class distribution and (ii) the quadratic blow-up in pairs. Specifically, it (a) rebalances the training set by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones with probabilities πk, then (b) for each retained example draws κ adversarial classes uniformly to form dyadic pairs. The authors provide generalization bounds for ERM under this two-stage sampling despite the induced dependence among pairs, relying on fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities. For prediction, they use a candidate class selection step based on nearest class centroids in the dyadic space. Experiments on LSHTC DMOZ/Wikipedia datasets (10K–100K classes) show competitive accuracy and macro-F1 with much reduced training/prediction time and memory compared to OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding baselines (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree).

This work builds on prior multiclass-to-binary dyadic reductions and analysis for interdependent data (e.g., Usunier et al., Amini et al.; fractional chromatic number bounds by Janson; entropy-based concentration for dependent variables by Ralaivola & Amini). It contrasts with label-tree and embedding methods (FastXML, PfastReXML, compressed sensing, LEML) that reduce prediction search space but suffer from cascading errors/low-rank assumptions, and with OVA/M-SVM/PD-Sparse which scale poorly in K. The use of local Rademacher complexities to obtain variance-sensitive bounds for dependent dyadic samples appears novel in this context.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important and practical pain point: scaling multiclass learning to tens/hundreds of thousands of classes with long-tailed distributions.
- Conceptually simple, implementation-friendly pipeline: dyadic reduction + double sampling + candidate selection.
- Theoretical contribution: new generalization bounds under class-distribution shift and pair dependence using local fractional Rademacher complexity; explicit dependence on π, κ, K, and class proportions (α, β).
- Empirical results on large LSHTC datasets demonstrating favorable runtime/memory trade-offs while remaining competitive in accuracy and macro-F1 versus strong baselines (PD-Sparse, OVA/M-SVM).
- Code and datasets provided; experiments consider both accuracy and macro-F1, the latter appropriate for imbalanced regimes.

Weaknesses:
- Incomplete methodological details in the main text: the base learner f (loss, regularization), optimization procedure, and exact role of surrogate losses are not clearly specified; dyadic feature mapping φ is only briefly described (10 hand-crafted features), which may limit generality and performance.
- Theoretical bounds hinge on α = max ηy/πy and β = max 1/πy; guidance on choosing π to control these constants is limited to πk ∝ 1/µk without analysis of optimality/tightness or discussion of importance weighting to correct sampling bias.
- No ablations to disentangle contributions of (i) class rebalancing vs (ii) κ subsampling vs (iii) candidate selection. Sensitivity to κ, π, and the candidate set size q is not reported.
- Candidate selection at prediction (nearest centroids) can miss the true label; the effect on accuracy vs speed is not systematically analyzed (e.g., accuracy as a function of q).
- Fairness and comparability: baselines likely score all classes at test time; the proposed method uses candidate pruning, potentially confounding time comparisons. Also, converting multi-label to multiclass by replicating instances may bias class frequencies and evaluation; macro-F1 under this conversion warrants discussion.
- Generalization beyond text: the mapping φ is tailored to text (LTR-inspired features) and the approach’s applicability to other modalities is stated but not demonstrated.

Quality:
The approach is technically sound in broad strokes. The derivations appear to correctly adapt local Rademacher tools to the dependent-pair setting via fractional covers, and Theorem 2 formalizes consistency under the double sampling, with clear dependence on K, M, κ, and π. However, the practicality of the bounds (e.g., magnitudes of α, β with proposed π) and concrete guidance on hyperparameter scaling are not elucidated. Empirical evaluation is strong in scope but lacks ablations and diagnostic analyses that would solidify causal claims about the benefits of each component. Reporting of training details (losses, regularization, stopping criteria) should be improved for reproducibility.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The introduction and motivation are clear. Some notation in Eq. (3) is hard to parse (indices and ordering of pairs), and the description of f and training losses is too terse. The feature mapping is summarized but would benefit from a brief explicit list and discussion in the main text. The prediction candidate selection algorithm is clear, but the choice of q and distance metric should be specified. Figures/tables are referenced; ensure that hyperparameters (π, κ, q) and classifier settings are accessible without relying solely on the supplement.

Originality:
Moderately high. While dyadic reduction and sampling are known, the specific double sampling scheme tailored to long-tailed distributions, together with the local Rademacher/fractional-cover analysis under dependence, is novel. Candidate-based prediction is standard; the handcrafted dyadic features are not new.

Significance:
High practical relevance for extreme text classification. If the reported resource savings hold widely, this could influence practice. The theoretical results contribute to understanding ERM under dependent/doubly sampled dyadic data. Broader impact would be stronger with demonstrations beyond text and with learned φ.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses a timely and important problem at scale.
- Simple, scalable method with strong empirical trade-offs on 50K–100K class benchmarks.
- Theoretical guarantees under dependence and sampling shift; consistency established.
- Public code and datasets.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Insufficient ablation and sensitivity analyses (π, κ, q); unclear which components drive gains.
- Limited methodological and training details; reliance on 10 handcrafted features may limit generality.
- Potential fairness issues in time comparisons due to candidate pruning; unclear effect on accuracy.
- Empirical scope limited to text; claims of broader applicability are not substantiated.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Specify the base learner f (loss, regularization, solver) and the surrogate used during training; clarify whether any importance weighting is applied to account for π.
- Provide ablations: vary κ and π (e.g., π uniform vs inverse-frequency), and report accuracy/MaF1/runtime/memory; study q vs accuracy/time.
- Report tail performance (e.g., macro-recall on rare classes) to support claims about long-tail mitigation.
- Clarify how class centroids and distances are computed and which features are used; discuss risk of candidate pruning errors.
- Analyze the constants α, β under πk ∝ 1/µk and provide practical guidance for setting π, κ as a function of K and m.
- Discuss reproducibility: random seeds, train/validation splits, exact hyperparameter settings (bring essential ones into the main text).

Overall, this is a promising and practically relevant contribution with solid theoretical underpinnings and strong empirical results, but it would benefit from clearer methodological details and more thorough empirical analysis to isolate the effects of its components.
