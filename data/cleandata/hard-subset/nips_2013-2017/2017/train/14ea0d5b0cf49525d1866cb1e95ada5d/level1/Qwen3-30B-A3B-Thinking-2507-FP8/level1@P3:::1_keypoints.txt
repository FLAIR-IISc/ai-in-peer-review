- Proposal: Aggressive double sampling transforms multi-class to binary over dyadic pairs and uses oversampling of small classes plus subsampling of large classes to reduce dyadic examples for K in the 10k–100k range.

- Theory: Derives new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity, linking the sampling strategy to graph covering and fractional chromatic number and showing ERM consistency.

- Experiments: On DMOZ and Wikipedia the method improves training/prediction time, memory usage, and predictive performance versus OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse.

- Strength — Novelty: The double sampling approach is well-motivated and addresses long-tailed class distributions that hurt one-vs-all methods, distinguishing itself from tree-based and label-embedding methods.

- Strength — Theory: Extends dyadic representation work with meaningful theoretical advances (Theorems 1–2) that justify the sampling strategy beyond empirical gains.

- Strength — Empirics: Comprehensive, well-designed comparisons demonstrate practical benefits in efficiency and accuracy.

- Strength — Presentation: Clear, well-structured paper with consistent notation and a reproducible experimental setup.

- Weakness — Sampling rationale: No rigorous theoretical justification for choosing π_k ∝ 1/μ_k; impact of other sampling choices is unexplored.

- Weakness — Sensitivity: Lacks sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters (π, κ) and trade-offs between sampling rates, accuracy, and efficiency.

- Weakness — Domain scope: Evaluations are confined to text classification (DMOZ, Wikipedia), limiting demonstrated generality to other domains.

- Weakness — Scalability claim: Claims up to 100k classes but does not provide evidence or detailed complexity analysis for scaling beyond that.

- Overall assessment: Strong, significant contribution with sound methodology, solid theory, and convincing experiments; minor weaknesses are addressable.

- Recommendation: Accept for NIPS 2017.

- Pro arguments: Novel sampling strategy for long-tailed distributions; strong theoretical foundation; comprehensive experiments showing clear efficiency and performance gains.

- Con arguments: Limited theoretical justification for the specific sampling choice; missing hyperparameter sensitivity analysis; limited experimental scope to text datasets.
