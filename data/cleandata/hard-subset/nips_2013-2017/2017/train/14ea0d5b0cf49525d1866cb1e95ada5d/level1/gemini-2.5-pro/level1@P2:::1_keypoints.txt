- Paper addresses extreme multi-class classification by scaling multi-class-to-binary reduction with a novel aggressive double sampling method (π, κ)-DS.  
- π-sampling: class-based sampling inversely proportional to class frequency to balance long-tail distributions.  
- κ-sampling: for each sampled example, sample only κ adversarial negatives instead of K−1 to drastically reduce training pairs.  
- Method yields large reductions in training set size, improving training time and memory while keeping competitive accuracy.  
- Theoretical contribution: new generalization bounds (Theorems 1–2) using local Rademacher complexities that justify consistency under π-sampling and pairwise data dependencies.  
- Empirical evaluation on large-scale text benchmarks (DMOZ, WIKI up to 100k classes) shows strong trade-offs of accuracy, speed, and memory versus state-of-the-art baselines.  
- Experimental comparisons are comprehensive, including tree-based methods and high-performance solvers like PD-Sparse.  
- Strength: solid technical quality with well-supported claims and thorough, convincing empirical results.  
- Strength: evaluation metrics include predictive performance and practical aspects (training/prediction time, memory).  
- Weakness: method depends on the quality of the projection φ mapping examples and classes to features, which is heuristic and domain-specific.  
- Weakness: theoretical section is dense and could use more intuition (e.g., explanation of fractional chromatic number for dependent sums).  
- Originality: combining class-balancing π with negative κ sampling in this reduction context is novel and creative.  
- Other components (feature representation and candidate selection at prediction) rely on established heuristics rather than new contributions.  
- Significance: highly impactful for practical extreme classification due to efficiency gains and strong empirical performance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretically-sound sampling strategy, strong empirical results on large benchmarks, high practical impact, and a complete theory+algorithm+experiments package.  
- Argument against acceptance: empirical validation is focused on text data and uses domain-specific φ, leaving generality to other domains (e.g., images) unproven.  
- Conclusion: overall an excellent, well-written paper with minor weaknesses; strong recommendation for acceptance.
