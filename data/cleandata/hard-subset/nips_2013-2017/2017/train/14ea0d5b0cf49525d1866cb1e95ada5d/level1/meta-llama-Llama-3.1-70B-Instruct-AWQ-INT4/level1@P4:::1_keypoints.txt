- Summary: Proposes a double-sampling strategy on top of a multi-class-to-binary reduction that builds pairwise examples to handle long-tailed distributions and reduce pair count; provides consistency guarantees and shows efficiency on large-scale text datasets.  
- Relation to prior work: Extends multi-class-to-binary reductions and contrasts with OVA and tree methods (computational/imbalance issues); related to label-embedding but does not assume low-rank label matrices.  
- Strength — Novel approach: Introduces a new double sampling strategy to mitigate limitations of prior reductions.  
- Strength — Theoretical guarantees: Proves consistency of empirical risk minimization under the double-sample reduction.  
- Strength — Efficiency: Presents an algorithm claimed to be scalable and suitable for large-scale problems.  
- Strength — Empirical results: Demonstrates strong performance on large-scale text classification datasets.  
- Weakness — Algorithm complexity: Method involves multiple steps (double sampling and data transformation) that may be computationally expensive.  
- Weakness — Hyperparameter tuning: Requires tuning several hyperparameters, increasing time and expertise needed.  
- Weakness — Limited evaluation scope: Experiments focus on text datasets, leaving generality to other data types unclear.  
- Originality: Offers a novel contribution to large-scale multi-class classification with theoretical and empirical support.  
- Significance: Addresses an important problem (extreme multi-class classification) with potential applicability across domains (text, image, recommender systems).  
- Quality: Well-written with clear exposition of methods, theory, and experiments.  
- Pro-acceptance arguments: Novel method with theory and experiments; tackles a significant problem; potential for wide applicability.  
- Con-acceptance arguments: Potential computational expense and complexity; heavy hyperparameter tuning; limited demonstrated applicability beyond text.
