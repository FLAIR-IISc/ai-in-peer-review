This paper addresses large-scale multi-class classification, a common problem in areas like text categorization. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction, aiming to overcome the challenges of long-tailed class distributions and computational cost. They provide theoretical justification for their approach, showing consistency of empirical risk minimization, and demonstrate its effectiveness on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets.

The core idea is well-motivated. Large-scale multi-class classification *is* a significant problem, and the limitations of existing approaches like one-vs-all and label embedding are accurately described. The proposed double sampling strategy – combining class balancing with reduction of dyadic examples – appears to be a sensible approach to tackling both class imbalance and computational burden. The theoretical analysis, specifically Theorem 1 and 2, are valuable, providing a theoretical foundation for the method's consistency. The experimental results on DMOZ and Wikipedia demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, particularly in terms of runtime and memory usage, where it outperforms several baselines, especially when dealing with a large number of classes. 

However, the paper has several weaknesses. Clarity is a significant issue. The notation is dense and often lacks sufficient explanation. For instance, the meaning of variables like ‘π’ and ‘κ’ are introduced but not clearly explained in context throughout the sections where they are used, requiring the reader to frequently refer back to Section 2.2. While the authors attempt to explain the mathematical details, the flow is often disrupted by technicalities without sufficient intuition. The description of the feature mapping φ (Section 3.1) is brief and lacks details. It's unclear *how* the feature vectors are constructed and what specific features are used beyond a general mention of similarity-based representations. While mentioning related work (e.g., [2, 5, 11, 16, 19, 28]) is present, the connections to these works could be strengthened. A more detailed comparison of the proposed method with these related works – highlighting specific advantages and disadvantages – would be beneficial. The experimental setup section is adequate but lacks details regarding parameter tuning. While the authors mention tuning hyperparameters on a heldout set (Appendix B), more specifics about the search space and the optimization procedure would increase reproducibility. The presentation of experimental results could be improved. While the tables provide numerical data, adding error bars or statistical significance tests would bolster the claims of superiority.  The bar plots in Fig. 3 are helpful but could be more informative with clearer labels and potentially different scales to better visualize the differences. While Theorem 2 offers generalization bounds, the constants and terms involving α and β make it difficult to interpret the practical implications. A discussion on how these bounds scale with the dataset size and the number of classes would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in large-scale machine learning.
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to tackle the challenges of class imbalance and computational cost.
*   Provides theoretical justification for the method's consistency.
*   Demonstrates empirical effectiveness on real-world datasets, especially regarding runtime and memory usage.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity is a significant issue, making the paper difficult to follow.
*   The description of key components (e.g., the feature mapping) is insufficient.
*   Comparison to related work could be more detailed.
*   Experimental setup and result presentation could be improved for reproducibility and interpretability.
*   The generalization bounds are difficult to interpret practically.

**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and potentially significant contribution to the field of large-scale multi-class classification. However, the lack of clarity and the insufficient details in certain sections hinder its impact. While the theoretical results and empirical performance are promising, addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper. I would lean towards a **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity and detail issues in a revision. Specifically, they should focus on improving the readability of the mathematical notation, providing more details on the feature mapping, and elaborating on the comparison to related work.
