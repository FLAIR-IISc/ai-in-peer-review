This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification, specifically multiclass classification where the output space ranges up to 100k different classes. Many approaches to extreme classification rely on inferring a tree structure among the output labels, as in the hierarchical softmax, so that only a logarithmic number of binary predictions need to be made to infer the output class, or a label embedding approach that can be learned efficiently through least-squares or sampling. However, the authors note the problems with these approaches: the inferred latent tree may not be optimal and lead to cascading errors, and the label embedding approach may lead to prediction errors when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

An alternative approach is to reduce extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. The authors operate in this framework and present a scalable sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees as to its consistency with the original multiclass ERM formulation. Their proposed method, called Aggressive Double Sampling (ADS), addresses the limitations of existing approaches by employing a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction strategy. This strategy transforms the original multi-class problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples, while also reducing the number of pairs of examples in the expanded data.

The ADS reduction is controlled by two parameters: one that controls the frequency of sampling each class, which is inversely proportional to the empirical class frequency in order to avoid entirely missing classes in the long tail, and another that sets the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly. This procedure is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier. At prediction time, inference is complicated by the fact that generating pairwise features with all possible classes is intractable. A heuristic is used to identify candidate classes by making an input space centroid representation of each class as the average of vectors in the class, with prediction being carried out by a series of pairwise classifications with only those candidates.

Experiments on five text datasets comparing a number of competing approaches (including standard one-vs-all classification when that is computationally feasible) show that the method is generally superior in terms of accuracy and F1 once the number of classes goes above 30k, and trains faster while using far less memory, although it suffers somewhat in terms of prediction speed. The results suggest that the method is least affected by long-tailed class distributions, mainly because the sampling tends to make the training set balanced, and the reduced binary dataset contains a similar number of positive and negative examples.

Theoretical analysis of the reduction is complicated by the fact that the reduction from multi-class to dyadic binary classification changes a sum of independent random variables into a sum of random variables with a particular dependence structure among the dyadic examples. The other complication is caused by the oversampling of rare classes, shifting the empirical distribution. The proof involves splitting the sum of dependent examples into several sums of independent variables based on the graph structure induced by the example construction, and using the concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables introduced by Janson. Although the finite-sample risk bound is biased by the ratios between the true class probabilities and the oversampled ones, these biases decrease linearly with the size of the sampled and re-sampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, and the method presents a strong approach for extreme classification, with small memory and computational requirements and superior performance on especially the largest datasets.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel approach to extreme classification, which is theoretically sound and practically effective.
* The proposed method, ADS, addresses the limitations of existing approaches and provides a scalable solution for large-scale multi-class classification problems.
* The experimental results demonstrate the superiority of the method in terms of accuracy, F1, training time, and memory usage, especially for large datasets.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes that the reader is familiar with the concept of extreme classification and the existing approaches, which may not be the case for all readers.
* The theoretical analysis is complex and may be difficult to follow for readers without a strong background in theoretical machine learning.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the method and potential avenues for future research.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to extreme classification, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the method, which is an important consideration for NIPS.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow for readers with a background in machine learning.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper assumes a high level of background knowledge in extreme classification and theoretical machine learning, which may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The theoretical analysis is complex and may be difficult to verify in detail, which may raise concerns about the soundness of the method.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the method and potential avenues for future research, which may impact its significance and impact.
