This paper introduces a novel reduction strategy for transforming multi-class classification problems, particularly those with a large number of classes, into a binary classification framework. The approach centers on a hypothesis that assigns scores to (input, class) pairs, utilizing a loss function that quantifies the proportion of incorrectly classified classes receiving higher scores than the true class. Specifically, the method employs a feature transformation, denoted as phi, to map these pairs into a p-dimensional feature space, subsequently learning a mapping from R^p to scores. Building upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which generated K-1 transformed points for each (x,y) representing comparisons between the true label and incorrect labels, this paper addresses the challenges posed by correlated training examples within the transformed dataset. The first key contribution lies in an enhanced generalization analysis, achieved through the derivation of new bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity tailored to this correlated setting. The second major contribution demonstrates the efficacy of employing sampling techniques during the reduction process to both balance class distributions and reduce the overall training set size. This involves sampling classes with replacement to achieve roughly equal representation in the transformed data and utilizing a random sample of classes rather than all K-1 possible comparisons. The authors demonstrate that these sampling procedures maintain the integrity of their generalization bounds. Empirical results demonstrate the competitive performance of the proposed method against established baselines.

The paper offers a compelling reduction from multi-class to binary classification, coupled with generalization bounds designed to handle the inherent correlations in the resulting dataset. The primary innovation resides in the sampling strategies employed to rebalance class distributions and reduce the transformed dataset’s size, extending prior work by Joshi et al. The empirical evaluation showcases strong performance relative to existing algorithms. 

However, the paper would benefit from a theoretical exploration of the prediction algorithm detailed in section 2.3, which limits comparisons to the k nearest class centroids. Furthermore, a more detailed discussion of the sample complexity bounds, potentially including a comparison to generalization bounds for standard, non-transformed multi-class problems, would enhance the analysis.
