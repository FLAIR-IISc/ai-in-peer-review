This paper presents a novel learning reduction for extreme classification, a multiclass classification problem where the output space consists of up to 100,000 different classes. Many existing approaches to extreme classification rely on inferring a tree structure among the output labels, such as hierarchical softmax, which reduces the number of binary predictions needed to infer the output class to a logarithmic number. Alternatively, label embedding approaches can be learned efficiently through least-squares or sampling. However, the authors note the limitations of these approaches: the inferred latent tree may not be optimal, leading to cascading errors, and the label embedding approach may result in prediction errors when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

The authors propose an alternative approach, reducing extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. This framework is more scalable and offers theoretical guarantees for consistency with the original multiclass Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) formulation. The proposed method, Aggressive Double Sampling reduction, is controlled by two parameters: one that determines the frequency of sampling each class, which is inversely proportional to the empirical class frequency to avoid missing classes in the long tail, and another that sets the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly. This procedure is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier.

The experimental results on five text datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, comparing it to several competing approaches, including standard one-vs-all classification when computationally feasible. The results show that the method generally outperforms others in terms of accuracy and F1 score once the number of classes exceeds 30,000. Moreover, it trains faster and uses significantly less memory, although it suffers somewhat in terms of prediction speed.

At prediction time, inference is complicated by the fact that generating pairwise features with all possible classes is intractable. To address this, the authors employ a heuristic that identifies candidate classes by creating an input space centroid representation of each class as the average of vectors in the class. Prediction is then carried out by a series of pairwise classifications with only those candidates.

The theoretical analysis of the reduction is complex due to the change from a sum of independent random variables to a sum of random variables with a particular dependence structure among the dyadic examples. Additionally, the oversampling of rare classes shifts the empirical distribution. The proof involves splitting the sum of dependent examples into several sums of independent variables based on the graph structure induced by the example construction and using the concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables introduced by Janson. Although the finite-sample risk bound is biased by the ratios between the true class probabilities and the oversampled ones, these biases decrease linearly with the size of the sampled and re-sampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, although I have not verified the proof in detail.

Overall, this paper presents a strong method for extreme classification, with small memory and computational requirements and superior performance on especially the largest datasets. The binary reduction algorithm appears theoretically sound, interesting, and useful to practitioners.
