- Problem: tackles extreme multiclass classification with up to 100k labels and notes limitations of label-tree (cascading errors) and label-embedding (low-rank assumption) approaches.  
- Method: reduces extreme classification to pairwise binary classification via an "Aggressive Double Sampling" reduction.  
- Sampling scheme: two parameters control inverse-frequency class sampling (to handle long tail) and the number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples, repeated to train a dyadic classifier.  
- Theoretical guarantees: provides consistency with multiclass ERM and a finite-sample risk bound, though analysis is complex due to dependent dyadic examples.  
- Proof approach: splits dependent sums using the induced graph structure and applies Janson’s concentration inequalities; bias from oversampling decreases linearly with sample size.  
- Inference: full pairwise feature generation is intractable, so prediction uses a centroid-based candidate selection heuristic followed by pairwise classifications.  
- Experiments: on five text datasets, outperforms competitors (including one-vs-all when feasible) in accuracy/F1 for >30k classes, trains faster and uses far less memory.  
- Trade-offs and caveats: prediction speed is slower; theoretical proof seems sound but was not fully verified by the reviewer.  
- Overall verdict: strong, practical, and theoretically interesting method useful for large-scale extreme classification.
