- Proposes Aggressive Double Sampling (ADS), a scalable sampling-based reduction from multiclass to pairwise binary classification for extreme classification.
- ADS comprises oversampling rare classes and subsampling large classes, then uniformly drawing adversarial classes to reduce dyadic examples.
- Provides theoretical consistency guarantees with the original multiclass ERM despite the reduction’s induced dependencies.
- Theoretical analysis handles dependent dyadic sums by splitting via the induced graph structure and applying Janson’s concentration inequalities.
- Evaluated on five text datasets; ADS outperforms competitors in accuracy and F1 once class counts exceed ~30k.
- ADS trains faster and uses far less memory than competitors on large datasets.
- Prediction speed is somewhat slower than some competing approaches.
- Requires careful tuning of two sampling-related hyperparameters (class sampling frequency and number of adversarial examples).
- Strengths: novel, scalable, theoretically sound, strong empirical performance, low memory/computational requirements.
- Weaknesses: hyperparameter sensitivity and slower prediction.
- Reviewer scores: Quality 9/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 9/10.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel scalable method with theoretical guarantees and superior results on very large-class problems.
- Arguments against acceptance: need for parameter tuning and reduced prediction speed.
