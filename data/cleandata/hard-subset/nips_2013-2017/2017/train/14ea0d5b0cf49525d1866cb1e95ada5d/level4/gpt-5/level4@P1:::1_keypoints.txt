- Paper reduces extreme multiclass to binary dyadic pairs and introduces (π, κ)-double sampling plus a candidate q-nearest-centroid selection for prediction.  
- Training: resample classes inversely to frequency (π), sample κ adversarial classes per example, and learn one binary classifier on dyadic features φ(x,y).  
- Prediction: restrict scoring to q nearest-centroid candidate classes to speed inference.  
- Theory: derives generalization bounds for dependent dyads using fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities.  
- Theory: proves ERM consistency under distribution shift induced by π and κ, with bounds depending on α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy.  
- Theory insight: larger κ improves bounds via a √((K−1)/(Mκ)) term, but very small π can inflate bounds through α, β.  
- Reviewer requests explicit comparison to prior bound in [16] and intuition/sensitivity for typical α, β values (e.g., when π ∝ 1/µ).  
- Empirics: evaluated on large LSHTC-derived datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia up to 100K classes) with strong accuracy and macro-F1.  
- Empirics: method offers substantial training-time and memory reductions versus OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, and often beats tree/embedding methods.  
- Empirics caveat: prediction time is slower than tree-based methods; candidate selection helps but q, throughput, and accuracy–speed trade-offs are not reported.  
- Reviewer asks for ablations isolating effects of π, κ, q and a single vs. double sampling comparison.  
- Clarity: paper is generally clear but notation is dense; a complexity summary (training/prediction as functions of M, K, κ, q) would help.  
- Clarity: dyadic feature map φ is hand-crafted for text; need systematic description and guidance for non-text domains.  
- Originality: contributions are incremental relative to [16], mainly adding practical double sampling and refined local-complexity bounds.  
- Significance: addresses scalability and long-tailed labels with strong training/memory gains but limited impact where low-latency prediction is required.  
- Pros: simple practical sampling targeting long tails, sound theoretical treatment of dependence/shift, large-scale experiments, tunable candidate selection.  
- Cons: modest novelty over [16], slower prediction than trees, hand-crafted φ limits generality, and some comparisons mix batch/online methods.  
- Requested experiments/details: compare new bounds to [16], sensitivity plots for κ/π/q, report docs/sec and model size, detail centroid computation/storage and fairness of memory comparisons.  
- Dataset concern: LSHTC multilabel-to-multiclass conversion by replication may exacerbate imbalance—discuss and quantify its effect.  
- Overall verdict: promising but borderline—strengthened theoretical comparison and deeper empirical analyses of κ, π, and q needed for stronger acceptance case.
