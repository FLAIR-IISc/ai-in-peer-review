- Proposes a novel (π, κ)-double sampling strategy to reduce multi-class to binary via dyadic examples for extreme classification (up to 100k classes).  
- Addresses limitations of tree-based methods (error propagation) and label-embedding methods (fail on non-low-rank label matrices).  
- π oversamples rare classes and subsamples frequent ones (π inversely proportional to class frequency); κ limits number of dyadic examples to reduce computational cost.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees showing empirical risk minimization remains consistent despite shifted class distribution and dependent dyadic examples.  
- Derives generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity, extending prior work to the dependence structure induced by sampling.  
- Handles dependence between dyadic examples via graph covering decomposition and concentration inequalities for dependent variables.  
- Finite-sample risk bound (Theorem 2) captures sampling bias and shows bias decreases linearly with sampled training set size.  
- Experimental evaluation on LSHTC (10k–100k classes) is thorough and compares to OVA, tree-based, and embedding baselines.  
- Demonstrates superior memory, training time, and predictive performance (accuracy and macro F1) on largest datasets (WIKI-50K, WIKI-100K).  
- Maintains competitive prediction speed using a candidate selection heuristic based on class centroids.  
- Paper is very clear and well-organized; technical contributions and experiments are precisely described.  
- Minor clarity issue: candidate selection heuristic explanation (Section 2.3) would benefit from a brief illustrative example.  
- Main weakness: prediction heuristic may fail if class centroids are poor representations for some data distributions.  
- Overall assessment: technically strong, novel sampling contribution with rigorous theory and practical gains; recommends acceptance.
