Summary:
This paper introduces a novel reduction from multi-class to binary classification, particularly well-suited for scenarios with a large number of classes. The approach considers a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, employing a loss function that quantifies the fraction of incorrectly predicted classes receiving higher scores than the true class. Specifically, the method utilizes a feature transformation, φ, mapping (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional feature space, and learns a mapping from R^p to scores. This reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each data point (x,y), generates K-1 transformed points representing comparisons between the true label y and incorrect labels y'. Recognizing the correlated nature of examples within the transformed dataset, which invalidates many standard generalization bounds, the paper's first contribution is an enhanced generalization analysis over Joshi et al. (2015) through new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity. The second key contribution demonstrates that sampling techniques can be effectively employed during the reduction process to balance class distributions and decrease the training set size. The authors achieve this by sampling classes (with replacement) to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed data and by utilizing a random sample of classes instead of all K-1 possible comparisons. They demonstrate that these sampling procedures do not significantly degrade their generalization bounds. Finally, empirical results show the proposed method achieves competitive performance against strong baseline algorithms.

Comments:
This paper presents a compelling reduction from multi-class classification, especially effective when dealing with a large number of classes, to binary classification, along with corresponding generalization bounds designed to address the correlated data inherent in the reduced dataset. The primary novelty of the reduction lies in the sampling technique used to rebalance class distributions and reduce the size of the transformed dataset; the foundational reduction itself was previously presented in Joshi et al. The empirical comparison to existing algorithms is strong and demonstrates competitive performance. 

However, the paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm described in Section 2.3, where comparisons are limited to the k classes with centroids closest to a given input point. 

Furthermore, I would have welcomed additional discussion regarding the sample complexity bounds, potentially including a comparison to generalization bounds derived for non-transformed multi-class problems.
