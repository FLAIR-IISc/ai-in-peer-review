- Addresses extreme multi-class classification via dyadic reduction and an aggressive double-sampling scheme (per-class inverse-frequency sampling π and κ negative classes per example), plus nearest-centroid candidate selection at prediction time.  
- Contrasts with tree-based and embedding methods, staying within pairwise learning while introducing principled sampling with generalization guarantees.  
- Theoretical contribution: handles dependencies among dyads and sampling-induced distribution shift using fractional covers, Janson concentration, and local/fractional Rademacher complexities to give finite-sample risk bounds and ERM consistency.  
- Method is simple and scalable: (π, κ)-double sampling and dyadic loss matching multiclass ranking surrogate; nearest-centroid candidate selection is a practical speedup.  
- Experiments on large benchmarks (including 50k–100k classes) show competitive or superior accuracy and macro-F1 for large K, with much lower memory and faster training than several baselines.  
- Main empirical trade-off: prediction can be slower than tree traversal; candidate selection mitigates but pairwise scoring over candidates still costly.  
- Need for sensitivity analyses: tune and report effects of π, κ, and q on accuracy/time and on α, β (oversampling bias) terms.  
- Clarify hypothesis class and optimization details for the dyadic classifier (e.g., linearity, regularization, solver).  
- Report candidate-set recall (probability true class ∈ Yx) versus q to quantify prediction-time loss.  
- Originality: novel aggressive double-sampling and accompanying generalization analysis; nearest-centroid selection is pragmatic; dyadic text features are sensible but less novel.  
- Significance: strong practical value for extreme classification under long-tailed labels due to memory/training gains and theoretical treatment of dependence and distribution shift.  
- Pros: scalable, theoretically grounded, strong empirical performance, simple implementable algorithms.  
- Cons: slower prediction than trees, limited ablation/sensitivity analyses, dependence on hand-designed dyadic features and unclear generality beyond text, missing some embedding baselines.  
- Suggested additions: accuracy/F1 vs. q and prediction time curves, ablations for κ and π, hypothesis-class/optimization details, discussion of integrating candidate selection into theory, and exploration of learned φ beyond text.  
- Overall: strong submission—simple effective reduction with solid theory and empirical results; recommend acceptance with requested clarifications and robustness analyses.
