

This paper presents a novel approach to large-scale multi-class classification by reducing the problem to binary classification through a double sampling strategy. The authors build upon the multi-class to binary reduction framework of Joshi et al. (2015), which transforms multi-class classification into a binary problem over pairs of examples. The key innovation lies in their aggressive sampling technique that addresses two critical challenges in large-scale multi-class problems: long-tailed class distributions and computational intractability from the exponentially growing number of dyadic examples.

The authors propose a (π, κ)-double sampling strategy that first oversamples small classes and subsamples large classes to balance class distributions, and then reduces the number of dyadic examples by sampling only a subset of possible class comparisons. They theoretically demonstrate that this sampling preserves the consistency of empirical risk minimization through new generalization bounds derived using local fractional Rademacher complexity. The analysis extends previous work by providing tighter bounds that incorporate variance information, leading to faster convergence rates. Empirically, the approach is evaluated on DMOZ and Wikipedia collections with 10,000-100,000 classes, showing significant improvements in training/prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

Strengths:
1. Quality: The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with new generalization bounds that properly account for the dependencies in the transformed dataset. The proof techniques are sound and build effectively upon prior work. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using appropriate datasets and evaluation metrics (accuracy and macro F1) that account for class imbalance.

2. Clarity: The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical formulations. The double sampling procedure is explained thoroughly with pseudocode, and the theoretical contributions are presented in a logical sequence. The experimental setup is detailed with appropriate baselines (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse) and careful parameter tuning.

3. Originality: While the core reduction strategy builds on Joshi et al. (2015), the double sampling technique represents a significant practical and theoretical contribution. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from prior work by addressing both the class imbalance problem and the computational complexity of the dyadic transformation, which previous methods had not adequately handled.

4. Significance: The approach directly addresses a major challenge in large-scale machine learning applications (extreme multi-class classification), with clear benefits for text classification tasks involving tens of thousands of classes. The method achieves the best trade-off among runtime, memory usage, and predictive performance compared to existing approaches.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the candidate selection approach used in prediction (Section 2.3). While the authors empirically demonstrate that selecting the k nearest centroid classes works well, they do not provide theoretical guarantees for this approximation. This is particularly concerning given the paper's strong focus on theoretical guarantees.

2. The sample complexity analysis could be strengthened. The paper provides generalization bounds for the reduced problem but does not explicitly compare these bounds to those for standard multi-class classification or discuss the implications for sample complexity in the original problem.

3. The paper mentions that "the number of classes pre-selected can be tuned to offer a prediction time/accuracy tradeoff" but does not provide empirical evidence of this tradeoff, which would be valuable for practitioners.

Pro and Con for Acceptance:
Pros: Strong theoretical contribution with practical significance for large-scale classification; comprehensive empirical evaluation showing clear advantages over baselines; well-structured and clearly written; addresses a meaningful problem in the machine learning community.

Cons: Missing theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm; limited discussion of sample complexity implications; the core reduction idea has precedent in Joshi et al. (2015), though the sampling technique is novel.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of extreme classification. The aggressive sampling strategy effectively addresses two major challenges in large-scale multi-class problems while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating the approach's superiority in terms of computational efficiency and predictive performance. The paper is well-written and makes a clear case for its methodological contribution.

The primary concern is the lack of theoretical analysis for the prediction algorithm, which is a critical component of the proposed method. While the authors provide strong empirical evidence that the candidate selection approach works well, a theoretical justification would strengthen the paper considerably. I would recommend addressing this concern in the final version.

Overall, this paper demonstrates significant technical merit, addresses an important problem with practical implications, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The proposed method represents a meaningful improvement over existing approaches for large-scale multi-class classification, particularly when dealing with extremely large numbers of classes and long-tailed distributions. The paper meets all the quality standards expected for NIPS and would be of interest to both practitioners and researchers working on large-scale classification problems.
