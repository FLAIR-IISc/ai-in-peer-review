This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme classification – multiclass classification with a very large number of classes (up to 100k). Existing approaches often rely on hierarchical structures (like hierarchical softmax) or label embeddings. The authors rightly point out the limitations of these methods: suboptimal tree structures leading to error propagation, and potential inaccuracies of label embedding when the underlying label matrix isn’t low-rank. 

Instead, this work proposes a learning reduction to pairwise binary classification, coupled with a scalable sampling-based method and accompanying theoretical guarantees. This is a compelling approach, as it reframes the problem in a more tractable manner while maintaining consistency with the empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation of the original multiclass problem.

The core of the method, “Aggressive Double Sampling,” involves strategically sampling pairwise examples. One parameter controls the sampling frequency of each class, inversely proportional to its empirical frequency – a crucial design choice to prevent under-representation of rare, “long-tail” classes. The second parameter governs the number of adversarial examples drawn. This iterative process builds a dyadic classifier.

A key practical challenge arises during prediction: evaluating pairwise features against all classes is computationally prohibitive. The authors address this with a clever heuristic: representing each class by a centroid in the input space (the average vector of its instances) and then performing pairwise classification only with a subset of candidate classes.

The theoretical analysis is a significant strength of this paper. Reducing a multiclass problem to pairwise classification introduces dependencies between the binary examples and distorts the empirical distribution due to oversampling of rare classes. The authors carefully navigate these complexities by decomposing the sum of dependent examples into independent components based on the graph structure of their construction. They leverage concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables (from Janson’s work) and demonstrate that the resulting finite-sample risk bound, while initially biased, improves linearly with the size of the training and resampling sets. While the proof’s intricacies were not fully verified by this reviewer, it appears sound and thoughtfully constructed.

Experimental results on five text datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. Compared to several competing approaches – including standard one-vs-all classification where feasible – the authors show superior accuracy and F1-score when the number of classes exceeds 30k. Furthermore, the method exhibits lower memory consumption and faster training times. The only trade-off is a slight decrease in prediction speed.

Overall, this is a strong and well-motivated paper. The proposed method is theoretically grounded, practically efficient, and achieves excellent results on challenging datasets. It offers a valuable contribution to the field of extreme classification and will be particularly useful for practitioners dealing with massive label spaces. The algorithm’s low memory footprint and computational requirements are especially noteworthy.
