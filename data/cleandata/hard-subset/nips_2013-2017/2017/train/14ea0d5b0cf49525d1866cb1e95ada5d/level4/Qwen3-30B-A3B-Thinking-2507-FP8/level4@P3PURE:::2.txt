

Summary:  
This paper introduces a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, specifically designed for scenarios with a very large number of classes. The approach defines a hypothesis mapping (input, class) pairs to scores, where the underlying loss function quantifies the fraction of incorrect classes assigned a higher score than the true class. The authors propose a feature transformation $\phi$ mapping (input, class) pairs to a $p$-dimensional feature space, followed by a learned mapping from $\mathbb{R}^p$ to scores.  

The reduction extends Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each training example $(x, y)$, generates $K-1$ transformed points corresponding to comparisons between the true label $y$ and each incorrect label $y'$. However, the resulting transformed dataset contains correlated examples, rendering standard generalization bounds inapplicable. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) an improved generalization analysis using tighter bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity for this correlated setting, and (2) the introduction of sampling techniques to rebalance class distributions and reduce training set size. Specifically, the authors sample classes with replacement to ensure equal representation in the transformed data and replace exhaustive $K-1$ comparisons with random sampling of classes. They prove these sampling procedures do not significantly degrade generalization guarantees. Empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves highly competitive performance against strong baselines.  

Comments:  
This work presents a compelling reduction from large-scale multi-class classification to binary classification, with rigorous generalization bounds addressing the challenge of correlated examples in the reduced dataset. The primary novelty lies in the sampling strategy for rebalancing class distributions and reducing the transformed dataset size, while the core reduction framework builds upon Joshi et al. (2015). The empirical evaluation against state-of-the-art baselines is robust and well-executed.  

A critical gap is the absence of a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm in Section 2.3, where comparisons are restricted to the $k$ classes whose centroids are closest to the input point. The authors should rigorously justify this heuristic or analyze its impact on generalization. Additionally, a deeper discussion of the sample complexity bounds—specifically, a comparison to generalization bounds for non-transformed multi-class problems—would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
