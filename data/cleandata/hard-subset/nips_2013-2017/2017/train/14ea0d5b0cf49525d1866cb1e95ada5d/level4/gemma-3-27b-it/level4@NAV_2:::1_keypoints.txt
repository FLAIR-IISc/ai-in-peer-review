- Addresses extreme multi-class classification with many classes via a double sampling strategy built on the multi-class-to-binary reduction of [16].
- Core idea: reduce long-tail class imbalance and computational cost by sampling both instances and label pairs during reduction.
- Theoretical contribution: new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexity (Theorem 2) to justify the sampled reduction.
- Empirical evaluation: comprehensive experiments on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia) up to 100k classes.
- Strong baselines included (OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PD-Sparse), showing competitive accuracy/F1 and improved training time and memory.
- Demonstrates scalability and practical relevance for applications like text categorization and recommendation.
- Novelty is incremental—method builds heavily on prior work [16], so not a paradigm shift.
- Prediction latency remains a concern; training is faster but prediction can be relatively slow and needs more discussion.
- Impact of the candidate selection strategy (Sec. 2.3) on prediction time and overall performance requires further investigation.
- Practical interpretation and comparative utility of the new generalization bounds are unclear; comparison to bounds in [16, 22] is needed.
- Experimental comparisons mix batch and online learners, potentially yielding unfair memory/time comparisons; a more nuanced discussion is required.
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, practical scalable approach, solid empirical results, and theoretical justification.
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental novelty, unresolved prediction-time limitations, unclear practical relevance of bounds, and imperfect experimental fairness.
