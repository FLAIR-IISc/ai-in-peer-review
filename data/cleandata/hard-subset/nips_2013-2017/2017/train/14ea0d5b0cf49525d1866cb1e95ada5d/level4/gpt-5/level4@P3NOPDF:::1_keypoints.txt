- Summary: Proposes a double sampling strategy built on [16], provides local Rademacher complexity bounds, and shows empirical scalability and reduced training time on large-scale text multiclass tasks.

- Strength: Comprehensive empirical evaluation against strong baselines showing competitive or superior accuracy.

- Strength: Significant reduction in training time versus some alternatives while maintaining predictive performance.

- Strength: Addresses a practically important extreme multiclass classification problem with large label spaces.

- Weakness: Incremental novelty over [16]; unclear what new capabilities or guarantees double sampling enables beyond prior work.

- Weakness: Inference is comparatively slow; test-time latency may dominate practical costs and is not fully quantified.

- Weakness: The provided generalization bound is hard to interpret—unclear tightness, dependence on classes/samples, and comparison to existing bounds.

- Weakness: Heterogeneous baselines (batch vs online) and incomplete resource reporting obscure fair comparison of algorithmic merit.

- Question: Provide ablation/sensitivity analyses isolating the effect of double sampling and its hyperparameters (e.g., compare [16], [16]+double sampling, full method).

- Question: Compare the LRC-based bound qualitatively and quantitatively to prior bounds (e.g., global Rademacher, margin-based), detailing assumptions and scaling with number of classes.

- Question: Empirically show whether the theoretical bound tracks generalization (e.g., plots of bound vs test error across settings).

- Question: State test-time complexity vs number of classes and whether candidate generation, hierarchical routing, ANN retrieval, or pruning can reduce prediction cost.

- Question: Clarify baseline tuning and fairness: hyperparameter budgets, early stopping, number of passes for online methods, and model selection procedures.

- Question: Confirm dataset/task specs—are all datasets single-label multiclass? Clarify metrics used (top-1 vs top-k).

- Question: Report performance under heavy class imbalance and provide robustness analyses for extreme-class distributions.

- Suggestion: Clearly position contributions relative to [16], with a schematic or pseudocode of the two sampling steps and their roles.

- Suggestion: Strengthen theory by stating the main bound with explicit dependencies (classes, sample size, norms, margins) and discuss tightness vs prior results.

- Suggestion: Report comprehensive efficiency metrics including test-time latency, throughput, and peak memory for training and inference; show accuracy–latency trade-offs.

- Suggestion: Standardize experimental comparisons (training passes, hyperparameter budgets, hardware) or separate batch vs online baselines in reporting.

- Suggestion: Expand empirical analysis with ablations, hyperparameter sensitivity, and explore approximate inference methods to trade accuracy for speed.

- Suggestion: Improve presentation: fix grammatical errors, define notation early, and unambiguously describe algorithmic components.

- Overall assessment: Important and timely work with empirical training-speed benefits but modest novelty, high prediction-time costs, and under-explained theoretical contributions; needs clearer positioning, inference-cost analysis, standardized experiments, and more interpretable bounds.
