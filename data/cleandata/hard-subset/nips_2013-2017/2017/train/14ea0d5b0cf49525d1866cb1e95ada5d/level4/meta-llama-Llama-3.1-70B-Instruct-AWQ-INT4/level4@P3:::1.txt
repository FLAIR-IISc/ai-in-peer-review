This paper presents a double sampling strategy to improve the multiclass classification approach in [16]. The authors present both theoretical and empirical analysis of their proposed approach. Experiments conducted on text data sets show that the proposed approach can handle multi-class classification problems with a large number of classes.

The main idea of the paper is to address the problem of multi-class classification in the case where the number of classes is very large. The authors propose a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction strategy, which transforms the original multi-class problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples. The aim of the sampling strategy is to overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions exhibited in majority of large-scale multi-class classification problems and to reduce the number of pairs of examples in the expanded data.

The authors provide a comprehensive analysis of their approach, including a theoretical analysis of the generalization error bounds based on local Rademacher complexities. They also conduct extensive experiments on several datasets, including the DMOZ and Wikipedia collections, with up to 100,000 classes. The results show that the proposed approach achieves high performance with shorter training time on several datasets.

One of the main strengths of the paper is the comprehensive experimental comparisons with several baseline methods. The authors compare their approach with popular baselines, including OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse. The results show that the proposed approach achieves high performance with shorter training time on several datasets.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main limitations is that the novelty of the paper is relatively thin. The paper is mostly based on [16], and despite the two main improvements, the proposed double sampling strategy and the new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexities, the paper does not provide significant new insights.

Another limitation is that the proposed approach still requires long prediction time compared with other approaches. In practice, prediction time is often more important than training time, and the authors should provide more analysis on the prediction time of their approach.

Additionally, it is unclear how to interpret the generalization bound presented in the paper. The authors should provide more analysis on how the generalization bound compares with other approaches.

Finally, some methods presented in the experiments are batch learning algorithms and some are online learning algorithms. Therefore, the memory usages are very different, and the authors should provide more analysis on the memory usage of their approach.

Overall, the paper presents a comprehensive analysis of a double sampling strategy for multi-class classification problems with a large number of classes. While the paper has some limitations, it provides a valuable contribution to the field of extreme classification.
