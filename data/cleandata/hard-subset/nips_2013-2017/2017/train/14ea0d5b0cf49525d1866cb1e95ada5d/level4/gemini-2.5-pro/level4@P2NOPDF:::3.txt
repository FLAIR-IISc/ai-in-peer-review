This paper proposes a learning reduction for extreme classification, a multiclass classification problem characterized by an extremely large output space, often containing hundreds of thousands of classes.

Existing approaches to extreme classification often fall into two categories. Tree-based methods, such as hierarchical softmax, reduce the problem to a logarithmic number of binary predictions. However, as the authors point out, a sub-optimal tree structure can lead to cascading prediction errors. Label embedding methods, on the other hand, learn low-dimensional representations of labels but can suffer from poor performance if the true label matrix is not inherently low-rank.

In contrast, this paper builds upon an alternative framework that reduces extreme classification to a series of pairwise binary classifications. The authors propose a scalable, sampling-based reduction method and provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating its consistency with the original multiclass Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) objective.

The empirical evaluation, conducted on five text datasets, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method against several competing approaches (including one-vs-all where feasible). The results show that the proposed method is generally superior in terms of accuracy and F1-score, particularly on datasets with over 30,000 classes. Furthermore, it exhibits significantly faster training times and lower memory consumption. A noted trade-off is a slight decrease in prediction speed compared to some baselines.

The proposed "Aggressive Double Sampling" reduction is governed by two key parameters. The first parameter controls the sampling frequency for each class, employing an inverse propensity score based on empirical class frequency. This oversampling strategy ensures that rare classes from the long tail are adequately represented during training. The second parameter determines the number of negative (or "adversarial") examples to be sampled uniformly. This process is iterated to train the final pairwise classifier.

Inference at prediction time presents a challenge, as evaluating pairwise classifications against all possible classes is computationally intractable. To address this, the authors employ a heuristic to prune the search space. They first identify a small set of candidate classes by finding the nearest neighbors to the test instance in a feature space where each class is represented by its centroid (the average of its training instances). The final prediction is then made by performing pairwise classifications only within this reduced candidate set.

The theoretical analysis is non-trivial due to two main factors. First, the reduction from multi-class to pairwise binary classification transforms a sum of independent random variables into a sum of variables with a complex dependence structure. Second, the oversampling of rare classes shifts the training distribution away from the true empirical distribution. To overcome these challenges, the proof cleverly decomposes the sum of dependent examples into several sums of independent variables, leveraging the graph structure induced by the sampling process. The authors then apply concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables, as introduced by Janson. While the resulting finite-sample risk bound includes a bias term related to the ratio between the true and oversampled class probabilities, this bias diminishes linearly as the size of the training set increases. The analysis appears sound, although I have not verified the proofs in detail.

In summary, this paper presents a compelling and well-motivated method for extreme classification. It achieves superior performance, particularly on the largest-scale datasets, while maintaining low memory and computational overhead during training. The proposed binary reduction algorithm is theoretically sound, novel, and likely to be of significant practical value to the community.
