This work introduces a learning reduction for the task of extreme classification, which involves multiclass problems with an exceptionally large output space, potentially reaching 100,000 classes.

The paper begins by discussing prevalent strategies for extreme classification, such as those that infer a tree-like hierarchy over the labels (e.g., hierarchical softmax) to reduce the problem to a logarithmic number of binary decisions, or those that utilize label embeddings learned via least-squares or sampling. The authors identify key limitations of these methods: inferred tree structures may be suboptimal, causing cascading errors, while label embedding techniques can fail when the underlying label matrix does not possess a low-rank structure.

As an alternative, the authors position their work within the framework that reduces extreme classification to a set of pairwise binary classification problems. They propose a scalable, sampling-based method for this reduction and provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating its consistency with the original multiclass Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) formulation.

Experimental results on five text datasets, compared against several competing methods (including one-vs-all where computationally tractable), demonstrate the proposed method's superiority. Specifically, it achieves better accuracy and F1-scores on datasets with more than 30,000 classes. Furthermore, it boasts faster training times and significantly lower memory consumption, though this comes at the cost of a moderate reduction in prediction speed.

The proposed Aggressive Double Sampling reduction is governed by two hyperparameters. The first dictates the sampling frequency of each class, using a rate inversely proportional to its empirical frequency to ensure that rare classes in the long tail are not overlooked. The second hyperparameter determines the number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples. The final dyadic classifier is constructed by repeating this sampling procedure during training.

At prediction time, the method addresses the intractability of generating pairwise features against all possible output classes. To overcome this, a heuristic is employed to select a smaller set of candidate classes. This is achieved by creating a centroid representation for each class (as the average of its feature vectors), and the final prediction is made by performing pairwise classifications only among the identified candidates.

The theoretical analysis of the reduction is non-trivial. One complication arises because the transformation from multi-class to dyadic binary classification converts a sum of independent random variables into a sum with a specific dependence structure. A second challenge stems from the oversampling of infrequent classes, which alters the empirical distribution. The proof addresses these issues by decomposing the sum of dependent examples into several sums of independent ones, guided by the graph structure of the sampling process, and then applies Janson's concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables. While the resulting finite-sample risk bound exhibits a bias related to the ratio of true to oversampled class probabilities, this bias is shown to decrease linearly as the sizes of the sampled and re-sampled sets grow. The analysis appears rigorous, though I have not verified the proofs in detail.

In summary, this paper contributes a robust method for extreme classification that is notable for its modest memory and computational footprint, coupled with superior performance, particularly on the largest-scale datasets. The underlying binary reduction algorithm is theoretically well-grounded, presenting an interesting and practical solution for the community.
