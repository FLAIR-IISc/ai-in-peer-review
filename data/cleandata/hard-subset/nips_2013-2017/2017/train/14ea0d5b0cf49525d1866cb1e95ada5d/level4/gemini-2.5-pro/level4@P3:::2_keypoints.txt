- Proposes a scalable multi-class-to-binary reduction for extreme classification using an "aggressive double sampling" scheme.  
- Double sampling consists of class-level π-sampling (rebalance long-tailed class distribution) and pair-level κ-sampling (sample few negatives per example).  
- Provides theoretical analysis: new generalization bounds via local fractional Rademacher complexities and consistency after double sampling (Theorems 1–2).  
- Empirical evaluation on large text benchmarks (including up to 100k classes) shows competitive or superior accuracy, Macro-F1, training time, and memory versus strong baselines.  
- Method is significant and practical, directly addressing computational/memory bottlenecks and class imbalance in extreme classification.  
- Strong empirical results demonstrate scalability (training with 100k classes) and favorable trade-offs compared to tree-based and one-vs-all methods.  
- Theoretical justification is solid and non-trivial, lending credibility beyond an engineering heuristic.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and well-organized with precise algorithmic descriptions.  
- Gap between theory and practice: prediction-time candidate selection (q-nearest-centroid heuristic) is not covered by the generalization analysis.  
- Generalization bounds need more contextual discussion—how they depend on M, κ, and α and how they compare to bounds for standard approaches (e.g., one-vs-all).  
- Joint feature map φ is hand-crafted for text; more discussion or methods for designing/learning φ in other domains (images, recommender systems) is needed.
