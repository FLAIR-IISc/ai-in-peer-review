**Summary**

This paper proposes a reduction from multi-class to binary classification, specifically designed for problems with a large number of classes. The approach learns a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, using a loss function that measures the fraction of incorrect classes scored higher than the true class. The method assumes a feature transformation, phi, that maps (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional feature space, from which a scoring function is learned.

This reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), where each data point (x,y) is converted into K-1 transformed points, each representing a comparison between the true label y and an incorrect label y'. Since the resulting transformed dataset contains correlated examples, standard generalization bounds are not applicable. The paper's first contribution is an improved generalization analysis of this reduction, surpassing that of Joshi et al. (2015) by deriving new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity for this correlated data setting.

The second main contribution is the introduction of sampling techniques into the reduction process to balance class distributions and reduce the training set size. Specifically, the authors sample classes with replacement to ensure balanced representation and use a random subset of incorrect classes for comparison, rather than all K-1 of them. The authors demonstrate that these sampling procedures have a minimal impact on the derived generalization bounds. Finally, empirical results show that the proposed procedure is highly competitive with several strong baselines.

**Comments**

This paper presents an interesting reduction from multi-class to binary classification, well-suited for problems with a large number of classes. The authors also provide corresponding generalization bounds that account for the correlated data generated by the reduction. The primary novelty lies in the proposed sampling techniques for rebalancing class distributions and reducing dataset size; the underlying reduction framework was previously introduced by Joshi et al. The empirical comparison against existing algorithms is strong.

A weakness of the paper is the lack of a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm described in Section 2.3. This algorithm restricts comparisons to the k classes with centroids closest to the input point, and its theoretical impact is not discussed.

Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of its sample complexity bounds, particularly a comparison to bounds for standard (non-reduced) multi-class problems.
