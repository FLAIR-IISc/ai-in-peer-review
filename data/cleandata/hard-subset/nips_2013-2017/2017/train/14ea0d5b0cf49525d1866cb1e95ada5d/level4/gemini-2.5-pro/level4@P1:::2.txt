### **Review**

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of multi-class classification in the "extreme" setting where the number of classes is very large. The authors propose a method based on reducing the multi-class problem to a binary one. The core idea is to learn a function that maps (input, class) pairs to a real-valued score. The learning objective, based on a ranking-style loss, is to ensure that for any given input, the score of the true class is higher than the scores of incorrect classes. This approach builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which transforms each training example (x, y) into K-1 binary classification examples, each corresponding to a comparison between the true class y and an incorrect class y'.

The main contributions of this work are twofold. First, the authors provide an improved generalization analysis for this type of reduction, which generates a dataset with correlated examples. They use local fractional Rademacher complexities to derive new bounds that account for this data dependency. Second, and most importantly, they introduce an "aggressive double sampling" strategy to make the reduction scalable. This strategy involves two steps: (1) re-sampling the training data to balance the class distribution, which is often long-tailed in extreme classification settings, and (2) for each example, creating binary comparison pairs using only a small, random subset of the incorrect classes, rather than all K-1 of them. The authors provide generalization bounds that incorporate this sampling scheme, demonstrating the consistency of their approach. Finally, they present a strong empirical evaluation on several large-scale text classification datasets, showing that their method is highly competitive with, and often superior to, state-of-the-art baselines in terms of accuracy, training time, and memory usage.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

This paper presents an interesting and practical approach to extreme multi-class classification. The proposed reduction to a binary problem, combined with the aggressive sampling strategy, is well-motivated and appears highly effective.

**Strengths:**

*   The primary novelty lies in the double sampling technique, which cleverly addresses two key challenges in extreme classification: the long-tailed class distribution and the computational cost of creating all pairwise comparisons.
*   The empirical results are very strong. The proposed method demonstrates a compelling trade-off between predictive performance, computational speed, and memory efficiency, outperforming a comprehensive set of strong baselines on challenging, large-scale datasets.
*   The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The authors derive new generalization bounds that explicitly account for the data dependencies introduced by the reduction and the distributional shift caused by the sampling strategy, ensuring the consistency of the learning procedure.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the candidate selection heuristic used at prediction time (Section 2.3). In this step, predictions for a given test point are made by considering only the subset of classes whose centroids are closest to the point's feature representation. While this is a sensible and necessary heuristic for efficiency, an analysis of its impact on the overall error would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.
*   The discussion of the sample complexity bounds could be expanded. It would be very insightful to compare the derived generalization bounds with those for standard multi-class classification algorithms that do not use this reduction. Such a comparison would help to better contextualize the theoretical results and clarify the price (in terms of sample complexity) paid for the computational gains of the reduction and sampling.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a well-executed paper with strong theoretical and empirical contributions. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and represent opportunities for future work rather than significant flaws. The proposed method is of clear practical value for researchers and practitioners working on large-scale classification problems.
