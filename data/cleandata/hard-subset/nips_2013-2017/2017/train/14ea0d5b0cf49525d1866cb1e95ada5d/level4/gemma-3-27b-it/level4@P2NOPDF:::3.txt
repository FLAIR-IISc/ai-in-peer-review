This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme classification, specifically multiclass classification with a very large number of classes (up to 100,000). Existing approaches commonly rely on either learning a hierarchical structure among labels, as seen in hierarchical softmax, or embedding labels into a lower-dimensional space and learning via least-squares regression or sampling. The authors critique these methods, noting the potential for error propagation in tree-structured approaches and prediction inaccuracies when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

Instead, they propose a reduction of extreme classification to a series of pairwise binary classifications, and present a scalable sampling-based method with theoretical consistency guarantees to the empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation. Their approach, termed Aggressive Double Sampling, involves two key parameters: one controlling the sampling frequency of each class (inversely proportional to empirical class frequency to mitigate the long-tail problem), and another determining the number of adversarial examples to sample uniformly. This process is iteratively applied to train a final dyadic classifier.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance on five text datasets, comparing against several competing methods, including standard one-vs-all classification where computationally feasible. Results indicate that the proposed method generally outperforms alternatives in both accuracy and F1-score when the number of classes exceeds 30,000. Critically, the method also exhibits faster training times and significantly reduced memory consumption, though prediction speed is somewhat lower.

A key challenge during prediction is the intractability of generating pairwise features with all possible classes. The authors address this by employing a heuristic: they represent each class with a centroid vector derived from the average of its training vectors. Prediction then proceeds through pairwise classifications, but only against a set of candidate classes identified via this centroid representation. 

The theoretical analysis is a notable contribution. Reducing multi-class classification to dyadic binary classification introduces a complex dependence structure among examples, and the oversampling of rare classes further complicates the analysis by shifting the empirical distribution. The authors tackle this by carefully decomposing the sum of dependent examples into independent components based on the graph structure induced by their sampling process, and leveraging concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables established by Janson. While the finite-sample risk bound is influenced by the ratio between true and oversampled class probabilities, these biases demonstrably diminish with increasing sample and re-sample sizes.  The analysis appears rigorous, although a detailed verification of the proof was beyond the scope of this review.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a theoretically grounded and practically effective method for extreme classification. The binary reduction algorithm is both interesting and highly relevant to practitioners dealing with problems involving a massive number of classes, offering a compelling combination of accuracy, scalability, and computational efficiency, particularly for large datasets.
