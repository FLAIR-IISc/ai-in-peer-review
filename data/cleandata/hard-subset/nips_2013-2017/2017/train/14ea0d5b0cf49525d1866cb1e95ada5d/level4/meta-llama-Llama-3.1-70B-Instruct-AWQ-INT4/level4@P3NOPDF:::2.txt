This paper proposes a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, particularly suited for scenarios with a large number of classes. The authors consider a hypothesis that maps input-class pairs to scores, with an underlying loss function that counts the fraction of incorrect classes scored higher than the true class. Specifically, they employ a feature transformation phi that maps input-class pairs to a p-dimensional feature space and learn a mapping from R^p to scores.

Their reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which creates K-1 transformed points for each data point (x,y), where each transformed point intuitively corresponds to the comparison of label y with some incorrect label y'. However, the transformed dataset contains correlated training examples, rendering many standard generalization bounds inapplicable. The paper's first contribution is an improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al. (2015) using new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity in this setting. The second main contribution is the demonstration that sampling techniques can be employed during the reduction to balance class distributions and decrease the training set size.

The authors propose two sampling procedures: sampling each class with replacement to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed training data, and using only a random sample of classes instead of including transformed points for all K-1 possible class comparisons. They show that these procedures do not significantly impact their generalization bounds. Finally, the paper presents competitive results against several strong baselines.

The paper presents an interesting reduction from multi-class classification with many classes to binary classification, along with corresponding generalization bounds for dealing with correlated data in the reduced dataset. The main novelty of the reduction lies in the sampling technique for rebalancing class distributions and reducing the size of the transformed dataset, while the basic reduction was introduced in an earlier paper by Joshi et al. The empirical comparison to existing algorithms is strong.

However, a theoretical analysis of the impact of the prediction algorithm presented in section 2.3 is lacking. This algorithm performs comparisons only for the k classes whose centroids are closest to a given input point. Additionally, a more in-depth discussion of the sample complexity bounds would be beneficial, possibly comparing them to the generalization bounds obtained for non-transformed multi-class problems. This would provide further insight into the strengths and limitations of the proposed reduction.

Overall, the paper contributes to the development of efficient and effective methods for multi-class classification problems with a large number of classes. The proposed reduction and sampling techniques demonstrate competitive performance, and the improved generalization analysis provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approach. With some additional analysis and discussion, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
