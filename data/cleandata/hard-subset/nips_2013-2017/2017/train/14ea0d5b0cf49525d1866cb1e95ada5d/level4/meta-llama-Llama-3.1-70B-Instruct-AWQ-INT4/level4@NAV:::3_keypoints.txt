- Introduces a learning-reduction approach for extreme classification handling up to ~100k classes.  
- Notes limitations of existing tree-based (hierarchical softmax) and label-embedding methods: suboptimal inferred trees and failure when the label matrix isn't low-rank.  
- Proposes reducing extreme classification to pairwise binary classification via a scalable sampling-based method with consistency guarantees to the original multiclass ERM.  
- Empirical results on five text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 for datasets with >30k classes, faster training, and lower memory usage, at the cost of slightly slower prediction.  
- Presents Aggressive Double Sampling with two controls: class sampling frequency (inverse to empirical frequency) and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples, repeated to train a dyadic classifier.  
- Uses a prediction heuristic that builds input-space centroids per class to select candidate classes and then performs pairwise classification among those candidates.  
- Theoretical analysis is complex due to transformation into dependent dyadic examples and distributional shifts from oversampling; proofs split dependent sums using induced graph structure and apply Janson’s concentration inequalities.  
- Finite-sample risk bound is biased by ratios of true vs oversampled class probabilities, but the bias decreases linearly with sampled/re-sampled training set size; the analysis appears sound though not exhaustively verified.  
- Concludes the binary-reduction algorithm is practically useful, memory- and computation-efficient, and theoretically interesting for extreme classification.
