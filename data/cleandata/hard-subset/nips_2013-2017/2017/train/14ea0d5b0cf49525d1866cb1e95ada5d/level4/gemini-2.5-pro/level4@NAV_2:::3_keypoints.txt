- Addresses extreme multi-class classification by reducing to binary classification over example pairs to handle very large label sets.
- Proposes "Aggressive Double Sampling": re-sample training to balance classes then sample a small set of adversarial classes per example to build dyadic pairs.
- Contrasts with tree-based methods (error propagation) and label-embedding methods (fail when label matrix is not low-rank).
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance: superior accuracy and Macro F1 on large text benchmarks as class count grows.
- Achieves much lower memory usage and faster training times, making the approach practical and scalable.
- The double sampling strategy is original and effectively addresses pair-generation cost and long-tailed class imbalance.
- Provides solid theoretical analysis with generalization bounds that account for sampling-induced distribution shift and dependency from the reduction.
- Paper is clearly written and the algorithm and prediction-time heuristic (class-centroid candidate selection) are well explained.
- Main weakness: prediction/inference speed is slower and more complex than tree-based methods, relying on a heuristic candidate selection.
- Prediction effectiveness depends on the quality of class centroids, which may limit performance in some cases.
- Theoretical bounds include terms depending on the ratio between true and sampling distributions; practical implications under extreme imbalance need more discussion.
- Recommendation: accept — novel, scalable, empirically strong, memory- and training-efficient with supporting theory, though inference-time complexity is a notable caveat.
