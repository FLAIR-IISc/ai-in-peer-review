Summary:
This paper introduces a reduction from multiclass to binary classification designed for settings with a very large number of classes. The approach learns a scoring function over (input, class) pairs, and the loss is the fraction of incorrect classes that receive a higher score than the true class. Concretely, a feature map φ embeds each (input, class) pair into a p-dimensional feature space, and a function is learned from R^p to scores.
The reduction extends Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each example (x, y), creates K−1 transformed examples, each representing a comparison between the true label y and an incorrect label y′. Because the transformed examples are correlated, many standard generalization bounds are inapplicable. The paper’s first contribution is an improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al. (2015) using new bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity in this setting. The second contribution shows that sampling can be integrated into the reduction to balance class distributions and shrink the training set: they sample each class (with replacement) to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed data, and instead of creating all K−1 comparisons, they keep only a random subset of classes. They show that these two sampling procedures do not significantly affect the generalization bounds. Finally, the method performs very competitively against several strong baselines.

Comments:
This paper presents an interesting reduction from large-class multiclass classification to binary classification, along with generalization bounds that account for dependence in the transformed dataset. The main novelty lies in the sampling scheme for rebalancing class distributions and reducing the size of the transformed data; the core reduction itself follows prior work by Joshi et al. The empirical comparisons to existing algorithms are very strong.
One omission is a theoretical analysis of the prediction procedure in Section 2.3, where comparisons are restricted to the k classes whose centroids are closest to a given input. I would also have appreciated additional discussion of the sample-complexity bounds, ideally with a comparison to the generalization guarantees for the original (non-transformed) multiclass problems.
