- Proposes a scalable extreme multi-class method using a double-sampling strategy within a multi-class-to-binary reduction framework.  
- Aggressive double sampling: oversample small classes and undersample large ones, and randomly sample class comparisons per example to reduce dataset size.  
- Proves consistency of ERM under the sampling scheme via new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity (Theorem 2).  
- Theoretical bounds explicitly handle dyadic dependency and incorporate class-imbalance parameters (π, κ).  
- Empirical results on DMOZ and Wikipedia (up to 100k classes) show large gains in training/prediction speed, memory efficiency, and accuracy versus OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, etc.  
- Strength: tighter generalization bounds than prior work (Joshi et al. 2015) by leveraging local Rademacher complexity for faster convergence.  
- Strength: comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and strong baselines, demonstrating true scalability where some methods fail.  
- Originality: novel double-sampling mechanism meaningfully extends prior dyadic reductions and addresses imbalance and computational intractability.  
- Significance: provides a practical, theoretically grounded tool for extreme text classification with strong runtime/memory/accuracy trade-offs.  
- Weakness: candidate selection (k nearest-centroid classes) lacks theoretical justification or bounds on its impact on generalization/accuracy.  
- Weakness: sample complexity discussion is underdeveloped; no explicit comparison of effective sample complexity to non-reduced multi-class methods.  
- Recommendation: Accept — rigorous theory, novel method, and compelling empirical evidence; weaknesses are minor and fixable.  
- Pro summary: novel double-sampling, tighter bounds, strong empirical scaling on WIKI-100K, addresses real-world problem.  
- Con summary: missing theoretical analysis for candidate selection and explicit sample complexity comparison.
