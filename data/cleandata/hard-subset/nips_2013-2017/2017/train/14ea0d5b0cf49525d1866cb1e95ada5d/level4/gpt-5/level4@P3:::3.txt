Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses extreme multiclass classification, where the number of classes can reach 10^4–10^5. Rather than using tree-based methods (e.g., hierarchical softmax, RecallTree) that trade accuracy for logarithmic-time inference and may suffer from cascading errors, or label-embedding approaches that rely on low-rank label structure (often violated in extreme settings), the authors adopt a multiclass-to-binary reduction. They transform the problem into dyadic binary classification over pairs (example, class), but crucially introduce an aggressive double sampling strategy to (i) counter long-tailed class distributions and (ii) drastically reduce the number of constructed dyads.

Concretely: (π, κ)-DS first re-samples the training set by oversampling rare classes and downsampling frequent ones (πk ∝ 1/µk), then, for each retained example, samples κ “adversarial” negative classes uniformly from Y\{y}. This yields a much smaller dyadic dataset while balancing positives and negatives. At prediction, since scoring all K classes is expensive, they select a candidate subset via nearest class centroids (computed once in the dyadic space) and score only those. Theoretically, the reduction creates dependencies among dyads (all dyads sharing an example) and shifts class proportions. The analysis uses graph-based fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities for dependent data to derive finite-sample excess-risk bounds for both the full dyadic reduction and the doubly-sampled variant. The bounds include factors that depend on the sampling distribution (α, β), the number of negatives per example (κ), and the number of classes (K), and establish consistency of ERM under the proposed sampling.

Experiments on LSHTC datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia subsets with up to 100K classes) compare against OVA, multiclass SVM (LibLinear), PD-Sparse, and tree-based methods (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree). The proposed method achieves strong accuracy and macro-F1 once K grows large, with markedly lower training time and memory than one-vs-all and PD-Sparse. Prediction is competitive when using candidate selection, though full scoring would be slower.

Quality:
- Theory: The use of local fractional Rademacher complexity to handle dependent dyads is appropriate and improves upon earlier fractional Rademacher analyses. Theorems account for both dependence and sampling-induced distribution shift and recover consistency. The roles of α = max_y ηy/πy and β = max_y 1/πy are clearly identified; the dependence on κ and K is explicit. While I did not check every proof step, the approach and references (Janson; Ralaivola & Amini) are sound and relevant.
- Empirics: The datasets and baselines are appropriate for extreme classification. The method scales to 100K classes and yields favorable train-time/memory profiles relative to OVA/M-SVM and PD-Sparse, and better accuracy/MaF1 than tree-based methods that suffer from error propagation. The evaluation uses accuracy and macro-F1, the latter being important under class imbalance.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written, but some parts could be streamlined. The dyadic transformation (Eq. 3) and indexing could be clarified, and the intuition for the loss in Eq. (1) (a ranking surrogate) could be briefly related to familiar multiclass surrogates. The candidate selection procedure would benefit from a short complexity analysis and a plot of candidate-recall versus q. There are a few typos and formatting issues (e.g., “Even-though,” spacing around equations and footnotes).

Originality:
The combination of (i) aggressive, class-balanced sampling at the example level with (ii) limited negative sampling at the dyad level, together with (iii) generalization bounds for the resulting dependent, distribution-shifted dyads, appears novel. The candidate selection step via dyadic centroids is simple but effective. The work builds meaningfully on prior dyadic reductions and dependent-data generalization theory.

Significance:
Extreme classification remains an important practical problem in text and beyond. The proposed method offers a compelling accuracy–efficiency trade-off for very large K, with theory that explains when and why the sampling is safe. The approach should be useful to practitioners who need strong performance under tight memory constraints.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for addressing long-tail imbalance and dyad explosion.
- Principled sampling scheme with explicit hyperparameters (π, κ) and theoretical guarantees (bounds and consistency) under dependence and distribution shift.
- Strong empirical performance on large benchmarks, with substantial gains in training time and memory and competitive accuracy/MaF1.
- Practical prediction-time strategy using candidate selection to control inference cost.
- Public code and datasets.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Sensitivity analyses are limited. Please report:
  - Performance and efficiency as functions of κ and of the candidate size q (and the candidate recall).
  - Ablations of π (e.g., different oversampling schedules) and their effect on α and empirical performance.
- Feature mapping φ is tailored to text (10 hand-crafted similarity features). Discuss portability to other domains (images, recommendations) and whether learned φ (e.g., WSABIE-style) could be combined with (π, κ)-DS. An ablation or a learned-φ variant would strengthen generality.
- Prediction-time trade-offs: Provide big-O and wall-clock comparisons versus tree methods for various q, and report candidate-recall to ensure the true label is rarely excluded.
- Metrics and data: Since LSHTC2 is originally multilabel, converting to multiclass by replication may distort class priors. Please comment on potential biases and, if possible, add a purely multiclass dataset. Reporting micro-F1 alongside macro-F1 would complete the picture.
- Theory presentation: Offer guidance on choosing π to control α and β, interpret the dominant terms in the bounds, and specify whether results extend to surrogate losses (hinge/logistic) used in practice.
- Minor: Clean up typos and unify notation; clarify Eq. (3) and the role of κ in Eq. (6); ensure all figures/tables are self-contained.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a central scalability bottleneck in extreme classification with a principled, practical approach.
- Solid theoretical analysis tailored to the problem’s dependence structure and sampling shift.
- Demonstrated state-of-the-art accuracy–efficiency trade-off on very large K, with substantial memory and training-time reductions.
- Simple, reproducible method with public code.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited sensitivity/ablation studies leave open questions about robustness to hyperparameters and candidate selection.
- Domain-specific φ may limit generality; empirical evaluation is text-only.
- Prediction-time advantages depend on heuristic candidate selection; more thorough analysis would be useful.

Overall evaluation:
A strong and timely contribution to extreme multiclass classification. The double sampling reduction is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically compelling for very large K. With additional ablations and a clearer presentation of prediction-time trade-offs and parameter choices, this work would make a valuable addition to NIPS.
