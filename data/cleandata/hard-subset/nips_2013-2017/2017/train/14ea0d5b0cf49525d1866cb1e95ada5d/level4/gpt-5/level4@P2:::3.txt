Summary
This paper addresses extreme multi-class classification with up to 100k labels. It builds on multiclass-to-binary reduction via dyadic pairs and introduces an aggressive double sampling strategy to handle long-tailed class distributions and control the explosion in pairwise examples. The method first rebalances the training set by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones (π), then for each retained instance draws a small set of adversarial classes (κ) to form dyadic pairs. The authors provide generalization bounds that account for (i) dependence among dyadic examples generated from the same instance and (ii) distribution shift induced by class reweighting. The analysis leverages fractional and local Rademacher complexities and graph-cover arguments to show that empirical risk minimization on the doubly sampled dyadic data remains consistent. At prediction time, to avoid scoring all classes, the method uses candidate selection via nearest class centroids computed in the dyadic feature space.

The approach is compared against strong baselines (OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding-style extreme classifiers such as RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML) on LSHTC DMOZ/Wikipedia datasets with up to 100k classes. Results indicate that the proposed method achieves competitive or superior accuracy and macro-F1 once K exceeds ~30k, with substantially lower training time and memory usage. Prediction is slower than tree methods but mitigated by candidate selection.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself among three lines of work: (i) tree-based methods that achieve logarithmic-time inference but suffer from cascading errors and tree-construction challenges; (ii) label-embedding methods that rely on low-rank assumptions often violated in extreme settings; and (iii) dyadic reductions that convert multiclass problems into binary comparisons. The proposed sampling scheme is designed to overcome the computational burden of constructing all pairs and the imbalance typical of extreme classification, while the analysis extends prior generalization bounds for dependent dyadic data.

Quality
- Theory: The generalization analysis explicitly handles dependent dyadic variables and class-distribution shift due to sampling. The use of local fractional Rademacher complexity and graph covers is technically appropriate, and the bounds clearly exhibit the role of sampling ratios. While I did not verify every proof step, the reasoning appears sound and well aligned with established techniques for dependent data.
- Experiments: The empirical section is thorough for text classification. Comparisons include strong baselines and report training/prediction time, memory, accuracy, and macro-F1. The method scales to 100k classes, where OVA and multi-class SVM become impractical. PD-Sparse remains competitive on accuracy but lags in memory/time at larger scales.
- Reproducibility: Implementation details, datasets, and hyperparameter tuning are described; code availability is a plus.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, though notation-heavy. The double sampling algorithm and the dyadic reduction are well specified. Some editorial improvements would help: streamline notation around the transformation and covers; clarify constants in the bounds (α, β) and their practical impact; and provide a brief complexity discussion (expected number of dyadic pairs vs. κ, and prediction complexity vs. candidate set size q). Minor language issues (e.g., hyphenation) should be cleaned up.

Originality
The combination of (π, κ) aggressive sampling with a dyadic reduction, and accompanying generalization bounds that incorporate both dependence and class-reweighting, appears novel. The centroid-based candidate selection is a practical heuristic; the dyadic feature mapping draws on learning-to-rank ideas, which is reasonable for text.

Significance
The approach addresses a central pain point in extreme classification: training and memory cost at very large K, especially under long-tailed label distributions. The method offers tangible gains in training time and memory with competitive or improved accuracy/MaF1 on large datasets, making it attractive for practitioners. The theoretical guarantees add credibility and may inspire further work on sampled dyadic reductions.

Strengths
- Scalable reduction with strong empirical performance at K ≥ 30k.
- Significant reductions in training time and memory compared to OVA/M-SVM and PD-Sparse at scale.
- Theory that addresses both dependency among dyadic examples and distribution shift from sampling.
- Practical prediction via candidate selection; code and datasets provided.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on a hand-crafted dyadic feature map tailored to text; applicability to other modalities would benefit from learned representations or ablations across φ choices.
- Prediction remains slower than tree-based methods; provide more analysis of the candidate selection tradeoff (accuracy vs. q) and its computational cost.
- Sensitivity to π and κ is not deeply explored; include ablations to guide practitioners.
- The bounds involve α and β terms tied to sampling rates and class proportions; some discussion of how to set π to keep these constants small in practice would be helpful.
- Experimental scope excludes some embedding baselines; while prior work suggests they underperform in extreme settings, including at least one would strengthen the empirical case.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important and challenging problem at scale.
- Clear algorithmic contribution with solid theoretical backing.
- Strong empirical gains in training efficiency and memory, with competitive or superior accuracy/MaF1 on large K.
- Practical design choices (negative sampling, candidate selection) that make deployment feasible.

Cons:
- Generality beyond text requires further evidence or learned φ.
- Prediction speed depends on heuristic candidate selection; more analysis/ablation would be valuable.
- Limited sensitivity studies for π, κ, and q; bounds may be loose in practice.

Recommendation
A well-motivated and carefully executed contribution to extreme multiclass classification. The method is practical, theoretically grounded, and empirically strong on large-scale text datasets. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on minor clarifications and expanded ablations as noted above.
