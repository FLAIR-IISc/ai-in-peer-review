

This paper proposes a double sampling strategy to enhance multiclass classification for large-scale problems, extending the framework in [16]. The authors provide theoretical analysis, including generalization bounds derived from local Rademacher complexities, and empirical validation on text datasets. While the approach demonstrates competitive performance and reduced training time, key limitations regarding novelty, practicality, and experimental rigor warrant consideration.

**Strengths:**  
The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines across diverse text datasets. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art accuracy in most settings while significantly reducing training time—a notable advantage for resource-constrained scenarios. The focus on extreme classification (large-scale multiclass problems) is timely, given its relevance to real-world applications like recommendation systems and document tagging, where scalability is critical.

**Weaknesses:**  
The core contributions are incremental relative to [16]. The double sampling strategy and the new generalization bound represent refinements rather than fundamental advances. Specifically, the sampling technique adapts existing principles (e.g., from [16]), and the generalization bound, while technically sound, does not substantially outperform established bounds in the literature. This modest novelty limits the paper’s impact.  

More critically, the method’s longer inference time—a direct consequence of the double sampling mechanism—undermines its practical utility. In deployment scenarios, inference speed often outweighs training efficiency (e.g., in real-time applications), yet the paper fails to address this trade-off or propose mitigation strategies. The claim that "shorter training time" suffices as a primary advantage overlooks a key operational constraint.  

**Additional Concerns:**  
The theoretical contribution requires clarification. The paper introduces generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexities but does not explicitly compare them to bounds from prior work (e.g., [16] or other extreme classification methods). Without such a comparison, it remains unclear whether the new bounds offer tighter guarantees or practical insights.  

Furthermore, the experimental design conflates batch and online learning algorithms (e.g., comparing batch methods like SVM with online learners like Vowpal Wabbit). This creates an unfair comparison, as memory usage and scalability differ fundamentally between these paradigms. For instance, online methods typically require minimal memory but may sacrifice accuracy, while batch methods need substantial storage but often achieve higher precision. The paper should either standardize the learning paradigm or explicitly justify this mixed comparison.

**Recommendation:**  
While the technical execution is solid and the empirical results are compelling, the paper’s incremental nature and unaddressed practical limitations prevent it from making a strong contribution to the field. To strengthen the submission, the authors should: (1) clarify the novelty by explicitly differentiating their generalization bounds from prior work; (2) address inference time as a critical drawback, perhaps by proposing optimizations or acknowledging its trade-offs; and (3) re-evaluate experiments to ensure methodological consistency (e.g., all batch or all online). Without these revisions, the paper’s significance remains constrained.  

**Overall:** This work offers a useful incremental improvement for large-scale multiclass classification but lacks the conceptual novelty or practical resolution of key bottlenecks required for top-tier publication. It would be more suitable for a venue emphasizing incremental engineering advances over theoretical or methodological breakthroughs.
