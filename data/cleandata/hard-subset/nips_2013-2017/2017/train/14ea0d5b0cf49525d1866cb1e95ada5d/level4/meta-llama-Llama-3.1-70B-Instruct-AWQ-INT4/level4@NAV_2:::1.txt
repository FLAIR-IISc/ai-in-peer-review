This paper proposes a novel approach to multi-class classification problems by employing a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class to binary reduction strategy. The authors aim to address the curse of long-tailed class distributions commonly found in large-scale multi-class classification problems. The proposed method transforms the original multi-class problem into a binary classification problem over pairs of examples, and then applies a double sampling strategy to balance the presence of classes and reduce the number of constructed dyadic examples.

The paper provides a comprehensive theoretical analysis, including a new generalization bound based on local Rademacher complexities. The authors also conduct extensive experiments on several datasets, including the DMOZ and Wikipedia collections, and compare their approach with several state-of-the-art methods.

The experimental results show that the proposed approach achieves high performance with shorter training time on several datasets. The authors also demonstrate that their method is highly competitive in terms of time, memory, and performance measures, especially for large-scale collections.

However, the paper has some limitations. The novelty of the approach is relatively thin, as it builds upon the work in [16]. Additionally, the prediction time of the proposed approach is still relatively long compared to other methods, which may be a concern in practice.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach. The writing is good, and the paper is well-organized. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the related work and the significance of their contributions.

Regarding clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, some of the theoretical sections may be challenging for non-experts to follow. The authors could consider adding more explanations or examples to make these sections more accessible.

In terms of originality, the paper provides a new approach to multi-class classification problems, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, as mentioned earlier, the novelty is relatively thin, and the paper builds upon existing work.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses an important problem in machine learning, and the proposed approach has the potential to make a significant impact in practice. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several datasets, and the results are promising.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the concerns mentioned above. The authors should consider adding more explanations or examples to make the theoretical sections more accessible and providing a clearer comparison of their generalization bound with other approaches.
