Summary:
This paper proposes a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, particularly well-suited for scenarios with a large number of classes. It considers a hypothesis that maps input-class pairs to scores, with the underlying loss function quantifying the fraction of incorrect classes that are scored higher than the true class. Specifically, the authors utilize a feature transformation phi that maps input-class pairs to a p-dimensional feature space and learn a mapping from R^p to scores.

Their reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which generates K-1 transformed points for each data point (x,y), with each transformed point intuitively corresponding to the comparison of label y with an incorrect label y'. However, the correlated nature of the transformed dataset renders many standard generalization bounds inapplicable. The paper's first contribution lies in its improved generalization analysis, which leverages new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity in this setting. The second main contribution demonstrates the feasibility of employing sampling techniques during the reduction to balance class distributions and decrease the training set size. By sampling each class with replacement, the authors ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed training data and use a random sample of classes instead of including all K-1 possible class comparisons. They show that these sampling procedures have a negligible impact on their generalization bounds. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate that their new procedure performs competitively against several strong baselines.

Comments:
This paper presents an interesting reduction from multi-class classification with numerous classes to binary classification, along with corresponding generalization bounds that address the correlated data in the reduced dataset. The main novelty of the reduction lies in the sampling technique for rebalancing class distributions and reducing the transformed dataset size, whereas the basic reduction has been previously presented in Joshi et al.'s work. The empirical comparison to existing algorithms is strong.

One notable omission is a theoretical analysis of the impact of the prediction algorithm outlined in section 2.3, where comparisons are only performed for the k classes whose centroids are closest to a given input point. Additional discussion on the sample complexity bounds, possibly comparing them to the generalization bounds obtained for non-transformed multi-class problems, would have been appreciated.
