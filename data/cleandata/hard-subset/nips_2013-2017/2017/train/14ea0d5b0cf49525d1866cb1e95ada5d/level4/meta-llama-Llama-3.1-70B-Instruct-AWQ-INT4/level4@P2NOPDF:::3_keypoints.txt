- Problem: addresses extreme multiclass classification with up to 100k labels and limitations of tree-based and low-rank embedding methods (cascading errors, non-low-rank labels).  
- Proposal: reduce extreme multiclass to pairwise binary classification via a scalable sampling-based reduction.  
- Algorithm: introduces "Aggressive Double Sampling" with two parameters: inverse-frequency sampling to cover rare classes and uniform adversarial example draws.  
- Training: repeat sampled dyadic construction to train a single dyadic classifier.  
- Prediction: uses a centroid-based candidate selection heuristic to avoid generating pairwise features for all classes.  
- Theory: provides consistency guarantees for the reduction to the multiclass ERM despite induced dependencies among dyadic examples.  
- Technical challenge & proof: handles dependence using graph-splitting and Janson-style concentration inequalities; accounts for oversampling bias that shrinks linearly with sample sizes.  
- Empirics: on five text datasets, outperforms competitors (and one-vs-all when feasible) in accuracy and F1 once classes exceed ~30k.  
- Efficiency: trains faster and uses much less memory than alternatives, at the cost of modestly slower prediction.  
- Overall assessment: method is theoretically interesting, practically useful, and strong on very large label sets (though formal proof details should be carefully verified).
