This paper proposes a learning reduction for extreme multiclass classification (up to roughly 100k classes). Prior approaches often (i) infer a label tree (e.g., hierarchical softmax) to make O(log K) binary decisions, or (ii) learn label embeddings via least squares or sampling. The authors highlight two pitfalls: learned trees can be suboptimal and induce cascading errors, and embedding methods can mispredict when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

The paper instead reduces multiclass prediction to dyadic (pairwise) binary classification, introducing a scalable, sampling-based training procedure with theoretical guarantees of consistency with the original multiclass ERM objective.

Experiments on five text datasets, compared against several competitive baselines (including one-vs-all when feasible), show that the method generally achieves higher accuracy and F1 once the number of classes exceeds about 30k. It also trains faster and uses substantially less memory, though it incurs some slowdown at prediction time.

The Aggressive Double Sampling (ADS) reduction is controlled by two parameters. The first sets the sampling frequency for each class inversely proportional to its empirical frequency, preventing long-tail classes from being missed. The second specifies how many adversarial negatives to sample uniformly. Repeating this process yields the final dyadic classifier.

At inference time, exhaustive pairwise feature generation over all classes is intractable. The authors use a heuristic candidate selection step: represent each class by the centroid of its training examples in input space, select a candidate set using these centroids, and then run pairwise classification only against those candidates.

The theoretical analysis faces two challenges. First, converting multiclass examples into dyadic pairs introduces dependencies among the resulting examples, replacing a sum of independent random variables with a sum of dependent ones. Second, oversampling rare classes shifts the empirical distribution. The proof strategy constructs a dependency graph induced by the dyadic sampling, partitions the sum into components with sufficient independence, and applies Janson’s concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables. The resulting finite-sample risk bound includes bias terms determined by ratios of true to oversampled class probabilities; these biases shrink linearly with the sizes of the sampled and resampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, though I did not verify every detail.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to extreme classification: the method offers favorable memory and training-time footprints and delivers superior accuracy on the largest problems, with the main trade-off being somewhat slower prediction. The binary reduction and ADS procedure are well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and practically valuable.
