- Proposes a multiclass-to-dyadic binary reduction with aggressive double sampling ((π, κ)-DS) to handle extreme multiclass classification (K up to 100K).  
- Re-samples examples to balance long-tailed classes (πk ∝ 1/µk) and samples κ negative classes per example to reduce dyad count.  
- Uses nearest-class centroids in dyadic space to select candidate classes at prediction and score only those to reduce inference cost.  
- Theoretical analysis accounts for dependencies among dyads and distribution shift from sampling using graph-based fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities.  
- Derives finite-sample excess-risk bounds showing dependence on sampling distribution (α, β), κ, and K, and proves ERM consistency under the sampling scheme.  
- Experiments run on large LSHTC subsets (up to 100K classes) comparing to OVA, multiclass SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree-based methods.  
- Empirically achieves strong accuracy and macro-F1 at large K with much lower training time and memory than OVA and PD-Sparse.  
- Prediction is competitive using candidate selection; full scoring would be slower.  
- Theory: use of local fractional Rademacher complexity for dependent dyads is appropriate and improves prior analyses.  
- Theorems clearly identify roles of α = max_y ηy/πy and β = max_y 1/πy and make κ and K dependence explicit.  
- Empirics: datasets and baselines are appropriate and method scales to 100K classes with favorable resource profiles.  
- Evaluation metrics include accuracy and macro-F1, correctly emphasizing macro-F1 under imbalance.  
- Paper is generally well-written but some sections (dyadic transformation, loss intuition) need clearer exposition.  
- Candidate selection procedure needs complexity analysis and a plot of candidate-recall versus q.  
- A few typos, formatting issues, and notation clarifications (e.g., Eq. (3), κ in Eq. (6)) are needed.  
- Originality: combination of class-balanced example sampling, limited negative dyad sampling, and generalization bounds for dependent, shifted dyads appears novel.  
- Candidate selection via dyadic centroids is simple and practically effective.  
- Significance: addresses a key scalability problem, offering a compelling accuracy–efficiency trade-off for very large K with theory explaining safety of sampling.  
- Strength: principled sampling scheme with explicit hyperparameters and theoretical guarantees under dependence and shift.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance, large reductions in training time and memory, and practical prediction-time strategy.  
- Strength: public code and datasets improve reproducibility.  
- Weakness: limited sensitivity analyses—need results varying κ, candidate size q, and candidate recall.  
- Weakness: ablations on π (oversampling schedules) and their effect on α and performance are missing.  
- Weakness: feature mapping φ is hand-crafted for text; portability to other domains and learned-φ variants are not evaluated.  
- Weakness: prediction-time trade-offs need big-O and wall-clock comparisons versus tree methods and candidate-recall reporting.  
- Weakness: conversion of multilabel LSHTC2 to multiclass by replication may distort priors—micro-F1 and a purely multiclass dataset are suggested.  
- Weakness: theory section should offer practical guidance for choosing π, interpret dominant bound terms, and clarify extension to practical surrogate losses.  
- Recommendation: strong and timely contribution but needs additional ablations, clearer prediction-time analysis, and presentation fixes before acceptance.
