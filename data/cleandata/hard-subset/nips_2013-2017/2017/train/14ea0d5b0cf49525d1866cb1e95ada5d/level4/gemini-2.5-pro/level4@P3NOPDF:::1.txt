### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of extreme multi-class classification, where the number of classes is very large. The authors propose a double sampling strategy to enhance the efficiency of the multi-class classification method previously introduced in [16]. The core idea is to sample both a subset of classes and a subset of features to train weak learners more efficiently. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of their method, including a new generalization bound based on local Rademacher complexities. Empirically, they demonstrate through experiments on several large-scale text datasets that their approach can significantly reduce training time while maintaining competitive accuracy compared to existing methods.

**Strengths**

*   The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification, a highly relevant and challenging problem with numerous practical applications in areas like document tagging, recommendation systems, and web advertising.
*   The empirical evaluation is thorough. The paper includes comparisons against a solid set of baseline methods on several challenging, large-scale datasets. The results compellingly demonstrate that the proposed double sampling strategy leads to significant reductions in training time without a substantial sacrifice in predictive accuracy.

**Weaknesses**

*   The primary concern is the limited novelty of the proposed method. The work is a direct extension of the framework presented in [16], with the main contributions being the introduction of a double sampling scheme and a corresponding theoretical analysis. While useful, this feels more like an incremental improvement rather than a novel conceptual contribution.
*   A significant practical drawback is the prediction time. While the paper emphasizes the reduction in training time, the prediction cost remains high compared to several competing methods (e.g., tree-based approaches). In many real-world applications, low-latency inference is more critical than training efficiency, which potentially limits the applicability of the proposed method.

**Questions and Suggestions for the Authors**

*   The theoretical analysis provides a new generalization bound based on local Rademacher complexities. However, the practical interpretation and implications of this bound are not sufficiently discussed. Could the authors provide more intuition on how this bound helps in understanding the behavior of the double sampling strategy? Furthermore, a comparison or discussion of this bound in relation to existing bounds for other extreme classification methods would greatly strengthen the theoretical contribution.
*   The experimental comparison includes both batch and online learning algorithms. This comparison might be slightly unfair without a discussion of other critical resources, particularly memory usage. Online methods typically have much lower memory footprints. It would be beneficial to include a discussion or even an empirical comparison of memory consumption to provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between the different approaches.
*   Given the high prediction time, have the authors considered any strategies to accelerate inference, such as model pruning, quantization, or using different data structures for the trained model? A discussion on potential avenues for improving prediction speed would be a valuable addition.
