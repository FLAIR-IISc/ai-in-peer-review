- Paper proposes a double sampling strategy for large-scale multiclass classification, with theoretical analysis using local Rademacher complexities and empirical validation on text datasets.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing multiple baselines across diverse text datasets.
- Method achieves state-of-the-art accuracy in most settings and significantly reduces training time.
- Focus on extreme classification and scalability is timely and relevant to real-world applications.
- Core contributions are incremental relative to [16]; the sampling technique and bound are refinements rather than fundamental advances.
- The new generalization bound is technically sound but does not demonstrably outperform existing bounds in the literature.
- Longer inference time from the double sampling mechanism undermines practical utility, especially for real-time deployments.
- Paper fails to address the inference–training trade-off or propose mitigation strategies for slow inference.
- Theoretical results lack explicit comparison to prior bounds (e.g., [16]), leaving unclear whether guarantees are tighter or more insightful.
- Experimental design mixes batch and online algorithms, leading to unfair or inconsistent comparisons in memory and scalability.
- Recommended revisions: clarify novelty vs prior work, address/mitigate inference-time issues, and standardize or justify experimental paradigms.
- Overall assessment: useful incremental improvement with solid execution, but limited novelty and unresolved practical bottlenecks make it more suitable for a less selective venue.
