

Summary:  
This paper introduces a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, specifically designed for scenarios with a very large number of classes. The authors propose a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, where the underlying loss function quantifies the fraction of incorrect classes assigned higher scores than the true class. Specifically, they assume a feature transformation φ mapping (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional space, followed by a learned mapping from ℝ^p to scores.  

The reduction extends Joshi et al. (2015), which for each data point (x, y) generates K−1 transformed points corresponding to comparisons between label y and each incorrect label y'. Since the transformed dataset contains correlated examples, standard generalization bounds do not apply. The paper’s first contribution is an improved generalization analysis using new fractional Rademacher complexity bounds for this setting. The second contribution demonstrates that sampling techniques can rebalance class distributions and reduce training set size during reduction. Specifically, classes are sampled with replacement to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed data, and only a random sample of classes (instead of all K−1 comparisons) is used. The authors prove these sampling procedures negligibly affect generalization bounds. Empirical results show the method competes strongly against multiple strong baselines.  

Comments:  
This paper presents an interesting reduction for large-scale multi-class classification to binary classification, with theoretical guarantees addressing correlated data in the reduced dataset. The primary novelty lies in the sampling techniques for rebalancing classes and reducing transformed dataset size, while the core reduction structure builds on Joshi et al. (2015). The empirical comparison against existing methods is robust and compelling.  

However, the paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm in Section 2.3, where comparisons are restricted to the K classes whose centroids are closest to an input point. Additionally, the authors would benefit from further discussion of sample complexity bounds, including a comparison to generalization bounds for non-transformed multi-class problems.
