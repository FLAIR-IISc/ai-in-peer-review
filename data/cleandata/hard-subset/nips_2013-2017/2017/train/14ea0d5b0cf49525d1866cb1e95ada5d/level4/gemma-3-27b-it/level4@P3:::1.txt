This paper addresses the challenging problem of large-scale multi-class classification, specifically focusing on scenarios with an extremely large number of classes, common in applications like text categorization. The authors propose a double sampling strategy layered on top of a multi-class to binary reduction technique (building upon their prior work [16]), aiming to mitigate the curse of dimensionality and class imbalance. They provide both theoretical guarantees in the form of generalization bounds, and empirical validation on benchmark datasets, demonstrating improvements in training time, memory consumption, and predictive performance compared to several state-of-the-art methods.

The core idea—aggressively sampling both examples and classes to create a more manageable training set—is well-motivated. The problem of extreme multi-class classification has received considerable attention at NIPS and other machine learning conferences (e.g., [1, 3, 5, 11, 21]), with various approaches attempting to overcome the computational and statistical challenges. Tree-based methods [4, 6, 7, 9, 15] and embedding-based methods [11, 19] represent dominant strategies, alongside binary reduction techniques such as the one this paper builds upon [1, 28]. This work positions itself within the latter category, attempting to improve its scalability.  The theoretical contribution, demonstrating the consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle under the proposed sampling scheme, is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation on the DMOZ and Wikipedia collections is comprehensive. The method demonstrates competitive or superior performance in terms of both accuracy/F1-score *and* computational efficiency (training time and memory usage) compared to several strong baselines. The speed improvements are particularly noteworthy given the scalability concerns in extreme classification.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of new generalization bounds, accounting for the introduced sampling and inter-dependency between pairs, adds rigor to the approach. While the applicability of the bound might not be immediately obvious without further analysis (addressed in weaknesses), the effort to provide theoretical backing is commendable.
*   **Practical Relevance:**  Extreme multi-class classification is a crucial problem in many real-world applications. The focus on both performance *and* resource consumption makes this work particularly relevant for practitioners dealing with massive datasets.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the proposed method is described clearly. The algorithm is explained with sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** While the double sampling strategy and the corresponding generalization bound are contributions, the paper relies heavily on the framework established in [16]. The novelty, while present, feels somewhat incremental. A clearer articulation of how this work substantially advances the state-of-the-art beyond the previous publication would strengthen the paper.
*   **Generalization Bound Interpretation:** The practical interpretation and usefulness of Theorem 2 are not fully elucidated. The theorem includes several parameters (α, β) whose impact on the bound and, consequently, the practical benefit of the method, are not discussed in detail. A more intuitive understanding of how these parameters affect generalization would be helpful.  How does this bound compare to existing bounds for similar problems?
*   **Prediction Time Trade-off:** The paper acknowledges that prediction time can be longer compared to some other methods. Given that prediction latency is often critical in many applications, a more detailed discussion of this trade-off, and potential strategies to mitigate it (e.g., candidate selection with optimized parameters), would be beneficial.  The current discussion of candidate selection feels somewhat cursory.
*   **Fairness of Comparisons:** The comment regarding batch vs. online learning methods and their impact on memory usage is valid.  The comparison of memory consumption could be misleading without a more nuanced discussion of the inherent differences between the algorithms being compared.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem.
*   The proposed method demonstrates significant practical improvements in terms of speed and memory consumption without sacrificing accuracy.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the method's behavior.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The novelty of the approach is relatively incremental.
*   The practical interpretation of the generalization bound is unclear.
*   The trade-off between training and prediction time is not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the demonstrated improvements in efficiency and the theoretical analysis make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS.  The weaknesses, while present, are not substantial enough to warrant rejection, especially given the practical benefits of the proposed approach.  I recommend **acceptance**, with a minor recommendation for the authors to clarify the interpretation of their generalization bound and further discuss the prediction time trade-offs in the final version.
