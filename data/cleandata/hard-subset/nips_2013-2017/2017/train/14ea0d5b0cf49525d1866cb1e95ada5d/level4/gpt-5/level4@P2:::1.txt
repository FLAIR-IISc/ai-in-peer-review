Summary
The paper tackles extreme multiclass classification by reducing the problem to binary classification on dyadic example–class pairs, and introduces an aggressive double sampling scheme to address long-tailed class distributions and reduce computational load. The first sampling stage rebalances the training set by oversampling minority classes and subsampling majority ones via class-specific probabilities π. The second stage limits the number of dyadic pairs per example to κ randomly sampled “adversarial” classes, thereby shrinking the expanded dataset. The authors analyze learning with dependent dyadic pairs using fractional/local Rademacher complexities and derive generalization bounds that account for both dependence and the distribution shift induced by sampling. For prediction, they propose a candidate selection heuristic that restricts scoring to q nearest class centroids to reduce inference cost. Experiments on LSHTC/DMOZ and Wikipedia subsets with up to 100K classes compare the method to OVA, multiclass SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse. The results indicate competitive or better accuracy/MaF1 with substantially reduced training time and memory, and competitive prediction time when using candidate selection.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds on prior dyadic reduction methods (e.g., [16]) but differs by (i) a double sampling scheme to mitigate class imbalance and limit pair explosion, and (ii) new bounds based on local Rademacher complexities adapted to dependent samples via fractional covers. Compared to tree/embedding methods [3–7, 9, 11, 15, 19, 21], it avoids cascading errors and low-rank assumptions but incurs higher per-query cost unless candidate selection is employed.

Strengths
- Relevance: Extreme classification with long-tailed labels is important and practical in text, tagging, and recommendation.
- Methodology: The double sampling idea is simple, scalable, and well-aligned with the data’s imbalance structure; the candidate selection step addresses inference bottlenecks.
- Theory: The use of local fractional Rademacher complexity to handle dependence and sampling-induced shift is technically sound and advances the analysis relative to earlier dyadic reductions.
- Empirics: Comprehensive comparisons on large benchmarks (up to 100K classes), with training/prediction time, memory, and accuracy/MaF1. The approach shows favorable runtime/memory while maintaining strong predictive performance. Code availability improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Novelty: The contribution is incremental relative to [16]. Double sampling and the refined bounds are useful but not paradigm-shifting.
- Bound interpretation: The theorems introduce α and β terms depending on π and class proportions, but the paper does not quantify their magnitude or compare tightness to [16] or other bounds. Practical guidance on choosing π and κ from the theory is limited.
- Prediction cost: Even with candidate selection, inference can lag tree-based methods. The dependence of latency on q, κ, and K should be more thoroughly analyzed and reported.
- Experimental fairness: Some baselines are batch while others are online/streaming; memory footprints and training protocols differ, complicating direct comparisons. Converting multi-label data to multiclass by duplicating instances may distort distributions and evaluation.
- Ablations/sensitivity: There is limited ablation on π and κ, no study of κ-only vs π-only sampling, and little analysis of the candidate set size q. The trade-off between accuracy and latency is not fully characterized.

Quality
- Technical claims are generally well-supported, but the empirical section would benefit from tighter latency and memory accounting (peak vs steady-state, model vs training buffers).
- The theory appears careful; however, a discussion connecting the bounds to practical hyperparameter choices (e.g., how α, β grow with class imbalance and sampling rates) would strengthen the contribution.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear, but some sections are dense (e.g., the cover-based complexity discussion). Adding an intuitive explanation of the bounds and a small numerical illustration would help readers.
- Minor editorial issues (e.g., hyphenation, typos) should be addressed in a revision.
- Clearly state the candidate selection procedure, how q is chosen, and its impact on accuracy/latency.

Originality
- Moderate. The paper extends an established reduction framework with pragmatic sampling and a refined generalization analysis. Positioning relative to related sampling/negative sampling methods (e.g., sampled softmax, NCE) would help clarify novelty.

Significance
- The method is practically valuable for large K and imbalanced labels, especially in text. If the prediction-time trade-offs are clarified and ablated, the work can be impactful for practitioners.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses a timely and significant problem with a simple, scalable method.
- Sound theoretical analysis for dependent dyadic pairs with sampling-induced shift.
- Strong empirical results on large public benchmarks; code released.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Incremental novelty relative to [16]; contributions may be viewed as engineering refinements.
- Limited interpretability and practical guidance from the bounds; missing comparison to prior bounds.
- Prediction latency and candidate selection trade-offs not fully explored; fairness of resource comparisons across heterogeneous baselines is unclear.

Questions and suggestions
- Please quantify α and β for your datasets and compare bound magnitudes to [16]. Can the bounds inform principled choices of π and κ?
- Provide ablations: (i) π-only vs κ-only vs both; (ii) sensitivity to κ and q; (iii) training/inference costs and accuracy as functions of κ and q.
- Report per-example prediction latency and throughput with and without candidate selection; vary q to expose the accuracy–latency frontier.
- Clarify how multi-label-to-multiclass conversion affects class imbalance and macro-F1; consider reporting additional metrics (e.g., top-k accuracy).
- Ensure fair comparisons: standardize hardware, threading, and feature pipelines; distinguish model memory from training-time buffers; note algorithms’ batch/online nature.
- Discuss applicability beyond text (e.g., images/recsys) and how φ would be constructed or learned there.

Overall, this is a solid, practical paper with a useful sampling strategy and a thoughtful theoretical treatment. Clarifying the bound’s implications, strengthening ablations, and sharpening the inference-time analysis would significantly improve the contribution.
