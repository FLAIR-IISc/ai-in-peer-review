Introduces an efficient reduction from large-scale multi-class to binary classification using a dyadic transformation and a double-sampling strategy for extreme label sets (up to 100k classes).  
Double-sampling: oversamples minority/undersamples majority classes and samples subsets of incorrect classes per example to control transformed dataset size.  
Provides new generalization bounds (local fractional Rademacher complexity) proving ERM consistency under aggressive sampling and accounting for inter-dependencies.  
Empirical validation on DMOZ and WIKI-50K/100K shows large improvements in training/prediction speed, memory efficiency, accuracy, and macro-F1 versus OVA, PD-Sparse, and tree-based methods.  
Strength — theoretical soundness: bounds explicitly handle class distribution shifts and dependency, addressing limitations of prior work (Joshi et al., 2015).  
Strength — empirical significance: demonstrates scalability and superior practical performance where OVA/M-SVM and PD-Sparse struggle.  
Strength — clarity and organization: reduction, sampling, theory, and experiments are clearly presented and thoroughly compared across metrics.  
Weakness — prediction step lacks theoretical justification: nearest-centroid candidate selection needs analysis or empirical evidence of its effect on generalization/accuracy.  
Weakness — missing sample complexity comparison: no explicit comparison to non-transformed multi-class approaches (e.g., via oracle inequalities).  
Originality/significance: builds on Joshi et al. but the aggressive double-sampling is a meaningful, practical advance for extreme classification and long-tailed distributions.  
Recommendation: Accept — solid contribution with rigorous theory and experiments; minor gaps are addressable in revision.
