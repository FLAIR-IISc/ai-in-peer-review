

This paper proposes a novel double sampling strategy for reducing multi-class classification to binary classification in extreme classification settings with very large output spaces (up to 100,000 classes). The authors address two key limitations of existing approaches: tree-based methods suffer from error propagation due to cascading decisions, while label embedding methods fail when the label matrix is not low-rank. The paper builds on the dyadic representation framework (e.g., [1, 16, 28]) but introduces an aggressive sampling mechanism that overcomes the computational burden of constructing all possible dyadic examples.

The paper makes two significant contributions: First, it introduces a (π, κ)-double sampling strategy that balances class distribution by oversampling small classes and subsampling large classes (with π inversely proportional to class frequency), while simultaneously reducing the number of dyadic examples through κ. Second, it provides theoretical guarantees showing that the empirical risk minimization principle remains consistent despite the shifted class distribution and inter-dependency between dyadic examples. The authors derive generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity, extending previous work by [22, 25] to handle the specific dependence structure introduced by their sampling procedure.

The theoretical analysis is a strong point of the paper. The authors carefully address two challenging aspects: the dependence between dyadic examples (due to shared original examples) and the shift in class distribution caused by oversampling. They cleverly decompose the dependent sum into independent parts using graph covering techniques from [14], then apply concentration inequalities for dependent variables from [22]. The finite-sample risk bound (Theorem 2) correctly captures the bias introduced by sampling, showing it decreases linearly with the size of the sampled training set. While I have not verified the full proof, the high-level argument appears sound and addresses a non-trivial technical challenge.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-motivated. The authors test on LSHTC datasets with 10,000-100,000 classes, comparing against relevant baselines including OVA (which becomes infeasible for large K), tree-based methods (RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML), and embedding-based approaches (PD-Sparse). The results clearly demonstrate that their method outperforms alternatives on the largest datasets (WIKI-50K, WIKI-100K) in terms of memory consumption, training time, and predictive performance (accuracy and macro F1), while maintaining competitive prediction speed through a candidate selection heuristic. The authors' claim that the method is "highly competitive" for large-scale classification is well-supported by the data.

The paper's clarity is excellent. The technical contributions are well-motivated, the algorithmic description is precise, and the experimental setup is clearly documented. The theoretical analysis, while complex, is presented in a logical flow with appropriate definitions. The only minor clarity issue is the explanation of the candidate selection heuristic in prediction (Section 2.3), which could benefit from a brief example to clarify how class centroids are used to select candidates.

Regarding the four review criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and comprehensive experiments. The theoretical analysis is particularly strong, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness.
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization and precise technical descriptions. The figures and algorithms effectively illustrate the approach.
- Originality: While the dyadic reduction framework is not new, the aggressive double sampling strategy and its theoretical justification represent a significant novel contribution that addresses a key practical limitation of existing methods.
- Significance: The method directly addresses a critical challenge in extreme classification (large K, long-tailed distributions), providing a practical solution with demonstrable improvements in memory and computational efficiency for real-world applications.

The paper is highly relevant to NIPS, as it makes a substantial contribution to a growing area of machine learning (extreme classification) with clear practical implications for text classification, recommendation systems, and other applications with large output spaces. The method's practical advantages (reduced memory usage, faster training) make it particularly appealing to practitioners.

The main weakness is the potential limitation of the candidate selection heuristic for prediction. While the authors acknowledge this as a tradeoff (Section 2.3), it's worth noting that the performance of this heuristic depends on the quality of the class centroids, which could be problematic for certain data distributions. However, this is a reasonable practical compromise given the intractability of pairwise comparisons with all classes.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of extreme classification. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, and the method offers clear practical advantages for large-scale applications. The paper addresses a significant problem in a novel and effective way, with results that advance the state of the art in extreme classification. I recommend acceptance.
