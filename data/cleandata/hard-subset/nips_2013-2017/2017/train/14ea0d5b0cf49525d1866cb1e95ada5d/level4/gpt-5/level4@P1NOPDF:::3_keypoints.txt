- Proposes a scalable learning reduction for extreme multiclass classification (up to ~100k classes).  
- Notes drawbacks of existing approaches: learned label trees can be suboptimal and cascade errors; embedding methods fail when the label matrix isn’t low-rank.  
- Adopts a dyadic (pairwise) binary-reduction framework and introduces a sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees, consistent with the multiclass ERM objective.  
- Empirical results on five text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 for >~30k classes, faster training and much lower memory use, but some prediction-time slowdown.  
- Introduces Aggressive Double Sampling with two parameters: class sampling frequency inversely proportional to empirical frequency to preserve rare classes, and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples; repeated to train the dyadic classifier.  
- Uses a heuristic inference shortlist by representing each class via its input-space centroid and performing pairwise comparisons only on candidate classes to avoid intractable full comparisons.  
- Theoretical analysis handles dependencies from dyadic construction and distribution shift from oversampling via dependency-graph decomposition and Janson’s inequalities; yields finite-sample risk bounds biased by sampling ratios that diminish linearly with sample sizes.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution—memory- and compute-efficient, scalable to very large label spaces, empirically state-of-the-art on largest datasets, and theoretically well-motivated.
