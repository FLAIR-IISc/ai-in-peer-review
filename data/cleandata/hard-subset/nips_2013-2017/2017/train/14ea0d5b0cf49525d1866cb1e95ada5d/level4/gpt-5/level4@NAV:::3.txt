This paper introduces a learning reduction for extreme classification, i.e., multiclass problems where the label space can reach up to 100k classes.

Many extreme classification methods infer a tree over labels, as in hierarchical softmax, so that only a logarithmic number of binary decisions are needed, or they use label embeddings learned efficiently via least squares or sampling. The authors point out issues with these strategies: the learned latent tree may be suboptimal and cause cascading errors, and label embedding can yield errors when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

An alternative is to reduce extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. The authors take this second route and propose a scalable, sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees of consistency with the original multiclass ERM formulation.

Experiments on five text datasets, comparing against several competing methods (including standard one-vs-all when computationally feasible), show that once the number of classes exceeds 30k the proposed approach is generally superior in accuracy and F1, trains faster, and uses far less memory, though it is somewhat slower at prediction time.

The Aggressive Double Sampling reduction is governed by two parameters: one controls how often each class is sampled, set inversely to the empirical class frequency to avoid missing long-tail labels; the other specifies the number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples. This procedure is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier.

At inference time, generating pairwise features against all classes is intractable. A heuristic narrows candidates by representing each class with an input-space centroid—the average of its member vectors—and prediction proceeds via a series of pairwise classifications against only these candidates.

The theoretical analysis is challenging because the reduction from multiclass to dyadic binary converts a sum of independent random variables into a sum with a specific dependence structure among dyadic examples. A further complication arises from oversampling rare classes, which shifts the empirical distribution. The proof splits the dependent sum into several sums of independent variables based on the graph structure induced by the construction and applies Janson’s concentration equalities for partially dependent variables. Although the finite-sample risk bound is biased by ratios between true class probabilities and their oversampled counterparts, these biases decay linearly with the sizes of the sampled and resampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, though I have not verified the proof in detail.

Overall, this paper offers a strong method for extreme classification, with low memory and computational demands and superior results especially on the largest datasets. The binary reduction algorithm appears theoretically sound, interesting, and practical for practitioners.
