This paper presents a double sampling strategy to improve the multi-class classification approach from prior work [16]. The authors provide both theoretical and empirical analysis of their proposed method. Experiments conducted on text datasets demonstrate the approach can effectively handle multi-class classification problems with a large number of classes.

**Strengths:**

*   The authors conduct comprehensive experimental comparisons with several baseline methods. Results show that the proposed approach achieves competitive performance with shorter training times on several datasets.
*   The extreme classification problem is an important and actively researched area with many practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The paper builds heavily on existing work [16]. While the authors introduce a double sampling strategy and a new generalization bound based on local Rademacher complexities, the novelty feels somewhat incremental.
*   Although the proposed approach reduces training time, it still exhibits longer prediction times compared to some other methods. Prediction time is often a critical factor in real-world applications.
*   The practical implications of the presented generalization bound are unclear. A comparison to existing bounds would be beneficial.
*   The experimental comparison includes both batch and online learning algorithms, which makes direct comparisons of memory usage difficult and potentially misleading.

**Detailed Comments:**

The paper addresses the challenging problem of large-scale multi-class classification, a critical area for applications involving massive numbers of categories, such as text categorization. The authors correctly identify the limitations of one-vs-all approaches and build upon the dyadic representation strategy introduced in prior work. The proposed double sampling technique, aimed at balancing class distributions and reducing the number of dyadic examples, is a reasonable approach. The theoretical analysis, providing generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexity, is a valuable contribution, although its practical significance is not clearly articulated.

The experimental results are well-presented and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of training time and, in some cases, predictive performance. The comparison with a diverse set of baselines is commendable. However, the discussion of prediction time could be more nuanced. While the training time improvement is significant, the longer prediction time may limit the applicability of the method in latency-sensitive scenarios. 

It would be helpful to provide a clearer interpretation of the generalization bound. How does it compare to bounds derived from other approaches, and what practical guidance does it offer for parameter tuning or dataset selection? Furthermore, given the mix of batch and online learning methods in the experimental comparison, providing a more equitable comparison of memory usage would be valuable, perhaps by focusing on peak memory consumption or memory usage per example.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning.
*   The proposed approach demonstrates competitive performance and offers a reduction in training time.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a valuable contribution to the understanding of generalization in this setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is somewhat limited.
*   The prediction time remains a concern.
*   The practical interpretation of the generalization bound is unclear.



Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a reasonable contribution to the field. However, the incremental nature of the novelty and the concerns regarding prediction time and the generalization bound raise questions about its suitability for acceptance at a highly selective conference like NIPS. I would lean towards **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding the clarity and practical implications of the theoretical results and providing a more nuanced discussion of the prediction time performance.
