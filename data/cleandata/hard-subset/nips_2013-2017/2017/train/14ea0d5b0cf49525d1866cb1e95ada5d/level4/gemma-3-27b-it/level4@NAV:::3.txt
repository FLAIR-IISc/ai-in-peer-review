This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme classification – multiclass classification with an exceptionally large number of classes, reaching up to 100,000.

Existing approaches often rely on constructing a hierarchical structure over the labels (like hierarchical softmax) to reduce the computational burden to logarithmic complexity, or on learning label embeddings via low-rank approximations. The authors correctly identify limitations of these methods: suboptimal tree structures can propagate errors, and low-rank assumptions for label embeddings may not always hold. 

Instead, this work frames extreme classification as a reduction to pairwise binary classification and proposes a scalable sampling-based method with accompanying theoretical consistency guarantees relative to the standard multiclass empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation.

Empirical evaluation across five text datasets demonstrates the method’s effectiveness. Compared to competing techniques – including standard one-vs-all where feasible – it generally achieves higher accuracy and F1-score when the number of classes exceeds 30,000.  Importantly, it offers improved training speed and substantially reduced memory usage, though prediction speed is somewhat lower. 

The “Aggressive Double Sampling” reduction utilizes two key parameters: one controlling the sampling frequency of each class (inversely proportional to empirical class frequency to mitigate long-tail issues) and another governing the number of adversarial examples sampled uniformly. This process forms the training data for the final dyadic classifier.

Prediction is handled via a heuristic to manage the intractability of generating pairwise features with all classes. This heuristic constructs input space centroids for each class and performs pairwise classifications only against a set of candidate classes identified through these centroids.

The theoretical analysis acknowledges the complications arising from the reduction’s impact on variable dependence and the distribution shift caused by oversampling rare classes. The proof leverages a decomposition of the dependent example sum into independent components, utilizing concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables as developed by Janson. While the resulting finite-sample risk bound contains biases stemming from the oversampling process, these biases diminish linearly with increasing training set size. The analysis appears rigorous, though a detailed verification of the proof was beyond the scope of this review. 

In conclusion, this paper introduces a compelling and theoretically grounded method for extreme classification. Its low memory footprint, computational efficiency, and strong performance – particularly on large-scale datasets – make it a valuable contribution to the field, and the binary reduction algorithm is both interesting and practically useful.
