This paper addresses extreme classification, a multiclass classification setting with an extremely large output space (e.g., up to 100,000 classes). Many existing methods tackle this problem by inferring a tree structure over the labels (e.g., hierarchical softmax) to reduce the problem to a logarithmic number of predictions, or by using label embedding techniques that are efficient to train. However, the authors correctly note the drawbacks of these approaches: inferred latent trees may be suboptimal and lead to cascading errors, while label embedding can fail when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

As an alternative, this paper proposes a reduction of extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. The authors present a scalable, sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees demonstrating its consistency with the standard multiclass Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) formulation.

The proposed "Aggressive Double Sampling" reduction is governed by two key parameters. The first controls the sampling frequency of each class, which is set inversely proportional to its empirical frequency to ensure that rare classes in the long tail are not missed. The second parameter determines the number of "adversarial" examples to draw uniformly. This process is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier. At prediction time, a full pairwise comparison with all classes is intractable. The authors employ a heuristic to manage this: they first identify a small set of candidate classes by finding the nearest class centroids (defined as the average feature vector for a class) in the input space. Prediction is then performed by running pairwise classifications only against this candidate set.

The theoretical analysis of this reduction is non-trivial. The primary challenges arise because the reduction from multiclass to dyadic binary classification introduces dependencies among the sampled examples, and the oversampling of rare classes shifts the data distribution. To address this, the proof splits the sum over dependent examples into several sums over independent variables, leveraging the graph structure induced by the sampling process. The analysis then applies concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables, as introduced by Janson. The resulting finite-sample risk bound contains a bias term dependent on the ratio between the true and sampled class probabilities; however, this bias is shown to decrease linearly with the size of the training set. The analysis appears sound, although I have not verified the proofs in detail.

Experiments on five text classification datasets demonstrate the method's effectiveness. Compared to several competing approaches (including one-vs-all, where feasible), the proposed method is generally superior in terms of accuracy and F1-score on datasets with over 30,000 classes. Furthermore, it trains significantly faster and uses far less memory, though it is somewhat slower at prediction time.

Overall, this paper presents a strong and practical method for extreme classification. Its low memory and computational requirements for training, combined with superior performance on the largest datasets, make it a valuable contribution. The binary reduction algorithm is theoretically well-founded, interesting, and should be highly useful to practitioners in the field.
