- Proposes Aggressive Double Sampling (ADS), a scalable pairwise binary reduction for extreme classification with theoretical consistency guarantees.
- Critiques tree-based methods (risk of cascading errors from latent structures) and label embeddings (fail when label matrix isn’t low-rank).
- ADS uses class-sampling frequency inverse to empirical frequency to avoid ignoring rare classes and samples uniform adversarial examples.
- Learns dyadic binary classifiers by sampling class pairs and trains a final dyadic classifier from these examples.
- Uses a centroid-based prediction heuristic (class represented by mean input) to restrict pairwise classification to candidate classes and avoid full pairwise computation.
- Shows superior accuracy and F1 on five large text benchmarks, especially for datasets with >30,000 classes.
- Trains much faster and uses orders-of-magnitude less memory than baselines, at the cost of slightly higher prediction latency.
- Performance gains increase with class cardinality, making ADS particularly effective for 100k+ class problems.
- Provides a finite-sample risk bound addressing dependent dyadic examples and distribution shift from oversampling.
- Analysis decomposes dependence via the sampling graph and applies Janson’s concentration inequalities; bias from oversampling shrinks linearly with training size.
- Minor concerns: prediction speed penalty and the need to tune two ADS parameters, but these are reasonable trade-offs.
- Well-written paper with thorough experiments and fair comparisons; strong candidate for acceptance.
