- Tackles extreme multiclass classification (up to ~100k classes) via a learning reduction.
- Notes problems with tree-based methods (cascading errors) and label-embedding (fails when label matrix not low-rank).
- Reduces extreme classification to pairwise binary classification with a scalable sampling-based method and consistency guarantees.
- Empirically outperforms competitors on five text datasets for >30k classes, trains faster and uses much less memory, but prediction is somewhat slower.
- Proposes Aggressive Double Sampling with two controls: inverse-frequency class sampling to cover the long tail and uniform adversarial example sampling.
- Training repeats the sampling procedure to produce dyadic examples for the final binary classifier.
- Inference uses a centroid-based candidate selection heuristic to avoid generating pairwise features for all classes.
- Theoretical analysis addresses dependency among dyadic examples and oversampling shifts using graph-splitting and Janson’s concentration inequalities.
- Finite-sample risk bound depends on true vs. oversampled class-probability ratios but these effects shrink with larger sampled/resampled sets; reviewer finds analysis plausible.
- Overall verdict: strong, scalable, theoretically grounded, and practically useful for very large-class problems.
- Pros for acceptance: important problem, novel sampling, strong empirical results, theoretical guarantees, clear writing.
- Cons against acceptance: slower prediction speed; reviewer could not fully verify the detailed proof.
