Summary:
This paper proposes a new reduction from multiclass to binary classification that is especially suitable when the number of classes is very large. The authors consider a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, and use a loss that counts the fraction of incorrect classes that are scored higher than the true class. Specifically, they assume a feature transformation phi that maps (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional feature space, and they learn a mapping from R^p to scores.

Their reduction extends the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each data point (x, y), creates K−1 transformed points, each corresponding to a comparison of label y with some incorrect label y'. Because the transformed dataset contains correlated training examples, many standard generalization bounds do not apply. The first contribution is an improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al. (2015), using new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity for this setting. The second main contribution is to show that sampling techniques can be employed during the reduction to balance class distributions and reduce the training set size: they sample each class (with replacement) to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed training data, and, instead of including transformed points for all K−1 possible class comparisons, they use only a random sample of classes. They show that these two sampling procedures do not drastically affect their generalization bounds. Finally, they demonstrate that the new procedure performs very competitively against several strong baselines.

Comments:
This paper presents an interesting reduction from multiclass classification with many classes to binary classification, together with generalization bounds that account for the correlated nature of the transformed data. The main novelty is the sampling technique for rebalancing class distributions and reducing the size of the transformed dataset, while the basic reduction appeared previously in Joshi et al. (2015). The empirical comparisons to existing algorithms are very strong.

One element missing from the paper is a theoretical analysis of the impact of the prediction algorithm in Section 2.3, where comparisons are performed only for the k classes whose centroids are closest to a given input point.

I would also appreciate additional discussion of the sample complexity bounds, ideally comparing them to generalization bounds for the non-transformed multiclass problems.
