Summary:
This paper proposes a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, particularly well-suited for scenarios with a large number of classes. The approach considers a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, and employs a loss function counting the fraction of incorrectly classified classes that receive higher scores than the true class. Specifically, the method utilizes a feature transformation, φ, mapping (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional feature space, followed by learning a mapping from R^p to scores. 
Their reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each data point (x,y), creates K-1 transformed points representing comparisons between label y and incorrect labels y'. Recognizing that the resulting transformed dataset contains correlated training examples, standard generalization bounds are often inapplicable. The first contribution of this paper is an improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al. (2015) achieved through new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity in this correlated setting. The second key contribution demonstrates that sampling techniques can be employed during the reduction to balance class distributions and decrease training set size. In detail, they sample classes (with replacement) to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed training data, and rather than considering all K-1 possible class comparisons, they utilize a random sample of classes. The authors demonstrate that these two sampling procedures do not significantly affect their generalization bounds. Finally, they empirically demonstrate that their new procedure performs competitively against several strong baselines.

Comments:
This paper presents an interesting reduction from multi-class classification with many classes to binary classification, along with corresponding generalization bounds to address the correlation inherent in the reduced dataset. The primary novelty of the presented reduction lies in the sampling technique used to rebalance class distributions and reduce the size of the transformed dataset; the fundamental reduction itself appeared in earlier work by Joshi et al. The empirical comparison to existing algorithms is compelling.

A theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm detailed in section 2.3, where comparisons are limited to the k classes with centroids closest to a given input point, is absent and would be a valuable addition. 

I would also appreciate further discussion of the sample complexity bounds, potentially including a comparison to the generalization bounds achieved on the original, non-transformed multi-class problems.
