

Summary:
This paper introduces a novel approach to multi-class classification for extremely large-scale problems (10,000-100,000 classes) by transforming the problem into a binary classification task through a double sampling strategy. The authors extend the work of Joshi et al. (2015) by proposing a multi-class to binary reduction that creates dyadic examples (x,y) vs. (x,y') for each training instance, where y' represents incorrect classes. Their key contributions include a refined theoretical analysis using local fractional Rademacher complexity to establish generalization bounds for the transformed dataset with correlated examples, and an aggressive double sampling technique that oversamples small classes and subsamples large classes to address long-tailed distributions while reducing the transformed dataset size. The method demonstrates significant improvements in computational efficiency and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art approaches on DMOZ and Wikipedia collections.

Strengths:
Quality: The paper provides technically sound theoretical analysis with Theorems 1 and 2 establishing generalization bounds for the proposed double sampling strategy. The authors carefully address the challenges of class imbalance and correlated examples in the transformed space, providing a complete theoretical foundation for the method. The experimental design is comprehensive, evaluating multiple large-scale datasets with up to 100,000 classes and comparing against six strong baselines.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The mathematical formulations are precise and well-explained, and the pseudocode for the sampling algorithm is helpful for reproducibility. The experimental setup and results are presented with sufficient detail to allow for replication.

Originality: While the basic multi-class to binary reduction strategy builds on Joshi et al. (2015), the authors make a significant contribution through their double sampling technique that addresses class imbalance and reduces computational complexity. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly in how it handles long-tailed distributions that plague large-scale multi-class classification. The theoretical analysis using local fractional Rademacher complexity is novel in this context.

Significance: The proposed approach solves a critical problem in large-scale classification where traditional methods become computationally intractable. The empirical results demonstrate substantial improvements in training time, prediction time, and memory consumption compared to state-of-the-art methods, making it highly relevant for real-world applications like text classification with massive label sets. The method's ability to handle extremely large class spaces (100,000 classes) represents a meaningful advance in the field.

Weaknesses:
The paper lacks theoretical analysis of the candidate selection prediction algorithm in Section 2.3, which samples only k nearest-centroid classes for prediction rather than considering all classes. This is an important practical component that affects the overall performance but is not analyzed theoretically.

The discussion of sample complexity bounds could be strengthened by providing a more detailed comparison between the bounds obtained for the transformed problem and the generalization bounds for non-transformed multi-class problems.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with improved generalization bounds, presents a practical solution to a well-known problem in large-scale classification, demonstrates clear computational advantages through comprehensive experiments, and provides a clear, well-structured presentation of the method. The sampling strategy effectively addresses the long-tailed distribution challenge that plagues large-scale classification.

Arguments against acceptance: The missing theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm's impact on overall performance is a notable gap, and the paper could provide more insight into how sampling parameters (π and κ) affect the trade-off between computational efficiency and predictive accuracy.
