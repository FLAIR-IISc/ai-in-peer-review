This paper proposes a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, particularly well-suited for scenarios with a large number of classes. The authors consider a hypothesis that maps input-class pairs to scores, with the underlying loss function quantifying the fraction of incorrect classes that receive higher scores than the true class. Specifically, they employ a feature transformation φ that maps input-class pairs to a p-dimensional feature space and learn a mapping from R^p to scores.

The proposed reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which creates K-1 transformed points for each data point (x,y), with each transformed point intuitively corresponding to the comparison of label y with an incorrect label y'. However, the correlated nature of the transformed dataset renders many standard generalization bounds inapplicable. The paper's first contribution lies in its improved generalization analysis, which leverages new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity in this setting, thereby extending the work of Joshi et al. (2015).

The second primary contribution is the demonstration of the efficacy of employing sampling techniques during the reduction to balance class distributions and decrease the training set size. By sampling each class with replacement, the authors ensure roughly equal representation of each class in the transformed training data. Furthermore, instead of including transformed points for all K-1 possible class comparisons, they utilize a random sample of classes. Notably, the authors show that these sampling procedures do not significantly impact their generalization bounds.

The paper also presents a competitive performance evaluation against several strong baselines, showcasing the efficacy of the proposed reduction. The empirical comparison to existing algorithms is particularly strong, highlighting the paper's contribution to the field.

One notable omission is a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm outlined in Section 2.3, where comparisons are only performed for the k classes whose centroids are closest to a given input point. A more in-depth examination of this aspect would strengthen the paper's contributions.

Additionally, a more extensive discussion of the sample complexity bounds, potentially comparing them to the generalization bounds obtained for non-transformed multi-class problems, would be beneficial. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed reduction's implications and its position within the broader context of multi-class classification.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, along with corresponding generalization bounds for addressing the correlated nature of the reduced dataset. The main novelty of the reduction lies in the sampling technique for rebalancing class distributions and reducing the size of the transformed dataset.
