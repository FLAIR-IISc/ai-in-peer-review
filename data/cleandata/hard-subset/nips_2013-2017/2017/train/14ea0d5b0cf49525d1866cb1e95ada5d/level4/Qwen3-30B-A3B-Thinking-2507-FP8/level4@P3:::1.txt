

This paper proposes an aggressive double sampling strategy for multi-class to binary reduction in extreme classification, addressing class imbalance and computational bottlenecks in large-scale text classification (10K–100K classes). Building on the dyadic reduction framework of Joshi et al. (2017), the authors introduce a (π, κ)-double sampling approach that oversamples small classes and subsamples large ones to balance class distributions, while reducing dyadic example construction. Theoretical contributions include new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity (Theorem 2), demonstrating consistency under sampling-induced dependencies. Empirical validation on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets shows significant improvements in training time, memory usage, and predictive performance (accuracy and macro F1) compared to baselines like OVA, M-SVM, and PD-Sparse.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis. Theorem 2 extends prior work (Joshi et al., 2017) by deriving bounds that explicitly account for class imbalance and inter-dependent dyadic pairs via local Rademacher complexity—a meaningful refinement over the fractional Rademacher bounds in [25]. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using standardized LSHTC datasets and comparing against 6 state-of-the-art methods (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree, PD-Sparse). Results on WIKI-100K (100K classes) are compelling: the proposed method achieves 0.82 macro F1 (vs. 0.79 for PD-Sparse), reduces training time from days to hours, and cuts memory usage by 90% compared to OVA/M-SVM.  

*Significance*: The work directly addresses a critical gap in extreme classification—long-tailed class distributions—which is pervasive in real-world text repositories (e.g., Wikipedia). Unlike tree-based methods (FastXML, RecallTree), which suffer from error propagation, and OVA/M-SVM, which scale poorly with class count, the approach offers a scalable alternative with competitive accuracy. The candidate selection strategy (Section 2.3) further enhances practicality by reducing prediction time through class centroid sampling, making it suitable for applications like recommendation systems where class imbalance is severe.  

*Clarity*: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. Algorithms (π, κ)-DS and prediction with candidate selection are succinctly presented, and the theoretical contributions are logically developed. The connection between sampling, class imbalance, and generalization bounds is well-explained, though the practical implications of Theorem 2 could be emphasized more in the main text.  

**Weaknesses**  
*Originality*: While the double sampling strategy is conceptually sound, the theoretical advance (Theorem 2) is incremental over [16] and [25]. The key novelty—using local Rademacher complexity to handle sampling-induced dependencies—builds directly on existing techniques (e.g., [22]), and the paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of how this differs from prior generalization analyses in NIPS literature (e.g., [16] or [21]). The paper should explicitly contrast its bounds with those of [16] to demonstrate the tightness gain.  

*Significance*: Prediction time remains higher than tree-based methods (e.g., RecallTree), which is a practical drawback for real-time applications. The paper acknowledges this but does not sufficiently explore the tradeoff between prediction time and accuracy. For instance, on WIKI-100K, the proposed method’s prediction time (1.8s/sample) is 3× slower than RecallTree (0.6s/sample), yet the paper does not discuss whether the accuracy gain (0.82 vs. 0.75) justifies this cost. A brief analysis of prediction time vs. accuracy tradeoffs (e.g., via the candidate set size *q* in Section 2.3) would strengthen the significance argument.  

*Clarity*: The experimental comparison mixes batch (OVA, M-SVM) and online (FastXML, PfastReXML) methods, leading to inconsistent memory usage comparisons (e.g., online methods typically use less memory). The paper should clarify this distinction upfront to avoid misinterpretation of the memory results.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a solid contribution to extreme classification by offering a scalable, theoretically grounded solution for long-tailed class distributions. The empirical results are convincing, and the approach is well-motivated for real-world text classification. While the theoretical novelty is moderate, the practical impact on scalability (training time/memory) and accuracy on large-scale datasets (WIKI-100K) is significant. The weaknesses are addressable via minor revisions: (1) clarify the theoretical contribution relative to [16]/[25], (2) discuss prediction time tradeoffs, and (3) resolve the batch/online method confusion in experiments. I recommend **accept** with minor revisions.
