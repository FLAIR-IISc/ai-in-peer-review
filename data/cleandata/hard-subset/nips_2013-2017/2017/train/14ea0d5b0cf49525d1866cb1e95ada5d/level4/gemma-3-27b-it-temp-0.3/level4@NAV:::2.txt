This paper introduces a novel reduction strategy for transforming multi-class classification problems – particularly those with a large number of classes – into a binary classification framework. The approach centers around a hypothesis that assigns scores to (input, class) pairs, utilizing a loss function that quantifies the proportion of incorrectly classified classes receiving higher scores than the true class. Specifically, the method employs a feature transformation, denoted as phi, to map these pairs into a p-dimensional space, subsequently learning a mapping from this space to scores. Building upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which generates K-1 transformed points for each (x,y) representing comparisons between the true label and incorrect labels, this paper addresses the challenges posed by correlated training examples in the transformed dataset. The first key contribution lies in a refined generalization analysis, leveraging new bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity to overcome limitations in the original Joshi et al. (2015) work. The second major contribution demonstrates the efficacy of sampling techniques – both for balancing class distributions and for reducing the size of the transformed dataset. This involves sampling classes with replacement to achieve roughly equal representation and utilizing a random sample of classes instead of all K-1 possible comparisons. The authors demonstrate that these sampling procedures maintain the integrity of their generalization bounds. Experimental results showcase competitive performance against established baseline algorithms.

In summary, the paper offers a compelling reduction from multi-class to binary classification, accompanied by generalization bounds tailored to handle the inherent data correlation. The primary innovation resides in the sampling techniques used to rebalance classes and reduce dataset size, extending the foundational reduction presented in Joshi et al. The empirical evaluation provides strong support for the proposed method. 

However, the paper would benefit from a theoretical investigation into the impact of the prediction algorithm detailed in Section 2.3, which limits comparisons to the k nearest class centroids. Furthermore, a more in-depth discussion of the sample complexity bounds, potentially contextualized against generalization bounds for standard multi-class problems, would enhance the analysis.
