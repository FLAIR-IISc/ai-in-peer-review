This paper proposes a learning reduction for extreme classification—multiclass prediction with output spaces of up to 100k classes.

Existing approaches commonly either learn a label tree (e.g., hierarchical softmax) to reduce inference to a logarithmic number of binary decisions, or use label embeddings learned efficiently via least squares or sampling. The authors point out the drawbacks of these strategies: inferred latent trees may be suboptimal and cause cascading errors, and label-embedding methods can incur errors when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

As an alternative, extreme classification can be reduced to pairwise binary classification. This paper follows that approach and introduces a scalable, sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees of consistency with the original multiclass ERM objective.

Experiments on five text datasets, comparing against several baselines (including one-vs-all when computationally feasible), show that once the number of classes exceeds roughly 30k, the proposed method generally achieves higher accuracy and F1, trains faster, and uses far less memory, though it is somewhat slower at prediction.

The Aggressive Double Sampling reduction is governed by two parameters: one controls the per-class sampling frequency, set inversely proportional to empirical class frequency to avoid missing long-tail classes; the other specifies the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly. This process is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier.

At inference time, generating pairwise features against all classes is intractable. The authors therefore use a heuristic that builds an input-space centroid for each class by averaging its vectors, selects candidates via these centroids, and then performs pairwise classification only among those candidates.

The theoretical analysis is challenging because the reduction from multiclass to dyadic binary classification transforms a sum of independent random variables into a sum with a specific dependence structure among dyadic examples. A second challenge arises from oversampling rare classes, which shifts the empirical distribution. The proof strategy splits the dependent sum into several sums of independent variables based on the graph structure induced by the construction of dyadic examples, and applies concentration results for partially dependent variables due to Janson. Although the finite-sample risk bound is biased by the ratio between true class probabilities and the oversampled ones, this bias decreases linearly with the sizes of the sampled and resampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, though I have not checked the proof in full detail.

Overall, the paper presents a strong method for extreme classification, with low memory and computational demands and especially strong performance on the largest datasets. The binary reduction algorithm appears theoretically well-grounded, interesting, and practically useful.
