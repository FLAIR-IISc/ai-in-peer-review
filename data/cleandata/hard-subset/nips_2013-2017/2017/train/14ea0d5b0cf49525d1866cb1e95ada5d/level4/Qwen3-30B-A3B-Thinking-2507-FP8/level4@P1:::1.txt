

This paper proposes a novel double sampling strategy for multi-class classification with extremely large numbers of classes, building upon the multi-class to binary reduction framework introduced in [16]. The authors address the two primary challenges of large-scale multi-class classification: computational intractability with respect to class count and the curse of long-tailed class distributions. Their approach consists of (1) a class-balanced subsampling strategy that oversamples small classes and subsamples large classes to mitigate class imbalance, and (2) a reduction in the number of dyadic examples by sampling only κ adversarial classes per example rather than all K-1 possible classes. The authors provide theoretical guarantees by deriving new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity, showing that their sampling strategy preserves the consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle. They evaluate their approach on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets with up to 100,000 classes, demonstrating improvements in training time, prediction time, memory consumption, and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

Regarding Quality, the paper presents a technically sound approach with well-supported theoretical claims and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The theoretical analysis extends previous work by providing new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity, which is a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of the problem. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple strong baselines on multiple datasets with appropriate metrics (accuracy and macro F1). The experimental design is sound, with careful hyperparameter tuning and appropriate dataset preprocessing. The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding prediction time.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce their results, though the theoretical proofs would benefit from more intuitive explanations in the main text. The writing is generally clear, though some technical terms could be better motivated for readers unfamiliar with the multi-class to binary reduction literature.

Originality is somewhat limited, as the paper builds substantially on [16]. While the double sampling strategy and new theoretical bounds represent genuine contributions, the core framework remains largely unchanged from the prior work. The authors clearly identify their two main improvements (double sampling and new generalization bounds), but these do not constitute a sufficiently novel approach to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The novelty is relatively thin compared to other recent advances in extreme classification, particularly given that the authors acknowledge their approach is based on [16].

Significance is moderate. The problem of extreme multi-class classification is indeed important, with applications in text classification, recommendation systems, and other large-scale machine learning tasks. The proposed method offers practical improvements in training time and memory consumption, which are valuable for real-world applications with extremely large class spaces. However, the paper does not demonstrate a significant advance over the state of the art in terms of predictive performance, and the prediction time remains relatively high compared to tree-based methods, which is arguably the most critical metric for large-scale applications.

Strengths:
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation against multiple strong baselines on multiple datasets
- Practical improvements in training time and memory consumption for extremely large class spaces
- Theoretical contribution with new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity
- Clear presentation of the sampling strategy and its theoretical justification
- Well-motivated approach to addressing the long-tailed class distribution problem

Weaknesses:
- Relatively thin novelty, as the approach is built upon [16] with incremental improvements
- Prediction time remains high compared to tree-based methods, which is a critical metric for large-scale applications
- Unclear how the theoretical generalization bounds compare to those in previous work
- The paper does not sufficiently address why the proposed method would be preferred over existing tree-based approaches for practical applications
- Inconsistent treatment of batch vs. online learning algorithms in the experimental comparison (affecting memory usage comparisons)

The authors make a reasonable attempt to address the problem of long-tailed class distributions, which is a significant practical challenge in extreme classification. However, the improvements offered by their approach do not sufficiently distinguish it from existing methods, particularly given the persistent prediction time disadvantage. The theoretical contribution is meaningful but not transformative, and the empirical results do not demonstrate a clear advantage in predictive performance that would justify the method's complexity.

For acceptance consideration, the paper has good technical quality and addresses a meaningful problem, but the novelty is insufficient for NIPS. The significance is limited by the lack of substantial performance improvements over existing methods, particularly with respect to prediction time, which is often the most critical factor for large-scale applications. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison of theoretical guarantees with prior work and a clearer justification for why their approach should be preferred in practical settings where prediction time is crucial.

Pro:
- Well-executed empirical evaluation
- Practical improvements in training time and memory usage
- Valid theoretical contribution to the generalization bounds
- Addressing a significant problem in extreme classification (long-tailed distributions)

Con:
- Relatively thin novelty (builds substantially on [16])
- Prediction time remains high compared to tree-based methods
- Insufficient demonstration of significant performance advantage over state-of-the-art
- Unclear theoretical comparison with prior work
- Inconsistent comparison of batch vs. online learning algorithms
