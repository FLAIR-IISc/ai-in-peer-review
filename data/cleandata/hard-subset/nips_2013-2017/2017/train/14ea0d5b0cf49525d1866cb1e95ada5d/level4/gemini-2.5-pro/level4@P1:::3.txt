This paper presents a method for extreme multi-class classification, where the output space can contain up to 100,000 distinct classes. The core idea is to reduce the multi-class problem to a binary classification task, augmented by a novel "Aggressive Double Sampling" strategy. This approach is positioned as an alternative to dominant paradigms in the field. Unlike tree-based methods, which can suffer from cascading prediction errors, or label embedding techniques, which may perform poorly when the underlying label matrix is not low-rank, this work builds upon the framework of pairwise binary reductions. The main contribution is a highly scalable, sampling-based method accompanied by theoretical guarantees of its consistency with the original multi-class empirical risk minimization (ERM) problem.

Experiments conducted on several large text datasets demonstrate the method's effectiveness. When the number of classes exceeds 30,000, the proposed approach is shown to be generally superior to a range of competing methods in terms of both accuracy and F1-score. Crucially, it achieves these results while being significantly more efficient, using far less memory and training faster than other high-performing approaches like one-vs-all, which become computationally infeasible at this scale.

**Strengths:**

The proposed **Aggressive Double Sampling** reduction is a key strength, tackling two major challenges in extreme classification simultaneously: class imbalance and computational scale. The procedure is controlled by two parameters. The first governs the sampling frequency for each class, which is set inversely proportional to its empirical frequency. This step effectively re-balances the training data, ensuring that classes in the long tail are not overlooked. The second parameter controls the number of "adversarial" negative classes sampled uniformly to construct the dyadic training pairs. This two-stage process drastically reduces the number of pairs needed to train the final binary classifier, making the approach tractable for huge output spaces.

The paper provides a sound **theoretical analysis** of the proposed reduction. This is a non-trivial task, as the reduction introduces two complications: first, it transforms a sum of independent random variables (in the original ERM formulation) into a sum over dependent variables (the dyadic pairs generated from a single example are correlated); second, the oversampling of rare classes induces a shift in the data distribution. The proofs handle these issues by splitting the sum of dependent variables based on the induced graph structure and leveraging concentration inequalities for sums of partially dependent variables. The resulting finite-sample risk bound, while biased by the ratio between the true and sampled class probabilities, shows that these bias terms decrease as the training set size increases, thus establishing the consistency of the approach. Although I have not verified the proofs in detail, the analysis appears solid.

At prediction time, generating pairwise features against all possible classes is intractable. The authors propose a practical **heuristic for candidate selection**, which identifies a small subset of likely classes by comparing the test example to pre-computed class centroids. Pairwise classification is then performed only on this reduced set. This makes inference efficient, though it results in prediction speeds that are slower than the fastest tree-based methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a strong and well-rounded contribution to the field of extreme classification. The proposed method is scalable, effective, and backed by solid theoretical justification. It achieves an excellent balance between predictive performance, memory footprint, and computational requirements, with particularly strong results on the largest datasets. The binary reduction algorithm is theoretically interesting and appears highly useful for practitioners. The combination of a novel, practical algorithm with rigorous theoretical analysis makes this a high-quality paper suitable for acceptance.
