- Proposes a novel reduction from extreme multi-class to binary classification using a score-based loss over (input, class) pairs.
- Maps (x,y) to R^p via a feature transform φ and learns a mapping from R^p to scores.
- Builds on Joshi et al. (2015) by creating K-1 comparison points per example, yielding correlated transformed data.
- Key theoretical contribution: improved generalization analysis using recent bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity to handle correlations.
- Introduces a sampling strategy (sampling classes with replacement and sampling comparisons) to rebalance classes and reduce dataset size.
- Theoretically demonstrates that the proposed sampling procedures preserve generalization performance.
- Empirically shows competitive/strong performance on benchmark extreme multi-class datasets.
- Strengths: addresses scalability in extreme multi-class learning, provides sound theory, strong empirical results, and clear writing.
- Weakness: no theoretical analysis of the candidate selection strategy (restricting prediction to k nearest-centroid classes) and its impact.
- Weakness: lacks comparative analysis of the derived sample complexity bounds versus non-transformed multi-class bounds.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated approach with solid theoretical and empirical support addressing a practical problem.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing analyses on candidate selection and comparative generalization bounds.
- Overall assessment: solid contribution with compelling theory and experiments; recommend acceptance subject to addressing noted weaknesses.
