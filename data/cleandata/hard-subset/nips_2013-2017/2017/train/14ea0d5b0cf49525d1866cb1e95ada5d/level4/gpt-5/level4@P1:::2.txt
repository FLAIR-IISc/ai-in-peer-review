Summary
This paper addresses extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem over dyadic (input, class) pairs. The loss optimized measures, for each example, the fraction of incorrect classes scored above the true class, aligning with ranking-style losses used in multiclass SVMs and AdaBoost.MR. The authors consider a feature map φ that embeds (input, class) pairs into a p-dimensional joint space, and learn a scoring function over this space. Building on the dyadic reduction of Joshi et al. (2015), which creates K−1 paired comparisons per training example and thus yields dependent training examples, the paper contributes improved generalization bounds using fractional and local Rademacher complexity for dependent data.

The main algorithmic contribution is an aggressive double sampling strategy, (π, κ)-DS. First, it resamples the training set to counter long-tailed label distributions by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones according to class-dependent probabilities π. Second, for each retained example, it constructs dyadic pairs against only κ randomly chosen “adversarial” classes instead of all K−1. The authors prove that empirical risk minimization over this doubly sampled reduction remains consistent, providing bounds that account for both distribution shift induced by resampling and dependencies among dyadic examples. For prediction, they further propose a candidate selection step that restricts scoring to a small set of q nearest-centroid classes per test point to reduce inference time. Experiments on LSHTC/DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets with up to 100K classes show strong performance in training/prediction time, memory, and accuracy/MaF1 relative to OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, and state-of-the-art tree/embedding methods.

Strengths
- Sound theory for dependent dyadic data: The paper extends prior analysis by deriving new bounds based on fractional/local Rademacher complexities, yielding tighter control via variance-sensitive terms and establishing consistency under double sampling.
- Practical and scalable: The (π, κ)-DS procedure addresses both long-tailed class imbalance and quadratic blow-up in pair construction, substantially reducing memory and time.
- Strong empirical results: On large Wikipedia subsets (50K and 100K classes), the method is competitive or superior in accuracy and MaF1 while using markedly less time and memory than OVA/M-SVM and often comparable runtime to tree-based methods without their error-propagation drawbacks.
- Clear connection to prior work: The paper situates itself with respect to dyadic reductions (Joshi et al.) and contrasts with tree and embedding approaches.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in reduction: The dyadic reduction is inherited from Joshi et al.; the primary algorithmic novelty lies in the double sampling scheme.
- Missing theoretical analysis of prediction pruning: The candidate selection in Section 2.3 (scoring only q nearest-centroid classes) is not analyzed theoretically. It would be valuable to quantify its effect on consistency or provide bounds on top-1 error under candidate pruning.
- Limited comparison of bounds: The sample complexity/generalization guarantees are not explicitly contrasted with those for non-reduced multiclass formulations, which would help contextualize the benefits and trade-offs of the dyadic approach.

Quality
- The methodological development is technically sound, with appropriate handling of dependency via fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities. The empirical evaluation is thorough and uses strong baselines on standard benchmarks. One theoretical gap is the lack of analysis for the candidate selection step.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The presentation of the reduction, sampling, and bounds is coherent. The role of parameters (π, κ, q) is clear operationally; adding brief intuition on how they influence the terms α and β in Theorem 2 would further aid readers.

Originality
- The theoretical refinement for dependent dyadic data and the consistency analysis under the double sampling scheme are original and valuable. Algorithmically, the reduction framework is known; the novelty is the aggressive sampling design and its analysis.

Significance
- The approach is practically significant for extreme classification, achieving strong accuracy-time-memory trade-offs at 50K–100K classes. The theoretical contributions for dependent samples may be useful beyond this specific setting.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance and scalability on large benchmarks.
- Solid theoretical guarantees for a practically motivated sampling scheme under dependent dyadic examples.
- Clear advancement over prior dyadic reductions in both analysis and efficiency.

Cons for acceptance
- Main algorithmic novelty is incremental relative to prior dyadic reductions.
- No theoretical guarantees for the candidate selection step used at prediction time.
- Limited discussion comparing derived bounds to non-reduced multiclass analyses.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide a theoretical or empirical analysis of the candidate pruning step (e.g., conditions ensuring the true class is retained with high probability as a function of q, or bounds relating centroid proximity to ranking margins).
- Discuss how α and β in Theorem 2 scale with π and the empirical class distribution; include guidance for tuning π and κ and sensitivity analyses.
- Add a brief comparison or discussion relating your bounds to standard multiclass generalization results to clarify the benefits of the dyadic route.
