- Presents a learning reduction for extreme multiclass classification with output spaces up to ~100k classes.
- Critiques tree-based (cascading errors) and label-embedding (fails if label matrix not low-rank) approaches.
- Proposes reducing extreme classification to pairwise binary classification with a scalable sampling-based method.
- Provides theoretical consistency guarantees with the original multiclass ERM formulation.
- Introduces Aggressive Double Sampling controlled by two parameters: class-frequency-based sampling and uniform adversarial draws.
- Trains a dyadic classifier by repeating the sampling and re-sampling procedure.
- Uses a centroid-based heuristic at prediction time to identify candidate classes and limit pairwise evaluations.
- Prediction speed is slower than some competing methods.
- Theoretical analysis is complicated by dependence among dyadic examples induced by the reduction.
- Oversampling of rare classes shifts the empirical distribution and biases finite-sample estimates.
- Analysis uses graph-based splitting and Janson’s concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables.
- Finite-sample risk bounds are biased by ratios of true to oversampled class probabilities but bias shrinks linearly with sample size.
- Experiments on five text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 when class counts exceed ~30k.
- Method trains faster and uses far less memory than competing approaches.
- Particularly strong performance and practicality on the largest datasets.
- Paper is well-written with clear notation, figures, and thorough experiments.
- Main weaknesses: prediction-time cost, complex dependence in analysis, and biased finite-sample bounds.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically superior and resource-efficient.
- Arguments against acceptance: slower prediction, analytical complications, and finite-sample bias.
