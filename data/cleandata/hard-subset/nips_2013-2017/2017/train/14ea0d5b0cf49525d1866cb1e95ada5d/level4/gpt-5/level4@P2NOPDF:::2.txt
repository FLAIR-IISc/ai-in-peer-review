Summary:
This paper introduces a reduction from multi-class to binary classification tailored for settings with a very large number of classes. The approach learns a scoring function over (input, class) pairs: given a feature mapping φ that embeds each (x, c) into R^p, the model maps R^p to real-valued scores. The loss is defined as the fraction of incorrect classes that receive a higher score than the true class.

The method extends Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each example (x, y), generates K−1 transformed examples by comparing the true label y against each incorrect label y′. Because these transformed examples are correlated, standard generalization analyses are not directly applicable. The paper’s first contribution is an improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al., using new bounds based on fractional Rademacher complexity that account for this correlation. The second contribution shows how to use sampling within the reduction to both balance class frequencies and reduce the number of transformed examples. Specifically, the authors sample classes (with replacement) to equalize representation in the transformed set, and they replace the full set of K−1 pairwise comparisons with a random subset. They prove that these sampling procedures do not substantially degrade the generalization bounds. Empirical results demonstrate competitive or superior performance relative to strong baselines.

Comments:
- The paper offers a well-motivated reduction for extreme multi-class problems and provides appropriate generalization bounds for the correlated examples induced by the transformation. The key novelty over prior work is the sampling strategy that both rebalances class distributions and significantly shrinks the transformed dataset. The empirical evaluation is thorough and compares favorably to strong alternatives.
- A missing piece is a theoretical analysis of the prediction strategy in Section 2.3, where, at test time, comparisons are restricted to the k classes with centroids closest to the input. It would help to quantify how this candidate-pruning step affects consistency or risk, and under what conditions it preserves the guarantees established for the full comparison set.
- I would also welcome more discussion of the sample complexity bounds, including a comparison to known generalization guarantees for the original (non-reduced) multi-class setting. Such a comparison would clarify when the reduction (with sampling) yields tighter or more practical bounds than directly learning a multi-class model.
