This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification, a multiclass setting where the output space can contain as many as 100,000 classes.

Many existing approaches to extreme classification rely on either inferring a tree structure over the labels (e.g., hierarchical softmax) or learning a label embedding. The former reduces the problem to a logarithmic number of binary predictions, while the latter can be trained efficiently via least-squares or sampling. The authors correctly identify the weaknesses of these methods: an inferred latent tree may be suboptimal and lead to cascading errors, and the label embedding approach can produce prediction errors when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

As an alternative, this paper explores the reduction of extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. The authors propose a scalable, sampling-based method within this framework and provide theoretical guarantees of its consistency with the original multiclass Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) formulation.

Experiments conducted on five text datasets against several competing methods (including one-vs-all where computationally feasible) demonstrate the proposed method's superiority. On datasets with more than 30,000 classes, it generally achieves higher accuracy and F1-scores, trains faster, and uses significantly less memory. A noted trade-off is a somewhat slower prediction speed.

The proposed "Aggressive Double Sampling" reduction is governed by two parameters. The first controls the sampling frequency of each class, which is set inversely proportional to its empirical frequency to ensure rare classes in the long tail are sampled. The second parameter sets the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly. This procedure is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier.

At prediction time, inference is complicated by the intractability of generating pairwise comparisons with all possible classes. The authors employ a heuristic to identify a smaller set of candidate classes. This is achieved by computing a centroid for each class (the average of its feature vectors) and using these to find likely candidates. The final prediction is then made by performing pairwise classifications only within this candidate set.

The theoretical analysis is non-trivial for two main reasons. First, the reduction from multiclass to dyadic binary classification transforms a sum of independent random variables into a sum of dependent ones. Second, the oversampling of rare classes shifts the empirical distribution. The proof addresses this by splitting the sum of dependent examples into several sums of independent variables, based on the graph structure induced by the example construction, and then applying concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables (Janson's inequalities). Although the finite-sample risk bound contains a bias term related to the ratios of true to oversampled class probabilities, this bias is shown to decrease linearly with the size of the sampled training set. While I have not verified the proofs in detail, the analytical approach appears sound.

Overall, this paper introduces a strong method for extreme classification that exhibits superior performance on the largest datasets while maintaining low memory and computational requirements for training. The proposed binary reduction algorithm is theoretically sound, interesting, and should prove useful to practitioners.
