

This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification, a multiclass problem with output spaces exceeding 100,000 classes. Existing approaches typically rely on either a tree-structured hierarchy (e.g., hierarchical softmax) to limit predictions to a logarithmic number of binary decisions, or label embedding methods learned via least-squares or sampling. The authors highlight key limitations: tree-based methods may produce suboptimal structures leading to cascading errors, while label embeddings risk significant prediction errors when the true label matrix is not low-rank.  

The authors instead adopt a reduction to pairwise binary classification, proposing a scalable sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees of consistency with the original multiclass empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation. Experiments on five text datasets demonstrate that their method consistently outperforms competitors in accuracy and F1-score once the class count exceeds 30,000. It also achieves significantly faster training with substantially lower memory requirements, though prediction speed is slightly reduced.  

The Aggressive Double Sampling reduction employs two key parameters: (1) a class sampling frequency inversely proportional to empirical class frequency to prevent omission of rare classes, and (2) a count for uniformly drawn adversarial examples, used iteratively to train the final dyadic classifier. At prediction time, the intractability of pairwise feature generation across all classes is addressed via a heuristic: each class is represented by a centroid (average input vector of its examples), and predictions are made through pairwise classifications only with candidate classes identified by this centroid proximity.  

Theoretical analysis faces two challenges: (1) the reduction transforms a sum of independent random variables into a sum with specific dependence structures across dyadic examples, and (2) oversampling rare classes shifts the empirical distribution. The proof resolves these by partitioning the dependent sum into independent components using the graph structure induced by example construction, then applying Janson’s concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables. While the finite-sample risk bound exhibits bias proportional to ratios between true and oversampled class probabilities, this bias diminishes linearly with the size of the sampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, though the reviewer has not verified the proof in detail.  

Overall, this work delivers a robust solution for extreme classification, offering low memory and computational footprint alongside superior performance on large-scale datasets. The binary reduction algorithm is theoretically well-founded, conceptually novel, and practically valuable for practitioners.
