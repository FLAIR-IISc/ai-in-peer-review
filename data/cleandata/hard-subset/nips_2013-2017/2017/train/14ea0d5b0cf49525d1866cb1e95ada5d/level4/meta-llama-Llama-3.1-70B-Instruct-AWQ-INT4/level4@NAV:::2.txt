Summary:
This paper introduces a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, particularly well-suited for scenarios with a large number of classes. The authors consider a hypothesis that assigns scores to (input, class) pairs, with an underlying loss function that measures the fraction of incorrect classes with higher scores than the true class. Specifically, they employ a feature transformation phi that maps (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional feature space and learn a mapping from R^p to scores.

The proposed reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which generates K-1 transformed points for each data point (x, y), corresponding to the comparison of label y with each incorrect label y'. However, the correlated nature of the transformed dataset renders many standard generalization bounds inapplicable. The paper's first contribution is an improved generalization analysis, leveraging new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity in this setting. The second main contribution is the incorporation of sampling techniques during the reduction to balance class distributions and reduce the training set size. By sampling each class with replacement and using a random sample of classes for transformed points, the authors demonstrate that these procedures do not significantly impact generalization bounds. The new procedure is shown to be highly competitive with several strong baselines.

Comments:
This paper presents a compelling reduction from multi-class classification with numerous classes to binary classification, along with corresponding generalization bounds to address the correlated data in the reduced dataset. The primary novelty of the reduction lies in the sampling technique for rebalancing class distributions and reducing the transformed dataset size, whereas the basic reduction was introduced in an earlier paper by Joshi et al. The empirical comparison to existing algorithms is robust.

One notable omission is a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm outlined in section 2.3, where comparisons are limited to the k classes with centroids closest to a given input point.

Additional discussion on the sample complexity bounds, potentially comparing them to the generalization bounds obtained for non-transformed multi-class problems, would have been appreciated.
