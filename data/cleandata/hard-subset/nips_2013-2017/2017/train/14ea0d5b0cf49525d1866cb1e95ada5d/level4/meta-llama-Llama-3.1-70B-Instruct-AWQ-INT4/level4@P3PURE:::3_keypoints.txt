- Problem: extreme multiclass classification with up to ~100k labels requires reductions to avoid linear-time prediction.
- Prior tree-based approaches (e.g., hierarchical softmax) reduce predictions to logarithmic but can incur cascading errors from suboptimal trees.
- Label-embedding methods (least-squares or sampling) are efficient but fail when the true label matrix is not low-rank.
- Proposed approach: reduce extreme classification to pairwise (dyadic) binary classification using a scalable sampling-based reduction.
- The method comes with theoretical consistency guarantees relative to the original multiclass ERM.
- Empirical results on five text datasets: superior accuracy and F1 once number of classes exceeds ~30k.
- Training is faster and uses significantly less memory than competitors, though prediction is somewhat slower.
- Aggressive Double Sampling: two parameters — class sampling frequency (inverse to empirical frequency) to cover long-tail classes, and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples.
- Inference uses a heuristic centroid-based candidate selection per class to avoid generating pairwise features for all classes.
- Theoretical challenges: dyadic reduction induces dependencies among examples and oversampling shifts empirical distribution.
- Proof technique: split dependent sums via induced graph structure and apply Janson’s concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables.
- Finite-sample risk bound is biased by oversampling ratios, but the bias decreases linearly with sampled and re-sampled training sizes.
- Reviewer judgment: analysis appears sound; method is theoretically interesting, practically useful, and strong on very large datasets.
