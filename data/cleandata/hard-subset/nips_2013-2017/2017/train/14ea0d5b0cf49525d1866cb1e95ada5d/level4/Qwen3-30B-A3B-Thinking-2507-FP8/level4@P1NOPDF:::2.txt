

Summary:
This paper introduces a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, specifically designed for scenarios with a very large number of classes. The authors propose a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, with the underlying loss measuring the fraction of incorrect classes assigned higher scores than the true class. They assume a feature transformation φ mapping (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional space, followed by a learned mapping from ℝ^p to scores.

The reduction builds upon Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each data point (x, y), generates K-1 transformed points corresponding to comparisons between the true label y and each incorrect label y'. However, the resulting transformed dataset contains correlated examples, rendering many standard generalization bounds inapplicable. The paper’s first contribution is an improved generalization analysis using new bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity for this correlated setting. The second key contribution demonstrates that sampling techniques can rebalance class distributions and reduce the transformed training set size. Specifically, the authors sample each class (with replacement) to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed data and replace all K-1 possible class comparisons with a random sample of classes. Crucially, they prove these sampling procedures do not significantly compromise the generalization bounds. Empirical results show the proposed method achieves competitive performance against strong baselines.

Comments:
This paper presents a compelling reduction for large-scale multi-class classification, effectively addressing the challenge of correlated examples in the transformed space through a refined generalization analysis. The core novelty lies in the sampling strategy for class rebalancing and dataset size reduction, while the foundational reduction technique was previously introduced by Joshi et al. The empirical evaluation against established baselines is robust and demonstrates strong performance.

A significant gap is the lack of theoretical analysis for the prediction algorithm described in Section 2.3. This algorithm limits comparisons to the k classes whose centroids are closest to a given input point. The paper does not provide a theoretical justification for this choice or an analysis of its impact on error rates or generalization. A discussion of how this heuristic selection affects the overall guarantee would strengthen the method.

Additionally, the paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of the sample complexity bounds derived in Section 3. Specifically, comparing these bounds to the generalization guarantees achievable directly on the original multi-class problem (without transformation) would provide valuable context for the practical utility of the reduction. While the bounds are derived for the transformed space, understanding their relative tightness compared to non-transformed approaches would help readers assess the trade-offs involved. This comparison is particularly relevant given the paper’s focus on scalability for large K.
