- Problem: proposes a learning reduction for extreme multiclass classification (up to ~100k classes).
- Prior approaches: label trees (hierarchical softmax) and embedding methods; trees can be suboptimal and cause cascading errors, embeddings fail if label matrix isn’t low-rank.
- Main idea: reduce multiclass prediction to dyadic (pairwise) binary classification with a scalable, sampling-based training procedure.
- The method comes with theoretical guarantees of consistency with the original multiclass ERM objective.
- Aggressive Double Sampling (ADS): two parameters — class sampling frequency inversely proportional to empirical frequency, and number of uniformly sampled adversarial negatives.
- Repeating ADS yields the final dyadic classifier and prevents long-tail classes from being missed.
- Inference: exhaustive pairwise scoring is intractable, so use centroid-based candidate selection (class centroids in input space) before pairwise classification.
- Theoretical challenges: dyadic conversion creates dependencies among examples and oversampling shifts the empirical distribution.
- Proof strategy: build a dependency graph from dyadic samples, partition to regain independence, and apply Janson’s concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables.
- Finite-sample risk bound: includes bias terms depending on true vs. oversampled class probability ratios; biases shrink linearly with sampled/resampled training sizes.
- Experiments: on five text datasets vs competitive baselines (including one-vs-all when feasible), shows higher accuracy and F1 once classes exceed ~30k, faster training, much lower memory, but some prediction-time slowdown.
- Overall assessment: strong, well-motivated, theoretically grounded contribution offering favorable memory/training tradeoffs and superior accuracy on the largest problems.
