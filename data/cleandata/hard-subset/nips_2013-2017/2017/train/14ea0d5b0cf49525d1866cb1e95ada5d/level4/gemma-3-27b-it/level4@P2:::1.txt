This paper addresses the challenge of multi-class classification with a very large number of classes, building upon the multi-class to binary reduction strategy introduced in [16]. The authors propose a double sampling technique designed to mitigate the curse of long-tailed class distributions and reduce computational cost. They support their approach with both theoretical analysis, providing generalization bounds, and empirical evaluation on large-scale text datasets.

The paper begins by contextualizing the problem of extreme classification, highlighting its importance in applications like text tagging, recommendation, and ranking. It correctly identifies the limitations of the one-vs-all approach, particularly its computational intractability and susceptibility to class imbalance. The authors then position their work within the existing literature, contrasting it with tree-based, embedding-based, and other binary reduction methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors present a thorough empirical comparison against a range of established baselines, including OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse. The results demonstrate competitive performance in terms of both accuracy and macro F1-score, alongside significant reductions in training time and memory consumption.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Extreme multi-class classification is a crucial area of research with wide-ranging real-world applications. Addressing the computational challenges associated with this problem is highly valuable.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis, deriving new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexities. This adds a level of rigor and insight into the behavior of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Novelty:** While the double sampling strategy and the derived generalization bounds represent contributions, the paper builds heavily on prior work [16]. The novelty feels somewhat incremental, and a stronger justification of the specific benefits of this approach *over* existing methods could be provided.
*   **Prediction Time Concern:** The authors acknowledge that prediction time remains a concern. In many practical applications, prediction speed is paramount. While the proposed method improves training time, its comparatively slower prediction speed relative to other approaches is a potential drawback that warrants further discussion.
*   **Generalization Bound Interpretation:** The practical implications of the derived generalization bound are not clearly articulated. A more intuitive explanation of what the bound tells us about the expected performance of the algorithm, and how it compares to existing bounds, would be beneficial.
*   **Heterogeneity in Baselines:** The comparison includes a mix of batch and online learning algorithms. Directly comparing memory usage across these paradigms can be misleading. While acknowledged, this difference deserves more explicit discussion when interpreting the results.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in machine learning.
*   The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed approach achieves competitive performance on large-scale datasets.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a valuable contribution to the understanding of the algorithm's behavior.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is relatively limited.
*   The prediction time remains a potential concern for practical applications.
*   The practical interpretation of the generalization bound is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. The proposed double sampling strategy appears to be a practical and effective way to improve the efficiency of existing binary reduction methods. However, the incremental nature of the novelty and the concern about prediction time prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. I believe the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between training time, prediction time, and accuracy, as well as a clearer explanation of the implications of the theoretical results.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. I would be willing to support acceptance if the authors address the concerns raised above. Specifically, clarifying the practical value of the generalization bound and providing a more in-depth discussion of the prediction time performance would strengthen the paper significantly.
