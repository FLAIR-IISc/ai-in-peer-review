- Addresses extreme multi-class classification using a dyadic pair reduction with a novel (π, κ)-double sampling strategy to make it scalable.  
- (π) step balances class distribution by over-sampling rare classes and under-sampling frequent ones; (κ) step samples few negative classes per example to reduce computation.  
- Provides theoretical justification via new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexities, showing consistency despite sampling-induced distribution shift and dependencies.  
- Empirical evaluation on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia, up to 100k classes) shows large training time and memory gains while maintaining or improving accuracy versus strong baselines.  
- Strength: Tackles a significant, practical problem with a method balancing computational efficiency and predictive performance.  
- Strength: Comprehensive and convincing experiments comparing against diverse baselines, demonstrating clear scalability benefits on the largest datasets.  
- Strength: Theoretical analysis (Theorems 1–2) gives non-trivial formal guarantees for the aggressive sampling scheme.  
- Weakness: Incremental contribution over prior dyadic-reduction work [16]; main novelty is an engineering-oriented two-step sampling rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic framework.  
- Weakness: Prediction latency remains slower than tree-based methods (FastXML, RecallTree); candidate selection mitigates but the q vs. accuracy trade-off is under-explored.  
- Comment: The interpretation and practical implications of the generalization bounds need deeper discussion, e.g., comparisons to existing bounds and guidance for choosing M and κ.  
- Comment: Memory comparisons mix batch and online/streaming methods with different paradigms; acknowledging this distinction would make the evaluation fairer.
