- Problem setting: extreme multiclass classification with up to 100,000 classes; existing tree-based and label-embedding methods suffer from cascading errors or fail when label matrices are not low-rank.  
- Proposed approach: reduce multiclass to pairwise binary classification using a scalable sampling-based reduction with consistency guarantees to the original ERM.  
- Empirical results: on five text datasets the method outperforms competitors in accuracy and F1 once class count >30,000, trains faster, and uses much less memory (prediction slightly slower).  
- Aggressive Double Sampling: two parameters—class sampling inversely proportional to observed frequency to retain rare classes, and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples—used to generate dyadic training examples iteratively.  
- Prediction-time heuristic: avoid intractable pairwise generation by selecting candidate classes via centroid (class-average) representations, then apply pairwise classifier on candidates.  
- Theoretical challenges and solution: dyadic reduction induces dependencies and distribution shift from oversampling; authors partition dependent sums using the induced graph and apply Janson’s concentration inequalities.  
- Risk bound and bias: finite-sample risk bound exhibits bias proportional to true/oversampled class-probability ratio, but this bias decreases linearly with sampled and re-sampled training sizes.  
- Overall verdict: method is novel, theoretically grounded, memory- and compute-efficient, and practically effective on very large extreme-class problems.
