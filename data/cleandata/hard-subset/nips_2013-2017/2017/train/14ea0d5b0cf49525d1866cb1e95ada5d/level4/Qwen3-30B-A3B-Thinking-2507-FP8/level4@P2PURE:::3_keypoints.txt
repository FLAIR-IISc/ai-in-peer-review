- Introduces a learning reduction for extreme classification (100k+ classes) as an alternative to hierarchical softmax and label embedding.  
- Identifies limitations of existing methods: hierarchical trees can be suboptimal and cascade errors; label embedding fails when label matrix isn’t low-rank.  
- Proposes reducing extreme classification to pairwise binary classification with a scalable, sampling-based algorithm and consistency guarantees to the original multiclass ERM.  
- Empirical results on five text datasets show better accuracy and F1 than competitors (including feasible one-vs-all) once class count exceeds ~30,000.  
- Method trains faster and uses much less memory than baselines, though prediction is slightly slower.  
- Introduces Aggressive Double Sampling with two parameters: class sampling inversely proportional to empirical frequency, and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples.  
- Trains the dyadic classifier via repeated sampling iterations to handle class imbalance and tail classes.  
- Uses a centroid-based heuristic at prediction time to limit pairwise comparisons to candidate classes, avoiding full pairwise generation.  
- Theoretical analysis handles dependent dyadic sums and oversampling shifts by decomposing dependencies and applying Janson’s concentration inequalities.  
- Finite-sample risk bound is biased by ratios of true vs. oversampled class probabilities, but bias decreases linearly with sample sizes; reviewer did not fully verify detailed proofs.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically sound, novel reduction that is practically valuable with minimal memory/computational demands and strong performance on large datasets.
