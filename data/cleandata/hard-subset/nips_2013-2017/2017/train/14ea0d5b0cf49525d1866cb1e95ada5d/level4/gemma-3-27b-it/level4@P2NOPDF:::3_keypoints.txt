- Tackles extreme multiclass classification with up to 100,000 classes, a setting where trees and low-rank embeddings have limitations.  
- Critiques hierarchical/tree methods for error propagation and low-rank embedding methods for poor performance when label matrix is not low-rank.  
- Proposes reducing multiclass to a sequence of pairwise binary (dyadic) classification problems.  
- Introduces "Aggressive Double Sampling": sample classes inversely proportional to empirical frequency and sample adversarial examples uniformly.  
- Two sampling parameters control class-frequency correction (mitigate long tail) and number of adversarial samples per class.  
- Trains a final dyadic classifier iteratively on the sampled pairwise examples.  
- Provides theoretical consistency guarantees to the ERM objective despite complex dependence and oversampling.  
- Develops a nontrivial risk analysis by decomposing dependent examples via the induced graph and using concentration bounds for partially dependent variables (Janson).  
- Finite-sample risk bounds depend on the ratio of true vs. oversampled class probabilities, but biases shrink with larger primary and re-sample sizes.  
- Empirical results on five text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 when classes >30,000, and competitive performance otherwise.  
- Method yields faster training and much lower memory use; prediction is slower due to pairwise comparisons.  
- Addresses intractable prediction by using class centroids to select candidate classes for pairwise evaluation.
