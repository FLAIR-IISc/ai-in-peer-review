Summary
The paper proposes a double sampling strategy to enhance the multiclass classification method introduced in [16]. It provides both theoretical results—via generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexities—and empirical evaluations on large-scale text datasets. The experiments indicate that the approach scales to problems with a very large number of classes and can reduce training time compared to several baselines.

Strengths
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation: The authors compare against multiple strong baselines and demonstrate competitive or superior accuracy on several datasets.
- Training efficiency: The proposed method significantly reduces training time relative to some alternatives while maintaining high predictive performance.
- Practical relevance: Extreme multiclass classification is an important problem with many real-world applications, and methods that scale to large label spaces are valuable.

Weaknesses and Concerns
- Limited novelty over [16]: While the double sampling strategy and the local Rademacher complexity (LRC) bounds are meaningful additions, the overall contribution feels incremental relative to [16]. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what new capabilities or guarantees are enabled by double sampling that were previously infeasible.
- Prediction-time cost: Although training is faster, inference appears comparatively slow against some baselines. In many applications, test-time latency dominates end-to-end cost. The paper should quantify and emphasize this trade-off more explicitly, ideally with both asymptotic analysis and wall-clock measurements for top-1/top-k prediction.
- Interpretation of the generalization bound: The bound’s implications are unclear. It is difficult to assess how tight or informative it is, how it depends on the number of classes, sample size, and problem parameters, and how it compares to existing bounds for related methods. Without such context, the theoretical contribution is hard to interpret.
- Heterogeneous baselines and resource reporting: Some baselines are batch learners and others are online, leading to substantial differences in memory usage and training dynamics. The current presentation makes it hard to separate algorithmic merit from computational budget. A more standardized reporting of peak memory, training epochs/passes, and hardware would improve fairness and clarity.

Questions for the Authors
- Ablation and sensitivity: How much improvement comes from double sampling alone, independent of other changes from [16]? Please include ablations contrasting (i) [16], (ii) [16] + double sampling, and (iii) your full method. Provide sensitivity analyses for the sampling rates and any associated hyperparameters.
- Bound comparison: How does your LRC-based bound compare (both qualitatively and quantitatively) to prior bounds for [16] and to common alternatives (e.g., global Rademacher or margin-based bounds)? What are the key assumptions, and how do the leading constants and dependence on the number of classes scale?
- Practical meaning of the bound: Can you provide empirical evidence that the bound tracks generalization behavior (e.g., plots of bound value vs. test error across training, datasets, or sampling settings)?
- Inference complexity: What is the test-time complexity as a function of the number of classes? Are there mechanisms (candidate generation, hierarchical structures, ANN-based retrieval, or other pruning) that can reduce prediction time without large accuracy loss?
- Baseline fairness and tuning: Were all baselines tuned with equal budget and early-stopping criteria? For online methods, how many passes over the data were used, and how was model selection performed?
- Data and task specification: Are all datasets strictly multiclass (single label per example), or do any have multi-label characteristics? Please clarify metrics (top-1 vs. top-k) and ensure they are appropriate for the task.
- Class imbalance: How does the method perform under heavy class imbalance, which is common in extreme classification? Any robustness analyses?

Suggestions for Improvement
- Clarify contributions: Explicitly position the work relative to [16] and highlight where double sampling changes the computational and statistical properties. A concise schematic or pseudo-code detailing the two sampling steps and their roles would help.
- Strengthen theory: Provide a clear, interpretable statement of the main bound, with its dependencies on number of classes, sample size, norms, and margins. Add a brief comparison to prior results and, if possible, a discussion of tightness.
- Report comprehensive efficiency metrics: Alongside training time, report test-time latency, throughput, and peak memory for both training and inference. Consider presenting accuracy–latency trade-off curves.
- Standardize comparisons: Where feasible, align baselines by (i) training data passes, (ii) hyperparameter budgets, and (iii) hardware; or clearly separate batch vs. online comparisons in the discussion.
- Expand empirical analysis: Include ablations isolating the effect of double sampling and hyperparameter sensitivity. If prediction is slow, explore approximate inference (e.g., candidate pruning or hierarchical routing) and quantify the accuracy–speed trade-off.
- Improve presentation: Fix grammatical issues (e.g., “has” instead of “have,” “interpret” instead of “interoperate,” “generalization,” “reduces” instead of “shortens”). Define notation early, and ensure all algorithmic components are described unambiguously.

Overall Assessment
The paper addresses a significant and timely problem and provides a method that empirically scales with improved training efficiency. However, the novelty relative to [16] appears modest, and the practical utility is tempered by comparatively high prediction time and an under-explained theoretical contribution. With clearer positioning, stronger analysis of inference-time costs, standardized experimental comparisons, and a more interpretable treatment of the generalization bound, the submission would be considerably strengthened.
