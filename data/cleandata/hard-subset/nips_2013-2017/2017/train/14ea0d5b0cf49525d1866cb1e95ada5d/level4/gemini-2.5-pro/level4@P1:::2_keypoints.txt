- Tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing to binary: learn a scoring function f(x,y) with a ranking-style loss to prefer true classes.
- Builds on Joshi et al. (2015) that transforms each example into K-1 binary comparisons between the true class and each incorrect class.
- Provides improved generalization analysis for reductions that produce correlated examples using local fractional Rademacher complexities.
- Introduces an "aggressive double sampling" strategy: re-sample to balance long-tailed class distributions and sample a small random subset of negative classes per example.
- Derives generalization bounds that incorporate the double sampling scheme, showing consistency of the approach.
- Empirical evaluation on large-scale text datasets shows the method is competitive or superior in accuracy, training time, and memory usage.
- Strength: double sampling effectively addresses both long-tailed distributions and the computational cost of pairwise comparisons.
- Strength: demonstrates a strong trade-off between predictive performance, computational speed, and memory efficiency versus strong baselines.
- Strength: provides solid theoretical grounding with novel bounds that account for data dependencies and sampling-induced distributional shift.
- Weakness: lacks theoretical analysis of the candidate selection heuristic at prediction time (nearest-centroid subset) and its effect on error.
- Weakness: discussion of sample complexity bounds is limited; a comparison to standard multi-class bounds would clarify the cost of the reduction and sampling.
- Overall: well-executed paper with strong theory and empirical results; weaknesses are minor and point to future work, and the method has clear practical value.
