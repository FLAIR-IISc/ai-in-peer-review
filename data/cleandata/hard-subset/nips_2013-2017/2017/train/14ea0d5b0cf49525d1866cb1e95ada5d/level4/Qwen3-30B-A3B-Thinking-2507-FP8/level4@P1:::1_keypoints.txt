- Proposes a double sampling strategy for extreme multi-class classification built on the multi-class-to-binary reduction of [16].  
- Method combines (1) class-balanced subsampling (oversample small, subsample large classes) and (2) sampling only κ adversarial classes per example instead of K−1.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees via new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity, showing consistency preservation for ERM.  
- Empirically evaluated on DMOZ and Wikipedia with up to 100,000 classes.  
- Reports improvements in training time, memory consumption, and predictive performance compared to state-of-the-art baselines.  
- Prediction time is reduced relative to some baselines but remains high compared to tree-based methods.  
- Theoretical analysis extends prior work meaningfully but would benefit from clearer comparisons to existing bounds.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, uses multiple strong baselines, metrics (accuracy and macro F1), and careful hyperparameter tuning.  
- Authors are transparent about limitations, particularly around prediction time.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent and well-explained mathematical notation.  
- Reproducibility is reasonable for experts, though main-text proofs lack more intuitive explanations.  
- Some technical terms could be better motivated for readers unfamiliar with multi-class-to-binary reductions.  
- Originality is limited because the approach builds substantially on [16], making the novelty incremental.  
- Significance is moderate: practical gains in training/memory are valuable, but predictive improvements are not substantial.  
- Prediction time disadvantage versus tree-based methods limits practical impact for large-scale applications.  
- Experimental comparisons inconsistently mix batch and online algorithms, affecting memory usage fairness.  
- Strengths: thorough experiments, practical training/memory gains, a valid theoretical contribution, and attention to long-tailed distributions.  
- Weaknesses: thin novelty, persistent prediction-time disadvantage, unclear theoretical comparisons to prior work, insufficient case for preferring over tree-based methods, and inconsistent experimental baselines.  
- Overall assessment: technically solid and relevant, but novelty and practical significance are insufficient for NIPS acceptance without stronger performance or clearer advantages over existing approaches.
