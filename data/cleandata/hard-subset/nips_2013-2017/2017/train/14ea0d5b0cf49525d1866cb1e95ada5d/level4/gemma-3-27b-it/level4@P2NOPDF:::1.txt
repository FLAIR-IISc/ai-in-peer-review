This paper introduces a double sampling strategy designed to enhance multiclass classification, building upon the work in [16]. The authors support their approach with both theoretical analysis, including novel generalization bounds, and empirical evaluation. Experiments conducted on text datasets demonstrate the method's effectiveness in handling multiclass classification problems with a large number of classes.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper features a comprehensive experimental evaluation, comparing the proposed method against several strong baselines. The results consistently demonstrate competitive performance and, notably, reduced training time across multiple datasets.
*   Extreme classification is a valuable and increasingly relevant research area with numerous practical applications. Addressing challenges in this domain is an important contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The work is heavily reliant on the foundation laid by [16]. While the authors introduce two key improvements – a double sampling strategy and new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher Complexities – the overall novelty feels incremental.
*   Although the proposed approach reduces training time, the prediction time remains comparatively long when benchmarked against other methods. In many real-world applications, prediction speed is a critical factor, potentially limiting the practical impact of this work.

**Questions/Comments:**

*   The interpretation of the presented generalization bound is unclear. It would be beneficial to include a more detailed explanation of its practical implications and a comparative analysis against existing generalization bounds for extreme classification. How does this bound relate to, and potentially improve upon, existing theoretical guarantees?
*   The experimental comparison includes both batch learning and online learning algorithms. This introduces a potential confound, as memory usage characteristics differ significantly between these approaches. A fairer comparison might involve focusing on algorithms with similar memory requirements, or explicitly acknowledging and discussing the implications of these differences in the results.
