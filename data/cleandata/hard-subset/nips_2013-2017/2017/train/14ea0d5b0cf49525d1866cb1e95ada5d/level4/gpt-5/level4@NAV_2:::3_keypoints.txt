- Proposes reducing extreme multiclass (up to 100k labels) to binary prediction over dyadic pairs.  
- Points out hierarchical methods risk compounding errors and embedding methods suffer when label matrices are not low-rank.  
- Introduces a scalable, sampling-based dyadic reduction with consistency guarantees relative to the multiclass ERM objective.  
- Experiments on five text datasets: beyond ~30k labels the method typically improves accuracy and macro-F1, trains faster, and uses much less memory, though prediction can be slower.  
- Aggressive Double Sampling uses class-wise sampling probabilities inverse to empirical frequencies and a parameter for the number of uniformly drawn adversarial classes per example to generate dyads.  
- Inference employs a heuristic candidate selection: represent each class by its input-space centroid and score only nearest-centroid candidate labels.  
- Theoretical analysis addresses dependencies from dyadic construction and oversampling by partitioning dependent sums and applying large-deviation tools (à la Janson), yielding finite-sample risk bounds with mismatch factors that shrink with sample sizes.  
- Reviewer considers the proofs plausible and the overall approach appealing, with strong empirical performance and practical theoretical support.
