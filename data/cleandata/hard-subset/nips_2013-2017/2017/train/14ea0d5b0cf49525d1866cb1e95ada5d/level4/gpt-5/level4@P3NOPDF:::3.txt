This paper proposes a learning reduction for extreme multiclass classification (up to ~100k labels) by converting the problem into dyadic binary classification. The approach, called Aggressive Double Sampling (ADS), is a scalable, sampling-based training scheme with theoretical guarantees showing consistency with the original multiclass ERM objective. The authors also address the practical challenges of inference via a candidate-generation heuristic based on class centroids. Empirical results on five text datasets indicate strong performance for very large label spaces: higher accuracy and F1 once the number of classes exceeds roughly 30k, faster training, and substantially lower memory usage than competing methods, albeit with slower prediction.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of a reduction from extreme classification to pairwise binary learning, with a principled sampling strategy that explicitly addresses the long-tail label distribution.
- Theoretical analysis that tackles two key complications: dependence among dyadic examples induced by the reduction and the distribution shift caused by oversampling rare classes. The use of graph-structured decompositions and Janson’s concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables is technically sound and well-motivated.
- Finite-sample risk bounds that characterize the effect of oversampling; although biased by the true-to-oversampled class probability ratios, the bias diminishes linearly with training set size. The consistency result with respect to multiclass ERM is a valuable guarantee.
- Strong empirical performance on large-scale text benchmarks. Above ~30k classes, the method typically outperforms tree-based and embedding-based alternatives in accuracy and F1, while training more quickly and using far less memory. This makes the approach attractive for practitioners with constrained resources.

Method and theory, in brief
- ADS uses two sampling controls: (1) per-class sampling frequency inversely proportional to empirical class frequency to ensure coverage of rare labels, and (2) a fixed number of “adversarial” negative examples drawn uniformly. Iterating this process yields a dyadic training set for a binary classifier.
- At inference time, enumerating all class–instance pairs is intractable. The authors form a centroid (mean feature vector) for each class to propose a candidate set of labels per instance, and perform pairwise comparisons only within this candidate pool.
- The finite-sample analysis handles dependence among dyadic examples by partitioning the dependency graph to obtain sums of independent terms amenable to concentration. Oversampling-induced bias enters the bound via class probability ratios, which shrink with increased sample sizes.

Weaknesses and questions
- The candidate-generation step at test time is heuristic. How sensitive are accuracy and runtime to the choice of centroid representation and candidate pool size? For multi-modal classes, a single centroid may be insufficient and could miss relevant labels.
- Prediction is slower than competing methods. Can the candidate selection be accelerated (e.g., via approximate nearest neighbors or multi-centroid representations) without degrading accuracy? Reporting end-to-end latency distributions would clarify practical trade-offs.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity is not fully explored. The two ADS parameters (inverse-frequency sampling schedule and the number of uniformly drawn negatives) likely affect both bias and variance. Please provide guidance on tuning, ablations removing each component, and robustness across datasets.
- Theoretical bounds are informative but could benefit from more interpretable corollaries. Can the authors instantiate the bounds under simple class-frequency models to illustrate scaling with label-space size and tail heaviness? Clarifying constants and typical magnitudes would help practitioners.
- Training complexity and memory footprint are key selling points; a clearer accounting of dyadic example counts, feature-construction cost, and per-epoch runtime would strengthen the case. How does the cost scale with the number of sampled negatives and with label cardinality?
- It would be helpful to detail how many adversarial negatives are sampled per instance versus globally, and whether sampling is with or without replacement.
- The empirical section focuses on large-K regimes where the method shines; including results for smaller label spaces would contextualize the crossover point more precisely and help readers decide when to deploy ADS. Similarly, a breakdown of micro vs. macro metrics would illuminate behavior on the long tail.
- The approach appears tailored to linear or sparse text features. How well would the reduction compose with deep feature extractors, and would that exacerbate prediction-time costs?

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally clear, but the algorithmic description of ADS and the test-time pipeline would benefit from concise pseudocode and a complexity summary.
- The discussion of the dependency graph and how it is partitioned for the concentration arguments is dense; a figure illustrating the construction and the role of Janson’s inequalities would improve readability.
- Please state precisely how candidate pool sizes are chosen and whether there is a principled trade-off curve between candidate count, accuracy, and speed.

Overall assessment
This is a strong paper that advances the state of the art in extreme classification through a well-motivated learning reduction with both practical benefits (training speed and memory) and solid theory. The empirical evidence is compelling on very large label spaces, and the analysis addresses nontrivial dependencies introduced by the reduction. The primary concerns center on prediction-time efficiency, reliance on a heuristic candidate generator, and limited guidance on hyperparameter tuning. Addressing these points—especially through ablations, clearer complexity accounting, and sensitivity analyses—would further strengthen the work. Subject to those clarifications, I view this as a valuable and impactful contribution for large-scale classification.
