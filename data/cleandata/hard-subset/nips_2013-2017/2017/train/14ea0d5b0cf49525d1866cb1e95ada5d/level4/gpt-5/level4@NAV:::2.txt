Review- Summary:
This paper introduces a new reduction from multi-class to binary classification tailored for scenarios with a very large number of classes. The approach uses a hypothesis that assigns scores to (input, class) pairs, with a loss that measures the fraction of incorrect classes that receive a higher score than the true class. Concretely, they assume a feature map phi that embeds (input, class) pairs into a p-dimensional space, and they learn a scoring function from R^p to the reals.
The reduction builds on Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each example (x, y), generates K−1 transformed examples, each comparing y against some incorrect label y'. Because the transformed dataset induces correlations among training points, many standard generalization bounds are not directly applicable. The first contribution is a tighter generalization analysis relative to Joshi et al. (2015), obtained via new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity for this setting. The second contribution shows that sampling can be used during the reduction both to rebalance class frequencies and to shrink the transformed dataset. Specifically, they sample each class (with replacement) to achieve roughly uniform representation and, rather than forming all K−1 pairwise comparisons, they draw a random subset of classes. They prove that these two sampling steps do not significantly degrade their generalization guarantees. Finally, they demonstrate highly competitive performance compared to strong baselines.

Comments:
The paper offers an appealing reduction from large-class multi-class classification to binary classification, together with generalization bounds that account for correlations in the reduced dataset. The chief novelty lies in the sampling strategy that both rebalances class distributions and reduces the size of the transformed data; the core reduction itself follows prior work by Joshi et al. The empirical comparisons against existing methods are very strong.
One notable omission is a theoretical treatment of the prediction procedure in Section 2.3, where comparisons are restricted to the k classes whose centroids are closest to the input.
I would also have liked more discussion of the sample complexity bounds, ideally including a comparison to generalization guarantees for the original (non-reduced) multi-class formulation.
