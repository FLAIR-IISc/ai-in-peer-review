- Introduces a multi-class-to-binary reduction for extremely large-scale classification using dyadic examples and a double sampling strategy.  
- Extends Joshi et al. (2015) by creating (x,y) vs. (x,y') pairs and aggressively oversampling small classes while subsampling large ones.  
- Provides refined theoretical analysis using local fractional Rademacher complexity to derive generalization bounds for correlated transformed examples.  
- Proposes an aggressive double sampling technique that reduces transformed dataset size and addresses long-tailed class distributions.  
- Demonstrates substantial computational and predictive improvements on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets with up to 100k classes.  
- Theorems 1 and 2 supply technically sound generalization guarantees for the proposed sampling strategy.  
- Carefully addresses class imbalance and correlation in transformed data, giving a complete theoretical foundation for the method.  
- Experiments are comprehensive: multiple large-scale datasets, up to 100k classes, and comparisons to six strong baselines.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, with precise mathematical formulations and helpful pseudocode for reproducibility.  
- The method is original in its double sampling approach and novel use of local fractional Rademacher complexity in this context.  
- Significant practical impact: reduces training/prediction time and memory, making large-scale classification feasible for real-world tasks.  
- Lacks theoretical analysis of the candidate-selection prediction algorithm (k nearest-centroid) used at inference.  
- Sample complexity bounds discussion could be strengthened by comparing to non-transformed multi-class generalization bounds.  
- Missing insight into how sampling parameters (π, κ) trade off computational efficiency versus predictive accuracy.
