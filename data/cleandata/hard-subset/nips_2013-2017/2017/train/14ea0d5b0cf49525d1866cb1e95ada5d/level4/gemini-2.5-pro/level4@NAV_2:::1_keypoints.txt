- Addresses extreme multi-class classification via a multi-class-to-binary reduction over example pairs.
- Proposes a "doubly-sampled" method: (1) sample training data to balance long-tail class distribution, (2) sample negative classes per example to reduce pair count.
- Provides theoretical analysis using local Rademacher complexities and new generalization bounds showing consistency under sampling.
- Empirically validated on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia) with up to 100K classes.
- Strong empirical results: competitive or superior Accuracy and Macro F1 vs many state-of-the-art baselines (Table 3).
- Demonstrates substantial reductions in training time and memory compared to high-performing baselines (OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse).
- Offers a practical trade-off between accuracy, training time, and memory suitable for real-world applications.
- Theoretical grounding (Theorems 1 & 2) adds rigor to the sampling strategy.
- Limited conceptual novelty: mainly an extension of Joshi et al. [16] with engineering and theoretical refinements.
- Prediction/inference time remains a bottleneck; slower than tree-based methods like FastXML and PfastReXML.
- Request for clearer discussion on practical implications of Theorem 2 and how bounds compare qualitatively to [16].
- Suggests clarifying memory comparisons by acknowledging differences between batch learning baselines and online/streaming methods.
