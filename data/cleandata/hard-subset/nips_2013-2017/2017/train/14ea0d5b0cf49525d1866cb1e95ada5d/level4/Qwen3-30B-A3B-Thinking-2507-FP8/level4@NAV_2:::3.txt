

This paper addresses extreme multi-class classification with up to 100,000 classes, proposing a novel double sampling strategy to overcome long-tailed class distributions in multi-class-to-binary reduction. The authors extend the dyadic binary reduction framework [1, 16, 28] by introducing aggressive double sampling: oversampling small classes (with probability πₖ ∝ 1/μₖ) and subsampling large classes, while limiting adversarial examples per instance via parameter κ. This contrasts with prior tree-based methods [4, 9, 21] that suffer from error propagation and label embedding approaches [5, 11, 19] that fail under non-low-rank label matrices. The theoretical contribution establishes generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity (Theorem 2), proving consistency despite distribution shifts from sampling and inter-dependency in dyadic examples. Experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia collections (10k–100k classes) demonstrate significant improvements in training time, memory efficiency, and predictive performance (accuracy, macro F1) over state-of-the-art methods (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PD-Sparse), particularly for large-scale settings (≥50k classes). Prediction uses class centroids for candidate selection, avoiding full pairwise computation.

Strengths:  
- **Quality**: Theoretical analysis rigorously addresses dependence and distribution shifts via Janson's concentration inequalities and fractional Rademacher complexity. Experiments comprehensively validate scalability (memory, time) and accuracy on realistic large-scale text datasets.  
- **Originality**: The double sampling strategy (π, κ)-DS is a novel, principled approach to mitigate class imbalance in dyadic reduction, distinct from prior tree/embedding methods.  
- **Significance**: Solves a critical bottleneck in extreme classification (memory/time explosion with K classes), with practical impact for text tagging/recommendation systems.  
- **Clarity**: Well-structured, with clear pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and thorough experimental setup (datasets, baselines, metrics).  

Weaknesses:  
- Prediction time remains higher than tree-based methods (e.g., RecallTree), though the paper notes this tradeoff is offset by superior scalability for K > 50k.  
- The candidate selection heuristic (class centroids) lacks ablation studies on centroid quality vs. alternative methods.  

Pro/con acceptance arguments:  
**Pro**: Demonstrates consistent superiority in memory/time for K ≥ 50k classes, provides theoretically sound reduction with provable consistency, and addresses a well-known scalability bottleneck in extreme classification.  
**Con**: Prediction time is not the fastest (though competitive with non-tree methods), and the centroid-based candidate selection could be further optimized.  

The paper makes a strong contribution to NIPS, advancing extreme classification with a scalable, theoretically grounded method that outperforms state-of-the-art approaches on large-scale benchmarks.
