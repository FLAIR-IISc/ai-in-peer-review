- Introduces a novel learning reduction for extreme classification (up to ~100k classes).  
- Identifies limitations of prior approaches: tree-based methods risk error propagation; label embeddings fail when label matrix is not low-rank.  
- Proposes reduction to pairwise binary classification with a scalable sampling-based method (Aggressive Double Sampling, ADS) and theoretical consistency guarantees.  
- Empirical results on five text datasets: ADS outperforms competitors in accuracy and F1 when classes exceed ~30k, with large gains in training speed and memory; prediction is slightly slower.  
- ADS uses two sampling parameters: class sampling frequency inversely proportional to empirical frequency to preserve rare classes, and a uniform draw of adversarial examples; sampling is iterated to train a dyadic classifier.  
- Prediction uses a centroid-based heuristic to shortlist candidate classes, then applies pairwise classification to avoid computing all pairwise features.  
- Theoretical analysis addresses dependencies from dyadic examples and bias from oversampling by decomposing the dependent sum via the sampling graph and applying Janson’s concentration inequalities; finite-sample risk bound includes a bias that decreases linearly with training size.  
- Reviewer judgment: analysis appears sound (not fully verified); overall contribution is well-motivated, technically solid, conceptually clear, practically useful, and experimentally thorough.
