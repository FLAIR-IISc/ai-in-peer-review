This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification, a multiclass classification setting with an extremely large number of classes, potentially in the hundreds of thousands.

The authors situate their contribution by first discussing the limitations of common approaches. Many existing methods rely on either inferring a tree structure over the labels (e.g., hierarchical softmax) to reduce prediction to a logarithmic number of binary decisions, or using a label embedding approach where labels are projected into a low-dimensional space. The paper correctly identifies the weaknesses of these strategies: an inferred latent tree may be suboptimal and lead to cascading errors, while label embedding methods can perform poorly if the true label matrix is not well-approximated by a low-rank matrix.

In contrast to these methods, this paper explores an alternative paradigm: reducing extreme classification to a series of pairwise binary classification problems. The authors propose a scalable, sampling-based method within this framework and provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating its consistency with the original multiclass Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) objective.

The empirical evaluation is conducted on five text classification datasets, where the proposed method is compared against several competing approaches, including standard one-vs-all (OVA) where computationally feasible. The results demonstrate that the proposed method is generally superior in terms of accuracy and F1-score, particularly on datasets with more than 30,000 classes. Furthermore, it exhibits significantly faster training times and lower memory consumption. A noted trade-off is a slight decrease in prediction speed compared to some baselines.

The core of the proposed method is a reduction technique named 'Aggressive Double Sampling'. This process is governed by two key parameters. The first controls the sampling frequency for each class, employing an inverse-proportionality rule with respect to the empirical class frequency. This strategy is crucial for ensuring that rare classes from the long tail are adequately represented during training. The second parameter determines the number of 'adversarial' negative examples to be drawn uniformly. This sampling procedure is repeated iteratively to train the final dyadic (pairwise) classifier.

Prediction at inference time presents a significant challenge, as evaluating pairwise classifiers against all possible classes is computationally intractable. To address this, the authors employ a heuristic to prune the search space. They first identify a small set of candidate classes by computing a centroid representation for each class (as the mean of its feature vectors) and finding the nearest neighbors to the test instance. Prediction is then performed by running the pairwise classifiers only on this reduced set of candidates.

The theoretical analysis of the reduction is non-trivial. The primary difficulty stems from the conversion of a multiclass problem into a dyadic one, which transforms a sum of independent random variables (in the original ERM objective) into a sum over dependent variables. A further complication arises from the oversampling of rare classes, which intentionally shifts the training distribution away from the true empirical distribution. To overcome these challenges, the authors' proof strategy involves decomposing the sum of dependent variables into several sums of independent variables, leveraging the graph structure induced by the sampling process. They then apply concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables, specifically those introduced by Janson. While the resulting finite-sample risk bound contains a bias term related to the ratio between the true and sampling probabilities, the authors show that this bias diminishes linearly as the size of the sampled training set increases. The analysis appears sound, although a detailed verification of the proofs was not conducted for this review.

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of extreme classification. The proposed method is well-motivated, addressing key weaknesses in existing tree-based and embedding approaches. It is computationally efficient, demonstrating low memory and training time requirements, and achieves excellent performance on the largest-scale datasets. The reduction to pairwise classification is supported by a rigorous and insightful theoretical analysis. The work is both technically interesting and practically useful, making it a valuable contribution to the community.
