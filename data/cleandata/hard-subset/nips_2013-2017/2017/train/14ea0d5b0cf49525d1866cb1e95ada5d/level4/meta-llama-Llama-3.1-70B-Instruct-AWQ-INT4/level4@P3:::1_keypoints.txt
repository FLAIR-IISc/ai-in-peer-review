- Proposes a double sampling strategy on top of a multi-class-to-binary reduction to handle extreme multi-class problems.  
- Aims to overcome long-tailed class distributions and reduce the number of example pairs after expansion.  
- Provides theoretical analysis with generalization error bounds based on local Rademacher complexities.  
- Conducts extensive experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets with up to 100,000 classes.  
- Empirical results show high performance and shorter training time on several datasets.  
- Compares comprehensively against baselines (OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse).  
- Strength: combination of theoretical and empirical evaluation with wide-scale experiments.  
- Weakness: novelty is limited and largely builds on prior work [16], with only two main additions.  
- Weakness: prediction time remains long relative to other approaches and needs further analysis.  
- Weakness: the new generalization bound’s interpretation and comparison to other bounds is unclear.  
- Weakness: experimental baselines mix batch and online methods, so memory-usage comparisons are inconsistent and should be analyzed.  
- Overall: paper makes a valuable contribution to extreme classification but has notable limitations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: comprehensive analysis, theoretical bounds, large-scale experiments, good performance and reduced training time.  
- Arguments against acceptance: thin novelty, long prediction time, unclear theoretical interpretation, and inconsistent memory comparisons.
