This paper proposes a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, particularly suitable for scenarios with a very large number of classes. The authors consider a hypothesis that maps (input,class) pairs to scores, with an underlying loss function that counts the fraction of wrong classes scored higher than the true class. Specifically, they employ a feature transformation φ that maps (input,class) pairs to a p-dimensional feature space, and learn a mapping from R^p to scores.

Their reduction extends the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which creates K-1 transformed points for each data point (x,y), where each transformed point corresponds to the comparison of label y with some incorrect label y'. Given that the transformed dataset contains correlated training examples, many standard generalization bounds cannot be applied. The first contribution of this paper is an improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al. (2015) using new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity in this setting.

The second main contribution is showing that it is possible to employ sampling techniques during the reduction to balance the class distributions and decrease the training set size. The authors sample each class (with replacement) to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed training data and use only a random sample of classes, rather than including transformed points for all K-1 possible class comparisons. They demonstrate that these two sampling procedures do not drastically affect their generalization bounds.

The authors also present an empirical comparison to several strong baselines, showcasing the competitiveness of their new procedure. However, a theoretical analysis of the impact of the prediction algorithm given in section 2.3 is missing, where comparisons are only performed for the k classes whose centroids are closest to a given input point.

Furthermore, additional discussion of the sample complexity bounds would be beneficial, possibly comparing them to the generalization bounds obtained for non-transformed multi-class problems. Overall, the paper presents an interesting reduction and corresponding generalization bounds for dealing with correlated data, with the main novelty being the sampling technique for rebalancing class distributions and reducing the size of the transformed dataset.

Strengths:

* Novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification
* Improved generalization analysis using new bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity
* Sampling techniques for rebalancing class distributions and reducing training set size
* Competitive empirical comparison to strong baselines

Weaknesses:

* Missing theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm in section 2.3
* Limited discussion of sample complexity bounds and comparison to non-transformed multi-class problems

Originality:

* The paper presents a novel reduction and corresponding generalization bounds, building upon existing work
* The sampling techniques for rebalancing class distributions and reducing training set size are a new contribution

Significance:

* The paper addresses a relevant problem in multi-class classification with a large number of classes
* The proposed reduction and sampling techniques have the potential to improve performance and efficiency in such scenarios

Quality:

* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the proposed reduction and generalization bounds
* The empirical comparison is thorough and demonstrates the competitiveness of the new procedure

Clarity:

* The paper is well-organized and easy to follow
* The notation and terminology are clearly defined and used consistently throughout the paper

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting contribution to the field of multi-class classification, with a clear and well-written presentation. However, some additional analysis and discussion would further strengthen the paper.
