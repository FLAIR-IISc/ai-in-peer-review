- Paper extends dyadic reduction from [16] with a (π, κ)-double sampling strategy to handle long-tailed extreme multi-class problems.  
- The (π, κ)-DS scheme oversamples small classes, subsamples large classes, and reduces dyadic examples via controlled sampling.  
- Theoretical contribution: new generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity (Theorem 2) proving ERM consistency under double sampling and non-i.i.d. dyadic examples.  
- Empirical validation on DMOZ and WIKI-{Small,50K,100K} shows competitive accuracy and macro-F1 with large reductions in training time, prediction time, and memory vs. baselines.  
- Strength: Rigorous theoretical analysis addressing class-distribution shifts and non-i.i.d. dyadic data (Theorems 1–2).  
- Strength: Comprehensive experiments against six baselines with runtime/memory metrics and statistical significance testing.  
- Strength: Clear presentation, intuitive motivation for double sampling, and an effective candidate selection strategy for prediction efficiency.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty—core dyadic reduction remains closely tied to prior work [16]; incremental rather than fundamentally new.  
- Weakness: Paper should better compare tightness of new bounds versus [16]’s fractional Rademacher analysis.  
- Weakness: Prediction-time advantage via candidate selection is underemphasized and not clearly positioned relative to tree-based methods.  
- Weakness: Mixing batch and online methods in comparisons makes memory/runtime claims potentially misleading; clearer segmentation is needed.  
- Overall: Valuable, practically scalable advance for extreme classification with strong empirical and theoretical support, but theoretical novelty and emphasis on prediction-time benefits need clearer contextualization.
