Summary:
This paper proposes a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, particularly well-suited for scenarios with a very large number of classes. The approach considers a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, employing a loss function that counts the fraction of incorrectly classified classes receiving higher scores than the true class. Specifically, the authors utilize a feature transformation, φ, mapping (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional feature space, and learn a mapping from R<sup>p</sup> to scores. 
Their reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each data point (x, y), creates K-1 transformed points representing comparisons between the true label y and incorrect labels y'. Recognizing that the resulting transformed dataset contains correlated training examples, standard generalization bounds are often inapplicable. The first contribution of this paper is an improved generalization analysis compared to Joshi et al. (2015), achieved through new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity in this correlated setting. The second main contribution demonstrates that sampling techniques can be effectively employed during the reduction process to balance class distributions and reduce the training set size. The authors achieve this by sampling classes (with replacement) to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed training data, and by using only a random sample of classes rather than all K-1 possible comparisons. They demonstrate that these sampling procedures do not significantly impact their generalization bounds. Finally, the proposed procedure demonstrates competitive performance against several strong baselines.

Comments:
This paper presents an interesting reduction from multi-class classification with many classes to binary classification, along with corresponding generalization bounds designed to address the correlation present in the reduced dataset. The primary novelty of the reduction lies in the sampling technique used to rebalance class distributions and reduce the size of the transformed dataset; the basic reduction itself was previously presented by Joshi et al. The empirical comparison to existing algorithms is strong.

One aspect missing from the paper is a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm described in Section 2.3, which limits comparisons to the k classes with the closest centroids to a given input point. A more in-depth analysis here would be valuable. 

I would also appreciate a more detailed discussion of the sample complexity bounds, perhaps with a comparison to the generalization bounds obtained for standard, non-transformed multi-class problems.
