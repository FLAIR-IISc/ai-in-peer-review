- Proposes a scalable learning reduction for extreme multiclass classification that converts the problem to pairwise binary classification with sampling and theoretical consistency guarantees.
- Notes limitations of existing approaches: tree-based methods (e.g., hierarchical softmax) can be suboptimal and cascade errors; label-embedding methods fail when the label matrix is not low-rank.
- Introduces a sampling-based "Aggressive Double Sampling" procedure with two parameters: class sampling frequency inversely proportional to empirical frequency (to cover long tail) and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples.
- Training repeats the sampling to produce dyadic examples and trains a final dyadic classifier; this yields lower memory use and faster training.
- At prediction time, exhaustive pairwise features are intractable, so a heuristic selects candidate classes by representing each class as the average of its vectors and runs pairwise classification only on candidates.
- Experimental results on five text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 compared to competitors (including one-vs-all where feasible) for problems with >30k classes; training is faster and memory-efficient but prediction is somewhat slower.
- Theoretical analysis addresses dependent dyadic examples and oversampling bias by splitting sums via the induced graph structure and applying concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables (Janson); finite-sample risk bounds are biased by oversampling ratios but biases shrink linearly with sample sizes.
- Overall assessment: method is theoretically sound, practically useful, memory- and compute-efficient, and performs especially well on the largest datasets.
