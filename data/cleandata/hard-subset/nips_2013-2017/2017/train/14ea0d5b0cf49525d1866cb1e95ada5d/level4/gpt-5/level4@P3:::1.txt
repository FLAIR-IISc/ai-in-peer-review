Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles extreme multi-class classification, where the number of classes K is very large and class frequencies are highly imbalanced. Building on dyadic multi-class-to-binary reduction (as in [16]), the authors introduce a “double sampling” scheme: (i) sample training examples per class with probabilities inversely proportional to class frequency (to mitigate long-tail imbalance), and (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ competing classes to form dyadic pairs instead of using all K−1. The approach reduces the size of the dyadic training set and balances positives/negatives in the binary formulation. At test time, the method further prunes the candidate classes using nearest-centroid selection in the dyadic space before scoring.

On the theory side, the paper provides generalization bounds for dependent dyadic examples under distribution shift induced by sampling. It derives bounds using fractional graph covers and local Rademacher complexities, leading to consistency guarantees for ERM on the reduced, dependent sample. Empirically, the method is evaluated on LSHTC (DMOZ and Wikipedia) subsets with up to 100K classes, and compared to OVA/M-SVM (LibLinear), tree-based methods (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree), and PD-Sparse. Results show competitive predictive performance with substantially reduced training time and memory relative to OVA/M-SVM, and higher accuracy than tree-based methods. Code and data are provided.

Quality:
- Theory: The generalization analysis is careful and appropriate for the dependence structure created by dyadic reduction and class reweighting. The appearance of α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy in the bounds makes the trade-offs explicit: oversampling rare classes (small πy) helps balance but increases α, β; shrinking κ reduces computational cost but worsens the √((K−1)/(Mκ)) term. While proofs are deferred to the supplement, the results are plausible and relevant to the proposed sampling scheme.
- Empirics: Experiments are comprehensive in scale, include strong baselines, and report accuracy, macro-F1, runtime, and memory. The method achieves a favorable time–memory–performance trade-off on large datasets. However, the study would benefit from ablations (varying κ, π, and the prediction-time candidate pool size q), and more nuanced test-time analysis.
- Reproducibility: Features for φ are described (10 similarity-based features inspired by learning to rank), datasets are standard, and code is released.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, but some notation and exposition could be tightened. The role of α and β in Theorem 2 and their practical implications (how to tune π across class frequencies) should be made more explicit. The candidate selection step (parameter q, centroid computation) would benefit from a concise complexity analysis and a sensitivity study. Minor typographical and formatting issues appear in the draft.

Originality:
The work is incremental relative to [16]: the main novelty is the (π, κ) double sampling for class balancing and dyadic reduction, a candidate selection heuristic at prediction, and a refined generalization analysis using local fractional Rademacher complexities. These are meaningful, but the overall algorithmic framework remains close to prior dyadic reduction work.

Significance:
Extreme multi-class is important in practice, especially in text. The proposed sampling scheme is simple, broadly applicable, and appears to deliver strong practical benefits on large datasets. The theoretical contribution helps justify ERM on sampled, dependent dyadic data. The reliance on hand-crafted φ for text raises questions about applicability to other modalities without further engineering.

Strengths:
- Addresses long-tail imbalance and computational blow-up in dyadic reduction with a simple, effective sampling strategy.
- Nontrivial generalization bounds under dependence and sampling-induced distribution shift; consistency is established.
- Strong large-scale experiments (up to 100K classes) with relevant baselines; competitive accuracy and macro-F1; substantially lower training time/memory than OVA/M-SVM.
- Practical candidate selection reduces test-time cost; code available.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty over [16] is modest; the main ideas are sampling and a refined bound rather than a fundamentally new reduction or model.
- Interpretation of bounds is not tied to empirical choices: no empirical study of how κ, π, and q affect accuracy/time and the bound’s terms (α, β).
- Test-time remains heavier than tree-based methods; the paper argues accuracy is higher, but many applications prioritize latency.
- Generalization beyond text is not demonstrated; φ is hand-engineered for text. The claim of easy portability to other domains is not empirically validated.
- The conversion of multi-label datasets to multi-class by duplicating instances can bias class frequencies and evaluation; discuss implications.
- Baselines mix batch and online/streaming methods; memory/time comparisons should carefully control for training regimes and implementation details.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Provide ablations varying κ and π (e.g., π ∝ 1/µk^γ) to quantify the trade-off between accuracy, bound terms (α, β), and time/memory. Likewise, report sensitivity to q in candidate selection.
- Compare the new bounds to those in [16] quantitatively (rates, constants, dependency on K, κ, and π). Under what regimes does the local Rademacher analysis tighten the guarantees?
- Add a clear complexity summary: expected training size κ|Sπ|, training and test-time complexities as functions of K, κ, q, and feature dimension p.
- Report top-k accuracy (or P@k) commonly used in extreme classification; macro-F1 and accuracy alone may not reflect practical retrieval quality.
- Clarify how centroids are computed and stored, and provide the wall-clock gains from candidate selection vs exhaustive scoring.
- If possible, include a non-text dataset with a different φ to demonstrate portability, or discuss how φ could be learned end-to-end.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a relevant, hard problem with a practical and scalable method.
- Sound and targeted theoretical analysis for the proposed sampling scheme.
- Strong empirical results at substantial scale with open-source release.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty over prior dyadic-reduction work.
- Limited interpretability/connection of theory to practice (no ablations).
- Test-time latency remains a concern relative to tree-based methods.
- Generality beyond text is not demonstrated.

Overall recommendation:
Borderline accept. The paper makes a solid, well-validated engineering and theory contribution to an important area, though the novelty is incremental and some empirical/theoretical connections and ablations are missing. Strengthening the experimental analysis and clarifying the bound’s practical implications would make this a clear accept.
