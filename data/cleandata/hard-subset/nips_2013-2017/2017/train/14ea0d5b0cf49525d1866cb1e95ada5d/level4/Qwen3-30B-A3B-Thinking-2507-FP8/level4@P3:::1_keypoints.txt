- Proposes an aggressive (π, κ)-double sampling strategy for multi-class→binary reduction to address class imbalance and computational bottlenecks in extreme classification.  
- Builds on the dyadic reduction framework (Joshi et al., 2017) and reduces dyadic example construction.  
- Uses oversampling of small classes and subsampling of large classes to balance class distributions.  
- Theoretical contribution: new generalization bounds (Theorem 2) using local fractional Rademacher complexity that account for sampling-induced dependencies and show consistency.  
- Empirical validation on DMOZ and WIKI datasets shows improvements in training time, memory usage, accuracy, and macro F1 versus baselines.  
- Strong WIKI-100K results: 0.82 macro F1 (vs. 0.79 PD-Sparse), training time reduced from days to hours, and ~90% memory reduction vs OVA/M-SVM.  
- Significance: directly tackles long-tailed class distributions common in real-world text repositories and provides a scalable alternative to tree-based and OVA/M-SVM methods.  
- Candidate selection via class centroid sampling reduces prediction time and enhances practicality for recommendation-like applications.  
- Clarity: paper is well-organized; algorithms and theoretical development are clearly presented and the link between sampling and generalization is well explained.  
- Originality concern: theoretical advance is incremental over prior work ([16], [25], [22]) and needs clearer articulation of how it differs and tightness gains over [16].  
- Prediction-time drawback: method is slower at prediction than tree-based methods (e.g., 1.8s/sample vs 0.6s/sample for RecallTree) and lacks analysis of accuracy vs. prediction-time tradeoffs (e.g., candidate set size q).  
- Experimental clarity issue: comparisons mix batch and online methods, producing potentially misleading memory usage comparisons; this distinction should be clarified.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—(1) clarify theoretical contributions relative to [16]/[25], (2) analyze prediction time vs. accuracy tradeoffs, and (3) resolve batch/online comparison confusion.
