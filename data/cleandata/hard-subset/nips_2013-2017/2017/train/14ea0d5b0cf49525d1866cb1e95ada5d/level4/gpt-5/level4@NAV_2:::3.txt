This paper proposes a reduction strategy for extreme multiclass classification—settings with up to 100k labels—by converting the task into binary prediction over dyadic pairs.

Many extreme classification methods construct a latent label hierarchy (e.g., hierarchical softmax) to achieve logarithmic-time inference, or rely on label embeddings trained via least squares or sampling. The authors highlight two well-known drawbacks: hierarchical methods risk compounding errors due to suboptimal tree structure, while embedding approaches can degrade when the label matrix is not well-approximated by a low-rank model.

The paper adopts the alternative paradigm of reducing to pairwise binary classification and introduces a scalable, sampling-based method with consistency guarantees relative to the original multiclass ERM objective.

Experiments on five text datasets, including cases where one-vs-all is still feasible, indicate that once the label count exceeds roughly 30k, the proposed method typically attains better accuracy and macro-F1, trains faster, and uses substantially less memory—though its prediction step can be slower.

The Aggressive Double Sampling scheme is governed by two controls: class-wise sampling probabilities inversely proportional to empirical class frequencies to mitigate long-tail underrepresentation, and a parameter specifying the number of uniformly drawn adversarial classes per example. This process generates the training dyads for the final classifier.

At inference time, enumerating all pairwise features against every class is impractical, so the method uses a heuristic candidate selection: each class is represented by the centroid of its member vectors in the input space; candidate labels are the nearest centroids, and scoring is applied only to these.

The theoretical analysis addresses two sources of difficulty: the dyadic reduction induces dependencies among examples that share the same original instance, and class reweighting via oversampling shifts the empirical distribution. The proof strategy partitions the dependent sum into collections of independent terms according to the induced graph structure and applies large-deviation tools for partially dependent variables (à la Janson). The resulting finite-sample risk bounds include factors reflecting the mismatch between true class proportions and the oversampling probabilities; these terms diminish at a rate proportional to the sizes of the sampled and resampled datasets. The arguments appear sound, though I did not check every step of the proof.

In sum, the paper introduces an appealing approach to extreme classification with strong empirical performance on the largest benchmarks, modest memory and training-time footprints, and a binary-reduction algorithm with plausible and useful theoretical support for practitioners.
