This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme classification – multiclass classification with an exceptionally large number of classes (up to 100k). Existing approaches often rely on hierarchical tree structures or label embeddings, but these can suffer from suboptimal tree inference leading to error propagation, or performance degradation when the underlying label matrix lacks low-rank structure. This work instead frames extreme classification as a pairwise binary classification problem and introduces a scalable sampling-based method with accompanying theoretical consistency guarantees to the empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation.

The authors demonstrate through experiments on five text datasets that their method generally outperforms competing techniques – including standard one-vs-all where feasible – in both accuracy and F1-score when the number of classes exceeds 30,000. Importantly, the method also exhibits faster training times and significantly reduced memory consumption, though prediction speed is somewhat lower. The core of the approach, “Aggressive Double Sampling,” utilizes two key parameters: one controlling class sampling frequency (inversely proportional to empirical class frequency to address long-tail distributions), and another governing the number of adversarial examples sampled uniformly. This process iteratively trains a final dyadic classifier.

To address the intractability of generating pairwise features with all classes during prediction, a heuristic leveraging input space centroids is employed to identify candidate classes, followed by a series of pairwise classifications restricted to this reduced set. The theoretical analysis is complex, stemming from the induced dependence structure among the dyadic examples resulting from the multi-class to binary reduction, and the distributional shift caused by rare class oversampling. The authors overcome these challenges by decomposing the sum of dependent examples into independent components based on the example construction graph, and leveraging concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables (Janson). While the finite-sample risk bound contains biases related to the oversampling ratios, these biases demonstrably diminish with increasing training set size. The analysis appears rigorous, though a detailed proof verification was beyond the scope of this review.

In conclusion, this paper presents a compelling and effective method for extreme classification. Its combination of low computational and memory requirements with strong performance, particularly on large-scale datasets, makes it a valuable contribution. The binary reduction algorithm is both theoretically well-supported and practically relevant for practitioners in the field.
