Summary:
This paper proposes a new reduction from multi-class to binary classification that is particularly suitable when the number of classes is very large. The authors consider hypotheses that map (input, class) pairs to scores, and use a loss function that measures the fraction of incorrect classes that receive a higher score than the true class. Concretely, they assume a feature transformation φ that maps (input, class) pairs into a p-dimensional feature space, and they learn a scoring function from R^p to the reals.

Their reduction extends Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each example (x, y), creates K−1 transformed points, each corresponding to a comparison between the true label y and an incorrect label y'. Because the transformed dataset contains correlated training examples, standard generalization bounds are not directly applicable. The first contribution of this paper is an improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al. (2015), leveraging new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity tailored to this correlated setting. The second contribution is to show that sampling can be incorporated into the reduction to both balance class distributions and reduce the size of the transformed dataset. Specifically, the authors (i) resample classes with replacement to ensure roughly equal representation across classes in the transformed data, and (ii) replace the full set of K−1 class comparisons with a random subset. They prove that these sampling strategies do not significantly degrade their generalization guarantees. Finally, the proposed method performs very competitively against strong baselines in empirical evaluations.

Comments:
This is an interesting and practical reduction for large-class multi-class classification, paired with appropriate generalization bounds that account for the correlation structure induced by the reduction. The primary novelty lies in the sampling strategies used to rebalance classes and shrink the transformed dataset; the underlying reduction itself follows prior work by Joshi et al. The empirical comparisons to existing methods are very strong.

One limitation is the absence of a theoretical analysis of the prediction procedure in Section 2.3, where comparisons are restricted to the k classes whose centroids are closest to the input. It would be valuable to understand how this approximation affects consistency, bias, and the derived generalization bounds.

I would also have appreciated additional discussion of the sample complexity bounds, ideally with a comparison to generalization guarantees for the original (non-transformed) multi-class problem. Such a comparison would clarify the trade-offs introduced by the reduction and the sampling steps, particularly in regimes with extremely large K.
