- Addresses extreme classification with up to 100k classes, proposing a reduction to pairwise binary classification.
- Notes limitations of existing methods: tree-based approaches risk cascading errors; label embeddings fail when label matrix is not low-rank.
- Proposes a scalable, sampling-based "Aggressive Double Sampling" reduction with theoretical consistency to multiclass ERM.
- Aggressive Double Sampling uses two parameters: inverse-frequency sampling to boost rare classes and uniform drawing of adversarial examples.
- Training repeats sampled dyadic example construction to produce a final binary classifier; training is memory- and computation-efficient.
- Prediction uses a heuristic candidate set: class centroids identify likely classes, then pairwise comparisons are run only within that set.
- Experiments on five text datasets (including >30k classes) show higher accuracy/F1, faster training, and much lower memory than competitors; prediction is somewhat slower.
- Theoretical analysis handles dependency and oversampling via graph decomposition and concentration inequalities (Janson); contains a bias term that shrinks linearly with sample size.
- Proofs appear sound though not fully verified by the reviewer.
- Overall verdict: method is theoretically interesting, practically effective on large datasets, and useful for practitioners.
