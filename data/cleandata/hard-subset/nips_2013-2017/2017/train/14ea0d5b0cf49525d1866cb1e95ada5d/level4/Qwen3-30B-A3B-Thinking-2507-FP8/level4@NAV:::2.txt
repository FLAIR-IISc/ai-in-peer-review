

Review Summary:  
This paper introduces a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, specifically designed for scenarios with a very large number of classes. The authors formulate a hypothesis mapping (input, class) pairs to scores, where the underlying loss function quantifies the fraction of incorrect classes assigned a higher score than the true class. They propose a feature transformation φ mapping (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional space, followed by a learned mapping from ℝ^p to scores.  

The reduction builds upon Joshi et al. (2015), who for each (x, y) generate K−1 transformed points representing comparisons between label y and each incorrect label y'. However, the resulting correlated training data in the transformed space invalidates standard generalization bounds. The paper’s first contribution is an improved generalization analysis using new fractional Rademacher complexity bounds for this setting. The second contribution demonstrates that sampling techniques during reduction can rebalance class distributions and reduce training set size: (1) sampling classes with replacement to ensure equal representation in the transformed data, and (2) using random subsets of classes instead of all K−1 comparisons. The authors prove these sampling procedures negligibly impact generalization guarantees. Empirical results show the method competes strongly with established baselines.  

Comments:  
This work presents a compelling reduction for large-scale multi-class classification to binary classification, with rigorous generalization bounds addressing correlated data in the transformed space. The primary novelty lies in the sampling techniques for class rebalancing and dataset size reduction, though the core reduction framework adapts prior work by Joshi et al. (2015). The empirical comparisons against strong baselines are robust and convincing.  

Two gaps warrant attention: First, the paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm in Section 2.3, which restricts comparisons to the k classes with centroids closest to the input. Second, a deeper discussion of sample complexity bounds—particularly a comparison to generalization bounds for non-transformed multi-class problems—would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
