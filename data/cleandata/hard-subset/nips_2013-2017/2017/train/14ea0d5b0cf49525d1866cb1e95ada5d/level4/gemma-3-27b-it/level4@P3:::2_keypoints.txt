- Proposes framing extreme multi-class classification as binary classification using a double sampling strategy to handle many classes.  
- Builds on prior pairwise reduction methods but targets class imbalance and computational cost in extreme settings.  
- First samples training data to balance classes, then samples example-class pairs to reduce dataset size while aiming to preserve generalization.  
- Provides new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity to handle dependencies from sampling.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia) with improvements in accuracy, training/prediction time, and memory.  
- Effectively situates work relative to tree-based, embedding-based, and binary reduction approaches and highlights OVA limitations.  
- Strength: tackles a relevant, practical problem in areas like text categorization and recommendation systems.  
- Strength: the double sampling strategy is novel and practically useful for skewed, large-class datasets.  
- Strength: solid theoretical grounding via derived generalization bounds.  
- Strength: clear presentation and well-organized writing.  
- Weakness: limited theoretical/empirical analysis of the candidate selection strategy and its accuracy-speed trade-offs.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison of derived sample complexity bounds to existing non-transformed multi-class bounds.  
- Weakness: method performance depends heavily on the feature mapping φ; broader discussion of applicability is limited.  
- Overall assessment: strong, theoretically sound, and empirically effective paper with minor weaknesses; recommended for acceptance.
