## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission: [Paper Title]

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel reduction from multi-class to binary classification, specifically designed for scenarios with a large number of classes. The approach centers around a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, utilizing a loss function that quantifies the fraction of incorrectly classified classes receiving higher scores than the true class. The core idea involves a feature transformation, φ, mapping (input, class) pairs into a p-dimensional feature space, followed by learning a mapping from this space to scores. 

Building upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which constructs K-1 transformed points for each data point (x, y) representing comparisons between the true label and each incorrect label, this paper addresses a critical limitation: the correlated nature of the transformed training examples. Standard generalization bounds are often inapplicable in such settings.  The first contribution is an improved generalization analysis leveraging new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity tailored to this correlated data structure.  The second significant contribution lies in the introduction of sampling techniques to both balance class distributions and reduce the training set size. Specifically, the authors employ sampling with replacement to ensure roughly equal representation for each class in the transformed data and utilize a random sample of classes instead of all K-1 possible comparisons. They demonstrate that these sampling procedures maintain the integrity of their generalization bounds. The paper concludes with compelling empirical results showing competitive performance against several strong baseline methods.

**Comments:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable reduction for addressing multi-class classification problems with a large number of classes. The central novelty lies in the judicious use of sampling – both for rebalancing class distributions and for diminishing the size of the transformed dataset – a refinement of the core reduction initially proposed by Joshi et al. (2015). The empirical evaluation is robust and demonstrates strong performance compared to existing algorithms.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methodology. However, a more detailed theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm described in Section 2.3 would strengthen the contribution. This algorithm, which restricts comparisons to the *k* classes with the closest centroids to a given input point, warrants further investigation. Understanding its impact on the overall generalization bound, or providing justification for its effectiveness, would be beneficial. 

Furthermore, while the generalization bounds presented are a key contribution, additional discussion regarding their practical implications and scaling properties would be appreciated.  Specifically, a comparison to the sample complexity bounds achievable for traditional, non-transformed multi-class problems would provide valuable context and highlight the advantages of this reduction in scenarios with a large number of classes.  For instance, how does the sample complexity grow with *K* in this setting compared to direct multi-class classification approaches?

**Strengths:**

*   Novel and effective sampling techniques for improving the scalability and generalization of the multi-to-binary reduction.
*   Improved generalization analysis for correlated data.
*   Strong empirical performance against competitive baselines.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited theoretical analysis of the approximation introduced by the nearest neighbor search in Section 2.3.
*   Lack of direct comparison to sample complexity bounds for non-transformed multi-class problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that addresses an important problem in multi-class classification. The proposed sampling techniques represent a significant improvement over existing methods, and the empirical results are encouraging. While some theoretical details could be expanded upon, the paper’s contributions are substantial and warrant acceptance. 

**Recommendation:** Accept.
