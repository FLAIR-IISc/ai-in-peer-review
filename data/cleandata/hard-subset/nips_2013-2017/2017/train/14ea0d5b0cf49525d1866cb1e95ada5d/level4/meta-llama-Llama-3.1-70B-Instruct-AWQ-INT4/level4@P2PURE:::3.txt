This paper proposes a learning reduction for extreme classification, a type of multiclass classification where the output space encompasses up to 100k distinct classes. Many existing approaches to extreme classification rely on either inferring a tree structure among the output labels, such as in hierarchical softmax, which reduces the number of binary predictions required to infer the output class to a logarithmic scale, or employing a label embedding approach that can be learned efficiently through least-squares or sampling. However, the authors point out the limitations of these approaches, including the potential suboptimality of the inferred latent tree, which can lead to cascading errors, and the label embedding approach's vulnerability to prediction errors when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

As an alternative, the authors adopt a framework that reduces extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. They present a scalable, sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees ensuring its consistency with the original multiclass empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation. Experimental results on five text datasets, comparing their method to several competing approaches, including standard one-vs-all classification where computationally feasible, demonstrate its superiority in terms of accuracy and F1 score once the number of classes exceeds 30k. Additionally, their method trains faster and requires significantly less memory, although it is somewhat slower in terms of prediction speed.

The Aggressive Double Sampling reduction is controlled by two parameters: one that regulates the frequency of sampling each class, inversely proportional to the empirical class frequency to avoid missing classes in the long tail, and another that sets the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly. This procedure is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier. At prediction time, inference is complicated by the intractability of generating pairwise features for all possible classes. To address this, the authors employ a heuristic that identifies candidate classes by representing each class as the average of its vectors, with prediction carried out through a series of pairwise classifications with only these candidates.

The theoretical analysis of the reduction is complicated by the fact that the reduction from multiclass to dyadic binary classification transforms a sum of independent random variables into a sum of random variables with a particular dependence structure among the dyadic examples. Another complication arises from the oversampling of rare classes, which shifts the empirical distribution. The proof involves splitting the sum of dependent examples into several sums of independent variables based on the graph structure induced by the example construction and utilizing concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables introduced by Janson. Although the finite-sample risk bound is biased by the ratios between the true class probabilities and the oversampled ones, these biases decrease linearly with the size of the sampled and re-sampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, although I have not verified the proof in detail.

Overall, this paper presents a strong method for extreme classification, characterized by small memory and computational requirements and superior performance, particularly on the largest datasets. The binary reduction algorithm is theoretically sound, interesting, and useful to practitioners.
