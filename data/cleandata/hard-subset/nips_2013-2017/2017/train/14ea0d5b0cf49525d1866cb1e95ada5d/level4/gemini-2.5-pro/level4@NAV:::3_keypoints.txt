- Task: Addresses extreme classification with very large output spaces (up to ~100k classes).
- Prior work: Reviews tree-based (hierarchical softmax) and label-embedding methods and their limits (cascading errors, failure on non–low-rank labels).
- Approach: Proposes a reduction to pairwise binary classification via a scalable, sampling-based method called Aggressive Double Sampling.
- Theoretical guarantee: Shows consistency with multiclass ERM and provides finite-sample risk bounds.
- Sampling scheme: Two hyperparameters—class sampling rate inversely proportional to empirical frequency (to upweight tail classes) and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples.
- Training: Builds the dyadic classifier by repeating the sampling procedure, achieving low memory and faster training.
- Prediction: Uses a centroid-based heuristic to select candidate classes and performs pairwise classification only among those to avoid O(K) pairwise feature generation.
- Empirical results: On five text datasets (including >30k classes) the method yields superior accuracy and F1, lower memory use, and faster training, with a moderate slowdown at prediction time.
- Theoretical challenges: Handles dependence introduced by dyadic transformation and bias from oversampling by decomposing dependent sums and applying Janson’s concentration inequalities.
- Risk-bias behavior: The finite-sample bound includes a bias term proportional to true vs. oversampled class probabilities, which decreases linearly with sampled/resampled set sizes.
- Overall contribution: Presents a practical, theoretically grounded reduction for extreme classification with strong empirical performance and resource efficiency.
