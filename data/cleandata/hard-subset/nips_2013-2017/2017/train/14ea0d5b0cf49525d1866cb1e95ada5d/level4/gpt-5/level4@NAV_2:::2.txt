Summary:
This paper tackles multi-class classification with an extremely large label set by reducing it to binary classification. The model assigns a score to each (input, class) pair, using a loss that measures the fraction of incorrect classes that receive a higher score than the true class. Concretely, a feature map φ sends (input, class) pairs to R^p, and a function f maps R^p to scores. Building on Joshi et al. (2015), the reduction creates, for each example (x, y), K−1 dyadic comparisons between y and every competing label y′. Because the resulting dyadic examples are dependent, classical generalization arguments do not directly apply. The first contribution is a sharper generalization analysis than Joshi et al., leveraging fractional/local Rademacher complexity to handle dependencies. The second main contribution is an aggressive double sampling scheme to both rebalance highly skewed class distributions and shrink the expanded training set: classes are sampled (with replacement) to equalize representation, and, instead of forming all K−1 comparisons, only a random subset of negatives is retained. The authors show that these two sampling steps preserve consistency and yield comparable bounds. Empirically, the approach performs competitively against strong baselines on large DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets, while improving training/prediction time and memory footprint.

Comments:
This work presents a compelling reduction for extreme multi-class classification and accompanying generalization bounds tailored to dependent dyadic data. The primary novelty lies in the double sampling used to rebalance classes and to reduce the size of the transformed dataset; the core dyadic reduction itself follows prior work by Joshi et al. The empirical comparisons are thorough and favorable.

A missing piece is a theoretical treatment of the candidate-selection prediction in Section 2.3, where scoring is restricted to the q nearest-centroid classes for a test input; an analysis of how this heuristic affects consistency or error would strengthen the paper.

It would also be helpful to include more discussion of the sample complexity bounds, ideally contrasting them with known generalization guarantees for direct (non-reduced) multi-class formulations.
