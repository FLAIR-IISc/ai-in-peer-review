- Paper addresses extreme multiclass classification with up to 100k classes and reviews limitations of tree-based and label-embedding methods (cascading errors, non-low-rank labels).  
- Proposes reducing extreme classification to pairwise binary (dyadic) classification with a scalable, sampling-based algorithm.  
- Introduces Aggressive Double Sampling controlled by two parameters: class sampling frequency (inverse to empirical frequency) and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples.  
- Uses a centroid-based candidate selection heuristic at prediction time to avoid pairwise comparisons with all classes.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees showing consistency with the original multiclass ERM despite dependent dyadic example structure.  
- Theoretical analysis tackles dependence and oversampling via graph-based decomposition and Janson’s concentration inequalities; finite-sample bounds have bias that decreases with sample size.  
- Experiments on five text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 for >30k classes, with faster training and much lower memory usage.  
- Method incurs a prediction-time speed penalty compared to some baselines.  
- Overall assessment: algorithm is theoretically sound, practically useful, memory/computation efficient, and strong on large-scale datasets.
