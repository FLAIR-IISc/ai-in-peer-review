This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification, which involves multiclass classification with an output space comprising up to 100k different classes. Many approaches to extreme classification rely on inferring a tree structure among the output labels, such as the hierarchical softmax, to reduce the number of binary predictions required to infer the output class to a logarithmic scale. Alternatively, label embedding approaches can be learned efficiently through least-squares or sampling. However, the authors note that the inferred latent tree may not be optimal, leading to cascading errors, and the label embedding approach may result in prediction errors when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

The authors propose an alternative approach, reducing extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. They present a scalable sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees ensuring consistency with the original multiclass empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation. Experiments on five text datasets, comparing the proposed method with several competing approaches, including standard one-vs-all classification when computationally feasible, demonstrate its superiority in terms of accuracy and F1 score once the number of classes exceeds 30k. The method also trains faster while utilizing significantly less memory, although it suffers somewhat in terms of prediction speed.

The Aggressive Double Sampling reduction is controlled by two parameters. The first parameter determines the frequency of sampling each class, which is inversely proportional to the empirical class frequency to avoid entirely missing classes in the long tail. The second parameter sets the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly. This procedure is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier.

At prediction time, inference is complicated by the fact that generating pairwise features with all possible classes is intractable. To address this, the authors employ a heuristic that identifies candidate classes by creating an input space centroid representation of each class as the average of vectors in the class. Prediction is then carried out by a series of pairwise classifications with only those candidates.

The theoretical analysis of the reduction is complicated by the fact that the reduction from multiclass to dyadic binary classification changes a sum of independent random variables into a sum of random variables with a particular dependence structure among the dyadic examples. Another complication arises from the oversampling of rare classes, which shifts the empirical distribution. The proof involves splitting the sum of dependent examples into several sums of independent variables based on the graph structure induced by the example construction and using the concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables introduced by Janson. Although the finite-sample risk bound is biased by the ratios between the true class probabilities and the oversampled ones, these biases decrease linearly with the size of the sampled and re-sampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, although I have not verified the proof in detail.

Overall, this paper presents a strong method for extreme classification, characterized by small memory and computational requirements and superior performance, especially on the largest datasets. The binary reduction algorithm appears theoretically sound, interesting, and useful to practitioners.
