- Addresses extreme multiclass classification with up to 100,000 classes and critiques tree-based and label-embedding approaches for error propagation and low-rank assumptions.  
- Proposes reducing extreme classification to pairwise binary (dyadic) classification via a scalable, sampling-based reduction with theoretical consistency to multiclass ERM.  
- Introduces "Aggressive Double Sampling" with two parameters: class sampling frequency inversely proportional to empirical frequency, and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples.  
- Sampling mitigates missing long-tail classes while adversarial draws increase training negatives; the process is iterated to train a final dyadic classifier.  
- Uses a heuristic at prediction time that restricts pairwise comparisons to candidate classes found via class centroids to avoid enumerating all classes.  
- Experiments on five text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 when classes exceed ~30,000, with faster training and much lower memory use but slightly slower prediction.  
- Theoretical analysis handles altered dependence structure and oversampling bias by decomposing dependent sums via a graph view and applying concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables.  
- Finite-sample risk bounds are biased by oversampling ratios, but biases decrease linearly with increasing sampled and re-sampled training sizes.  
- Reviewer finds the analysis sound (without full verification) and regards the method as well-motivated, theoretically interesting, and practically valuable.
