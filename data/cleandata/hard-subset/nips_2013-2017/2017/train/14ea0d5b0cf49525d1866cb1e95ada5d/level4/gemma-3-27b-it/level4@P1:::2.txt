This paper proposes a new reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, particularly well-suited for scenarios with a very large number of classes. They consider a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, using a loss function that counts the fraction of incorrectly predicted classes receiving higher scores than the true class. Specifically, they propose a feature transformation φ mapping (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional feature space, and then learn a mapping from R<sup>p</sup> to scores. 
Their reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which, for each data point (x, y), creates K-1 transformed points representing comparisons between label y and incorrect labels y'. Recognizing that the transformed dataset contains correlated training examples, where standard generalization bounds may not apply, the paper makes two key contributions. First, they present an improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al. (2015) using new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity tailored to this setting. Second, they demonstrate that sampling techniques can be effectively employed during the reduction to balance class distributions and reduce training set size. This involves sampling each class with probabilities designed to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed training data, and using a random sample of classes, rather than all K-1 possibilities, during the transformation. They prove that these sampling procedures do not drastically affect their generalization bounds. Finally, they empirically demonstrate that this new procedure performs very competitively against several strong baselines on large-scale datasets.

This paper presents an interesting reduction from multi-class classification with a large number of classes to binary classification, along with corresponding generalization bounds for handling the inherent correlation in the reduced dataset. The primary novelty lies in the sampling technique for rebalancing class distributions and reducing the transformed dataset's size; the underlying reduction was previously introduced by Joshi et al. The empirical comparison to existing algorithms is compelling.

One aspect missing from the paper is a theoretical analysis of the impact of the prediction algorithm described in section 2.3, where performance is evaluated only considering the k classes with centroids closest to a given input point. Investigating the theoretical implications of this candidate selection strategy would strengthen the paper. 

I would also appreciate further discussion of the sample complexity bounds, potentially comparing them to generalization bounds derived for non-transformed multi-class problems. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the benefits gained through the proposed reduction and sampling techniques.
