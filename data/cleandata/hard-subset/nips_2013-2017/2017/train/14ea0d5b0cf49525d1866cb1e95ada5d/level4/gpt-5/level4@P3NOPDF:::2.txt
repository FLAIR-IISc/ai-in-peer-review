Summary:
This paper introduces a reduction from multi-class to binary classification tailored for problems with a very large number of classes. The method operates on (input, class) pairs through a feature transformation φ mapping them into a p-dimensional space, and learns a scoring function over this space. The loss measures the fraction of incorrect classes scored higher than the true class, aligning with ranking-based objectives.

Building on Joshi et al. (2015), which constructs K−1 transformed examples per original example by contrasting the true label against each incorrect label, this paper tackles two key challenges: (1) the induced correlations among transformed examples, which complicate generalization analysis, and (2) the computational burden of generating all K−1 comparisons. The first contribution is an improved generalization analysis that leverages new bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity to better handle the dependence structure in the transformed dataset. The second contribution introduces sampling strategies to rebalance class frequencies and reduce dataset size: classes are resampled (with replacement) to equalize representation in the transformed data, and only a random subset of the K−1 class comparisons is used per example. The authors show that these sampling procedures preserve favorable generalization guarantees. Empirically, the approach performs competitively against strong baselines.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important and practically relevant setting: multi-class classification with very large K, where standard approaches can be both statistically and computationally inefficient.
- Provides a careful generalization analysis for the correlated examples produced by the reduction. The use of fractional Rademacher complexity is well-motivated and represents a meaningful advance over prior analyses of this reduction framework.
- Introduces simple, effective sampling schemes to both rebalance class representation and subsample pairwise comparisons, reducing training set size while maintaining theoretical guarantees.
- Strong empirical results against competitive baselines, demonstrating both accuracy and scalability benefits.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Novelty relative to Joshi et al. (2015) is primarily in the sampling strategies and the refined analysis. While these are significant, the core reduction is not new; the paper should clearly position and quantify the added value (both theoretically and empirically) over Joshi et al., e.g., through ablations that isolate the gains from sampling and through side-by-side bounds.
- The prediction-time heuristic in Section 2.3—restricting comparisons to the k classes with centroids closest to the input—lacks theoretical support. It would be valuable to analyze how this approximation affects consistency, error rates, or the derived bounds. For example, conditions under which the top-k centroid filter preserves the correct ranking with high probability, or bounds on the induced approximation error, would strengthen the contribution.
- The presentation would benefit from a more explicit comparison between the provided sample complexity bounds and known bounds for multiclass learning without reduction (e.g., direct multiclass margin-based methods), to clarify when the proposed approach is preferable. This could include dependence on K, class imbalance, and the dimensionality p, as well as on the number of sampled comparisons per example.
- Practical guidance on setting the sampling rates (for class rebalancing and pair subsampling) is limited. A discussion of trade-offs—variance vs. bias, computational savings vs. accuracy—and sensitivity analyses would help practitioners adopt the method.

Clarity and presentation:
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The description of the reduction and the induced correlations is clear.
- Please ensure a consistent and precise statement of assumptions (e.g., on the score function class and norms used in the analysis), and explicitly connect them to the terms appearing in the bounds.
- It would help to summarize the key terms in the final bounds, highlighting their dependence on K, the number of sampled negatives per example, and the degree of class imbalance.
- Minor: correct “Joshi etl al.” to “Joshi et al.”

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a theoretical treatment (even if preliminary) of the prediction-time top-k centroid filtering: assumptions under which it is safe, the probability of missing the true class among the k candidates, or a regret decomposition that isolates the effect of this heuristic.
- Add a section comparing your sample complexity with known results for non-reduced multiclass methods, and include a brief discussion on when the reduction with sampling is expected to dominate (e.g., extremely large K with heavy imbalance).
- Include ablations quantifying: (i) benefits of class rebalancing vs. no rebalancing, (ii) accuracy vs. number of sampled negatives per example, and (iii) end-to-end speedups and memory savings.
- Offer practical defaults or heuristics for choosing sampling parameters and k in the prediction step.

Overall assessment:
This is a solid and timely contribution to large-scale multiclass learning. The refined generalization analysis for correlated transformed data and the sampling strategies for balancing and subsampling are valuable and well supported empirically. The main gap is the absence of theoretical guarantees for the top-k centroid prediction heuristic and limited discussion contrasting the derived bounds with those of direct multiclass approaches. Addressing these points would appreciably strengthen the paper. As it stands, the work is promising and practically relevant, with clear potential impact on large-K classification.
