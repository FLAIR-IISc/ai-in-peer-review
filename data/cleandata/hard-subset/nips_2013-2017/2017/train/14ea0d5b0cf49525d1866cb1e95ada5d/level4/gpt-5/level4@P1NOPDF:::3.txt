This paper proposes a learning reduction for extreme multiclass classification, where the label space can contain up to 100k classes. 

Many existing methods impose a tree over labels (e.g., hierarchical softmax), yielding logarithmic-time inference, or use label embeddings learned via least squares or sampling. The authors correctly highlight the drawbacks: a learned latent tree may be suboptimal and propagate cascading errors, while embedding-based methods can fail when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

An alternative is to reduce extreme classification to pairwise (dyadic) binary classification. The authors adopt this framework and introduce a scalable, sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees, showing consistency with the original multiclass ERM objective.

Empirical results on five text datasets, comparing against several strong baselines (including one-vs-all where feasible), indicate that the proposed method is generally superior in accuracy and F1 once the number of classes exceeds roughly 30k. It also trains faster and uses substantially less memory, though it incurs some loss in prediction speed.

The Aggressive Double Sampling reduction is governed by two parameters. The first controls the sampling frequency of each class, set inversely proportional to the empirical class frequency to reduce the risk of omitting rare classes in the long tail. The second specifies the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly. This sampling procedure is iterated to train the final dyadic classifier.

At inference time, generating pairwise features against all classes is intractable. The authors therefore use a heuristic candidate-generation step: they represent each class by the centroid of its training examples in input space and restrict prediction to a shortlist of candidate classes, on which pairwise comparisons are performed.

The theoretical analysis is nontrivial for two reasons. First, the reduction transforms a sum of independent random variables into a sum with a specific dependency structure induced by dyadic construction. Second, oversampling rare classes shifts the empirical distribution. The proof strategy decomposes the sum over dependent examples into several sums over independent subsets, using the dependency graph implied by the construction, and applies Janson’s concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables. The resulting finite-sample risk bound is biased by the ratios between true class probabilities and their oversampled counterparts; however, these biases diminish linearly with the sizes of the sampled and re-sampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, though I have not checked every step in detail.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to extreme classification. The method offers low memory and computational cost, scales to very large label spaces, and delivers state-of-the-art accuracy on the largest datasets. The binary reduction is theoretically well-motivated and practically useful.
