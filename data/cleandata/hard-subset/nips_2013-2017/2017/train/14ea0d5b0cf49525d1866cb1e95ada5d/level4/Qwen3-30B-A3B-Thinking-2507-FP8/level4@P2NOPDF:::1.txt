

This paper proposes a double sampling strategy to enhance multiclass classification methods, extending the framework in [16]. The authors provide theoretical analysis, including novel generalization bounds derived from local Rademacher complexities, and empirical validation across multiple text datasets. Results demonstrate the method’s efficacy in handling large-scale multiclass problems with reduced training time.  

**Strengths:**  
- The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple baselines across diverse datasets. The proposed approach achieves competitive accuracy while significantly reducing training time, a notable practical advantage.  
- The focus on extreme classification addresses a critical and widely applicable problem domain, with relevance to real-world systems requiring efficient handling of thousands of classes.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- The core contributions are incremental. While the double sampling strategy and new generalization bounds represent two specific improvements over [16], the overall framework and methodology remain heavily derivative, limiting theoretical novelty. The paper does not sufficiently articulate how these modifications fundamentally advance the state of the art.  
- Prediction time remains substantially longer than competing methods, which is a significant drawback. In practical deployment scenarios, prediction latency often outweighs training efficiency, making this limitation a critical concern for real-world adoption.  

**Additional Concerns:**  
- The theoretical contribution lacks contextualization. The paper fails to clarify how the proposed generalization bounds compare to existing bounds in terms of tightness or practical relevance. Without this, the theoretical novelty remains ambiguous.  
- The experimental design mixes batch and online learning algorithms, creating unfair comparisons due to divergent memory requirements. For instance, batch methods (e.g., [16]) inherently require higher memory for full dataset storage, while online methods (e.g., [12]) operate incrementally. This inconsistency undermines the validity of the reported performance metrics.  

**Recommendation:**  
While the paper offers a technically sound incremental improvement with clear empirical benefits in training efficiency, its limited novelty and unresolved practical drawbacks (particularly prediction time) weaken its contribution. The experimental setup also requires refinement to ensure fair comparisons across learning paradigms. The authors should address the theoretical gaps and clarify the practical trade-offs before the work can be considered for publication.
