This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification, a multiclass classification setting where the output space contains a very large number of classes – up to 100,000 in this work. Many existing approaches to extreme classification rely on either inferring a tree structure among the output labels, as in hierarchical softmax, to reduce the number of binary predictions required, or on learning a label embedding that can be efficiently trained via least-squares or sampling. The authors identify limitations of these methods: inferred latent tree structures may be suboptimal and prone to error propagation, while label embedding approaches can suffer when the underlying label matrix is not low-rank.

Instead, the authors propose an alternative approach, reducing extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. They present a scalable, sampling-based method with theoretical consistency guarantees to the original multiclass empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation. 

Experiments on five text datasets, comparing their method against several competing approaches (including standard one-vs-all classification when computationally feasible), demonstrate that it generally outperforms these alternatives in terms of accuracy and F1-score when the number of classes exceeds 30,000. Importantly, their method trains faster and requires significantly less memory, although prediction speed is somewhat lower. 

The “Aggressive Double Sampling” reduction is governed by two parameters. The first controls the sampling frequency of each class, inversely proportional to its empirical frequency, thereby mitigating the risk of completely missing classes in the long tail. The second parameter defines the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly. This process is iterated to train the final dyadic classifier.

At prediction time, generating pairwise features with all possible classes is intractable. The authors address this by using a heuristic to identify candidate classes. This involves creating an input space centroid representation for each class (the average vector of instances belonging to that class), and performing pairwise classification only with these candidates.

The theoretical analysis of this reduction is complex, as transforming multiclass classification into dyadic binary classification alters the dependence structure of the random variables involved, and the oversampling of rare classes shifts the empirical distribution. The proof leverages the graph structure induced by the example construction to decompose the sum of dependent examples into several sums of independent variables, and employs concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables introduced by Janson. While the finite-sample risk bound is biased by the ratio between true and oversampled class probabilities, these biases diminish linearly with the size of the sampled and re-sampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, although I have not verified the proof in detail.

Overall, this paper presents a strong and well-motivated method for extreme classification, achieving superior performance, especially on the largest datasets, while maintaining low memory and computational requirements. The binary reduction algorithm is theoretically sound, interesting, and potentially valuable for practitioners.
