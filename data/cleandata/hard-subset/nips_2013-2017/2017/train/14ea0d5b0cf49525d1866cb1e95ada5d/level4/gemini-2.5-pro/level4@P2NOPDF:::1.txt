This paper proposes a double sampling strategy to extend and improve the multi-class classification framework presented in [16]. The authors support their method with both theoretical analysis, including a new generalization bound, and an empirical evaluation. Experiments conducted on large-scale text datasets demonstrate that the proposed approach is effective for extreme multi-class classification problems involving a vast number of classes.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper includes a comprehensive experimental evaluation against several relevant and strong baseline methods. The results show that the proposed approach achieves competitive classification performance while significantly reducing training time on multiple benchmark datasets.
*   The work addresses the problem of extreme multi-class classification, which is a challenging and highly relevant research area with numerous practical applications in fields like document tagging and recommendation systems.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary concern is the limited novelty of the work. The paper is heavily based on the framework of [16], and the contributions—the double sampling strategy and a new generalization bound—feel like incremental improvements rather than a substantial conceptual advance.
*   While the proposed approach significantly shortens training time, it appears to require a considerably longer prediction time compared to several competing methods. Arguably, inference speed is more critical than training speed in many real-world, production environments, which may limit the practical applicability of this method.

**Comments and Questions for the Authors:**
*   The interpretation of the new generalization bound is unclear. To better contextualize the theoretical contribution, could the authors provide a more detailed discussion on how this bound compares, either qualitatively or quantitatively, to existing bounds for similar extreme classification methods?
*   The experimental comparison includes both batch and online learning algorithms. These two classes of algorithms have fundamentally different memory usage profiles and computational models. A direct comparison of training time without a corresponding analysis of memory consumption can be misleading. The authors should clarify this distinction and, if possible, provide a more detailed analysis of the memory footprint of each method to ensure a fairer and more complete comparison.
