This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification—multiclass classification where the output space ranges up to 100,000 different classes. 

Many approaches to extreme classification rely on inferring a tree structure among the output labels, as in hierarchical softmax, so that only a logarithmic number of binary predictions need to be made to infer the output class, or a label embedding approach that can be learned efficiently through least-squares or sampling. The authors note the problems with these approaches: the inferred latent tree may not be optimal and can lead to cascading errors, and the label embedding approach may lead to prediction errors when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

An alternative approach is to reduce extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. The authors operate within this framework and present a scalable sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees as to its consistency with the original multiclass empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation.

Experiments on five text datasets, comparing a number of competing approaches (including standard one-vs-all classification when computationally feasible), show that the method is generally superior in terms of accuracy and F1-score once the number of classes exceeds 30,000. It also trains faster and uses significantly less memory, though it is somewhat slower during prediction.

The Aggressive Double Sampling reduction is controlled by two parameters. One controls the sampling frequency of each class, inversely proportional to its empirical frequency to prevent under-representation of classes in the long tail. The second parameter sets the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly. This procedure is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier.

At prediction time, generating pairwise features with all possible classes is intractable. A heuristic is used to identify candidate classes by creating an input space centroid representation for each class (the average vector within that class), with prediction carried out by a series of pairwise classifications using only these candidates.

Theoretical analysis of the reduction is complicated by the fact that it transforms a sum of independent random variables into a sum with a specific dependence structure among the dyadic examples. Another complication arises from the oversampling of rare classes, which shifts the empirical distribution. The proof involves splitting the sum of dependent examples into sums of independent variables based on the graph structure induced by the example construction, and leveraging concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables introduced by Janson. Although the finite-sample risk bound is affected by the ratios between the true class probabilities and the oversampled ones, these effects diminish linearly with the size of the sampled and resampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, though I have not verified the proof in detail.

Overall, this paper presents a strong method for extreme classification with low memory and computational requirements, and superior performance, especially on the largest datasets. The binary reduction algorithm appears theoretically sound, interesting, and likely useful to practitioners.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method consistently outperforms baselines on large-scale datasets in terms of accuracy, F1-score, training time, and memory usage.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a non-trivial theoretical analysis demonstrating the consistency of the proposed approach with the original ERM formulation, despite the sampling and dependency issues.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The method addresses a significant challenge in machine learning – extreme multi-class classification – with a scalable and effective solution.
*   **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally clear and well-structured, effectively presenting the method, its theoretical foundations, and experimental results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Prediction Speed:** While training and memory are strong, prediction speed is a potential drawback, especially compared to some tree-based methods.
*   **Complexity of Analysis:** The theoretical analysis, while impressive, is complex and might be difficult for some readers to fully grasp.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of extreme classification. The combination of strong empirical results and theoretical analysis makes it a compelling submission. I recommend acceptance.
