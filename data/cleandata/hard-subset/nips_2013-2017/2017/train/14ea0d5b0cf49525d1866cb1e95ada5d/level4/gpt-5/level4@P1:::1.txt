This paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing the problem to binary classification over dyadic example–class pairs, and introduces an aggressive double sampling scheme to address long-tailed label distributions and computational cost. Concretely, the authors: (i) resample the training set across classes with probabilities π inversely proportional to class frequency to balance the label distribution; (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ adversarial classes to construct dyads; and (iii) learn a single binary classifier on dyadic features φ(x, y). They also propose a candidate selection mechanism at prediction time that scores only a small set of q nearest-centroid classes. Theoretically, they derive generalization bounds for the dependent dyads using fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities, and show consistency of ERM under the induced distribution shift. Empirically, on large LSHTC-derived text datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia, up to 100K classes), the method achieves competitive accuracy and macro-F1 with substantially reduced training time and memory compared to strong baselines (OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse) and often better accuracy than tree/embedding methods (RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML).

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on dyadic multi-class to binary reductions [1, 16, 28]. Compared to [16], the main additions are (a) the (π, κ)-double sampling to control class imbalance and dyad count, and (b) generalization bounds based on local fractional Rademacher complexities [22, 25], aiming at sharper rates via variance control. The nearest-centroid candidate selection is a pragmatic acceleration for prediction.

Quality:
- Theory: Theorem 1 adapts local Rademacher complexity tools to dependent dyads via fractional covers, and Theorem 2 handles the distribution shift induced by π and the subsampling via κ, with explicit dependencies through α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy. The bounds highlight the benefit of larger κ via a √((K−1)/(Mκ)) term, but also show potential inflation when π is very small (α, β large). This is useful guidance, though an explicit comparison to the earlier bound in [16] would help quantify when the local-complexity bound is tighter and by how much. Some intuition on typical magnitudes of α, β for the chosen π (e.g., π ∝ 1/µ) and a sensitivity plot would make the results more interpretable.
- Empirics: The evaluation is thorough in scale and includes relevant baselines. The proposed method is strong on training-time and memory efficiency and competitive in accuracy/MaF1. However, prediction time remains higher than tree-based methods; the candidate selection helps, but the paper should report q, throughput (docs/sec), and accuracy–speed trade-offs as q varies. Ablations isolating the effects of π, κ, and candidate selection would strengthen the claims.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally clear, but some notation is dense. A brief complexity summary (training and prediction) as functions of M, K, κ, q, and feature costs would aid readers. The mapping φ is described for text with 10 features; a more systematic description and a discussion of how to construct φ in non-text domains would improve clarity.

Originality:
- The contributions are incremental relative to [16]: a principled and practical double sampling strategy and bounds derived with local complexities for dependent dyads. These are valuable but not a substantial departure from the reduction framework.

Significance:
- The approach addresses a pressing practical issue—scalability with long-tailed labels—and demonstrates strong training-time and memory improvements at large K. Its reliance on hand-crafted dyadic features and the relatively slower prediction vs. trees limit immediate impact in latency-critical settings, but the method is promising for batch or nearline scenarios.

Pros:
- Addresses extreme classification with a simple, practical sampling mechanism that directly targets long-tailed label distributions.
- Sound theoretical treatment of dependence and distribution shift with local Rademacher complexity, establishing consistency.
- Comprehensive experiments on very large datasets, with clear advantages in training time and memory and competitive accuracy/MaF1.
- Candidate selection at prediction provides a tunable accuracy–latency trade-off.

Cons:
- Novelty is modest relative to [16]; the core reduction remains the same, with sampling and refined bounds as the main additions.
- Prediction time remains higher than tree-based methods; practical deployments often prioritize latency.
- The dyadic feature map φ is hand-crafted for text; generality to other domains is not demonstrated.
- Comparisons mix batch and online methods; memory usage and runtime are not strictly apples-to-apples.

Questions and suggestions:
- Please compare your new bounds explicitly to [16]: provide a corollary or lemma showing the conditions under which the local-complexity bound is tighter, and quantify the role of α, β in practice for your chosen π.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for κ (training time vs. accuracy/MaF1), π (degree of balancing), and q (prediction time vs. accuracy). An ablation of single vs. double sampling would clarify each component’s contribution.
- Report prediction throughput (e.g., docs/sec) and model size, and detail the candidate selection cost (how centroids are computed and stored; whether distances are in input space or dyadic space).
- Clarify fairness of memory comparisons across batch and online solvers; consider reporting peak memory during training and at inference.
- The LSHTC multilabel-to-multiclass conversion by replication could exacerbate class imbalance; please discuss and, if possible, quantify its effect.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong empirical evidence at unprecedented scales; practical advances in training-time and memory; theoretically grounded.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty over [16] and weaker prediction-time performance than hierarchical/embedding methods; limited evidence of generality beyond text.

Overall, I find the paper promising but borderline due to limited novelty and prediction-time concerns. Strengthening the theoretical comparison to prior bounds and deepening the empirical analysis of κ, π, and q would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
