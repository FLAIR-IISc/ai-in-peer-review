- Paper proposes a reduction of extreme multiclass classification to pairwise (dyadic) binary classification with a scalable, sampling-based algorithm.  
- Critiques common alternatives: tree-based methods suffer cascading errors; label-embedding fails when label matrix is not low-rank.  
- Introduces "Aggressive Double Sampling" with two parameters: inverse-proportional class sampling to upweight rare classes, and uniform sampling of adversarial negatives.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees showing consistency with the original multiclass ERM despite the reduction.  
- Theoretical analysis handles dependence induced by dyadic conversion and oversampling via graph decomposition and Janson concentration inequalities.  
- Derives finite-sample risk bounds that include a bias term from sampling, which decreases linearly with sampled training size.  
- Empirical evaluation on five text datasets shows superior accuracy and F1, especially on datasets with >30,000 classes.  
- Demonstrates much faster training times and lower memory usage compared to baselines.  
- Notes a trade-off: prediction is slightly slower than some competing methods.  
- Inference uses a centroid-based nearest-neighbor heuristic to prune candidate classes before applying pairwise classifiers.  
- Method is well-motivated, technically interesting, and practically useful according to the reviewer.  
- Reviewer finds analysis convincing but did not verify proofs in full detail.
