Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses extreme multi-class classification, where the number of labels can reach 100k. It pursues the reduction-to-binary route by transforming the multi-class problem into a dyadic binary classification problem over example–class pairs. To make this scalable and to mitigate long-tailed class distributions, the authors introduce an aggressive double sampling strategy: (i) class-balanced sampling of training examples via per-class probabilities inversely proportional to empirical class frequencies, and (ii) for each retained example, uniform sampling of κ “adversarial” classes to form dyadic pairs. They also propose a candidate-selection step at prediction time that restricts scoring to q nearest-centroid classes.

In contrast to tree-based methods (e.g., hierarchical softmax variants, FastXML, RecallTree) that offer logarithmic-time prediction but suffer from cascading errors and tree construction issues, and embedding methods (e.g., LEML, SLEEC) that rely on low-rank label structure often violated in extreme settings, the proposed approach stays within a pairwise learning framework akin to prior dyadic reductions, but introduces a principled sampling scheme with generalization guarantees.

Quality:
- Theory: The reduction creates dependencies among dyadic examples sharing the same original instance and shifts the empirical label distribution due to oversampling. The paper tackles both issues. The analysis leverages exact fractional covers and concentration for partially dependent variables (Janson), together with local/fractional Rademacher complexities, to obtain finite-sample risk bounds. The bounds capture the oversampling-induced bias via α and β terms (ratios of true and sampled class probabilities) and yield consistency of ERM under the proposed sampling. The high-level argument—splitting sums of dependent variables into independent blocks and applying Talagrand-type inequalities—appears sound; I have not fully verified the proofs.
- Method: The (π, κ)-DS procedure is simple and scalable. The empirical loss in the sampled dyadic space matches the multiclass ranking loss surrogate. Prediction with nearest-centroid candidate selection is a practical heuristic to control time.
- Experiments: On LSHTC/Dmoz/Wikipedia text datasets (including 50k and 100k classes), the method is competitive or superior in accuracy and macro-F1 once K exceeds roughly 30k, while training faster and using much less memory than OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree methods. The main trade-off is prediction time: candidate selection helps, but pairwise scoring over candidates is slower than tree traversal. Baselines are reasonably tuned on held-out sets. The conversion of multi-label data to multi-class via replication is standard but should be acknowledged as potentially affecting class imbalance.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear, with precise definitions of the dyadic mapping and loss, and explicit algorithms for sampling and prediction. A few suggestions:
- Provide sensitivity analyses for π (class probabilities), κ (number of negatives per example), and q (candidate set size), including accuracy–time trade-offs and the effect on the α, β terms.
- Clarify the function class F used for the dyadic classifier (e.g., linear model, regularization, optimizer).
- Report the recall of the candidate-set heuristic (probability that the true class is in Yx) vs. q to quantify potential loss at prediction time.
- Clean minor typos and formatting issues, and centralize hyperparameter settings in the main text.

Originality:
The aggressive double sampling scheme and the accompanying generalization analysis using local fractional Rademacher complexity in this dyadic setting are the main novel contributions. The candidate selection via nearest centroids is a pragmatic addition. The dyadic feature design for text (10 similarity features inspired by learning-to-rank) is sensible but less novel; the methodological contribution is largely in the reduction and theory.

Significance:
The approach targets a central problem in extreme classification: scalability under long-tailed label distributions. The empirical gains in memory and training time, together with strong predictive performance at very large K, make the method practically valuable. The theoretical treatment of dependence and distribution shift strengthens its scientific contribution.

Pros:
- Scalable reduction with principled double sampling that addresses long-tail imbalance.
- Generalization bounds for dependent dyadic examples under distribution shift, yielding ERM consistency.
- Strong empirical results on large benchmarks: good accuracy and macro-F1, major savings in memory and training time.
- Simple, implementable algorithms (sampling and candidate selection).

Cons:
- Prediction can be slower than tree-based methods; performance depends on the candidate selection heuristic.
- Limited ablation/sensitivity analysis for π, κ, and q; unclear robustness across settings.
- Dependence on the chosen dyadic features φ; generality beyond text (and beyond the 10-feature design) is not empirically demonstrated.
- Exclusion of some embedding baselines is justified by prior work, but a brief empirical check would strengthen claims.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Provide curves showing accuracy/F1 and prediction time as functions of q, and the candidate-set recall.
- Include ablations varying κ and π (e.g., uniform vs. inverse-frequency sampling) to confirm their roles.
- Specify the hypothesis class and optimization details for f (e.g., linear model, regularization).
- Discuss whether the candidate selection can be integrated into the theoretical analysis (e.g., as an approximate inference step).
- Comment on extending φ beyond text (e.g., learned representations) and on how the theory depends on φ.

Overall evaluation:
This is a strong submission. The reduction is simple and effective, the theory addresses real complications of dyadic reductions, and the empirical results are compelling at extreme scale. I recommend acceptance, with the above clarifications to improve clarity and to document the method’s robustness and prediction-time trade-offs.
