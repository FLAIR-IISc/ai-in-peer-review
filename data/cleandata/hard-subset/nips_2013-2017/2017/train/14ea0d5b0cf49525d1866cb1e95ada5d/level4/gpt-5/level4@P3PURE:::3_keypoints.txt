- Proposes a learning reduction for extreme classification (up to ~100k classes) that converts multiclass prediction into pairwise (dyadic) binary classification.  
- Critiques existing approaches: hierarchical trees can be suboptimal and suffer cascading errors; label embeddings fail when the label matrix is not low-rank.  
- Introduces a scalable sampling-based dyadic method with theoretical guarantees establishing consistency with the original multiclass ERM.  
- Empirical results on five text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 for problems with >30k classes, faster training, and much lower memory usage, at the cost of some prediction-time slowdown.  
- Aggressive Double Sampling uses two parameters: per-class sampling frequency set inversely to empirical class frequency to preserve rare classes, and a number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples; sampling is repeated to train the final classifier.  
- At prediction time, exhaustive pairwise evaluation is avoided by proposing candidate classes via class centroids in input space and running pairwise classification only among these candidates.  
- Theoretical analysis addresses dependence introduced by dyadic construction and distribution shift from oversampling by decomposing sums via a graph structure and applying concentration bounds for partially dependent variables (Janson); finite-sample risk bounds contain bias terms that decay linearly with sample sizes.  
- Overall assessment: a theoretically grounded, practically useful method combining low memory/computation with state-of-the-art performance on the largest datasets.
