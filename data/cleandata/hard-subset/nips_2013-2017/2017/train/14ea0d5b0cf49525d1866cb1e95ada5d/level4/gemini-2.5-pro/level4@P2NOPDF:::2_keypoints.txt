- Proposes a reduction from multi-class to binary classification by learning a score over (input, class) pairs, with loss based on fraction of incorrect classes ranked above the true class.  
- Maps (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional feature space via phi and learns a linear scoring function in that space.  
- Builds on Joshi et al. (2015) which creates K-1 binary comparisons per example, and addresses the resulting correlated training examples.  
- Provides improved generalization analysis using fractional Rademacher complexity tailored to the correlated-data structure.  
- Introduces two sampling techniques to scale to very large K: class-rebalancing sampling and sampling a small random subset of negatives per example.  
- Shows theoretically that the sampling procedures can be incorporated into the generalization bounds with limited degradation.  
- Presents strong empirical results, outperforming or matching state-of-the-art baselines on large-scale classification tasks.  
- Strength: addresses an important extreme multi-class problem with a practical, theoretically-grounded reduction and thorough empirical validation.  
- Novelty: analysis and practical sampling methods that make a known reduction feasible for millions of classes.  
- Omission: lacks theoretical analysis of the practical prediction heuristic (nearest-centroid candidate selection) used at test time.  
- Suggestion: paper should compare its sample complexity bounds to standard multi-class bounds (e.g., one-vs-all, tree methods) to contextualize dependence on K and sampling parameters.
