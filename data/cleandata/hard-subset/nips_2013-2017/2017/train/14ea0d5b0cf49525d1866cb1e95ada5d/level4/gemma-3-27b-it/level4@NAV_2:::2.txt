This paper proposes a novel reduction from multi-class classification, particularly suited for scenarios with a very large number of classes, to a binary classification problem. The authors consider a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, employing a loss function that quantifies the fraction of incorrectly labeled classes receiving higher scores than the true class. Specifically, they utilize a feature transformation φ to map these pairs into a p-dimensional space, learning a subsequent mapping from R^p to scores. Their reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which constructs K-1 transformed points for each data point (x,y), representing comparisons between the true label y and incorrect labels. Recognizing the correlated nature of examples in the transformed dataset – a key challenge – this paper presents an improved generalization analysis leveraging recent bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity. A second core contribution lies in demonstrating the efficacy of sampling techniques during the reduction process to balance class distributions and reduce dataset size. The authors propose sampling classes (with replacement) to achieve balanced representation and, instead of considering all K-1 class comparisons, employ a random sample. They theoretically demonstrate that these sampling procedures maintain generalization performance. Finally, they empirically demonstrate competitive performance against established baselines.

This paper introduces a compelling reduction from large-scale multi-class to binary classification, accompanied by relevant generalization bounds addressing the correlated data inherent in the reduction. The primary novelty resides in the proposed sampling strategy for rebalancing class distributions and reducing dataset size, building on the foundational reduction presented by Joshi et al. (2015). The empirical evaluation showcases strong performance compared to existing algorithms.

Strengths: The paper addresses a significant problem in machine learning – extreme multi-class classification – with a technically sound and well-motivated approach. The theoretical analysis, building on recent advances in Rademacher complexity, is a key strength. The empirical results on challenging datasets demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method. The writing is generally clear and the paper is well-organized.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the candidate selection strategy detailed in Section 2.3, where prediction is restricted to a subset of k nearest-centroid classes. A discussion of the potential impact of this approximation on the overall performance would be valuable. Furthermore, a comparative analysis of the derived sample complexity bounds with those established for non-transformed multi-class problems would enhance the paper’s contribution.

Arguments for acceptance:
*   Novel and well-motivated approach to extreme multi-class classification.
*   Sound theoretical analysis with relevant generalization bounds.
*   Strong empirical performance on benchmark datasets.
*   Addresses a practical challenge of scalability in multi-class learning.

Arguments against acceptance:
*   Lack of theoretical analysis of the candidate selection strategy.
*   Absence of a comparative analysis with generalization bounds for non-transformed problems.

Overall Assessment:
This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. The technical contributions are solid, the empirical results are compelling, and the writing is clear. While the identified weaknesses are not critical, addressing them would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance.
