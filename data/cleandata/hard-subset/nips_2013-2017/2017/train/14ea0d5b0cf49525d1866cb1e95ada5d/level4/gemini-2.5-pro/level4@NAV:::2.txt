**Summary:**
This work introduces a novel reduction from multi-class to binary classification, specifically designed for scenarios with a large number of classes. The approach is based on a hypothesis that assigns a score to each (input, class) pair, with a loss function defined as the proportion of incorrect classes that are scored higher than the correct class. Specifically, the method employs a feature transformation, `phi`, to map each (input, class) pair into a p-dimensional feature vector, and a scoring function is then learned on this feature space.

This reduction builds upon the work of Joshi et al. (2015), where each data point (x,y) is expanded into K-1 transformed instances, with each instance representing a comparison between the true label y and an incorrect label y'. A key challenge with this approach is that the transformed dataset contains correlated examples, precluding the use of standard generalization bounds. The authors' first main contribution is to address this with an improved generalization analysis, surpassing that of Joshi et al. (2015) by deriving new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity for this setting. The second primary contribution is the introduction of sampling strategies to both balance the class distribution and reduce the size of the final training set. Specifically, the authors employ two techniques: sampling classes (with replacement) to ensure more uniform representation in the transformed data, and sampling a subset of the K-1 incorrect classes for comparison instead of using all of them. The paper demonstrates that these sampling procedures can be integrated without significantly weakening the derived generalization bounds. Finally, empirical results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves highly competitive performance when compared against several strong baseline algorithms.

**Comments:**
The paper offers a compelling reduction from large-scale multi-class classification to a binary setting, supported by generalization bounds that account for the correlated nature of the transformed data. The primary innovation lies in the use of sampling to rebalance class distributions and manage the size of the resulting dataset, building upon the foundational reduction from Joshi et al. The experimental evaluation provides strong evidence of the method's effectiveness against existing approaches.

A notable omission is a theoretical analysis of the prediction-time algorithm presented in Section 2.3. This heuristic, which limits comparisons to the k classes with the nearest centroids to an input point, lacks a formal justification for its impact on performance.

Furthermore, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the derived sample complexity bounds. In particular, a comparison of these bounds to those for standard, non-reduced multi-class classification problems would provide valuable context and better highlight the trade-offs of the proposed reduction.
