This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification, a multiclass classification setting where the output space can contain up to 100,000 different classes.

Many existing approaches to extreme classification rely on one of two main strategies. The first involves inferring a tree structure over the output labels (as in hierarchical softmax), which reduces prediction to a logarithmic number of binary classifications. The second is a label embedding approach, where embeddings are learned efficiently via methods like least-squares or sampling. The authors highlight the limitations of these common approaches: inferred latent trees may be suboptimal, leading to cascading errors, while label embedding methods can suffer from high prediction error when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

This paper explores an alternative strategy: reducing extreme classification to a series of pairwise binary classification problems. Within this framework, the authors propose a scalable, sampling-based method that comes with theoretical guarantees of consistency with the original multiclass Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) formulation.

The authors conduct experiments on five text datasets, comparing their method against several competing approaches (including standard one-vs-all, where computationally feasible). The results show that the proposed method is generally superior in terms of accuracy and F1-score on datasets with more than 30,000 classes. Furthermore, it trains faster and uses significantly less memory, though its prediction speed is somewhat slower than some baselines.

The proposed method, named 'Aggressive Double Sampling,' is controlled by two main parameters. The first governs the sampling frequency for each class, which is set to be inversely proportional to the empirical class frequency. This oversampling strategy for rare classes helps prevent them from being missed in the long tail. The second parameter determines the number of 'adversarial' examples to be drawn uniformly. This sampling procedure is repeated to train the final set of dyadic classifiers.

At prediction time, inference is challenging because generating pairwise comparisons with all possible classes is intractable. To overcome this, the authors employ a heuristic to identify a smaller set of candidate classes. This involves creating a centroid for each class in the input space by averaging the feature vectors of its members. Prediction is then performed by running pairwise classifications only against this candidate set.

The theoretical analysis is non-trivial. The reduction from multiclass to dyadic binary classification introduces a particular dependence structure among the sampled examples, transforming a sum of independent random variables into a sum of dependent ones. A further complication arises from the oversampling of rare classes, which shifts the data distribution. The authors' proof cleverly addresses this by splitting the sum of dependent examples into several sums of independent variables, based on the graph structure induced by the sampling process. They then apply concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables (due to Janson). While the resulting finite-sample risk bound contains a bias term related to the ratio between the true and sampling probabilities, this bias is shown to decrease linearly with the size of the training set. The analysis appears sound, although I have not verified the proofs in detail.

Overall, this paper presents a strong contribution to the field of extreme classification. The proposed method achieves superior performance, particularly on the largest datasets, while maintaining small memory and computational footprints during training. The binary reduction algorithm is theoretically sound, interesting, and should prove highly useful to practitioners working with massive-scale classification problems.
