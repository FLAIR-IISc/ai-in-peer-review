- Addresses extreme multiclass classification and notes drawbacks of label-tree methods (cascading errors) and label-embedding approaches (fail on non-low-rank label matrices).
- Proposes reducing extreme classification to pairwise binary classification via a scalable, sampling-based method with consistency guarantees relative to multiclass ERM.
- Introduces "Aggressive Double Sampling" with two parameters: inverse-frequency sampling to upsample rare classes and uniform sampling of a specified number of "adversarial" examples.
- Uses a prediction heuristic that first finds nearest class centroids to produce a small candidate set, then runs pairwise classifiers only against those candidates.
- Theoretical analysis handles dependencies and distribution shift by decomposing sums using the sampling-induced graph and applying Janson-style concentration inequalities; yields a finite-sample risk bound with a bias term that shrinks linearly with training size.
- Empirical results on five text datasets show superior accuracy and F1 for datasets with >30k classes, much faster and lower-memory training, but somewhat slower prediction.
- Overall assessment: a strong, practical, and theoretically grounded method that is valuable for practitioners tackling extreme classification.
