This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification—multiclass prediction with output spaces of up to 100k classes.

Many existing approaches build a latent tree over labels (e.g., hierarchical softmax), enabling logarithmic-time inference via a sequence of binary decisions, or use label embeddings learned efficiently via least squares or sampling. The authors highlight well-known limitations of these strategies: tree structures can be suboptimal and suffer from cascading errors, while label embeddings can underperform when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

As an alternative, the paper reduces extreme classification to pairwise (dyadic) binary classification. Within this framework, the authors propose a scalable sampling-based method and provide theoretical guarantees establishing consistency with the original multiclass ERM formulation.

Experiments on five text datasets, including comparisons to several strong baselines (and one-vs-all where computationally feasible), show that the proposed method generally attains superior accuracy and F1 once the number of classes exceeds 30k. It also trains faster and uses substantially less memory, though it incurs some loss in prediction speed.

The Aggressive Double Sampling reduction is governed by two parameters. The first controls the per-class sampling frequency, set inversely proportional to empirical class frequency to avoid missing rare classes in the long tail. The second specifies the number of adversarial examples drawn uniformly. This sampling procedure is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier.

At prediction time, exhaustive pairwise feature generation over all classes is intractable. The authors therefore use a heuristic to propose candidate classes by representing each class via a centroid in input space (the mean of the vectors in that class), and then perform pairwise classification only among these candidates.

The theoretical analysis is nontrivial because the reduction from multiclass to dyadic binary classification transforms a sum of independent random variables into a sum with a specific dependence structure across dyadic examples. A further complication arises from oversampling rare classes, which shifts the empirical distribution. The proof strategy decomposes the sum over dependent examples into multiple sums over independent subsets based on the graph structure induced by the construction, and then applies concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables due to Janson. Although the finite-sample risk bound is biased by ratios between true and oversampled class probabilities, these biases decrease linearly with the sizes of the sampled and resampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, though I have not verified the proof in detail.

Overall, this paper presents a strong method for extreme classification, combining low memory and computational requirements with state-of-the-art performance on the largest datasets. The binary reduction algorithm is theoretically well-motivated and practically useful.
