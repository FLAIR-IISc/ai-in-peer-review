- Tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to binary classification over example-class pairs for large-scale, long-tailed settings.  
- Proposes an aggressive double sampling strategy that balances class representation and reduces the number of dyadic pairs.  
- Extends Joshi et al. (2015) by strategically sampling both classes and pairs to address computational bottlenecks.  
- Provides consistency of empirical risk minimization under the proposed sampling scheme.  
- Derives new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity to handle dependency in transformed data.  
- Demonstrates competitive training/prediction time, memory use, and accuracy on large benchmarks (DMOZ and Wikipedia).  
- Core contribution is the practical double sampling strategy that scales binary-reduction methods to extreme settings.  
- Strength: addresses a timely and important problem with a novel, well-motivated approach.  
- Strength: solid theoretical grounding with bounds that account for sample dependency.  
- Strength: strong empirical evidence against state-of-the-art baselines.  
- Strength: paper is well-written and clearly organized.  
- Weakness: candidate selection strategy (k nearest-centroid classes) lacks theoretical or empirical analysis of its impact.  
- Weakness: no comparison of sample complexity bounds with those for non-transformed multi-class formulations.  
- Weakness: dependence on prior work (Joshi et al. 2015) could use clearer distinction of novel contributions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, empirically effective, and well-presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unexplored candidate selection analysis and missing sample-complexity comparisons limit the work’s evaluation.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution with minor gaps; recommend acceptance.
