Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary learning problem over dyadic (example, class) pairs. Using a joint feature map φ(x, y), the method learns a scorer f(φ(x, y)) and optimizes a pairwise ranking-style loss that counts the fraction of impostor classes scoring above the true class. This builds on prior dyadic reductions (e.g., Joshi et al., 2015) where each training point (x, y) produces K−1 paired comparisons against all other classes. Because these dyadic examples are dependent, standard i.i.d. generalization tools are not directly applicable.

The paper contributes two main advances. First, it derives new generalization bounds for the dyadic reduction using fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities, tightening prior analyses based on fractional Rademacher complexity for interdependent data. Second, it proposes an aggressive double sampling scheme, (π, κ)-DS: (i) sample examples per class with probabilities inversely proportional to class frequency to mitigate long-tailed label distributions, and (ii) for each kept example, compare only against κ randomly selected “adversarial” classes instead of all K−1. The authors prove that ERM over the subsampled dyadic set remains consistent and provide explicit risk bounds that account for distribution shift and dependence.

At prediction time, they further accelerate inference by selecting a small set of candidate classes per test example via nearest class centroids in the dyadic space, evaluating f only on that shortlist. Experiments on LSHTC/DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets with 10K–100K classes show competitive or superior macro-F1 and accuracy, while substantially reducing training time, prediction time, and memory compared with OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding-based extreme classification methods such as FastXML, PfastReXML, and RecallTree.

Strengths:
- Technical: Sound reduction framework with careful treatment of dependence via fractional covers and local Rademacher complexity; extensions beyond prior dyadic analyses.
- Practical: Simple, scalable sampling strategy that greatly reduces dyadic set size and balances class frequencies, offering strong wall-clock and memory gains.
- Empirical: Comprehensive comparison on large benchmarks with clear wins or parity in predictive metrics, especially macro-F1, alongside substantial efficiency benefits.
- Clarity of algorithms: The (π, κ)-DS procedure and candidate selection at prediction are described clearly and are easy to implement. Code availability is a plus.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Prediction-time candidate selection lacks theoretical analysis. How does restricting to q nearest centroids impact excess risk or top-1 accuracy? A bound relating q, κ, and the margin distribution would strengthen the contribution.
- Sensitivity/ablation: Although κ is central to the trade-off between efficiency and accuracy, systematic ablations on κ (and on the class-sampling schedule π) are limited. Guidance for choosing κ and q would help practitioners.
- Assumptions in bounds: The constants α and β depend inversely on π and on class proportions η, potentially becoming large for extreme imbalance. Discussion of practical ranges and how to set π to keep α, β controlled would clarify applicability.
- Feature map φ: The study uses hand-crafted, text-specific features. A brief exploration of learned φ (e.g., linear metrics or simple neural encoders) or cross-domain results would broaden impact.
- Evaluation details: Some datasets were converted from multilabel to multiclass by replicating instances; this can distort class priors and macro-F1. A short discussion of potential biases and whether results change under alternative conversions would be useful.

Quality:
- The theoretical results are carefully stated and appear technically sound, with appropriate use of tools for dependent data (fractional covers, local Rademacher complexities). While full proofs are in the supplement, the main statements are plausible and relevant to the proposed sampling scheme.
- Empirical claims are supported by experiments on large, relevant datasets, with strong baselines. Reporting includes accuracy, macro-F1, time, and memory, which matches the paper’s stated goals.
- One gap is the lack of theory or controlled study for the candidate-selection step, which is important to the practical speedups.

Clarity:
- Overall well-organized and readable. The problem, reduction, and algorithms are presented clearly.
- Minor suggestions: define all constants (α, β, η) early in Theorem 2; state explicitly whether class and negative sampling are with or without replacement; consolidate notation for losses (ranking vs. 0/1 surrogate); and fix scattered typos/formatting issues.

Originality:
- The base dyadic reduction is known, but the combination of aggressive double sampling with new generalization bounds tailored to the subsampled, dependent setting is original. The deployment of local Rademacher complexity in this context is a novel and meaningful refinement.

Significance:
- Extreme classification is an active area at NIPS/NeurIPS. The method offers a practical and theoretically grounded alternative to OVA, trees, and low-rank embeddings, with clear benefits on long-tailed data. The simplicity of (π, κ)-DS makes adoption likely.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong scalability and predictive performance on 10K–100K class benchmarks.
- Solid theory addressing dependence and sampling-induced distribution shift.
- Simple, implementable method with code available.
- Relevant to a broad set of extreme classification problems.

Cons against acceptance:
- No theoretical guarantee for the prediction-time candidate selection; limited ablation on κ and π.
- Text-specific φ may limit immediate generality; cross-domain validation is minimal.
- Bounds involve constants that may be loose in highly imbalanced regimes.

Questions for authors:
- Can you provide ablations showing performance versus κ and q, and guidance for setting them?
- Is there an analysis linking candidate shortlist size q to accuracy (e.g., via centroid-margin arguments)?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of π and to oversampling with/without replacement?
- Do the conclusions hold with learned φ or in non-text domains?

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a practical, well-motivated method with sound theory and compelling empirical results on large-scale datasets. Addressing the candidate-selection analysis and providing more ablations would further strengthen the contribution.
