

This paper introduces a learning reduction for extreme classification, a multiclass problem with output spaces exceeding 100,000 classes. Existing approaches often rely on hierarchical softmax, which infers a tree structure over output labels to reduce predictions to logarithmic binary classifications, or label embedding methods learned via least-squares or sampling. The authors identify key limitations: the inferred tree may be suboptimal, causing cascading errors, and label embedding may fail when the true label matrix is not low-rank.  

The authors adopt an alternative framework, reducing extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. They propose a scalable sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees of consistency with the original multiclass ERM formulation. Experiments on five text datasets demonstrate that their method outperforms competitors (including computationally feasible one-vs-all classification) in accuracy and F1 score once the class count exceeds 30,000. It also trains faster and uses significantly less memory, though prediction speed is slightly lower.  

The Aggressive Double Sampling reduction employs two parameters: one controls class sampling frequency (inversely proportional to empirical class frequency to prevent missing tail classes), and the other sets the number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples. This procedure trains the final dyadic classifier through repeated iterations.  

At prediction time, pairwise feature generation with all classes is intractable. The authors use a heuristic: each class is represented by the centroid of its input vectors, and prediction proceeds via pairwise classification only over candidate classes identified by this centroid representation.  

Theoretical analysis faces two complications: (1) the reduction transforms a sum of independent random variables into a sum with a specific dependence structure across dyadic examples, and (2) rare class oversampling shifts the empirical distribution. The proof addresses these by decomposing the dependent sum into independent sums using the graph structure induced by example construction, then applying Janson's concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables. While the finite-sample risk bound is biased by ratios between true and oversampled class probabilities, these biases decrease linearly with the size of the sampled and re-sampled training sets. The analysis appears sound, though the detailed proof verification was not completed.  

Overall, this paper presents a strong solution for extreme classification, offering minimal memory and computational demands alongside superior performance on the largest datasets. The binary reduction algorithm is theoretically sound, novel, and practically valuable.
