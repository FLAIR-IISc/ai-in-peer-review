- Addresses extreme multi-class classification by reducing to a binary task over pairs and building on Joshi et al. (2015).
- Proposes an "aggressive double sampling" strategy: resample training to over-sample rare classes and under-sample common ones.
- Second part of sampling: for each example, create only a small random subset of the K−1 negative pairs to improve scalability.
- Provides theoretical analysis using local fractional Rademacher complexities to derive new generalization bounds.
- Shows ERM consistency despite pairwise data dependencies and distribution shift introduced by sampling.
- Empirical evaluation on large-scale text datasets (up to 100k classes) demonstrates competitive or superior predictive performance.
- Experimental results also show improvements in training time and memory consumption compared to state-of-the-art baselines.
- Core novelty and significance lie in the double sampling technique addressing computational and statistical challenges.
- Theoretical contribution is valuable for accounting for dependencies and sampling-induced shifts.
- Paper is well-written and the method and sampling strategy are clearly explained.
- Weakness: theoretical analysis omits the prediction-time heuristic that restricts search to nearest-centroid candidates.
- Weakness: lacks comparison or contextualization of the derived sample complexity bounds versus standard multi-class learners.
