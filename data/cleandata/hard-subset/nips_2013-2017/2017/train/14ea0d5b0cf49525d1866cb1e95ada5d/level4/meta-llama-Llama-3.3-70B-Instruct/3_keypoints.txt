- Proposes a novel reduction-based approach for extreme classification (up to 100k classes) that converts the problem to pairwise binary classification.
- Highlights limitations of hierarchical softmax (cascading errors from suboptimal trees) and label embedding (fails when label matrix is not low-rank).
- Introduces a scalable, sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees ensuring consistency with multiclass ERM.
- Empirical results on five text datasets show the method generally outperforms competitors in accuracy and F1 when classes exceed ~30k.
- Method trains faster and uses significantly less memory, though prediction is slightly slower.
- Proposes "Aggressive Double Sampling" controlled by two parameters: class sampling frequency (inverse to empirical class frequency) and number of uniformly drawn adversarial examples.
- Repeats the sampling process to train a final dyadic classifier.
- Uses a centroid-based candidate selection heuristic at prediction time to avoid generating pairwise features for all classes.
- Theoretical analysis is complex due to dependencies among dyadic examples and distribution shift from oversampling.
- Proof technique decomposes dependent sums via the induced graph structure and applies concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables (Janson); finite-sample risk bound is biased but bias decreases linearly with sampled/re-sampled set sizes.
- Overall assessment: method is theoretically sound, memory- and compute-efficient, and practically useful for very large extreme classification tasks.
