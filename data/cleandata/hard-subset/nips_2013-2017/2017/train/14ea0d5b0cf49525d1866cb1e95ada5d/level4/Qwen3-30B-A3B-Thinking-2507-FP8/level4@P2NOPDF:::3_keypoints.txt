- Problem: Tackles extreme multiclass classification with output spaces >100,000 by reducing to pairwise binary classification.
- Limitations of prior work: Hierarchical/tree methods risk cascading errors; label-embedding fails when label matrix is not low-rank.
- Main contribution: Proposes Aggressive Double Sampling (ADS), a scalable sampling-based reduction with theoretical consistency guarantees.
- ADS design: Two parameters—inverse-frequency sampling to preserve rare classes, and uniform adversarial example sampling during training.
- Prediction heuristic: Represent each class by centroid of input features and run pairwise classification over a centroid-derived candidate subset to avoid full comparisons.
- Theoretical analysis: Handles dependent loss terms and oversampling distortions via graph decomposition and concentration inequalities (Janson 2004).
- Risk bound: Provides a finite-sample bound with bias terms scaling with true/oversampled class probabilities that decay linearly with sample size.
- Empirical performance: On five text datasets, ADS outperforms baselines for class counts >30,000 in accuracy and F1, while cutting training time and memory.
- Scalability: ADS scales linearly with class size, contrasting with logarithmic/quadratic scaling of tree-based and embedding methods.
- Efficiency trade-offs: Training is much faster and memory-light; prediction latency is slightly higher but remains practical for large-scale use.
- Implementation and parameterization: Sampling parameters are well-justified and the implementation is efficient with minimal memory footprint.
- Novelty and impact: The reduction framework is novel, sidesteps prior pitfalls, and is broadly applicable in real-world systems.
- Proof caveat: Proof strategy appears sound, though the reviewer did not verify all proof details.
- Minor limitation: Not optimized for latency-critical prediction settings.
- Overall verdict: Theoretical grounding, practical scalability, and strong empirical results make the paper a strong NeurIPS submission.
