This paper proposes a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, particularly suited for scenarios with an extremely large number of classes. The authors consider a hypothesis that maps (input, class) pairs to scores, utilizing a loss function that counts the fraction of incorrect classes with higher scores than the true class. By employing a feature transformation φ that maps (input, class) pairs to a p-dimensional feature space, they learn a mapping from R^p to scores.

The proposed reduction extends the work of Joshi et al. (2015), which creates K-1 transformed points for each data point (x, y), intuitively corresponding to the comparison of label y with some incorrect label y'. Given the correlated nature of the transformed dataset, standard generalization bounds are inapplicable. The paper's first contribution is an improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al. (2015), leveraging new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity in this setting.

The second main contribution is the demonstration that sampling techniques can be employed during the reduction to balance class distributions and decrease the training set size. Specifically, the authors sample each class (with replacement) to ensure roughly equal representation in the transformed training data and use a random sample of classes instead of including transformed points for all K-1 possible class comparisons. They show that these sampling procedures do not drastically affect their generalization bounds.

The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation, demonstrating that the proposed method performs competitively against several strong baselines. The results suggest that the method is least affected by long-tailed class distributions, primarily due to the sampling strategy's ability to balance the training set and reduce the binary dataset to contain a similar number of positive and negative examples.

Strengths:

* The paper presents a novel reduction from multi-class classification to binary classification, which is particularly suitable for scenarios with an extremely large number of classes.
* The authors provide an improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al. (2015), leveraging new bounds on the fractional Rademacher complexity in this setting.
* The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of sampling techniques in balancing class distributions and reducing the training set size.
* The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the results are competitive against several strong baselines.

Weaknesses:

* The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the impact of the prediction algorithm given in section 2.3, where comparisons are only performed for the k classes whose centroids are closest to a given input point.
* Additional discussion of the sample complexity bounds, possibly comparing them to the generalization bounds obtained for the non-transformed multi-class problems, would be appreciated.
