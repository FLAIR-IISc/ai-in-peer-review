This paper proposes a novel approach to multi-class classification with a very large number of classes, framing it as a binary classification problem through a double sampling strategy. The core idea builds upon prior work reducing multi-class problems to binary ones by creating pairs of examples and classes [16], but addresses the challenges of imbalanced class distributions and computational cost inherent in extreme multi-class scenarios. The authors present a method that first samples the original training data to balance class representation and then further samples the generated pairs, aiming to reduce the dataset size while maintaining generalization performance. They provide new generalization bounds leveraging local fractional Rademacher complexity to account for the induced dependencies in the sampled data and demonstrate strong empirical results on large-scale text classification datasets.

The paper effectively summarizes existing approaches to extreme multi-class classification, contrasting tree-based [4, 3, 6, 7, 9, 21, 5, 15] and embedding-based methods [11, 5, 19] with binary reduction techniques [1, 28, 16]. It correctly identifies the limitations of one-vs-all (OVA) [18] in terms of scalability and class imbalance. The contribution lies in the aggressive double sampling strategy, offering a practical solution to the computational burden and skewed distributions often encountered in real-world datasets. The theoretical analysis, providing generalization bounds for the proposed method, is a valuable addition and demonstrates a sound understanding of the underlying principles.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a relevant problem:** Extreme multi-class classification is a challenging problem with growing importance in areas like text categorization and recommendation systems.
*   **Novelty:** The proposed double sampling strategy, combining class balancing and pair reduction, is a practical and insightful contribution.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The derivation of generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity provides a strong theoretical justification for the approach.
*   **Strong empirical results:** The extensive experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the method, achieving competitive performance against state-of-the-art baselines. The results clearly show improvements in training/prediction time and memory usage.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited analysis of candidate selection:** While the candidate selection strategy (Section 2.3) is mentioned for improving prediction speed, the paper lacks a detailed theoretical analysis of its impact on the overall performance. A discussion of the trade-off between accuracy and computational efficiency would be beneficial.
*   **Sample complexity bounds:** A comparison of the derived sample complexity bounds to existing generalization bounds for non-transformed multi-class problems would strengthen the theoretical contribution and provide a better understanding of the method's efficiency.
*   **Dependency on feature mapping:** The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the feature mapping φ. While the paper provides a concrete example for text classification, a broader discussion of the applicability and potential limitations of this mapping in different contexts would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a significant problem in machine learning with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the methodology and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The analysis of the candidate selection strategy is limited.
*   The lack of comparison to sample complexity bounds for non-transformed problems represents a missed opportunity for further insight. 

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. The proposed method is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2017.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
