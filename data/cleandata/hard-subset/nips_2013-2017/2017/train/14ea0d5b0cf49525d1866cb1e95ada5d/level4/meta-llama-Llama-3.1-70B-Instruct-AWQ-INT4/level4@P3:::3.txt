This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification, specifically multiclass classification where the output space ranges up to 100k different classes. The authors address the problem of large-scale multi-class classification, which is a popular branch of machine learning with applications in tagging, recommendation, and ranking.

The authors note that many approaches to extreme classification rely on inferring a tree structure among the output labels, such as the hierarchical softmax, so that only a logarithmic number of binary predictions need to be made to infer the output class. Another approach is label embedding, which can be learned efficiently through least-squares or sampling. However, the authors point out the problems with these approaches: the inferred latent tree may not be optimal and lead to cascading errors, and the label embedding approach may lead to prediction errors when the true label matrix is not low-rank.

An alternative approach is to reduce extreme classification to pairwise binary classification. The authors operate in this framework and present a scalable sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees as to its consistency with the original multiclass ERM formulation. The proposed method, called Aggressive Double Sampling (ADS), consists of two main steps: (1) oversampling the most small-size classes and subsampling the few big-size classes in the original training set, and (2) reducing the number of dyadic examples by drawing uniformly a set of adversarial classes.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the ADS reduction, which is complicated by the fact that the reduction from multi-class to dyadic binary classification changes a sum of independent random variables into a sum of random variables with a particular dependence structure among the dyadic examples. The other complication is caused by the oversampling of rare classes, shifting the empirical distribution. The proof involves splitting the sum of dependent examples into several sums of independent variables based on the graph structure induced by the example construction, and using the concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables introduced by Janson.

The authors evaluate their method on five text datasets, comparing it to several competing approaches, including standard one-vs-all classification when that is computationally feasible. The results show that the ADS method is generally superior in terms of accuracy and F1 once the number of classes goes above 30k, and trains faster while using far less memory, although it suffers somewhat in terms of prediction speed.

The strengths of this paper include:

* The authors present a novel and scalable method for extreme classification, which is a challenging problem in machine learning.
* The method has theoretical guarantees as to its consistency with the original multiclass ERM formulation.
* The experimental results show that the method is superior to several competing approaches in terms of accuracy and F1, especially when the number of classes is large.

The weaknesses of this paper include:

* The method requires careful tuning of two parameters, which control the frequency of sampling each class and the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly.
* The prediction speed of the method is somewhat slower than some of the competing approaches.

Overall, this paper presents a strong method for extreme classification, with small memory and computational requirements and superior performance on especially the largest datasets. The binary reduction algorithm appears theoretically sound, interesting, and useful to practitioners.
