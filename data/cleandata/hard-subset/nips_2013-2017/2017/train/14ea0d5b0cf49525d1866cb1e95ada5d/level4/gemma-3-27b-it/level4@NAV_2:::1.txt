This paper addresses the challenging problem of multi-class classification with a very large number of classes, proposing a double sampling strategy built upon the multi-class to binary reduction framework of [16]. The core idea is to mitigate the curse of long-tailed class distributions, common in such settings, and reduce computational costs by intelligently sampling both instances and pairs during the reduction process. The authors support their method with both theoretical analysis, providing new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexity, and empirical evaluation on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia).

The paper's strengths lie in its comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors compare their approach against a strong set of baselines – including OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PD-Sparse – demonstrating competitive performance in terms of both accuracy/F1-score and, importantly, computational efficiency (training time and memory consumption). The reported results on datasets with up to 100,000 classes are particularly compelling, showcasing the scalability of the proposed method. The problem setting is also highly relevant, as extreme multi-class classification is increasingly important in applications like text categorization, recommendation systems, and information retrieval. The theoretical contribution, deriving generalization bounds for the sampled reduction, adds rigor to the approach and justifies its consistency. 

However, the paper’s novelty is somewhat incremental. While the double sampling strategy and the refined generalization bounds are valuable contributions, the work builds heavily on the foundation laid by [16]. The improvements, while significant in practice, may not represent a paradigm shift in the field. A further concern is the prediction time. While the authors demonstrate reduced training time, they acknowledge that prediction can still be relatively slow. In many real-world applications, prediction latency is a critical factor, and this aspect deserves more thorough discussion. The impact of the candidate selection strategy introduced in Section 2.3, and its effect on prediction time, could be further investigated. 

Several points could benefit from clarification. The practical interpretation and comparative utility of the generalization bounds presented (Theorem 2) remain somewhat unclear.  How do these bounds compare to those derived in related work, like [16, 22]? Furthermore, the experimental setup mixes batch learning (e.g., M-SVM) and online learning (e.g., RecallTree) algorithms, leading to potentially unfair comparisons in terms of memory usage. A more nuanced discussion of these differences would be beneficial.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and increasingly relevant problem in machine learning.
- Presents a practical and scalable approach with strong empirical results.
- Provides theoretical justification for the proposed method via novel generalization bounds.
- Offers a clear and well-written exposition of the method and experiments.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental novelty; builds heavily on prior work ([16]).
- Prediction time remains a concern, potentially limiting practical applicability.
- The interpretation and practical relevance of the generalization bounds are not fully elucidated.
- Experimental comparison could be improved by considering the different nature of batch vs. online learning methods.
