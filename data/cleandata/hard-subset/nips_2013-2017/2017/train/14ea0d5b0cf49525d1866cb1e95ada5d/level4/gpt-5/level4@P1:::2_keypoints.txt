- Summary: Reduces extreme multiclass to binary dyadic pairs, learns joint (input,class) scoring with φ, and introduces (π,κ)-double sampling plus q-nearest-centroid pruning; provides improved generalization bounds for dependent dyadic data and shows strong empirical performance up to 100K classes.

- Strength: Provides sound theory for dependent dyadic data via fractional/local Rademacher complexities, yielding variance-sensitive bounds and consistency under double sampling.

- Strength: (π,κ)-DS is practical and scalable, addressing class imbalance and quadratic pair blow-up to reduce memory and time.

- Strength: Strong empirical results on large Wikipedia/LSHTC datasets — competitive or superior accuracy/MaF1 with lower time and memory than OVA/M-SVM and often comparable to tree methods.

- Strength: Clearly connected to prior dyadic reduction work and contrasts appropriately with tree and embedding approaches.

- Weakness: Dyadic reduction itself is not novel (inherited from Joshi et al.), with main algorithmic novelty limited to the sampling scheme.

- Weakness: No theoretical analysis of the candidate pruning (q-nearest-centroid) step and its effect on consistency or top-1 error.

- Weakness: Lacks explicit comparison of the derived sample-complexity/generalization bounds to those for non-reduced multiclass formulations.

- Quality: Methodology and analysis for dependent samples are technically sound and empirical evaluation is thorough on strong baselines.

- Clarity: Paper is generally clear and well-organized; role of (π,κ,q) is operationally explained but could better connect to theorem terms α and β.

- Originality: Theoretical refinements for dependent dyadic data and consistency under double sampling are original; algorithmic novelty is incremental.

- Significance: Practically significant for extreme classification (50K–100K classes) with useful theoretical tools for dependent samples.

- Pro-accept: Strong empirical scalability and performance on large benchmarks.

- Pro-accept: Solid theoretical guarantees for a practically motivated sampling scheme with dependent dyadic examples.

- Pro-accept: Advances prior dyadic reductions in analysis and efficiency.

- Con-accept: Main algorithmic novelty is incremental relative to prior dyadic work.

- Con-accept: No theoretical guarantees for the prediction-time candidate selection.

- Con-accept: Limited discussion comparing derived bounds to standard multiclass analyses.

- Suggestion: Provide theoretical or empirical guarantees for candidate pruning (probability true class retained as function of q or centroid-margin relations).

- Suggestion: Explain how α and β in Theorem 2 scale with π and class distribution; give tuning guidance and sensitivity analysis for π and κ.

- Suggestion: Add comparison/discussion relating your bounds to standard multiclass generalization results to clarify trade-offs of the dyadic approach.
