### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of extreme multi-class classification, where the number of classes is very large and often follows a long-tailed distribution. The proposed method builds upon a prior multi-class to binary reduction strategy [16], which transforms the problem into a binary classification task over dyadic pairs of examples and classes. The key challenge with this reduction is the massive expansion of the training data, which becomes computationally prohibitive.

The main contribution of this work is an "aggressive double sampling" strategy to mitigate this issue. The first sampling step rebalances the training set by oversampling rare classes and undersampling frequent ones, directly targeting the long-tailed distribution problem. The second step reduces the computational burden by sampling a small subset of negative classes for each example when forming the dyadic pairs. The authors provide a theoretical analysis with new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexities, demonstrating that the empirical risk minimization principle remains consistent despite the distribution shift and data dependencies introduced by their sampling scheme. Empirically, the method is evaluated on large-scale text classification datasets with up to 100,000 classes, where it shows strong performance in terms of accuracy, memory efficiency, and training time compared to a suite of state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths**

*   The paper addresses extreme classification, an important and highly relevant research problem with numerous practical applications.
*   The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed approach against several strong and diverse baselines, including one-vs-all, multi-class SVMs, recent tree-based methods (FastXML, PfastReXML), and other state-of-the-art solvers (PD-Sparse).
*   The results are compelling, showing that the proposed method achieves competitive, and often superior, predictive performance (especially in terms of Macro-F1 score) while being significantly more efficient in training time and memory usage than other high-performing methods like OVA, M-SVM, and PD-Sparse on the largest datasets.

**Weaknesses**

*   The main weakness of the paper is its incremental nature. The work is presented as a direct extension of the approach in [16]. The primary new contributions—the double sampling strategy and the updated generalization bound—are valuable engineering and theoretical refinements, but the overall novelty of the framework is somewhat limited.
*   While the proposed method excels in reducing training time, its prediction time remains significantly longer than that of fast tree-based methods like FastXML and RecallTree. In many practical applications, inference speed is more critical than training time, which may limit the adoption of this method in latency-sensitive scenarios.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   The interpretation of the generalization bound in Theorem 2 is unclear. To better assess the theoretical contribution, it would be helpful if the authors could provide more context on the tightness of the bound. How does this bound compare, even on a qualitative level, with those for other extreme classification methods or related reduction techniques?
*   The comparison of memory usage could be made more informative. The set of baseline methods includes both batch and online learning algorithms, which inherently have very different memory consumption profiles. A brief discussion of this point would add valuable nuance to the experimental comparison.
