

This paper addresses extreme classification—multiclass problems with output spaces exceeding 100,000 classes—by introducing a novel reduction to pairwise binary classification. Existing approaches, such as hierarchical softmax (which constructs latent trees) or label embedding (which assumes low-rank label matrices), face key limitations: tree-based methods risk cascading errors due to suboptimal structures, while embedding techniques fail when the true label matrix is not low-rank. The authors propose a scalable sampling-based reduction to overcome these issues, providing theoretical guarantees of consistency with the original multiclass empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation.

The core contribution is the Aggressive Double Sampling (ADS) reduction, governed by two parameters. The first parameter adjusts sampling frequency inversely to empirical class frequency, ensuring rare classes in the long tail are not omitted. The second parameter controls the number of adversarial examples drawn uniformly during training. This process iteratively trains a pairwise classifier. At prediction time, the method avoids intractable pairwise comparisons with all classes by using a heuristic: each class is represented as the centroid of its input-space feature vectors, and prediction proceeds via pairwise classification only over a candidate subset derived from these centroids.

Experiments on five text datasets demonstrate ADS’s superiority when the class count exceeds 30,000. Compared to competitors—including standard one-vs-all (feasible only for smaller class sizes)—ADS achieves higher accuracy and F1 scores, while significantly reducing training time and memory consumption. Prediction latency is slightly higher than some baselines but remains practical for large-scale deployment. The method’s efficiency stems from its linear scaling with class size, contrasting with the logarithmic or quadratic scaling of tree-based and embedding approaches.

The theoretical analysis addresses two major challenges. First, the reduction transforms the multiclass problem into a dyadic binary classification task, introducing dependence among loss terms that complicates standard risk analysis. Second, oversampling rare classes distorts the empirical distribution. The authors resolve these by decomposing the dependent loss sum into independent components using the graph structure induced by the sampling procedure. They leverage concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables (Janson, 2004) to derive a finite-sample risk bound. Crucially, while the bound includes bias terms proportional to the ratio of true to oversampled class probabilities, these biases decay linearly with the size of the sampled training set. The proof strategy is sound, though the reviewer notes the detailed verification of the proof steps was not conducted.

The paper makes a strong case for ADS as a practical solution to extreme classification. Its theoretical foundation is rigorous, its implementation efficient, and its empirical results compelling for large-scale scenarios. The method’s minimal memory footprint and training speed advantage are particularly valuable for real-world systems where resources are constrained. The reduction framework itself is novel, effectively sidestepping the pitfalls of prior approaches while maintaining theoretical grounding. The authors’ choice of sampling parameters is well-justified, and the prediction heuristic is both simple and effective.

One minor limitation noted is that prediction speed is not optimized for latency-critical applications, though this is a reasonable trade-off given the significant gains in training efficiency and accuracy. The experiments thoroughly validate the method across diverse text datasets, and the comparison with state-of-the-art baselines is comprehensive. The writing is clear, and the contributions are well-motivated.

Overall, this work presents a significant advancement in extreme classification. The ADS reduction is theoretically sound, practically scalable, and empirically superior for large output spaces. It offers a valuable tool for practitioners dealing with massive label sets, and the method’s simplicity ensures broad applicability. The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS, advancing both the theoretical understanding and practical implementation of extreme classification.
