- Problem: tackles extreme multiclass classification with up to ~100,000 labels.  
- Prior approaches: label trees (hierarchical softmax) and label embeddings have drawbacks (cascading errors, poor performance when label matrix isn't low-rank).  
- New strategy: reduce extreme classification to pairwise binary (dyadic) classification problems.  
- Method: introduces a scalable, sampling-based reduction called "Aggressive Double Sampling" with theoretical consistency guarantees.  
- Sampling controls: oversample rare classes inversely to empirical frequency and draw a fixed number of uniformly sampled "adversarial" examples.  
- Inference: uses a centroid-based heuristic to produce a small candidate set and run pairwise classifiers only on those candidates.  
- Theory: non-trivial analysis handles dependent sampled examples via graph splitting and concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables, yielding finite-sample risk bounds with a bias term that decreases with sample size.  
- Experiments: evaluated on five text datasets and outperforms baselines (including one-vs-all where feasible) on accuracy and F1 for datasets with >30k classes.  
- Efficiency: trains faster and uses much less memory than competitors, though prediction is somewhat slower than some baselines.  
- Overall assessment: solid, practically useful contribution with sound theoretical analysis (proofs not fully verified by reviewer).
