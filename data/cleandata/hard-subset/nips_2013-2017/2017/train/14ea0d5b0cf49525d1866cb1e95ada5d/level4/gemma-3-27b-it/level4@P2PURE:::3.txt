This paper introduces a learning reduction for extreme classification – multiclass classification with an exceptionally large number of classes, reaching up to 100,000. Existing approaches often rely on either inferring a tree structure over the output labels (like hierarchical softmax) to reduce the number of binary predictions needed, or on learning label embeddings via techniques like least-squares or sampling. However, the authors point out limitations of these methods: inferred tree structures may be suboptimal and propagate errors, while label embedding approaches can struggle when the underlying label matrix is not low-rank.

Instead, the authors propose reducing extreme classification to a series of pairwise binary classifications. They develop a scalable sampling-based method within this framework, accompanied by theoretical guarantees demonstrating its consistency with the original multiclass empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation.

Experimental results on five text datasets, compared against several competing approaches (including standard one-vs-all classification where feasible), demonstrate that their method generally outperforms others in terms of accuracy and F1-score when the number of classes exceeds 30,000. Furthermore, it exhibits faster training times and significantly lower memory usage, although prediction speed is somewhat slower. 

The “Aggressive Double Sampling” reduction is governed by two parameters. The first controls the sampling frequency of each class, inversely proportional to its empirical frequency, preventing the omission of rare, “long-tail” classes. The second parameter dictates the number of adversarial examples to sample uniformly. This process is iterated to train the final dyadic classifier.

During prediction, the intractability of generating pairwise features for all possible classes is addressed using a heuristic. This involves representing each class by an input space centroid – the average vector of instances belonging to that class – and performing pairwise classifications only with a set of candidate classes identified through this centroid representation.

The theoretical analysis of this reduction is challenging due to the transformation of a sum of independent random variables into a sum of dependent ones resulting from the dyadic example construction, and the distributional shift caused by oversampling rare classes. The proof leverages the graph structure induced by the example construction to decompose the sum of dependent examples into independent components, and applies concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables developed by Janson. While the finite-sample risk bound is affected by the ratio between true and oversampled class probabilities, these biases diminish linearly with the size of the training and resampling sets. The analysis appears rigorous, although a detailed verification of the proof was beyond the scope of this review. 

In conclusion, this paper presents a compelling and effective method for extreme classification. It offers a strong balance of theoretical soundness, low memory and computational demands, and superior performance, particularly on very large datasets. The binary reduction algorithm is a valuable contribution, likely to be of interest and practical use to researchers and practitioners in the field.
