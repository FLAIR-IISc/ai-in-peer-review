- Proposes a novel reduction from multi-class to binary classification tailored for extremely large numbers of classes using a score function h(x,y) and a loss counting incorrect classes scoring higher than the true class.  
- Uses a feature transform φ mapping (input, class) pairs to R^p and learns a mapping from R^p to scores.  
- Extends Joshi et al. (2015) by creating correlated transformed points corresponding to comparisons between the true label and incorrect labels.  
- Provides improved generalization analysis over Joshi et al. using new bounds on fractional Rademacher complexity for the correlated transformed dataset.  
- Introduces sampling techniques that sample classes (with replacement) to balance class representation and reduce transformed training set size.  
- Uses random sampling of classes instead of all K−1 comparisons and shows sampling does not substantially worsen generalization bounds.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive and shows competitive performance against strong baselines.  
- Method is robust to long-tailed class distributions due to sampling that balances positives and negatives in the binary dataset.  
- Strength: novel, scalable reduction suitable for extremely large class spaces.  
- Strength: improved theoretical generalization guarantees via fractional Rademacher complexity bounds.  
- Strength: effective sampling scheme that balances class distributions and reduces training cost.  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical analysis of the prediction algorithm in Sec. 2.3 that restricts comparisons to k nearest centroids.  
- Weakness: needs more discussion and comparison of sample complexity bounds to non-transformed multi-class generalization bounds.  
- Clarity: well-written with clear method and theoretical exposition, though missing some theoretical details noted above.  
- Quality: technically sound analysis and thorough empirical validation, with significant practical implications (text classification, recommendation, ranking).  
- Acceptance arguments: novel reduction, improved theory, effective sampling, and strong empirical results.  
- Rejection arguments: missing theoretical analysis of the centroid-based prediction step and limited discussion/comparison of sample complexity bounds.
