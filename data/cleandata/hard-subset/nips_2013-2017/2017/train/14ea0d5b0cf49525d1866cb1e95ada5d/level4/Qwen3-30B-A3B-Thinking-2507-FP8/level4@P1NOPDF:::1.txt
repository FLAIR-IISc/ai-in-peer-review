

This paper proposes a double sampling strategy to enhance multiclass classification methods, building upon the framework in [16]. The authors provide both theoretical analysis, including new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexities, and empirical validation on text datasets. Experiments demonstrate the approach’s effectiveness for large-scale multiclass problems, achieving high accuracy with reduced training time.  

**Strengths:**  
- The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines across several datasets. The method consistently achieves strong performance while significantly reducing training time relative to competitors.  
- Extreme classification remains a critical and practically relevant research direction, making this work timely and applicable to real-world scenarios.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- While the paper introduces two key improvements—double sampling and the novel generalization bound—the core framework remains heavily reliant on [16]. The novelty is somewhat limited, as the contributions primarily refine an existing approach rather than introducing a fundamentally new paradigm.  
- The proposed method reduces training time but incurs substantially longer prediction times than alternatives. Given that prediction latency often outweighs training efficiency in practical deployments (e.g., real-time systems), this trade-off warrants deeper discussion.  
- The interpretation of the generalization bound requires clarification. How does this bound compare to those derived in prior work (e.g., [16] or other extreme classification methods)? A direct comparison would strengthen the theoretical contribution.  
- The experimental setup mixes batch and online learning algorithms, leading to inconsistent memory usage profiles. This disparity complicates fair performance evaluation, as memory requirements differ significantly between these algorithmic categories.  

**Overall:** The paper makes a modest incremental contribution to extreme classification. While the double sampling strategy offers practical training efficiency gains, the limited novelty and unresolved prediction-time trade-off weaken its impact. Clarifying the generalization bound’s novelty and addressing the experimental methodology’s consistency would substantially improve the work.
