- Addresses extreme multiclass classification with up to 100,000 classes.  
- Points out limitations of hierarchical label trees (error propagation) and low-rank label embeddings (assumption may fail).  
- Proposes a reduction to pairwise binary classification with a scalable sampling-based approach.  
- Provides theoretical consistency guarantees relative to standard multiclass ERM.  
- Empirical results on five text datasets: better accuracy and F1 when classes exceed ~30,000.  
- Offers faster training and substantially reduced memory usage; prediction speed is somewhat slower.  
- Introduces "Aggressive Double Sampling": class sampling inversely proportional to empirical frequency and uniform sampling of adversarial examples.  
- Trains a dyadic classifier on the sampled pairwise examples.  
- Uses a centroid-based prediction heuristic to narrow candidate classes and avoid exhaustive pairwise features.  
- Theoretical analysis addresses dependency and distribution shift via decomposition and Janson’s concentration inequalities.  
- Finite-sample risk bound includes oversampling-induced bias that decays linearly with training set size.  
- Overall: a theoretically grounded, low-memory, computationally efficient, and practically useful method for large-scale extreme classification.
