Paper proposes a reduction to pairwise binary classification for extreme multiclass problems (100k+ classes).  
Identifies limitations of prior methods: tree-structured models cause cascading errors; label embeddings fail when label matrix is not low-rank.  
Presents a scalable sampling-based "Aggressive Double Sampling" method with theoretical consistency to multiclass ERM.  
Aggressive Double Sampling uses (1) class sampling inversely proportional to empirical frequency to retain rare classes and (2) uniformly drawn adversarial examples for iterative dyadic classifier training.  
Prediction avoids full pairwise feature generation via a centroid-based heuristic that selects candidate classes by input-centroid proximity.  
Theoretical challenges (dependent dyadic examples and distribution shift from oversampling) are handled by partitioning graph-induced dependencies and applying Janson’s concentration inequalities.  
Finite-sample risk bound incurs bias tied to true vs. oversampled class probability ratios, with bias diminishing linearly as sampled training size increases.  
Empirical results on five text datasets: superior accuracy and F1 when classes >30k, substantially faster training and much lower memory use, but slightly slower prediction.  
Reviewer considers the analysis sound (not fully verified) and the method conceptually novel, theoretically well-founded, and practically valuable.
