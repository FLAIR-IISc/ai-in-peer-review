- Proposes a reduction from multi-class to binary classification using a scoring hypothesis on (input, class) pairs and a loss measuring fraction of incorrect classes ranked above the true class.  
- Assumes a feature transform φ mapping (input, class) to a p-dimensional feature space and learns a scoring function over that space.  
- Extends Joshi et al. (2015)’s reduction, which creates K−1 transformed comparison points per example, introducing correlations among transformed examples.  
- Standard generalization bounds do not apply due to correlations; paper provides improved generalization analysis.  
- Main theoretical contribution: new bounds leveraging fractional Rademacher complexity for correlated transformed data.  
- Practical contribution: two sampling strategies—sampling classes with replacement to rebalance and sampling a random subset of negative classes—to reduce transformed dataset size.  
- Shows these sampling procedures do not significantly degrade the derived generalization bounds.  
- Empirical results are strong, with competitive performance against several strong baselines.  
- Novelty primarily in the sampling techniques and generalization analysis; the underlying reduction follows prior work.  
- Weakness: no theoretical analysis of the prediction-time acceleration (comparing only k nearest centroids) or its effect on performance and relation to bounds.  
- Suggestion: include more detailed discussion and comparison of sample complexity bounds versus standard multi-class generalization bounds to clarify trade-offs.
