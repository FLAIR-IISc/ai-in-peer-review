## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission – [Paper ID]

This paper investigates a double sampling strategy to enhance multiclass classification, building upon the framework established in [16]. The authors provide both theoretical analysis, including a new generalization bound, and empirical evaluation demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach, particularly for problems with a large number of classes and text data.

**Strengths:**

The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing the proposed method against a range of established baselines. Results consistently demonstrate strong performance – achieving competitive accuracy with notably reduced training time across several datasets. Addressing the extreme classification problem is a valuable contribution, as it arises in many practical applications. The practical benefit of faster training, especially for large-scale datasets, is a clear strength.

**Weaknesses:**

While the paper builds upon a strong foundation ([16]), the novelty of the contributions feels incremental. The two primary additions – the double sampling strategy and a generalization bound based on local Rademacher Complexities – represent improvements, but do not constitute a fundamentally new paradigm. The work feels largely derivative, and a more substantial advancement would strengthen its impact.

A critical practical consideration is prediction time. Although the proposed method shortens training, the reported prediction times are considerably longer than those of other approaches. In many real-world scenarios, prediction speed is paramount, potentially limiting the applicability of this method despite its training efficiency. 

**Questions and Comments:**

The presented generalization bound is difficult to interpret in a practical context. It remains unclear how to effectively utilize this bound to guide model selection or hyperparameter tuning. A clearer explanation of the bound's practical implications, and a comparative analysis against existing generalization bounds for extreme classification, would significantly enhance the paper. Specifically, how does this bound relate to, and improve upon, the theoretical guarantees offered by [16] and other relevant works?

The experimental setup includes a mix of batch and online learning algorithms. This introduces a confounding factor when comparing memory usage, as batch methods inherently require more memory. A more focused comparison, either limiting the analysis to batch methods or explicitly acknowledging and addressing the differing memory characteristics of each approach, would be beneficial.  It's not immediately clear whether the observed memory savings are a direct consequence of the double sampling strategy or merely a result of comparing against online learners. 

Further clarification is needed regarding the choice of the sampling rate in the double sampling strategy. How sensitive is the performance to this hyperparameter, and what strategies are recommended for selecting an appropriate value?  Providing insights into this parameter's influence would improve the paper’s practicality.

The discussion of related work, while adequate, could benefit from a more detailed comparison to recent advances in approximate nearest neighbor search and tree-based methods (e.g., implementations using FAISS or similar libraries) which are frequently employed for large-scale multiclass classification.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound, well-executed study. However, the incremental nature of the contributions and the potential limitation of long prediction times raise concerns. While the reduced training time is valuable, the practical impact may be limited if prediction speed is critical. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding the interpretability of the generalization bound and a more nuanced discussion of prediction time. A stronger emphasis on the limitations of the approach, and a clearer articulation of the scenarios where it provides the greatest advantage, would also be beneficial.
