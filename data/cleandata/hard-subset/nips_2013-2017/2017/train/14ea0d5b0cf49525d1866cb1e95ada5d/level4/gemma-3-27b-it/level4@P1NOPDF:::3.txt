This paper presents a learning reduction for extreme classification, a multiclass classification problem where the output space contains a very large number of classes – up to 100,000. Many existing approaches to extreme classification rely on inferring a tree structure among the output labels, similar to hierarchical softmax, to reduce the number of binary predictions needed to determine the output class. Alternatively, label embedding approaches are used, which can be learned efficiently via least-squares or sampling. The authors point out limitations of these methods: the inferred tree structure may be suboptimal and lead to error propagation, and label embedding can suffer from prediction errors if the true label matrix is not low-rank.

Instead, this work explores reducing extreme classification to a series of pairwise binary classifications. The authors operate within this framework, presenting a scalable sampling-based method with theoretical guarantees for consistency with the original multiclass empirical risk minimization (ERM) formulation. 

Experiments on five text datasets, comparing the proposed method to several competing approaches (including standard one-vs-all classification where computationally feasible), demonstrate that it generally outperforms others in terms of accuracy and F1-score when the number of classes exceeds 30,000. Furthermore, the method trains faster and requires significantly less memory, although prediction speed is somewhat slower.

The "Aggressive Double Sampling" reduction is controlled by two parameters. The first governs the frequency of sampling each class, inversely proportional to its empirical frequency, to prevent rare classes in the long tail from being missed entirely. The second parameter determines the number of adversarial examples to draw uniformly. This procedure is repeated to train the final dyadic classifier.

At prediction time, evaluating pairwise features against all possible classes is intractable. To address this, the authors employ a heuristic: they represent each class with an input space centroid – the average vector of instances belonging to that class. Prediction is then performed through a series of pairwise classifications, limited to these candidate classes.

The theoretical analysis of this reduction is challenging because transforming multiclass classification into dyadic binary classification alters the independence structure of the random variables involved.  Additionally, oversampling rare classes shifts the empirical distribution. The proof involves decomposing the sum of dependent examples into sums of independent variables, leveraging the graph structure induced by the example construction, and applying concentration inequalities for partially dependent variables introduced by Janson. Although the resulting finite-sample risk bound is biased by the ratios between true class probabilities and oversampled frequencies, these biases decrease linearly with the size of the training and re-sampling sets. The analysis appears sound, though I haven't verified the proof in detail.

Overall, this paper presents a strong and practical method for extreme classification, characterized by low memory and computational requirements and superior performance, particularly on large datasets. The binary reduction algorithm is theoretically well-grounded, interesting, and likely to be valuable for practitioners in the field.
