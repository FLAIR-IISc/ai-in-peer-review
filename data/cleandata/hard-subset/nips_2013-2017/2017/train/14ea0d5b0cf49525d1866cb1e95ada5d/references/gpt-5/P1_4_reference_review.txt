Summary
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem over dyadic example–class pairs, but without incurring the usual O(mK) blow-up in training set size. The core proposal is an aggressive double sampling scheme: (i) resample the training set across classes with probabilities πk to counter long-tailed label distributions (oversample rare classes, subsample frequent ones), and (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ “adversarial” labels instead of using all K−1 negatives. Prediction is made efficient via a candidate-selection step that prefilters a small set of classes per test instance using nearest centroids in the dyadic feature space, and then scores only these.

On the theory side, the paper builds on dyadic reductions for multiclass ranking loss, fractional covers for dependent variables, and (local) Rademacher complexity. Theorem 1 gives a generalization bound for the standard dyadic reduction when examples are dependent (all pairs from the same x are dependent). Theorem 2 extends this to the double-sampled setting with shifted label distribution and interdependent pairs. The bounds feature fractional Rademacher complexities and variance-localized classes, and introduce constants α=maxy ηy/πy and β=maxy 1/πy that reflect the sampling scheme. Empirically, the method is evaluated on LSHTC (DMOZ, Wikipedia) variants up to 100K classes. Compared with OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, RecallTree, FastXML, and PfastReXML, the proposed approach achieves competitive or superior macro-F1 and accuracy at large K, with markedly lower training time and memory; OVA/M-SVM become infeasible at 50–100K.

Relation to prior work
The reduction to dyadic pairwise comparisons is standard (e.g., AdaBoost.MR, multiclass SVMs, and prior dyadic reduction for extreme classification), as is the use of fractional covers for dependent samples and Rademacher complexity bounds. The novelty here lies in (i) an explicit double sampling scheme for class-imbalance and negative downsampling tailored to dyadic reductions; (ii) new generalization bounds that handle both dependency and distribution shift due to sampling via local Rademacher complexity. The candidate selection at test time is related to common nearest-centroid filtering and sampled softmax/negative sampling ideas, but applied in a dyadic feature space.

Quality
- Theory: The dependence-aware bounds are plausible and technically relevant. Using local Rademacher complexity to obtain faster rates and to reflect variance is appropriate. However, the bounds contain α and β that can be large when π is very small for head classes; more guidance on choosing π to control these constants is needed, and explicit corollaries for common hypotheses (e.g., linear f with bounded norm and Lipschitz loss) would improve interpretability. The proofs are in the supplement; the main text could better explain the role of the fractional cover (e.g., how it is constructed here) and provide intuition for terms and rates.
- Method: The algorithm is simple, scalable, and likely robust. The dyadic map φ is hand-crafted for text (10 features inspired by learning-to-rank, including centroid distance). This helps speed but limits generality; a learned φ or a discussion of portability beyond text would be useful.
- Experiments: The datasets and scales are appropriate; results demonstrate strong efficiency and competitive accuracy/MaF1. The comparison set is reasonable for the time period, but some choices raise questions: (i) the baselines use original BoW features while the proposed method uses a compact dyadic map; an ablation comparing on the same representation or showing BoW vs φ performance would help fairness; (ii) no ablation on κ, π, or candidate set size q; (iii) the conversion of multilabel to multiclass via instance replication may bias distribution—please discuss possible effects. Reporting κ, π, and q per dataset, and sensitivity plots, would strengthen claims. Clarify the base binary learner, loss, and optimizer used for h/f.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but has typos and some notational inconsistencies. Precise definitions of φ and f, the loss optimized in practice, and the training complexity as a function of κ, M, and p should be stated in the main text. The description of the fractional cover briefly assumes ωk=1; reconcile this with the definition of exact fractional covers. The candidate selection procedure and centroid computation are clear; please specify q and discuss its impact on accuracy/runtime.

Originality
- Algorithmically, the double sampling and candidate selection are conceptually related to negative sampling and approximate nearest-centroid retrieval, but the combination within a dyadic reduction for extreme multiclass and the accompanying distribution-shift/dependence-aware bounds are novel.
- The theoretical contribution—local complexity bounds for doubly-sampled, dependent dyadic ERM—is the most original aspect.

Significance
The problem is important and common in practice. The method is simple to implement, efficient, and supported by theory. The scale of experiments (up to 100K classes) and practical gains in time/memory are compelling. If clarified and strengthened as suggested, the work is likely to be used and extended (e.g., to learned φ, improved negative sampling, or approximate nearest-neighbor candidate selection).

Pros
- Addresses a key bottleneck in dyadic reductions (O(mK) blow-up) with a simple, effective sampling scheme.
- Provides dependence- and shift-aware generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity.
- Strong empirical scaling, memory efficiency, and competitive accuracy/MaF1 at large K.
- Open-source code.

Cons
- Bounds have large constants α, β depending on π; limited guidance on choosing π, κ; no explicit rates for standard hypothesis classes.
- Limited and text-specific φ; no learned representation; unclear generality beyond text.
- Comparisons may be unfair due to different representations; missing ablations on κ, π, and candidate selection q; limited discussion of multilabel-to-multiclass conversion effects.
- Some clarity/notation/presentation issues; several references incomplete.

Questions for authors
1) What binary learner, loss, and regularization do you use for h/f? Provide training complexity and convergence details. 2) What values of κ, π, and q did you use per dataset? Please add sensitivity analyses for κ and q, and an ablation with π=η (no resampling). 3) Can you specialize Theorem 2 to linear f with bounded norm and a Lipschitz surrogate to give explicit rates? 4) How do you normalize/cap π∝1/µ to keep π≤1, and what are typical α, β values observed? 5) Can you provide an ablation without candidate selection to quantify its contribution? 6) Discuss fairness of feature comparisons and, if possible, compare baselines on the same φ.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a practical, scalable method with nontrivial theoretical support and convincing large-scale experiments. Addressing clarity and ablation concerns in the final version would substantially strengthen it.
