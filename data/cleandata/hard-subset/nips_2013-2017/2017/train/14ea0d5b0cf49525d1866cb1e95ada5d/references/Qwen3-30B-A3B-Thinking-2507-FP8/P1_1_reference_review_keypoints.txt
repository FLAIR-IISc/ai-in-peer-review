- Addresses extreme multi-class classification by proposing "aggressive double sampling" to reduce multi-class to binary problems for up to 100k classes.  
- Method balances classes via oversampling/subsampling (π) and reduces dyadic examples by selecting κ adversarial classes per example.  
- Provides theoretical generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity to handle dependent dyadic examples and sampling-induced distribution shift.  
- Empirical evaluation on LSHTC datasets shows improved computational efficiency and competitive or better predictive performance versus multiple baselines.  
- Practical contribution: method scales to 100k+ classes, addressing real-world long-tailed text classification problems.  
- Theoretical soundness: solid theorems (1 and 2) and appropriate use of local fractional Rademacher complexity for tighter bounds.  
- Comprehensive experiments: compares predictive metrics and computational resources across many state-of-the-art baselines.  
- Clear problem formulation and precise mathematical presentation.  
- Novelty: novel combination of class-balancing sampling and dyadic reduction plus theoretical analysis.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity for π and κ and reliance on heldout tuning without detailed exploration.  
- Weakness: omitted comparison with some recent methods (e.g., SLEEC, LEML) that could be relevant.  
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of how sampling changes class distributions in the sampled training set and its effect on learning.  
- Weakness: theoretical results assume a bounded variance term r without practical guidance on estimating or validating this assumption.  
- Weakness: lack of ablation study isolating the contributions of class balancing versus dyadic reduction.  
- Specific comment: need guidance on estimating/bounding variance term r to make theory more applicable.  
- Specific comment: algorithm pseudocode does not address cases where a class has fewer than πk examples.  
- Specific comment: lacks discussion of trade-offs between candidate set size q and prediction time/accuracy in candidate selection.  
- Specific comment: experimental setup omits details on heldout set size and selection procedure for hyperparameter tuning.  
- Specific comment: related work section could better distinguish differences with recent methods like PfastReXML and FastXML.  
- Recommendation: reviewer recommends acceptance, citing technical soundness, originality, and strong empirical results.  
- Pro acceptance: practical scalability, theoretical contributions, comprehensive evaluation, clear writing, and addressing class imbalance.  
- Con acceptance: limited hyperparameter discussion, missing comparisons, unexamined theoretical assumption, and no ablation study.
