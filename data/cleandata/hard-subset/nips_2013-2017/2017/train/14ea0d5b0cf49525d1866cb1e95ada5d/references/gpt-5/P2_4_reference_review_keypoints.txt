- Summary: Reduces extreme multiclass to binary dyadic example–class pairs with double sampling (class-rebalancing π and negative sampling κ) and q-nearest-centroid candidate selection; provides generalization bounds and large-scale text experiments.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on dyadic reductions, ranking surrogates, and fractional Rademacher analyses; novelty is the joint double-sampling strategy with theoretical guarantees and a compact text feature map φ.

- Theory strength: Uses fractional graph covers and local Rademacher complexity to handle dependent dyadic pairs; bounds reflect sampling tradeoffs via α, β and a 1/√(Mκ) term.

- Theory gaps: Practical training loss (surrogate) is not connected to the analyzed 0/1 loss; candidate selection at prediction time lacks theoretical analysis; dependence of RT(`∘H_r) on κ, p, and data is not detailed.

- Empirical strength: Strong, relevant experiments on LSHTC/Wikipedia up to 100k classes showing favorable accuracy–time–memory tradeoffs and improved macro-F1 from rebalancing.

- Empirical gaps: Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses for κ, π, q, and φ; candidate selection impact on accuracy/speed not quantified; multi-label to multi-class conversion may distort priors; some important baselines (e.g., DiSMEC, representative embedding methods) are omitted.

- Clarity strengths: Mostly well written with clear algorithm description and concise theoretical statements.

- Clarity issues: Main text omits training loss and optimizer, π normalization/oversampling details, exact dyadic φ features, and hyperparameter selection protocol; minor typos remain.

- Originality: Individual components are known, but the combined method and principled analysis of sampling-induced distribution shift using local fractional Rademacher complexity is a meaningful contribution.

- Significance: Addresses key scalability problems in extreme classification with practical gains and reassuring theory, though practical guidance for choosing π and κ and validation beyond text are limited.

- Pros: Practical scalable algorithm, insightful theoretical bounds accounting for dependence and sampling, strong large-scale empirical results, and released code.

- Cons: No guarantees for candidate pruning, limited sensitivity analyses, underspecified training/optimization details, text-specific feature map, and some baseline omissions.

- Questions for authors: Specify the loss/model used (hinge vs logistic), how π is normalized/capped and typical α,β values, sensitivity curves for κ and q, true-class exclusion rates under candidate selection, and any proxies for RT(`∘H_r).

- Recommendation: Lean weak accept — solid idea with nontrivial theory and compelling large-scale experiments, contingent on clarifying training objective, adding sensitivity analyses (κ, q), and tightening descriptions of π and φ.
