

# Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper proposes a "doubly-sampled multi-class to binary reduction strategy" for large-scale multi-class classification. The authors introduce a (π, κ)-double sampling approach that aims to balance class distributions and reduce the number of dyadic examples, followed by theoretical analysis of generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity. The paper claims to address the computational challenges of large-scale multi-class classification, particularly the class imbalance problem and high computational complexity.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a genuine challenge in large-scale multi-class classification (class imbalance and computational complexity) and proposes a method that appears to address these issues. The experimental evaluation on text classification datasets demonstrates reasonable performance improvements over baseline methods, particularly in terms of memory usage and computational complexity. The theoretical analysis attempts to provide justification for the sampling approach, which is a positive aspect.

## Major Weaknesses

1. **Technical Inconsistencies and Typos**: The paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. The most glaring issue is the misplaced conference reference "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" inserted mid-sentence in the introduction. The paper also uses inconsistent notation throughout (e.g., "K .= [K]" instead of standard "K = [K]"). Equations are poorly formatted (e.g., "e(g,xy) = 1 K − 1 ∑" should be "(1/(K-1))∑"), and the paper contains multiple grammatical errors that make it difficult to follow (e.g., "the construction of the dyadic training observations is generally time consuming and prevails over the training and prediction times").

2. **Theoretical Weaknesses**: The theoretical contributions are overstated. The paper claims to derive "new" generalization bounds using "local fractional Rademacher complexity" but fails to clearly articulate how these bounds improve upon existing work (e.g., [25] or [16]). Theorem 1 contains multiple formatting errors (e.g., "5 (√ RT (S)(` ◦ Hr) + √ r )√ log 1δ m" should be "5(√RT(S)(` ◦ Hr) + √r)√(log(1/δ)/m)"). The proof is claimed to be in supplementary material but the paper itself doesn't provide a clear statement of the novelty of the bounds.

3. **Lack of Clear Novelty**: The paper fails to clearly distinguish its approach from existing work. The sampling strategy resembles methods in [16, 28], which are cited but not sufficiently contrasted. The authors claim to "derive new generalization bounds" but don't demonstrate how these bounds are superior to existing ones. The paper states they "did not consider other recent methods SLEEC [5] and LEML [31]" but doesn't provide a convincing justification for this exclusion.

4. **Experimental Issues**: The experimental setup has several weaknesses. The paper converts multi-label datasets to multi-class by "replicating instances," but doesn't justify this approach or discuss potential biases introduced. The paper claims "the memory consumption of both approaches exceeds the Terabyte" without providing actual numerical comparisons. The hyperparameters for baseline methods are not sufficiently detailed, making the results difficult to reproduce. The paper also uses non-standard dataset names (WIKI-Small, WIKI-50K, WIKI-100K) instead of referring to standard LSHTC datasets.

5. **Theoretical Claims vs. Evidence**: The paper claims "the empirical risk minimization principle over the transformation of the sampled training set remains consistent" but only provides a generalization bound, not a formal proof of consistency. The paper also states that "the reduction of the original problem to a binary classification problem brings inter-dependency between the dyadic examples" but fails to clearly explain how their approach handles this dependency beyond citing [16].

## Minor but Cumulative Issues

- In the (π, κ)-DS algorithm, the line "Draw uniformly a set Yxy of κ classes from Y\{y}. κ K;" is confusing and poorly formatted.
- The prediction algorithm mentions "candidate set of q nearest-centroid classes" without defining what q is.
- The paper uses inconsistent notation for example-class pairs (sometimes "xy", sometimes "xyi", sometimes "xyii").
- The reference list contains multiple formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "In Proceedings of the 10 ACM SIGKDD,," with extra commas).
- The paper claims "we validate our approach by conducting a series of experiments on subsets of DMOZ and the Wikipedia collections with up to 100,000 target categories" but doesn't clearly state the actual number of classes in the largest dataset.

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses a genuine problem in large-scale multi-class classification and presents some promising experimental results, the cumulative effect of numerous technical inconsistencies, theoretical weaknesses, and unclear novelty statements significantly undermines its contribution. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find the paper's technical execution to be lacking in several critical areas that would require substantial revision before publication. The numerous formatting errors, inconsistent notation, and unclear theoretical claims are particularly problematic for a NIPS submission, which should demonstrate the highest standards of technical writing and precision.

The paper's theoretical claims are overstated, the novelty is not clearly established, and the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient detail to be fully convincing. The minor technical issues accumulate to the point where the paper fails to meet the quality standards expected for NIPS. I recommend rejection based on these cumulative technical shortcomings, despite the paper's potentially useful approach to a challenging problem.
