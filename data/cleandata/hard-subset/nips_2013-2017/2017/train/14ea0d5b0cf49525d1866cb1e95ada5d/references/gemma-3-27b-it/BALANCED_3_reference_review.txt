This paper addresses the challenging problem of large-scale multi-class classification, specifically focusing on the computational and statistical difficulties arising from the number of classes. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling strategy to reduce the problem to binary classification, aiming to balance class distribution and reduce the number of dyadic examples. They also provide theoretical guarantees via generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexity and demonstrate empirical effectiveness on large text datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying the limitations of existing approaches like OVA and tree-based methods, as well as embedding-based methods and previous dyadic reduction techniques. The contribution of the aggressive double sampling is significant, as it directly tackles the scalability issues while also considering class imbalance. The theoretical analysis providing generalization bounds is a strong point, demonstrating a rigorous understanding of the underlying principles. The experimental validation on large datasets like DMOZ and Wikipedia further strengthens the paper's claims. The inclusion of comparisons against several strong baselines, including recent methods like PD-Sparse, is commendable. The presentation of results with both accuracy and macro-F1 is also good practice, addressing the potential impact of class imbalance.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the connection between the derived bounds and practical parameter tuning isn’t entirely clear. Specifically, the parameters 'r' in Theorem 1 and α, β in Theorem 2, while mathematically defined, lack intuitive explanations in terms of the practical application of the algorithm. This makes it difficult for a practitioner to leverage the bounds for hyperparameter selection. The description of the feature mapping φ is somewhat brief; while it references existing work, a more detailed explanation tailored to the text classification context would be beneficial.  The ablation study assessing the impact of the (π, κ) parameters individually is limited. The paper primarily shows the combined effect, but analyzing each parameter’s sensitivity would offer deeper insights. The writing could be improved in places – some sentences are overly dense and complex. While technically sound, readability suffers in certain sections.  Finally, the description of the candidate selection step is a bit cursory. While the idea is clear, elaborating on how the nearest-centroid selection is implemented and its potential impact on performance would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Technical Contribution:** The proposed aggressive double sampling strategy is a novel and effective approach to address the scalability challenges of large-scale multi-class classification.
*   **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides generalization bounds that support the proposed method and demonstrate a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on large datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the method compared to strong baselines.
*   **Relevance to NIPS Audience:** The problem of extreme classification is a significant area of research within the machine learning community, and the proposed solution contributes to advancements in this field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Practical Guidance:** The connection between the theoretical bounds and practical parameter tuning is not clearly articulated.
*   **Brief Description of Feature Mapping:** The explanation of the feature mapping could be more detailed and tailored to the text classification task.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** The impact of individual parameters in the sampling strategy is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Readability Issues:** Some sections of the paper are dense and could benefit from improved clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a significant contribution to the field of large-scale multi-class classification. The technical soundness, theoretical analysis, and empirical results are all strong. However, the lack of practical guidance for parameter tuning and some readability issues prevent it from being a clear accept. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to address the identified weaknesses in a revised version. Specifically, providing more intuitive explanations of the theoretical parameters, elaborating on the feature mapping, performing a more detailed ablation study, and improving the overall clarity of the writing would significantly strengthen the paper. I believe that addressing these points would make this a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
