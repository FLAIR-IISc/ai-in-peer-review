- Tackles extreme multi-class classification by proposing Aggressive Double Sampling (DS) to reduce dyadic examples and address class imbalance.  
- DS uses a (π, κ)-probabilistic sampling strategy combined with dyadic reduction to improve scalability and balance class distributions.  
- Provides theoretical generalization bounds adapting Rademacher complexity and graph covering to the sampled setting.  
- Empirical results on DMOZ and Wikipedia show competitive accuracy with substantially improved runtime and memory; scaling results are strong.  
- Positions work relative to label embedding (low-rank limitations) and dyadic reduction (computational cost), arguing DS mitigates both issues.  
- Novelty: combining probabilistic sampling with dyadic reduction is a new, practical approach.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous but dense and hard to follow; needs more intuitive explanations of assumptions and implications.  
- Performance appears sensitive to hyperparameters (π, κ); the paper lacks detailed guidance on tuning and their impact.  
- Comparison set is limited—missing recent SOTA methods like SLEEC and LEML for stronger benchmarking.  
- Joint example/class feature mapping is described superficially; unclear why chosen features are optimal or broadly applicable beyond text.  
- Code repository is provided, but reproducibility is not fully confirmed.  
- Writing is generally clear; theoretical sections in particular could be simplified for readability.  
- Overall technically sound with solid empirical backing; reviewers scored Quality 8/10, Clarity 7/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 8/10.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—simplify theoretical presentation and expand comparisons to recent methods.  
- Pros: well-motivated, scalable, novel, theoretically justified, and empirically effective.  
- Cons: complex theory, limited SOTA comparisons, and hyperparameter sensitivity.
