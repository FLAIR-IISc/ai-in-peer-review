Summary
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem via dyadic example–class pairs, but avoids the prohibitive O(mK) construction by “aggressive double sampling”: (i) subsample/oversample the training set across classes with probabilities πk (to counter long tails), and (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ adversarial classes to form dyadic pairs. The authors provide generalization bounds for the resulting dependent samples using fractional and local Rademacher complexities, and propose a nearest-centroid candidate selection at test time. Experiments on LSHTC (DMOZ/Wikipedia) variants report favorable accuracy/MaF1 vs. OVA, multi-class SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse, with lower runtime/memory on large K. A simple 10-D dyadic feature map is used for text.

Relation to prior work
The dyadic reduction is well known (e.g., Usunier et al.; Joshi et al. 2015) and ranking-style losses are standard (Weston & Watkins; AdaBoost.MR). Handling dependence among dyadic pairs via fractional Rademacher complexity has been studied (Ralaivola & Amini; Janson). This paper combines these tools with a double sampling scheme and adds a local Rademacher analysis. The candidate selection idea echoes negative sampling and nearest-centroid heuristics, common in large-scale ranking and extreme classification, but theoretical treatment for test-time pruning is absent.

Strengths
- Addresses a real scalability pain point of dyadic reductions with a simple, intuitive sampling scheme.
- Provides nontrivial generalization bounds for dependent, subsampled dyads using local fractional Rademacher analyses.
- Practical focus on long-tailed label distributions; πk design is sensible.
- Experimental results suggest improved time/memory and competitive accuracy/MaF1 on very large K; code is provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theory–practice gap: The bounds in Theorems 1–2 depend on α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy, which can be large under aggressive rebalancing. This weakens the claimed “consistency” and does not clearly justify unweighted ERM on the reweighted distribution. Importance weighting to correct for the π-shift is not considered empirically.
- Limited novelty in analysis: The use of fractional covers and local Rademacher complexity is incremental over existing dependent-sample analyses; key proofs are deferred. It is unclear how much tighter the bounds are vs. prior results for dyadic reductions.
- Heavy reliance on hand-crafted 10-D features tailored to text. Claims of general applicability are not substantiated beyond text; no results on other modalities or with learned φ (e.g., bilinear models).
- Evaluation setup raises questions:
  - LSHTC multi-label data are converted to multi-class by duplicating instances, which alters distributions and evaluation semantics. The impact on baselines designed for multilabel/extreme classification is unclear.
  - Missing ablations: no systematic study of κ, π, or test-time candidate size q; no trade-off curves; no results without candidate selection to quantify its contribution.
  - Candidate selection lacks any theoretical guarantee (e.g., probability of excluding the true class or its impact on regret), and q is unspecified in the main text.
  - Baselines: The core dyadic baseline [16] (which this work extends) is not reported; comparisons to simple negative sampling within pairwise ranking or to error-correcting output codes/tournaments are missing. Excluding SLEEC/LEML purely by citation weakens the empirical case; at least one representative embedding baseline should be included for completeness.
  - Reproducibility: While code is linked, crucial implementation details (exact π schedule, κ and q per dataset, feature normalization) are not in the paper; variance across runs and statistical significance are not reported.
- The empirical superiority claims may hinge on the feature engineering and candidate selection heuristic rather than on the proposed sampling per se; this is not disentangled.

Quality
Technically competent, but the theoretical contribution appears modest and does not tightly support the aggressive subsampling choices. Empirical results are promising but lack critical ablations, stronger/more relevant baselines, and analysis of the distribution shift induced by π. The candidate selection step is heuristic without analysis.

Clarity
Generally readable, with a clear problem statement and algorithmic description. However, some notation is dense; the dependence on π, κ, α, β in bounds should be interpreted for practitioner choices. Experimental details (π, κ, q, feature definitions beyond a brief table) should be moved from supplement to the main text. Please define evaluation metrics and preprocessing decisions more precisely.

Originality
Moderate. The double sampling idea is a straightforward combination of class rebalancing and negative sampling; the theoretical tools are adapted from prior work. The local Rademacher twist is incremental.

Significance
Potentially useful in practice for text with long-tailed labels, but the current evidence is limited to one domain and hand-crafted features. Without stronger baselines and ablations, it is hard to judge broader impact.

Questions for the authors
- Do you use any importance weighting to correct for π during training or evaluation? If not, under what conditions on π do your bounds imply consistency to the original distribution?
- Please report ablations over κ and q, and sensitivity to π (including no rebalancing). What κ achieves near-OVA accuracy?
- What is the error rate of candidate selection alone (probability that the true class is not in Yx)? Any bound or empirical estimate?
- Compare to the dyadic reduction baseline [16] without aggressive sampling and to a pairwise-ranking with standard negative sampling. Also include at least one embedding baseline (LEML/SLEEC) for completeness.
- Provide per-dataset π schedules, κ and q, and report standard deviations over multiple seeds.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important scalability issue in a widely used reduction technique.
- Simple approach with encouraging large-K results and public code.
- Some theoretical backing for dependent, subsampled dyads.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental theory; weak connection between bounds and practical choices.
- Limited, text-specific evaluation with hand-crafted features; missing ablations and key baselines.
- Heuristic candidate selection without guarantees; unclear contribution split.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is plausible and practical, but the paper falls short on rigorous empirical validation and on demonstrating theoretical novelty/impact. Strengthening the experimental section (ablations, stronger baselines, clarity on π/κ/q), analyzing candidate selection, and aligning theory with practice would make this more compelling.

Confidence
High on methodological assessment; medium on exact experimental outcomes due to missing details.
