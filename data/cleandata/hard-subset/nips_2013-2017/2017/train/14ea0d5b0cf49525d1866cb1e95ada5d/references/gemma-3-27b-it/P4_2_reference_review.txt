This paper addresses the problem of large-scale multi-class classification, specifically focusing on the challenges of computational intractability and class imbalance inherent in one-vs-all (OVA) approaches. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling strategy to reduce the problem to binary classification, aiming to balance class distributions and reduce the number of dyadic examples. They also provide theoretical analysis, deriving generalization bounds based on local fractional Rademacher complexity, and validate their approach on large text datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia).

The core idea – aggressive sampling to reduce the computational burden of dyadic representations – is well-motivated. The theoretical contribution, establishing generalization bounds for this sampling strategy, is valuable. The experimental validation on large datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of both performance and efficiency, particularly in comparison to other methods that struggle with scalability. The careful consideration of the curse of long-tailed distributions through the sampling strategy is a strong point.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The presentation of the theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) is dense and difficult to follow. The notation is heavy, and the connection between the theoretical results and the practical algorithm is not always clear. More intuitive explanations of the mathematical derivations would significantly improve readability. While the experimental results are comprehensive, the presentation could be improved. The tables are large and somewhat difficult to parse. More insightful visualizations comparing the performance of different methods across various dataset sizes could be included. The discussion of hyperparameters is also brief; more details on the tuning process would be beneficial. While the authors acknowledge related work, a more detailed discussion of how their approach differs from and builds upon existing methods (e.g., label embedding trees, sparse methods) would strengthen the contribution. A potential limitation is the dependency on the feature mapping φ, which is application-dependent. The authors demonstrate this mapping on text data, but its applicability to other domains may require further investigation.  The explanation of Algorithm 1 ("Prediction with Candidate Selection") is brief and lacks sufficient detail for complete understanding, particularly regarding the choice of the 'q' nearest-centroid classes.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The aggressive double sampling strategy is a novel approach to address the computational and statistical challenges of large-scale multi-class classification.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides generalization bounds supporting the proposed method, demonstrating its consistency.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on large datasets demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the approach in terms of performance and efficiency.
*   **Significance:** The scalability of the method makes it potentially useful for a wide range of applications involving extremely large numbers of classes.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The theoretical presentation is dense and difficult to follow, hindering understanding.
*   **Presentation:** The experimental results could be presented more effectively with improved visualizations and more detail on hyperparameter tuning.
*   **Limited Discussion:** The connection to and differentiation from related work could be more thoroughly explored.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses in clarity and presentation, this paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem. The experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness, and the scalability of the method is a significant advantage. I believe the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and, with some revisions to improve clarity and presentation, would be suitable for acceptance to NIPS. I recommend a borderline accept decision with a request for major revisions focusing on clarity of the theoretical derivations and improved presentation of the experimental results.
