This paper tackles the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, specifically addressing the computational and class imbalance issues inherent in the one-vs-all (OVA) approach. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling strategy to reduce the dimensionality and balance the training data before applying a binary classification framework. The core idea – strategically sampling both instances and classes – is a sensible and potentially effective way to mitigate the drawbacks of existing approaches like tree-based methods and label embedding. The inclusion of theoretical analysis, with generalization bounds derived using local Rademacher complexity, strengthens the contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Extreme multi-class classification is a relevant and active research area with significant practical applications.
* **Novel approach:** The aggressive double sampling strategy is a novel combination of ideas designed to address both computational cost and class imbalance. It's a pragmatic attempt to improve upon existing methods.
* **Theoretical grounding:** Providing generalization bounds, even with assumptions, adds rigor and provides insights into the performance of the proposed method. The use of local Rademacher complexity is a nice touch.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments on the DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in terms of runtime and memory usage, compared to several baselines. The results suggest a good trade-off between performance and scalability.
* **Clear presentation (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to proposed solution and experimental evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Notation:** Several equations (e.g., Eq. 6) are not clearly explained in the surrounding text. While the authors likely define these variables elsewhere, immediate context is lacking, hindering comprehension.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance heavily relies on the choice of the (π, κ) parameters. The paper lacks a detailed discussion on how to effectively tune these parameters in practice. While the supplementary material mentions hyperparameter tuning, more guidance within the main paper would be beneficial.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the baselines are reasonable, the comparison could be strengthened by including more recent and specialized methods for extreme multi-class classification beyond those listed. For instance, exploring hierarchical softmax-based approaches might be relevant.
* **Feature Mapping Detail:** The feature mapping φ is described briefly and lacks detail. The specific features used (listed in Table 1) aren’t fully motivated in the context of the double sampling strategy. It's not obvious *why* these features are particularly well-suited for this approach.
* **Generalization Bound Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on several assumptions, which are not thoroughly discussed. The impact of violating these assumptions on the practical performance of the method needs to be addressed. For example, the variance constraint `Hr` could be restrictive in practice.
* **Algorithm 1 lacks detail:** The Candidate Selection algorithm, while conceptually straightforward, lacks sufficient detail about the nearest centroid selection process. What distance metric is used? How is the number of candidate classes (`q`) determined?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem with a novel and well-motivated approach.
* The theoretical analysis, while not trivial, provides valuable insights.
* The empirical results demonstrate promising performance, particularly in terms of scalability.
* The paper is generally well-written and organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed explanation for key aspects (e.g., parameter tuning, feature mapping) hinders reproducibility and practical application.
* The theoretical results rely on potentially restrictive assumptions.
* The baseline comparison could be more comprehensive.
* The notation sometimes lacks sufficient context.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a worthwhile contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. The proposed aggressive double sampling strategy is a promising approach for addressing the limitations of existing methods. However, the paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding clarity, parameter sensitivity, and the discussion of assumptions. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The core ideas are strong, and with further refinement and clarification, this paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to NIPS. Specifically, I suggest focusing on:

1.  Providing more detailed guidance on parameter tuning.
2.  Expanding the baseline comparison with more recent and relevant methods.
3.  Clarifying the feature mapping and its relation to the proposed sampling strategy.
4.  Discussing the implications of the assumptions underlying the theoretical results.
5.  Improving the clarity of the notation and providing more context for equations.
6. Expanding on the details of the candidate selection algorithm (Algorithm 1).
