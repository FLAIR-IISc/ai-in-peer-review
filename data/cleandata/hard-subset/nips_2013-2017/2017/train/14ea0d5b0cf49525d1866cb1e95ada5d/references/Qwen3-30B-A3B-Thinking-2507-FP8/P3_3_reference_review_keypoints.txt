- Proposes (π, κ)-DS: oversample small classes and subsample large ones, then apply dyadic transformation to reduce multi-class to binary for large-scale text classification.
- Provides theoretical generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity accounting for sampling-induced distribution shift and dyadic dependency.
- Empirically shows large gains in computational efficiency (memory and runtime) over OVA and M-SVM while keeping competitive accuracy and macro F1 on WIKI datasets.
- Effectively addresses long-tailed class distributions common in extreme classification tasks.
- Theoretical novelty is limited: analysis builds on prior local Rademacher work and lacks clear differentiation from [22,25].
- Theorem 2 includes sampling-dependent terms (α, β) that are not well-justified or analyzed for practical implications.
- No clear guidance or convergence-rate analysis for the consistency claim or how optimal π and κ relate to real class distributions.
- Sampling-as-a-solution is not novel; similar ideas exist ([2], dyadic methods in [16]) and "aggressive" sampling is not rigorously defined or compared.
- Lacks comparison to other sampling strategies (class-balanced, adaptive) and insufficient articulation of how the proposed sampling differs.
- Experimental comparisons omit detailed per-method memory measurements and only superficially compare with tree-based methods without exploring hybrids.
- Candidate selection via class centroids is not justified against alternatives (random, k-NN), and the choice of q (candidates) is not analyzed across datasets.
- Literature coverage misses some recent fast extreme classification methods (e.g., [21,13]) and should compare more with SLEEC [5] and LEML [31].
- Paper is well-written and practically significant, but theoretical contribution is incremental and originality is limited.
- Recommendation: weak accept—practically useful with solid experiments but requires stronger theoretical novelty, clearer comparisons, and more analysis of sampling parameters.
