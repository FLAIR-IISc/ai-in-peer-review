Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles large-scale multi-class (extreme) classification by reducing to a single binary problem over dyadic example–class pairs. Generating all O(mK) dyads is expensive and yields dependence among pairs. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling (π, κ)-DS: (i) resample the training set to mitigate long-tailed class imbalance (oversample rare classes, subsample frequent ones), then (ii) for each kept example, sample only κ adversarial classes to form dyads. They provide generalization bounds for the standard dyadic reduction using local fractional Rademacher complexity (Theorem 1) and, crucially, for the double-sampled setting (Theorem 2), accounting for distribution shift (via π) and dependence across dyads. Prediction uses a candidate-class preselection heuristic based on nearest class centroids in the dyadic feature space to reduce scoring cost.

Experiments on LSHTC DMOZ/Wikipedia datasets (up to 100K classes) compare against OVA, multi-class SVM, tree/embedding-based methods (RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML), and PD-Sparse. Using a simple 10-dimensional dyadic mapping inspired by learning-to-rank, (π, κ)-DS achieves competitive or better accuracy and macro-F1 at much lower memory/runtime than OVA/M-SVM and PD-Sparse, and better accuracy than tree methods at comparable time/memory.

This work connects to prior dyadic reductions and interdependent-data generalization bounds [Usunier & Amini; Joshi et al.], extreme classification with trees/embeddings [FastXML, RecallTree], and recent sparse multiclass methods [PD-Sparse]. The novelty is the double-sampling scheme with accompanying dependent-data, data-dependent risk bounds, and a pragmatic candidate-selection mechanism.

Quality
- Theory: Theorems are stated for dependent dyadic examples using fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities. The move from global to local complexity is meaningful and can yield faster rates. The double-sampling bound (Theorem 2) contains explicit dependence on the sampling parameters via α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y, plus terms for κ and M. This appropriately captures the bias-variance trade-off introduced by sampling and dependence. However, the bounds appear potentially loose in long-tailed regimes if π is made inversely proportional to η, since α can grow with 1/π, and constants/log terms are not discussed; the role of κ in tightening the variance terms is intuitive but not quantified with practical guidance. Proofs are deferred; the main ideas are plausible and consistent with [Janson, Ralaivola & Amini], but clarity of assumptions (loss, boundedness of h, cover construction) would help.
- Algorithms: The (π, κ)-DS procedure is simple and sound. The prediction candidate selection is sensible; however, it is heuristically justified and not integrated into the theory (i.e., end-to-end risk with candidate pruning is not analyzed).
- Experiments: Strong large-scale benchmarks and wall-clock/memory reporting. Using macro-F1 is appropriate for imbalance. Results convincingly show speed/memory advantages and competitive accuracy on 50K–100K classes. Missing ablations: sensitivity to κ and q (candidate set size), and to different π choices; impact of oversampling on overfitting; comparison to hard-negative sampling. The base learner f and loss (logistic vs hinge, regularization) are not clearly specified in the main text. Converting multi-label datasets to multi-class by replication is pragmatic but may distort distributions; precision@k (typical in extreme classification) is not reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, but several notational issues and typos hinder comprehension (e.g., indices in the dyadic transformation, “if k < y” in Algorithm, inconsistent δ inside logs, definitions of V and Hr). The base classifier f and optimization details belong in the main paper, not only the supplement. A brief complexity analysis with respect to m, K, κ, and p would help. The mapping features should be summarized (currently Table 1 is referenced but not shown). Candidate selection needs a succinct description of q and computational benefit.

Originality
- The double-sampling scheme for dyadic reductions and the accompanying dependent-data generalization analysis using local fractional Rademacher complexity appear novel. Prior work considered dyadic reductions and fractional Rademacher bounds but not this particular aggressive sampling with distribution shift and the explicit α, β dependency.
- The candidate-selection heuristic is not novel per se but is a reasonable engineering complement.

Significance
The method addresses a central bottleneck in dyadic reductions—quadratic blow-up—while preserving accuracy, and scales to 100K classes with competitive performance and memory. Practitioners working with extreme multi-class problems can benefit. The theoretical contribution increases confidence in such sampling-based reductions. Broader impact would be strengthened by more thorough ablations and by demonstrating portability beyond text (the current φ is text-specific).

Strengths
- Practical algorithm that is simple to implement and scales well.
- Theory for dependent dyadic data with sampling; shift-aware bounds with local complexities.
- Strong empirical evidence on large benchmarks; compelling runtime/memory gains.
- Sensible handling of long-tailed distributions; macro-F1 improvements.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablation and sensitivity analysis for κ, π, and candidate set size q.
- Base learner f, loss, and optimization details are underspecified in the main text.
- Theory does not cover candidate selection; bounds may be loose when π is highly imbalanced (large α, β).
- Evaluation focused on text and macro-F1; missing precision@k and non-text datasets.
- Some clarity/notation issues and minor errors.

Questions for authors
1. What base classifier and loss were used for f? How sensitive are results to regularization/C?
2. How do κ and q trade off accuracy vs. time? Please provide plots and recommended settings.
3. How is π normalized, and how do alternative choices (e.g., tempered inverse frequency) affect α and performance?
4. Did you explore hard-negative mining instead of uniform sampling of Y\{y}?
5. Can you integrate candidate selection into the theoretical analysis (e.g., recall of candidate generator)?

Minor comments
- Clarify the dyadic construction indices and the “if k < y” condition in the pseudo-code.
- Define all symbols where used (V[h], Hr, RT(S)(H), ωk).
- Fix inconsistent log(1/δ)/m terms and check constants.
- Briefly describe the 10 features in the main text.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper presents a practically useful and well-motivated reduction with novel dependent-data generalization analysis and strong large-scale results. With improved clarity and additional ablations, it would make a solid NIPS contribution.
