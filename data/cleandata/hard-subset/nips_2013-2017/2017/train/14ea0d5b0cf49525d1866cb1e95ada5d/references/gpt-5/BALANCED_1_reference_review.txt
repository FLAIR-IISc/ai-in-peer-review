Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to binary classification over dyadic example–label pairs. Instead of constructing all m(K−1) dyads, the authors propose an aggressive “double sampling” scheme: (i) resample the training set to counteract long-tailed label frequency (oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones), and (ii) for each retained instance, sample only κ “adversarial” negative labels rather than all K−1. A simple dyadic feature mapping tailored to text (10 similarity-style features inspired by learning-to-rank) is used. To justify learning on the reduced and dependent dyadic sample, the paper derives generalization bounds: first, for the full dyadic reduction with dependent examples using fractional/local Rademacher complexity (Theorem 1), and second, for the doubly sampled case (Theorem 2), with multiplicative factors dependent on the resampling probabilities (α, β). For prediction, the paper proposes a candidate selection step (q nearest class centroids) to further reduce inference cost. Experiments on LSHTC/DMOZ/Wikipedia subsets (up to 100K classes) compare to OVA, multi-class SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse, reporting accuracy and macro-F1 along with time and memory.

The work is closely related to prior dyadic reductions for multiclass and ranking [e.g., Usunier et al. 2006; Joshi et al. 2015], tree-based and embedding-based extreme classification [FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree], and negative sampling ideas common in extreme classification and NCE-style training. The theoretical analysis builds on fractional chromatic numbers for dependent data (Janson 2004), fractional Rademacher complexity (Usunier & Amini 2006), and local Rademacher complexity.

Strengths
- Practical and scalable idea: combining class-rebalancing with κ-negative sampling within a dyadic reduction is simple, effective, and broadly applicable.
- Theoretical contribution: bounds for learning with dependent dyads using local fractional Rademacher complexity; extension to the doubly sampled setting establishing ERM consistency despite distribution shift and dependence.
- Strong empirical emphasis on computational metrics (time, memory) alongside macro-F1, which is appropriate under long-tail class distributions.
- Candidate selection reduces prediction cost and is well motivated; the dyadic feature set is simple and cheap.
- Open-source code and datasets are provided, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical analysis of key design choices: no ablations on π (class reweighting), κ (number of negatives), or q (candidate set size), and no sensitivity analysis of accuracy vs. runtime/memory.
- The theoretical bounds involve α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y, which can become large when aggressively oversampling rare classes; the paper does not discuss regimes where these constants are controlled nor provide practical guidance to keep bounds meaningful.
- Clarity/notation issues in the theory: several typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., log terms with δ, “12” for 1/2, mismatch of indices), and limited intuition about the covering construction and how r (variance constraint) is chosen in practice.
- Feature mapping is highly heuristic and task-specific (10 hand-crafted text features). It is unclear how well the approach transfers to non-text modalities without learned φ, and whether gains are due mostly to rebalancing/negative sampling rather than the dyadic viewpoint.
- Baselines: while many strong baselines are included, the omission of some embedding methods is justified only briefly; more recent extreme linear methods (for the 2017–2018 timeframe) are not compared. Reporting for OVA/M-SVM on largest datasets is partly qualitative (statements about TB memory) rather than measured.
- Candidate selection introduces another approximation; a clear accuracy-vs-q tradeoff curve is missing.

Quality
The method is technically sound at a high level, and the theoretical framework is plausible and builds on established tools for dependent data. However, the presentation of the bounds could be tightened, and some constants/assumptions deserve scrutiny. Empirical results are encouraging but would be more convincing with ablations and clearer evidence that improvements stem from the proposed double sampling rather than only class rebalancing.

Clarity
Overall understandable, but the paper suffers from typos, dense notation, and occasional inconsistencies in equations and symbols. The role of α, β, r should be better explained, and the practical procedure for setting π, κ, q should be explicit. A brief complexity analysis (training/inference big-O in m, K, κ, q, and p) would help.

Originality
Moderate. Dyadic reductions and negative sampling are known; the main novelty lies in (i) combining aggressive class- and dyad-sampling for extreme multiclass, and (ii) providing local fractional Rademacher-based generalization bounds under dependence and sampling shift. This theoretical angle is a meaningful extension of prior work.

Significance
Potentially significant for practitioners dealing with extreme classification under long-tail distributions, given the demonstrated time/memory advantages and competitive macro-F1. The impact would be larger with stronger empirical validation (ablations, more datasets/modalities) and clearer guidance on parameter setting.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: vary κ and π (including uniform vs inverse-frequency), and q for candidate selection; plot accuracy/macro-F1 vs runtime/memory.
- Provide theoretical discussion/empirical checks of α and β magnitudes under your π choices; include a lemma or remark bounding them under inverse-frequency sampling.
- Clarify the feature mapping φ: which features dominate performance? Provide an experiment comparing the dyadic features to a simple linear OVA baseline on bag-of-words to isolate the effect of the reduction+sampling.
- Tighten the theory section: fix typos, standardize notation, and add intuition for the cover construction and the role of r; state assumptions explicitly (e.g., boundedness/Lipschitz).
- Report measured resource usage for all baselines wherever feasible; if not, provide precise extrapolation with methodology.
- Include accuracy-vs-q curves for candidate selection, and discuss centroid choice vs. alternative candidate generators.

Reasons to accept
- Practical, scalable method with strong computational savings and competitive macro-F1 on large K.
- Nontrivial theoretical analysis addressing dependence and sampling shift.
- Open-source implementation and clear relevance to NeurIPS topics.

Reasons to reject
- Missing ablations/sensitivity analyses on core design choices; unclear robustness.
- Theoretical bounds may be loose in regimes induced by aggressive resampling; limited guidance to practitioners.
- Clarity and presentation issues in the theory and experimental reporting.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea is promising and the theory is interesting, but the current version lacks crucial empirical ablations and clarity in the theoretical exposition. Addressing the above issues could elevate this to a solid contribution.
