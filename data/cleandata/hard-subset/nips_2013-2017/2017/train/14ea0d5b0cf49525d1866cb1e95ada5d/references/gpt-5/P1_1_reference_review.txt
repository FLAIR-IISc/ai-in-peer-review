Summary
The paper tackles extreme/multi-class classification by reducing it to a single binary problem over dyadic example–class pairs, while aggressively subsampling to keep the training set size and computational cost tractable. The key ideas are: (i) a “double sampling” strategy, first rebalancing the original dataset by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones (Sπ), then, for each retained example, sampling only κ “adversarial” (negative) classes to form dyadic pairs Tκ(Sπ); (ii) learning a single scorer f on dyadic features φ(x, y); and (iii) a candidate selection strategy at prediction time that restricts scoring to q nearest class centroids to reduce latency. Theoretically, the authors extend generalization bounds for dyadic reductions with dependent pairs using fractional graph covers to a local Rademacher complexity setting (Theorem 1) and derive bounds that explicitly account for the double sampling (Theorem 2), with dependence on the sampling probabilities π and κ. Empirically, on LSHTC/ Wikipedia-scale text datasets (up to 100k classes), the method shows strong accuracy/Macro-F1 with much lower training time and memory than OVA/M-SVM and competitive or better performance than tree-based extreme classifiers and PD-Sparse. The dyadic mapping uses 10 simple similarity features between document and class “megadocument/centroid,” inspired by learning-to-rank.

Relation to prior work
The reduction to dyadic binary classification is established (e.g., Weston & Watkins; AdaBoost.MR; Joshi et al. 2015 [16]). The use of fractional Rademacher complexity for dependent dyads is known, but the local complexity-based generalization bound appears novel and tighter in principle. Compared to label-embedding and tree-based approaches, this paper takes a different axis: keep a single scorer but aggressively thin the dyadic dataset. Distinct from error-correcting tournaments / logarithmic-time prediction, this is still essentially a one-vs-all scorer with candidate pruning. The empirical setting is similar to extreme classification literature (FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse).

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound reduction and a clear computational motivation: double sampling directly attacks long-tail imbalance and the quadratic blowup in dyads.
- Theoretical contribution: generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity for dependent data; Theorem 2 reflects the impact of κ and π, and accounts for distribution shift induced by sampling.
- Practicality: very compact dyadic features; single model; simple candidate selection strategy; open-source implementation.
- Solid empirical coverage on LSHTC/Wikipedia; competitive accuracy/MaF1 with substantially lower time/memory than OVA/M-SVM and competitive with or better than PD-Sparse/tree methods, especially at 50k–100k classes.

Weaknesses/limitations:
- The local RC-based bounds, while interesting, include factors α = max_y ηy/πy and β = max_y 1/πy that may be large when π is set inversely proportional to η. This potentially weakens the guarantees; guidance on how to set π to balance variance and bias is limited. An empirical sensitivity analysis of α, β, κ is missing.
- The method’s success hinges on the hand-crafted φ (10 features) tailored to bag-of-words text. Generality beyond text is asserted but not demonstrated; comparisons to learned φ (e.g., joint embeddings, deep encoders) are absent.
- Training objective/learner f is underspecified in the main text (loss/regularization/optimizer). The theory is for 0/1 loss; practical training presumably uses a convex surrogate, but the link is not discussed.
- Conversion from multilabel to multiclass via instance replication may distort class priors; it is unclear whether baselines are evaluated comparably and whether Macro-F1 is affected by this transformation.
- Candidate selection: q selection strategy and its impact on accuracy/runtime are not analyzed; no ablation to show the trade-off and failure modes (e.g., when the true class is not among q nearest centroids).
- Empirical comparisons do not include some strong recent extreme text classifiers that learn better representations or employ ANN candidate generation; while some are discussed (SLEEC/LEML), the landscape has evolved.

Clarity
- The paper is generally understandable but has several typos/formatting issues. Some notation is introduced abruptly; a small running example for the cover/graph dependency structure would help. Provide algorithmic details for f (loss, regularizer, hyperparameters) in the main text; currently deferred to the appendix.
- Please summarize Theorem 1/2 intuitions: how κ, π, K, and sample size M trade off, and when the bounds improve over prior results. A short paragraph interpreting α, β would be valuable.
- The feature mapping φ should be fully enumerated in the main paper (a small table or list), and an ablation on feature groups would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The double sampling scheme for dyadic reduction is a reasonable, pragmatic idea that, to my knowledge, is not standard; the theoretical analysis via local fractional RC in this context is the main novelty.
- The candidate selection at test time via centroids is simple and not new per se, but it integrates well here.

Significance
- The approach addresses a central bottleneck in dyadic reduction methods—data explosion—with practical speed/memory gains at scale and no accuracy loss. This is useful to practitioners working with large K on sparse text.
- The theoretical results will interest researchers studying learning with dependent examples and reduction strategies.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Provide sensitivity plots for κ, q, and π (e.g., π ∝ η^-γ with γ ∈ [0,1,2]) on at least one dataset, and report α, β to contextualize the bounds.
- Clarify the training objective for f, and, if using a surrogate, state any calibration/consistency argument w.r.t. the 0/1 loss in the bounds.
- Add ablations: (i) no rebalancing (uniform π), (ii) no negative subsampling (κ = K − 1), (iii) no candidate selection at prediction, to quantify each component.
- Compare to at least one learned dyadic embedding (e.g., WSABIE-style) or a strong linear baseline using bag-of-words with OVA but with class subsampling and negative sampling to isolate the benefit of the reduction vs feature engineering.
- Report the overhead for computing and storing class centroids and whether they need updates for online/streaming settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important scalability issue with a simple, effective method.
- Nontrivial theoretical contribution for dependent dyadic data with sampling.
- Strong empirical results on large benchmarks with code release.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and bound constants; practical guidance is thin.
- Heavy reliance on hand-crafted text-specific features; limited evidence of generality.
- Some missing details and ablations reduce reproducibility/diagnosability.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a useful, theoretically grounded and practical contribution to extreme multiclass classification. With improved clarity and more empirical analysis of design choices, it would be a strong accept.
