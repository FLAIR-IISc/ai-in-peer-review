- Proposal: aggressive double sampling—resample classes with probabilities πk to rebalance and sample κ adversarial negatives per example—to avoid O(mK) training blow-up; at test time use nearest-centroid candidate selection in dyadic feature space.

- Theory: Theorem 1 gives dependence-aware generalization bounds for dyadic reductions; Theorem 2 extends to the doubly-sampled, distribution-shifted setting using fractional covers and local Rademacher complexity, introducing α and β constants from sampling.

- Empirics: Evaluated on LSHTC variants up to 100K classes; shows competitive or superior macro-F1 and accuracy with much lower training time/memory than many baselines; OVA/M-SVM become infeasible at high K.

- Novelty: explicit double sampling for class-imbalance and negative downsampling in dyadic reductions, plus local-complexity generalization bounds handling dependency and distribution shift.

- Candidate selection: test-time nearest-centroid prefiltering is related to sampled softmax/negative sampling but applied in dyadic feature space.

- Theoretical critique: bounds are plausible and relevant, but α and β can be large for small π; more guidance on choosing π and explicit corollaries for standard hypothesis classes are needed.

- Presentation of theory: proofs in supplement; main text should better explain construction and role of the fractional cover and provide intuition for terms and rates.

- Method strengths: simple, scalable, robust; dyadic map φ is hand-crafted (10 features for text) which speeds inference but limits generality.

- Representation concern: baselines use BoW while the proposed method uses compact φ—fairness questions; need ablation comparing performance on identical representations (BoW vs φ).

- Missing ablations: no sensitivity analysis for κ, π, or candidate-set size q; paper should report κ, π, q per dataset and include ablations (e.g., π=η).

- Multilabel conversion: converting multilabel to multiclass via instance replication may bias distributions—effects should be discussed.

- Implementation details missing: specify base binary learner, loss, regularization, optimizer, and training complexity in terms of κ, M, and p.

- Clarity issues: typos and notational inconsistencies; precise definitions of φ and f, the loss used, and training complexity should be in main text.

- Fractional cover detail: description briefly assumes ωk=1—reconcile this simplification with formal fractional-cover definitions.

- Candidate selection clarity: centroid computation is clear but q should be specified and its impact on accuracy/runtime discussed.

- Originality emphasis: theoretical contribution—local-complexity bounds for doubly-sampled, dependent dyadic ERM—is the most original aspect.

- Significance: addresses an important practical bottleneck with a simple, implementable method supported by theory and large-scale experiments, likely useful if clarified.

- Pros summary: solves dyadic O(mK) blow-up via effective sampling; provides dependence- and shift-aware bounds; demonstrates strong empirical scaling; open-source code available.

- Cons summary: bounds contain large sampling-dependent constants with little tuning guidance; φ is text-specific and not learned; potential unfair comparisons and missing crucial ablations; clarity/presentation issues.

- Questions for authors requested: specify binary learner/loss/regularization and training complexity; provide κ, π, q per dataset and sensitivity analyses; specialize Theorem 2 to linear f with Lipschitz loss; detail π normalization and typical α, β values; ablation without candidate selection; compare baselines using same φ.

- Recommendation: Weak accept—practical, scalable method with nontrivial theory and convincing large-scale results, but requires clearer presentation and thorough ablations.
