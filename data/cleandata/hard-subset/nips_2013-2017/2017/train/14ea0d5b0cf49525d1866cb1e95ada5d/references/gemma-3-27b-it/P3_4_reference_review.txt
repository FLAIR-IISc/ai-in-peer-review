## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and practically motivated approach to tackling the computational challenges of extreme multi-class classification. The core idea of aggressive sampling, combining class balancing with reduced dyadic example construction, is well-justified and supported by both theoretical analysis (generalization bounds) and empirical results on large-scale text datasets. While the presentation could benefit from some clarity improvements, the work is technically sound, original, and demonstrates significant potential impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the well-known limitations of the one-vs-all (OVA) approach to extreme multi-class classification – computational intractability and class imbalance.  It positions itself within the two main branches of research addressing these limitations: label embedding methods and dyadic approaches.  The authors correctly identify the shortcomings of existing label embedding techniques (violation of low-rank assumptions) and dyadic methods (high computational cost of constructing all pairwise examples).  Their contribution, "Aggressive Double Sampling," aims to bridge the gap by strategically sampling the training data and reducing the number of dyadic examples constructed, while still providing strong theoretical guarantees. This builds upon prior work by Joshi et al. (2016) on dyadic representations but crucially adds the sampling strategy to address scalability. The theoretical foundation leverages local Rademacher complexity and graph covering arguments, drawing from recent advances in generalization bounds for dependent data. 

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of class-balancing sampling *with* a reduction in dyadic example construction is a genuinely novel contribution.  Simply sampling isn’t enough; the interplay between balancing the distribution and the dependency structure created by the dyadic transformation is clever.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of new generalization bounds is a significant strength.  The use of Local Rademacher complexity and the connection to graph covering theory provide a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The proof sketch in the supplementary material seems reasonable, and the theorems presented offer valuable insights into the consistency of the approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experiments on the DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.  The results clearly show that (π, κ)-DS achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of both runtime and memory usage – critical factors for extreme multi-class problems. The comparison against baselines like OVA, M-SVM, FastXML and PD-Sparse is well-conducted.
* **Practical Relevance:** The proposed method directly addresses a key bottleneck in extreme multi-class classification – scalability.  The demonstrated ability to handle datasets with 100,000 classes is highly significant.
* **Clear Motivation:** The problem statement and motivation are clearly articulated. The authors effectively highlight the challenges of existing approaches and position their work as a compelling solution.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Presentation:**  The paper suffers somewhat from dense writing and a lack of intuitive explanation in certain sections.  For example, the discussion of fractional Rademacher complexity (Section 2.1) feels rather abstract and could benefit from a more concrete explanation of how it relates to the sampling strategy.  Similarly, the connection between the graph covering definition and the generalization bounds isn’t immediately obvious.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the experiments demonstrate good performance, the sensitivity of the (π, κ)-DS algorithm to the hyperparameters π and κ isn’t thoroughly investigated. A more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of these parameters on performance would strengthen the paper. The hyperparameters used in the experiments are reported in the supplementary material, but a discussion of *why* those values were chosen is missing.
* **Feature Mapping:** The feature mapping φ described in Section 3.1 is somewhat glossed over.  While the authors mention its inspiration from learning to rank, more details about the specific features used and their relevance to the task would be helpful.  Is this feature mapping optimal, or are there alternative mappings that could further improve performance?
* **Limited Comparison:** The authors justify excluding SLEEC and LEML by citing the work of [30]. However, a brief discussion of why those methods are particularly susceptible to the issues that (π, κ)-DS addresses would be more convincing.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a crucial problem in extreme multi-class classification.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on large-scale datasets.
* Offers a practical solution with significant scalability advantages.
* The theoretical contributions regarding generalization bounds are valuable.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  The presentation can be dense and difficult to follow in certain sections.
*  The sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters needs further investigation.
*  The feature mapping details are lacking.



**5. Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.  Specifically, the authors should:

* **Improve Clarity:**  Revise sections 2.1 and 2.2 to provide more intuitive explanations of the key concepts.  Consider adding illustrative examples.
* **Hyperparameter Analysis:**  Conduct an ablation study to assess the sensitivity of the (π, κ)-DS algorithm to the values of π and κ.  Justify the chosen parameter settings.
* **Feature Mapping Details:** Provide more details about the feature mapping φ and discuss its limitations and potential alternatives.
* **Address Comparison Concerns:** Briefly discuss why SLEEC and LEML were excluded and how the proposed method differs in its approach.




This paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. Addressing the minor revisions suggested above will further enhance its clarity and impact.
