

# Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper proposes a "doubly-sampled" approach to reduce multi-class classification to binary classification by aggressively sampling from the original training set and reducing dyadic examples. The authors claim their method addresses two key limitations of existing approaches: computational intractability with large class numbers and class imbalance.

## Technical Issues and Inconsistencies

The paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies that undermine its theoretical contributions. The most severe issue appears in Theorem 1, where the generalization bound is written as:
"R(h) ≤ R̃T(S)(h) + RT(S)(` ◦ Hr) + 5(√RT(S)(` ◦ Hr) + √r)√log 1δ m + 25 48 log 1δ m"

This expression is mathematically incoherent. The term "√log 1δ m" should be "√(log(1/δ)/m)" or similar, and "25 48 log 1δ m" is missing proper spacing and parentheses. The authors appear to have missed basic mathematical notation conventions, making the bound impossible to interpret correctly. This is a serious flaw in a paper claiming theoretical contributions.

Theorem 2 contains similar issues with inconsistent notation. The expression "α√(K−1)log 2δ Mκ" lacks proper parentheses and should be "α√((K-1)log(2/δ)/(Mκ))" to be meaningful. The authors also inconsistently use "log 1δ" (missing slash) throughout, which should be "log(1/δ)".

The paper claims to use "Local Rademacher Complexities" but fails to properly define them or explain why they're superior to the fractional Rademacher complexity used in previous work [25]. The authors cite [22] as the source for "Local Rademacher Complexities" but the cited work [22] doesn't actually introduce this concept - it's a misattribution that undermines the theoretical foundation.

## Methodological Concerns

The sampling strategy (π, κ)-DS is described but lacks clear guidance on hyperparameter selection. The authors claim πk ∝ 1/μk (where μk is class proportion) but don't provide a theoretical justification for this specific choice. The paper also claims to "overcome the curse of long-tailed class distributions" but provides no theoretical guarantee for this claim.

The experimental section has significant issues. The paper claims to convert LSHTC2 datasets from multi-label to multi-class format but doesn't explain the exact conversion procedure. The paper refers to "WIKI-50K" and "WIKI-100K" as datasets but doesn't specify the exact number of classes (the paper claims "up to 100,000 target categories" but doesn't clarify if WIKI-100K has exactly 100,000 classes).

The evaluation metrics are problematic - the paper uses both accuracy and macro F1, but claims macro F1 "is not affected by class imbalance" which is incorrect. Macro F1 is affected by class imbalance, though less so than accuracy.

## Presentation and Formatting

The paper contains numerous formatting errors that make it difficult to read. The phrase "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" appears to be a typo (should be "at"). The paper claims to be from NIPS 2017 but references work from 2017, which is inconsistent with the submission year.

Equation (1) has inconsistent notation with "g(xy)≤g(xy′ )" without proper spacing around the inequality. The paper also uses inconsistent spacing in mathematical expressions throughout.

The related work section is inadequate - it fails to properly compare with recent extreme classification methods like SLEEC and LEML, which the authors claim were outperformed by baselines but don't actually evaluate against.

## Strengths

The paper identifies a genuine problem in large-scale multi-class classification and proposes an interesting approach. The experimental results do show promising improvements in memory usage and runtime compared to some baselines. The idea of reducing inter-dependency between dyadic examples through sampling is conceptually interesting.

## Conclusion

Despite the interesting problem and promising experimental results, the paper suffers from multiple critical technical errors in its theoretical contributions. The mathematical formulations contain fundamental notational errors that make the theoretical results impossible to verify. The paper's claims about theoretical guarantees are undermined by these inconsistencies.

The formatting and presentation issues further diminish the paper's credibility. As a peer-reviewed publication, the paper should meet the highest standards of mathematical rigor and clarity. The numerous technical errors in the theoretical contributions, combined with the presentation issues, make it impossible to evaluate the paper's scientific contribution with confidence.

The authors should address these fundamental mathematical errors before considering submission to a top-tier venue. The paper as currently written does not meet NIPS' standards for technical rigor and clarity.

**Recommendation: Reject**
