- Problem: Addresses extreme multi-class classification’s computational and class imbalance issues by reducing multi-class to binary via dyadic representation.
- Method: Proposes "aggressive double sampling"—oversample minority classes and subsample majority classes plus reduce dyadic example construction.
- Theory: Provides new generalization bounds based on local Rademacher complexity and connects analysis to graph covering ideas.
- Empirics: Shows runtime, memory, accuracy, and MaF1 improvements on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia) versus strong baselines.
- Relation to prior work: Positions itself between label-space reduction (tree/embedding) and problem-space dyadic reductions, building on prior dyadic methods.
- Motivation vs prior methods: Highlights limitations of tree-based balancing and embedding low-rank assumptions that its method avoids.
- Novelty strength: The aggressive double sampling strategy is creative and well-motivated for balancing and computational savings.
- Theoretical strength: The local Rademacher-based generalization bounds are a significant theoretical contribution.
- Empirical strength: Demonstrates substantial gains for very large class counts (50k, 100k) and includes practical candidate selection for prediction.
- Writing strength: Paper is generally well-written, organized, and uses clear mathematical notation.
- Hyperparameter weakness: Sensitivity to sampling hyperparameters (π, κ) is not sufficiently explored; needs ablation.
- Feature representation weakness: The feature mapping φ is treated somewhat ad-hoc and lacks detailed discussion of limitations or alternatives.
- Comparison weakness: Experimental comparisons omit some recent extreme classification methods (additional tree- or embedding-based baselines desirable).
- Proof/supplement weakness: Theorem 2 proof is deferred to supplement; the supplement must be especially clear and self-contained.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically grounded, empirically compelling, and practically mindful approach to a hard problem.
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited hyperparameter analysis, ad-hoc feature choices, and incomplete baseline comparisons.
- Overall assessment: Strong paper with convincing theory and experiments; merits acceptance with minor revisions.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions requesting (1) ablation for (π, κ), (2) expanded discussion of φ, (3) comparison to more recent methods if possible, and (4) clearer supplementary proofs.
