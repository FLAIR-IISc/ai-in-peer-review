Summary
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem over dyadic (example, class) pairs, but without constructing all pairs. It proposes an aggressive double sampling scheme (π, κ)-DS: (i) resample the training set to balance long-tailed class distributions (oversample rare classes, subsample frequent ones) and (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ “adversarial” negative classes to form dyads. This substantially shrinks the dyadic dataset. Because dyadic examples are dependent (multiple pairs share the same input example) and the sampling skews the original class distribution, the authors develop generalization bounds using fractional/local Rademacher complexity for dependent data, showing ERM consistency under the proposed sampling. For prediction, they further prune candidate classes using nearest centroids to cut test-time cost. Experiments on LSHTC (DMOZ/Wikipedia; up to 100K classes) report competitive or better macro-F1 and accuracy with much lower training time and memory than OVA/M-SVM/PD-Sparse, and accuracy advantages over tree/embedding baselines.

Quality
– Theory: The paper addresses dependence induced by dyadic reduction and distribution shift introduced by sampling. Theorems 1 and 2 are plausible instantiations of concentration and Rademacher complexity tools for dependent sums (fractional covers/Janson, local Rademacher complexities). The introduction of α and β terms to capture sampling distortion is sensible. However:
  • Important details are deferred to the supplement; the main text does not provide interpretable upper bounds on the fractional/local Rademacher complexities in terms of m, K, κ, class imbalance, or graph fractional chromatic number. As written, the bounds are hard to interpret or apply.
  • Some terms and constants look suspicious (e.g., the last term in Theorem 2 appears dimensionally inconsistent; several √log(1/δ)/m-style factors appear without clear derivation).
  • The impact of π choices on α and β (and thus on rates) is not analyzed; guidelines are empirical rather than theoretically grounded.
  • There is at least one citation mismatch: the text attributes “fractional Rademacher complexity” to [25], but the reference list shows [25] as “Collaborative ranking…”, which is unrelated. This undermines confidence in the literature grounding of the analysis.
Overall, the analysis direction is technically interesting but insufficiently tight or transparent in the main paper to fully validate the claims.

– Empirics: The empirical study is thoughtfully designed for large K, uses popular LSHTC datasets, and compares against solid baselines (OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree). The method shows favorable time/memory and competitive or better macro-F1 for 50K–100K classes, which supports the practicality of the proposal. Concerns:
  • Missing ablations on π and κ to quantify the accuracy/runtime trade-offs and to corroborate the balancing claim.
  • The candidate selection parameter q is not analyzed; no study of its effect on accuracy and latency.
  • The dyadic feature mapping φ is only sketched (p=10 features inspired by L2R); details are said to be in Table 1 but are not in the main text, making it hard to judge whether gains are due to sampling vs features.
  • Converting multi-label to multi-class by duplicating instances may distort distributional properties; a discussion of implications for macro-F1 would help.
  • No statistical significance tests are reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable at a high level but suffers from notation/typo issues and missing details:
– The dyadic transform notation is hard to parse (indices k reused for classes and examples; z definitions mix φ(xk) and φ(xy)).
– Several equations contain formatting errors, and some terms appear inconsistent dimensionally.
– The φ mapping and candidate selection procedure require clearer description in the main text to ensure reproducibility.
– References have mismatches and some key attributions are unclear.

Originality
Algorithmically, the double sampling is a fairly natural combination of class-balanced subsampling and negative sampling for pairwise losses—ideas known in extreme classification and ranking (e.g., WSABIE/WARP, sampled negatives). The main novelty is the generalization analysis for a double-sampled, dependent dyadic reduction using local fractional Rademacher complexity. That theoretical angle is original and potentially useful if clarified. The candidate-selection at prediction is a standard nearest-centroid pruning.

Significance
The problem is important and the method is simple, scalable, and effective on large K. Practitioners could adopt (π, κ)-DS easily, and the results suggest a favorable accuracy/runtime/memory trade-off versus strong baselines. The theoretical contribution could stimulate further work on dependent-data learning with sampling, but its impact is limited by clarity and missing explicit, interpretable rates. Overall, significance is moderate: practical utility is solid; theoretical novelty is promising but underdeveloped in the main text.

Pros
– Addresses a key bottleneck in dyadic reductions with a simple, effective sampling scheme.
– Provides dependence-aware generalization bounds; interesting use of local Rademacher complexity.
– Strong scaling properties demonstrated on up to 100K classes; competitive macro-F1 versus strong baselines.
– Open-source code.

Cons
– Clarity issues in notation, theorem statements, and references; some terms appear incorrect.
– Limited theoretical interpretability; no concrete bounds in terms of m, K, κ, imbalance.
– Missing ablations on π, κ, and candidate selection q; φ mapping underspecified in main text.
– Algorithmic novelty is incremental relative to known negative sampling/balancing strategies.

Questions/requests for authors
1) Provide explicit upper bounds for RT(` ◦ Hr) under your dependency graph (per-example cliques) and show how they scale with m, K, κ, and class imbalance.
2) Clarify and correct the reference for fractional Rademacher complexity, and fix the apparent typos in Theorem 2.
3) Detail how π is set in practice (normalization, caps to ensure π ∈ [0,1]); analyze how α, β behave under your policy.
4) Add ablations for κ and q, and a sensitivity analysis for π; report the trade-off curves.
5) Describe φ fully in the main text (or an appendix) to ensure reproducibility; clarify how class centroids are computed given the dyadic nature of features.
6) Discuss implications of converting multi-label to multi-class via duplication on evaluation.

Overall rating: 6 (weak accept)
Rationale: Strong practical contribution with convincing large-scale results and a theoretically motivated framework. However, clarity and rigor need improvement, and algorithmic novelty is moderate. If the authors can clean up the analysis and presentation, this would be a useful addition for NeurIPS. Confidence: 3/5.
