## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel approach to address the scalability challenges of multi-class classification, particularly in extreme settings. The proposed aggressive sampling technique, combined with theoretical analysis and experimental validation, demonstrates a promising direction for improving the efficiency and effectiveness of multi-class learning. While the paper has strengths in its technical soundness, clarity, and experimental results, some areas could benefit from further elaboration and comparison to more recent work. I recommend **accepting** the paper, with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of extreme multi-class classification, focusing on the limitations of one-vs-all (OVA) approaches and existing dimensionality reduction techniques. It proposes a novel “aggressive double sampling” strategy within a dyadic representation framework. This involves balancing class distributions and reducing the number of dyadic examples to improve computational efficiency. A key contribution is the derivation of new generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity, establishing the consistency of the empirical risk minimization principle even with the introduced sampling biases. The paper appropriately situates itself within the existing literature, acknowledging both tree-based and embedding-based methods, and related work on dyadic representations.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis providing generalization bounds is a significant strength. Deriving bounds for a sampled distribution and dependent variables is non-trivial, and the authors convincingly demonstrate consistency. The use of local Rademacher complexity and graph covering concepts is appropriate and well-explained.
* **Novelty:** The aggressive double sampling strategy is a novel approach to addressing the class imbalance and computational cost issues in extreme multi-class classification. It’s a clever way to combine balancing and dimensionality reduction.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia) demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The results show competitive performance compared to strong baselines, particularly regarding runtime and memory usage, crucial for large-scale problems.  The careful parameter tuning and reporting of results add to the credibility.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are relatively easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with the technical details of Rademacher complexity.
* **Significance:** The scalability improvements offered by the method are significant for real-world applications dealing with massive multi-class datasets, such as text classification and recommendation systems.

**3. Weaknesses & Areas for Improvement:**

* **Comparison to More Recent Baselines:** While the chosen baselines are well-established, the field of extreme multi-class classification has progressed since some of them were published.  Including comparisons to more recent methods like those leveraging deep learning (e.g., methods based on embedding spaces and neural networks) would strengthen the evaluation.  A discussion of why those approaches weren’t included (e.g., computational cost of training) would be helpful.
* **Elaboration on Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions parameter tuning but lacks detailed discussion on the sensitivity of performance to the parameters π (sampling probabilities) and κ (number of adversarial classes). Providing insights into how these parameters affect results and guidelines for choosing appropriate values would enhance the paper’s practical utility.
* **Discussion of Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on certain assumptions (e.g., i.i.d. data).  A discussion on the robustness of the method to violations of these assumptions in real-world scenarios would be valuable.
* **Candidate Selection Detail:** Algorithm 1 (Prediction with Candidate Selection) seems important for runtime, but the explanation is brief. Expanding on the choice of ‘q’ nearest-centroid classes and how it impacts performance would be beneficial.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and novel approach.
* Compelling experimental results demonstrating improved scalability and competitive accuracy.
* Clear writing and organization.
* Addresses a significant problem in machine learning.

**Against:**

* Limited comparison to the most recent advancements in extreme multi-class classification.
* Insufficient discussion of parameter sensitivity and robustness to assumptions.

**5. Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper with minor revisions. The authors have presented a well-motivated and technically sound approach to a challenging problem. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly by including comparisons to more recent baselines and providing more detail on parameter tuning – would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. The theoretical contributions and practical benefits of the proposed method are significant enough to warrant publication in NIPS.
