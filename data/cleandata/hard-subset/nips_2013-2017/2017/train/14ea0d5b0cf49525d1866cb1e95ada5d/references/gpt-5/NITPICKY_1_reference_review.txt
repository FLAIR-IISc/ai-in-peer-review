Summary
The paper tackles large-scale multiclass (extreme) classification by reducing to a binary dyadic problem while aggressively subsampling. The core idea is a two-stage sampling scheme: (i) rebalance the original dataset by oversampling small classes and subsampling large ones according to class-dependent probabilities π; (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ “adversarial” classes to form dyadic pairs, instead of all K−1. The authors provide generalization bounds under dependence via fractional graph covers and local Rademacher complexities, arguing consistency despite class-prior shift and pairwise dependence. A candidate selection heuristic at prediction time considers only q nearest-centroid classes. Experiments on LSHTC/Wikipedia subsets (up to 100k classes) suggest competitive accuracy/MaF1 with substantially lower time/memory vs strong baselines. Code is provided.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important regime (K up to 100k) with a simple, scalable reduction and sound intuition (mitigate long-tail imbalance; avoid O(mK) dyads).
- The theoretical development attempts to marry fractional covers with local Rademacher complexity to reflect dependence and variance, and extends prior dyadic bounds.
- The approach is easy to implement and tune (π, κ, q) and is accompanied by code.
- Empirically competitive on large text datasets; large memory/time savings vs OVA/M-SVM and PD-Sparse; better accuracy/MaF1 than tree/embedding methods on reported tasks.

Major weaknesses
- Clarity and completeness: Several core components are underspecified.
  - Learning algorithm: The binary learner f is not concretely defined (loss, regularization, optimization). The text mentions 0/1 loss in theorems but experiments presumably use a convex surrogate (e.g., linear SVM via LibLinear?). This needs to be explicit, including hyperparameters and convergence criteria.
  - Feature map φ: You state “p=10 features” inspired by learning to rank and refer to Table 1, but the features are not listed/described here. For reproducibility and to assess fairness against baselines, these must be enumerated and justified.
  - Candidate selection: The definition of class centroids and the metric used for nearest-centroid selection is ambiguous. Centroids cannot be in the dyadic space (φ(x,y) depends on x), yet you claim they are “computed once in the preliminary projection … in the dyadic feature space.” Likely you use input-space centroids; please clarify. Also, q is not reported, nor is the accuracy drop as a function of q.
- Theoretical issues:
  - The dependence graph and fractional chromatic number are not characterized, leaving unclear why the bound in Theorem 1 scales with m (original examples) rather than N=m(K−1) dyads. A short lemma analyzing χ∗ for the star-clique structure induced by each x would resolve this.
  - Theorems rely on Hr with variance constraint V[1ỹh(z)]≤r and on RT(S)(`◦Hr), but assumptions on H/F (e.g., linear class with bounded norms; bounded features) are not stated. Without such conditions, the bounds are vacuous.
  - The constants and terms in Theorem 2 are hard to interpret. α=maxy ηy/πy and β=maxy 1/πy appear alongside both M and m in denominators; why does the bound depend on m when training uses M? A short proof sketch explaining the two-stage sampling’s contribution (S→Sπ then Tκ) would help. Also, the κ dependence appears only as 1/√(Mκ) in one term; is that tight?
  - Consistency under class-prior shift is claimed, but the algorithm does not reweight the loss to correct for π; the bound introduces α, yet the practical learner seems unweighted. A discussion of the bias introduced by π (especially when reporting accuracy) is missing.
  - No guarantees are provided for candidate selection (e.g., probability that argmax over Y lies in Yx as a function of q; a recall@q analysis).
- Experimental methodology and fairness:
  - Data handling: LSHTC2 is multilabel; converting to multiclass by replication changes priors and dependence. How are train/test splits affected? Are duplicates in test? How does this impact accuracy/MaF1?
  - Representation mismatch: It seems your method uses a compact 10-d feature representation, whereas baselines likely use the original high-dimensional text features. This makes time/memory comparisons unfair and may also affect accuracy. Baselines should also be run with the same 10-d representation, or your method should be run with the same bag-of-words input, to disentangle algorithmic from representational gains.
  - Missing ablations: There is no sensitivity study for κ, π (shape of rebalancing), or q (candidate set size). These hyperparameters are central to the claimed trade-offs.
  - Statistics: No variance/error bars, no multiple random seeds/splits, and no statistical tests.
  - Baseline tuning details are relegated to the supplement; key settings (e.g., PD-Sparse hashing dimension; FastXML/PfastReXML parameters) should be summarized. Some baselines (tree/embedding methods) are designed for multilabel; converting to multiclass may not be the most favorable choice—please discuss.
- Presentation and formatting:
  - Numerous typographical/formatting issues: stray text (“st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems…” in the middle of the intro); inconsistent spacing, punctuation, and equation formatting; inconsistent notation (e.g., indices, ≤0 in indicators); footnote markers without footnotes; missing figure/table content in the main text (Tables 1–3, Figure 3 referenced but not shown here).
  - References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing years/venues, missing page numbers; some arXiv entries lack details). Several relevant lines of work on sampled losses/negative sampling and candidate retrieval are not cited (e.g., NCE/negative sampling for large output spaces; sampled softmax).
  - The name “local fractional Rademacher complexity” is nonstandard; you seem to combine local Rademacher complexity with fractional covers. Please use standard terminology and clearly separate the two notions.

Quality: The core idea is reasonable and the empirical results are promising, but the paper lacks critical methodological details, thorough ablations, and a clear, tight theoretical exposition. As written, claims are only partially supported.

Clarity: Currently below NIPS standards. The manuscript needs substantial editing for precision and readability, with complete definitions, assumptions, and a consistent notation.

Originality: The combination of dyadic reduction with aggressive double sampling and the use of fractional-cover bounds is a useful twist on prior pairwise reductions. However, the sampling ideas are closely related to existing negative sampling/sampled softmax strategies; this connection should be made explicit.

Significance: If properly validated and clarified, the method could be useful in extreme classification pipelines. In its current form, the impact is limited by the presentation and evaluation issues.

Pros
- Simple, scalable reduction with practical speed/memory gains
- Attempted theoretical analysis under dependence
- Open-source implementation
- Competitive performance on large datasets

Cons
- Insufficient methodological detail and unclear training setup
- Theoretical bounds lack stated assumptions and clear interpretation
- Unfair/unclear experimental comparisons; missing ablations and statistics
- Numerous presentation and formatting problems; incomplete references

Recommendation: Reject

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely specify f, loss, regularization, optimization, and all hyperparameters in the main paper. List the 10 features explicitly.
- Provide a formal complexity analysis in terms of m, K, κ, p for training and prediction; discuss memory footprint.
- Clarify centroid computation and candidate selection; report q and its effect on accuracy/latency; consider providing recall@q for the true label.
- Add ablations over κ and π, and sensitivity to q. Include error bars over multiple runs.
- Ensure fair comparisons by controlling for feature representations and data handling; discuss implications of the multilabel-to-multiclass conversion.
- Tighten the theory: state assumptions, characterize the dependence graph, justify m vs N scaling, and explain the roles of α, β, M, m. Include at least a proof sketch in the main text.
- Thoroughly edit the manuscript; fix references and remove formatting glitches.
