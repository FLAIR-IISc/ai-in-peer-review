- Problem & approach: Reduce extreme/multi-class classification to dyadic binary comparisons using joint representation φ(x,y) and learn h(z)=f(φ(x,y))−f(φ(x,y′)).
- Double sampling: Introduce (π,κ)-DS—class-dependent resampling π to handle long tails and sampling κ negative labels per example to avoid K−1 dyads.
- Theory: Provide generalization bounds for dependent dyads using fractional/local Rademacher complexity and for the double-sampled setting under label-shift, with terms depending on π, κ, K.
- Prediction: Speed up inference via centroid-based candidate class selection in the dyadic space.
- Experiments: Competitive accuracy and macro-F1 on large text datasets (up to 100K classes) with lower time/memory than OVA/M-SVM and better than many tree methods.
- Strength: Practical, simple sampling recipe that mitigates quadratic dyadic blow-up and is easy to implement.
- Strength: Nontrivial generalization guarantees accounting for dependent dyads and sampling-induced distribution shift.
- Strength: Strong empirical trade-offs between accuracy, time, and memory demonstrating feasibility at scale.
- Strength: Pragmatic pipeline with sensible candidate selection to further reduce costs.
- Weakness: Missing reproducibility details—exact φ features, learning algorithm (loss/regularization/optimizer), and key hyperparameters π, κ, q not specified in main paper.
- Weakness: Theory lacks concrete rates, comparisons to existing bounds, and empirical validation of theoretical predictions (e.g., dependence on κ, K, class imbalance).
- Weakness: Candidate selection is heuristic with no analysis of recall or of accuracy vs. candidate set size q; centroid definition unclear for pairwise φ.
- Weakness: Experimental scope limited to text; no non-text datasets or learned φ; missing comparisons to close methods (WSABIE/LEML/SLEEC, sampled-softmax/negative-sampling rankers).
- Weakness: Converting multi-label to multi-class by duplicating instances may bias comparisons; implications not discussed.
- Quality: Technically sound approach and appropriate use of graph-cover/fractional Rademacher tools, but proofs omitted from main text and theory–practice linkage underused.
- Experiments need: ablations on κ and q, sensitivity analyses for π, accuracy/time breakdown with/without candidate selection, and binary learner training details.
- Clarity: Generally organized but dense notation; main text should include φ feature list, classifier/training details, and fractional cover description.
- Originality: Novel package combining aggressive example/negative sampling with dyadic reduction and dependent-data generalization bounds.
- Significance: High practical significance for extreme text classification; moderate theoretical significance needing clearer takeaways.
- Questions for authors: Which binary loss/optimizer/regularization was used? concrete κ and q values and sensitivities? exact centroid construction for pairwise φ? tried importance weighting for π-shift? candidate recall vs q?
- Suggestions: Add φ and training details, ablation/sensitivity analyses, explicit corollary with rates (dependence on κ,K,μ), comparisons to sampled negative rankers, and report Precision@k.
- Pros for acceptance: Simple, scalable method with strong empirical performance and solid theoretical treatment of dependency and shift.
- Cons against acceptance: Missing crucial experimental details/ablations, incomplete baseline/domain coverage, and unrigorous candidate selection evaluation.
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on adding ablations and clarifying implementation and theoretical/practical links.
