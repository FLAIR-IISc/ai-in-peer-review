This paper addresses the problem of extreme multi-class classification, focusing on improving the scalability and performance of the multi-class to binary reduction approach. The core idea is an “aggressive double sampling” strategy, which balances class distributions and reduces the number of dyadic examples constructed. The authors provide theoretical generalization bounds and demonstrate empirical improvements on large-scale text datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly presenting the limitations of existing approaches (OVA, tree-based, embedding-based, and previous dyadic reduction methods) and positioning their work as a response to those limitations. The introduction effectively summarizes the state-of-the-art and highlights the contributions of the paper. The theoretical analysis, particularly the derivation of generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity, is a significant strength. The experimental results on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets are convincing, showing competitive performance with improved efficiency in terms of both memory and runtime.  The inclusion of a detailed experimental setup with hyperparameter tuning details is commendable, enhancing reproducibility.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the connection between the theoretical results (Theorem 1 & 2) and the practical benefits observed in the experiments isn’t explicitly drawn. It would be helpful to discuss how the parameters of the sampling strategy (π, κ) relate to the theoretical bounds and how they were chosen based on those bounds. The description of the feature mapping φ is brief, and it's unclear how sensitive the performance is to this choice. A more thorough ablation study varying the features used within φ would be valuable.  The paper frequently refers to “state-of-the-art” methods without fully justifying why these specific baselines were chosen. For example, while PD-Sparse is included, other recent and potentially relevant methods (e.g., SLEEC, LEML – acknowledged in the references but dismissed without detailed justification) could also have been considered.  The writing, while generally clear, occasionally suffers from dense mathematical notation and could benefit from more intuitive explanations, especially when introducing new definitions (e.g., Definition 1 – fractional cover). The presentation of the results, while comprehensive, could be streamlined. While Table 3 provides a lot of information, it might be easier to interpret the key findings with a few focused charts highlighting performance vs. runtime/memory.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The aggressive double sampling strategy is a novel approach to address the scalability challenges of multi-class to binary reduction.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous generalization bounds, grounding the empirical results in theoretical analysis.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in performance and efficiency compared to existing methods on large-scale datasets.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The proposed method addresses a challenging problem in machine learning with real-world applications in text classification and beyond.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Ablation Study:** The impact of the feature mapping φ is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Justification of Baselines:** The selection of baseline methods could be better justified and compared with more recent approaches.
*   **Connection between Theory and Practice:** The link between the theoretical bounds and the practical parameter tuning of the sampling strategy is not fully articulated.
*   **Dense Notation:** The mathematical presentation can be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the specific theoretical tools used.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. The novelty of the proposed method, coupled with its solid theoretical foundation and compelling empirical results, makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While there are areas for improvement, particularly in clarifying the connection between theory and practice and conducting a more thorough ablation study, these weaknesses do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the relationship between the parameters of the sampling strategy (π, κ) and the theoretical bounds presented in the paper.
2.  Provide a more detailed justification for the selection of baseline methods and consider including other relevant recent approaches.
3.  Elaborate on the feature mapping φ and conduct an ablation study to assess its impact on performance.
4.  Improve the clarity of the mathematical notation and provide more intuitive explanations of key concepts.
5.  Streamline the presentation of the results with focused charts highlighting the key findings.
