This paper addresses the problem of large-scale multi-class classification, focusing on the challenges of computational intractability and class imbalance inherent in one-vs-all (OVA) approaches. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling strategy to reduce the dyadic representation used in previous work, aiming for scalability and improved performance. They also provide theoretical guarantees in the form of generalization bounds and validate their approach on large text datasets.

The core idea – aggressively sampling to balance class distributions and reduce the number of dyadic examples – is a sensible approach to tackling the computational burden of existing methods. The derivation of generalization bounds, even with the introduced shifts in distribution and inter-dependencies, is a significant theoretical contribution. The experimental results on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets demonstrate competitive performance, particularly in terms of runtime and memory usage, compared to existing state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a relevant problem:** Large-scale multi-class classification is an important and challenging problem in machine learning.
*   **Novel approach:** The aggressive double sampling strategy offers a potentially effective way to improve the scalability of dyadic representation methods.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The provided generalization bounds offer a theoretical justification for the proposed approach.
*   **Empirical validation:** The experimental results on large datasets demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the method, particularly in terms of efficiency.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its theoretical foundations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The performance likely depends on careful tuning of the (π, κ) parameters. The paper provides some details in the supplementary material, but a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity to these parameters would be beneficial.
*   **Limited comparison:** While comparisons are made to several baselines, the selection is not exhaustive. More recent methods beyond those excluded (e.g., based on deep learning) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
*   **Complexity of implementation:** The algorithm involves several steps (dyadic transformation, sampling, classifier training), which could make implementation and optimization challenging.
*   **Feature Mapping:** The paper relies on a specific feature mapping (φ) tailored to text classification. While the authors note its applicability to other domains, a discussion of how to adapt or learn φ in different contexts would be valuable.
*   **Clarity of Theorem 2:** Theorem 2 is complex and relies on several parameters (α, β). A clearer explanation of these parameters and their relationship to the underlying data distribution would enhance understanding.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the practical effectiveness and efficiency of the approach.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The hyperparameter sensitivity and implementation complexity may limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The comparison to existing methods is not fully comprehensive.
*   Some aspects of the theoretical analysis could be clarified for better understanding.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale multi-class classification. The proposed aggressive double sampling strategy offers a promising approach to addressing the computational challenges of existing methods, and the theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the method. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the comments regarding hyperparameter sensitivity, comparison to recent methods, and clarity of Theorem 2.
