

# Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper proposes a "double sampling" strategy for reducing multi-class classification to binary classification, aiming to address scalability issues in extreme multi-class problems. The authors introduce a (π, κ)-DS algorithm that first oversamples small classes and subsamples large classes, then reduces the number of dyadic examples. The paper claims to provide generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity, and demonstrates empirical improvements in memory usage and prediction time on large text classification datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The problem addressed is relevant to the NIPS community, as large-scale classification remains a significant challenge.
- The proposed double sampling approach appears to be a practical solution for handling long-tailed class distributions.
- The experimental results on Wikipedia datasets show promising improvements in memory usage and runtime compared to state-of-the-art methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Inconsistencies:**
   - Equation (1) contains a formatting error: "1 K − 1" should be "1/(K-1)".
   - Equation (3) redundantly defines j = (i-1)(K-1)+k twice.
   - Equation (6) has inconsistent normalization: R̃Tκ(Sπ)(h) = 1/(κM) * sum, while Eq. (2) uses 1/[m(K-1)].
   - Theorem 2 contains an unclear term: "√ 2α log 4Kδ β(m− 1)" appears to be missing parentheses and should be "√[2α log(4K/δ)/β(m-1)]" or similar.

2. **Methodological Gaps:**
   - The paper claims to use "local fractional Rademacher complexity" but fails to clearly connect this to the referenced work in [22] and [25].
   - The explanation of why the (π, κ)-DS strategy overcomes long-tailed distributions is vague and lacks theoretical justification beyond "oversampling small classes and subsampling large classes."
   - The paper states "the low-rank assumption of the label-matrix is generally transgressed" (should be "violated"), but provides no evidence or explanation for this claim.

3. **Experimental Issues:**
   - The paper cites [2] as the source for "curse of long-tailed class distributions" but [2] is not in the reference list (it's a text citation without reference).
   - The description of datasets is confusing: "For the largest dataset (WIKI-large) used in LSHTC2 challenge, we used samples with 50,000 and 100,000 classes." LSHTC2 has a single dataset with approximately 100,000 classes, not a "WIKI-large" dataset.
   - Hyperparameter tuning is described too vaguely: "For each methods, we tuned the hyperparameters over a heldout set" provides no details about the tuning process or heldout set size.
   - The paper claims "the proposed approach is highly competitive" but fails to provide statistical significance tests for performance differences.

4. **Writing and Formatting:**
   - The paper uses inconsistent capitalization of K and k in mathematical notation.
   - There are grammatical errors, such as "it is a challenging task to find a balanced tree structure which can partition the class labels" (should be "that can partition").
   - The reference to "NIPS 2017" in the header is incorrect as NIPS is now NeurIPS.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents a potentially useful method, the technical inconsistencies, methodological gaps, and experimental shortcomings significantly undermine its quality. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues before consideration for publication. The theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported, and the experimental evaluation lacks necessary details for reproducibility.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper contains multiple technical errors and inconsistencies that prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for a high-quality scientific contribution. While the problem is relevant and the approach shows promise, the current presentation is insufficient for publication. The authors should address the technical inconsistencies, provide clearer theoretical justification, and improve the experimental details before resubmission.
