Summary
The paper tackles extreme/multi-class classification by reducing the problem to a binary dyadic classification task with two aggressive sampling steps: (i) rebalancing the training set via class-dependent sampling probabilities π (oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones), and (ii) constructing only κ dyadic pairs per example by sampling κ adversarial classes rather than enumerating all K−1. The authors derive generalization bounds for (a) the standard dyadic reduction with dependent examples using fractional/local Rademacher complexity and graph covers, and (b) their doubly-sampled reduction, quantifying the effect of the induced distribution shift (parameters α, β) and dependence. For prediction, they further prune the set of candidate classes using nearest class centroids computed in the dyadic space. On LSHTC text corpora (including up to 100K classes), the method is compared against OVA, multiclass SVM, tree-based extreme classifiers (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree), and PD-Sparse. Results suggest improved or competitive accuracy/MaF1 with substantially better memory/runtime than OVA/M-SVM and more stable accuracy than tree-based methods, while being competitive with PD-Sparse in performance and better in memory.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on dyadic reduction approaches for multiclass problems [1,16,28] and on generalization analysis with dependent examples using fractional chromatic numbers and Rademacher complexities [14,16,22,25]. It contrasts with label-embedding methods [5,11,19] and tree-based/log-time prediction [3,4,6,7,9,15,21], highlighting their failure modes (low-rank assumption violations, error propagation). The proposed double sampling is reminiscent of negative sampling strategies but is tailored to the dyadic ranking loss and accompanied by bounds that explicitly account for dependence and class-imbalance-induced reweighting.

Strengths
- Practical scalability: The (π, κ)-DS scheme drastically reduces the number of dyadic pairs and memory footprint, and helps with long-tailed label distributions common in extreme classification.
- Theoretical analysis under dependence: The use of fractional/local Rademacher complexity and graph covers to handle interdependent dyadic examples is technically appropriate and extends prior analyses in a meaningful direction. The doubly-sampled bound incorporates the sampling-induced distribution shift via α, β.
- Sensible prediction-time candidate pruning: The nearest-centroid preselection is simple and effective, reducing test-time cost.
- Empirical results at scale: Experiments up to 100K classes demonstrate the method’s viability and favorable runtime/memory-accuracy trade-offs. Code and data are provided for reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Domain specificity of φ: The 10-dimensional feature mapping is handcrafted for text (IR-style features + centroid distances). It is unclear how the method fares with other domains where φ must be learned or engineered differently. Claims of general applicability would benefit from non-text datasets.
- Theoretical–algorithmic gap: The prediction-time candidate selection is not reflected in the theory (no analysis of the additional approximation error or the required q to preserve top-1 recall). Similarly, the theory is stated for 0/1 loss; practical training likely uses a surrogate—details and connections are not provided.
- Limited ablations: There is little sensitivity analysis for π (how strong the rebalancing), κ (negatives per positive), and q (candidate pool size), nor studies of the α, β trade-offs to validate the bounds’ guidance.
- Experimental fairness/coverage: Some strong contemporary baselines in extreme classification are missing, and tree-based baselines are multi-label methods adapted to multi-class. The conversion of multi-label datasets to multi-class via replication can distort distributions and evaluation. Details on hyperparameter tuning and model capacity parity are insufficient in the main text.
- Clarity and notation: Theorems introduce many constants (α, β, r) and complexity terms; their interpretations and practical magnitudes are not discussed. The link to “Local Rademacher complexities” would benefit from clearer attribution and intuition, and a concrete graph cover for the dyadic dependency structure.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The core idea is sound, and the dependence-aware analysis is a solid contribution. However, the lack of analysis for candidate selection, missing details on the training loss/regularization, and limited ablations weaken the empirical and theoretical cohesion.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but key components (how f is trained, exact φ features, choice of κ, π, q) are scattered or delegated to supplementary material. Theorems would benefit from more intuition and a brief sketch of the covering construction for T(S).
- Originality: Combining aggressive double sampling with dependence-aware generalization bounds is reasonably novel for extreme multiclass. The sampling idea resembles known negative sampling techniques, but the theoretical treatment for the doubly-sampled setting appears new.
- Significance: The approach is practical and could be adopted in text classification pipelines. Broader impact depends on demonstrating domain generality and stronger comparisons.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real scalability issue in extreme multiclass with a simple and effective recipe.
- Provides nontrivial generalization bounds under dependence and sampling shift.
- Strong empirical scale and open-sourced implementation.

Cons for acceptance
- Domain-specific φ and lack of non-text experiments limit generality.
- Important parts of the practical method (candidate selection) lack theoretical treatment.
- Limited ablations and incomplete baseline coverage reduce evidential strength.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide detailed ablations: vary κ and q to show accuracy–efficiency trade-offs; study π schedules (e.g., inverse-frequency temperature); report candidate recall curves (probability the true class is in Yx vs q).
- Theoretical extension: bound the additional error due to candidate selection (e.g., via a miss-probability term depending on centroid similarity and q).
- Clarify training objective and regularization (e.g., hinge/logistic on h with L2/L1), and relate the surrogate risk to the 0/1 bounds.
- Expand to at least one non-text dataset and/or learned φ (e.g., simple learned bilinear embedding), to demonstrate generality.
- Strengthen baseline comparisons and fairness (document hyperparameter grids; include additional extreme classifiers where feasible).
- Improve clarity of the dependency graph cover and compute/upper-bound its fractional chromatic number for the dyadic structure.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a practically useful idea with solid dependence-aware theory and promising results, but lacks sufficient empirical breadth, ablations, and theoretical treatment of candidate selection to meet the NeurIPS bar. Addressing the above points would substantially strengthen the submission. Confidence: moderate.
