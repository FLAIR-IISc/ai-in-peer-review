## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to tackling the challenges of extreme multi-class classification, specifically focusing on improving the scalability and performance of the dyadic representation method. The proposed aggressive sampling strategy appears promising, backed by theoretical analysis (generalization bounds) and empirical results on large-scale text datasets. While the theoretical contributions are a bit dense and could benefit from further clarification, the paper is generally well-written and addresses a significant problem in the field. I believe this paper is a **strong accept** pending minor revisions to improve clarity and potentially address a few points raised below.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the limitations of the one-vs-all (OVA) approach for extreme multi-class classification, namely its computational intractability and class imbalance issues. It builds upon the dyadic representation framework, which avoids the need for complex tree structures or low-rank embeddings. The key innovation lies in the “aggressive double sampling” technique – a combination of class re-weighting (π) and dyadic example reduction (κ) – designed to balance class distributions and reduce computational cost. The authors position their work within the broader context of existing approaches (tree-based, embedding-based, dyadic representations) and effectively highlight the shortcomings that their method aims to overcome.  The citations are relevant and cover the key literature in this area. 

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The aggressive sampling strategy is a novel contribution. Combining class re-weighting with a targeted reduction in the number of dyadic examples is a clever idea to mitigate the computational burden without sacrificing too much information.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides generalization bounds (Theorem 1 and Theorem 2) for the proposed method, demonstrating a rigorous theoretical analysis. This is a significant strength, as many practical approaches in extreme classification lack strong theoretical guarantees.  The connection to local Rademacher complexity and graph covering theory is commendable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive, and often superior, performance in terms of accuracy, macro F1-score, and computational efficiency (runtime and memory usage) compared to several state-of-the-art baselines.  The results on the 100K-class Wikipedia dataset are particularly impressive.
* **Practical Relevance:** The scalability improvements offered by aggressive sampling are crucial for real-world applications dealing with extremely large numbers of classes.  The work directly addresses a significant bottleneck in the field.
* **Clear Writing (Generally):** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly explains the motivation and methodology. The algorithm is presented in a clear and concise manner.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Complexity:** While the theoretical analysis is a strength, it's also a point of weakness. The derivation of the generalization bounds is dense and requires substantial expertise in Rademacher complexity and related concepts.  Simplifying the presentation of these bounds, or providing more intuitive explanations of their implications, would improve accessibility.  For example, a discussion of the practical impact of parameters like 'r' (variance control) would be helpful.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the method likely depends on the choice of hyperparameters (π, κ). While the authors mention hyperparameter tuning, a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be valuable.  A sensitivity analysis, showing how performance changes with varying values of π and κ, would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
* **Feature Mapping:** The feature mapping (φ) used in the experiments is described but feels somewhat ad-hoc. While appropriate for text classification, it's unclear how well this mapping would generalize to other domains.  A brief discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of this feature mapping would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to Recent Work:** While the authors acknowledge some related work, a more thorough comparison to very recent advances in extreme classification (e.g., deep learning-based approaches) would provide a more complete picture of the state-of-the-art.

**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Theorem 2:** The terms α and β in Theorem 2 seem crucial but are defined in terms of class proportions.  How robust is the bound to changes in these proportions, especially when the training data is noisy or imperfectly representative of the true distribution?
* **Candidate Selection:** The candidate selection step based on nearest centroids is a good optimization.  However, could the choice of 'q' (number of candidates) significantly impact performance?  Was it tuned, and if so, how?
* **Memory Usage:** While the paper reports lower memory usage compared to some baselines, providing a breakdown of memory usage for different components of the proposed method (e.g., storing the feature mapping, the sampled training set) would be helpful for understanding its memory footprint.
* **Fig. 3:** The figure visually effectively highlights the performance differences.



**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and well-motivated approach to a significant problem.
* Strong theoretical foundation with generalization bounds.
* Compelling empirical results on large-scale datasets.
* Potential for significant scalability improvements in real-world applications.
* Clear and well-organized presentation (overall).

**Con:**

* Theoretical analysis may be too complex for some readers.
*  Parameter sensitivity requires further investigation.
*  Feature mapping is domain-specific and might limit generalizability.
*  Comparison to very recent deep learning-based methods could be strengthened.

**6. Final Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept** with minor revisions. Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1.  Simplify the presentation of the generalization bounds, providing more intuitive explanations.
2.  Include a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters (π, κ).
3.  Expand the discussion of the feature mapping and its limitations.
4.  Briefly address the relationship to more recent deep learning-based approaches.



I believe that addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification.
