- Proposes double sampling (resample to rebalance labels + sample κ negative classes per example) to reduce extreme multiclass to dyadic binary; uses a 10-feature domain-specific φ and nearest-centroid candidate selection; code released.  
- Theoretical contributions: generalization bounds for dependent dyadic ERM via local fractional Rademacher complexity (Theorem 1) and extension to double-sampled distribution-shifted setting (Theorem 2).  
- Novelty: primarily the combination of aggressive double sampling with a bound that explicitly handles dyadic dependency and sampling bias; builds on prior dyadic and fractional Rademacher work.  
- Theory quality: derivations appear technically sound and use appropriate tools (graph fractional covers, local complexities).  
- Practical caveat: reweighting factors α and β from sampling can be large under aggressive rebalancing, possibly weakening bound tightness; authors should quantify typical magnitudes.  
- Limitation: the test-time candidate-selection heuristic (nearest centroids) is not covered by the theoretical analysis and should be acknowledged.  
- Method quality: double sampling is simple, well-motivated, and yields computational gains and improved balance.  
- Reproducibility issue: φ is hand-crafted and domain-specific; training objective, loss, model class, and hyperparameters for f are underspecified in the main text.  
- Empirical results: competitive accuracy/MaF1 and strong runtime/memory improvements on large LSHTC text datasets (up to 100k classes).  
- Evaluation concern: converting multi-label data to multiclass by duplicating instances can change class priors and affect macro-F1; the protocol needs justification.  
- Missing baselines: no comparisons to pairwise ranking with negative sampling (e.g., WARP, sampled SVMrank) or OVA with hard-negative mining, which are relevant given the method’s nature.  
- Missing ablations: no sensitivity analysis for π (sampling), κ (negatives), or candidate set size q, and no ablation of the candidate-selection heuristic.  
- Generality concern: experiments are text-only with a specific φ; portability to learned features or other domains is not demonstrated.  
- Clarity issues: paper is readable but has typos and notational clutter; needs explicit statement of the training objective, a concise cost analysis showing κ and |S_π| effects, and clearer φ feature descriptions.  
- Originality assessment: algorithmic elements combine known ideas (rebalancing, negative sampling); main novelty is the rigorous theoretical treatment of dependent dyads and sampling-induced shift.  
- Significance: practically useful for extreme text classification and theoretically interesting for dependent-sample learning, but limited by domain-specific features and narrow evaluation.  
- Pros: simple scalable method, theoretical bounds addressing dependency and sampling shift, competitive large-scale results, and released code.  
- Cons: limited experimental scope, missing key baselines and ablations, potential looseness of bounds under aggressive sampling, candidate selection untheorized, and underspecified training details.  
- Reviewer suggestions: detail learner and hyperparameters, add ablations over κ/π/q, compare to pairwise ranking and OVA hard-negative mining, quantify α and β on datasets, discuss multi-label→multiclass implications, and test a learned φ or non-text domain.  
- Reproducibility: positive that code/data are available and algorithms outlined, but missing complete feature definitions, training objective details, and hyperparameter settings.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept (score 6) — interesting and scalable with a nontrivial theoretical contribution, but needs more comprehensive experiments and clarifications; reviewer confidence medium.
