

# Peer Review: "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach to large-scale multi-class classification by reducing the problem to binary classification through a "doubly-sampled" strategy. The authors address two main limitations of existing approaches: the computational intractability of one-vs-all (OVA) for large K and the poor performance of embedding-based methods due to violated low-rank assumptions. Their method, called "aggressive double sampling" ((π, κ)-DS), first balances the class distribution by oversampling small classes and subsampling large classes, then reduces the number of dyadic examples by sampling only κ classes per example. The paper provides theoretical guarantees using local fractional Rademacher complexity and demonstrates significant improvements in computational efficiency while maintaining competitive predictive performance on large-scale text classification tasks (up to 100,000 classes).

The work builds on existing dyadic representation approaches [1, 16, 28] but introduces a more efficient sampling strategy that addresses class imbalance, a critical issue in real-world large-scale classification problems. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of extreme classification by providing both theoretical and empirical evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.

## Strengths

1. **Practical Contribution**: The paper addresses a real-world problem (long-tailed class distributions in large-scale classification) with a practical solution that significantly reduces memory consumption and training time while maintaining competitive accuracy. The results on large datasets (WIKI-100K) are particularly compelling.

2. **Theoretical Novelty**: The use of local fractional Rademacher complexity to derive generalization bounds is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper extends previous work [16] by incorporating second-order (variance) information, which leads to faster convergence rates.

3. **Clear Experimental Design**: The experiments are well-designed and cover a range of datasets with varying numbers of classes (WIKI-Small, WIKI-50K, WIKI-100K). The comparison against multiple relevant baselines (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree, PD-Sparse) is comprehensive.

4. **Strong Empirical Results**: The method achieves the best balance among time, memory usage, and predictive performance. For the largest dataset (WIKI-100K), the proposed method is the only one that can train within reasonable time and memory constraints, while still achieving competitive accuracy and macro F1 scores.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Comparison Lacking**: While the paper claims to provide "new generalization bounds," it doesn't clearly demonstrate how these bounds improve upon those in [16] (which also uses Rademacher complexity for dyadic representations). A direct comparison of the rates would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

2. **Algorithmic Ambiguity**: The (π, κ)-DS algorithm (Algorithm 1) contains unclear notation. Specifically, the line "Draw uniformly a set Yxy of κ classes from Y\{y}. κ K" is confusing. What does "κ K" mean? Is this a typo or a specific operation? This ambiguity makes it difficult to fully understand and reproduce the method.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis Missing**: The paper doesn't investigate how sensitive the method is to the choice of π and κ, which are critical hyperparameters. For instance, how does performance change when κ is set to 1 or 100? This is a significant gap for a method that relies on sampling.

4. **Limited Comparison to Recent Work**: The authors state they didn't include SLEEC and LEML because they "have been shown to be consistently outperformed," but don't provide evidence for this claim. This seems like an unsubstantiated assertion without supporting evidence.

5. **Candidate Selection Details Missing**: The candidate selection algorithm (2.3) is vague. How are class centroids computed? How is "nearest-centroid" determined? What is the value of q (number of candidate classes)? These details are crucial for reproducibility.

6. **Theoretical Assumptions Not Justified**: The use of fractional chromatic number and exact proper fractional cover is introduced without sufficient justification for why these specific concepts are necessary for the analysis.

7. **Scalability Beyond 100K Classes Unclear**: While the paper demonstrates success up to 100,000 classes, it doesn't provide insight into how the method would scale beyond this point.

## Assessment Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experiments are well-designed. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., hyperparameter sensitivity, the need for candidate selection). The paper is a complete work, not merely a position paper. *Strength: 4/5*

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The main contributions are clearly stated, and the mathematical notation is consistent. The experimental setup is clearly described. The supplementary material appears to contain additional details (proofs, parameter settings). *Strength: 5/5*

**Originality**: The "aggressive double sampling" strategy is novel. While the dyadic representation approach is not new, the specific sampling strategy and the theoretical analysis using local fractional Rademacher complexity are significant contributions. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work. *Strength: 4/5*

**Significance**: The paper addresses a real and important problem in machine learning (large-scale multi-class classification) with a method that offers practical benefits (reduced memory usage, faster training) while maintaining competitive predictive performance. The results suggest this method could be widely adopted in real-world applications with large class spaces. *Strength: 5/5*

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. Clarify the (π, κ)-DS algorithm, particularly the meaning of "κ K" in Algorithm 1.

2. Include a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis showing how performance changes with different values of π and κ.

3. Provide more detail on the candidate selection algorithm (2.3), including how class centroids are computed and the value of q.

4. Include a comparison to SLEEC and LEML with evidence supporting the claim that they're outperformed by the methods included.

5. Add a direct comparison of the theoretical bounds with those in [16] to demonstrate the improvement.

6. Discuss the scalability of the method beyond 100,000 classes.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of extreme classification. The aggressive double sampling strategy effectively addresses the class imbalance problem and significantly reduces computational requirements while maintaining competitive predictive performance. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and extends previous work. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the practical utility of the method.

While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of algorithmic clarity and theoretical comparison, these are relatively minor compared to the significant practical contribution. The method is likely to be widely adopted in real-world applications with large-scale classification problems.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a solid contribution to the field of machine learning, particularly for large-scale multi-class classification tasks, and provides both theoretical and empirical evidence for its effectiveness.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Summary

**Pros for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a real-world problem with practical significance
- Provides both theoretical and empirical evidence for effectiveness
- Demonstrates significant improvements in computational efficiency
- Well-written and well-organized
- Competitive results on challenging datasets

**Cons for Acceptance:**
- Some algorithmic details are unclear (e.g., "κ K" in Algorithm 1)
- Missing hyperparameter sensitivity analysis
- Unsubstantiated claim about excluding SLEEC and LEML
- Limited comparison to recent work on dyadic representations

Despite the minor weaknesses, the paper's strengths outweigh the weaknesses. The contribution is significant, well-executed, and has clear practical implications for real-world applications.
