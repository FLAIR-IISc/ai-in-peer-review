Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme/multiclass classification by reducing the problem to a binary task over dyadic example–class pairs. To address the prohibitive O(mK) number of dyadic pairs and class imbalance, it proposes a doubly aggressive sampling scheme: (i) class-balanced subsampling/oversampling of training examples with per-class probabilities πk, and (ii) per-example negative class subsampling of size κ. A single binary classifier is trained on the sampled dyadic pairs. For prediction, the method evaluates only a small candidate set of q classes per test point using nearest class centroids in the dyadic space. Theoretically, the paper provides generalization bounds for (a) dyadic learning with dependent pairs using fractional/local Rademacher complexity (Theorem 1), and (b) the proposed double sampling (Theorem 2), showing consistency of ERM with explicit dependence on π, κ, class priors, and K. Empirically, on LSHTC text datasets up to 100K classes, the method achieves competitive accuracy/MaF1 with substantially reduced time and memory vs OVA/M-SVM and better accuracy than tree-based methods, and comparable or better efficiency than PD-Sparse. The work builds on prior dyadic reductions [Usunier et al., Amini et al., Joshi et al.] and recent extreme classification baselines (FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse), and extends the fractional Rademacher-complexity analysis to a local version with sampling.

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability: The (π, κ)-DS scheme directly targets the main bottlenecks of dyadic reduction (imbalance and O(mK) pairs). Reported wall-clock/memory savings at 50–100K classes are compelling.
- Theoretical contribution: Extends generalization analysis for dependent dyadic data using local fractional Rademacher complexity. Theorem 2 explicitly captures the effect of distribution shift induced by π and subsampling κ, with clear α, β terms and K-dependence.
- End-to-end story: From sampling to prediction-time candidate selection, the system is designed for extreme regimes and evaluated on standard large-scale text benchmarks. Code and datasets are provided.
- Empirical results: Competitive accuracy/MaF1 vs strong baselines, particularly where OVA/M-SVM are infeasible and tree methods underperform in accuracy.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity and completeness of technical details:
  - The learning objective/classifier f used in practice is not specified (loss, regularization, optimizer). The theory is for 0/1 loss, but experiments must use a surrogate; this mismatch and implementation details should be described.
  - The dependency graph and fractional cover underlying the complexity terms are only sketched; the resulting constants (e.g., χ* or ωk choices) and their scaling are not made explicit. This limits interpretability of the rates.
  - Theorem 2 mixes m and M; clearer statements about when each term dominates and the regimes where the bound improves over unsampled learning would help.
  - The α = max η/π term can become large under the suggested πk ∝ 1/ηk (α ∝ ηk^2), potentially hurting bounds; a discussion (or clipping) is needed.
- Scope and generality:
  - The dyadic mapping φ is hand-crafted with 10 rank-inspired features tailored to text. No ablations show sensitivity to φ or portability beyond text; claiming generality to other modalities is not empirically supported.
  - Candidate selection (q-nearest centroids) is a heuristic without analysis. There is no recall analysis of whether the true class remains in the candidate set as q varies, nor ablations showing the effect of q on accuracy/time.
- Experimental evaluation:
  - The paper converts multi-label data to multi-class by replication; potential bias and its impact on macro-F1 should be discussed.
  - Missing ablations: effects of κ and π, with/without double sampling, with/without candidate selection; this would strengthen the causal link from the proposed components to the gains.
  - Baselines: For extreme multi-class, precision@k is also standard; including it would facilitate comparison to extreme classification literature. Some references are omitted on purpose (SLEEC/LEML), but more recent deep/extreme baselines are not considered.
- Presentation: Numerous typographical issues and incomplete references make the paper harder to follow; some algorithms/equations would benefit from clearer pseudocode and complexity analysis.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The core idea is sound; the theoretical analysis is nontrivial and relevant to the dependency introduced by dyadic reductions and sampling. However, some technical details are under-specified (learner, constants in bounds), and empirical evaluation lacks key ablations and candidate-selection analysis.
- Clarity: Overall understandable but needs significant polishing. Specify the learning algorithm, normalize notation (m vs M), and better explain the dependency graph and cover. Improve reference formatting and fix typos.
- Originality: Dyadic reduction and negative sampling are known; the novelty lies in the double sampling scheme with a consistency analysis via local fractional Rademacher complexity. This is a meaningful, if incremental, theoretical advance tied to a practical algorithm.
- Significance: The approach addresses a real bottleneck in extreme multiclass with evidence of strong efficiency/accuracy tradeoffs on large text datasets. With better clarity and ablations, it could be a useful tool and inspire further work on sampled dyadic learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Precisely describe the trained model (loss, regularization, solver), and report its hyperparameters.
- Provide ablations: κ and π sensitivity; effect of candidate selection q on recall/accuracy/time; results without double sampling.
- Discuss and, if needed, adjust π to control α and β (e.g., smoothing/clipping).
- Analyze/visualize the dependency graph and resulting fractional chromatic number to make the complexity terms concrete.
- Add precision@k; consider an additional modality or a learned φ to support generality claims.
- Clean up references, notation, and typos.

Reasons to accept
- Addresses an important scalability problem with a simple, effective sampling framework.
- Nontrivial theoretical analysis for dependent dyadic data with sampling; consistency is shown.
- Strong empirical efficiency and competitive accuracy on 50–100K class text benchmarks; code available.

Reasons to reject
- Missing key implementation details and ablations; candidate selection is heuristic without analysis.
- Clarity/presentation issues and incomplete references impede reproducibility.
- Generality beyond the chosen φ and modality is not demonstrated.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a practical and theoretically grounded approach to scaling dyadic reductions for extreme multiclass classification, with promising empirical results. With improved clarity and ablations, it would make a solid NeurIPS contribution.
