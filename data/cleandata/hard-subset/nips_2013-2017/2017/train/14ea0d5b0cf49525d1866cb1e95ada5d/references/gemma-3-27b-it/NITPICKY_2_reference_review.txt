This paper addresses the problem of multi-class to binary reduction, a common approach for extreme classification, and proposes an “aggressive double sampling” strategy to mitigate computational issues and class imbalance. The core idea is to oversample minority classes and subsample majority classes during the construction of dyadic examples, coupled with theoretical analysis using local Rademacher complexity to justify the approach. While the motivation is sound and the problem setting is important, the paper suffers from a multitude of small, yet significant, flaws in presentation, technical detail, and justification, ultimately leading to a negative recommendation.

The introduction, while adequate in outlining the problem, lacks a compelling narrative. The discussion of existing approaches feels cursory and doesn’t fully contextualize the contribution. Claims about the limitations of tree-based and embedding-based methods are presented as definitive statements without sufficient nuance. For instance, stating that the low-rank assumption is *generally* transgressed in extreme multi-class classification requires more support or qualification. The contribution section feels overly ambitious given the incremental nature of the proposed method.

Section 2 introduces the mathematical framework. The notation is mostly standard, but the transition between equations (1) and (2) is abrupt and lacks explanation. Why is the loss function defined as ranking-based? A clear explanation of the connection to multi-class SVM and AdaBoost.MR would be beneficial. The definition of the dyadic transformation T(S) (Eq. 3) is complex and difficult to parse.  The use of “zj” and “ỹj” feels unnecessarily convoluted. More importantly, the explanation of the dependency between the dyadic examples is superficial. While acknowledged, the implications of this dependency aren't fully explored before invoking the Rademacher complexity analysis.

The theoretical results (Theorems 1 and 2) are a major weakness. The introduction of “local fractional Rademacher complexity” feels unjustified.  There's no clear explanation of *why* this specific complexity measure is appropriate given the dependencies introduced by the sampling scheme. The theorems themselves are dense and filled with unexplained parameters (α, β, r). The connection between these parameters and the sampling probabilities (π, κ) isn't immediately obvious.  The proof sketch mentions splitting the sum (5) into independent variables but provides no details. Referring to the "supplementary material" for the full proof is insufficient; crucial insights should be present in the main text. The generalization bounds, even if correct, appear overly complex and likely to be loose, hindering their practical utility. The claims of "faster convergence rates" based on variance information are not substantiated by any empirical evidence. The definition of Hr is also not clearly linked to the sampling strategy.

Section 2.2 “Aggressive Double Sampling” introduces Algorithm 1, which is poorly described. What does “draw randomly a set Sπk” mean in terms of the sampling distribution? Is it with or without replacement? The discussion of balancing classes is intuitive but lacks mathematical rigor.  The claim that oversampling/subsampling “overcomes the curse of long-tailed class distributions” is a strong statement requiring stronger justification.  Why is the choice of κ arbitrary, and what impact does it have on the performance?  The connection between α in Theorem 2 and the sampling probabilities π is still unclear.

Section 2.3 “Prediction with Candidate Selection” is a brief addition that doesn’t significantly enhance the core contribution.  The rationale behind using class centroids is weak. The discussion of a time/accuracy tradeoff feels superficial.

Section 3, the experimental section, further exacerbates the concerns. The description of the datasets and baselines is adequate, but the implementation details are vague. The hyperparameters used for each method are relegated to the supplementary material, hindering reproducibility and thorough evaluation. The results are presented in tables and figures, but the analysis is superficial.  The claim that the proposed method is “highly competitive” is not convincingly supported by the data. While (π, κ)-DS appears to perform well on certain datasets, the performance gains are often marginal. The discussion focuses heavily on runtime and memory usage, which are important, but neglects a deeper analysis of the predictive performance.  The conclusion reiterates the strengths of the method without acknowledging its limitations.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* **Lack of theoretical clarity:** The theoretical contributions, while attempting to provide justification, are overly complex, poorly explained, and lack practical relevance.
* **Superficial analysis:** The experimental section lacks a rigorous analysis of the results, failing to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* **Numerous minor flaws:**  The paper suffers from a multitude of small issues in presentation, notation, and explanation, which collectively detract from its overall quality.
* **Incremental contribution:** The proposed method is an incremental improvement over existing techniques rather than a significant breakthrough.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Relevant problem:** The problem of extreme multi-class classification is important and actively researched.
* **Sound motivation:** The motivation behind the aggressive sampling strategy is plausible and addresses a practical challenge.
* **Some empirical validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method can achieve competitive performance on certain datasets.



**Overall:** Despite addressing a relevant problem, the paper’s technical shortcomings, lack of clarity, and incremental contribution outweigh its strengths. The numerous flaws in presentation and analysis raise concerns about the rigor of the work. I recommend **rejection**.  Significant revisions addressing the points outlined above would be required for reconsideration.
