**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of extreme multi-class classification (XMC) by proposing a novel reduction of the multi-class problem to a binary one. The core idea builds upon prior work that transforms the problem into a dyadic space of (example, class) pairs, but tackles the resulting computational explosion. The authors introduce a two-stage sampling strategy, termed "(π, κ)-double subsampling" or (π, κ)-DS. The first stage (π-sampling) re-balances the training set by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones to combat long-tailed distributions. The second stage (κ-sampling) reduces the number of generated negative pairs for each example, from K-1 to a much smaller number κ. The paper provides theoretical justification for this approach with generalization bounds based on local fractional Rademacher complexities, showing that empirical risk minimization on the sampled, dependent data remains consistent. For prediction, a candidate selection strategy using class centroids is employed to maintain efficiency. The method is evaluated on large-scale text classification datasets, where it demonstrates strong performance in terms of accuracy, Macro F1, training time, and memory usage compared to several state-of-the-art baselines.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and Intuitive Approach:** The proposed (π, κ)-DS strategy is a sensible and practical solution to the two primary challenges of the dyadic reduction approach: class imbalance and computational cost. The π-sampling directly targets the long-tailed distribution problem, while the κ-sampling directly addresses the quadratic growth of the binary training set.

2.  **Theoretical Support:** The authors provide non-trivial generalization bounds (Theorems 1 and 2) for their proposed learning setting. Acknowledging and formally addressing the challenges introduced by their sampling scheme—namely, the distribution shift from π-sampling and the inter-dependency of dyadic pairs—is a significant strength and adds rigor to the paper.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is extensive, comparing against a strong suite of modern XMC methods on large, standard benchmark datasets. The proposed method appears to achieve a superior trade-off between predictive performance (Accuracy and MaF1) and computational resources (time and memory), which is the central goal of this line of research. The results on the WIKI-100K dataset are particularly impressive.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising core idea, the paper is marred by a significant number of minor flaws, ambiguities, and presentation issues that collectively undermine its quality and readiness for publication at NIPS.

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   The description of the (π, κ)-DS algorithm is imprecise. The paper states, "Draw randomly a set Sπk of examples of class k from S with probability πk". This is ambiguous. Does this mean each *instance* of class k is included with probability πk? Or is a subset of a certain size sampled? The formal definition of πk as P(xy ∈ Sπ|xy ∈ S) is helpful, but the operational description of the sampling procedure itself lacks rigor.
    *   The hyperparameter `q` for the "Prediction with Candidate Selection Algorithm" is critical for the method's efficiency, but its selection is not discussed. The footnote mentions it can be tuned, but the experimental section does not state what value was used or how it was chosen, which is a crucial detail for reproducibility.
    *   The dyadic transformation in Eq. (3) uses an asymmetric condition: `ỹ = -1` if `k < yi` and `ỹ = +1` otherwise. The motivation for this specific ordering over a simpler `k ≠ yi` rule is not explained. While likely not impactful, such arbitrary choices should be justified or noted as such in a rigorous paper.

2.  **Methodological Nuances and Unaddressed Questions:**
    *   The feature representation in Section 3.1 seems problematic. Feature 9, the distance to a class centroid, is used both for the candidate pre-selection at prediction time and as a feature for the learned ranking function `f*`. This creates a potential dependency where the model might learn to heavily rely on the output of its own selection heuristic, which seems circular. This deserves discussion and perhaps an ablation study.
    *   The theoretical analysis, while a strength, is not fully connected back to the practical implications. For instance, Theorem 2 includes terms `α` and `β` which depend on `1/πy`. The paper's sampling is designed to increase `πy` for rare classes, thus controlling these terms. However, a more detailed discussion of this interplay and the behavior of the bound would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
    *   The paper repeatedly refers to Figures 1 and 3, and Tables 1, 2, and 3, none of which are included in the provided manuscript. The entire experimental results section (Section 4) discusses Table 3 in detail, but the table itself is missing. This is a major oversight that makes the empirical claims entirely unverifiable for a reviewer.
    *   There is a formatting artifact at the bottom of the first page ("1st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)..."), which suggests a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   The text contains several instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors (e.g., "oversampling the most small size classes", "the low-rank assumption...is generally transgressed", "Even-though", "these datesets").
    *   The citation list `[4, 3, 6, 7, 9, 21, 5, 15]` in the introduction is not numerically sorted, which is a minor but noticeable stylistic issue.

**Decision**

While the paper presents a novel and effective method for a significant problem, the numerous flaws in its current form lead me to recommend rejection. The inability to verify the experimental results due to missing tables is a critical failure. Furthermore, the ambiguities in the algorithmic descriptions and the unaddressed methodological questions detract from the paper's technical quality. The work shows considerable promise, but it requires a thorough revision to address the clarity, reproducibility, and presentation issues before it meets the high standards of NIPS.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification.
*   The proposed (π, κ)-DS sampling method is novel, intuitive, and empirically effective.
*   Provides non-trivial theoretical bounds to justify the consistency of the learning procedure.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Major:** The paper is incomplete; key Tables (1, 2, 3) and Figures (1, 3) that are central to the experimental section are missing, rendering the results unverifiable.
*   **Major:** The description of the core sampling algorithm is ambiguous, hindering reproducibility.
*   **Moderate:** Several methodological details are glossed over, such as the circular use of the centroid distance feature and the selection of the hyperparameter `q`.
*   **Minor:** The paper suffers from a general lack of polish, with formatting artifacts and grammatical errors.
