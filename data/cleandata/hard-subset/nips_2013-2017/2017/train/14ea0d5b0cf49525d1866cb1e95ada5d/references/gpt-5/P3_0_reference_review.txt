Summary
This paper tackles extreme/multi-class classification by reducing the problem to binary classification in a dyadic (x, y)-feature space, but avoiding the O(mK) blow-up via an aggressive double sampling scheme: (i) class-rebalancing of the training set by oversampling tail classes and subsampling head classes (π), and (ii) for each retained instance, sampling only κ negative classes rather than using all K − 1. The authors analyze the dependence induced by dyadic construction and the distribution shift introduced by sampling using fractional/local Rademacher complexities, giving generalization bounds (Theorems 1–2) that aim to justify ERM consistency under dependence and reweighting. They also use a nearest-centroid candidate selection at prediction time to avoid evaluating all classes. Experiments on LSHTC text datasets (up to 100K classes) indicate favorable time–memory–accuracy trade-offs over OvA, multi-class SVM, tree-based methods (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree), and PD-Sparse.

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability: The (π, κ)-DS reduction directly addresses the mK expansion and long-tailed label distributions prevalent in extreme classification. The κ-negative sampling is simple and effective, and the nearest-centroid candidate selection is a pragmatic inference speedup.
- Theoretical contribution: Extends generalization analysis for dyadic reductions with interdependent examples by using fractional graph covers and local Rademacher complexities. The bounds explicitly account for distribution shift (π) via α and β, and the dependence structure via fractional Rademacher complexity. This goes beyond earlier bounds for interdependent data (e.g., Usunier et al. 2006; Ralaivola & Amini 2015).
- Empirical results: On large LSHTC variants, the method offers competitive accuracy/MaF1 while substantially reducing training time and memory compared to strong baselines. It is notably more accurate than tree-based methods while retaining comparable runtime.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Theory interpretability and tightness:
  - The scaling and roles of α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y are not discussed sufficiently. With π_y ∝ 1/η_y (as used), α can scale like η_y^2 (max over head classes), potentially making bounds loose/vacuous. Provide guidance on choosing π to control α, β, and an interpretation of the rates in Theorems 1–2 (e.g., how κ, M, K should scale for nonvacuous guarantees).
  - Theorems use m (original sample size) and notional N = m(K − 1) in places; explain the dependence on m vs N after fractional covering. Clarify constants and whether “local” complexity yields faster rates in practice (e.g., O(1/√(Mκ)) vs O(1/√m)).
  - The ERM is performed on the sampled dataset without importance weights. Discuss why importance weighting (η_y/π_y) is not used to remove bias, and whether the bound suggests doing so or using a control variate to reduce variance.
- Methodological details:
  - The training objective for f is not clearly specified (SVM hinge? logistic? linear?), nor is the optimization procedure. The theory assumes 0/1 loss; experiments need alignment or at least a statement on convex surrogate and regularization used.
  - The κ negative classes are sampled uniformly. Hard negative mining or nonuniform sampling (e.g., by centroid proximity or classifier margin) could substantially improve sample efficiency; some discussion or ablation would help.
  - Candidate selection: Selecting q nearest centroids entails O(Kp) per test point unless additional indexing is used. Please clarify computational cost and whether test-time numbers include centroid search. Consider approximate NN (e.g., PQ/LSH) to make this sublinear.
- Empirical evaluation:
  - Ablations are missing. Sensitivity analyses over κ, π (strength of class rebalancing), and q (candidate set size) are important to understand the speed/accuracy trade-offs and robustness.
  - Metrics: Extreme classification commonly reports P@k and propensity-scored variants; macro-F1 is useful but not standard alone. Provide P@k and PSP@k to compare with tree-based extreme classifiers more fairly.
  - Dataset handling: Converting multi-label to multiclass by duplicating instances changes label distribution and does not reflect typical extreme multi-label use-cases; discuss implications and, ideally, include a multi-label evaluation.
  - Baselines: The omission of label-embedding baselines is motivated by PD-Sparse comparisons, but including at least one strong embedding baseline would strengthen the case. Also clarify PD-Sparse memory settings and training budgets to ensure fairness.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Notation is occasionally inconsistent (e.g., mixing m and N in bounds; definition of z and h). Provide a compact description of the graph cover used and an intuitive explanation of the resulting complexity terms.
  - The joint feature mapping φ is domain-specific with just 10 ranking-inspired features. While effective for text, discuss generalization to other modalities (images, recommendation) where φ is learned, and how the method interacts with learned embeddings.

Relation to prior work
- The dyadic reduction is related to multiclass SVM variants (Weston & Watkins; Crammer & Singer), preference/label ranking (Hüllermeier & Fürnkranz), and previous work on generalization with interdependent data (Usunier et al., Ralaivola & Amini). The aggressive sampling parallels negative sampling/sampled softmax used in large-vocabulary settings. Tree-based extreme classifiers (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree) are appropriate baselines. It would help to position this work relative to more recent candidate-generation approaches and to discuss when dyadic reduction is preferable to hierarchical partitioning.

Questions for the authors
1. What exact loss and optimizer are used for training f? How do you regularize, and how does that map to the theoretical class H and Hr?
2. Did you try importance weighting by η_y/π_y during training? If not, why? If yes, how did it affect variance and accuracy?
3. How should practitioners pick π and κ? Can you provide a practical rule-of-thumb derived from Theorem 2 (e.g., target Mκ ≳ c(K − 1))?
4. How is nearest-centroid candidate selection implemented computationally? Are test-time numbers including the centroid search? Any use of ANN indexing?
5. Provide ablations over κ, π, q and report P@k/PSP@k on LSHTC to align with extreme classification literature.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a key scalability bottleneck with a simple, effective idea.
- Nontrivial theoretical analysis for dependent, biased sampling using local fractional Rademacher complexities.
- Strong empirical trade-offs on large benchmarks with public code.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical bounds are hard to interpret; potential looseness via α, β; lack of guidance on choosing π, κ.
- Missing ablations and standard extreme classification metrics; some methodological details unclear.
- Generality beyond text and dependence on engineered φ not fully addressed.

Overall evaluation
The paper presents a practical and conceptually clean contribution with some novel analysis and promising empirical benefits. With improved clarity on the theory, added ablations/metrics, and a clearer account of test-time candidate selection cost, this would be a solid addition. I lean toward weak accept, contingent on addressing the above concerns. Confidence: moderate.
