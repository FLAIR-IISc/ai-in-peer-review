This paper addresses the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, specifically focusing on scaling one-versus-all (OVA) approaches. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling strategy to reduce the computational burden and address class imbalance, coupled with theoretical analysis justifying the approach via Rademacher complexity bounds. While the motivation is strong and the problem well-defined, I find the paper ultimately lacking in convincing empirical evidence and relying heavily on theoretical justification without sufficient practical demonstration of its benefits, especially compared to well-established baselines.

The core idea of double sampling – balancing class representation and reducing dyadic example construction – is reasonable. The theoretical contribution, providing generalization bounds for the proposed method, is appreciated, though the complexity of the bounds makes it difficult to extract clear practical implications. The connection to fractional Rademacher complexity and graph covering provides a solid theoretical foundation, but feels somewhat detached from the practical benefits claimed. 

However, my primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation. While the authors compare against several baselines, the presentation of results is insufficiently detailed. The parameter settings for each method, beyond a brief mention in the supplementary material, are not adequately justified within the main text. More importantly, the gains reported over strong baselines like OVA and M-SVM, particularly on the largest datasets (WIKI-100K), seem modest given the algorithmic complexity introduced by the sampling strategy.  The statement that OVA and M-SVM become "impossible" due to memory limitations feels like a convenient justification for not achieving substantial improvements *over* these methods, rather than a genuine demonstration of scalability. It’s unclear why these established methods completely fail for 100K classes on the provided hardware – typical NIPS papers demonstrate careful engineering to push baselines to their limits.

The description of the feature mapping (φ) is also rather brief. While the use of common word overlap features is understandable, the lack of detail hinders assessment of its effectiveness. Similarly, the candidate selection strategy, while a sensible optimization, isn't thoroughly evaluated in isolation – how sensitive are the results to the number of candidates (q)?

The paper also suffers from clarity issues in several sections. The notation is dense and requires careful reading. The progression of ideas within the theoretical sections feels somewhat disjointed. While the authors attempt to explain the intuition behind their approach, it's not always readily apparent, particularly for readers less familiar with Rademacher complexity. 

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in machine learning.
*   Provides theoretical justification for the proposed method.
*   Introduces a reasonable heuristic for balancing class distributions and reducing computational cost.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Empirical results are not compelling, with modest gains over established baselines.
*   Experimental setup lacks sufficient detail regarding parameter tuning and justification.
*   The scalability claims regarding OVA and M-SVM feel unsubstantiated.
*   Clarity and readability are hindered by dense notation and a disjointed presentation.
*   The connection between the theoretical analysis and practical benefits is unclear.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution is non-trivial and could be of interest to researchers working on generalization bounds for dependent data.
*   The proposed sampling strategy might be beneficial in specific scenarios where class imbalance is severe and computational resources are extremely limited.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The empirical evaluation is unconvincing and does not demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods.
*   The paper lacks a clear demonstration of scalability beyond simply stating that other methods "fail".
*   The clarity issues make it difficult to assess the full contribution of the work.




**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject**. While the paper presents a technically sound approach with a theoretical foundation, the lack of compelling empirical evidence and clarity issues outweigh its strengths. The claims of scalability require more rigorous justification, and the modest improvements over established baselines do not warrant acceptance to NIPS at this time. I would encourage the authors to conduct a more thorough experimental evaluation, focusing on comparing their method to optimized implementations of existing techniques, and to address the clarity issues in the presentation.
