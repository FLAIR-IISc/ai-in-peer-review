Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles large-scale multiclass (including extreme) classification by reducing the problem to a binary classification over dyadic example–class pairs, while aggressively subsampling to keep computation and memory manageable. The key idea is a “double sampling” scheme: (i) oversample minority classes and subsample majority classes to counter long-tailed label distributions, producing a balanced subset Sπ; (ii) for each retained example, sample κ “adversarial” negative classes to form a reduced set of dyadic pairs, avoiding the O(mK) blow-up. The learned scorer operates on a joint example–class feature map φ, and prediction uses a candidate selection heuristic that scores only a small set of nearest centroid classes. Theoretical contributions include generalization bounds for the dyadic ERM under dependence and distribution shift induced by double sampling, using fractional/local Rademacher complexities and graph-cover arguments. Empirically, on LSHTC/Wikipedia datasets up to 100K classes, the approach shows competitive accuracy and macro-F1 with substantially lower time/memory than OVA/M-SVM/PD-Sparse, and comparable or better performance than tree-based methods (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree). The work builds on dyadic reduction and dependent-data generalization bounds (e.g., Usunier & Amini; Janson; Ralaivola & Amini), and on extreme classification methods (FastXML, RecallTree, PD-Sparse). The empirical feature map for text draws from IR/learning-to-rank heuristics.

Quality
- Theory: The dependent-variable/local fractional Rademacher analysis is a solid direction and relevant to dyadic reductions. The bounds account for class-prior shift (π) and sampling (κ) via α, β terms. However, the practical implications are under-explained: α=max(η/π) and β=max(1/π) can be large under aggressive subsampling, potentially dominating rates; guidance on choosing π, κ to keep α, β controlled is missing. The connection between the bound and the implemented ERM (which uses unweighted empirical risk) could be strengthened; importance-weighted ERM would more directly correct the induced shift.
- Methodology: The double sampling is simple and potentially effective. The prediction-time candidate selection is a useful heuristic but unanalysed; its effect on accuracy/latency should be isolated. The joint feature map uses only 10 hand-crafted features tailored to text; this choice simplifies implementation but may limit generality and capacity.
- Experiments: The datasets and scale are appropriate. The comparisons are mostly fair for the time (2017), though some strong contemporaries in extreme classification are omitted. There is limited ablation: no sensitivity study over κ, π, or candidate set size q; no analysis of the separate impact of (i) class rebalancing vs (ii) dyadic subsampling vs (iii) candidate pruning. Converting multilabel data to multiclass by duplicating instances changes the problem; implications for baselines (especially those tailored to multilabel) should be discussed. Reporting lacks standard errors/confidence intervals.

Clarity
The paper is readable but has several notation glitches and typos (e.g., indices in Eq. (3), (4), (6); occasional confusion between g and f∘φ). The high-level rationale for the bounds and the role of the fractional cover are only briefly sketched; adding intuition and a short proof roadmap would help. The feature map is said to be in Table 1, but the paper should include enough detail to reproduce it without relying on the supplement. Hyperparameter choices (π normalization, κ, q) and their tuning ranges should be more explicit in the main text. The candidate selection procedure should specify the distance metric and q per dataset.

Originality
The combination of (i) dyadic reduction with (ii) aggressive double sampling and (iii) new generalization bounds that handle dependence and sampling-induced shift is novel and nontrivial. Sampling negatives in multiclass/ranking is common, but formal learning guarantees under this specific dependent/doubly-sampled construction seem new. The prediction-time candidate pruning via nearest centroids is a pragmatic addition, though heuristic.

Significance
At-scale multiclass classification remains important, and the proposed approach offers a practical trade-off between accuracy and resource usage. If broadly validated, the double sampling strategy could be adopted beyond text. However, significance is tempered by limited empirical scope (only text with very low-dimensional hand-crafted features), lack of ablations, and the gap between theory and practice (no weighting, limited guidance for π, κ). Stronger baselines and more comprehensive analyses would increase impact.

Pros
- Addresses scalability and class imbalance in a unified, simple framework.
- Theoretical bounds tailored to the dependent dyadic setting with sampling shift.
- Competitive accuracy/macro-F1 on large datasets with substantial gains in time/memory.
- Open-source code.

Cons
- Limited empirical analysis: few ablations; unclear sensitivity to κ, π, q.
- Feature map is simplistic and domain-specific; unclear generality to non-text modalities.
- Theory–practice gap: no importance weighting to correct sampling shift; lack of guidance to control α, β.
- Conversion of multilabel to multiclass may bias comparisons; some strong baselines omitted; no statistical significance reporting.
- Clarity issues in notation and exposition.

Questions for the authors
1. How should practitioners set π and κ? Can you provide data-driven rules or validation curves showing the accuracy/time/memory trade-offs?
2. Did you try importance-weighted ERM using weights proportional to η/π to correct the class-prior shift suggested by Theorem 2? How does it affect performance?
3. What is the impact of the candidate set size q on accuracy/latency? Is nearest-centroid selection competitive with other candidate generators (e.g., ANN on learned class embeddings)?
4. Can you report ablations isolating the effects of (i) class balancing, (ii) negative sampling κ, and (iii) candidate selection?
5. How does the method perform with richer linear features (e.g., TF-IDF on concatenated [x; class profile]) or learned embeddings?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add importance weighting to the empirical risk to match the theoretical setting; analyze stability and variance.
- Provide ablations over π, κ, q and learning curves to illuminate trade-offs and robustness.
- Expand evaluation to additional datasets (e.g., Amazon, other extreme benchmarks) and consider non-text domains with an appropriate φ.
- Strengthen baselines where possible (e.g., scalable OVA variants, more recent extreme classifiers) and clarify fairness of multilabel-to-multiclass conversion.
- Improve exposition: clean up notation, add intuition for the bounds, include full details of the 10 features, and report statistical variability.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents a promising and practically relevant idea with interesting theory, but the empirical evaluation and the theory–practice connection need strengthening. With clearer guidance on hyperparameters, importance-weighted training, and more comprehensive experiments/ablations, this work could become a solid contribution.
