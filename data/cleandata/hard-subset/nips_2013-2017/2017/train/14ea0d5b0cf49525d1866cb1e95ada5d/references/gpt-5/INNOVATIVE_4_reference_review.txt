Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme/multiclass classification by revisiting dyadic reduction: map (x, y) to a joint feature space φ(x, y) and learn a single scoring function f, so g(x, y) = f(φ(x, y)). Instead of constructing all m(K−1) dyads, the paper proposes an aggressive double sampling (π, κ)-DS: (i) re-sample the training set to counter long-tail class imbalance (oversample rare, subsample frequent classes with probabilities πk), and (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ adversarial classes (negative labels). This yields dramatic savings in training examples. At prediction time, they further sample a candidate set of q classes using nearest centroids in the dyadic space before scoring.

On the theory side, the dyadic construction induces dependencies across pairs sharing the same original example. Building on fractional graph covers (Janson) and fractional Rademacher complexity (Usunier & Amini), the paper derives new generalization bounds that incorporate local Rademacher complexities, capturing variance-sensitive “fast rate” behavior. Theorem 1 handles the standard dyadic reduction under dependence; Theorem 2 covers the distribution shift induced by the π-reweighting and the κ negative sampling, introducing α and β factors that depend on π and the true class proportions η. The bounds show consistency of ERM on the sampled dyads and make explicit the dependence on M, κ, and K.

Empirically, on LSHTC/Wikipedia subsets up to 100K classes, the method shows favorable time/memory vs. accuracy/MaF1 tradeoffs compared to OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding baselines (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree). The dyadic mapping uses 10 lightweight, IR-inspired features (including distance to class centroids), which also enables fast candidate selection at test time. Code is provided.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with a bold emphasis on scalability: the double sampling idea (class-rebalancing + negative sampling) is pragmatic and highly impactful for extreme classification.
- Theoretical contribution: combining local Rademacher complexity with fractional covers to handle interdependent dyads and sampling-induced distribution shift is novel and interesting. The dependence of the bound on κ and reweighting factors is explicit and informative.
- Practical relevance: the approach achieves near tree-like training/prediction cost while avoiding error cascades, and it scales to 100K classes with competitive accuracy/MaF1 and strong memory/runtime advantages.
- Clear link between algorithmic choices and the bounds (e.g., how κ, π affect the rates); candidate selection is well-motivated by the dyadic centroid feature.
- Reproducibility: datasets, code, and tuning strategy are provided.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Limited feature representation: the 10-feature dyadic mapping may handicap accuracy relative to strong linear models on high-dimensional TF-IDF. It is unclear how much of the performance comes from the sampling vs. the feature design. An ablation (same features across methods, or richer φ for the proposed method) would clarify fairness and headroom.
- Missing sensitivity analyses: results as functions of κ, π, and q (candidate set size) would be valuable. For example, how does accuracy/time trade off with κ, and where do diminishing returns set in?
- Training objective and optimizer details are under-specified: is f linear? hinge vs. logistic loss? regularization? class/instance weighting to correct for π (importance weighting) is discussed in the theory but not in the implementation details.
- Bounds’ tightness/interpretability: while novel, the bounds involve several constants (α, β) and a fractional Rademacher term that may be opaque to practitioners. A corollary instantiating typical choices (e.g., uniform κ-negatives, π ∝ 1/µk) and yielding a clean rate would increase accessibility.
- Empirical scope: all experiments are text classification; the paper claims broader applicability (e.g., recommendation, images), but no evidence is shown. A small-scale non-text experiment (even synthetic) with a different φ would strengthen the generality claim.
- Baselines: the comparison set is reasonable for the time, but methods like WSABIE/AnnexML-style label embedding with candidate selection and negative sampling are closely related; a direct comparison would better position novelty. Also, report whether tree methods used candidate pruning akin to the proposed q-selection for fairness on test-time cost.
- Clarity and polish: some notation is heavy and contains minor typos (e.g., logs in bounds, indices). Streamlining Section 2.1–2.2 and moving more intuition into the main text would help.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a simple, scalable reduction with a theoretically grounded treatment of dependence and sampling-induced shift—novel and potentially influential.
- Strong practical gains on truly large label spaces with open-source code.
- Bridges a gap between heuristic negative sampling used widely in practice and formal generalization guarantees.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental evaluation missing key ablations and broader domains; fairness of feature choices could be questioned.
- Some implementation details and theoretical constants lack interpretability for non-experts.

Questions for authors
1) What loss/regularizer/optimizer do you use for f, and do you apply importance weights to compensate for π during training?
2) Please include sensitivity curves for κ, π (or an exponent on 1/µk), and q, reporting accuracy, MaF1, time, and memory.
3) Can you provide an ablation showing the effect of candidate selection alone (with and without q) and of feature 9 (centroid distance)?
4) How does performance change with a richer φ (e.g., add BM25/TF-IDF similarities) and does the runtime remain favorable?
5) Could you compare to a WSABIE-style method with negative sampling and candidate selection?

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Novelty and practical impact of the double-sampling reduction with supporting theory outweigh the empirical and clarity limitations. With stronger ablations and minor revisions, this could be a solid contribution to extreme classification.
