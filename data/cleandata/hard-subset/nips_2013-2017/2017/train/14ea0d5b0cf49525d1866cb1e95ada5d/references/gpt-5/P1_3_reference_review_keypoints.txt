- Paper proposes (π, κ)-double-sampling dyadic reduction for extreme multi-class classification, with candidate-class preselection and generalization bounds for both standard and double-sampled dyads.
- Theorem 1/2 use local fractional Rademacher complexity to handle dependent dyadic examples and show explicit dependence on sampling via α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y, plus κ and M terms.
- Bounds plausibly capture bias–variance trade-offs from sampling but may be loose in long-tailed regimes (α, β large); constants/log terms and some assumptions are unclear and proofs are deferred.
- The (π, κ)-DS algorithm is simple and sensible; the candidate-selection heuristic reduces scoring cost but is not integrated into the theoretical analysis.
- Experiments on LSHTC DMOZ/Wikipedia (up to 100K classes) show competitive or better accuracy and macro-F1 with much lower memory/runtime than OVA/M-SVM/PD-Sparse and better accuracy than tree methods at similar cost.
- Missing experimental ablations: sensitivity to κ, candidate set size q, different π choices, oversampling overfitting, and comparison to hard-negative mining; precision@k and non-text datasets are not reported.
- Base learner f, chosen loss (logistic vs hinge), and regularization/optimization details are underspecified in the main text and relegated to the supplement.
- Paper is generally readable but hampered by notational issues, typos, inconsistent symbols (e.g., δ), unclear dyadic indices/pseudo-code, and missing summary of the 10 dyadic mapping features.
- Novelty lies in the aggressive double-sampling scheme for dyadic reductions and the dependent-data, data-dependent risk bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity.
- Candidate-selection heuristic is practical but not novel and lacks theoretical guarantees (e.g., recall analysis).
- Significance: addresses dyadic quadratic blow-up, scales to 100K classes, and offers a practically useful, memory/time-efficient approach for extreme classification.
- Strength: practical, easy-to-implement scalable algorithm with theory for dependent dyadic data and strong large-scale empirical results, especially on long-tailed distributions using macro-F1.
- Weakness: limited ablations/sensitivity analysis, underspecified base learner and loss, theoretical gap for candidate pruning, potential looseness under extreme reweighting, and evaluation confined to text datasets/metrics.
- Questions for authors: specify base classifier and loss and sensitivity to regularization/C; provide κ vs q trade-off plots and recommended settings; clarify π normalization and alternatives (e.g., tempered inverse frequency); report experiments with hard-negative mining; discuss integrating candidate selection into theory (recall guarantees).
- Minor comments: fix dyadic index/pseudo-code bugs (e.g., “if k < y”), define all symbols (V[h], Hr, RT(S)(H), ωk), correct inconsistent log(1/δ)/m terms and constants, and summarize the 10 dyadic features in the main text.
- Recommendation: Weak accept.
