- Tackles extreme multi-class classification, addressing scalability limits of one-vs-all, tree-based, and embedding methods.  
- Proposes "aggressive double sampling": oversample minority classes, subsample majority classes, and reduce dyadic example generation.  
- Provides theoretical generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity for the sampled dyadic approach.  
- Demonstrates competitive accuracy with significantly improved memory usage and runtime on large text datasets (DMOZ, WIKI-100K).  
- Builds on dyadic representation literature but focuses on efficient sampling rather than exhaustive dyadic generation.  
- Leverages recent generalization analyses for dependent data in its theoretical development.  
- Strength: addresses a relevant, practical problem in large-scale classification.  
- Strength: novel sampling strategy that balances classes and reduces computational cost.  
- Strength: rigorous theoretical foundation via local Rademacher complexity.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance and scalability advantages on large datasets.  
- Strength: generally clear writing and well-documented experiments and algorithms.  
- Weakness: theoretical sections (Theorems 1–2) are dense and may be hard to follow without statistical learning background.  
- Weakness: introduces two hyperparameters (π and κ) with limited discussion on sensitivity and selection guidance.  
- Weakness: brief description of the feature mapping φ; needs more detail and rationale.  
- Weakness: comparisons lack deeper analysis (e.g., detailed runtime and memory breakdowns explaining why it outperforms baselines).  
- Weakness: minor typographical and grammatical errors.  
- Qualitative: technically sound, original, and significant for scalability; algorithm descriptions are clear.  
- For acceptance: relevant problem, novel effective approach, strong theory, and impactful empirical results.  
- Against acceptance: theoretical density, hyperparameter sensitivity, and insufficient feature/method-detail analysis.  
- Overall: strong paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to clarify theory, expand hyperparameter tuning discussion, and elaborate feature mapping.
