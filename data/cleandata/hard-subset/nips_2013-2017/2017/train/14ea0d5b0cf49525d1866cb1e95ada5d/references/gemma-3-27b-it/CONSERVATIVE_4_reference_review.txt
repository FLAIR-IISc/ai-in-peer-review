This paper addresses the problem of extreme multi-class classification, a common challenge in text categorization and related fields. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling approach to reduce the computational burden associated with the standard one-vs-all (OVA) paradigm and to mitigate the class imbalance inherent in such settings. The core idea involves subsampling frequent classes and oversampling rare classes before constructing a reduced set of dyadic examples for binary classification. The authors also provide theoretical guarantees based on Rademacher complexity.

While the problem addressed is relevant and the motivation for exploring more scalable solutions is clear, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing. My primary concern lies with the lack of a sufficiently rigorous and comprehensive empirical evaluation, particularly in comparison to strong, established baselines. 

The authors acknowledge the existence of several state-of-the-art methods (e.g., FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse) and include them in their experimental comparison. However, the results, as presented in Table 3 and Figure 3, are not dramatically compelling. The proposed (π, κ)-DS method achieves competitive, but not overwhelmingly superior, performance.  Critically, the gains appear marginal, and are often accompanied by the need for careful hyperparameter tuning (π and κ).  The authors claim their method demonstrates the "best compromise among the compared methods over the three time, memory and performance measures." This claim is weakly supported by the figures. The advantage in runtime and memory usage, while present, doesn’t seem substantial enough to justify the added complexity of the sampling scheme and the associated hyperparameters. The authors mention that OVA and M-SVM become intractable due to memory limitations, but fail to demonstrate how their approach scales *significantly* beyond PD-Sparse, which already attempts to address memory constraints.  

Furthermore, the theoretical contribution, while interesting, feels somewhat disconnected from the empirical results. Deriving generalization bounds is valuable, but the practical implications of these bounds are not clearly articulated. The conditions under which these bounds hold, and how they relate to the parameter choices in the algorithm, are not fully explored. The use of "Local Rademacher complexity" is mentioned but not sufficiently explained for a reader unfamiliar with the technique. The theorems themselves are dense and difficult to parse without a deeper understanding of the underlying mathematical machinery. 

The paper also suffers from a degree of imprecision in its presentation. For example, the description of the feature mapping φ is vague. Claiming it’s application dependent without a more thorough discussion of common applications and the choice of features for *this* application weakens the argument. The connection between the theoretical analysis (Rademacher complexity) and the practical implementation (sampling scheme) could be more clearly elucidated. 

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*   Presents a novel approach to reduce computational complexity and mitigate class imbalance.
*   Attempts to provide theoretical justification for the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Empirical evaluation is not sufficiently compelling. The gains over strong baselines are marginal.
*   Theoretical contributions are difficult to follow and their practical implications are unclear.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity and hyperparameter tuning.
*   Vague description of the feature mapping.
*   The connection between theory and practice is weakly established.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel approach to a challenging problem.
*   Includes a theoretical analysis, though its practical relevance is questionable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The theoretical contribution is too abstract and lacks practical guidance.
*   The paper suffers from a lack of clarity and precision in its presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**  Reject. While the paper addresses an important problem and proposes a potentially interesting approach, the lack of a strong empirical validation and the abstract nature of the theoretical contribution make it unsuitable for NIPS at this time.  I recommend the authors significantly expand their experimental evaluation, focusing on a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and provide a clearer and more practical interpretation of their theoretical results. They should also consider providing a more detailed discussion of the parameter sensitivity of their proposed method.
