- Proposal: Reduce extreme/multi-class classification to dyadic (x,y) binary problems with double sampling—class rebalancing (π) and κ negative-class sampling—plus nearest-centroid candidate selection at inference.
- Theory: Provide generalization bounds for dependent dyadic examples using fractional/local Rademacher complexities that account for sampling-induced distribution shift.
- Empirics: Shows competitive MaF1 and substantially reduced training time/memory on LSHTC datasets (up to 100K classes) versus OvA, multiclass SVM, tree methods, and PD-Sparse.

Strengths
- Practicality/scalability: (π, κ)-DS directly tackles mK blow-up and long-tailed labels; κ sampling and centroid candidate selection are simple and effective.
- Theoretical novelty: Extends analysis for interdependent data with fractional covers and local Rademacher complexity, explicitly modeling sampling bias.
- Empirical trade-offs: More accurate than tree methods while achieving comparable runtime and much lower memory/training cost.

Weaknesses / clarifications needed
- α, β scaling: Role and scaling of α = max η_y/π_y and β = max 1/π_y are underexplained; π∝1/η_y may make bounds loose or vacuous.
- m vs N dependence: Inconsistencies using m versus N = m(K−1) need clarification and constants explained; practical rates (e.g., O(1/√(Mκ))) unclear.
- Importance weighting: ERM on sampled data lacks importance weights (η_y/π_y); rationale or analysis of weighting/control variates absent.
- Optimization details: Loss, surrogate, optimizer, and regularization are unspecified and not connected to theoretical assumptions.
- Negative sampling strategy: Uniform κ sampling may be suboptimal; no ablation on hard or nonuniform negative sampling variants.
- Candidate selection cost: Nearest-centroid search is O(Kp) unless indexed—cost/accounting at test time not clarified and ANN options not discussed.
- Missing ablations: No sensitivity analysis over κ, π, and q to show robustness and speed/accuracy trade-offs.
- Evaluation metrics: Lacks standard extreme-classification metrics (P@k, propensity-scored P@k); MaF1 alone is insufficient for comparison.
- Dataset treatment: Converting multi-label to multiclass by duplicating instances alters distribution and may not reflect multi-label settings.
- Baselines: Omission of label-embedding baselines and unclear PD-Sparse settings/training budgets limit fairness of comparisons.
- Notation and presentation: Inconsistent notation (m vs N, z/h definitions) and insufficient intuitive explanation of the graph cover and complexity terms.
- Feature mapping φ: Uses engineered ranking features for text; unclear how method generalizes to learned embeddings or other modalities.

Relation to prior work
- Connects to multiclass SVMs, label-ranking/preference learning, negative-sampling/sampled softmax, and tree-based extreme classifiers; positioning relative to recent candidate-generation methods should be clarified.

Key questions for authors
- What exact loss, optimizer, and regularization were used and how do they map to H and Hr in theory?
- Did you try importance weighting by η_y/π_y and what were the effects on bias/variance?
- Practical rule-of-thumb for choosing π and κ (e.g., target Mκ relative to K)?
- How is nearest-centroid candidate selection implemented; do reported test times include centroid search; any ANN used?
- Please provide ablations over κ, π, q and report P@k/PSP@k on LSHTC.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses scalability bottleneck with a simple, effective approach; nontrivial theoretical analysis and strong empirical trade-offs with public code.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical bounds are hard to interpret and may be loose; missing ablations, standard metrics, and several methodological clarifications.
- Generality beyond text and reliance on engineered φ is not sufficiently demonstrated.

Overall assessment
- Promising, conceptually clean contribution with novel analysis and empirical benefits; leans toward weak accept contingent on addressing theoretical clarity, ablations/metrics, and inference-cost details.
