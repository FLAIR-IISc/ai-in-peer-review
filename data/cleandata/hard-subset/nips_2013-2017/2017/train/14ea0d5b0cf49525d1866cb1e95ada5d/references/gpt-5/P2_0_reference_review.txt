Summary
The paper addresses extreme multiclass classification by reducing it to a binary dyadic problem while controlling computational cost via a “double sampling” scheme: (i) resampling the training set to counter long-tailed label distributions (oversample rare classes, subsample frequent ones), and (ii) for each retained example, sampling only κ adversarial (negative) classes to form dyads. The approach is paired with a simple, domain-specific joint example–class representation (10 IR-inspired features) and a candidate-selection heuristic at test time (nearest centroids). Theoretically, the paper derives generalization bounds for the dyadic ERM with dependent pairs using local fractional Rademacher complexity (Theorem 1) and extends the analysis to the double-sampled setting with distribution shift (Theorem 2). Empirically, on LSHTC text datasets (up to 100k classes), the method shows favorable time/memory trade-offs and competitive accuracy/MaF1 versus OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding baselines. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
- Dyadic reduction and pairwise ranking losses are well-established (e.g., multiclass SVM, AdaBoost.MR, WARP, pairwise SVMrank). Prior work [16] analyzed dependent dyads via fractional Rademacher complexity. This paper builds on that line, adding local complexity techniques [22] to obtain variance-dependent rates and handling sampling-induced label shift.
- In extreme classification, alternatives include tree-based routing, label embeddings, and sparse linear OVA/PD-Sparse. Negative sampling and candidate selection are common ideas (e.g., sampled softmax, ANN over label embeddings). The paper’s novelty is mainly in the combination of aggressive double sampling with a bound that explicitly accounts for dependency and sampling bias.

Quality
- Theory: The derivations appear technically sound and carefully reference the right tools (graph fractional covers, local Rademacher complexities). The bounds correctly account for dependence among dyads and for sampling-induced reweighting via factors α, β. A caveat is that α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y can be large under aggressive rebalancing, potentially weakening the practical tightness of the bound; a brief discussion quantifying their typical magnitude under the recommended π_k ∝ 1/μ_k would strengthen the claims. The candidate-selection step at test time is not covered by the theory, which should be acknowledged more explicitly.
- Method: The double sampling is simple and well-motivated (computational gains and improved balance). However, the approach leans on a domain-specific hand-crafted φ with only 10 features; the training objective and learner for f (loss, regularization, linear vs nonlinear) are not spelled out in the main text, which impedes reproducibility and assessment.
- Experiments: Results suggest good accuracy/MaF1 with strong runtime/memory improvements on large text datasets. Still, some concerns remain:
  - Fairness: Several baselines (FastXML, PfastReXML) are primarily multi-label methods; the paper converts LSHTC multi-label to multiclass by duplicating instances, which can alter class priors and may favor some methods. Please justify this protocol and discuss its implications on macro-F1. 
  - Missing baselines: No comparison to pairwise ranking with negative sampling (e.g., WARP, sampled SVMrank) or to simple OVA with hard negative mining. Given the method’s pairwise and sampling nature, such baselines are important.
  - Ablations: No sensitivity analysis for π, κ, and the candidate set size q; no ablation of the candidate-selection heuristic. These are key to understanding accuracy/speed trade-offs and robustness.
  - Generality: All experiments are in text with a specific φ; a small study showing portability to a learned φ or to another domain would improve significance.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but there are typos and some notational clutter. The core ideas could be conveyed more clearly with:
  - An explicit statement of the training objective for f (loss function, hypothesis class, optimizer).
  - A concise complexity analysis illustrating how κ and |S_π| control training/test cost.
  - A brief discussion of the effect of α, β in Theorem 2 and when the bound is informative.
  - A clearer description of the 10 features used in φ (Table 1 is referenced but not visible here).
- The algorithms are helpful; the prediction candidate selection is described clearly.

Originality
- Algorithmically, double sampling for dyadic reduction combines known ideas (class rebalancing, negative sampling) in a natural way. The novelty lies primarily in the theoretical treatment of dependency under dyadic construction and of distribution shift from sampling using local fractional Rademacher complexity. This is a meaningful addition to the theory of multiclass-to-binary reductions with dependent samples.
- The candidate-selection heuristic is standard.

Significance
- Practical significance is plausible for extreme text classification: the method scales to 100k classes with good accuracy/time/memory trade-offs and code is released. 
- The theoretical contribution may interest researchers working on learning with dependent samples and reduction analyses.
- Broader impact could be limited by the reliance on a domain-specific φ and lack of evaluation beyond text; demonstrating portability would increase significance.

Pros
- Simple and scalable method with substantial runtime/memory savings.
- Theoretical bounds addressing both dependency among dyads and sampling-induced label shift.
- Competitive empirical results on large-scale datasets; code availability.

Cons
- Limited experimental scope (text only), missing key baselines, and no sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Practical tightness of bounds may suffer under aggressive sampling (large α, β); no empirical link between bounds and practice.
- Candidate selection not covered by theory; φ is hand-crafted and domain-specific; training details for f are underspecified.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please detail the learner for f (loss, hypothesis class, regularization, optimization) and report hyperparameters.
- Provide ablations over κ, π scheduling, and q, showing accuracy–speed trade-offs.
- Compare against pairwise ranking with negative sampling (e.g., WARP) and OVA with hard-negative mining.
- Quantify α, β for your chosen π in your datasets to contextualize Theorem 2.
- Discuss the implications of converting multi-label to multiclass for evaluation fairness.
- Consider adding a learned φ or a non-text domain to demonstrate generality.

Reproducibility
- Positive: code and data link provided; algorithms outlined; datasets standard.
- Needs improvement: complete feature definitions, training objective details, and hyperparameter settings in the main paper.

Overall recommendation
- Score: 6 (weak accept). The paper tackles an important scalability problem with a simple, effective method and makes a nontrivial theoretical contribution. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and some details clarified, the combination of theory, scalability, and competitive performance merits consideration. Confidence: medium.
