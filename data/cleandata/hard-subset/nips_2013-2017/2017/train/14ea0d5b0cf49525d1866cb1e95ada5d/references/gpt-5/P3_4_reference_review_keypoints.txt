- Paper reduces extreme/multi-class classification to binary dyadic pairs with aggressive class resampling (π) and negative sampling (κ) to shrink training data.
- Prediction uses centroid-based shortlisting to evaluate only q candidate classes, improving runtime/memory.
- Theoretical contribution: generalization bounds under dependent sampled pairs via fractional and local Rademacher complexities (Theorems 1–2).
- Empirical claims: favorable memory/time/accuracy trade-offs on large text datasets (up to 100k classes) versus OVA, multiclass SVM, tree-based methods, and PD-Sparse.
- Relation to prior work: builds on well-known dyadic/negative-sampling methods; novelty lies in combining class-rebalancing with negative sampling plus dependent-data analysis.
- Strength: practical recipe ((π, κ)-DS) that aggressively reduces examples and negatives with a clear algorithmic description.
- Strength: nontrivial theoretical analysis accounting for pairwise dependence and class-distribution shift.
- Strength: scalable empirical evaluation at nontrivial label counts and competitive baselines.
- Strength: simple, implementable approach usable with existing binary learners.
- Weakness: algorithmic novelty is incremental since class-balancing and negative sampling are already standard.
- Weakness: theoretical bounds may be vacuous in practice—α, β can be large when downsampling frequent classes and no practical guidance for setting π, κ.
- Weakness: experimental fairness unclear because the method uses a hand-crafted 10-d dyadic mapping and baselines may not use the same features.
- Weakness: missing ablations isolating effects of class rebalancing, negative sampling, dyadic mapping, and shortlisting.
- Weakness: key implementation details missing (π normalization with oversampling, learner/loss choice, selection of κ and q, efficient candidate selection).
- Weakness: important baseline coverage missing (sampled-softmax/fastText-style, shortlist+linear reranker); some included baselines may be handicapped by dataset conversions.
- Weakness: scope limited—experiments only on text with a specific feature mapping, so broader applicability is unproven.
- Quality: theory appears sound but practical implications and parameter guidance are not distilled; proofs deferred.
- Quality: empirical results promising but lack sensitivity analyses, ablations, and feature-parity clarity.
- Clarity: generally readable but has formatting/typo issues and omitted technical details (π normalization, loss, candidate-selection complexity).
- Originality: most original part is the theoretical treatment for double sampling; algorithmic parts are known combinations.
- Significance: could be a useful scalable baseline for extreme multiclass text classification if empirical claims hold under fair comparisons and clearer parameterization.
- Requested clarifications: define π precisely (replacement, normalization) and bound α, β when π ∝ 1/µk.
- Requested clarifications: specify binary learner/loss and training details for h.
- Requested clarifications: confirm whether baselines use the same 10-d dyadic features or original bag-of-words; ensure feature parity.
- Requested clarifications: provide ablations varying π, κ, q to show effects on accuracy/MaF1/time/memory.
- Requested clarifications: explain efficient candidate selection (ANN index?) and report q vs accuracy/time trade-offs.
- Requested clarifications: compare against sampled-softmax/fastText-style and shortlist+linear reranker baselines.
- Pros for acceptance: addresses scalability with a simple recipe, offers nontrivial generalization analysis, and shows promising empirical trade-offs.
- Cons for acceptance: limited algorithmic novelty, incomplete/fair empirical validation, weak practical guidance from theory, and reproducibility concerns.
- Recommendation: borderline reject pending clarifications, fairer comparisons, ablations, parameter guidance, and ANN-based candidate selection description; could be acceptable after revision.
