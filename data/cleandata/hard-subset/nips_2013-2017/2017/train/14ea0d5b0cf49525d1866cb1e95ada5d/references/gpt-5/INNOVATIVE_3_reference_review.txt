Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme/multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem via dyadic example–class pairs, but avoids the O(mK) blow-up by “aggressive double sampling”: (i) rebalance the training set through per-class sampling probabilities π (oversample tail classes, subsample head classes), and (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ adversarial classes rather than all K − 1. A linear scorer is trained on dyadic features φ(x, y) built from simple query–document style features; at test time, candidate classes are pruned to the q nearest centroids to keep prediction fast. Theoretically, the reduction induces dependence among dyadic pairs and a distribution shift from class reweighting; the authors provide generalization bounds using fractional local Rademacher complexity (Theorem 1) and, critically, bounds that incorporate the double sampling parameters (Theorem 2) via α, β terms reflecting the sampling scheme, thereby establishing consistency of ERM under this reduction. Empirically, on LSHTC/Wikipedia datasets up to 100K classes, the method is competitive to or better than OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, and several tree/embedding approaches in a time/memory/accuracy trade-off, with particularly strong macro-F1.

This connects to earlier dyadic reductions for multiclass (e.g., Usunier/Amini et al.), to tree-based methods (RecallTree, FastXML/PfastReXML), and to label embeddings. The sampling idea resonates with widely used negative sampling/candidate sampling in large-vocabulary models, but prior work seldom provides generalization guarantees under dependent dyadic samples and explicit class-reweighting; here, the local fractional RC analysis is a notable addition.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity with pragmatic impact: the (π, κ)-double sampling simultaneously addresses long-tailed label imbalance and quadratic expansion in dyadic reductions.
- Theory: Extends prior fractional Rademacher-based analysis to local complexities and to the double-sampled setting, capturing dependence and class-prior shift. Theorems 1–2 are technically nontrivial and, if correct, provide useful guidance for practice.
- Scalability: Demonstrated on up to 100K classes with clear gains in memory and runtime; candidate selection makes prediction feasible.
- Macro-F1 improvements: Particularly relevant under long-tailed distributions. Public code is a plus.

Weaknesses
- Novelty of the algorithmic core is moderate: oversampling tails, subsampling heads, and sampling a small number of negatives per example are common heuristics; the main novelty lies in the theoretical grounding and in tailoring these to dyadic reduction. The candidate selection by nearest centroids is also standard.
- Theory–practice gap: Bounds are stated for 0/1 loss, while training presumably uses a convex surrogate (hinge/logistic); the implications for the actual optimizer are not discussed. The magnitude and practical effect of α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy are not analyzed; for highly skewed distributions and inverse-π schedules, these constants can be large and could weaken the bounds.
- Clarity/missing details: The learning algorithm for f in the dyadic space is not explicitly specified (linear SVM? logistic?), nor are regularization and optimization details. Important implementation choices (π schedule normalization, κ and q selection) and sensitivity analyses are not systematically reported. A controlled ablation of single vs double sampling, and of candidate selection, would strengthen the empirical case.
- Experimental comparability: The φ mapping uses only 10 task-specific features, which may handicap baselines designed for high-dimensional sparse tf-idf features. Several strong extreme classifiers are geared to multilabel rather than multiclass and may be disadvantaged by converting multilabel to multiclass via duplication; the paper acknowledges this but does not analyze the effect. The fairness of resource constraints (e.g., hashing in PD-Sparse vs full models) could be discussed more.

Quality
Overall technically sound, with careful attention to dependencies in dyadic reductions. The bounds look plausible, though the paper would benefit from intuition about rates and comparison to [16] beyond a formal statement. Empirical evaluation is broad, but lacks ablations and sensitivity to π, κ, and q.

Clarity
Generally well organized, but notation is dense and some definitions are buried. Please clarify the learning algorithm for f, the precise π normalization, and provide a brief complexity analysis in terms of m, K, κ, q, and p. Proof sketches or intuition about how local complexities improve rates would help.

Originality
Algorithmically incremental but theoretically novel in this specific context. The use of local fractional Rademacher complexities for dependent dyadic samples with class reweighting is, to my knowledge, new and valuable.

Significance
Addresses a real bottleneck in dyadic reductions and extreme classification with a simple method practitioners can adopt. The theoretical results can inform future work on sampled reductions beyond text. While some components are standard, the combined pipeline plus guarantees is a useful contribution.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Specify the training loss/optimizer for f and discuss surrogate-to-0/1 transfer.
- Provide intuition/empirical estimates for α and β under your π choices; how large do they get on LSHTC?
- Add ablations: effect of κ on time/accuracy; effect of candidate set size q; single vs double sampling.
- Report results with a high-dimensional sparse φ baseline to ensure fair comparison with OVA/PD-Sparse.
- Discuss whether hard-negative sampling (e.g., using centroid distances) further helps and how it would affect theory.

Pros
- Simple, scalable method with strong macro-F1 and resource usage.
- Nontrivial theoretical guarantees under dependence and sampling shift.
- Public code and experiments up to 100K classes.

Cons
- Algorithmic novelty is limited; some components are standard.
- Missing ablations/sensitivity and some implementation details.
- Potential mismatch between theory (0/1) and practice (surrogates) not addressed.

Recommendation
Weak Accept. The theoretical contribution plus a practical, effective pipeline make this a worthwhile addition, despite some incremental aspects and missing analyses. Confidence: medium.
