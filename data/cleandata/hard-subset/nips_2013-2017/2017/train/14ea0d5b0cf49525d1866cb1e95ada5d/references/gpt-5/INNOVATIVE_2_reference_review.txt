Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles large-scale multiclass (extreme) classification by reducing it to a binary problem over dyadic example–class pairs, but without constructing all O(mK) pairs. The core idea is an aggressive double sampling scheme: (i) sample training examples with class-dependent probabilities π to counter long-tailed label skew (oversample minority, subsample majority), then (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ negative classes to form dyadic pairs. This aims to reduce both computational and memory burden while mitigating imbalance.

On the theory side, the dyadic reduction introduces dependence among pairs sharing the same example and a distribution shift due to sampling. The authors derive generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization in this setting using fractional and local Rademacher complexities under dependence (building on [14, 22, 25]) and show consistency despite dependence and class-prior shift. They further propose a pragmatic prediction-time candidate selection (q nearest centroids) to avoid scoring all classes.

Experiments on LSHTC/Wikipedia datasets (up to 100K classes) compare against OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding methods (RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML). The proposed method achieves competitive or better accuracy/MaF1, with substantially lower memory/runtime than one-vs-all and PD-Sparse, and more robust accuracy than tree methods. The feature mapping φ for text is a 10-dimensional hand-crafted similarity vector inspired by learning-to-rank.

Relative to prior multiclass-to-binary dyadic reductions (e.g., [1, 16, 26]) and extreme classification (trees [4, 9, 21], embeddings [11, 19]), the novelty lies in the aggressive double sampling coupled with new generalization bounds that explicitly handle dependence and sampling bias, and in showing strong practicality at 100K classes.

Strengths
- Conceptual boldness: The double sampling scheme directly addresses both dyadic explosion and long-tailed class imbalance, a key pain point in extreme classification. It is simple, scalable, and broadly applicable.
- Theory under dependence and shift: The use of fractional/local Rademacher complexities to derive bounds for interdependent dyadic pairs with shifted class priors is nontrivial and, to my knowledge, novel in this reduction context. The results provide a principled basis for the aggressive sampling strategy.
- Practical impact: Clear reductions in memory and time with competitive performance on large benchmarks (50K/100K classes). The candidate selection at prediction further reduces cost with minimal engineering overhead (centroids are precomputed).
- Clarity of reduction: The dyadic transformation and the (π, κ)-DS algorithm are clearly presented; the relationship to the ranking-style multiclass loss is well motivated.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Dependence on hand-crafted φ: The empirical success on text uses a bespoke 10-dim feature mapping. This raises questions about generality to other domains and about how much of the gain comes from feature engineering versus the reduction itself. No ablations on φ or learned φ are provided.
- Loose constants in bounds: The risk bounds depend on α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y. Under aggressive oversampling/subsampling, β can be large, potentially making the bounds vacuous. Some guidance on choosing π to control α, β (beyond π ∝ 1/µ) would help, as would empirical verification that the theory’s scaling matches observed behavior.
- Sampling choices and sensitivity: κ and π are central but there is little sensitivity analysis or learning of these parameters. Uniform negative sampling may be suboptimal; “hard negative” schemes might further improve performance, yet are not explored/theorized.
- Candidate selection theory: The q-nearest-centroid candidate pruning is effective in practice, but there is no analysis of the additional approximation error it introduces. Ablations varying q and quantifying the trade-off are limited in the main text.
- Comparisons and scope: While the baselines are reasonable for the time, it would be stronger to include more dyadic/embedding-based competitors (e.g., WSABIE-like approaches [28]) and a non-text dataset to demonstrate domain generality. The conversion of multilabel to multiclass by replication may also distort distributions; a comment on this impact would help.
- Training details: The paper does not fully specify f and the loss/optimizer used in practice (hinge/logistic, regularization). Reproducibility would benefit from those details in the main paper (though code link is provided).

Quality
The theoretical development is interesting and appears sound at a high level; proofs are in the supplement. The empirical section is solid for text benchmarks and convincingly demonstrates scalability and robustness to long tails. However, stronger ablations (π, κ, q) and broader domains would further substantiate the claims.

Clarity
Generally clearly written and well organized. The exposition of the reduction, bounds, and algorithms is accessible. Some notational details (e.g., precise normalization of π) and the practical training objective for f could be clarified in the main text. Minor editorial issues exist but do not impede understanding.

Originality
The combination of aggressive double sampling with dependent-data generalization bounds is novel. While sampling negatives is common, tying it to class-rebalancing π with theoretical guarantees under dependence and class-prior shift is a fresh and valuable contribution in this space.

Significance
Given the prevalence of long-tailed extreme classification problems, the proposed approach is likely to be useful to practitioners and inspire follow-up work on sampled dyadic reductions with guarantees. The theory may also be of independent interest to researchers studying learning with dependent samples.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide guidance or empirical sensitivity on setting π and κ, and report α, β for your runs?
- What is f (model class, loss, regularization) in your experiments? Any differences across datasets?
- How does performance vary with q in candidate selection? Any failure cases when q is too small?
- Have you tried learned φ (e.g., linear or neural) and does the theory extend straightforwardly?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel theoretical treatment of sampled dyadic reduction under dependence and shift
- Strong practical scalability and competitive accuracy at 100K classes
- Simple, generalizable idea likely to spur further work

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on hand-crafted text features; limited domain breadth
- Limited analysis of key hyperparameters and candidate selection trade-offs
- Bounds may be loose under aggressive sampling without practical guidance

Overall score: 7 (accept)
Confidence: 3.5/5
