Summary
This paper tackles extreme multiclass classification by reducing it to a binary dyadic problem while aggressively subsampling to address both computational cost and long-tailed label distributions. The (π, κ)-DS scheme (i) rebalances the training set by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones, and (ii) for each retained example samples only κ negative classes instead of constructing all K−1 dyads. The authors provide generalization bounds for the dependent dyadic setting using fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities (Theorem 1), and extend them to the double-sampled setting with distribution shift (Theorem 2), showing ERM consistency up to factors depending on the sampling distribution. Prediction is sped up by selecting a candidate set of classes via nearest centroids in a joint example–class feature space. Experiments on LSHTC/DMOZ/Wikipedia subsets with up to 100K classes show competitive accuracy/MaF1 with markedly reduced time/memory vs OVA/M-SVM/PD-Sparse and better accuracy than tree/embedding baselines. Code is provided.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on dyadic reductions for multiclass (e.g., Joshi et al. 2015) and generalization analysis with dependent examples via fractional covers (Janson 2004; Usunier & Amini 2005) and local Rademacher complexities (Bartlett et al.). On the algorithmic side, it is related to negative sampling/sampled softmax and pairwise ranking approaches that sample a small number of impostor classes; here, the novelty is coupling that with class-rebalancing and providing bounds that explicitly account for both sampling and dependence. Compared to tree-based log-time methods (RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML) and label-embeddings (LEML/SLEEC), the approach remains flat, avoids cascading errors, and targets macro-F1 under label imbalance.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple but impactful idea: an explicit, double sampling scheme that simultaneously tames K-dependence and long-tailed class imbalance.
- Theoretical contribution: generalization bounds for dependent dyadic reductions using local fractional Rademacher complexities; extension to sampled negatives and class-reweighting with explicit α, β factors. This is a nontrivial refinement over prior dependent-data analyses.
- Practical scalability: κ controls training cost; candidate selection controls test-time cost. Strong time/memory improvements at 50K–100K classes while retaining or improving MaF1.
- Clear target metric (macro-F1) and motivation aligned with extreme classification practice. Public code.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty vs sampled-softmax/negative sampling: The aggressive κ-negative sampling is very close in spirit to widely-used techniques in large-vocabulary classifiers. While the theoretical treatment is valuable, the algorithmic novelty by itself is modest. Please position more directly wrt sampled softmax/NCE and pairwise ranking in extreme multiclass.
- Theory constants and dependence: The bounds involve α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y. With π inversely proportional to class frequencies, β can be large, risking vacuity. How do α, β scale in your setups? Any empirical estimation of these quantities and their effect on excess risk? Can importance weighting (by η_y/π_y) during ERM reduce α and improve calibration?
- Sensitivity/ablations: Results would benefit from systematic ablations on κ (training/runtime vs MaF1) and q (candidate set size at inference). How small can κ be before accuracy degrades? Is there a principled way to adapt κ per-example?
- Joint feature map φ: Using only 10 hand-crafted features may bottleneck performance on text. How does performance change if φ includes bag-of-words interactions or learned representations? Any evidence that the method’s gains persist with stronger φ?
- Baseline coverage: Missing several strong XMC baselines (for the era): DiSMEC, Parabel/Bonsai/AnnexML, fastText, and LOMTree. PD-Sparse and RecallTree are included, but a more complete comparison would strengthen significance claims.
- Dataset protocol: LSHTC2 is multi-label; converting to multiclass by replication can skew class priors and evaluation. Please clarify how this affects macro-F1, and whether stratified sampling was used. Also report results on standard XMC benchmarks if possible.
- Clarity issues: Some notation typos and formatting glitches (e.g., broken lines, undefined constants; occasionally assuming ω_k=1 after introducing fractional covers). Please provide a concise description of the dependency graph and its fractional chromatic number before and after κ-sampling, and state the exact assumptions needed for consistency.

Quality
The approach is technically sound and the analysis, while dense, appears correct in method and citations. Empirical evaluation is thorough on selected datasets with meaningful metrics and practical resource reporting. Some methodological choices (π, κ, q) need more empirical characterization, and stronger baselines would increase confidence.

Clarity
Overall readable, but the theory section would benefit from tighter exposition: define the dependency graph explicitly; clarify where local Rademacher vs global is used; and state assumptions leading to consistency. Include the full list of φ features in the main text or a succinct pointer.

Originality
The theoretical framing with local fractional Rademacher complexity for dyadic reductions and the consistency results under double sampling are the main novel contributions. The algorithmic components are pragmatic combinations of known ideas (negative sampling, class rebalancing, candidate selection).

Significance
Given the extreme-class setting, a simple, provably consistent reduction that materially reduces time/memory while improving macro-F1 is useful. With broader baselines and ablations, this could be impactful for practitioners.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a simple, scalable method.
- Nontrivial theoretical contribution tailored to the dependence and sampling in dyadic reductions.
- Strong empirical efficiency with competitive accuracy/MaF1 at 50–100K classes.
- Public code and reproducibility.

Cons against acceptance
- Algorithmic novelty is limited relative to negative sampling literature.
- Missing several competitive XMC baselines; limited ablations on key hyperparameters.
- Theory may yield loose bounds in highly imbalanced regimes (large α, β); no mitigation via importance weighting.
- Hand-engineered φ may limit generality; unclear performance with richer representations.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper proposes a bold, practical direction with supporting theory. With improved positioning vs negative sampling, added baselines/ablations, and clarified theory exposition, it would be a solid contribution.

Confidence
High on the general assessment; medium on detailed theory tightness pending supplementary proofs.
