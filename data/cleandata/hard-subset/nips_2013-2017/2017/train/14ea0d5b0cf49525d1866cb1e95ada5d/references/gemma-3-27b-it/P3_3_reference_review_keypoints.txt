- Overall assessment: Novel, practical approach with strong empirical results; recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Main contribution: “Aggressive double sampling” ((π, κ)-DS) to subsample classes and examples to make dyadic expansion scalable.  
- Problem addressed: Tackles computational cost and class imbalance in extreme multi-class classification (OVA limitations).  
- Positioning: Builds on dyadic expansion methods and contrasts with tree-based and embedding-based approaches.  
- Practicality/scalability strength: Significantly reduces runtime and memory, enabling application to 100k+ class datasets.  
- Empirical performance strength: Competitive and often superior results on large text datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia).  
- Theoretical grounding strength: New generalization bounds justify the sampling strategy, including consistency under distribution shift and sample dependence.  
- Clarity strength: Problem definition is clear and paper is generally well-written, though theory sections are dense.  
- Theoretical weakness: Theorem derivations are dense and obscure core insights; needs more intuitive explanation and linkage to practical gains (e.g., fractional Rademacher complexity).  
- Hyperparameter weakness: Performance likely sensitive to (π, κ); more analysis and practical guidance on tuning required.  
- Feature representation weakness: Feature mapping φ is described briefly; more detail needed for reproducibility and rationale.  
- Comparison weakness: Limited direct comparison to recent methods (SLEEC, LEML); should include or justify omission with experiments.  
- Originality: Novel combination—aggressive sampling makes dyadic expansion practical for extreme multi-class problems.  
- Significance: High potential impact for text categorization, recommendation, and other large-scale applications.  
- Pro acceptance points: Strong empirical/scalability results, practical approach, and theoretical support.  
- Con acceptance points: Dense theory, hyperparameter sensitivity, and sparse feature/benchmark comparisons.  
- Questions for authors: Request intuitive explanation of theoretical benefits and guidance for choosing (π, κ).  
- Questions for authors: Ask for more details on feature mapping φ and rationale for chosen features.  
- Questions for authors: Were SLEEC and LEML tried in prelim experiments; if excluded, why?  
- Questions for authors: What is the computational cost of computing class centroids and its scalability impact?  
- Final recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—simplify theory, expand hyperparameter/feature details, and address comparison questions.
