Summary
This paper tackles extreme multiclass classification via a reduction to binary classification in a dyadic space. The core idea is an aggressive double sampling scheme: (i) rebalance the training set by oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones (via class-dependent probabilities πk), and (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ adversarial classes to build dyadic pairs instead of all K−1. This substantially reduces the number of dyadic pairs and mitigates long-tailed label distributions. The authors analyze the dependent-sample nature of the dyadic construction with fractional/graph-cover arguments and introduce generalization bounds based on local fractional Rademacher complexities; they also provide bounds for the doubly sampled setting, with explicit dependence on π and κ. For prediction, they use candidate selection via nearest class centroids computed in the joint space. Experiments on LSHTC/Wikipedia subsets up to 100K classes show competitive accuracy/MaF1 versus OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree-based methods, with markedly lower memory and training time.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on (i) dyadic reductions for multiclass to binary (e.g., Joshi et al. 2015; Weston & Watkins; AdaBoost.MR; multiclass SVMs optimizing ranking surrogates), (ii) extreme classification methods (tree-based: FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree; embedding-based: SLEEC/LEML), and (iii) generalization bounds with dependent samples using fractional chromatic numbers and Rademacher complexities (Usunier & Amini; Janson; Ralaivola & Amini). The double sampling idea is closely related to negative sampling and sampled softmax used in large-output problems (not cited), and to importance/hard-negative sampling in ranking/retrieval.

Strengths
- Practical contribution: A simple, scalable reduction with strong empirical savings in time/memory and competitive performance at 50K–100K classes.
- Addresses class imbalance and computational explosion in dyadic reductions in a unified way (π, κ).
- Theoretical analysis: Tailored generalization bounds for dependent dyadic samples and for the double-sampled distribution shift; explicit dependence on class proportions (η) and sampling (π, κ).
- Sensible prediction-time candidate selection with negligible overhead.
- Reproducibility: Code and datasets are provided.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate. The double sampling (class rebalancing + sampled negatives) is conceptually straightforward and overlaps with established practices (negative sampling, sampled softmax, NCE) not discussed/cited. The theoretical component combines known tools (fractional covers, local Rademacher complexities) with limited new technical depth in the main paper; proofs are deferred.
- Clarity/notation issues: The dyadic transformation T (S) uses notation like φ(xk i), φ(x y i) that is hard to parse without a clear definition; some typos and incomplete references; the claim that local Rademacher complexities were “introduced in [22]” appears mismatched to the cited work. The link between Theorem assumptions and practice could be better explained.
- Empirical evaluation could be more thorough:
  - No ablations on π, κ, or the candidate set size q; no analysis of trade-offs or sensitivity to aggressive sampling.
  - The joint feature mapping uses only 10 hand-crafted features specific to text; it is unclear whether baselines use the same representation or standard tf-idf. Comparability may be affected.
  - Conversion from multilabel to multiclass by replication can distort distributions and evaluation; potential implications for tree-based methods and MaF1 are not discussed.
  - Missing more recent or strong extreme classifiers for the time (e.g., Parabel, DiSMEC, PPDSparse) and comparisons to sampled-softmax style approaches would strengthen claims.
- Theory-practice gap: Bounds depend on α=maxy ηy/πy and β=maxy 1/πy, which can be large under strong oversampling; guidance on setting π to balance bias/variance is limited. The learning objective does not appear to use importance weighting to correct for sampling; this could bias the estimator.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The method is sound and empirically effective; the bounds are plausible given prior machinery, but the technical novelty and in-paper proof detail are limited. Experimental protocol lacks ablations and some fairness clarifications.
- Clarity: The paper is generally readable but suffers from notation ambiguities, typos, and several incomplete or mismatched citations. More explicit algorithms/details (e.g., learning algorithm for f, features used by baselines) would help.
- Originality: Incremental. The combination of double sampling with dependent-sample generalization bounds is nice, but the core algorithm overlaps with known sampling strategies.
- Significance: Practical for extreme text classification with resource constraints; however, broader impact is constrained by domain-specific features and limited comparisons/ablations.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, scalable method with clear practical wins on large K.
- Addresses class imbalance and dyadic blow-up elegantly.
- Provides nontrivial generalization analysis under dependence and sampling shift.

Cons for acceptance
- Conceptual novelty is limited; overlap with known sampling techniques not acknowledged.
- Clarity and experimental rigor issues (missing ablations, fairness of comparisons).
- Theory-practice linkage (choice of π, importance weighting) underdeveloped.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations varying π (e.g., inverse-frequency, capped/tempered oversampling) and κ, and study their effect on α/β and test performance; include importance weighting to debias the sampled empirical risk.
- Evaluate candidate selection q vs. accuracy/prediction time; report candidate recall.
- Clarify representations used by all baselines; consider using the same joint features where applicable or justify differences.
- Compare against sampled-softmax/negative sampling baselines and more recent extreme classification methods; include hard-negative mining variants.
- Tighten notation (define xk i explicitly), fix citation mismatches, and include key proof sketches in the main paper.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The paper demonstrates a useful and well-engineered approach with promising results, but the novelty is moderate, clarity and experimental analysis need strengthening, and the connection between theory and practice should be tightened. My decision would likely flip with a more rigorous and transparent empirical study and better positioning relative to related sampling-based approaches.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
