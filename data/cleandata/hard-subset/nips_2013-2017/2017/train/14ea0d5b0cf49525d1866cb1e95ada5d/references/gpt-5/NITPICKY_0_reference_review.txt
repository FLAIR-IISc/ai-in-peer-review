Summary
The paper tackles extreme/multi-class classification by reducing the K-way problem to a binary problem over dyadic (example, class) pairs, combined with an “aggressive double sampling” scheme: (i) resample training examples per class with probabilities πk to mitigate long-tail class imbalance, and (ii) for each retained example sample κ negative classes uniformly. The resulting reduced dyadic dataset is used to learn a scorer f over a joint representation φ(x, y) (10 simple, ranking-inspired features). For prediction, the method optionally prunes candidates via nearest-centroid selection. Theoretically, the authors provide generalization bounds under dependence using fractional/local Rademacher complexities, and empirically evaluate on LSHTC text datasets, reporting favorable time/memory/accuracy trade-offs versus OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, FastXML, PfastReXML, and RecallTree.

Relation to prior work
The reduction to dyadic comparisons and binary ranking losses is classical (e.g., Weston & Watkins multi-class SVM; AdaBoost.MR; WSABIE/WARP; pairwise ranking). Handling dependent pairs via fractional Rademacher complexity traces to Usunier et al./Janson-type dependency graphs, with follow-ups on concentration for dependent variables. Negative sampling and candidate pruning are widely used in extreme classification. The paper’s novelty lies in combining class-imbalance sampling with dyadic negative sampling, and attempting to analyze the resulting dependence/shift with local fractional Rademacher tools.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important problem (extreme multi-class text classification) with a simple, scalable recipe.
- Double sampling is intuitive and often effective; the method likely improves memory/runtime materially when K is very large.
- Theoretical attempt to justify learning under dependence and class-distribution shift is timely.
- Empirical evaluation on large LSHTC datasets with both accuracy and macro-F1; code purportedly available.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/theoretical
- Several inconsistencies/typos in theorems/notation that call correctness into question:
  - Logs such as “log 2δ” appear repeatedly; presumably meant log(2/δ). As written, terms are negative or ill-defined.
  - Theorem 1 uses m in deviation terms despite the effective sample size being N = m(K−1) for dyadic pairs; justification for m vs N is unclear.
  - Definition of fractional Rademacher complexity uses ωk but then “assume all ωk=1,” which contradicts exact fractional cover constraints. The dependency graph/cover used is never specified.
  - The local Rademacher complexity reference [22] is not the standard LRC source (Bartlett et al., 2005); the manuscript conflates entropy-based concentration for dependent variables with LRC. Key references missing.
  - The function classes F/H, loss, and surrogate used for training are not clearly specified; yet bounds are stated for 0/1 loss.
  - α, β in Theorem 2 depend on π; with πk ∝ 1/µk (not normalized), α, β are ill-defined. If πk are probabilities, oversampling (<1) cannot produce more than the available examples, contradicting “oversampling.”
  - Mixed dependence on M, m, κ and K across different terms (e.g., both √(log/ (Mκ)) and √(log/(m−1))) is not well motivated.
- Without the proofs in the main text and given these issues (and multiple constant factors like 5, 25/48 that look carried over), it is difficult to trust the bounds.

Methodology/algorithmic clarity
- φ(x, y) is only sketched; Table 1 is referenced but not present. Feature 9 “distance to centroid” is vague (which metric? normalization?). It is used both as a feature and for candidate selection; details matter.
- Training objective and optimizer are unspecified: hinge/logistic? regularization? linear vs non-linear f? The repository is cited but not discussed; this undermines reproducibility.
- Candidate selection: q is not reported, nor is an ablation showing its effect on accuracy/time. Using centroids in the dyadic space is non-standard; how are class centroids computed when φ depends on y?
- Negative sampling is uniform; no exploration of harder adversarial negatives or non-uniform sampling that often help in extreme classification.

Experimental design and fairness
- LSHTC2 is multi-label; converting to multi-class by duplicating instances is problematic. This can induce train/test leakage and distorts distributions; how are multi-label test instances handled? No analysis of the impact of duplication on macro-F1.
- Baselines: some are multi-label tree methods (FastXML, PfastReXML) applied in a multi-class setting; fairness is questionable. Important contemporaneous baselines for extreme multi-class (e.g., AnnexML, DiSMEC) are not included; SLEEC/LEML are dismissed via citation rather than empirical verification.
- Feature mismatch: baselines likely operate on high-dimensional BoW features, while the proposed method uses 10-dimensional dyadic features. This raises fairness concerns and makes accuracy comparisons hard to interpret.
- Missing ablations/sensitivity: no study of κ, π, q, or the effect of double sampling vs single sampling. No comparison to prior dyadic-reduction methods [1, 16, 28].
- Reporting: Table 3 and Fig. 3 are referenced but not shown; hyperparameters are relegated to Appendix B (not provided). Without exact settings, replicability is low.

Clarity/formatting/anonymity
- Numerous typos/formatting issues (e.g., “applicationdependent,” “Even-though,” stray “st Conference…” line, inconsistent use of . =, subscripts/superscripts missing spaces).
- Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages), and key related work (e.g., Bartlett & Mendelson on LRC) is missing.
- The GitHub link contains author identifiers, violating double-blind policy—a serious issue for NeurIPS review.

Pros
- Simple, scalable idea with practical appeal.
- Attempts to provide theory under dependence.
- Demonstrates strong time/memory reductions on large datasets.

Cons
- Significant theoretical/notation errors and missing details cast doubt on correctness.
- Algorithmic details and training setup insufficiently specified; limited reproducibility.
- Experimental fairness and completeness concerns; missing ablations; unclear handling of multi-label-to-multi-class conversion.
- Formatting quality and anonymization violation.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely define f, the loss, and optimizer used. Provide the surrogate, regularization, and all hyperparameters in the main paper.
- Clarify π: are you oversampling with replacement? If so, how is this reflected in the probability model and bounds?
- Fix Theorems 1–2: correct logs, specify the dependency graph and fractional cover, and justify sample size terms. Cite standard LRC sources and clearly separate tools used.
- Provide ablations over κ, q, and π; compare uniform vs hard negative sampling; include a comparison to a non-sampled dyadic baseline.
- Justify the conversion from multi-label to multi-class; ensure no leakage; report statistics and impact on metrics.
- Ensure double-blind compliance; remove identifying links; include full references.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is promising and practically relevant, the accumulation of technical inaccuracies, missing details, fairness concerns in experiments, and violation of anonymity outweigh the contributions. With substantial revisions to theory, clarity, and experimental rigor, this could become a solid submission. Confidence: high.
