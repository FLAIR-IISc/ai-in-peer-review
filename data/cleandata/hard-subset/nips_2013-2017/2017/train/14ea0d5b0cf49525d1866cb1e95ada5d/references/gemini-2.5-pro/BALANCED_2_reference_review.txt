### Paper Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of extreme multi-class classification (XMC), where the number of classes `K` is very large. The authors build upon the multi-class to binary reduction framework, which transforms a K-class problem on `m` examples into a binary classification problem on `m(K-1)` dyadic pairs. The primary contribution is a novel and aggressive "double sampling" strategy, termed (π, κ)-DS, designed to overcome the prohibitive computational cost of this reduction. The strategy consists of two stages: 1) A class-aware sampling of the original training data to create a smaller, more balanced dataset `S_π`. This is achieved by oversampling rare classes and undersampling frequent ones, addressing the common long-tailed distribution issue. 2) For each example in `S_π`, instead of generating pairs with all `K-1` other classes, the method samples only a small, fixed number `κ` of negative classes. This drastically reduces the size of the final binary training set.

The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for this approach, deriving generalization error bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity. This analysis demonstrates that empirical risk minimization on the doubly-sampled data remains a consistent learning principle, despite the induced distribution shift and dependencies between the generated pairs. Empirically, the method is evaluated on large-scale text classification benchmarks with up to 100,000 classes. The results show that (π, κ)-DS is highly competitive with state-of-the-art methods, achieving strong predictive performance (accuracy and Macro F1) while being significantly more efficient in terms of training time and memory usage than other high-performance baselines like OVA or PD-Sparse.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Practicality:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and challenging problem in machine learning. The proposed (π, κ)-DS method is simple, intuitive, and offers a compelling practical solution to the scalability issues inherent in many XMC approaches. The demonstrated ability to achieve state-of-the-art performance with a fraction of the computational resources makes this work highly valuable for practitioners.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** While sampling techniques are not new, the proposed two-stage sampling strategy is a novel and effective combination of ideas tailored to the dyadic reduction framework. Simultaneously addressing class imbalance (π-sampling) and the combinatorial explosion of negative pairs (κ-sampling) is a clever approach that directly targets the two main bottlenecks of the underlying reduction method.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The authors provide a non-trivial theoretical analysis to justify their sampling scheme. Proving generalization bounds (Theorem 2) for a procedure that combines importance sampling with the generation of dependent data points adds significant rigor to the paper. This theoretical backing elevates the work from a purely heuristic-based approach to a principled one.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive and well-executed. The method is benchmarked against a strong and diverse set of baselines, including classic methods (OVA), tree-based XMC methods (FastXML, PfastReXML), and other recent state-of-the-art solvers (PD-Sparse). The use of large, standard datasets up to 100K classes convincingly demonstrates the scalability and effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reporting of multiple metrics (accuracy, MaF1, time, memory) provides a complete picture of the method's advantages.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Details of the Feature Mapping (`φ`):** The performance of the method hinges on the joint example/class representation `φ`. The paper describes a 10-feature mapping for text classification inspired by learning-to-rank. However, the motivation behind the choice of these specific features could be elaborated upon. More importantly, the paper claims that a similar mapping could be learned or defined for other domains like image classification, but provides little guidance on how this would be done. A more general discussion or concrete examples for another domain would significantly broaden the paper's applicability and impact.

2.  **Ablation Study of the Sampling Strategy:** The (π, κ)-DS strategy has two key components. An ablation study would be very valuable to disentangle their individual contributions. For instance:
    *   What is the performance with only κ-sampling (i.e., uniform sampling of training instances, without class balancing)? This would isolate the benefit of the π-sampling stage.
    *   How sensitive is the method to the choice of κ? The experiments seem to use a fixed value. A plot showing the trade-off between performance and training time as κ varies would be highly informative for practitioners seeking to apply this method.

3.  **Clarification on Prediction-Time Candidate Selection:** The paper proposes a candidate selection step at prediction time to maintain efficiency, selecting `q` nearest-centroid classes. This is a crucial practical detail that could be expanded upon. How are these class centroids computed (e.g., on the original dataset or the balanced set `S_π`)? A sensitivity analysis on the number of candidates `q` would be helpful to illustrate the accuracy/speed trade-off at inference time.

4.  **Connecting Theory to Practice:** The theoretical bounds in Theorem 2 contain terms like `α` and `β` that depend on the sampling probabilities `π_y`. It would be beneficial to include a short, intuitive discussion on how the practical choice of the sampling strategy (e.g., how aggressively one oversamples rare classes) influences these theoretical bounds and what this implies for the learning process. This would bridge the gap between the theoretical and practical sections of the paper more effectively.

---

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical problem (XMC).
*   Proposes a novel, simple, and highly effective sampling method.
*   Provides strong empirical results on large-scale benchmarks, demonstrating a superior trade-off between accuracy, training time, and memory usage.
*   Includes non-trivial theoretical analysis that provides a principled justification for the method.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The generality of the crucial feature mapping `φ` to domains beyond text is not well-established.
*   The paper would benefit from ablation studies to better understand the contribution of each component of the sampling strategy.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

This is a strong paper that makes both a practical and a theoretical contribution to the important field of extreme multi-class classification. The proposed method is elegant, effective, and thoroughly validated. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to areas for further clarification and future work rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed approach. This work is a solid contribution to the field and is likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners.
