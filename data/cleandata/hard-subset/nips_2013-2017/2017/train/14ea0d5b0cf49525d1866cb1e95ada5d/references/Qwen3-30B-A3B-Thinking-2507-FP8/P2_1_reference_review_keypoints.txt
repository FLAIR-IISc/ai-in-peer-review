- Proposes (π, κ)-DS: an aggressive double sampling strategy that balances class representation and samples κ classes per example to reduce dyadic examples.  
- Targets extreme multi-class classification with very large label sets (e.g., 50K–100K classes).  
- Addresses class imbalance (long-tailed distributions) and computational/memory complexity of standard approaches.  
- Provides theoretical generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity to justify consistency.  
- Empirically validated on Wikipedia and DMOZ datasets showing improved memory, speed, and predictive performance versus several baselines.  
- Experimental setup is thorough, with clear metrics (accuracy, macro F1) and resource reporting.  
- Theoretical analysis is technically sound with formal proofs (Theorems 1 and 2) building on prior work.  
- Theoretical presentation is dense and uses abstract concepts (exact proper fractional covers) that lack sufficient motivation for general readers.  
- Method sensitivity to hyperparameters π and κ is not well explored; guidance for selecting them is unclear.  
- Comparison set omits recent methods (SLEEC, LEML) without strong justification, potentially limiting empirical claims.  
- Connection between theoretical bounds and observed empirical gains could be explained more clearly.  
- Some figures and intuitive explanations (e.g., dyadic transformation) are missing or insufficiently linked to the text.  
- The double-sampling idea is practically useful but not fundamentally novel; novelty lies in the specific combination and analysis.  
- Theoretical contribution is incremental—extends existing fractional Rademacher complexity work rather than introducing a new framework.  
- Significant practical relevance for large-scale/text classification and long-tailed label distributions.  
- Limited scope: experiments focus only on text classification and lack domain-general validation.  
- Strengths: practical, simple-to-implement method with solid empirical results and meaningful theoretical justification.  
- Weaknesses: incremental theory, limited experimental diversity, unclear hyperparameter sensitivity, and some clarity issues.  
- Recommendation: accept — paper is a solid, practically valuable contribution despite some limitations.  
- Suggested improvements: expand hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, provide more intuitive theoretical explanations, discuss applicability beyond text, and include brief comparisons to excluded methods (SLEEC, LEML).
