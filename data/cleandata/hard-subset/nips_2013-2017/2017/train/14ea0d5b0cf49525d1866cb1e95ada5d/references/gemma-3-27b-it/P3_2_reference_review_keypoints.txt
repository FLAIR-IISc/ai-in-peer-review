- Overall assessment: Strong accept — novel, well-motivated approach with promising empirical and theoretical results, pending minor revisions for clarity.

- Summary & related work: Addresses limitations of OVA in extreme multi-class settings via dyadic representation and introduces "aggressive double sampling" (class re-weighting π and dyadic reduction κ) as scalable alternative.

- Novelty: Aggressive sampling combining class re-weighting and dyadic example reduction is a novel, clever way to cut computational cost while retaining performance.

- Theoretical foundation: Provides rigorous generalization bounds (Theorems 1–2) linking to local Rademacher complexity and graph covering theory.

- Empirical validation: Strong results on DMOZ and large Wikipedia (100K classes) datasets showing competitive or superior accuracy, macro F1, runtime, and memory.

- Practical relevance: Method offers meaningful scalability improvements for real-world extreme classification problems.

- Writing quality: Generally clear and well-structured presentation; algorithm exposition is concise.

- Theoretical complexity weakness: Generalization bound derivations are dense and hard to access; need simpler explanations and intuition (e.g., role of parameter r).

- Parameter sensitivity weakness: Performance likely depends on π and κ; paper needs a detailed sensitivity analysis.

- Feature mapping weakness: φ used for experiments seems ad-hoc and domain-specific; unclear generalization to other domains.

- Comparison weakness: Lacks thorough comparison to very recent deep learning–based extreme classification methods.

- Theorem 2 question: Robustness of bounds to changes/noise in class proportion parameters α and β is unclear.

- Candidate selection question: Impact and tuning of candidate count q (nearest centroids) on performance needs clarification.

- Memory usage question: More detailed breakdown of memory components (feature mapping, sampled set, etc.) would clarify footprint.

- Figure comment: Fig. 3 effectively highlights performance differences.

- Pro arguments for acceptance: Novel, well-motivated, strong theory, compelling large-scale empirical results, and clear presentation.

- Con arguments against acceptance: Complex theory may limit accessibility, hyperparameter sensitivity and domain-specific feature mapping need further study, and comparisons to recent deep-learning baselines are limited.

- Final recommendation: Strong accept with minor revisions.

- Required revisions suggested: Simplify/explain generalization bounds intuitively; add hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for π and κ; expand discussion on feature mapping limitations and extensions; briefly relate work to recent deep learning approaches.
