Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem on dyadic (example, class) pairs, while aggressively subsampling to address computational cost and class imbalance. The method has two stages: (i) reweight the training set via classwise sampling probabilities π (oversample rare classes and subsample frequent ones) to mitigate long-tailed distributions; (ii) for each retained example, sample κ “adversarial” negative classes, forming a smaller dyadic set. Training minimizes a pairwise 0/1 loss with a function of the form g(x, y) = f(φ(x, y)), where φ maps example/class pairs into a joint space. For prediction, a candidate set of classes is selected via nearest centroids, and the class with highest score is chosen.

Theoretically, the paper derives generalization bounds for: (a) the standard dyadic reduction with dependent pairs using fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities, and (b) the proposed double-sampled reduction, accounting for distribution shift from π and dependence among dyadic pairs. The bounds involve fractional Rademacher complexities on dependent data, variance-localized classes, and parameters α, β capturing the sampling distortion, implying consistency of ERM under the proposed reduction.

Empirically, the method is evaluated on large text datasets (LSHTC/Wikipedia, up to 100K classes), using a 10-dimensional, text-specific φ inspired by learning-to-rank, and compared against OVA, multiclass SVM, tree/embedding baselines (RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML), and PD-Sparse. The method shows favorable trade-offs among accuracy/MaF1, runtime, and memory, especially at large K. Code is provided.

This work relates to: OVA baselines; logarithmic-time/tree and embedding methods for extreme classification; dyadic reductions and analysis for interdependent data; fractional chromatic number-based generalization bounds; and local Rademacher complexity. The paper extends prior dyadic-reduction analyses by combining fractional covers with variance-localized bounds and by treating class-imbalance-induced distribution shift due to sampling.

Strengths
- Addresses two practical bottlenecks in dyadic reduction for extreme multiclass: dataset size and severe class imbalance. The (π, κ) scheme is simple and effective.
- Clear theoretical contribution: bounds for dependent dyadic data with variance localization, and explicit treatment of distribution shift due to sampling (α, β), yielding consistency guarantees.
- Scales to 100K classes with competitive or superior time/memory compared to strong baselines; open-source implementation.
- Sensible prediction-time candidate selection that reduces inference cost.

Weaknesses
- Experimental scope: all results are on text; φ is domain-specific, hand-crafted, and only briefly described (Table 1 is referenced). Generality beyond text is asserted but not validated.
- Comparisons: several baselines are primarily designed for multilabel settings and/or trees; no results against dyadic reductions without sampling or against strong multiclass logarithmic-time methods on these datasets. The conversion from multilabel to multiclass by duplicating instances may disadvantage some baselines; this deserves discussion.
- Limited ablations: no systematic sensitivity study of π, κ, or candidate set size q; no analysis of negative sampling strategies beyond uniform; no study of the effect of candidate selection on recall/accuracy.
- Theory–practice gap: the prediction-time candidate selection is not accounted for in the generalization analysis (risk of excluding the true class), and constants α, β can be large when π is small; practical guidance on tuning π to control these terms is limited.
- Some notation and presentation issues (typos, dense derivations deferred to supplement) may hinder reproducibility of the theory; the feature mapping φ is not fully specified in the main text.

Quality
- The theoretical analysis is sound in approach: it extends known techniques for dependent samples via fractional covers and local Rademacher complexities. The dependence on κ, M, K, and sampling distortion parameters is explicit. While full proofs are in the supplement, the results are plausible and relevant.
- The empirical evaluation demonstrates scalability and competitive accuracy at large K. However, the fairness of comparisons (multilabel-to-multiclass conversion, domain-specific φ, absence of dyadic baselines without sampling) and lack of ablations weaken the empirical evidence.
- Code availability is a plus; hyperparameter details are reportedly in the appendix.

Clarity
- The high-level ideas and algorithms are understandable; pseudocode is helpful. Some notation is heavy, and the main text omits important details (e.g., full φ definition). Minor grammatical and typographical issues are present.

Originality
- The double sampling strategy in the dyadic reduction, together with a local fractional Rademacher complexity analysis that explicitly handles sampling-induced distribution shift, appears novel. Candidate selection at prediction and class balancing are known ideas, but the combined reduction and analysis in this setting is a meaningful contribution.

Significance
- For extreme multiclass text classification, the method offers a practical and theoretically grounded alternative that reduces training and inference cost while maintaining accuracy. Broader impact depends on demonstrating generality across domains and feature mappings or integrating learned φ.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide ablations: effect of κ, choices of π (e.g., inverse-frequency vs. tempered), and candidate set size q on accuracy/time/memory.
- Report a dyadic baseline without sampling and/or with only one of the two sampling stages to isolate contributions.
- Analyze candidate selection: probability the true class is excluded; trade-offs between q and accuracy; perhaps a bound under centroid-based filtering.
- Clarify φ in the main text; report the time to compute φ and centroids; consider learned φ variants.
- Discuss fairness of comparisons on multilabel-to-multiclass conversion; justify baseline choices; if possible, include strong multiclass log-time baselines on these datasets.
- Comment on the magnitude of α, β in practice and how to set π to control them.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, relevant theory for dependent dyadic reductions with sampling shift.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with strong empirical performance at large K.
- Open-source implementation; applicability to long-tailed distributions.

Cons against acceptance
- Experiments limited to a single domain with domain-specific features; limited ablations and potentially uneven baseline comparisons.
- Prediction-time candidate selection not covered theoretically; potential loss unquantified.
- Some clarity/reproducibility issues in the description of φ and proofs in main text.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying φ and adding ablations that quantify the impact of π, κ, and candidate selection on performance and guarantees.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
