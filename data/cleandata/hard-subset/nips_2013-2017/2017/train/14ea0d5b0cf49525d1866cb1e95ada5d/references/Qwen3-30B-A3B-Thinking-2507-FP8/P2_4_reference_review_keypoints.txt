- Proposes "aggressive double sampling": oversample small classes, subsample large ones, and sample dyadic pairs to reduce multi-class-to-binary complexity.  
- Provides theoretical generalization guarantees using local fractional Rademacher complexity with two main theorems and proofs in the supplement.  
- Demonstrates substantial computational efficiency gains while maintaining or improving predictive performance on large text classification datasets.  
- Empirically scales to 100K classes where competing methods fail due to memory, comparing against strong baselines (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree, PD-Sparse).  
- Technically sound and well-justified; empirical results comprehensively support claims.  
- Experimental evaluation includes relevant metrics (train time, prediction time, memory, accuracy, macro F1) and clear visualizations.  
- Paper is well-written and organized, but theoretical sections could use more intuitive explanations for broader accessibility.  
- Lacks practical guidance on choosing hyperparameters (π and κ) and does not analyze sensitivity to these choices.  
- Related work coverage is adequate but could better differentiate this sampling approach from recent sampling-based extreme classification methods.  
- High significance: addresses a real-world scalability bottleneck and achieves a strong runtime-memory-performance tradeoff at extreme scales.  
- Strengths: tackles an important problem, has theoretical grounding, shows practical advantages, and presents thorough experiments.  
- Weaknesses: weaker connection between theory and practice, incomplete comparison to the latest methods, and insufficient hyperparameter tuning details.  
- Recommendation: Accept — valuable, novel, and practically useful contribution despite minor shortcomings.
