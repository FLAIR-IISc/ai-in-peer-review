The paper proposes (π, κ)-DS: class-balanced resampling plus sampling κ negative classes per example to drastically reduce dyadic training pairs.  
Uses fractional/local Rademacher complexity to derive generalization bounds for dependent dyadic data and a nearest-centroid pruning for fast prediction.  

Theory: presents plausible concentration/Rademacher-based bounds but defers key details to the supplement, making bounds hard to interpret.  
Theory: several terms/constants look suspicious or dimensionally inconsistent (notably in Theorem 2).  
Theory: no explicit, interpretable upper bounds in terms of m, K, κ, class imbalance, or graph parameters are provided.  
Theory: impact of sampling policy π on α, β and rates is not analyzed theoretically; only empirical guidance is given.  
Theory: citation mismatch for fractional Rademacher complexity undermines literature grounding.  

Empirics: experiments on large LSHTC datasets (up to 100K classes) show strong scaling, lower resource use, and competitive macro-F1 versus strong baselines.  
Empirics: missing ablations for π and κ to quantify accuracy/runtime trade-offs and validate balancing claims.  
Empirics: candidate selection parameter q is not analyzed for accuracy vs latency trade-offs.  
Empirics: dyadic feature mapping φ is underspecified in the main text, obscuring whether gains come from sampling or features.  
Empirics: converting multi-label to multi-class by duplicating instances may distort metrics (macro-F1) and is not discussed.  
Empirics: no statistical significance tests reported.  

Clarity: notation and dyadic-transform presentation are confusing (index reuse, inconsistent z/φ definitions) and some equations contain formatting/dimensional errors.  
Clarity: references contain mismatches and key attributions are unclear; reproducibility hindered by missing φ and candidate-selection details.  

Originality: algorithmic ideas (balanced subsampling + negative sampling + centroid pruning) are incremental relative to prior extreme-classification/ranking work.  
Originality: theoretical contribution—analysis of double-sampled dependent dyadic reduction with local fractional Rademacher complexity—is novel and potentially useful if clarified.  

Significance: practical method is simple, scalable, and effective for very large K; likely useful to practitioners.  
Significance: theoretical angle could stimulate further work, but impact limited by lack of clear, interpretable rates and clarity.  

Pros: addresses dyadic reduction bottleneck with an effective sampling scheme; provides dependence-aware generalization analysis; strong scaling on 100K classes; open-sourced code.  

Cons: unclear notation and theorem statements; limited theoretical interpretability; missing ablations (π, κ, q) and underspecified φ; algorithmic novelty is modest.  

Requests for authors: provide explicit upper bounds for RT(` ◦ Hr) under the dependency graph showing scaling with m, K, κ, and imbalance.  
Requests: correct and clarify the reference for fractional Rademacher complexity and fix typos in Theorem 2.  
Requests: detail how π is set in practice (normalization/capping) and analyze how α, β behave under that policy.  
Requests: add ablations/sensitivity analyses for κ, q, and π showing accuracy/runtime trade-offs.  
Requests: fully describe φ in the main text/appendix and clarify centroid computation given dyadic features.  
Requests: discuss implications of converting multi-label to multi-class via duplication on evaluation (e.g., macro-F1).  

Overall: practical, well-scaled contribution with promising but underdeveloped theory; weak accept (6) conditioned on clarification and improved rigor.
