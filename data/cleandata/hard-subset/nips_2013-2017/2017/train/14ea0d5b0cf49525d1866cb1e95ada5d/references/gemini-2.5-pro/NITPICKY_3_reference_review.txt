### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of extreme multi-class classification by proposing a multi-class to binary reduction strategy coupled with a novel sampling scheme. The core idea is a "doubly-sampled" approach. First, it re-balances the training data by over-sampling rare classes and under-sampling frequent ones (π-sampling). Second, for each example in the re-balanced set, instead of creating negative pairs with all K-1 other classes, it samples only a small number, κ, of negative classes (κ-sampling). This significantly reduces the size of the resulting binary classification problem. The authors provide a theoretical analysis with generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity to show that learning on this doubly-sampled, dependent dataset is consistent. Experimentally, the method is evaluated on large-scale text classification datasets (up to 100K classes), where it demonstrates a compelling trade-off between predictive performance (Accuracy and Macro F1), training/prediction time, and memory usage compared to state-of-the-art methods like tree-based classifiers and one-vs-all SVMs.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and challenging problem. The proposed (π, κ)-double sampling strategy is an intuitive and practical solution to the two main bottlenecks of dyadic reduction methods: the class imbalance problem and the computational explosion from creating m(K-1) pairs.
2.  **Theoretical Support:** The authors provide non-trivial generalization bounds (Theorems 1 and 2) for their proposed learning setting. This is commendable, as it formally accounts for the distribution shift introduced by π-sampling and the inherent dependencies in the dyadic data, lending theoretical credence to the empirical risk minimization approach.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are impressive. The method achieves state-of-the-art or competitive predictive performance on very large datasets while being significantly more efficient in terms of time and memory than other high-performing methods like OVA, M-SVM, and PD-Sparse. It presents a very attractive practical solution for extreme classification.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising core contribution, the paper is marred by a significant number of minor flaws in its presentation, methodological details, and experimental analysis, which collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

**1. Lack of Clarity and Notational Inconsistencies:**

*   **Equation (3):** The notation for the dyadic transformation is confusing. The expression `zj = ( φ(xki ), φ(x yi i ) )` is ambiguous. Does `xki` refer to a new example `x_k` with index `i`, or the original example `x_i` paired with a class `k`? The latter seems intended, in which case the notation should be `φ(x_i, k)`. The condition `if k < y` is also opaque, as `k` is a class label from a set, not necessarily an ordered integer. This critical equation needs to be defined with much greater care.
*   **Indexing and Typos:** The paper contains numerous minor notational errors and typos, such as `(xyii )mi=1` instead of `(x_i, y_i)_{i=1}^m`, inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., `1 K − 1`), and repeated footnote numbering. The statement "If other is not specified explicitly we assume below all ωk = 1" in Section 2.1 is abrupt and lacks justification.
*   **(π, κ)-DS Algorithm:** The description "Draw randomly a set Sπk of examples of class k from S with probability πk" is imprecise. It should be clarified whether this means each example is included independently with probability πk, or if a fixed-size subset is drawn.

**2. Opaque Methodological Details:**

*   **Multi-label to Multi-class Conversion:** The authors state they convert multi-label datasets to multi-class "by replicating the instances". This is a major data transformation that fundamentally alters the problem and class distributions. Instances with multiple labels are duplicated, artificially inflating the size of certain classes and the dataset itself. The potential impact of this on the results and the validity of the comparison is not discussed at all.
*   **Feature Engineering:** The features in Table 1 are heuristic. Feature 9, the distance to a class centroid, is used both as a feature for the classifier and as the basis for the candidate selection at prediction time. This seems circular. Furthermore, it is not specified how these centroids are calculated (e.g., in the original feature space, using which vector representation) and whether they are computed on the original dataset `S` or the re-sampled set `Sπ`.
*   **Theoretical Implications:** The constants `α` and `β` in Theorem 2 are critical to the bound, yet their behavior is not discussed. Given `πk ∝ 1/ηk`, `α = max_k ηk/πk` will depend on the constant of proportionality and the distribution of class sizes. A discussion on how these terms affect the bound, especially in the context of long-tailed distributions, is missing.

**3. Insufficient Experimental Analysis:**

*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The main contribution is the (π, κ)-DS sampling scheme. However, there is no analysis of its components. How does the method perform with only κ-sampling (i.e., uniform π)? How does performance vary with different choices of κ? The paper presents results for a single, presumably optimal, setting of hyperparameters without providing any insight into their sensitivity, which is crucial for understanding the method's behavior and for practitioners seeking to apply it.
*   **Weak Justification for Baseline Exclusion:** The exclusion of recent methods like SLEEC and LEML is justified by citing a single paper [30]. This is insufficient, as their performance relative to the baselines could be different on the specific datasets used here.
*   **Anonymity Violation:** The paper includes a footnote with a GitHub link to the source code, which violates the double-blind reviewing policy. The footnote with conference information on the first page should also have been removed.

### Recommendation: Reject

While the paper presents a novel and highly effective method for a significant problem, the execution is not up to the standards of NIPS. The large number of minor flaws in clarity, notation, and methodological description, combined with more substantial omissions in the experimental analysis (ablation studies, impact of data conversion), prevent a thorough and confident assessment of the work. The core idea is strong, but the paper needs a major revision to clarify the technical details, improve the presentation, and provide a more rigorous and insightful empirical evaluation.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Significant lack of clarity in core methodological descriptions (Eq. 3, sampling procedure).
*   Major data preprocessing step (multi-label to multi-class conversion) is not justified or analyzed.
*   Absence of crucial ablation studies to understand the contribution of the different components of the proposed method (π and κ sampling).
*   Insufficient analysis of key hyperparameters.
*   Numerous minor presentation and notational errors that indicate a lack of careful preparation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important problem in machine learning.
*   The proposed sampling method is novel, intuitive, and highly effective in practice.
*   The work is supported by non-trivial theoretical analysis.
*   Demonstrates a superior trade-off between performance and efficiency compared to many strong baselines on large-scale tasks.
