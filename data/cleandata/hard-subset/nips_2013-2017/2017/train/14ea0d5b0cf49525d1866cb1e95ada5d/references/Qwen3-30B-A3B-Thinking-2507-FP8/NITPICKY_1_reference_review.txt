

# Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper proposes a "doubly-sampled multi-class to binary reduction" approach for extreme multi-class classification. The authors introduce a (π, κ)-double sampling strategy that oversamples small classes and subsamples large classes to address long-tailed class distributions, followed by a dyadic transformation to reduce the problem to binary classification. The theoretical contribution claims new generalization bounds using "local fractional Rademacher complexity," while experiments on Wikipedia and DMOZ datasets demonstrate improved scalability.

**Strengths:** The problem addressed is relevant to large-scale classification, and the paper's double sampling approach is conceptually interesting. The experimental results on Wikipedia datasets with up to 100,000 classes are promising for practical applications. The paper properly situates itself within the literature on multi-class reduction techniques.

**Critical Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Errors:** The paper contains significant theoretical inconsistencies. Theorem 2 defines α = max_y η_y/π_y but fails to define η_y (the proportion of class y in S) before this usage. The paper claims to derive "new generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity" but the analysis relies on standard fractional Rademacher complexity from [25], not "local" complexity as claimed. The bound in Theorem 1 includes ambiguous notation "√ log 1δ m" which could mean √(log(1/δ)/m) or (√log(1/δ))/m - the formatting is unclear and mathematically incorrect.

2. **Methodological Flaws:** The paper's approach to converting multi-label to multi-class (by "replicating instances belonging to different class labels") is fundamentally flawed and not standard practice. This would artificially inflate the dataset size and create a biased training set. The claim that "OVA and M-SVM memory consumption exceeds the Terabyte" for 100K classes is exaggerated - for a typical document classification problem with 10,000 features, 100K OVA classifiers would require ~4GB, not TB.

3. **Experimental Issues:** The paper claims to use "WIKI-50K and WIKI-100K" datasets but LSHTC2's Wikipedia dataset has 1,000,000 classes, so these are just subsets. The paper should clarify this. The experimental section lacks proper statistical validation of the claimed "highly competitive" results, and Table 3 (which would show these results) is absent from the provided text.

4. **Formatting and Presentation:** The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies: inconsistent mathematical notation (K vs. |Y|), ambiguous expressions (like "√ log 1δ m"), and missing figures (Figure 1, Figure 3). The paper cites "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" which should be "The Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)".

5. **Theoretical Overclaiming:** The paper states "we derive new generalization bounds" but the bounds in Theorem 1 are similar to those in [16] and [25], with no substantial novelty. The claim that "the empirical risk minimization principle over the transformation of the sampled training set remains consistent" lacks proper theoretical justification.

**Summary of Key Concerns:**

- Theoretical section contains critical definition errors (η_y not defined) and misrepresentation of the complexity analysis
- Methodology contains a fundamental flaw in dataset conversion from multi-label to multi-class
- Experimental claims are unsubstantiated due to missing results tables and lack of statistical validation
- Numerous formatting inconsistencies and ambiguous mathematical expressions
- Overstated theoretical contributions compared to existing literature

**Recommendation:**

While the paper addresses a relevant problem and contains some interesting ideas, the numerous technical errors, methodological flaws, and unsubstantiated claims significantly undermine its contribution. The theoretical claims are not properly supported, the experimental approach contains a fundamental flaw, and the presentation is riddled with formatting inconsistencies. The paper does not meet the technical standards expected at NIPS.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial methodological and theoretical flaws that prevent the claims from being properly evaluated. The paper would benefit from significant revision to address these issues before being considered for publication.
