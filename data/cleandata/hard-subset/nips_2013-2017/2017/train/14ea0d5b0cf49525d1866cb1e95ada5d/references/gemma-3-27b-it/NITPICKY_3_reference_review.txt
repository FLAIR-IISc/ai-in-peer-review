This paper addresses the problem of large-scale multi-class classification, specifically focusing on mitigating the computational and class imbalance issues inherent in the one-vs-all (OVA) approach. The authors propose an “aggressive double sampling” strategy to reduce the size of the training set and the number of dyadic examples constructed, building upon previous work utilizing dyadic representations. They also provide generalization bounds for their approach, accounting for the induced distribution shift and inter-dependency between pairs.

While the problem addressed is important and the core idea of aggressive sampling is intuitively appealing, several aspects of the paper raise significant concerns regarding its technical rigor, clarity, and overall contribution justifying acceptance to NIPS.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Large-scale multi-class classification is a crucial problem with numerous applications.
*   **Intuitive Approach:** The concept of aggressive sampling to address both computational cost and class imbalance is sensible.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The attempt to provide generalization bounds is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Novelty:** The paper builds incrementally on existing techniques (dyadic representations, sampling). The presented aggressive sampling feels like a heuristic optimization rather than a fundamentally new theoretical contribution. The “double sampling” is simply a combination of class-balancing sampling (π) and example reduction (κ) which are established techniques.
*   **Theoretical Gaps & Imprecision:** Theorem 1 and 2 are stated without sufficient clarity regarding the conditions under which they hold. For example, the meaning of 'Hr' isn't immediately clear. The relationship between the fractional Rademacher complexity and the practical implications is not thoroughly discussed. The dependence of the bounds on parameters like α and β, while defined, isn’t clearly explained in terms of the original class distribution. The claim about faster convergence rates is made but lacks justification. The proofs are relegated to supplementary material and hence are not immediately verifiable.
*   **Experimental Evaluation – Insufficient Details and Comparisons:** The experimental section suffers from significant shortcomings. The description of the feature mapping (φ) is insufficient. Table 1 refers to features inspired from learning to rank but the specifics are not detailed. The hyperparameter tuning process is mentioned but the actual hyperparameters used aren’t listed in the main text (only referenced to the supplementary material), hindering reproducibility. Critically, the comparisons against baselines are not thorough enough. The authors acknowledge that OVA and M-SVM become intractable, yet they include them in the table only to dismiss them. It’s unclear *why* PD-Sparse is limited by intermediary weight vectors - a more detailed explanation would be useful. The claim of "best compromise" requires stronger justification. The impact of κ is not rigorously studied – how sensitive are the results to its choice?
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The writing is often dense and convoluted. The introduction is verbose and lacks a concise statement of the paper’s contribution. The notation is not always consistent. Several sentences are awkwardly worded. Equation (6) is simply a restatement of (2) using the new notation, providing minimal insight. Definition 1 feels out of place and overly detailed for the main text.
*   **Minor Issues:** The reference to "[26, Th. 2.1, p. 38]" is an unusual citation style. The link in the reference "[14]" is broken.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:** Addresses a relevant problem and presents a potentially useful heuristic optimization for existing methods. Attempts theoretical analysis.

**Con:** Incremental contribution, weak theoretical justification, insufficient experimental detail, unclear writing, and a lack of compelling evidence that the proposed approach significantly advances the state-of-the-art. The paper feels more like a technical report than a substantial contribution to NIPS.

**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be below the standard for NIPS acceptance. While the problem is important and the idea has some merit, the technical weaknesses, lack of clarity, and insufficient experimental validation outweigh the strengths. The generalization bounds, while present, lack sufficient detail and justification to be truly convincing. The experimental evaluation needs significant improvement to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method and compare it fairly to existing techniques. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should consider significantly strengthening the theoretical analysis, providing a more thorough experimental evaluation with detailed hyperparameter tuning, and improving the clarity of their writing before resubmitting this work.
