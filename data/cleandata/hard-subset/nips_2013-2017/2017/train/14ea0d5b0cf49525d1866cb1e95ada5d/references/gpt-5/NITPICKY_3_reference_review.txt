Summary
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing the problem to binary via dyadic example–class pairs, combined with an “aggressive double sampling” scheme: (i) class-balanced subsampling/oversampling of the training set (π), followed by (ii) per-example sampling of κ “negative” classes to form a reduced dyadic dataset. The authors analyze generalization via fractional and local Rademacher complexities under interdependent dyadic examples and distribution shift induced by sampling, and propose a candidate-selection scheme at prediction time using nearest class centroids in the dyadic space. Experiments on LSHTC datasets (converted to multi-class) compare against OVA/M-SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding methods, reporting better time–memory–accuracy trade-offs.

Strengths
- Addresses a real scalability pain point for dyadic reductions in extreme classification with a simple, implementable sampling scheme.
- Attempts to provide theory for dependent dyadic examples and sampling-induced covariate/label shift, using fractional Rademacher complexity and variance-sensitive (local) complexity tools.
- Empirical evaluation on large text datasets with memory/runtime metrics is valuable; macro-F1 is appropriate under imbalance.
- The candidate shortlisting idea for prediction is practical and often essential in extreme settings.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical and theoretical
- Theorems and notation are hard to audit. Key quantities (fractional cover {Ck, ωk}, χ*, construction of dependency graph, exact role of ωk) are stated but not instantiated. No explicit cover or bound on χ* is provided for the dyadic dependency structure; the analysis hinges on these.
- Local Rademacher complexity is invoked but not properly referenced or specified (standard references include Bartlett & Mendelson 2005; Koltchinskii 2006). The link to [22] (entropy-based concentration for dependent variables) is not sufficient to justify local complexity rates; please clarify the exact inequality used and assumptions (e.g., Bernstein-type).
- The bound terms in Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 contain potentially mis-typed factors (e.g., 5(√RT(…)+√r)√(log(1/δ)/m) and a “25/48” term elsewhere; in Theorem 2, a dangling “α / log 2δ M” term). Several terms mix m, M, N and K ambiguously. Please check dimensions and consistency.
- The role and behavior of α = max_y ηy/πy and β = max_y 1/πy are not discussed. With πy ∝ 1/ηy as proposed, α grows like max_y ηy^2 up to normalization, which may be large for head classes, weakening the bound; β can also be large. Provide guidance for setting π to control α,β and a data-dependent normalization to avoid blow-ups.
- The dyadic construction T(S): indices and signs are confusing (k<y vs else; z = (φ(xk_i), φ(xy_i)); ỹ = ±1). It appears z uses the same x_i with class y’ as “negative,” but notation x^k_i or (x_i, k) should be clearly defined. The asymmetry k<y is unnecessary and obfuscates the label definition.
- No computational complexity analysis is provided; a simple O(Mκp) estimate for building Tκ(Sπ) and prediction-time cost with q candidates would help position the method versus OVA and tree methods.

Methodology and experimental rigor
- Critical missing details prevent reproducibility: the mapping φ is said to use p=10 features “Table 1,” but the table is not present. Several features are described vaguely (e.g., which distance? cosine? are features normalized? TF-IDF weighting?). The candidate selection depends on “nearest-centroid”; define centroid in what space and metric; report q used.
- No ablations: results do not vary κ, π, or q, and there is no analysis of the speed–accuracy trade-off as κ and q change, nor an “all negatives” vs sampled negatives comparison. How sensitive are results to π’s balancing strength?
- The LSHTC2 datasets are originally multi-label; converting to multi-class by “replicating” instances changes the distribution and may unfairly advantage/penalize methods designed for multi-label (FastXML, PfastReXML). At minimum, justify this choice, ensure no leakage across splits, and clarify how per-instance duplication affected class priors, memory, and baselines. Consider including a proper multi-label evaluation or multi-class datasets natively.
- Baseline fairness: OVA and M-SVM are declared “impossible” for WIKI-50K/100K without reporting actual measured partial runs or memory caps; PD-Sparse uses hashing—please report hashes and their collision rates, and ensure all methods are tuned comparably (appendix not provided). Missing comparisons to other shortlist/embedding/ANN-based candidate generation approaches common in extreme classification.
- Reporting: Tables/Figures referenced (Fig. 1, Table 1–3, Fig. 3, Appendix B) are missing. Without these, it is impossible to verify the claims, hyperparameters, and detailed results.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous grammatical/style issues hinder clarity: “even-though,” “most small size classes,” “few big size classes,” “applicationdependent,” etc. Several math typos: “.=” used inconsistently; “κ K” likely “κ ≪ K”; stray footnote markers and a stray “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)”.
- Inconsistent and incomplete references (many lack years/venues). Foundational references for local Rademacher complexity are missing. Please standardize and complete the bibliography.
- The GitHub link reveals authorship, violating anonymity requirements.

Relation to prior work
- Dyadic reductions and pairwise formulations for multiclass/ranking are well-studied; negative sampling and shortlist-based prediction are also common in extreme classification. The paper’s main novelty is the two-stage sampling plus a dependency-aware bound. Please better differentiate from prior binary-reduction work (e.g., Usunier et al. NIPS 2006; Weston & Watkins; ECOC variants) and recent extreme classification shortlisting methods.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely define π-sampling (per-example Bernoulli? with/without replacement? target class counts?). How is π normalized?
- Provide the exact feature definitions for φ, the metric used, and the values of κ and q per dataset. Include ablations.
- Instantiate the dependency graph and its fractional cover; provide the resulting χ* or an upper bound specific to your construction.
- Clarify and correct Theorems 1–2, with consistent notation and dependency on m, M, N, K; justify the use of local complexities and assumptions needed.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a simple, scalable idea.
- Attempts to provide nontrivial theory under dependence and sampling shift.
- Promising empirical performance claims on large datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing tables/figures/supplement; insufficient detail for reproduction.
- Theoretical development underspecified and possibly inconsistent.
- Baseline fairness and dataset conversion concerns; no ablations.
- Numerous presentation, notation, and formatting issues; anonymity violation.

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the current submission has too many technical, methodological, and presentation deficiencies to be evaluated or reproduced reliably. I encourage the authors to fix the theory, provide complete experiments and ablations, clean up the writing and references, and resubmit. Confidence: high.
