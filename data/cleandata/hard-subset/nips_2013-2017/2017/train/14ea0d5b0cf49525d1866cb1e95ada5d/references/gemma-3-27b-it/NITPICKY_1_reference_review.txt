This paper tackles the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, specifically focusing on improving the scalability and performance of the dyadic representation approach. The core idea – aggressive double sampling – aims to reduce computational cost and address class imbalance by strategically sampling both examples and classes. While the problem is relevant and the proposed approach has intuitive appeal, the paper suffers from numerous flaws in presentation, technical detail, and justification, ultimately leading to a negative recommendation.

The introduction provides adequate background but is somewhat verbose and repetitive. The phrasing “evolved as a popular branch” feels unnecessary, and the discussion of tree-based methods lacks precision. The contributions section is too high-level; a more concrete summary of the technical novelty would be beneficial. 

Section 2 introduces the mathematical framework. The notation is mostly standard, but the definition of the loss function (Equation 1) is unusual and not clearly motivated. While the ranking criterion is mentioned, a comparison to standard multi-class loss functions beyond a single citation would be helpful. The motivation for considering functions of the form g = f ◦ φ is weak; it’s a standard approach but deserves better justification. The definition of the dyadic transformation T(S) (Equation 3) is clear, but the explanation lacks intuition. The introduction of fractional Rademacher complexity and graph covering theory feels abrupt and lacks sufficient context for readers unfamiliar with these concepts. Definition 1 is presented without adequate explanation of its relevance. Theorem 1, while mathematically stated, is difficult to parse. The dependence on variance (r) is concerning – how is ‘r’ controlled in practice, and what happens if it’s large? The connection between the theoretical bounds and practical algorithm design isn’t clear. Theorem 2, similarly, is complex and the meanings of α and β are not immediately clear from the text – the explanation is too brief. The proof sketches mentioned as “provided in the supplementary material” are unacceptable; full proofs are expected in NIPS submissions.

Section 2.2, outlining the aggressive double sampling, is where the paper begins to significantly falter. The explanation of the (π, κ)-DS algorithm is unclear.  What is the rationale behind setting πk ∝ 1/µk? Is this the *only* strategy considered? A sensitivity analysis to different sampling strategies is missing. The claim that this balances the dataset needs rigorous verification, not just assertion.  The use of “adversarial classes” is not well-defined; the uniform sampling of κ classes seems arbitrary. There is no discussion of the computational cost of calculating class centroids, particularly for large datasets. Equation (6) simply restates the empirical risk without providing substantial new insight.

Section 2.3 discusses prediction with candidate selection. The rationale for nearest-centroid selection is reasonable, but the discussion lacks detail. How is the number of candidates (q) chosen? What is the computational cost of this step? The claim that class centroids are computed once is misleading – they need to be updated if the model is updated.

Section 3, the experimental evaluation, is the weakest part of the paper. The dataset descriptions are adequate. The baselines chosen are relevant, but the hyperparameter tuning process is glossed over ("combination which gave best predictive performance") without specific details. The platform description is insufficient. Crucially, the experimental results are presented in a disorganized manner. Table 3 is difficult to interpret; the formatting is poor, and the significance of the results isn't emphasized. The discussion of the results is highly subjective and relies heavily on qualitative observations ("seems to present the best compromise"). The claim that (π, κ)-DS is "least affected by long-tailed class distributions" needs substantial evidence beyond the observed accuracy and F1 scores. There's no ablation study demonstrating the impact of the double sampling strategy (π and κ) on performance. The observation that OVA and M-SVM become intractable is expected and doesn't necessarily constitute a significant finding. The bar plots in Fig. 3 are helpful but add little new information.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* **Lack of Technical Rigor:** The theoretical contributions (Theorems 1 and 2) are poorly explained and the associated proofs are relegated to a supplementary material, which is unacceptable.
* **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experimental setup is inadequately described, the results are poorly presented, and the conclusions are weakly supported.
* **Unclear Algorithm Description:** The (π, κ)-DS algorithm is difficult to understand and lacks justification for key design choices.
* **Poor Writing Quality:** The paper is verbose, repetitive, and lacks clarity in many sections.
* **Missing Ablation Studies:** No investigation into the impact of key parameters (π, κ, q) on performance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Relevance of the Problem:** Extreme multi-class classification is an important and challenging problem.
* **Intuitive Approach:** The idea of aggressive double sampling is conceptually appealing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an idea with potential, but it is severely hampered by technical deficiencies, poor presentation, and a lack of rigorous experimental validation. While the problem is important, the proposed solution is not convincingly demonstrated to be effective or scalable. The numerous flaws outweigh the limited strengths. **Strongly Reject.**
