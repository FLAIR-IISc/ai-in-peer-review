Summary
This paper tackles large-scale multiclass (including extreme) classification by reducing it to a binary problem over dyadic example–label pairs, but avoiding the prohibitive O(mK) blowup via an aggressive double sampling scheme. Concretely: (i) a class-rebalancing sample Sπ is drawn, oversampling rare classes and subsampling frequent ones; (ii) for each retained example, only κ “adversarial” negative labels are sampled uniformly, yielding κ|Sπ| dyadic pairs. The authors derive new generalization bounds for this setting using a combination of fractional graph covers (to handle dependence among dyads from the same example) and local Rademacher complexity (to exploit variance-based, potentially faster rates), explicitly accounting for the distribution shift induced by π and the constructed dependence structure. For prediction, they further accelerate inference by candidate selection: restricting the label set for a test example to the q nearest class centroids and then scoring f*(φ(x, y)) within that subset. Experiments on LSHTC (DMOZ and Wikipedia) with up to 100K labels show favorable trade-offs in time/memory versus OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding baselines, with competitive accuracy and macro-F1. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
The dyadic reduction and ranking-based multiclass losses connect to prior work by Usunier/Amini and multiclass SVM/AdaBoost.MR. The dependence-aware analysis leverages fractional chromatic numbers (Janson) and local Rademacher complexities. Sampling few negatives is reminiscent of negative sampling / sampled softmax and WARP/NCE ideas, while class rebalancing addresses long-tail distributions common in extreme classification. Compared to tree-based/embedding-based methods (FastXML, RecallTree, label embeddings), this approach keeps a flat decision with fewer decisions at test time.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, bold reduction: the double sampling (π, κ) is pragmatic and directly targets the two core pain points—class imbalance and quadratic dyadic expansion—without elaborate structures.
- Theory for the right object: generalization bounds that explicitly handle (a) dyadic dependence, (b) distribution shift induced by re-weighting π, and (c) local complexity. This is a nontrivial contribution beyond prior fractional Rademacher results.
- Scalability and practicality: candidate selection via centroids is a straightforward, effective accelerator; experiments scale to 100K classes with strong time/memory profiles and competitive accuracy/MaF1.
- Reproducibility: code and datasets are provided; hyperparameter tuning on heldout described.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Clarity and completeness:
  - φ and f are under-specified in the main text. The 10 features (Table 1) are referred to but not presented; the function class F (linear? margin-based? loss used in practice?) and training details of f are unclear.
  - The precise choices of κ and q, and their selection protocol, are not detailed. An ablation on κ, π, and q would be very helpful.
  - It is not entirely clear whether baselines are trained in the same feature space as the proposed method. If the proposed method uses a compact, engineered φ while baselines operate on raw bag-of-words, time/memory comparisons may conflate feature effects with algorithmic ones.
- Fairness and metrics:
  - LSHTC2 is multi-label; converting to multi-class by duplicating instances changes the problem (and the label distribution). Some baselines are designed for multi-label extreme classification. Please justify the transformation and discuss its effect on comparisons.
  - Candidate selection may exclude the true label; its impact on accuracy/F1 versus speed is not quantified (e.g., recall of true label in candidate set as a function of q).
- Theory:
  - The constants α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy can be large; guidance on practical π choices that keep α, β controlled (beyond “π ∝ 1/µ”) would strengthen the utility of the bounds.
  - How is the fractional cover instantiated for the dependency graph of dyads produced by (π, κ)-DS? Any explicit bounds on χ* for this graph would improve interpretability.
  - The connection between optimizing the empirical ranking loss and the reported accuracy/F1 could be discussed (e.g., whether hinge/logistic surrogates are used, and the implications).
- Positioning:
  - The relationship to negative sampling / sampled softmax and pairwise ranking approaches popular in NLP and recommendation (NCE, WARP, BPR) deserves mention, as the training-time idea is similar.
  - No ablation on the impact of the candidate selection module or on training on all labels at test-time would help isolate contributions.

Quality
The approach is technically sound at a high level, and the theoretical analysis addresses the key challenges of dependence and reweighting. Proofs are deferred, so some details cannot be verified here. The empirical evaluation is broad and indicates meaningful gains in runtime/memory with competitive performance; however, the absence of ablations and some missing implementation specifics hinder full assessment.

Clarity
Readable overall, but the paper would benefit from:
- Including the feature list for φ in the main text and specifying F, loss, optimizer, and regularization.
- Detailing how κ, π normalization, and q are chosen, and reporting sensitivity analyses.
- Stating whether all methods share the same base feature space, and, if not, explaining the implications.

Originality
Moderately strong. The double sampling recipe is simple but, combined with a dependence-aware, local complexity analysis tailored to this reduction, feels fresh. The candidate selection is more of an engineering add-on. Relative to prior dyadic reductions, the explicit handling of class-imbalance shift and κ-negative sampling within the theory is novel.

Significance
High practical relevance for extreme classification. If clarified and strengthened with ablations, the method is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., with learned φ, adaptive negative sampling). The theoretical framing could spur further work on sampled reductions with dependent structures.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, scalable idea that addresses a pressing practical bottleneck.
- Theoretical guarantees adapted to the sampling and dependence structure.
- Strong empirical time/memory/performance trade-offs at 50K–100K labels.
- Reproducible.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing details on φ, F, training loss; limited ablations.
- Potential fairness issues in feature spaces across baselines.
- Limited analysis of candidate selection’s effect and parameter sensitivity.
- Some theoretical constants may be loose; practical guidance is limited.

Questions for the authors
1) Precisely define φ (include Table 1), F, loss, and optimizer used. Are models linear in φ?
2) How are π normalized in practice, and what α, β values do you observe?
3) Provide ablations over κ and q (candidate set size), including recall of true label in candidate set versus speed.
4) Do all baselines use the same feature representation? If not, please discuss and, if possible, include a control.
5) Can you relate your approach to sampled softmax/NCE/WARP and discuss differences?

Recommendation
Accept, contingent on clarifying implementation details and adding key ablations. The paper introduces a compelling reduction with nontrivial analysis and strong practical impact in extreme multiclass settings, which outweighs the current presentation gaps. Confidence: high.
