- Proposes a (π, κ)-double sampling ((π, κ)-DS) strategy to convert extreme multi-class problems into fewer dyadic binary examples by balancing classes and sampling κ classes per example.  
- Leverages dyadic representation and provides theoretical generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity for tighter guarantees and faster convergence.  
- Demonstrates substantial computational efficiency gains on large datasets (up to 100k classes) while maintaining competitive accuracy and macro F1.  
- Addresses practical bottlenecks of OVA, tree-based, and embedding-based methods, positioning (π, κ)-DS as a practical alternative for long-tailed, large-class problems.  
- Well-structured paper with clear problem formulation, methodology, theoretical analysis, and comprehensive experiments.  
- Theoretical results (Theorems 1–2) are opaque: parameters α and β (involving η_y and π_y) are not clearly motivated or compared to prior bounds.  
- Lacks analysis of sensitivity to hyperparameters π and κ and insufficient justification for the method’s "aggressiveness" beyond handling long tails.  
- Omits comparisons to some relevant prior methods (e.g., SLEEC, LEML) and doesn’t fully substantiate claims about being outperformed.  
- Candidate selection strategy for prediction is under-analyzed; impact of candidate set size q on accuracy vs. speed is not explored.  
- Paper lacks visualizations (learning curves, class distribution before/after sampling) that would clarify sampling effects.  
- Claims about "local fractional Rademacher complexity" need clearer linkage to improvements over prior fractional Rademacher analyses.  
- No empirical plot or evidence showing dataset class distributions or how sampling alters them.  
- Unclear how the 50k/100k class samples from WIKI-large were chosen; potential sampling bias not addressed.  
- No formal time/space complexity analysis of the (π, κ)-DS algorithm to complement runtime/memory results.  
- Relation to other dyadic methods is not fully detailed; differences from related projection/sampling techniques are underexplained.  
- Overall assessment: strong, practically relevant contribution with meaningful theory and convincing experiments, though presentation of theory could improve.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Suggest clarifying theoretical bounds and explicitly comparing them to [16] to show improvements and practical implications.  
- Suggest performing hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for π and κ and better justifying aggressive sampling choices.  
- Suggest adding visualizations of class distributions before/after sampling and learning curves to illustrate effects.  
- Suggest including comparisons to additional recent baselines (e.g., SLEEC, LEML) to strengthen empirical claims.  
- Suggest analyzing prediction time/accuracy tradeoffs for candidate selection (q) and providing a formal complexity analysis.  
- Final thought: solid paper combining theoretical rigor and practical gains, suitable for NIPS if theoretical clarity and additional empirical analyses are addressed.
