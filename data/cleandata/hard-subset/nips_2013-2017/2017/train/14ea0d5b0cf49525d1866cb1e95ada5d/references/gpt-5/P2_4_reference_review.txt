Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme multiclass classification by reducing it to a binary problem over dyadic example–class pairs, combined with an aggressive double sampling scheme. The method samples training examples per class with probabilities inversely proportional to class frequency to mitigate long-tail imbalance (π), and for each kept example samples only κ adversarial classes to form dyadic pairs. A candidate selection step at prediction time restricts scoring to q nearest centroid classes. Theoretically, the paper develops generalization bounds for (i) the dyadic reduction with dependent pairs using fractional covers and local Rademacher complexity, and (ii) the double-sampled setting, incorporating the distribution shift induced by π and pair subsampling κ. Empirically, the method is evaluated on LSHTC/Wikipedia datasets up to 100k classes, comparing against OVA, Multiclass SVM, tree-based methods (RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML), and PD-Sparse.

This builds on prior dyadic reductions and ranking-based multiclass surrogates (e.g., Usunier et al., Weston & Watkins, Joshi et al.) and fractional Rademacher analyses for dependent data. The novelty lies in the combined double sampling strategy with theoretical guarantees under distribution shift, and its practical instantiation for text via a compact similarity feature map.

Quality
- Theory: The use of fractional graph covers and local Rademacher complexity to handle dependence among dyadic pairs is appropriate, and the stated bounds are plausible. The bounds in Theorem 2 explicitly reflect sampling tradeoffs via α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y and a 1/√(M κ) term, giving insight into how aggressive subsampling affects risk. However:
  - The learning objective used in practice is not clearly specified (0/1 loss is analyzed; experiments presumably optimize a convex surrogate such as hinge or logistic). A discussion connecting the bounds to the surrogate actually optimized would strengthen soundness.
  - The candidate selection at prediction time is not analyzed; there is no bound on the probability that the true class is excluded from the q candidates or its impact on excess risk.
  - The dependence of the complexity term RT(`∘H_r) on κ, p, and data statistics is not unpacked, so it’s hard to assess tightness or compare to existing dyadic analyses beyond the leading 1/√(M κ) factor.
- Experiments: The empirical section is generally solid and relevant for extreme text classification:
  - Baselines include strong linear methods (OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse) and popular tree-based approaches; results indicate that the proposed approach achieves a favorable accuracy–time–memory tradeoff at 50k–100k classes where OVA/M-SVM are infeasible, and improves macro-F1 due to balancing.
  - The setting is realistic and the candidate selection speeds prediction.
  - On the downside, important ablations are missing: sensitivity to κ, π, and q; effect of the candidate selection on accuracy and speed; and the impact of the particular φ. Also, claims of generality beyond text are not empirically supported.
  - The multi-label LSHTC2 sets are converted to multi-class by replicating instances; this may distort class priors and evaluation. A short discussion of this choice and its implications would help.
  - The choice to omit SLEEC/LEML is reasonable for multi-class, but the exclusion rationale citing another paper’s results is weak; stronger would be including one representative embedding method. More importantly for the 2017 timeframe, comparisons to DiSMEC or other linear extreme classifiers would improve the case.

Clarity
The paper is mostly well written and organized, with a clear algorithmic description of the double sampling and candidate selection. The theoretical section concisely states assumptions and results. Some clarity issues:
- The actual training loss and optimizer used are not specified in the main text.
- π setting details (normalization, caps to ensure valid probabilities) and how oversampling is implemented are not fully described.
- The dyadic feature map φ for text is only sketched; the exact 10 features and preprocessing should be enumerated in the main text or summarized more concretely.
- Hyperparameter choices for κ and q, and their selection protocol, should be stated in the main paper (not only in the supplement).
- Minor typos and formatting glitches suggest a need for polishing.

Originality
The high-level ingredients (dyadic reduction, negative sampling, class rebalancing) are known, but their joint use for extreme multiclass with a principled analysis under sampling-induced distribution shift is novel. The use of local fractional Rademacher complexity to derive fast-rate style bounds in this dependent setting is a meaningful theoretical contribution. The prediction-time candidate pruning via centroids is simple and not new, but practical.

Significance
The problem is important, and the proposed method addresses key pain points: memory and time blow-up of OVA and instability/accuracy degradation of trees in the extreme regime. The empirical gains in runtime and memory with competitive accuracy at 50k–100k classes are valuable for practitioners. The theoretical results provide reassurance that aggressive sampling does not break consistency, though practical guidance on choosing π and κ remains limited. Broader impact could be higher if demonstrated beyond text or with learned φ.

Pros
- Addresses an important and practical extreme classification setting.
- Simple, scalable algorithm with clear engineering benefits.
- Theoretical bounds accounting for dependency and sampling shift; insightful dependence on M and κ.
- Strong empirical results on large benchmarks; code released.

Cons
- Missing analysis of candidate selection; no guarantees if true label is pruned.
- Limited ablations; sensitivity to κ, π, and q not explored.
- Practical training objective and optimization details are underspecified.
- Text-specific φ; generality to other domains not demonstrated.
- Some baseline omissions and reliance on external claims to exclude methods.

Questions for authors
- What loss and model are used to train f on Tκ(Sπ)? How does performance vary with hinge vs logistic?
- How are π normalized and capped? What are typical α and β values observed?
- How sensitive are results to κ and q? Is there an empirical tradeoff curve?
- How often is the true class excluded by candidate selection, and how does q affect this?
- Can you report RT(`∘H_r) proxies or bounds to illustrate dependence on κ and p?

Recommendation
Overall, the paper meets the NeurIPS criteria reasonably well: solid idea, nontrivial theory adapted to the dependent dyadic setting, and compelling large-scale experiments. Clarity and empirical analysis could be strengthened, but the contribution is useful and likely to be adopted. I lean to weak accept, contingent on clarifying the training objective, adding sensitivity analyses for κ and q, and tightening the description of π and φ.
