- Tackles extreme multi-class classification and limitations of OVA, tree-based, embedding, and dyadic methods.  
- Proposes "aggressive double sampling": oversample minority and subsample majority classes before dyadic construction to reduce examples.  
- Novelty centers on the order of operations—sampling prior to dyadic construction—rather than the dyadic idea itself.  
- Provides new generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexity to theoretically justify the sampling strategy.  
- Empirically validated on large-scale text datasets (DMOZ, Wikipedia) with competitive accuracy, MaF1, runtime, and memory improvements.  
- Positions and cites related work comprehensively, contrasting with OVA, tree-based, embedding, and prior dyadic approaches.  
- Strength: addresses a significant scalability and class-imbalance problem in extreme classification.  
- Strength: strong theoretical grounding via new generalization results.  
- Strength: compelling empirical performance and practical speed/memory gains on large datasets.  
- Strength: clear writing, well-defined notation, straightforward algorithm description, and claimed reproducibility (code/datasets).  
- Weakness: introduces hyperparameters (π and κ) but lacks thorough sensitivity/ablation analysis.  
- Weakness: uses a simple word-overlap feature mapping φ; unclear how richer features affect results.  
- Weakness: candidate selection (nearest-centroid) is briefly described; impact of candidate count q on runtime/accuracy is unclear.  
- Weakness: limited discussion on why certain baselines (e.g., RecallTree, FastXML) underperform in this setting.  
- Weakness: proofs are in supplementary material; main text could use a concise summary of key proof insights.  
- For acceptance: important problem, novel and well-motivated method, rigorous theory, strong empirical evidence, and clear presentation.  
- Against acceptance: insufficient hyperparameter analysis, limited feature representation evaluation, and sparse candidate-selection details.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with meaningful contributions; minor weaknesses are addressable.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—require ablation on π and κ, discussion of richer feature impacts, more candidate-selection details, and explanation of baseline failures.
