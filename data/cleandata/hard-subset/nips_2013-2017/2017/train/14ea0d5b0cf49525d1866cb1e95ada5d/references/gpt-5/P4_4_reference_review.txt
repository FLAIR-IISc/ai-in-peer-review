Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses large-scale multiclass (including extreme) classification by reducing it to a binary problem over dyadic example–class pairs. The key elements are: (i) an aggressive double sampling scheme that oversamples minority classes and subsamples majority classes to construct a balanced subset Sπ, and then (ii) constructs only κ negative dyadic pairs per retained example (instead of all K−1), yielding a much smaller binary dataset Tκ(Sπ). The learning objective is the pairwise ranking loss associated with the average number of impostor classes per example, with predictors of the form g(x,y)=f(φ(x,y)) where φ maps example–class pairs to a joint space. The paper further proposes a candidate selection heuristic at inference using nearest centroids to reduce prediction time.

On the theory side, the paper derives generalization bounds for empirical risk minimization on the dependent dyadic data using fractional graph covers and local (fractional) Rademacher complexities. The first result (Theorem 1) gives a bound for the full dyadic expansion T(S) using local Rademacher complexity of Hr (variance-controlled subclass). The main result (Theorem 2) handles the double-sampled regime, introducing factors α=maxy ηy/πy and β=maxy 1/πy that quantify the mismatch between the original class distribution η and the sampling π, and includes terms scaling with √((K−1)/(Mκ)).

Empirically, the method is evaluated on LSHTC text datasets (DMOZ/Wikipedia), converted to multiclass when necessary, with up to 100k classes. Compared against OVA, multiclass SVM, tree-based extreme methods (RecallTree, FastXML, PfastreXML), and PD-Sparse, the proposed (π,κ)-DS shows competitive accuracy and macro-F1 while using substantially less time/memory than OVA/M-SVM and comparable or better efficiency than tree methods. The dyadic mapping uses a simple 10-dimensional feature set inspired by learning-to-rank, including a centroid-distance feature also used for candidate selection at prediction.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The reduction to dyadic pairs and use of pairwise ranking loss are standard and appropriate. The dependence introduced by sharing x across K−1 pairs is addressed using fractional covers and Rademacher complexities, which is a principled approach. Extending to local complexities is reasonable and can yield faster rates. The double sampling introduces a distribution shift; the bounds reflect this via α and β. However, there is no corresponding importance weighting in the training loss to correct the shift, so the learning procedure can be biased; the bounds essentially degrade with α, rather than eliminating the bias. Proofs are deferred; the main text does not detail assumptions (e.g., boundedness, Lipschitzness if a surrogate is used) nor the exact learning algorithm (loss, regularization, solver), which limits verifiability. Some notation is imprecise (e.g., the dyadic construction indices in Eq. 3).
- Empirical evaluation: Results are promising for scalability and competitive accuracy. However, key ablations are missing: the effect of κ, π, and candidate set size q on accuracy/efficiency; the contribution of oversampling versus negative subsampling; and the effect of candidate selection versus full argmax. The text domain and low-dimensional φ may not reflect broader applicability. The conversion of multilabel LSHTC to multiclass by replication may skew distributions; this choice and its impact are not discussed.

Clarity:
The paper is generally understandable but suffers from typos/notation glitches (e.g., indices in Eq. 3, missing explicit descriptions of all 10 features, some undefined constants/weights ωk). Theorems are stated with many terms and constants but with limited intuition. Important implementation details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., hyperparameters, feature table), making it harder for the reader to reproduce from the main paper.

Originality:
- The dyadic reduction and negative sampling of impostor classes are known ideas in pairwise ranking and extreme classification (e.g., WSABIE; negative sampling in large-scale embedding methods). Class-balanced sampling is also a standard technique for long-tailed distributions. The main novelty lies in combining these sampling strategies within the dyadic reduction and providing generalization bounds that account for dependency and sampling-induced distribution shift using local fractional Rademacher complexities. This theoretical integration appears incremental but meaningful.
- The candidate selection via nearest centroids at prediction is a simple heuristic; similar candidate pruning is common in extreme classification and retrieval.

Significance:
The method addresses an important scalability challenge, showing solid practical improvements in time/memory with competitive accuracy on large text benchmarks up to 100k classes, which is valuable. The theoretical results provide some justification for the sampling scheme’s consistency, although constants and dependence on α,β may limit practical tightness. The reliance on domain-specific low-dimensional φ raises questions about generalization to other modalities; demonstrating broader applicability would strengthen significance.

Pros:
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem (extreme multiclass) with a simple, scalable approach.
- Provides nontrivial generalization bounds for dependent dyadic data under double sampling, using local fractional Rademacher complexity.
- Competitive empirical performance with strong efficiency gains; open-source code.
- Handles long-tailed distributions via class-balanced sampling.

Cons:
- Novelty of the sampling idea is limited; related strategies exist in ranking/embedding literature; positioning to prior negative sampling work is brief.
- Clarity issues in notation and missing details (feature definitions, solver/loss/regularization), with proofs relegated to the supplement.
- No ablations for κ, π, or candidate size q; no importance weighting to correct sampling shift; bounds suggest degradation via α,β.
- Evaluation restricted to text; effects of converting multilabel to multiclass and candidate pruning on accuracy are not analyzed.
- Comparison set omits some modern extreme multiclass/multilabel methods beyond the cited rationale.

Suggestions and questions:
- Provide ablations for κ, π, and q, including accuracy–efficiency trade-offs and sensitivity to long-tail severity.
- Consider importance-weighted ERM to correct for π≠η and report both empirical and theoretical impacts.
- Clarify the learning objective (loss, regularization), solver used, and computational complexity per iteration.
- Expand related work discussion to explicitly contrast with negative sampling and pairwise ranking methods (e.g., WSABIE and successors).
- Include an experiment without candidate pruning, and a study of q vs. accuracy/time.
- Improve notation and complete definitions (e.g., the 10 features, assumptions for Theorems).

Overall recommendation:
The paper offers a practical, scalable approach with supportive theory and promising results, but the novelty is moderate and the presentation lacks key details and ablations. I lean toward a weak reject, with the expectation that addressing clarity and empirical analysis would make it a stronger contribution.
