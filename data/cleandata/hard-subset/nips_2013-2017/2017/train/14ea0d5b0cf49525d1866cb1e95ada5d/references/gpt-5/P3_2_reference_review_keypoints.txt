- Tackles extreme multiclass classification via dyadic example–class pairs with aggressive double sampling to avoid O(mK) blow-up.
- Sampling scheme: oversample minority and subsample majority classes (πk ∝ 1/μk) and draw κ adversarial negative classes per example.
- Provides ERM generalization bounds for dependent dyads using fractional and local Rademacher complexities, accounting for pair dependency and sampling-induced distribution shift.
- Implements prediction-time candidate selection using nearest-centroid to evaluate only a small set of classes.
- Empirical results on LSHTC/Wikipedia (up to 100k classes) show competitive accuracy/MaF1 and much lower time/memory vs OVA/M-SVM; code released.
- Strength: simple, practically impactful double sampling addressing a key scalability bottleneck.
- Strength: nontrivial theory extending dyadic-dependent analyses to local fractional Rademacher complexity with variance-sensitive bounds.
- Strength: careful large-scale experiments with strong baselines and clear resource/time gains.
- Strength: prediction-time acceleration is general (compatible with any dyadic scorer) and implementation is open-source.
- Weakness: constants α and β in Theorem 2 can be large on long-tailed distributions; authors should quantify these on datasets and discuss implications.
- Weakness: no importance weighting to correct sampling bias; an ablation comparing unweighted vs importance-weighted ERM is missing.
- Weakness: lacks systematic sensitivity analysis of κ (negative sampling) and q (candidate size), and missing candidate-rec recall curves and performance/time/memory trade-offs.
- Weakness: dyadic feature mapping φ is a handcrafted 10-D text-specific set; need fair baselines using the same φ or evaluation of a learned φ to show generality.
- Weakness: nearest-centroid candidate selection lacks analysis of exclusion error (true-class recall) and computational cost at scale; need candidate recall and amortized prediction time with/without ANN.
- Weakness: experimental fairness/details unclear — multi-label→multi-class conversion effects, incomplete hyperparameter documentation, and missing comparisons to non-tree candidate-generation baselines.
- Weakness: theory does not account for approximation error from candidate selection; a discussion or conditions for preserving ranking consistency is needed.
- Quality: technically sound and well-connected to prior work, but bounds may be loose and experiments miss key ablations.
- Clarity: generally readable but notation is dense; feature mapping should be fully specified in the main text and minor typos fixed.
- Originality: novel combination of aggressive double sampling with dyadic reduction and new analysis; candidate-based prediction is standard but well-integrated.
- Significance: high practical relevance offering a useful time/memory–accuracy tradeoff; broader impact limited until a learned φ is demonstrated.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important scalability issue, provides new theory, strong empirical/resource results, and open-source code.
- Arguments against acceptance: potentially loose bounds (α, β), missing ablations and candidate-recall analysis, limited φ generality, and no theoretical treatment of candidate selection.
- Recommendation: weak accept, contingent on adding experimental ablations and clarifications on α, β and candidate selection for the camera-ready.
