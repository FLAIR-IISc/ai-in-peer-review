Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles large-scale multiclass (extreme) classification by reducing to a binary problem over dyadic example–class pairs. The key idea is a “double sampling” strategy: (i) subsample/oversample examples per class to mitigate long-tailed label distributions (π-sampling), and (ii) for each retained example, sample only κ negative classes to form dyadic pairs (κ-sampling), instead of enumerating all K−1 pairs. The authors analyze the dependent dyadic samples via fractional graph covers and derive generalization bounds using local Rademacher complexities, both for the full dyadic expansion (Theorem 1) and for the doubly sampled setting (Theorem 2). They instantiate the approach for text classification using a 10-dimensional dyadic feature mapping (documents vs. “mega-document” classes) and show strong empirical results on LSHTC/Wikipedia subsets up to 100K classes, with favorable time/memory trade-offs vs. OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, and tree/embedding baselines.

This sits at the intersection of: (a) one-vs-all and tree/embedding methods for extreme classification; (b) dyadic reductions and pairwise ranking losses (e.g., Weston-Watkins multiclass SVM; AdaBoost.MR; label ranking); and (c) learning with interdependent samples using fractional Rademacher complexity (e.g., Usunier et al.; Ralaivola & Amini). Sampling a subset of negatives per example is conceptually close to practices in sampled softmax/NCE and negative sampling widely used in extreme classification and recommendation, though those are not explicitly cited.

Strengths
- Practicality and scalability: The (π, κ)-DS procedure is simple, reduces memory and training cost dramatically, and yields competitive or better accuracy/MaF1 than strong baselines on large text corpora. The candidate-class selection at prediction further reduces latency.
- Theoretical contribution: The paper adapts local Rademacher complexity tools to the dependent dyadic setting via fractional covers, and extends the analysis to account for both dyadic dependence and class-prior shift induced by π-sampling. This is a nontrivial synthesis and, to my knowledge, novel in this combination.
- Sound loss formulation: The target loss is the average pairwise ranking error (Eq. 1), for which κ-sampling yields an unbiased estimator when negatives are sampled uniformly over Y\{y}, supporting consistency claims for fixed π.
- Reproducibility: Code link is provided; datasets are standard; hyperparameters are said to be tuned on held-out sets.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity/incompleteness of the learning setup: The paper never fully specifies the learning algorithm for f (loss surrogate, regularizer, optimizer). Equations and theorems present 0/1 loss, but experiments presumably use a convex surrogate (hinge/logistic) and a linear model with a specific regularization. This should be explicit, as it affects both empirical performance and how the theory applies (e.g., contraction).
- Role and consequences of π-sampling in theory: Theorem 2 bounds depend on α = max_y η_y/π_y and β = max_y 1/π_y. For the suggested heuristic π_y ∝ 1/η_y (inverse-frequency sampling), α can be large for head classes and β large for rare classes, potentially making the bound loose. The paper claims “consistency,” but this seems to require π that are bounded away from zero and not too different from η (or importance weighting). Please clarify the conditions under which α, β remain O(1) and whether consistency still holds when π deviates strongly from η.
- Missing importance weighting: Because π-sampling changes the marginal over classes, the unweighted empirical risk R̃Tκ(Sπ) is a biased estimator of the true risk unless π=η. The theory compensates with α,β factors, but a natural fix is to use importance-weighted empirical risk (η_y/π_y) to achieve unbiasedness. Why was this not used? At least an ablation comparing weighted vs. unweighted ERM would strengthen both theory and practice.
- Dependence handling details: The fractional cover construction and the choice ω_k=1 are mentioned but not described; exact/proper covers typically require nontrivial weights. A brief sketch of the cover used for dyadic dependence (K−1 pairs per example) and how χ*(G) scales with m and K would help readers assess tightness.
- Candidate selection at prediction: Pruning to q nearest centroids is a major heuristic that could both speed up and harm accuracy. There is no analysis of the probability of pruning away the true class, nor ablations showing sensitivity to q. Also, centroids are computed in the dyadic feature space—please clarify exactly which representation is used and whether this biases toward head classes.
- Experimental fairness and ablations: 
  - The proposed method uses a 10-D engineered dyadic feature mapping, whereas baselines likely use the original sparse text features; this conflates “better representation” with “better reduction/sampling.” A fair comparison would either (i) provide the same φ to OVA/M-SVM/PD-Sparse, or (ii) compare also to those baselines on the original features but include your method with a comparable base representation.
  - No ablations for π, κ, and candidate selection are shown. How do accuracy/MaF1/time/memory vary with κ and with different π schedules (uniform, η-proportional, inverse-frequency)? What is the impact of disabling candidate selection at test?
  - Converting multilabel data to multiclass by duplicating instances changes label priors and dependencies. Some baselines (e.g., FastXML/PfastReXML) are designed for multilabel; please discuss potential biases and ensure proper configuration for multiclass.
- Missing related work: The paper should reference and discuss connections/differences to sampled softmax/NCE/negative sampling and hierarchical softmax/candidate generation widely used in extreme classification and recommendation, which share the κ-sampling spirit.
- Presentation issues: Numerous typos/formatting glitches; figures/tables referenced but not shown in the main text; some definitions (e.g., Hr) could be streamlined. A brief complexity analysis (big-O in m, K, κ, p) for training and prediction would be useful.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important problem with a simple, scalable method that performs well in practice.
- Provides nontrivial generalization analysis for interdependent dyadic samples with sampling-induced shift.
- Code available; empirical results on large datasets.

Cons:
- Learning details and fairness of comparisons need clarification; missing ablations.
- Theoretical guarantees hinge on α,β that can be unfavorable for the advocated π; no importance weighting.
- Prediction-time candidate selection is unanalysed and not ablated.
- Limited demonstration beyond text-specific features.

Suggestions for improvement
- Specify the training pipeline (model class F, loss, regularizer, optimizer) and ensure the theory applies via standard contraction arguments.
- Either use importance-weighted ERM to correct π-shift or clearly state/practice constraints on π that keep α,β controlled; include empirical comparisons across π choices.
- Add ablations for κ and q, and report trade-offs in accuracy/time. Analyze the probability of pruning the true class under simple assumptions.
- Harmonize feature representations across baselines, or include additional baselines trained on the same φ.
- Discuss connections to sampled softmax/negative sampling and hierarchical methods; compare against a sampled-softmax OVA baseline if feasible.
- Include a concise complexity analysis and tighten exposition of the fractional cover used.

Overall evaluation
This is a promising and practically relevant contribution with a solid theoretical angle. However, the current paper leaves important methodological details and fairness concerns unresolved, and lacks critical ablations. I lean toward borderline acceptance if these issues can be addressed; otherwise, it falls short of NeurIPS standards for clarity and empirical rigor.
