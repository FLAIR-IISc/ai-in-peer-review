Summary
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem via dyadic example–class pairs, but in a way that avoids the O(mK) blow-up. The key idea is an aggressive double sampling scheme: (i) sample a balanced subset Sπ by oversampling minority classes and subsampling majority ones (πk ∝ 1/µk), and (ii) for each sampled example, draw only κ adversarial classes to form dyads. The authors provide generalization bounds for ERM on dependent dyads using fractional and local Rademacher complexities, accounting for both dependency among pairs from the same x and the distribution shift induced by sampling. They also propose a candidate-selection step at prediction time, evaluating only a small set of nearest-centroid classes. Empirically, on LSHTC/Wikipedia subsets up to 100k classes, the method attains competitive accuracy/MaF1 while greatly reducing time/memory vs OVA/M-SVM and competing well with tree-based extreme classification and PD-Sparse. Code is released.

Strengths
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck in multiclass-to-binary reductions; the double sampling is simple and practically impactful.
- Nontrivial theory: extends prior dyadic-dependent analyses to local fractional Rademacher complexity, yielding variance-sensitive bounds; also analyzes the effect of class-imbalance sampling and κ-negative subsampling.
- Careful large-scale experiments with strong baselines (OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse) on LSHTC/Wikipedia; clear gains in memory and wall-clock.
- Practical prediction-time acceleration via candidate selection; compatible with any dyadic scoring function.
- Open-source implementation.

Weaknesses and questions
- Role of α, β in bounds: Theorem 2 introduces α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy. With πy ∝ 1/ηy, α scales like maxy η2 y (up to a normalization constant), and β scales with the largest inverse sampling probability. On long-tailed distributions, these can be large and make the bounds loose. Please quantify typical α, β on your datasets and discuss practical implications.
- Bias vs importance weighting: The risk bound multiplies the empirical risk by α, effectively acknowledging bias from class reweighting and κ-negatives. Why not use importance weighting to yield an unbiased estimator of the original ranking loss (or a calibrated surrogate), which might also yield tighter bounds? An ablation comparing unweighted vs importance-weighted ERM would clarify.
- κ and q sensitivity: The approach introduces two key knobs (κ for dyad construction; q for prediction candidates). There is no systematic study of their effect on accuracy/time/memory or of the recall of the true label in the candidate set. Please include curves showing performance vs κ and q and the candidate recall.
- Feature mapping φ: The experiments use a handcrafted 10-D dyadic feature set tailored to text (learning-to-rank style, plus centroid distance). This narrows generality and may underrepresent the information available to strong baselines that operate on high-dimensional bag-of-words. It would help to (i) compare against baselines with equivalent feature constraints (e.g., same φ for OVA), or (ii) evaluate a learned φ (e.g., linear interactions over TF-IDF via a bilinear model) to demonstrate portability beyond text-specific features.
- Prediction candidate selection: The nearest-centroid heuristic is plausible, but there’s no analysis of its error (probability that the true class is excluded) or of its computational cost at scale (e.g., need for ANN over class centroids when K=100k). Please report candidate set recall and amortized prediction time with/without ANN structures.
- Experimental fairness/details: 
  - Multi-label to multi-class conversion by duplicating instances can distort frequency and introduce dependence; discuss impacts on macro-F1 and memory.
  - Hyperparameter ranges for all baselines and your method (π and κ choices, regularization/solver settings) should be fully documented; some are deferred to the supplement.
  - More recent extreme classifiers (for the time, PD-Sparse is strong; label trees/embeddings are included) are covered, but an additional non-tree extreme method with candidate generation (e.g., negative sampling or one-vs-rest with ANN candidate generators) would strengthen the case.
- Theory for candidate selection: The current bounds do not cover the additional approximation introduced by restricting prediction to a candidate set. A short discussion (even without full bounds) of conditions under which candidate selection preserves ranking consistency would be valuable.

Quality
- The technical development is sound and connects appropriately to prior work on dependent Rademacher complexities and graph covers. However, constants in Theorem 2 can be unfavorable, and the lack of importance weighting is a missed opportunity. Empirical work is substantial but lacks key ablations (κ, q, π schedules) and candidate recall analyses.

Clarity
- Generally readable with a clear setup of the dyadic reduction and sampling algorithm. Some notation is dense; a concise intuitive explanation of the bounds and the effect of α, β would help. The feature mapping (Table 1) should be fully described in the main text for reproducibility. Minor typos and formatting issues exist.

Originality
- The combination of aggressive double sampling with dyadic reduction and an analysis via local fractional Rademacher complexity appears novel beyond Joshi et al. (IDA’15). The candidate-based prediction is standard but well-integrated here.

Significance
- Practical relevance for extreme classification is high; the approach offers an appealing time/memory–accuracy tradeoff and a theoretically grounded alternative to trees and embeddings. Broader impact may be limited by reliance on task-specific φ unless a learned φ is demonstrated.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, important scalability issue with a simple, implementable method.
- Provides new theory for sampled dyadic reductions with dependence.
- Strong empirical performance and resource savings on large benchmarks; code released.

Arguments against acceptance
- Bounds may be loose in the regimes of interest due to α, β; no importance weighting.
- Missing ablations and candidate-recall analysis; limited discussion of φ generality.
- Prediction candidate selection lacks theoretical treatment; reproducibility of φ is under-detailed in the main text.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a solid, practically useful contribution with nontrivial theoretical analysis. Addressing the experimental ablations and clarifying the role of α, β and candidate selection would significantly strengthen the camera-ready.
