Summary
The paper tackles extreme multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary dyadic problem and aggressively subsampling. The proposed (π, κ)-DS scheme first rebalances the training set by oversampling minority classes and subsampling majority classes (π), then constructs only κ negative dyadic pairs per training example instead of all K−1. The authors derive generalization bounds using fractional and local Rademacher complexities to handle dependence among dyadic pairs and the distribution shift induced by sampling. For prediction, they further prune the candidate set via nearest class centroids. Experiments on LSHTC/DMOZ/Wikipedia datasets suggest competitive accuracy and macro-F1 with substantially reduced time/memory vs OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse and tree/embedding baselines. Code is released.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on dyadic reductions for multiclass classification and pairwise ranking (e.g., [16], WSABIE/WARP-style sampling), and on tree and embedding methods for extreme classification. Theoretical tools (fractional chromatic number, fractional Rademacher complexity, local Rademacher) follow [14,25,22]. The sampling idea resembles standard class rebalancing and negative sampling; the candidate pruning is related to centroid/ANN preselection.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical bottleneck (K−1 blowup) with a simple, scalable sampling scheme that is easy to implement.
- Theoretical analysis accounts for dependent dyadic examples and sampling-induced distribution shift; using local Rademacher complexities is a reasonable refinement over prior bounds.
- Large-scale experiments on standard datasets; code availability is a plus.
- Candidate set pruning is a practical engineering contribution for inference efficiency.

Weaknesses
- Novelty appears incremental. Oversampling minorities, subsampling negatives (κ), and centroid-based candidate pruning are established heuristics; the core idea is close to prior dyadic reductions and negative sampling in ranking. The theoretical results largely adapt existing machinery; no sharp, instantiated rates or concrete guidance emerge.
- The theory-to-practice gap is significant. Theorems are for 0/1 loss, yet the training loss/optimizer for f are unspecified; no surrogate consistency discussion. Key constants α, β depend inversely on π and can be large; there is no principled selection of π, κ beyond heuristics, no importance weighting to correct sampling bias, and no bound instantiation on the studied datasets.
- Experimental fairness and diagnostics are insufficient.
  - The proposed method uses a 10-dimensional hand-crafted dyadic feature mapping, while baselines likely operate on high-dimensional sparse text features. Time/memory comparisons are thus not apples-to-apples; reduced dimensionality alone can drive speed/memory gains.
  - No ablations: no sensitivity to κ, π, or the candidate set size q; no results without candidate pruning; no comparison to the full dyadic T(S) vs sampled Tκ(Sπ); no analysis of the long-tail effect claimed to be mitigated.
  - Converting multi-label datasets to multi-class by duplicating instances biases the distribution and may favor the proposed rebalancing; tree/multilabel methods may be disadvantaged.
  - Missing comparisons to closely related dyadic/pairwise baselines (e.g., WSABIE/WARP-like negative sampling, earlier dyadic reducers) and to strong extreme-classification methods of the era that use OVA efficiently (e.g., DiSMEC) or hierarchical partitioning for multiclass.
- Clarity/Completeness issues:
  - f, F, the training objective, regularization, and optimization details are not specified. It is unclear whether f is linear and which surrogate loss is used.
  - The φ mapping is only sketched; reproducibility of the 10 features is unclear without the full Table 1; generalization to non-text domains is speculative.
  - Prediction candidate selection details (q, distance metric, centroid computation space) and its impact on accuracy are not reported.
  - Theorems lack discussion of assumptions (e.g., Lipschitzness, boundedness) and a comparison to previous bounds to establish a concrete advantage.

Quality
The paper is technically competent in adapting known generalization tools to the dependent dyadic setting, but key practical training details are missing. The empirical study is promising but lacks crucial ablations and fairness controls, making it hard to attribute gains to the proposed ideas rather than feature dimensionality or heuristic tuning.

Clarity
Overall structure is clear, but important components (definition and training of f, full feature mapping φ, hyperparameters κ/π/q, and proof assumptions) are under-specified. The bounds are presented without guidance on their practical implications.

Originality
Moderate. The double sampling scheme and centroid-based candidate selection are not particularly novel; the theoretical contribution refines prior dependent-data bounds but does not yield actionable new insights or methods.

Significance
Potentially useful as an engineering recipe for scaling dyadic reductions, but without stronger, fair comparisons and ablations, and without principled selection/weighting under sampling, the impact is limited.

Questions for the authors
1. What is f (model class), what loss/optimizer/regularization do you use, and how does it relate to the 0/1-loss analysis? Are there any guarantees for the surrogate training?
2. How are π and κ chosen per dataset? Did you try importance weighting to correct for sampling bias?
3. What is the effect of κ and q on accuracy and runtime? Please provide sensitivity curves and ablations (with/without candidate pruning).
4. Are baselines trained on the same 10-dim representation or on standard bag-of-words? If the latter, please report a control where all methods use the same features to ensure fair time/memory comparisons.
5. How does the method compare to prior dyadic/negative-sampling approaches (e.g., WSABIE/WARP or [16]) under the same features?

Pros
- Simple, scalable idea addressing a real bottleneck
- Some theoretical treatment of sampling and dependence
- Large-scale experiments; code release

Cons
- Limited novelty; incremental theory without actionable guidance
- Missing training details; unclear alignment between theory and practice
- Questionable fairness of comparisons; missing ablations and sensitivity analyses
- Evaluation choices (multi-label to multi-class conversion) may bias results

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is reasonable and likely useful in practice, but the current submission falls short on methodological clarity, novelty, and rigorous, fair empirical validation. Strengthening the experimental section with fair feature controls and thorough ablations, clarifying the training procedure, and providing principled guidance for π/κ (or importance-weighted ERM) would significantly improve the paper.
