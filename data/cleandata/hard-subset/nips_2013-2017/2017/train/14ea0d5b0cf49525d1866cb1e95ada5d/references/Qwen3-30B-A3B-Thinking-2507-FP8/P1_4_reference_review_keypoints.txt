- Tackles extreme multi-class classification (up to 100k classes) by converting to a binary problem via "aggressive double sampling" ((π, κ)-DS) to reduce computation.  
- (π, κ)-DS balances class distribution by oversampling rare classes and undersampling frequent ones, then reduces dyadic example creation.  
- Provides theoretical guarantees using local fractional Rademacher complexity, showing ERM remains consistent despite sampling-induced shift and dyadic dependencies.  
- Empirical evaluation on WIKI-Small/50K/100K shows large improvements in memory and runtime while maintaining competitive accuracy and macro F1.  
- Clearly identifies limitations of existing approaches: error propagation in trees, low-rank violations in label embeddings, and dyadic explosion with large K.  
- Theoretical analysis is a major strength, extending prior work to dependent dyadic settings with new generalization bounds (Theorems 1 and 2).  
- Experimental comparisons are comprehensive, benchmarking against strong baselines (OVA, M-SVM, FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree, PD-Sparse).  
- Authors discuss why tree-based and standard OVA/M-SVM methods underperform at extreme K.  
- Weakness: insufficient connection to prior sampling work for imbalanced classification and limited theoretical justification for choosing πk ∝ 1/µk.  
- Weakness: novelty claim could be clearer since dyadic representations and sampling strategies have prior use; need to articulate the unique combination more explicitly.  
- Weakness: experimental ablations are lacking—need analyses showing effects of κ and π and when aggressive sampling helps most.  
- Weakness: candidate selection strategy for prediction is mentioned but its impact on speed–accuracy trade-offs is not discussed.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-motivated, and practically significant contribution deserving acceptance.  
- Recommendation: accept, with suggestions to strengthen novelty articulation, theoretical motivation for π, and add ablation studies.
