Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme/multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem via dyadic example–class pairs and then making this reduction computationally feasible through an aggressive double sampling scheme. Specifically:
- Reduction: Use a joint representation φ(x, y), a scoring function f, and learn h(z) = f(φ(x, y)) − f(φ(x, y′)) on dyadic pairs to minimize a ranking-style 0/1 loss over class comparisons.
- Double sampling (π, κ)-DS: (i) re-sample the training set across classes using class-dependent probabilities πk to counter long-tailed distributions (oversample tail, subsample head), and (ii) for each sampled example, sample only κ “negative” labels y′ instead of all K − 1, shrinking the dyadic dataset.
- Theory: Derive generalization bounds for the full dyadic reduction using fractional/local Rademacher complexities (handling dependence between pairs sharing the same example) and for the double-sampled setting under label distribution shift, with terms depending on π, κ, and K.
- Prediction: Further speed prediction via candidate class selection using nearest class centroids in the dyadic space.
- Experiments: On LSHTC/Wikipedia datasets up to 100K classes, the approach is competitive in accuracy and macro-F1 while using much less time/memory than OVA/M-SVM and being more accurate than tree-based methods; compares also to PD-Sparse.

This builds on prior dyadic reductions and label ranking losses (e.g., Weston & Watkins, AdaBoost.MR), fractional Rademacher/graph cover tools for dependent data, and extreme classification methods (tree/embedding-based, WSABIE, PD-Sparse, FastXML). The main novelty is an explicit double sampling scheme with accompanying bounds, and a simple, low-dimensional φ tailored to text.

Strengths
- Addresses a central bottleneck of dyadic reductions—quadratic blow-up—via a simple, effective sampling recipe that practitioners can implement.
- Provides nontrivial generalization guarantees in the presence of interdependent dyads and distribution shift induced by sampling, leveraging fractional/local Rademacher complexity; this is technically interesting and relevant.
- Very good empirical story on large-scale text classification: strong trade-offs between accuracy, time, and memory; shows feasibility at K=100K where OVA/M-SVM become impractical.
- Sensible candidate selection at prediction further reduces costs; the full pipeline is pragmatic.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Missing details hamper reproducibility: the exact φ features (the paper mentions p=10 and alludes to Table 1), the learning algorithm for f (loss, regularization, optimizer), and key hyperparameters (π schedule, κ, and candidate set size q) are not specified in the main paper. Ablations on π, κ, and q are essential.
- The theory is stated but its implications are not made concrete. There are no explicit rates or comparisons to existing bounds (e.g., dependence on K, κ, and class imbalance) and no empirical validation of the theoretical predictions (e.g., how accuracy varies with κ). The constants α, β (involving 1/πy) may become large for head classes; practical guidance is needed.
- The candidate selection step is only heuristically motivated. There is no analysis of its recall (probability that the true class is in the candidate set) or of accuracy vs q. Also, centroids in the dyadic space are not fully specified given φ is pairwise.
- Experimental scope is limited to text; claims about generality would be strengthened by at least one non-text dataset (recommendation or images) or by learning φ. Comparisons to methods closer in spirit (e.g., WSABIE/LEML/SLEEC or more recent sampled-softmax/negative-sampling rankers) are missing; PD-Sparse and tree methods are relevant but not exhaustive.
- Converting multi-label datasets to multi-class by duplicating instances may bias comparisons to multi-label methods optimized for different metrics; a short discussion of implications is needed.

Quality
- Technically sound at a high level. The use of graph cover/fractional Rademacher/local complexity for dependent dyads is appropriate, and the bounds plausibly justify ERM consistency under sampling. However, without proofs in the main paper and without empirical validation of bound-driven design choices (π, κ), the theoretical contribution feels under-leveraged.
- Experiments are promising but need: (i) ablations on κ and q, (ii) sensitivity to π choices (beyond π ∝ 1/μ), (iii) accuracy/time breakdown with/without candidate selection, and (iv) clarity on the binary learner and training details.

Clarity
- Generally well organized, though notation is dense. The(π, κ)-DS algorithm is clear. Provide in the main text: the list of φ features; the exact classifier used (loss, regularization), training hyperparameters, and κ, q settings; and a brief description of the dependency graph and its fractional cover.
- Improve the description of centroid-based candidate selection in the context of a pairwise φ, and report q and its effect on performance.

Originality
- Combining aggressive sampling in both example and negative-label dimensions with dyadic reduction, and providing dependent-data generalization bounds for the sampled setting, is novel to my knowledge. The high-level ideas relate to negative sampling and prior dependent-learning bounds, but the package is new.

Significance
- Practical significance is high for extreme text classification: the method seems to offer a very favorable accuracy–efficiency trade-off and is simple to deploy. Theoretical significance is moderate: sound but would benefit from clearer takeaways and tighter links to practice.

Questions for the authors
- What binary loss and optimizer were used to learn f? Is it linear SVM/logistic? Any regularization? How is class imbalance handled within the binary task?
- What are the concrete values of κ and q used per dataset, and how sensitive are results to them? Please include ablations.
- How exactly are class centroids defined when φ is pairwise? Are you averaging document embeddings and then constructing φ(x, centroid_y), or averaging φ components?
- Did you try importance weighting to correct for the π-induced shift (e.g., weights ∝ ηy/πy as suggested by α)? Any effect on performance?
- Could you report recall of the candidate selection (probability that true class ∈ Yx) vs q?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include φ details and training setup in the main paper; add ablation/sensitivity analyses and an experiment quantifying the effect of candidate selection.
- Provide an explicit corollary with rates highlighting dependence on κ, K, and class imbalance (μ). Discuss practical settings of π to control α, β.
- Compare to at least one ranking/embedding method trained with sampled negatives (e.g., WSABIE/LEML) on the same multi-class conversion.
- Consider reporting Precision@k in addition to accuracy/MaF1, given extreme settings.
- Clarify fairness of the multi-label-to-multi-class conversion for baselines originally designed for multi-label.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, scalable method with strong empirical performance at K up to 100K.
- Solid theoretical treatment of dependency and distribution shift in dyadic sampling.
- Addresses an important and practical problem.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing crucial experimental details and ablations; theory–practice linkage incomplete.
- Limited baseline coverage and domain scope; candidate selection not rigorously evaluated.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on adding ablations and clarifying implementation details. The approach is practical and likely to be used; tightening the empirical and expository aspects would make it a solid NeurIPS contribution.
