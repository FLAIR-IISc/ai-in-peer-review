This paper addresses the problem of multi-class classification, specifically focusing on scaling to a large number of classes. The authors propose an aggressive double sampling strategy to reduce the computational burden associated with the dyadic representation approach. They also provide theoretical generalization bounds for their method. While the problem addressed is important and relevant to the NIPS community, I find the paper lacking in several crucial aspects, primarily concerning the thoroughness of the empirical evaluation and the justification of the proposed method against established baselines.

The core idea of aggressive sampling – balancing class distributions and reducing the number of dyadic examples – is intuitively reasonable. The theoretical analysis, while presenting generalization bounds, feels somewhat detached from the practical implications and benefits claimed. The connection between the derived bounds and the observed performance improvements remains unclear. The reliance on Local Rademacher Complexity, while not inherently flawed, adds complexity without a clear demonstration of superior insight compared to more standard generalization analyses.

However, my primary concern lies with the experimental validation. While the authors compare against several baselines (OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PD-Sparse), the evaluation lacks depth. The description of hyperparameter tuning is brief, and the presentation of results feels selective. The claim that the proposed method consistently outperforms others, particularly in terms of both runtime and memory usage *and* predictive performance, is not fully convincing. The bar plots in Fig. 3 are helpful, but the discussion remains superficial. For example, the authors state tree-based methods suffer from cascading errors, but do not deeply analyze *why* their method avoids this pitfall or how much performance is lost in comparison.

Furthermore, the feature mapping (φ) used in the experiments seems somewhat arbitrary. While the authors acknowledge its dependence on the text classification setting, they don't explore the sensitivity of their method to different feature representations. The claim that a similar mapping could be learned for other applications feels unsubstantiated without demonstrating its adaptability. 

The choice of datasets (LSHTC) is appropriate, but the reporting of results is sometimes vague. For instance, the discussion regarding the WIKI-100K dataset mentions memory consumption exceeding a terabyte for OVA and M-SVM, but doesn't provide similar memory usage numbers for their proposed method or other baselines, making it difficult to assess the claimed efficiency gains objectively.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses an important problem: scaling multi-class classification.
*   The aggressive sampling idea is intuitively appealing and offers a potential for computational efficiency.
*   Theoretical analysis provides generalization bounds (although their practical relevance is unclear).

**Weaknesses:**

*   Insufficiently thorough experimental evaluation; lacks detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   The reported results, while promising, are not convincingly demonstrated to consistently outperform established baselines across various metrics and datasets.
*   The feature mapping is somewhat arbitrary and its impact on performance is not fully explored.
*   The theoretical analysis feels detached from the empirical results and doesn't offer significant insights beyond standard generalization bounds.
*   Limited discussion of the relationship between the theoretical guarantees and observed performance.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Potentially efficient solution for large-scale multi-class classification.
*   Provides theoretical justification for the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigorous and comprehensive empirical validation.
*   The performance gains are not demonstrably significant or consistent.
*   The theoretical contribution is incremental and doesn't provide substantial new insights.
*   Insufficiently detailed comparison to strong baselines.




**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the authors tackle an important problem and present a potentially interesting approach, the lack of a robust and detailed empirical evaluation raises serious concerns about the practical significance of their findings. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and compelling contribution to the field. I would encourage the authors to significantly expand their experimental evaluation, providing a more thorough comparison to established baselines, exploring the sensitivity of their method to different parameters and feature representations, and clarifying the connection between their theoretical analysis and observed performance. A more convincing demonstration of consistent and substantial improvements is needed for this work to be suitable for NIPS.
