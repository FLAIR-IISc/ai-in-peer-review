Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles extreme/multiclass classification by reducing it to binary via dyadic example–class pairs, but avoids the O(mK) blow-up by an aggressive double sampling scheme: (i) class-balanced sampling of training examples with probabilities πk (oversampling rare, subsampling frequent classes), and (ii) for each sampled example, sampling only κ “impostor” classes to form dyads. A simple joint feature map φ(x, y) is used (10 features inspired by learning-to-rank, including distance to class centroid), and prediction optionally preselects q candidate classes via nearest centroids before scoring.

On the theory side, the paper studies generalization under dependencies induced by dyads and under the distribution shift introduced by sampling. It builds on fractional Rademacher complexity for dependent data and local Rademacher complexities to derive: (a) a generalization bound for the full dyadic reduction (Theorem 1), and (b) a bound for the doubly-sampled setting (Theorem 2) with dependence on imbalance/sampling factors α, β, the number of sampled negatives κ, and sample sizes. Experiments on LSHTC DMOZ/Wikipedia variants (up to 100K classes) compare to OVA/M-SVM (LibLinear), tree-based methods (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree), and PD-Sparse, showing favorable time–memory–accuracy trade-offs. Code is released.

This builds on a line of work on multiclass-to-binary dyadic reductions and dependent-data generalization bounds (e.g., Usunier et al., Amini & Ralaivola), tree/embedding methods for extreme classification (FastXML, PfastReXML, label embeddings), and practical negative sampling ideas common in ranking/sampled softmax. The novelty is combining double sampling with dyadic reduction together with consistency guarantees under dependency and shifted class distributions.

Quality
- Theory: The use of fractional/local Rademacher complexities to handle interdependent dyads is appropriate and, to my knowledge, novel in this specific setting. Theorems 1–2 are plausible and clearly state the roles of α = maxy ηy/πy, β = maxy 1/πy, K, κ, M. However, the main text does not provide nonasymptotic upper bounds or interpretable scaling for RT(·)(`◦H) in terms of φ and κ, nor empirical validation of bound tightness. The candidate selection heuristic at test time lacks theoretical analysis (could misrank the true class if it is excluded).
- Method: The (π, κ)-DS strategy is simple and practical. The class-rebalancing rationale is sound for long-tailed label distributions. The dyadic φ is described at a high level; specifics are deferred to a missing table.
- Experiments: The large-scale results are valuable; demonstrating 100K-class viability with good accuracy and memory/runtime is compelling. That said, the evaluation is limited to text; LSHTC2’s multilabel-to-multiclass conversion by replication could bias results; and there is little ablation (no sensitivity to π/κ/q, no analysis of candidate selection vs full scoring, no study of φ variants). Some strong extreme classification baselines are absent (perhaps due to timeframe), and OVA/M-SVM are unsurprisingly infeasible at 100K.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but has issues: typos and formatting glitches, incomplete/inconsistent references, and missing details in the main text (e.g., the full definition of φ’s 10 features, hyperparameters moved to appendix, figures/tables heavily referenced but not fully described). The algorithms are understandable, but the interplay between training sampling and test-time candidate selection could be clearer.

Originality
The high-level ideas (class balancing; negative sampling; dyadic pairwise reductions) are known, but their specific combination with a dependency-aware, local fractional Rademacher analysis for both the full and sampled dyadic problems appears original. The theoretical contribution is the most novel aspect.

Significance
The problem is important. Practitioners need methods that scale while remaining accurate under long-tailed label distributions. This work provides a practical recipe with supporting theory and code. The impact would be stronger with broader modality coverage and deeper empirical analysis (ablation, sensitivity, candidate selection accuracy/speed trade-offs). As is, it advances the state of practice in text extreme classification and contributes new theoretical tools for dependent reductions.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses an important scalability/imbalance challenge in extreme multiclass classification.
- Nontrivial theoretical contribution: generalization bounds for dyadic reductions with dependencies and under double sampling; consistency despite distribution shift.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with code release; scales to 100K classes with favorable time/memory.
- Competitive accuracy/MaF1 versus strong baselines; better trade-off than tree methods on large datasets.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Empirical evaluation is narrow (only text; multilabel-to-multiclass replication), with limited ablations and no analysis of sensitivity to π, κ, and candidate set size q.
- Candidate selection is heuristic and lacks theoretical guarantees or empirical misselection analysis.
- Some key details (exact φ features, practical computation of centroids and preselection cost) are underspecified in the main text.
- Comparison set omits some relevant extreme classifiers; OVA/M-SVM infeasibility is expected and less informative.
- Theoretical bounds are not instantiated numerically; no guidance on setting π/κ from the bounds.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are performance and bounds to the choice of π (e.g., πk ∝ 1/µk vs capped schedules) and κ? Can you report ablations?
- How is the candidate class preselection implemented computationally, and what fraction of true labels are excluded as q varies?
- Can you provide the full feature definitions for φ and discuss portability beyond text (e.g., images, recommendation)?
- Do you reweight losses during training to correct for the class-distribution shift, or is the consistency entirely via analysis (α, β)?

Overall assessment
The paper meets the quality bar on theory and shows promising practical impact, but the empirical section and clarity could be stronger. If the committee prioritizes novel generalization analysis for scalable multiclass reductions, this is a solid contribution. If empirical breadth and ablation are weighted more, it is borderline. I lean slightly positive given the theoretical novelty and demonstrated scalability.
