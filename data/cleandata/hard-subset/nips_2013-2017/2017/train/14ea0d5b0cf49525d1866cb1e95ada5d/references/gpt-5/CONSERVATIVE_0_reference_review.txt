Summary
The paper tackles large-scale multiclass (including extreme) classification via a reduction to a dyadic binary problem. Instead of constructing all m(K−1) pairs, the authors propose a double sampling scheme: (i) a class-balanced resampling of training examples (π) to counter long-tail distributions by oversampling minority classes and subsampling majority ones, and (ii) for each retained example, sampling only κ “adversarial” negative classes. The resulting reduced dyadic set is used to train a single binary classifier over a hand-crafted joint example/class feature map φ. They derive generalization bounds under dependency via fractional/local Rademacher complexities, and propose a prediction-time candidate selection that evaluates only q nearest-centroid classes. Experiments on LSHTC datasets (Wikipedia/DMOZ-derived, up to 100k classes) show improved time/memory and competitive accuracy/MaF1 against OVA, multi-class SVM, PD-Sparse, FastXML, PfastReXML, and RecallTree.

Relation to prior work
The dyadic reduction and pairwise loss framing connect to earlier work on multi-class to binary reductions and label ranking (e.g., Usunier & Amini; Weston & Watkins; AdaBoost.MR), and dependent-sample generalization via fractional covers (Janson; Ralaivola & Amini). The sampling over negatives resembles negative sampling/sampled softmax ideas common in extreme classification and recommendation. The tree/embedding baselines are standard. The work most closely related appears to be Joshi et al. (2015) on binary reduction for large-scale multiclass, and PD-Sparse for extreme classification.

Strengths
- Practicality/scalability: The double sampling is simple, yields substantial reductions in memory and training time, and the candidate selection further reduces prediction cost.
- Theoretical effort: The authors attempt to account for dependence induced by dyads and class distribution shift from sampling, employing fractional and local Rademacher complexities and providing bounds (Theorems 1–2).
- Empirical scope: Experiments up to 100k classes with comparisons to several recognized baselines; macro-F1 reported to mitigate imbalance effects; code purportedly available.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality (soundness and evidence)
- Evaluation protocol: Converting multi-label LSHTC data to multi-class “by replicating the instances belonging to different class labels” is problematic. This creates identical feature vectors with conflicting labels, which is ill-posed for multiclass methods that assume a single true label per x, and can unfairly penalize baselines (OVA/M-SVM) not designed for such duplication. A principled conversion (e.g., selecting one label per instance or using genuine multiclass datasets) is needed. Results on such multi-label-to-multiclass conversions are hard to interpret and may not reflect true multiclass performance.
- Missing ablations: There is no sensitivity analysis for κ (negative class sampling), π (class resampling), and q (candidate set size). The method’s performance-computation trade-offs hinge on these, yet there is no study of accuracy vs κ, robustness to π choices, or impact of candidate selection on accuracy/runtime.
- Baseline coverage/fairness: Comparisons omit closely related reductions (e.g., a non-sampled dyadic baseline and/or prior dyadic sampling approaches), and strong extreme classification methods contemporaneous with the time frame (e.g., DiSMEC). Also absent are importance-weighted OVA/logistic regression with class-balanced sampling or sampled softmax/negatives, which would be natural comparators.
- Theoretical bounds: The bounds depend on α=max_y η_y/π_y and β=max_y 1/π_y, which can be large under aggressive resampling, risking vacuity. There is no numerical instantiation to demonstrate non-vacuousness under practical π, κ, K, m. Moreover, the training procedure does not appear to use importance weighting to correct sampling bias implied by α, yet the analysis assumes consistency under shifted distribution. This disconnect should be reconciled.
- Reproducibility/detail: The base learner (loss, regularizer) used for the binary classifier is not clearly specified in the main text; φ is described as 10 IR-inspired features but the exact definitions are deferred; hyperparameters are in appendix. For experts to reproduce results, more in-text detail would help.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable but key components (exact φ features, training loss/optimizer, complexity analysis with respect to m, K, κ, p) should be stated more concretely in the main text.
- The prediction “nearest centroids” step could be explained more precisely (space used, normalization, q selection).

Originality
- The combination of class-balanced resampling + negative class sampling + dyadic reduction is a straightforward aggregation of known ideas. The theoretical angle via local fractional Rademacher complexities is the most novel aspect, but the presentation does not convincingly translate into actionable guidance (e.g., how to set π, κ to tighten bounds). Candidate selection via nearest centroids is a common heuristic.

Significance
- If validated under a sound protocol, the approach could be practically useful as a scalable reduction. However, given the concerns about the evaluation setup, missing ablations, and lack of comparison to the most pertinent alternatives, it is difficult to conclude that the method advances the state of the art.

Additional issues
- Anonymity: Linking a public repository in the submission can compromise double-blind review. Please ensure anonymity compliance.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you justify the multi-label to multi-class conversion by duplicating instances? Can you provide results on truly multiclass datasets or alternative conversions (e.g., selecting a single label) and report per-dataset ablations?
2) What binary loss/regularizer/optimizer are used? Are any importance weights applied to correct for π?
3) Please include ablations for κ, π, and q, including accuracy/runtime trade-offs.
4) Can you compare against (i) a full dyadic baseline (no κ sampling), (ii) a dyadic sampling method from prior work, and (iii) sampled-softmax/OVA with negative sampling and/or class-balanced weighting?
5) Provide empirical evidence that the generalization bounds are non-vacuous for your chosen π, κ (numerical bound values).

Pros
- Simple, scalable reduction with apparent time/memory gains
- Attempts to address dependence and sampling shift theoretically
- Competitive empirical results on large label spaces

Cons
- Questionable evaluation protocol (multi-label→multiclass duplication)
- Missing key ablations and pertinent baselines
- Theoretical bounds may be vacuous under aggressive sampling; no practical guidance
- Some clarity/reproducibility gaps; possible anonymity breach

Recommendation
Score: 4 (borderline reject)
Confidence: 4/5

While the direction is promising and the practical gains are appealing, the methodological and evaluation concerns prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. Addressing the protocol issues, adding thorough ablations and stronger baselines, and tightening the empirical-theoretical link would significantly strengthen the paper.
