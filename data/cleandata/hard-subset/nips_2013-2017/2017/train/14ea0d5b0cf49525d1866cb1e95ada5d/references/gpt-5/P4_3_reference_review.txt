Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses extreme/multi-class classification by reducing it to a binary problem via dyadic representations of example–class pairs, then aggressively subsampling both training instances and per-example negative classes. The core idea is a “double sampling” scheme: (i) reweight/redistribute the class frequencies by oversampling minority classes and subsampling majority classes using probabilities πk (to mitigate long-tailed distributions), and (ii) for each retained example, construct only κ dyadic pairs by sampling κ adversarial classes rather than using all K − 1 negatives. Training is then done as binary classification on the sampled dyadic data. Prediction uses a candidate selection mechanism that restricts scoring to q nearest class centroids in the dyadic space.

On the theory side, the paper revisits the dependency structure induced by dyadic expansion (multiple pairs share the same x), and derives generalization bounds that combine fractional graph covers (to handle dependent examples) with local Rademacher complexities (to incorporate variance/second-order information), yielding bounds for both the standard dyadic expansion (Theorem 1) and the proposed double-sampled setting (Theorem 2). The latter accounts for distribution shift introduced by π and for subsampling via κ, through multiplicative factors α, β and terms that scale with (K − 1)/(Mκ).

Experimentally, the approach is evaluated on LSHTC/Dmoz/Wikipedia subsets with up to 100k classes, compared against OVA and multi-class SVM (Liblinear), tree/embedding-based extreme classifiers (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree), and PD-Sparse. The proposed method shows competitive or better accuracy/Macro-F1 while using substantially less memory and training/prediction time on large K. The dyadic mapping φ uses 10 similarity/ranking features between document and class “mega-document”/centroid; candidate selection exploits precomputed class centroids.

This work builds directly on dyadic reduction methods and theory for dependent examples (e.g., Usunier & Amini 2006; Joshi et al. 2015) and is related to sampled negative ranking/embedding methods such as WSABIE and WARP, and tree-based/embedding-based extreme classification (FastXML, PfastReXML). The novelty is in the combination of aggressive double sampling with new bounds tailored to both dependence and class-distribution shift.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The generalization bounds are plausible given the stated tools: fractional graph covers for dependency and local Rademacher complexities for fast rates. The presence of α = max_y ηy/πy and β = max_y 1/πy appropriately reflects the impact of sampling design on risk inflation. The dependence on κ and M is explicit.
- The reduction from multiclass ranking loss to a binary dyadic problem is standard, and the analysis carefully accounts for interdependence among dyads sharing the same example.
- However, important training details are missing or under-specified: the actual classifier f (linear? margin-based?), the loss optimized (hinge/logistic vs 0–1 used for analysis), and optimization hyperparameters are not fully described in the main text. The mapping φ is only sketched; the referenced Table 1 is critical to reproducing results but absent from the main narrative here.
- The candidate selection step is reasonable, but an analysis of its impact (approximation vs exact argmax) and guarantees is not provided.
- The experimental protocol for converting multi-label to multi-class by duplicating instances can bias class balance and evaluation; the paper should justify this choice and assess its effect.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but there are several typographical issues, truncated lines, and missing details (e.g., full feature list, explicit training objective). Presentation of Theorems is concise but would benefit from more intuition on the terms and a brief sketch of proofs in the main text.
- Algorithm descriptions are clear at a high level. The definition of π and how it is set in practice is provided (inverse-frequency), but sensitivity is not analyzed.
- References are sometimes incomplete.

Originality
- The double sampling strategy (balancing classes via π and sampling only κ negatives per example) is conceptually related to existing negative sampling approaches, but the theoretical treatment that jointly handles (i) dyadic dependence, (ii) class distribution shift, and (iii) local Rademacher complexities appears novel.
- The use of a compact, similarity-based dyadic feature set for text is pragmatic; less novel than the theoretical contribution.

Significance
- The approach targets a central problem in extreme classification and demonstrates practical benefits (reduced time/memory, competitive accuracy/Macro-F1) at large K. The bounds may guide design of π and κ and are of independent interest for learning with interdependent data under sampling.
- The reliance on task-specific φ may limit generality; however, the framework can accommodate learned φ.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for sampling both examples and dyads; strong scalability benefits.
- Theoretical bounds tailored to the proposed sampling under dependence and class-shift.
- Competitive empirical results on large public datasets with reasonable baselines.
- Simple prediction-time acceleration via candidate selection.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient detail on the learning objective, model class F, and optimization procedure.
- No ablation/sensitivity analysis for π, κ, and q; no study of trade-offs vs accuracy.
- Fairness concerns in comparisons: the proposed method uses engineered dyadic features involving class mega-documents/centroids, while baselines likely use raw BOW features. This may advantage the proposed method; a variant using the same raw features for all methods or learned φ would strengthen claims.
- The conversion from multi-label to multi-class is nonstandard and may distort comparisons; precision@k or other extreme classification measures could be more informative.
- Limited discussion of failure modes; no theoretical or empirical analysis of candidate selection errors.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact form of f and the loss used during training? Is it linear with hinge or logistic loss? How is regularization chosen?
- How sensitive are results to κ and π? Can you report accuracy/time/memory as κ varies and for alternative π choices (e.g., square-root balancing)?
- What is q during prediction, and how does candidate selection affect accuracy relative to scoring all classes?
- Can you provide comparisons where all methods use the same document representation (e.g., raw features), or alternatively equip baselines with similar class-side features?
- How does the method perform on the original multi-label setting with standard metrics (P@k, nDCG@k)?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important scalability issue in extreme classification.
- Provides nontrivial, tailored generalization bounds under dependence and sampling.
- Demonstrates strong practical efficiency and competitive accuracy on large datasets.
- Method is simple and broadly applicable given a dyadic mapping.

Cons:
- Experimental evaluation lacks ablations and fairness controls for feature representations.
- Some methodological details are missing; reproducibility depends on supplementary material.
- Limited theoretical/empirical treatment of the candidate selection step.
- Evaluation protocol (multi-label to multi-class conversion) may not reflect standard extreme classification practice.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The theoretical contribution and practical scalability are valuable; strengthening the experimental rigor (ablations, fair feature parity, candidate selection analysis) would solidify the paper.
