## Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to address the challenges of extreme multi-class classification, specifically focusing on improving the scalability and performance of the dyadic representation method. The proposed aggressive double sampling strategy appears promising, and the theoretical analysis providing generalization bounds is a valuable contribution. However, the experimental evaluation, while extensive, lacks a more thorough comparison against strong, recent baselines and a deeper ablation study of the proposed method’s components. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on addressing the concerns outlined below.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the well-known limitations of the one-vs-all approach for extreme multi-class classification – computational cost and class imbalance. It positions itself within the existing landscape of solutions, contrasting against tree-based and embedding-based methods, as well as previous dyadic reduction techniques. The authors correctly identify the key bottleneck in dyadic methods: the construction of all possible dyadic examples. Their solution, aggressive double sampling, cleverly addresses this by intelligently subsampling and oversampling classes to balance the training data and reduce the number of dyadic pairs. The connection to existing theoretical work on Rademacher complexity and graph covering is appropriately highlighted, building a solid foundation for their analysis.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The aggressive double sampling strategy is a novel contribution. It's a pragmatic and intuitive approach to addressing the computational burden of dyadic reduction.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The derivation of generalization bounds based on local fractional Rademacher complexity is a significant strength.  It provides theoretical justification for the effectiveness of their method, even with the induced distribution shift and dependencies. The connection to graph covering theory is well-established and supports the bound.
* **Scalability Focus:** The paper directly addresses the scalability issues that plague many multi-class classification techniques. The experiments on datasets with up to 100,000 classes demonstrate a clear advantage in terms of memory usage and runtime.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear and the explanation of the method is understandable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on DMOZ and Wikipedia datasets are comprehensive, demonstrating consistent improvements over several baselines.

**3. Weaknesses & Areas for Improvement:**

* **Baseline Comparison:** While the authors compare against a range of baselines, several recently published, strong methods are missing. Specifically, methods leveraging neural embeddings (e.g., FastText, or specialized embeddings for hierarchical classification) and more recent sparse methods designed for extreme classification (beyond PD-Sparse) should be included.  The claim that PD-Sparse is consistently outperformed needs further scrutiny against these newer methods.
* **Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study of the (π, κ)-DS algorithm is necessary.  What is the impact of the sampling probabilities (π) – is a uniform sampling also competitive? How sensitive is the performance to the choice of κ?  Exploring different strategies for setting π and κ would provide valuable insights.
* **Feature Mapping:** The paper mentions the joint example/class representation as being crucial, but the choice of the 10 features feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks justification beyond being inspired by learning to rank. Exploring different feature mapping techniques (learned representations, different similarity measures) and their impact on performance would be valuable.
* **Candidate Selection:**  The candidate selection step is introduced as a potential optimization but isn't thoroughly evaluated. How much speedup does it provide, and at what cost in accuracy?  A more detailed analysis of this component is needed.
* **Generalization Bounds – Practical Implications:** While the theoretical bounds are impressive, discussing their practical implications (e.g., how to choose parameters to satisfy the bounds) would make them more impactful.



**4. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses an important problem in large-scale multi-class classification.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated solution.
* Provides a strong theoretical foundation with generalization bounds.
* Demonstrates promising experimental results on large datasets.
* Scalable solution with lower memory footprint and runtime.

**Con:**

* Baseline comparison could be more comprehensive.
* Lack of detailed ablation study on the proposed method.
* Feature mapping choice seems arbitrary.
* Candidate selection needs more thorough evaluation.
* Practical implications of the generalization bounds could be explored further.



**5. Specific Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Page 3:**  "even-though" should be "even though."
* **Page 7, Theorem 2:**  Clarify the meaning of α and β in the theorem statement.
* **Page 8:** The discussion on the generalization bounds feels a bit cursory. Adding a brief example of how to use these bounds to estimate the required sample size would be helpful.
* **Experimental Setup:**  Please provide a link to the repository mentioned in the footnote.
* **Results:**  Consider presenting results in a more visually informative way, perhaps with error bars.




**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of extreme multi-class classification. The proposed aggressive sampling strategy is a clever and effective approach, and the theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for its use. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly strengthening the baseline comparison and adding a more thorough ablation study, will significantly improve the paper's impact and increase confidence in its claims. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
