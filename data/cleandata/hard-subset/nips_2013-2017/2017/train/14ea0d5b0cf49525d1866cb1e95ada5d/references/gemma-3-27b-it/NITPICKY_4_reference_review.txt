This paper proposes an aggressive double sampling strategy to address the computational and class imbalance challenges in multi-class to binary reduction for large-scale classification, particularly in text classification. The core idea is to oversample minority classes and subsample majority classes before constructing the dyadic representation, coupled with a theoretical analysis using local Rademacher complexity to justify the approach. While the motivation is sound and the problem setting relevant, the paper suffers from a remarkable number of shortcomings, ranging from minor stylistic issues to more substantial flaws in presentation and justification.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial problem in extreme multi-class classification – scalability and class imbalance.
*   **Novelty (Partial):** The aggressive sampling approach is a reasonable heuristic to improve efficiency and potentially accuracy.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The attempt to provide generalization bounds is commendable, albeit complex and not entirely convincing (see weaknesses).

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally dense and poorly organized. Equations are introduced without sufficient context, and the flow between sections feels disjointed. Notation is inconsistent (e.g., use of both *xy* and *x<sub>yi</sub>* to denote the same thing). The introduction is overly verbose and could be significantly condensed.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The generalization bounds (Theorems 1 & 2) are presented as major contributions, but the connection to practical benefit is unclear. The introduction of graph covering and local Rademacher complexity feels unnecessarily complex and adds little to the intuitive understanding of the proposed method. The conditions and assumptions required for these theorems to hold are not clearly stated or discussed. The reliance on existing results from Janson (2004) and Ralaivola et al. (2015) without adequately explaining their relevance is concerning.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments cover large datasets, the hyperparameter tuning details are relegated to the supplementary material, hindering reproducibility. The justification for choosing specific baselines is weak; the authors state SLEEC and LEML were omitted because they were outperformed by others in a prior work, but this justification feels superficial. The description of the feature mapping (φ) is inadequate. Table 1 is extremely difficult to interpret, and the text doesn’t adequately explain the rationale behind each feature. The claim that the proposed approach is "highly competitive" is not strongly supported by the results. Differences in performance are often marginal, and the runtime improvements seem modest. The figures (particularly Fig. 3) are poorly labelled.
*   **Minor Issues:** Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the manuscript. For example, equation (1) is missing a closing parenthesis. The use of the term "instantaneous loss" is unconventional. “Eq.” should consistently be used instead of alternating between “Eq.” and “Equation”. The sentence structure is often awkward and convoluted.
*   **Lack of Discussion:** There’s a lack of discussion regarding the limitations of the proposed method. For instance, the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of the parameters *π* and *κ* is not addressed.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Introduction:** Overly long and repetitive. The discussion of prior work is a list of papers rather than a synthesized overview. The contribution statement is too verbose.
*   **Section 2.1:** The dyadic transformation *T(S)* is explained insufficiently. Why is this transformation necessary? What are the practical implications of the inter-dependency introduced?
*   **Section 2.2:** The motivation for the Hr class of functions is unclear. The connection between variance and convergence rate is not explained adequately.
*   **Section 2.3:** The candidate selection strategy feels tacked on and not fully integrated into the overall approach. The justification for using class centroids is weak.
*   **Section 3:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. The hyperparameter search space for each baseline is not provided. The choice of evaluation metrics (accuracy and MaF1) is standard, but the justification for selecting these metrics specifically is missing.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper's lack of clarity and numerous minor errors detract significantly from its readability and credibility.
*   The theoretical contributions, while ambitious, are not clearly justified or explained, and their practical relevance is questionable.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
*   The overall presentation is poor, and the paper reads more like a collection of ideas than a cohesive scientific contribution.

**For Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is important and relevant to the NIPS audience.
*   The aggressive sampling strategy is a reasonable heuristic that may offer some benefits in certain scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite addressing a relevant problem, this paper falls significantly short of the quality expected at NIPS. The lack of clarity, theoretical rigor, and a convincing experimental evaluation outweigh the novelty of the proposed approach. The numerous minor errors and inconsistencies further detract from the manuscript's credibility.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. While the authors may have a promising idea, substantial revisions, including a significant rewrite for clarity, a more rigorous justification of the theoretical contributions, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, are required before this work would be suitable for publication at NIPS. I suggest the authors focus on simplifying their presentation, clearly articulating the benefits of their approach, and providing more compelling evidence to support their claims.
