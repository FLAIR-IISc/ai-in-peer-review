This paper tackles the challenging problem of extreme multi-class classification, specifically focusing on scaling one-vs-all approaches. The core idea – aggressive double sampling to reduce the computational burden of the dyadic representation – is intriguing and addresses a key bottleneck in existing methods. The theoretical contribution, providing generalization bounds for this sampled approach using local Rademacher complexity, is a notable strength. The empirical validation on large datasets (DMOZ and Wikipedia) demonstrates the practical benefits, especially in terms of memory usage and runtime, without sacrificing predictive performance.

The paper’s strength lies in its boldness. It doesn’t attempt incremental improvements; instead, it proposes a significant modification to a standard technique with a clear justification and theoretical underpinning. The focus on practical scalability – a critical issue for extreme classification – is commendable. The connection to existing work on Rademacher complexity and graph covering is well-established, and the derivation of new bounds is a valuable contribution. The ablation study showing the impact of the (π, κ)-DS parameters would be beneficial, but is not critical for initial acceptance.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is present, it's dense and might be difficult for some readers to fully grasp without a strong background in statistical learning theory. The experimental section, while comprehensive in terms of datasets, lacks a deeper analysis of *why* the proposed method performs well. A more detailed investigation into the characteristics of the sampled data and how they relate to the observed performance gains would be valuable. Specifically, it would be good to see some analysis on the distribution of classes *after* sampling, and how it compares to the original distribution. The description of the feature mapping (φ) could be more detailed; while the rationale is clear, a slightly more elaborate explanation would enhance clarity. The choice of hyperparameters is mentioned, but the specific values and the tuning process could be elaborated in the main text rather than relegated to the supplementary material. While the claim of consistency in the ERM principle is made, a deeper connection to the observed empirical performance, grounded in the sampling strategy, would add further confidence.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The aggressive double sampling strategy is a genuinely new approach to addressing scalability in extreme multi-class classification.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of generalization bounds, though complex, provides a strong theoretical foundation.
*   **Practical Impact:** The demonstrated improvements in memory usage and runtime, without sacrificing accuracy, are significant.
*   **Addresses a Core Problem:** The paper directly tackles a fundamental challenge in the field: scaling to extremely large numbers of classes.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Complexity:** The mathematical notation and derivations might be inaccessible to some readers.
*   **Limited Insight into *Why* it Works:** The experimental section could benefit from a deeper analysis of the mechanism behind the performance gains.
*   **Feature Mapping Detail:** The description of the feature mapping could be more complete.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and impactful contribution. While the theoretical complexity and lack of in-depth analysis are minor weaknesses, the boldness of the approach and the demonstrated practical benefits outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper has the potential to influence future research in extreme multi-class classification. I recommend **acceptance**, with a request for the authors to consider elaborating on the explanation of the feature mapping and including some additional analysis on the characteristics of the sampled data in the camera-ready version. The focus on scalability and the theoretical grounding make it a particularly valuable contribution to NIPS.
