

# Review of "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper proposes a "doubly-sampled" approach to reduce multi-class classification to binary classification, addressing two key challenges in large-scale multi-class problems: computational intractability with large class counts and class imbalance. The authors introduce a (π, κ)-DS algorithm that first oversamples small classes and subsamples large classes to balance the training set, then reduces the number of constructed dyadic examples.

The paper builds upon the dyadic representation framework established in prior work (e.g., [1, 28, 16]), but proposes aggressive sampling as a means to reduce computational complexity. The authors provide theoretical generalization bounds using local fractional Rademacher complexity to justify their approach, claiming consistency of empirical risk minimization over their transformed training set.

**Strengths:**
The paper clearly identifies a real problem in large-scale multi-class classification. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, and the method is well-motivated by the challenges of class imbalance and computational complexity. The experimental setup is reasonable, using standard datasets and evaluation metrics (accuracy and macro F1).

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is insufficient empirical validation. The authors claim their method is "highly competitive" with state-of-the-art approaches but fail to provide thorough comparison with the most recent and relevant baselines. They exclude SLEEC [5] and LEML [31] without proper justification, stating only that "they have been shown to consistently outperformed by the above mentioned state-of-the-art approaches" without providing evidence. This is a significant omission, as these methods represent the current state of the art in extreme multi-class classification.

The experimental results (Table 3) show that their method performs well on the WIKI-100K dataset, but the comparison is limited to only a few methods (OVA, M-SVM, PD-Sparse, and FastXML). The paper fails to include the most recent and relevant approaches like SLEEC, LEML, and other recent extreme classification methods that would provide a more complete picture of their method's relative performance.

The theoretical contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking. The use of local fractional Rademacher complexity builds directly on [22, 25], and the bounds presented in Theorem 2 appear to be straightforward extensions of prior work rather than novel theoretical insights. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their theoretical results advance the field beyond existing work.

The paper lacks crucial ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component of their method. For instance, it's unclear how much of the observed performance improvement is due to the class-balancing aspect (π) versus the dyadic reduction aspect (κ). Without these experiments, it's difficult to assess the true value of their approach.

The projection function φ for text classification (Section 3.1) is described as "simple" but lacks sufficient detail. The authors mention using "p = 10 features inspired from learning to rank" but don't provide the specific feature set or how these features were selected. This is a significant omission as the quality of the feature representation directly impacts the performance of their method.

The paper also fails to address the most critical question: how does this approach compare to the current state of the art in extreme multi-class classification? The authors claim "the proposed approach is highly competitive" but don't provide convincing evidence that it outperforms the most recent methods. Their claim that their method "is the least affected by long-tailed class distributions" is not sufficiently supported by the experimental results.

**Overall Assessment:**
While the problem addressed is important and the method is well-motivated, the paper's contribution is incremental and lacks sufficient empirical validation against the most relevant baselines. The theoretical analysis is sound but not particularly novel, and the experimental results are insufficient to support the paper's claims of significant improvement over existing approaches. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, detailed ablation studies, and more thorough description of the feature representation.

This work represents a modest incremental improvement to existing dyadic reduction approaches but does not provide compelling evidence that it advances the state of the art in a meaningful way. The paper would be significantly stronger with more thorough empirical validation against the most recent methods in the field and clearer demonstration of the relative contribution of each component of their approach.

**Recommendation:** Reject

**Arguments for rejection:**
- Insufficient empirical validation against relevant baselines (missing key recent methods)
- Theoretical contribution is incremental rather than novel
- Lack of ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component
- Incomplete description of the key feature representation component
- Overstated claims about performance without sufficient evidence

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Well-motivated problem statement
- Technically sound theoretical analysis
- Reasonable experimental setup with standard datasets
- Clear presentation of the method

The paper's main weakness is the lack of convincing evidence that the proposed method represents a meaningful advance over existing approaches. The problem is important, but the paper fails to demonstrate that their solution is substantially better than current methods.
