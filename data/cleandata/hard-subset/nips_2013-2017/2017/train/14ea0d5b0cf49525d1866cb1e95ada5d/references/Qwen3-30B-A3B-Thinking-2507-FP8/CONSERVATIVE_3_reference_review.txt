

# Review for "Aggressive Sampling for Multi-class to Binary Reduction with Applications to Text Classification"

This paper proposes a "doubly-sampled" approach for extreme multi-class classification, addressing the common challenges of computational complexity and class imbalance. The authors build upon the dyadic representation framework for multi-class to binary reduction, introducing an aggressive sampling strategy that oversamples small classes and subsamples large classes to balance the dataset, followed by a reduction in the number of constructed dyadic examples.

## Strengths

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experimental validation. The theoretical contribution (Theorems 1 and 2) attempts to provide generalization bounds that account for the sampling strategy and the inter-dependency between dyadic examples. The experimental section provides a reasonable comparison against several baselines (OVA, M-SVM, RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, PD-Sparse) on three text classification datasets of varying scale (WIKI-Small, WIKI-50K, WIKI-100K). The results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive performance with significantly reduced memory consumption and training time compared to OVA and M-SVM, which become infeasible on large-scale datasets.

## Weaknesses

The primary concern is the limited novelty of the core approach. The "aggressive double sampling" strategy - oversampling small classes and subsampling large classes - is a standard technique for handling class imbalance in machine learning, not a novel contribution for extreme multi-class classification. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how this sampling strategy differs from or improves upon existing class balancing techniques applied to this specific problem.

The theoretical analysis, while technically sound, fails to provide meaningful improvements over existing bounds. The claim that the bounds "implies second-order (i.e. variance) information inducing faster convergence rates" is unsubstantiated. The paper cites [22] as the basis for their local Rademacher complexity analysis, but doesn't clearly demonstrate how their bounds are superior to those in [25] or other existing works. The complexity of Theorem 2 makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the theoretical contribution.

The experimental validation is insufficiently thorough. The authors explicitly state they excluded recent methods (SLEEC [5] and LEML [31]) because they "have been shown to be consistently outperformed by the above mentioned state-of-the-art approaches." However, the paper provides no evidence that these methods were indeed outperformed by the baselines they chose, and the exclusion of these methods weakens the comparative evaluation. The paper also fails to provide statistical significance testing for the performance differences observed.

The paper claims "dramatic improvements" in memory consumption, computational complexity, and accuracy, but the results don't clearly support this. The accuracy and macro F1 scores are competitive with other methods but not substantially better. The main advantage appears to be reduced memory usage and training time, but the paper doesn't clearly establish how much better their method is compared to the most efficient existing approaches (like tree-based methods).

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a method that is an incremental improvement over existing approaches rather than a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical contribution is marginal, the experimental validation is incomplete, and the novelty claims are overstated. The core idea of sampling to address class imbalance is well-established, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that their specific implementation offers substantial advantages over existing techniques.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods (including SLEEC and LEML)
2. Clearer demonstration of how their sampling strategy differs from existing class balancing techniques
3. Stronger justification for the theoretical improvements
4. Statistical significance testing for performance comparisons

As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological soundness and rigorous validation over incremental improvements, I cannot recommend acceptance. While the paper is technically sound and addresses a meaningful problem, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and the evidence provided does not support the claims of substantial advantages over existing methods. The paper would be better suited for a more application-focused venue rather than NIPS, where theoretical and methodological novelty are paramount.

The paper has potential but requires substantial revision to strengthen the theoretical claims, broaden the experimental comparison, and more accurately represent the novelty of the proposed approach.
