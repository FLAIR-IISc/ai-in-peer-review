Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles large-scale multiclass (including extreme) classification by reducing to a binary problem via dyadic example–class representations and learning a single scoring function f over joint features φ(x, y). The key contribution is an aggressive double sampling scheme: (i) class-rebalancing of the training set S to Sπ using per-class sampling probabilities π (oversampling rare, subsampling frequent classes), and (ii) for each kept example, subsampling κ adversarial classes to form a reduced dyadic training set Tκ(Sπ). This dramatically reduces memory and time versus constructing all m(K−1) dyads.

On the theory side, the authors analyze generalization for dependent dyadic data using a fractional cover view of dependencies and local fractional Rademacher complexities. They first give a bound for the full dyadic reduction (Theorem 1), then extend to the double-sampled case (Theorem 2), accounting for distribution shift from π via α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy. They also propose a candidate-selection prediction scheme that restricts scoring to q nearest centroid classes to further speed test-time.

Empirically, on LSHTC text datasets (WIKI-Small, WIKI-50K, WIKI-100K), the method achieves competitive accuracy and macro-F1 with substantially lower runtime and memory compared to OVA and M-SVM, and better accuracy than tree-based extreme classification baselines (FastXML, PfastReXML, RecallTree). PD-Sparse is competitive in accuracy but heavier in memory/runtime. The joint features φ consist of 10 ranking-inspired similarity features between document and class “megadocument,” including distance to class centroid.

This work builds on prior dyadic reductions and pairwise ranking losses (e.g., Weston & Watkins multi-class SVM, AdaBoost.MR; reductions in [16, 28]) and on generalization with dependent examples using fractional covers and (local) Rademacher complexity [14, 22, 25]. Relative novelty lies in the double sampling strategy with analysis under distribution shift and dependencies, and in demonstrating practical scalability for extreme text classification.

Strengths:
- Practical scalability: aggressive sampling reduces dyadic size from O(mK) to O(κ|Sπ|), enabling training at 50K–100K classes with competitive accuracy and low memory.
- Sound focus on class imbalance: rebalancing via π empirically mitigates long-tailed distributions; macro-F1 improvements support this.
- Theoretical contribution: data-dependent generalization bounds for dependent dyadic data with an explicit treatment of sampling-induced shift (α, β), using local fractional Rademacher complexity.
- Clear motivation and alignment with extreme classification needs; candidate selection further improves prediction efficiency.
- Reproducibility: code link provided; datasets standard; hyperparameter tuning described (details in appendix).

Weaknesses:
- Clarity and notation: several typos and inconsistent notation (e.g., ωk set to 1 despite fractional cover definition; some bound terms appear malformed such as “α/log 2δ M”), which hinders precise understanding. The definition of local fractional Rademacher complexity is not fully self-contained.
- Missing training details: the paper does not clearly state the surrogate loss and optimizer used for f (hinge/logistic/linear model), regularization, and how φ is normalized. This is needed to reproduce and interpret results and to connect the theory (0/1 loss) to practice (surrogates).
- Limited ablations: no sensitivity analysis for κ and π, nor study of the accuracy–time tradeoff with test-time candidate size q. The contribution would be strengthened by curves showing performance vs. κ, π-schedule, and q.
- Feature engineering dependence: φ is hand-crafted for text; generality to non-text domains is asserted but not validated. Comparison to learned joint embeddings (e.g., WSABIE-style) on text would contextualize this choice.
- Dataset handling: converting multilabel datasets to multiclass by duplicating instances may alter distributions; results might not reflect multilabel extreme classification, which is common for LSHTC. No multilabel metrics are reported.
- Comparisons: while many baselines are included, some strong XMC baselines (contemporary or slightly later) are not compared; nevertheless, the chosen baselines are reasonable for the paper’s timeframe.

Quality:
- The empirical study is broad and uses appropriate metrics (accuracy, macro-F1), with meaningful runtime and memory reporting. However, missing implementation details, ablations, and some unclear theory terms detract from completeness.
- The theory is plausible and relevant; treating dependencies via fractional covers and local complexities is appropriate. Still, presentation gaps (definitions, constants, and term scalings) and the lack of proof sketches make it hard to verify rigorously within the paper. Discussion of how r is chosen (e.g., fixed-point/localization) is missing.

Clarity:
- The high-level narrative is clear. Algorithm pseudocode is helpful. However, notation inconsistencies and missing details (surrogate loss, feature table reference, parameter choices) reduce clarity. Proofs are deferred; more intuition would help.

Originality:
- The double-sampling strategy for dyadic reduction with class rebalancing and the corresponding analysis under dependency and distribution shift appear novel relative to [16, 25]. The candidate-selection idea is pragmatic but not new.

Significance:
- The approach offers a practical route to scale pairwise reductions to extreme settings with competitive accuracy, which is valuable for text classification. The theoretical treatment may interest researchers working on learning with dependent data.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a real and challenging scaling issue with a simple, effective method.
- Competitive empirical results on large benchmarks with favorable resource usage.
- Theoretical analysis tailored to the proposed sampling and dependency structure.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Presentation and notation issues; some bound terms appear erroneous or undefined.
- Insufficient experimental ablations and missing training details limit reproducibility and insight.
- Feature engineering limits generality; no validation beyond text; no multilabel evaluation on LSHTC.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the learning objective for f (loss, regularization, model class) and optimization details.
- Fix notation and typos, and ensure all bound terms are correctly stated and defined; provide intuition and a brief proof sketch for Theorems 1–2, including how r is set in localized bounds.
- Add ablations on κ, π, and q, showing accuracy/runtime/memory tradeoffs; analyze sensitivity to long-tail severity.
- Report results with and without candidate selection to quantify its effect.
- Compare to a learned embedding baseline for φ; consider evaluating on a multilabel metric or a purely multiclass dataset.
- Discuss asymptotic time/memory complexity in terms of m, K, κ, and |Sπ|.

Overall, the paper proposes a useful and scalable approach with promising results and a relevant theoretical framing, but it would benefit from clearer presentation and more thorough experimental analysis.
