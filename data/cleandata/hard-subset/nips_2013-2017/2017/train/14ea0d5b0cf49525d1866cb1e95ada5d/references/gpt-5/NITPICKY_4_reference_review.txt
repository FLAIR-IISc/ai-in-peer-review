Summary
The paper tackles extreme/multi-class classification by reducing the problem to binary via dyadic example–class pairs, but aggressively downsamples to keep computation tractable. The method has two sampling steps: (i) class-balanced sampling of training examples with probabilities πk (oversampling rare classes, subsampling frequent ones), and (ii) per-example negative sampling of κ adversarial classes to construct dyadic pairs. A nearest-centroid candidate selection is used at prediction time to avoid scoring all K classes. On the theory side, the paper derives generalization bounds for the dyadic reduction with dependent pairs using fractional Rademacher complexity and local Rademacher techniques (Theorem 1), and extends this to the doubly-sampled setting (Theorem 2) with parameters α and β that depend on π and the original class proportions. Experiments on LSHTC/Wikipedia subsets compare to OVA, multi-class SVM (LibLinear), RecallTree, FastXML, PfastReXML, and PD-Sparse, showing favorable time/memory trade-offs and competitive accuracy/MaF1. The joint representation φ uses 10 IR-inspired features including distance to class centroids.

Relation to prior work
The paper’s reduction is related to prior dyadic reductions [Usunier et al. 2005; Joshi et al. 2015] and pairwise/ranking losses (e.g., AdaBoost.MR, Weston & Watkins SVM). The negative sampling idea (κ ≪ K) parallels widespread practice in ranking/contrastive learning. Tree/embedding baselines (FastXML, PfastReXML, label embedding) and sparse OVA (PD-Sparse) are appropriate comparators, though the paper excludes other embedding methods (LEML, SLEEC) citing prior results. The theoretical part builds on fractional chromatic covers [Janson 2004], Rademacher complexity with dependent data [Usunier et al.], and local Rademacher complexities [Bartlett et al.].

Strengths
- Practical idea: combining class rebalancing with negative sampling within a dyadic reduction is sensible and likely impactful for long-tailed label distributions.
- Clear computational benefits: complexity scales with κ|Sπ| rather than m(K−1); prediction uses candidate selection (q) to reduce scoring costs.
- Theoretical attempt: extending generalization bounds to the dependent dyadic setting with local Rademacher complexity is ambitious and, if correct, would be a meaningful contribution.
- Empirical scope: evaluation on large public LSHTC datasets; comparisons to several strong baselines; reporting of time/memory/accuracy.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/theoretical
- Theorem statements and notation contain inconsistencies that cast doubt on correctness:
  - In Definition/usage of fractional cover weights, the text “assume all ωk=1” contradicts the definition of exact fractional covers; this cannot generally hold.
  - Theorem 1 and 2 bounds include terms like 25/48 log(1/δ)/m without a square root; others have log 2δ or log 4Kδ with δ in the denominator missing; several terms appear dimensionally inconsistent or with likely typos (e.g., log 4Kδ instead of log(4K/δ)).
  - The constants α = maxy ηy/πy and β = maxy 1/πy are poorly behaved under the recommended πy ∝ 1/ηy: α then scales as ηy^2 up to normalization, potentially exploding and making bounds vacuous for head classes. There is no discussion of choosing π to control α, β.
  - The reduction shifts the class prior; the empirical risk is not importance-weighted to correct this shift. The theory recognizes this via α, but the algorithm ignores the bias in practice.
  - No proof sketches or key lemmas are provided; without the supplement it is hard to assess subtle dependencies (graph covers, variance-localization) and whether the splitting argument is valid.

Methodological/experimental
- φ mapping: only 10 hand-crafted IR features are used for text; this is very limiting for modern text classification. It is unclear whether the performance gains stem from sampling vs. representation.
- Missing ablations:
  - No ablation on κ (negative sampling rate) vs. accuracy/time, or on π (different balancing policies).
  - No ablation of candidate selection q: effect on accuracy and runtime not disentangled from the method’s core.
  - No comparison to the same dyadic reduction without aggressive sampling (full T(S)) to quantify the trade-off.
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Excluding SLEEC/LEML solely based on another paper’s results is weak; at least one representative label-embedding baseline should be included or a stronger justification provided.
  - Some baselines may be handicapped computationally by requiring evaluation over all K, whereas the proposed method uses candidate selection; a fair comparison would allow shortlisting for baselines too, or report both full and shortlisted prediction.
  - PD-Sparse is used with hashing; details of hyperparameters are deferred to the supplement, and not enough detail is provided in the main text for reproducibility.
- Dataset handling:
  - Multi-label LSHTC converted to multi-class by replicating instances introduces dependence and class-prior distortions; potential leakage or evaluation bias is not discussed. Hierarchical nature of LSHTC is ignored; hierarchical metrics are not reported.
- Reporting:
  - Tables/Figures (Table 1, 2, 3; Figure 1, Fig. 3) are referenced but not included; thus claims about speed/memory/accuracy cannot be verified from the paper as-is.
  - No standard deviations or statistical significance; no details on train/validation/test splits beyond “held-out”.
  - Memory/time measurement methodology lacks detail (e.g., I/O, precomputation of centroids, parallelization).

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typographical errors, missing spaces, grammatical issues, and LaTeX artifacts (“st Conference…”, footnote markers mid-sentence, inconsistent notation µk vs. ηy). Several equations are hard to parse; some symbols are undefined or overloaded.
- Related work omits closely related sampling/contrastive approaches in extreme classification and ranking; discussion of how this differs from WARP/WSABIE negative sampling is minimal.
- The prediction algorithm describes centroids “computed once in the preliminary projection,” but φ depends on x,y; it is unclear in which space centroids are computed and how they are used consistently at test time.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and challenging problem (extreme classification with long-tail distributions).
- Simple, practical ideas that can bring large computational savings.
- Theoretical direction (dependent-data generalization bounds with local RC) is interesting.

Cons for acceptance
- Significant issues in theoretical presentation and likely correctness/utility of the bounds (inconsistent constants, dubious ωk assumption, unaddressed α,β blow-up).
- Experimental section lacks critical ablations and fair baseline treatment; key tables/figures are missing; reproducibility is limited.
- Many clarity/formatting problems; incomplete references.

Suggestions/questions for authors
- Fix theory typos and provide a clear, self-contained Theorem 1/2 with correct log terms, constants, and assumptions (including the exact fractional cover and dependence structure). Discuss how to choose π to control α and β; consider importance weighting to correct the class-prior shift.
- Provide thorough ablations on κ, π, and q; include a “no-candidate-selection” variant for fair comparison; compare to full dyadic T(S) on smaller K.
- Add at least one strong label-embedding baseline; consider more modern text features (e.g., linear BoW with TF-IDF) to isolate the effect of the reduction.
- Clarify φ and centroid computation; define all features precisely in the main text.
- Include all referenced tables/figures, hyperparameters, and full experimental details in the main paper or an accessible supplement; report variances.
- Clean up writing, notation consistency, and references.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper targets an important problem with promising practical ideas, but the current submission has numerous technical, methodological, and presentation issues that undermine confidence in the claims. With corrected theory, stronger and fairer experiments, and improved clarity, this work could be competitive in a future submission.
