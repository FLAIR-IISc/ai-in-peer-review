

# Review of "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

This paper attempts to establish a theoretical connection between f-GANs and information geometry by showing that f-GANs can be viewed as optimizing a Bregman divergence under certain conditions involving deformed exponential families. The authors claim to provide a "variational information geometric identity" that connects f-divergences to Bregman divergences, and to show how deep architectures can model these geometric structures.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically rigorous in its theoretical development, with careful definitions and proofs for its central claims. The authors have clearly engaged with prior work in information geometry (Amari, Nielsen, etc.) and f-GANs (Nowozin et al.). The paper is well-organized and clearly written for experts in the field.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the theoretical depth, the paper fails to adequately demonstrate the practical significance of its theoretical claims. The most significant weakness is the lack of substantial experimental validation that would show how this information-geometric perspective leads to tangible improvements in GAN performance over established baselines.

The paper makes the strong claim that their "variational information geometric identity" "completes the supervised understanding of the generalization of GANs introduced in [30]." However, the experiments provided (Section 6) are limited to comparing different activation functions (µ-ReLU, Softplus, LSU) and different link functions in the discriminator. These comparisons are relatively minor, with results showing only marginal improvements (e.g., "the ReLU baseline was essentially beaten for values of µ yielding smaller variance"). The paper does not compare against state-of-the-art GAN variants (WGAN, WGAN-GP, SNGAN, etc.) or show that their theoretical insights lead to significant performance gains.

The paper's central claim—that f-GANs can be viewed as geometric optimization problems—remains largely unvalidated. While the authors provide theoretical connections between f-divergences and Bregman divergences, they fail to demonstrate how this connection leads to better GAN training, more stable optimization, or improved sample quality compared to existing methods. The paper states "the f-GAN problem can be equivalent to a geometric optimization problem," but provides no evidence that this equivalence results in practical benefits.

The paper also makes strong claims about the applicability of their framework to "most popular activation functions," but only provides a limited experimental validation. The claim that "the standard deep generator architecture is powerful at modelling complex escorts" is not substantiated by evidence showing that networks with these properties actually produce better samples.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a theoretical rephrasing of the f-GAN objective rather than a new method that delivers significant improvements. As a reviewer who values established methods and rigorous validation, I find the lack of substantial empirical evidence for practical impact to be a significant weakness. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons against state-of-the-art GAN methods and demonstration of how the information-geometric perspective leads to tangible improvements.

**Quality:** The theoretical development is sound, but the experimental validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims about practical significance. The paper is well-written and organized, but the lack of comprehensive experiments undermines the quality of the contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written for experts in the field, though some of the notation is dense. The main contribution is well-motivated and the theoretical development is logically presented.

**Originality:** The connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences through deformed exponential families is not entirely novel, as the paper itself references prior work that has explored similar connections. The paper's main contribution is more of a synthesis and formalization of existing concepts rather than a significant new insight.

**Significance:** The paper's significance is limited by the lack of empirical evidence demonstrating that the theoretical insights lead to practical improvements. Without showing that this information-geometric perspective results in better GAN performance, the paper's contribution remains largely theoretical without clear implications for the field.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between f-GANs and information geometry, but fails to demonstrate how this connection leads to meaningful improvements in practice. As a reviewer who prioritizes rigorous validation against established baselines and practical significance, I find the paper's claims to be unsubstantiated by sufficient empirical evidence. The theoretical insights are interesting, but without demonstrating their practical utility, the paper does not make a compelling contribution to the field of GANs or generative modeling.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's theoretical contribution is interesting but insufficiently validated, and the experimental results fail to demonstrate meaningful improvements over established methods. The paper would need substantially more empirical evidence showing how the information-geometric perspective leads to tangible improvements in GAN performance to be considered for acceptance.
