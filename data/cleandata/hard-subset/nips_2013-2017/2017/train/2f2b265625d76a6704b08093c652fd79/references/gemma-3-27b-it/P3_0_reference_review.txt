## Review of "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and mathematically rigorous connection between f-GANs and information geometry, going beyond the original formulation to provide a deeper understanding of the underlying mechanisms. The theoretical results are significant, and the experimental validation, while limited in scope, provides encouraging evidence for the practical relevance of the proposed insights.  I recommend acceptance, albeit with minor revisions to improve clarity and expand on the experimental analysis.

**Summary:** The authors address a key gap in the understanding of f-GANs – the connection between the variational formulation and the underlying geometric optimization problem. They establish a core identity linking f-divergences to Bregman divergences within the framework of deformed exponential families. This allows them to interpret the f-GAN objective as a geometric optimization problem, akin to Wasserstein GANs.  Further, they provide a complete parameterization of the supervised game (discriminator/adversary) and demonstrate how deep neural networks naturally model escorts of deformed exponential families.  The paper culminates in experiments supporting the benefits of principled generator and discriminator design based on these insights.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds upon a solid mathematical foundation, leveraging concepts from information geometry and statistical physics. The key result, Eq. (3) and its derivations, are clearly presented and rigorously justified.
* **Novel Perspective:** The connection between f-GANs and Bregman divergences offers a novel and insightful perspective on GAN training, potentially opening new avenues for analysis and algorithm design.
* **Completeness of the Supervised Game:**  Addressing the open parameter in Nowozin et al. [30] and fully characterizing the supervised learning component is a significant contribution.
* **Deep Generator Analysis:** Demonstrating the suitability of deep architectures for modeling escorts of deformed exponential families is a valuable result with direct implications for GAN design.  The analysis of activation functions (Lemma 11) is particularly insightful.
* **Well-Written & Structured:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The authors make a concerted effort to explain complex concepts in an accessible manner.
* **Experiments Validate Theory:** The experiments, while preliminary, demonstrate the potential benefits of the theoretical insights, particularly in generator design and discriminator link function tuning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental results are somewhat limited in scope, focusing on MNIST and LSUN with relatively simple architectures (DCGAN and MLP).  The gains observed are modest, and a more comprehensive evaluation on larger, more complex datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would strengthen the claims.
* **Complexity of Presentation:** While generally well-written, the paper occasionally suffers from mathematical density. Certain sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations and concrete examples to aid understanding.
* **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** The authors do not explicitly address the computational cost associated with the proposed modifications to the GAN training process, particularly in relation to the added complexity of manipulating deformed exponential families or optimizing the link function.
* **Link to Existing Geometric GANs:** While the authors explicitly contrast their approach with geometric GANs like Wasserstein GANs, a more detailed discussion of how the proposed framework *extends* or *complements* existing geometric approaches would be beneficial.



**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Theorem 5:** The assumption "there exists a selection of the subdifferential which is upperbounded on IP,Q" feels somewhat strong. Could the authors elaborate on the practical implications of this assumption and provide examples of scenarios where it might not hold?
* **Equation (3):** The Penalty(Q) term is mentioned but not fully explored. A deeper analysis of this term and its impact on training would be valuable.
* **Lemma 11:** The analysis of activation functions is impressive.  However, the distinction between “strongly admissible” and “weakly admissible” feels a little arbitrary.  Could the authors discuss the practical consequences of using a weakly admissible activation function?
* **Section 6 (Experiments):** The experimental section could be significantly strengthened by including quantitative comparisons with state-of-the-art GAN architectures and training techniques (e.g., spectral normalization, gradient penalty).  Additionally, providing ablation studies to isolate the effects of the different design choices (activation function vs. link function) would be helpful. Visual inspection of generated samples is helpful, but insufficient on its own.  FID/IS scores should be reported.
* **Proper Composite Losses:** The discussion of proper composite losses in Section 5 is excellent, but could be even more impactful by illustrating how different choices of the link function Ψ affect the behavior of the discriminator and the overall training dynamics.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advancement in our understanding of f-GANs.
* The connection to information geometry provides a powerful new framework for analyzing and designing GANs.
* The experimental results, while limited, are encouraging and suggest that the proposed insights can lead to practical improvements.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and addresses a relevant and important problem.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
* The paper's mathematical density may make it challenging for some readers.
* The computational cost of the proposed modifications is not adequately addressed.




**Final Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1. **Expand the experimental section:** Include more comprehensive evaluations on larger datasets and quantitative comparisons with state-of-the-art GANs.
2. **Clarify complex sections:**  Provide more intuitive explanations and concrete examples to aid understanding of the mathematical concepts.
3. **Discuss computational cost:** Address the potential computational overhead associated with the proposed modifications.
4. **Expand the discussion of related work:** Elaborate on the relationship between the proposed framework and existing geometric GAN approaches.



This is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant impact on the field of GAN research. Addressing the aforementioned weaknesses would further enhance its value and contribution.
