Summary
The paper aims to connect the variational f-GAN framework to information geometry. The main claims are: (i) any relevant f-divergence in the GAN setting can be represented as a KLχ divergence (a particular f-divergence induced by a χ-logarithm); (ii) an information-geometric identity holds for deformed exponential families (χ-exponential families), yielding a variational identity (Eq. 11) that decomposes the GAN objective into a Bregman divergence DC(θ‖ϑ) over natural parameters plus a penalty term J(Q); (iii) standard deep generators can model escorts of deformed exponential families, with inner layers factoring many escorts under invertibility assumptions (Theorem 9); (iv) a proper composite loss perspective clarifies the role of the discriminator output link and suggests principled alternatives beyond the sigmoid (Section 5). Some experiments indicate that tuning generator activations and discriminator links can help.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on Nowozin et al. (f-GAN), Amari et al. (information geometry and deformed exponentials), and Reid & Williamson (proper composite losses). The novelty claimed is marrying f-GAN’s variational formulation with deformed exponential families to obtain a parameter-space Bregman objective plus a Q-only penalty, and showing architectural implications for deep generators and for discriminator links.

Strengths
- Ambitious and interesting conceptual bridge between f-divergence variational estimation and information geometry of deformed exponential families.
- If correct, Corollary 8 provides a clean parameter-space interpretation (via Bregman DC) of f-GAN-type objectives.
- The proper composite loss view is appropriate and helps clarify the role of the link; pointing out that the “gf” in Nowozin et al. is not arbitrary is useful.
- The idea that activation choices implicitly specify χ and hence escorts is intriguing, and the constructive direction (derive χ from v and vice versa) could be practically valuable.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and rigor
- The core reduction from general f-divergences to KLχ (Theorem 5) hinges on a bounded selection of ∂f over the ratio range IP,Q. This is strong and often violated in practice (density ratios can be unbounded on continuous domains). The assertion that it “covers most GAN cases” is not justified. The construction of χ that works “for all P,Q” is only stated for specific f and deferred to the supplement/extended version, weakening the main paper.
- Escort sampling and practicality: The variational identity (Eq. 11) uses Q̃ in both the expectation and the conjugate term, and the penalty J(Q) depends on Q̃. How is Q̃ accessed in practice for training? The paper does not explain how to sample or reweight from Q to Q̃ stably, which is nontrivial and can break the claimed equivalence operationally.
- Theorem 7 domain inconsistency: The domain for T is written as R++ . = R\R++ which is nonsensical; later T∗(x) is negative, contradicting a positive domain if that was intended. This needs correction and careful treatment of domains of conjugates.
- Section 5 equations: Eq. (16) appears malformed (e.g., “Ψ−1(z)/−Ψ−1(z)”). The choice “Ψ(z) = f′(z/(1 − z)) with z := T(x)” is dimensionally inconsistent with links mapping (0,1) → R. These errors cast doubt on the correctness of the proper composite loss derivation as written in the main text.
- The invertibility assumption for deep networks (Theorem 9) is very strong. DCGANs with strided/deconvolutions and padding are typically not bijective; ReLU is not invertible; even leaky-ReLU requires careful domain restrictions. The paper’s statement that this is “a reasonable price” is unpersuasive without a robust argument or practical scheme (e.g., coupling layers, flow models) and an analysis of approximation error when invertibility fails.
- Claims about ELU/ReLU admissibility: ELU is not globally convex on R; the admissibility definitions require convexity and strict monotonicity on the domain used. The paper needs to precisely specify domains to make these claims correct.
- Notational sloppiness is prevalent: the same symbols for densities and distributions, inconsistent use of logχ and expχ, base measure switches “implicit,” and occasional reversal of arguments in DC (e.g., Eq. (2) vs later). These undermine reproducibility.

Empirical evaluation
- Experiments lack quantitative, standardized metrics (e.g., Inception Score/FID; even at the time, IS was common). “Beating ReLU” is reported without numbers. Figures are referenced but not shown; many details are delegated to SM.
- The LSU activation is referenced without definition in the main paper. The practical mapping from v to χ and the asserted factorization (Theorem 9) are not empirically validated (e.g., via density estimation diagnostics or likelihood ratios).
- Comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., WGAN-GP, improved training techniques) are missing; datasets are limited.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and grammatical errors: “We leaving implicit”, “reguire”, “The difference vanish”, “Q. involves”, “R++ . = R\R++”, etc.
- Several equations are hard to parse or contain errors (notably Eq. 16 and around Theorem 7/12). The main text should include at least sketches of the pivotal proofs; too much is deferred.
- References are inconsistently formatted (missing years/pages, capitalization), and some claims are not precisely tied to citations.

Originality and significance
- Conceptually novel bridge, but under strong assumptions and with substantial technical gaps. The practical impact is unclear until escort sampling and invertibility issues are resolved and stronger experiments are presented.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting theoretical direction linking f-GANs and information geometry with a potentially impactful identity (Eq. 11).
- Useful clarification of discriminator link via proper composite loss framework.
- Could inspire new architectures/regularizers if formalism is tightened.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical inconsistencies and errors in key equations; strong and possibly unrealistic assumptions (bounded subgradient, invertibility, admissibility).
- Missing operational details (escort sampling/training), undermining the practical equivalence.
- Weak empirical support and missing metrics; figures and key definitions absent from main text.
- Numerous presentation/notation issues.

Questions for authors
1. Under what precise conditions on f and on the supports of P,Q does Theorem 5 produce a χ that is independent of P,Q? Can you give a complete statement in the main text?
2. How do you train with Q̃ in practice? Do you reweight Q samples or modify the generator objective to avoid direct sampling from Q̃? What are the stability implications?
3. Please correct and clarify Eq. (16) and the link mapping. What is the exact composite loss used, and how does it recover Eq. (7)?
4. Theorem 7: what is the correct domain of T, and how does it align with T∗ being negative?
5. Theorem 9: provide concrete examples of invertible architectures used in practice (or approximate results) and quantify the approximation error when invertibility is violated.
6. Clarify admissibility: on what domain are ELU/ReLU convex/strictly increasing? How is the domain restricted in your theorems?
7. Provide quantitative results (e.g., IS/FID) and ablations showing the benefit of specific links/activations across datasets and stronger baselines.

Minor comments and typos (non-exhaustive)
- “st Conference on…” header artifact; remove.
- Eq. numbering: ensure consistent references (e.g., “eqs. (1 — 3)”).
- “R++ . = R\R++” is incorrect; define R++ unambiguously.
- Consistently distinguish distributions (P,Q) from densities (P,Q) or use different fonts.
- Several grammar issues: “We leaving implicit”, “reguire”, “The difference vanish”, “Q. involves”.
- Reference formatting inconsistent; add years/pages; standardize capitalization.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper presents an appealing conceptual connection but suffers from significant technical, clarity, and empirical shortcomings. With corrected derivations, clarified assumptions (and their practical implications), operational training procedures for escorts, and stronger quantitative experiments, this work could become a valuable contribution in a revised submission. My confidence is high regarding the presentation and empirical concerns, and moderate regarding the deeper technical claims pending full proofs in the main text.
