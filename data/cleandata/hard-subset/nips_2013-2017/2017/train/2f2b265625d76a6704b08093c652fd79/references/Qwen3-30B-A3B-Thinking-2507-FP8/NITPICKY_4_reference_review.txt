

# Review of "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

This paper attempts to establish a connection between f-GANs and information geometry through deformed exponential families. The authors claim to derive an identity (eq. 3) relating f-GAN objectives to Bregman divergences, and explore how deep architectures can model escorts of deformed exponential families. While the topic is relevant to NIPS, the paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies and lacks sufficient theoretical grounding.

**Major Technical Flaws**

The paper's central claim (eq. 3) appears fundamentally flawed. The authors state: "f-GAN(P, escort(Q)) = D(θ‖ϑ) + Penalty(Q)" but fail to properly define what "f-GAN(P, escort(Q))" means. In the main text, f-GAN is defined as a variational objective (eq. 7), not a function of distributions. The authors later derive Corollary 8 as a variational identity, but this is not equivalent to their main claim. The paper incorrectly assumes that if If(P‖Q) = KLχ(Q‖P), then the variational f-GAN objective equals D(θ‖ϑ) + Penalty(Q), but these are fundamentally different mathematical objects.

Theorem 5 contains a critical error: "If P,Q are such that there exists a selection ξ ∈ ∂f with sup ξ(IP,Q) <∞, then ∃χ: R+→R+ non decreasing such that If(P‖Q) = KLχ(Q‖P)." The KLχ divergence is defined as EX~P[-logχ(Q(X)/P(X))], while the f-divergence is EX~Q[f(P(X)/Q(X))]. These expressions cannot be equal for arbitrary f without specific constraints on f and χ that the authors don't establish.

**Inconsistent Notation and Definitions**

The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. In Definition 4, KLχ(P‖Q) = EX~P[-logχ(Q(X)/P(X))], but in Theorem 6, they use KLχQ̃(Q̃‖P) which would be EX~Q̃[-logχQ̃(P(X)/Q̃(X))], contradicting their own definition. The definition of χ•(t) = 1/χ⁻¹(1/t) in Theorem 12 is ambiguous (what does χ⁻¹ mean?).

The paper claims "the difference vanish only for exponential families" but doesn't clearly define what "the difference" refers to. The penalty term in eq. (3) is defined as part of the equation, so it's unclear what "difference" they're referring to.

**Weak Theoretical Support**

Theorem 9 claims "for any continuous signature χnet, there exists activation v" but provides no constructive method to determine v from χnet. The proof is relegated to the supplement, but the main paper should contain the core construction. For ReLU, they claim it's "weakly admissible" but don't provide a concrete sequence of strongly admissible activations converging to ReLU.

The paper makes unsubstantiated claims about activation functions: "it turns out that this condition is satisfied, exactly or in a limit sense, by most popular activation functions." They cite ELU, ReLU, Softplus as examples, but don't demonstrate this for each one.

**Experimental Weaknesses**

The experiments section lacks statistical significance testing. The authors claim "the ReLU baseline was essentially beaten for values of µ yielding smaller variance" but provide no p-values or confidence intervals. Their Figure 1 (left) shows results for varying µ in µ-ReLU but doesn't explain why the best results occurred at µ=0.7 for GAN DCGAN.

The comparison of link functions in the discriminator (Figure 1, right) shows Matsushita entropy outperforming sigmoid for GANs, but they don't explain why this should be the case theoretically. The experiments are limited to two datasets (MNIST and LSUN) and two architectures (DCGAN and MLP), which is insufficient to establish the broader claim that their method provides "substantial ground to be competitive with mainstream approaches."

**Other Issues**

The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing throughout. The abstract contains a formatting error: "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems" should be "The Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems." The paper inconsistently uses "Section § 2" versus "Section 2" in the introduction.

The paper fails to adequately contextualize its contribution relative to [30]. It claims to "complete the supervised understanding of the generalization of GANs introduced in [30]" but doesn't clearly articulate what [30] missed or how their work specifically addresses it.

**Conclusion**

While the paper addresses a relevant topic in GAN theory, the technical flaws are severe enough to undermine the main contribution. The central identity (eq. 3) is not properly established, the notation is inconsistent, and the theoretical claims are unsubstantiated. The experiments are insufficient to support the broad claims made in the paper.

The paper's significance is also questionable. Even if their identity were correct, it's unclear how this would lead to practical improvements in GAN training. The connection to information geometry is presented as a theoretical curiosity rather than a practical tool.

I recommend rejection due to fundamental technical errors in the main claim, inconsistent notation, and insufficient theoretical and experimental support for the claims made.
