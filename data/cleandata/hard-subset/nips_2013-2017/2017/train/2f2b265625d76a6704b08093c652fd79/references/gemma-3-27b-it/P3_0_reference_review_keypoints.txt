- Overall: Strong, rigorous connection between f-GANs and information geometry; recommend accept with minor revisions.
- Main contribution: Derives identity linking f-divergences to Bregman divergences within deformed exponential families.
- Interpretation: Recasts the f-GAN objective as a geometric optimization problem, analogous to Wasserstein GANs.
- Supervised game: Provides a full parameterization of the discriminator/adversary, resolving an open issue from Nowozin et al.
- Generator analysis: Shows deep nets naturally model escorts of deformed exponential families; activation-function analysis (Lemma 11) is insightful.
- Writing: Well-structured and generally clear, with rigorous derivations (notably Eq. (3)).
- Experiments: Preliminary empirical validation on MNIST and LSUN shows modest gains and supports theoretical insights.
- Strength: Novel information-geometric perspective that could inform new GAN analyses and designs.
- Strength: Completeness in characterizing the supervised learning component is a notable theoretical advance.
- Weakness: Experimental scope is limited (datasets, architectures) and reported gains are modest.
- Weakness: Paper is mathematically dense in places and needs more intuitive explanations and examples.
- Weakness: Lacks discussion of computational cost from proposed modifications (deformed exponentials, link optimization).
- Weakness: Needs clearer comparison and relation to existing geometric GANs (e.g., Wasserstein).
- Theorem 5 concern: Assumption on bounded subdifferential selection may be strong; needs clarification and examples where it could fail.
- Eq. (3) concern: The Penalty(Q) term is mentioned but not fully analyzed; its training impact should be explored.
- Lemma 11 concern: Distinction between “strongly” and “weakly admissible” activations seems arbitrary; discuss practical consequences.
- Experimental suggestions: Add comparisons to state-of-the-art GANs, use spectral norm/gradient penalty baselines, report FID/IS, and include ablations isolating activation vs. link effects.
- Proper composite losses: Illustrate how different link choices Ψ affect discriminator behavior and training dynamics.
- Arguments for acceptance: Significant theoretical advancement, powerful new framework, encouraging experiments, clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: Limited experimental evaluation, mathematical density, and missing computational-cost analysis.
- Final recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—expand experiments, clarify complex/math-heavy sections, discuss computational cost, and elaborate relation to geometric GAN approaches.
