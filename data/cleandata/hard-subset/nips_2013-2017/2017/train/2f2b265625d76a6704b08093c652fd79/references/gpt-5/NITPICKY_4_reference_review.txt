Summary
The paper aims to place f-GANs within an information-geometric framework. The main theoretical thread is: (i) show that many f-divergences can be represented as KLχ divergences (Definition 4, Theorem 5); (ii) connect KLχ divergences to Bregman divergences between coordinates of deformed (χ-) exponential families via escorts (Theorem 3 → Theorem 6); (iii) derive a variational identity (Theorem 7, Corollary 8) equating a GAN-like saddle objective to a Bregman divergence DC(θ‖ϑ) plus a generator-only penalty J(Q). On the modeling side, the paper claims that standard deep generators can factorize products of escorts of χ-exponential families (Theorem 9), with activation functions determining the χ signature (Lemma 11). Finally, it reframes the discriminator objective using proper composite losses, clarifying the role of the link function (Section 5, Theorem 12). Experiments explore generator activations and discriminator links on MNIST and LSUN-tower.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Nowozin et al. (f-GAN), Amari’s information geometry, and Nguyen–Wainwright–Jordan’s variational characterizations of f-divergences. The use of escorts and deformed exponential families follows Amari/Naudts. The proper composite-loss perspective traces to Reid & Williamson. However, important related work on invertible/normalizing flows (e.g., NICE/RealNVP) is not cited or contrasted, despite central reliance on invertibility and density computations.

Strengths
- Ambitious unification: connects variational f-GAN objectives to Bregman divergences over deformed exponential families, which, if correct and usable, is theoretically interesting.
- Clarifies the “output activation/link” in the discriminator via proper composite losses; this is a useful lens that may guide practical choices.
- Suggests an activation–χ correspondence that could inspire architectural design.
- Attempts to analyze deep generators’ density factorization via Jacobians and escorts.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and assumptions
- Theorem 5: the existence of χ such that If(P‖Q)=KLχ(Q‖P) under “there exists ξ∈∂f with sup ξ(IP,Q)<∞” depends on P,Q through IP,Q and ξ; later the paper claims a χ may exist “for all densities P,Q” under “mild assumptions,” but this is not established. The constructive choice of χ for the GAN f is given as χGAN(z)=log(1+1/z), which is decreasing on R+; yet the paper requires χ to be non-decreasing for −logχ to be convex (Definition 4). This appears contradictory and undermines Theorem 5’s applicability to GAN’s f.
- Theorem 7: domain of T uses R++ with a self-referential definition “R++ . = R\R++” (nonsense). The sign of the optimum T*(x)=−(1/Z)·χ(Q(x))/χ(P(x)) also clashes with the stated domain of T. These are not minor typos; they affect the variational form and optimal discriminator characterization.
- Theorem 9 relies on full invertibility of the deep generator (including fractional-strided convolutions and elementwise nonlinearities). The claim that “popular examples can be invertible (e.g., DCGAN)” is not generally correct: typical DCGANs use dimensionality changes, padding/striding, and non-invertible ops; even with leaky/ELUs, convolutions with stride/padding are not generically invertible. Without a precise architectural class (as in NICE/RealNVP/i-ResNet) the assumption is unrealistic, jeopardizing the density and factorization claims.
- The construction of Q̃deep as a product of escorts across layers/units (Eq. 14) is nonstandard and is only sketched; with the invertibility issue unresolved, the result is hard to trust. Many crucial proofs are deferred to the supplement; the main text lacks checks or special cases to build confidence.
- Section 5: Eq. (15) has a malformed factor “− 1 · If(P‖Q)”; domains/invertibility of f′ and Ψ are not carefully handled; differentiability is assumed after earlier subdifferential treatment. Theorem 12 introduces χ•(t)=1/χ−1(1/t) without addressing domain/monotonicity constraints required for a χ-logarithm.

Clarity and presentation
- Heavy notation with many inconsistencies: e.g., switching argument order in Bregman divergence (and relying on Csiszár dual to explain it), multiple overloading of d vs dl, φl dimensions, and R++ definition. Several grammar/typos: “reguire,” “the difference vanish,” “Rdl 3 φl(x),” “eq. (1 — 3).”
- Many key steps are asserted as “constructive” but details are only in SM; the main text does not provide sanity checks, limiting readability and verifiability.
- The connection “DC is a Bregman divergence ⇒ convergence in parameter space” is stated without discussing identifiability and model misspecification; deformed exponential families may not contain P, so DC(θ‖ϑ)→0 is not assured by optimizing the variational objective with finite-capacity discriminators/generators.

Experimental validation
- Extremely limited: MNIST and a single LSUN class; no standard quantitative metrics (even for 2017, IS on CIFAR-10 was common). Results are largely qualitative/subjective (“tends to display,” “almost consistently better/worse”), with no statistical analysis beyond a few runs. The activation/link tweaks yield small, inconsistent gains; no ablations to isolate the proposed theory’s predictions (e.g., escort factorization effects).
- Claims about “beating ReLU” depend on cherry-picked settings; counter-cases exist in the paper itself.

Missing/under-cited related work
- No discussion of invertible/flow-based generative models (Dinh et al., Rezende & Mohamed) that rigorously ensure invertibility and tractable densities, directly relevant to Theorem 9.
- Broader literature on f-divergence variational forms and density ratio estimation is cited, but the novelty over these is not crisply separated.

Specific questions for authors
1) How do you reconcile the monotonicity requirement for χ with χGAN(z)=log(1+1/z), which is decreasing? Please provide a corrected χ and revisit Theorem 5’s scope.
2) Clarify the domain and sign in Theorem 7: what is the correct codomain of T, and is T* negative?
3) Precisely specify the architectural constraints that guarantee invertibility (no striding/pooling? circular padding? square full-rank convolution matrices?) and cite connections to flows. Under these constraints, are your experiments actually using invertible models?
4) Provide a concrete χ and activation pair (beyond the existence statements) with a worked example where Eq. (14) can be verified against ground truth.
5) Can χ in Theorem 5 be chosen independent of P,Q for a given f beyond KL? If not, what is the practical meaning of the identity for training?
6) In Section 5, please fix Eq. (15) and state the precise conditions on f, Ψ for properness and the variational identity.

Minor issues and typos (non-exhaustive)
- “st Conference on NIPS 2017” line should be removed.
- Eq. (9) χGAN likely violates required monotonicity; also units/dimensions unclear.
- Multiple grammar issues (“reguire,” “the difference vanish,” “we leaving implicit”).
- Notation: dl vs d; R++ definition is incorrect; “Rdl 3 φl(x)” is odd; unify symbol sets.

Reproducibility
- Code link is provided, but experiments are insufficiently described in main paper; proofs are deferred. The theoretical claims rely on nontrivial assumptions that are not verifiable from the given text.

Pros
- Ambitious theoretical synthesis linking f-divergences, escorts, and Bregman geometry.
- Proper composite-loss view is insightful and practically relevant.
- The activation–χ perspective could inspire design choices.

Cons
- Multiple technical inconsistencies and unclear/incorrect assumptions (χ monotonicity, invertibility).
- Overreliance on supplemental proofs without adequate main-text validation.
- Experiments are too limited and not convincingly tied to the theory.
- Numerous notational/formatting errors impede clarity.

Recommendation: Reject
While the overarching vision is appealing, the paper in its current form suffers from significant technical and presentation issues and insufficient empirical support. A substantially revised version addressing the mathematical inconsistencies, clarifying assumptions (especially invertibility), adding rigorous examples, and strengthening experiments could be impactful.

Confidence: High (familiar with f-divergence variational forms, information geometry, and generative modeling).
