## Review of "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a theoretically interesting connection between f-GANs and information geometry, providing a more complete understanding of the underlying principles and potential for generalization. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the experimental validation feels somewhat limited and doesn't fully demonstrate the practical benefits claimed.  I believe the paper is **above average** and leans towards acceptance, contingent on addressing some concerns regarding clarity and experimental scope.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper bridges the gap between the recently popularized f-GAN framework (Nowozin et al., 2017) and information geometry. It establishes a key identity (eq. 3) showing that solving an f-GAN problem is equivalent to a geometric optimization problem involving Bregman divergences within the framework of deformed exponential families.  Further, it elucidates previously unspecified parameters in the supervised game (discriminator’s link function) and demonstrates that standard deep generator architectures are well-suited for modeling escorts of these families, hinting at architectural design principles.  The authors also show how the f-GAN game can be framed within a proper composite loss framework, linking it to more established optimization techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The central identity (eq. 3) and its subsequent derivations provide a valuable theoretical foundation for understanding f-GANs.  Connecting f-divergences to Bregman divergences and demonstrating the role of deformed exponential families is a significant contribution.
* **Completeness of the Framework:**  The paper addresses open questions from Nowozin et al. (2017) regarding the supervised learning aspect of f-GANs, clarifying the role of the link function and providing a more complete picture of the optimization process.
* **Insightful Analysis of Generator Architecture:** The analysis of deep generator architectures in relation to escorts of deformed exponential families is insightful and provides a theoretical basis for choosing appropriate activation functions.
* **Connection to Proper Composite Losses:** Framing the f-GAN within the proper composite loss framework provides a connection to well-established optimization techniques and clarifies the role of the discriminator.
* **Clear Writing (mostly):**  The paper is generally well-structured and the authors make a concerted effort to explain complex concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Experimental Scope and Impact:** The experiments, while demonstrating *some* benefit from the theoretical insights, are limited in scope. The improvement observed in the results is not always dramatic and doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant practical advantage over existing GAN techniques. The experimental section feels somewhat tacked on, rather than deeply integrated with the theoretical contributions.
* **Clarity of Notation:** The paper uses a lot of mathematical notation, which, while generally correct, can be dense and difficult to follow at times. Some definitions could benefit from more intuitive explanations.  The constant switching between different notation for the same concepts (e.g., switching arguments in Bregman divergence) can be confusing.
* **Lack of Discussion of Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge that applying the geometric convergence results requires caution when *P* is complex, a more thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach (e.g., computational complexity of dealing with deformed exponential families) would strengthen the paper.
* **Some Terminology is Not Standard:** For example, the use of "strongly admissible" and "weakly admissible" activations, while well-defined, is not common in the field and requires more justification.

**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous mathematical derivations. Claims are generally well-supported, although further experimental validation would strengthen the argument. It's a complete piece of work in terms of theoretical development.
* **Clarity:** The writing is generally clear, but the density of mathematical notation and occasional shifts in notation can hinder understanding.
* **Originality:** The connection between f-GANs and information geometry, the elucidation of the link function, and the analysis of generator architectures are novel contributions. The authors adequately reference related work.
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant, as they provide a deeper understanding of f-GANs and suggest avenues for improved generator design and training. However, the significance is currently limited by the relatively modest experimental results.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a novel and theoretically sound connection between f-GANs and information geometry.
* Addresses open questions in the f-GAN literature.
* Offers potentially valuable insights into generator architecture and training.
* The mathematical framework is rigorously developed and generally correct.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Experimental validation is limited in scope and impact.
* The paper could benefit from improved clarity of notation and explanations.
* A more thorough discussion of limitations would strengthen the paper.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1. **Expand the experimental section:** Include more comprehensive experiments, perhaps exploring different datasets and architectures.  Focus on demonstrating a clear practical benefit from applying the theoretical insights.
2. **Improve Clarity:** Simplify the notation where possible and provide more intuitive explanations of complex concepts.
3. **Address Limitations:**  Discuss the computational challenges of working with deformed exponential families and any other limitations of the approach.
4. **Standardize Terminology:** Consider using more standard terminology or providing stronger justification for new terms.
