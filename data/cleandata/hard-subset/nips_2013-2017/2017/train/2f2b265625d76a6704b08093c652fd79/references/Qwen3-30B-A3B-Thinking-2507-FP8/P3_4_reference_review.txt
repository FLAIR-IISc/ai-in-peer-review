

# Peer Review: "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

## Summary and Context

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting the f-GAN framework [30] to information geometry through deformed exponential families. The authors establish a variational identity (equation 3) showing that f-GANs can be interpreted as optimizing a Bregman divergence between parameters of deformed exponential families, with an additional escort term. This work addresses a key gap in [30] regarding the relationship between the generator's architecture and the information-theoretic formulation of GANs. The paper also provides a proper loss framework for the discriminator, clarifying the role of the link function that was left "somewhat arbitrary" in [30].

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty**: The core identity (equation 3) is a significant contribution that bridges information theory and information geometry. The connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences via deformed exponential families generalizes the known KL-Exponential family relationship, providing a more unified framework for understanding GANs.

2. **Practical Implications**: The paper offers concrete insights for practitioners, showing how activation functions relate to the χ-signature of deformed exponential families (Lemma 11). This provides a principled way to choose activations (e.g., showing ReLU is weakly admissible, while ELU is strongly admissible).

3. **Completing the Theoretical Picture**: The paper successfully resolves a critical ambiguity in [30] by showing the discriminator's link function must be chosen as a proper composite loss (Theorem 12), which is a substantial theoretical contribution.

4. **Constructive Theorems**: Theorems 9 and 11 provide constructive methods for relating neural network architectures to deformed exponential families, which is valuable for both theory and practice.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Limited Practical Validation**: The experiments (Section 6) are insufficient to demonstrate the claimed practical benefits. The comparison of activation functions (Figure 1) shows marginal improvements at best, with no clear statistical significance. The paper claims "uplift" but provides no rigorous statistical comparison with baselines. The experiments don't convincingly show that the theoretical insights lead to substantial practical improvements.

2. **Restrictive Assumptions**: The key identity (equation 3) requires P and Q to lie in deformed exponential families, which is a strong assumption. Real-world GAN applications often deal with complex distributions that don't fit this parametric family, limiting the practical applicability of the theoretical results.

3. **Comparison with Related Work**: The paper doesn't adequately compare with other geometric approaches to GANs, particularly Wasserstein GANs [6] which connect to optimal transport geometry. The claim that their approach "hints on a simple sufficient condition" for activation functions is not sufficiently differentiated from existing work on activation functions in deep learning.

4. **Clarity and Accessibility**: While the paper is technically sound, the dense notation and heavy reliance on information geometry concepts may limit its accessibility to the broader ML community. A clearer exposition of the practical implications would strengthen the paper.

5. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims their framework "completes the supervised understanding" of f-GANs, but the experimental validation doesn't fully support this claim. The "uplift" mentioned in the abstract isn't convincingly demonstrated.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The theoretical contributions are sound and well-motivated, with detailed proofs provided in the supplement. However, the practical validation is weak, and the assumptions may be too restrictive for real-world applications. (Score: 7/10)

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written for an expert audience familiar with information geometry. The notation is consistent but could be made more accessible to a broader ML audience. (Score: 8/10)

**Originality**: The core identity connecting f-GANs to information geometry via deformed exponential families is novel. The paper builds on but significantly extends the work in [5, 13, 30]. (Score: 9/10)

**Significance**: The theoretical insights are important for deepening our understanding of GANs, but the practical significance is not convincingly demonstrated. The paper would benefit from more extensive experiments on complex datasets and comparisons with state-of-the-art GAN variants. (Score: 6/10)

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the information-geometric foundations of GANs. The core identity (equation 3) is a significant step forward in connecting f-divergences to information geometry. However, the practical significance is limited by insufficient experimental validation and restrictive assumptions.

The paper should be accepted with minor revisions to strengthen the experimental section and better contextualize the practical implications. Specifically, the authors should:
1. Provide more rigorous statistical comparison showing the claimed "uplift" is significant
2. Extend experiments to more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet)
3. Better contextualize their approach relative to other geometric GAN formulations (e.g., Wasserstein GANs)

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution, while theoretically interesting, may not have sufficient impact to justify acceptance at NIPS. With the proposed revisions, it could be a strong contribution to the field.

**Final Recommendation**: Accept with Minor Revisions (assuming the authors address the experimental concerns)
