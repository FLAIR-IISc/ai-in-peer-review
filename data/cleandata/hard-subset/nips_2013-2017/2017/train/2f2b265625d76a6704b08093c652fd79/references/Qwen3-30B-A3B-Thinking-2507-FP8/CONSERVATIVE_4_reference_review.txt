

# Review of "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

This paper attempts to connect f-GANs to information geometry by establishing a theoretical relationship between f-divergences and Bregman divergences for deformed exponential families. The authors claim to provide an identity (eq. 3) that connects the f-GAN game to geometric optimization, and they analyze how deep neural networks can model the required escorts of deformed exponential families. The paper builds on Nowozin et al.'s f-GAN work and extends it with connections to information geometry literature.

**Strengths:** The theoretical contributions are mathematically sound, with detailed proofs provided in the supplement. The connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences for deformed exponential families represents a meaningful extension of existing information geometry literature (Amari, etc.). The paper also provides a more complete characterization of the supervised GAN game by specifying the role of the link function, which was left ambiguous in the original f-GAN work.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant weakness is the lack of substantial empirical validation. The authors present limited experiments on MNIST and LSUN tower datasets with only a few activation functions and link functions. The results are not compared against established GAN baselines (WGAN, DCGAN, etc.) in a rigorous manner. The claim that "the best results were obtained for GAN DCGAN" is vague and lacks proper comparison with state-of-the-art methods. The paper doesn't demonstrate how their theoretical insights translate to practical improvements in GAN performance.

The paper makes an important theoretical contribution but fails to convincingly establish its significance for practical GAN applications. The experiments show marginal improvements (e.g., "Matsushita is indeed better than the sigmoid link for both DCGAN and MLP"), but these improvements don't appear to be substantial enough to justify the complexity of the theoretical framework. The paper doesn't address whether the proposed approach would scale to more challenging datasets or tasks.

The paper also overstates its contributions. While the theoretical connection is novel, the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from or improves upon existing approaches like Wasserstein GANs, which also have strong geometric interpretations. The claim that "deep architectures are powerful at tackling the generative part of the game" is not sufficiently supported by the limited experiments.

**Quality:** The theoretical content is strong, but the empirical validation is insufficient. The paper lacks thorough comparison against established baselines, making it difficult to assess the practical value of the proposed approach. The authors don't adequately address the limitations of their method or provide a clear explanation for when their approach might fail.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the connection to information geometry is well-explained. The supplementary material appears to contain the detailed proofs, which is helpful.

**Originality:** The theoretical connection between f-GANs and information geometry for deformed exponential families is novel, but the paper doesn't clearly distinguish this from related work in information geometry or GAN literature. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing approaches that also leverage geometric interpretations.

**Significance:** The significance of the work is questionable. While the theoretical connection is interesting, the paper fails to demonstrate how this insight leads to practical improvements in GAN performance. The limited experiments don't provide convincing evidence that this approach would be widely useful in practice. The paper doesn't address whether the theoretical framework would be useful for addressing major challenges in GAN training (mode collapse, training instability, etc.).

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the theoretical contribution is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient empirical validation to support its claims about practical significance. The experiments are limited, lack proper comparison with established baselines, and don't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach improves upon state-of-the-art GAN methods. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I cannot endorse this paper without more substantial evidence of its practical utility.

The authors should either substantially expand their experimental evaluation to include comparisons with current state-of-the-art GAN methods on standard benchmarks, or provide a clearer explanation of why this theoretical insight would be valuable for practitioners. Without this, the paper remains an interesting but incomplete theoretical contribution that doesn't sufficiently advance the state of the art in GAN applications.
