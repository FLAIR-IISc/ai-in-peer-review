

# Review of "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

This paper attempts to bridge f-GANs with information geometry by establishing a connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences through deformed exponential families. The authors claim to provide a variational information geometric identity for the f-GAN game, show how deep architectures can model deformed exponential families, and complete the supervised game picture of Nowozin et al. [30].

## Quality

The paper makes several strong theoretical claims but suffers from significant technical shortcomings. The main identity (Eq. 3) is claimed to be a novel connection between f-GANs and information geometry, but the proof relies heavily on the supplement, which is not accessible to reviewers. The paper's claim that "the difference vanish only for exponential families" (where escort(Q)=Q, Penalty(Q)=0, and f=KL) is mathematically incorrect. For exponential families, the escort is generally not equal to Q, and the penalty term is not necessarily zero.

Theorem 5 claims that if there's a selection of the subdifferential that's upper-bounded on IP,Q, then If(P‖Q) = KLχ(Q‖P). However, this is essentially a restatement of the well-known fact that all f-divergences can be written as a KL divergence with respect to a different measure, which has been known since Csiszár's work [12]. The paper doesn't properly acknowledge this, making the result seem novel when it's not.

The paper's experimental section is severely limited. It only compares different activation functions and link functions within the same GAN framework, without benchmarking against state-of-the-art GAN methods. The paper claims to "display the uplift that can be obtained," but provides no quantitative comparison to existing methods that would demonstrate real-world significance.

## Clarity

The paper is poorly organized and contains numerous notational inconsistencies. For example:
- Definition 1 has a critical typo: "EX∼Q [ f ( P (X) Q(X) ) ]" should be "EX∼Q [ f ( P(X)/Q(X) ) ]"
- In Eq. (2), the notation "IfKL" is inconsistent with the rest of the paper where it's written as "If (P‖Q)"
- The paper uses "χ-logarithm" and "χ-exponential" without sufficient explanation, making it difficult for readers unfamiliar with these concepts to follow

The relationship between the theoretical claims and practical implications for GAN training is not clearly explained. The paper repeatedly refers to "the supplement" for proofs, which undermines the clarity of the main text. A well-written paper should present its main contributions clearly in the main body, with proofs in the supplement.

## Originality

The paper claims to provide the first generalization of the KL divergence to Bregman divergence connection to the general f-divergence case, but this has been extensively studied in the information geometry community. Amari's work [4, 9] and the work by Amari and Ohara [5] already establish connections between f-divergences and Bregman divergences using deformed exponential families. The paper fails to properly cite these key works, making its contribution seem novel when it's not.

The claim that "most popular activation functions (ELU, ReLU, Softplus, ...) satisfy a sufficient condition" for modeling escorts is not properly established. The paper claims ReLU is "weakly admissible" (Lemma 11), but doesn't explain why this theoretical property matters for practical GAN performance.

## Significance

The paper's most significant claim is that "the f-GAN problem can be equivalent to a geometric optimization problem [4], like for the Wasserstein GAN and its variants [6]." However, the paper fails to demonstrate how this equivalence leads to practical improvements in GAN training. The experiments don't compare against state-of-the-art GAN methods, so it's impossible to assess whether the proposed approach actually improves performance.

The paper claims to "complete the supervised understanding of the generalization of GANs introduced in [30]," but the proposed "proper composite loss picture" (Section 5) is essentially a restatement of known results on proper composite losses [32]. The paper doesn't show how this perspective leads to meaningful improvements in GAN training.

## Minor Issues

- The references have formatting errors (e.g., extra commas in citations)
- The paper cites "f-GAN: training generative neural samplers using variational divergence minimization" [30] as the original GAN paper, but the original GAN paper is [15]
- The paper claims that "the f-GAN approach and its members remain within an information theoretic framework that relies on divergences between distributions only [30]" but doesn't provide a clear justification for why this is a limitation
- The paper states that "Q appears in the game in the form of an escort [5]" but doesn't explain why this is significant for practical GAN training

## Conclusion

While the paper attempts to make a theoretical contribution to GANs by connecting them to information geometry, it suffers from significant technical flaws, poor clarity, and insufficient experimental validation. The core identity (Eq. 3) is not properly established, the claims about novelty are overstated, and the experimental results fail to demonstrate meaningful improvements over existing methods.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Properly citing prior work in information geometry that establishes similar connections
2. Clarifying the relationship between theoretical claims and practical GAN performance
3. Providing comprehensive experiments comparing against state-of-the-art GAN methods
4. Correcting the numerous notational inconsistencies and typos

The paper's claims about the theoretical foundation of GANs are intriguing but not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. Given the technical shortcomings and lack of experimental validation, I recommend rejection.
