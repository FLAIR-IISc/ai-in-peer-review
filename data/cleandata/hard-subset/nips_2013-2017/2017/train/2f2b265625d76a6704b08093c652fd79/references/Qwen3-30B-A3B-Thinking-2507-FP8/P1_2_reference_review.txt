

# Peer Review: "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

## Summary

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a connection between f-GANs (a generalization of GANs introduced by Nowozin et al. [30]) and information geometry. The authors demonstrate that the f-GAN objective can be equivalently formulated as a geometric optimization problem using Bregman divergences, specifically for distributions in deformed exponential families (a generalization of exponential families). The main theoretical contribution is the identity:

f-GAN(P, escort(Q)) = D(θ‖ϑ) + Penalty(Q)

where D is a Bregman divergence between the natural parameters θ and ϑ of the distributions P and Q (which lie in a superset of exponential families called deformed exponential families), and Penalty(Q) is a term that vanishes for exponential families.

The paper builds on previous work by Nowozin et al. [30] who showed f-GANs generalize GANs to all f-divergences, and by Amari and others who established connections between KL divergence and Bregman divergences for exponential families. It significantly extends these results to the general f-divergence case, which has been a long-standing open problem in information geometry.

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical contributions. The authors establish several important theorems that build a complete theoretical framework connecting f-GANs to information geometry. The proofs are provided in the supplement and appear to be sound. The paper makes a significant step forward in understanding the theoretical foundations of GANs, which have previously been largely treated as black-box optimization problems.

The experimental section provides concrete validation of the theoretical claims, showing that carefully designed activation functions (e.g., μ-ReLU) and link functions can improve GAN performance. The experiments are well-designed, comparing multiple architectures (DCGAN, MLP) and datasets (MNIST, LSUN), and they isolate the effects of the two theoretical contributions (activation functions in generators and link functions in discriminators).

### Originality
This work is highly original. While connections between KL divergence and Bregman divergences for exponential families have been established [2,4,7,9,35], the authors extend this to the general f-divergence case for deformed exponential families, which is novel. The specific identity in Equation (3) appears to be a new theoretical contribution that fills a significant gap in the literature. The connection between deep neural networks and deformed exponential families (Theorem 9) is also novel, providing a theoretical justification for why deep architectures work well in GANs.

The paper also makes important contributions to understanding the supervised game in GANs by relating it to proper composite losses, which resolves a key ambiguity in Nowozin et al.'s formulation [30, Section 2.4].

### Clarity
The paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors carefully build the theoretical framework step by step, starting from basic definitions and gradually arriving at the main identity. The connections to previous work are clearly articulated, and the paper's contributions are well-positioned within the literature. The notation is consistent and well-motivated.

The experimental section is clear and well-structured, with appropriate comparisons and visualizations. The paper provides enough detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results.

### Significance
This work addresses a fundamental theoretical question about GANs that has been a topic of interest since the introduction of GANs. Understanding the theoretical underpinnings of GANs is crucial for advancing the field, as it can lead to better algorithms, improved training stability, and better theoretical guarantees. The authors provide a new theoretical framework that can guide future research on GANs and related generative models.

The practical implications are also significant, as the paper demonstrates that carefully designed activation functions (e.g., μ-ReLU) and link functions can improve GAN performance. This provides concrete guidance for practitioners, which is valuable for the machine learning community.

## Weaknesses

### Scope and Impact
While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper's impact may be limited to the GAN research community. The connections to information geometry are significant, but it's unclear whether this framework will lead to major practical improvements in generative modeling beyond what's already demonstrated in the experiments. The paper would benefit from more discussion about how these theoretical insights could lead to substantially better generative models or broader applications beyond image generation.

### Technical Depth
The connection between deep networks and deformed exponential families (Theorem 9) is theoretically interesting, but the practical implications for model design are somewhat limited. The paper shows that certain activation functions can be used to model escorts, but it doesn't provide clear guidance on how to select the optimal activation function for a given problem. The experiments show improvements for specific activation functions, but don't establish a systematic methodology for choosing them.

### Experimental Validation
The experiments are good but somewhat limited. They only consider MNIST and LSUN tower category, which are relatively simple datasets. It would be valuable to see results on more challenging datasets like CIFAR-10 or ImageNet to demonstrate the broader applicability of the approach. The paper also doesn't provide comparisons to other state-of-the-art GAN variants beyond the standard GAN and WGAN.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a connection between f-GANs and information geometry, filling a gap in the literature.
- The results are technically sound with rigorous proofs and experimental validation.
- The work provides a complete theoretical picture of the supervised GAN game, resolving an ambiguity in previous work.
- The paper demonstrates practical benefits of the theoretical insights through improved GAN performance with carefully designed activation and link functions.
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- The practical impact beyond the specific improvements demonstrated in the experiments is somewhat limited.
- The experiments are limited to a few datasets and architectures, which might not fully demonstrate the generality of the approach.
- The paper doesn't provide a clear methodology for selecting optimal activation functions or link functions for new problems.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the understanding of GANs and their connections to information geometry. The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important theoretical question in the field. While the practical implications are somewhat limited to the specific improvements demonstrated in the experiments, the theoretical framework provides valuable insights that could guide future research on GANs and generative modeling.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community, as it addresses a fundamental question about a highly influential class of models (GANs) and provides a new theoretical framework that connects to well-established concepts in information geometry. The work significantly advances our understanding of GANs beyond the empirical results that have dominated the literature.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the following points:
1. Expand the discussion on the broader implications of the theoretical framework for future research.
2. Consider adding experiments on more challenging datasets to better demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.
3. Provide more detailed guidance on how to select optimal activation functions and link functions for new problems.

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of GANs and will be of interest to researchers working on generative models, information geometry, and optimization. I believe it represents a high-quality contribution that would be valuable to the NIPS community.
