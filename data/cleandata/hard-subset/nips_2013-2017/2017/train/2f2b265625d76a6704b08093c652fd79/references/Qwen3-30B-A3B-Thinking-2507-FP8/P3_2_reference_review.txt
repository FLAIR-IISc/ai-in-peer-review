

# Peer Review: "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a formal connection between f-GANs and information geometry through a variational identity (eq. 3) that relates the f-GAN objective to Bregman divergences between parameters of distributions in deformed exponential families. The authors successfully demonstrate how deep neural networks can naturally model the escort distributions required by this framework, providing a theoretical foundation for why common activation functions (ReLU, ELU, Softplus) work well in GANs.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Novelty:** The paper fills a critical gap in the f-GAN literature by connecting the variational formulation to information geometry. Theorem 5-8 provide a rigorous bridge between f-divergences, KLχ divergences, and Bregman divergences, which is a substantial theoretical contribution. The connection between deep network architectures and deformed exponential families (Theorem 9) is particularly insightful, showing how neural network layers can be interpreted as modeling escort distributions.

2. **Completing the GAN Picture:** The paper successfully completes the "proper loss" framework for GANs (Section 5), which was previously left ambiguous in [30]. Theorem 12 explicitly shows how the link function in the discriminator should be chosen for proper composite losses, which is a valuable theoretical clarification.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments (Section 6) provide empirical support for the theoretical claims, showing that tuning activation functions and link functions can improve GAN performance. The comparison of µ-ReLU variations and Matsushita entropy demonstrates the practical relevance of the theory.

4. **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs. The mathematical development is rigorous, and the connection between different concepts is well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Practical Impact:** While the theoretical connection is elegant, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this perspective leads to *practical* improvements in GAN training beyond what's already known. The experimental results show modest improvements (Figure 1), but don't establish that this framework can solve major GAN challenges (e.g., mode collapse, training instability).

2. **Narrow Scope of Application:** The focus on deformed exponential families may be too restrictive for many practical GAN applications. Most real-world GAN applications involve complex data distributions that don't neatly fit into exponential family frameworks, making the theoretical connection less directly applicable.

3. **Ambiguity in "Information Geometric" Advantage:** The paper frequently emphasizes the "information geometric" nature of the framework but doesn't clearly articulate *why* this perspective is superior to the original f-divergence formulation for GAN training. The connection to geometric optimization (mentioned in the introduction) isn't sufficiently developed to show practical benefits.

4. **Comparative Analysis:** The experiments compare only a limited set of activation functions (µ-ReLU, Softplus, LSU) and link functions (sigmoid vs. Matsushita). A more comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art GAN architectures and training techniques would strengthen the claims.

**Comparison to Related Work:**

The paper builds effectively on Nowozin et al.'s f-GAN work [30] and Amari's information geometry framework [4]. It properly cites the relevant work on deformed exponential families [5, 13, 25] and proper composite losses [32]. However, it doesn't sufficiently engage with the broader literature on GAN theory, such as the work by Arjovsky et al. on Wasserstein GANs [6] or the theoretical analyses of GAN convergence [21, 22]. The connection to the more practical GAN literature could be strengthened.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality theoretical paper that makes a meaningful contribution to understanding the mathematical foundations of GANs. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. While the practical implications aren't fully demonstrated, the theoretical insights are valuable for the field.

The main limitation is the disconnect between the elegant theory and tangible improvements in GAN performance. The paper would benefit from a stronger discussion of how these insights could lead to practical algorithmic improvements, rather than just theoretical understanding.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of GANs through the lens of information geometry. While the practical impact isn't fully demonstrated, the theoretical novelty and rigor are sufficient for NeurIPS. The paper would be strengthened by more concrete examples of how these insights could lead to new GAN architectures or training methods, but the current work is already of sufficient quality for publication.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Provides a rigorous theoretical foundation connecting f-GANs to information geometry
- Completes the theoretical picture of the supervised GAN game
- Demonstrates how common activation functions naturally fit into the information geometric framework
- Well-written and well-structured mathematical development

*Con:*
- Limited demonstration of practical impact beyond modest experimental improvements
- Focus on deformed exponential families may be too narrow for general GAN applications
- Doesn't sufficiently articulate why the information geometric perspective offers advantages over the original f-divergence formulation

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that will be of interest to the information geometry and GAN communities, and it meets NeurIPS standards for technical quality and novelty.
