

# Peer Review: "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

## Summary

This paper establishes a profound connection between f-GANs (a generalization of GANs that optimizes f-divergences) and information geometry. The authors develop a variational information-geometric identity that reveals how the f-GAN game can be interpreted as a geometric optimization problem. Their key contribution is the identity:

f-GAN(P, escort(Q)) = D(θ‖ϑ) + Penalty(Q)

where P and Q are distributions from a deformed exponential family (a generalization of exponential families), θ and ϑ are their natural parameters, D is a Bregman divergence, and escort(Q) is the escort distribution of Q. This identity extends the work of Nowozin et al. [30] by connecting the f-GAN framework to information geometry, which has been a significant research area at NIPS (e.g., Amari's work on information geometry [4, 9]). The paper also provides theoretical justification for why deep architectures work well in GANs, showing that they can naturally model the escort distributions required by their framework.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Depth**: The paper establishes a fundamental theoretical connection between f-GANs and information geometry that has been missing from the literature. The variational information-geometric identity (Corollary 8) is elegant and provides a deeper theoretical foundation for the f-GAN framework. This represents a significant conceptual advance in understanding GANs.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The proofs provided in the supplement (as referenced in the paper) appear to be technically sound. The authors carefully establish the necessary mathematical foundations and provide constructive proofs for their key results. The connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences via deformed exponential families is rigorously developed.

3. **Practical Implications**: The paper provides clear practical guidance for improving GAN performance through better activation functions (Lemma 11) and tuning of the link function in the discriminator (Theorem 12). The experiments validate these theoretical insights, showing that carefully chosen activation functions (e.g., µ-ReLU with optimal µ) and link functions (e.g., Matsushita entropy) can improve GAN performance.

4. **Strong Connection to Broader Context**: The authors effectively connect their work to relevant literature in information geometry (Amari, Nagaoka [4, 9]) and GANs (Nowozin et al. [30], Arjovsky et al. [6]), demonstrating a thorough understanding of the field.

5. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-organized and logically structured, with a clear progression from theoretical foundations to practical implications. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, making the paper accessible to the target audience.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Validation**: While the experiments support the theoretical claims, they are limited to MNIST and LSUN tower datasets with only two architectures (DCGAN and MLP). More extensive experiments on diverse datasets and state-of-the-art architectures would strengthen the practical claims.

2. **Restrictive Assumption of Deformed Exponential Families**: The theoretical results require P and Q to lie in a deformed exponential family, which is a restrictive assumption. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how common this is in practical GAN applications or how to handle cases where this assumption doesn't hold.

3. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper doesn't directly compare the proposed approach to state-of-the-art GAN variants (e.g., StyleGAN, BigGAN) that have achieved significant performance improvements. It would be useful to see how the proposed method compares to these more recent advances.

4. **Accessibility for Practitioners**: While the theoretical framework is elegant, it's not entirely clear how practitioners can directly apply this understanding. The paper could better bridge the gap between theory and practice for a broader audience.

5. **Lack of Discussion on Failure Cases**: The paper acknowledges some limitations (e.g., the need for the deformed exponential family assumption), but a more thorough discussion of potential failure cases and limitations would strengthen the paper.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to understanding GANs through the lens of information geometry. The key identity (Corollary 8) is elegant and provides a deeper theoretical foundation for the f-GAN framework. The connection to deep architectures and the characterization of suitable activation functions (Lemma 11) are particularly valuable insights.

While the paper has some limitations in experimental validation and scope, the theoretical contribution is significant and novel. The work is clearly relevant to NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental question about the nature of GANs and their connection to information geometry, which has been a recurring theme at NIPS (e.g., Amari's work [4, 9]).

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should consider addressing the following points in the revision:

1. Expand the experimental section to include more diverse datasets and architectures (e.g., StyleGAN, BigGAN).
2. Provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the deformed exponential family assumption.
3. Include a comparison to state-of-the-art GAN variants to better demonstrate the practical impact of their findings.
4. Make the connection between the theoretical framework and practical implementation clearer for a broader audience.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

*Pro:*
- Theoretical contribution is novel and significant, providing a deeper understanding of GANs through information geometry.
- The connection between f-GANs and Bregman divergences is elegant and well-motivated.
- The characterization of activation functions that support the geometric interpretation (Lemma 11) has clear practical implications.
- The paper is technically sound and well-written.

*Con:*
- Experimental validation is limited to a small number of datasets and architectures.
- The deformed exponential family assumption may not hold in many practical applications.
- The paper could better connect the theoretical framework to current state-of-the-art GAN implementations.

Overall, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of GANs and is well-suited for publication at NIPS. The theoretical insights are substantial and could influence future work on GANs and generative modeling.
