

# Review of "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

This paper explores the connection between f-GANs [30] and information geometry through the lens of deformed exponential families. The authors establish a variational information-geometric identity (eq. 3) that relates the f-GAN game to Bregman divergences, demonstrating that f-GANs can be interpreted as geometric optimization problems. They further characterize the parameters of the supervised game, showing how the link function in the discriminator relates to proper composite losses, and analyze how deep architectures can model escorts of deformed exponential families.

**Strengths:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting two important frameworks: the f-divergence formulation in GANs and the Bregman divergence formulation in information geometry. This connection is novel and well-motivated, filling a gap in the understanding of the f-GAN framework. The theoretical results (Theorems 5-9, 12) are carefully derived and well-supported with proofs in the supplement. The paper also provides valuable insights into the design of generator architectures through the lens of information geometry, demonstrating how common activation functions (ReLU, ELU, Softplus) can satisfy the necessary conditions for modeling escorts of deformed exponential families.

The experimental section is a strength, as it provides empirical validation of the theoretical claims. The authors test how different activation functions and link functions affect GAN performance across multiple architectures (DCGAN, MLP) and datasets (MNIST, LSUN). The results show modest but consistent improvements with the proposed theoretical insights, particularly with the Matsushita link function and modified ReLU activations.

**Weaknesses:**

The main weakness is the limited practical significance of the results. While the theoretical connection is elegant, the paper doesn't present a novel algorithm or method that would substantially improve GAN training in practice. The experimental improvements are modest (e.g., "the ReLU baseline was essentially beaten for values of µ yielding smaller variance"), and the paper doesn't compare against state-of-the-art GAN methods beyond the basic GAN and WGAN baselines. The connection to practical GAN training could be strengthened by demonstrating how the proposed insights could be integrated into existing successful GAN implementations.

The paper also assumes a high level of familiarity with information geometry, which may limit its accessibility to the broader NIPS audience. While the authors provide definitions, the dense mathematical treatment might be challenging for readers without a background in differential geometry. A more intuitive explanation of the key insights would benefit the paper.

The connection to the f-GAN framework [30] could be more thoroughly discussed in terms of how this work advances the state of the art. While the paper correctly identifies a gap in [30] regarding the link between distributions and the generator, it doesn't clearly articulate how this fills a significant gap in the literature beyond being a theoretical extension.

**Pro and Con Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Makes a significant theoretical contribution connecting two important areas (f-GANs and information geometry)
- Provides novel insights into the mathematical foundations of GANs
- Theorems are well-supported with careful proofs
- Experiments provide empirical validation of theoretical claims
- Offers a principled approach to activation function design

*Con:*
- Limited practical impact compared to other GAN work at NIPS
- Modest experimental improvements that don't significantly advance the state of the art
- Assumes high mathematical sophistication from readers
- Doesn't clearly articulate how this work advances the field beyond theoretical interest
- Limited comparison to state-of-the-art GAN methods

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a theoretically interesting connection between information geometry and the f-GAN framework. While the theoretical contribution is strong, the practical significance is limited, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how these insights would substantially improve GAN training in practice. The modest experimental improvements don't justify a major advancement in the field.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Expand the discussion of practical implications and how these insights could be integrated into existing GAN frameworks for more substantial performance gains.

2. Include comparisons against state-of-the-art GAN methods beyond basic GAN and WGAN baselines to better demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach.

3. Provide more intuitive explanations of the key insights for readers without a strong background in information geometry.

4. Consider adding a section discussing how the proposed insights could be used to improve other aspects of GAN training beyond activation functions and link functions.

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to our understanding of GANs, but to strengthen its impact, the authors should more clearly demonstrate how these insights translate to meaningful improvements in practical GAN applications.
