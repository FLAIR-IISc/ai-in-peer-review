

# Peer Review: "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

## Summary
This paper makes a significant contribution by establishing a theoretical connection between f-GANs and information geometry. The authors show that the f-GAN objective can be expressed as a geometric optimization problem using Bregman divergences, when distributions lie in deformed exponential families (χ-exponential families) rather than just standard exponential families. Their key identity (eq. 3) demonstrates that f-GAN(P, escort(Q)) = D(θ‖ϑ) + Penalty(Q), where D is a Bregman divergence. This work builds upon Nowozin et al.'s [30] generalization of GANs to f-divergences and Amari's information geometry framework [4], but extends it to a much broader class of distributions and provides the missing geometric interpretation that was lacking in previous work.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contributions (Quality & Originality):** The paper makes several strong theoretical contributions. Theorem 5 establishes that f-divergences can be represented as KLχ divergences under mild assumptions, while Theorem 9 demonstrates that deep neural networks can naturally factorize escort distributions of deformed exponential families. The characterization of "strongly admissible" activation functions (Lemma 11) is particularly valuable, showing that most common activations (ReLU, ELU, Softplus) satisfy the necessary conditions. This is a novel connection between deep learning architectures and information geometry.

**Completing the GAN Picture (Significance):** The paper successfully completes the "proper loss" picture of the supervised GAN game, showing that the link function in the discriminator is not arbitrary but should be chosen as part of a proper composite loss (Theorem 12). This provides a principled way to tune the discriminator, which is a significant practical contribution to the GAN literature.

**Experimental Validation (Quality & Significance):** The experiments (Section 6) provide empirical support for the theoretical claims, demonstrating that tuning activation functions (e.g., µ-ReLU) and link functions (e.g., Matsushita entropy) can improve GAN performance across different architectures (DCGAN, MLP) and datasets (MNIST, LSUN). The careful separation of generator and discriminator tuning in the experiments is a strength.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope of Experiments (Quality):** While the experiments support the theoretical claims, they are limited to MNIST and LSUN datasets with relatively simple architectures (DCGAN and MLP). More comprehensive experiments on challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) would strengthen the significance claim by demonstrating broader applicability.

**Technical Depth of Information Geometry (Clarity):** The paper assumes familiarity with information geometry, which may be a barrier for many NIPS readers. While the authors provide definitions, the connection between f-GANs and information geometry could be made more accessible with a brief intuitive explanation of why Bregman divergences matter for GANs.

**Comparison to Related Work (Originality):** The paper mentions that previous work on generalizing the KL-Exponential family identity to f-divergences [5, 13] does not extend to the variational GAN formulation. However, it would be helpful to explicitly contrast their approach with recent works on geometric GANs [6, 14, 16, 21, 22] that explicitly optimize geometric distortions.

## Pro/Con Acceptance

**Pros:**
- Strong theoretical contribution connecting two important areas (f-divergences and information geometry)
- Novel characterization of activation functions that enable geometric modeling
- Practical implications for tuning GAN architectures
- Well-structured and clearly written paper
- Comprehensive supplement with proofs and additional experiments

**Cons:**
- Experiments limited to relatively simple datasets and architectures
- Some technical depth in information geometry might hinder broader impact
- Limited comparison to recent geometric GAN approaches

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of GANs by establishing a deep connection between f-GANs and information geometry. The main identity (eq. 3) is elegant and powerful, providing a principled geometric interpretation of the f-GAN game that was missing in previous work. The characterization of activation functions as "strongly admissible" is particularly valuable for practitioners, as it provides a theoretical basis for why many common activation functions work well in GANs.

The paper's significance is high because it provides both a deeper theoretical understanding of GANs and practical guidance for improving them. While the experiments could be more extensive, they are sufficient to support the theoretical claims. The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims and careful attention to the limitations of the approach.

This work advances the state of the art in understanding GANs by bridging the gap between information theory and information geometry. The connection between deep learning architectures and information geometry is particularly compelling, as it explains why certain neural network designs naturally align with the geometric structure of the optimization problem.

I recommend acceptance of this paper as a strong contribution to NIPS. It represents a substantial step forward in understanding the theoretical foundations of GANs, with clear implications for both theory and practice. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**
