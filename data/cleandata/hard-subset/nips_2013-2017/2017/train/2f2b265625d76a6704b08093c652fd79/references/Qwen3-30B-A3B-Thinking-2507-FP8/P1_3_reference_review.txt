

# Peer Review: "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

## Summary

This paper establishes a novel connection between f-GANs (a generalization of GANs that optimizes f-divergences) and information geometry. The authors demonstrate that the f-GAN objective can be interpreted as a Bregman divergence (a key concept in information geometry) when working with deformed exponential families (a generalization of exponential families), establishing the identity: f-GAN(P, escort(Q)) = D(θ‖ϑ) + Penalty(Q). They further show that deep generator architectures can naturally model escorts of deformed exponential families, providing a theoretical justification for why deep networks work well in GANs. The paper also completes the information-theoretic picture of the supervised GAN game by relating the discriminator's link function to proper composite losses.

This work builds on Nowozin et al.'s f-GAN framework [30] and Amari's information geometry [4], connecting two important but previously separate lines of research in machine learning. The authors position their contribution as filling a gap in understanding how the f-GAN approach relates to information geometric optimization, which is significant given the popularity of GANs.

## Strengths

1. **High theoretical quality**: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution with rigorous proofs of new identities connecting f-divergences, Bregman divergences, and deformed exponential families. The proofs appear sound, with references to supplementary materials for completeness.

2. **Novel conceptual insight**: The connection between f-GANs and information geometry is genuinely novel and addresses a gap in the literature. The authors correctly identify that the f-GAN framework has been largely viewed through the lens of f-divergences without linking to information geometry, which has been successfully applied to other generative models like Wasserstein GANs.

3. **Strong practical implications**: The paper provides concrete guidance for practitioners, showing that common activation functions (ReLU, ELU, Softplus) satisfy the theoretical conditions for modeling escorts of deformed exponential families. This bridges the gap between theory and practice.

4. **Comprehensive treatment**: The paper not only establishes the theoretical identity but also derives consequences for deep learning, completes the information-theoretic picture of the supervised game, and provides experimental validation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited experimental validation**: While the theoretical contributions are strong, the empirical validation is somewhat limited. The authors test only a few activation functions (µ-ReLU variants) and one alternative link function (Matsushita entropy), without comparing against other state-of-the-art GAN architectures or training techniques. The improvements shown are modest (e.g., "Matsushita is better than sigmoid for both DCGAN and MLP" without quantifying the improvement).

2. **Accessibility**: The paper is highly technical and assumes significant background in information geometry, making it challenging for readers outside this niche. The connection between the theoretical results and practical GAN implementation could be made more explicit for a broader audience.

3. **Overstated significance**: The claim that their work "completes the supervised understanding" of f-GANs might be overstated. While they provide important theoretical insights, it's not clear how much this changes the practical implementation of GANs beyond suggesting specific activation functions.

4. **Lack of comparison to related work**: The paper references [5, 13] as generalizations of exponential families that fall short of the f-divergence formulation, but doesn't thoroughly compare their approach to other recent works on GAN theory and information geometry (e.g., [14, 21, 22] mentioned in the introduction).

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that connects two important areas in machine learning (GANs and information geometry). The theoretical insights are sound, novel, and well-motivated. While the experimental validation is somewhat limited, the theoretical contribution stands on its own merits. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

## Specific Comments for Authors

1. **Clarity**: Consider adding a more intuitive explanation of the key identity (equation 3) before diving into the technical details. A simple example with a specific choice of f (e.g., the original GAN f) would help readers grasp the concept.

2. **Experiments**: Expand the experimental section to include more comprehensive comparisons against state-of-the-art GAN architectures and training techniques. Quantify the improvements (e.g., by reporting FID scores or Inception scores) to better demonstrate the practical impact.

3. **Context**: While the paper references relevant work, explicitly compare your approach to recent information-geometric GAN formulations (e.g., [14, 21, 22]) to better position your contribution.

4. **Practical implications**: Discuss how your theoretical insights might inform the design of future GAN architectures beyond activation functions (e.g., network architecture, loss functions).

## Conclusion

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing a novel connection between f-GANs and information geometry. The theoretical results are sound and significant, though the practical validation could be strengthened. The paper would be a strong addition to NIPS, advancing our theoretical understanding of GANs and providing a foundation for future work in the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity and experimental concerns mentioned above.
