- Core contribution: establishes a theoretical connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences via deformed exponential families, framing f-GANs as a geometric optimization problem.  
- Shows deep generator architectures can model escorts of deformed exponential families and derives implications for activation function and discriminator loss design.  
- Correctly identifies and fixes a missing link-function issue in Nowozin et al.'s composite-loss formulation.  
- Well-motivated approach using information geometry, providing a solid theoretical foundation and clever framing.  
- Writing and organization are clear overall, with proofs provided in supplement as standard.  
- Theoretical results are elegant but abstract; connection to practical GAN training and actionable architectural design is tenuous.  
- Experimental validation is weak and limited (MNIST and a single LSUN category), lacking strong comparisons to state-of-the-art GANs.  
- Evaluation metrics for activation-function impact are not fully justified.  
- Heavy reliance on the deformed exponential family assumption; applicability to complex, real-world distributions is unclear.  
- Notation is dense and heavy use of definitions/theorems occasionally obscures the main narrative.  
- Lacks in-depth comparison to more recent GAN advances (e.g., spectral normalization, alternative losses).  
- Strengths summary: novel theoretical grounding in information geometry; insights into deep generators and correction to prior work.  
- Weaknesses summary: limited practical implications, weak experiments, strong modeling assumptions, and dense presentation.  
- Recommendation: weak accept — interesting theoretical contribution but needs stronger experimental validation and discussion of practical applicability; encourage follow-up work linking to recent GAN techniques.
