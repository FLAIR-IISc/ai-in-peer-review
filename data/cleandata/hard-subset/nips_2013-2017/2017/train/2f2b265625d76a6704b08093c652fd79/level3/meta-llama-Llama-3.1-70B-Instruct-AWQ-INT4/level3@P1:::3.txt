This paper provides a novel and interesting perspective on the theoretical foundations of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors address several key questions about the link between the GAN solution and parameter convergence, the choice of generator and discriminator architectures, and the selection of output activation functions for compositeness.

One of the main contributions of this paper is the introduction of a variational generalization connecting f-divergences between distributions to Bregman divergences between parameters. This result is derived using the framework of deformed exponential distributions, which provides a powerful tool for analyzing the behavior of GANs. The authors show that under a reversibility assumption, deep generators can factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions, which has important implications for the design of GAN architectures.

The paper also provides several practical suggestions for improving the performance of GANs, including careful choices of generator hidden activations and discriminator output activation functions. Additionally, the authors offer an alternative GAN interpretation via expected utility theory, which provides a new perspective on the GAN framework.

However, the reviewer raises several concerns about the practical relevance and usefulness of the results in their current form. One major concern is that many of the results assume that the data and model lie in the deformed exponential family, and it is unclear whether these results extend beyond this assumption. The reviewer also questions the practical benefits and implementability of the parameter-space f-GAN identity (Eq. 7), and requests clarification on how this result can be used in practice.

Furthermore, the reviewer criticizes the authors for underusing the right-hand side of Eq. 7 and requests an explanation of why convergence in divergence might not imply parameter convergence with a realistic example. The reviewer also questions the practical implication of the result on output activation functions and requests experimental comparison to prior heuristics (Ref. [34]).

The experiments presented in the paper are inconclusive, and the reviewer requests more extensive experimental evaluation to support the theoretical claims. Specifically, the reviewer notes that μ-ReLU (theoretically preferred) performs worse than ReLU, and requests an explanation for this result. The reviewer also requests a discussion on the reversibility of DCGAN and comparison to WGAN.

In terms of minor comments, the reviewer requests more intuition on deformations, signatures, and escorts for readers unfamiliar with deformed exponential densities. The reviewer also asks for a comparison among the upper bounds for J(Q) in Theorem 8, implications for activation choice, and an explanation of why decreasing bound with escort normalization Z is beneficial.

Overall, while the theoretical insights presented in this paper are interesting, the reviewer concludes that they are not substantial or convincing enough for GAN practitioners in their current form. The reviewer recommends that the authors address the concerns raised above and provide more extensive experimental evaluation to support their theoretical claims.
