Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper develops an information-geometric perspective on f-GANs. Its main technical contribution is a variational identity that ties an f-divergence between distributions to a Bregman divergence between the parameters when the data and model lie in a deformed (χ-)exponential family and the generator distribution is replaced by its escort: sup_T E_P[T] − E_{Q̃}[(−log_χ_{Q̃})*(T)] = D_C(θ‖ϑ) + J(Q). This variational generalization extends the classical KL/Bregman identity for regular exponential families to a broader class (deformed exponentials), and connects distribution-level divergences to parameter-space geometry in a way that resembles the geometric motivations of WGANs. Under a reversibility assumption on the generator, the authors also show that deep generators factor as products of escorts of deformed exponential distributions, which yields design implications for hidden activations. On the discriminator side, the paper reframes f-GAN training via proper composite losses, identifying the discriminator “output activation” g_f as the link function Ψ, thereby grounding the choice of output activation from first principles and suggesting alternative links (e.g., Matsushita). There is a brief alternative interpretation via expected utility/certainty equivalents. Experiments on MNIST and LSUN compare μ-ReLU/Softplus/LSU in the generator and link functions in the discriminator; results are mixed.

The work builds on Nowozin et al. (f-GAN), the KL/Bregman identity for exponential families, information geometry (Amari), and proper composite losses (Reid & Williamson), while intersecting with GANs that explicitly optimize geometric objectives (WGAN).

Strengths:
- Conceptually interesting: a clean bridge from f-divergences to Bregman divergences via χ-escorts, expanding the classical KL/exponential family identity.
- The escort-factorization theorem for reversible deep generators is novel and provides a constructive link between activations and the χ-signature; the admissibility conditions cover popular activations (ELU, Softplus, ReLU weakly).
- Clarifies the discriminator’s output activation as a proper composite link, making “compositeness” explicit and suggesting principled choices beyond sigmoid.
- The variational identity articulates conditions under which convergence in divergence implies convergence in parameters.

Weaknesses and major concerns:
- Heavy modeling assumptions: Many results require that both P and Q lie in a χ-exponential family and that the generator be reversible. This is strong for practical GANs. Please justify why realistic image distributions and standard DCGAN/WGAN architectures would satisfy (or approximate) these assumptions, and clarify how the conclusions degrade when they do not.
- Practicality of the parameter-space identity (Eq. 11): The RHS involves D_C(θ‖ϑ) + J(Q) with an escort normalization Z(Q). In typical GANs, the generator is implicit, so evaluating densities, χ, escorts, and Z is intractable. What is the concrete benefit for practitioners? Can one implement parameter-space optimization using only samples (e.g., stochastic bounds/estimators for J(Q) and Z)? Please include an experiment optimizing the parameter-space objective and compare to standard distribution-based f-GAN training.
- Underuse of the RHS of Eq. 11: The paper does not exploit J(Q) or the Bregman term algorithmically. If the point is that convergence in divergence may not imply parameter convergence, provide a realistic counterexample (e.g., non-identifiable neural generators with weight symmetries, or mixture models with label switching) and explain how Eq. 11 avoids this pitfall under your assumptions.
- Reversibility assumption: DCGAN layers (strided/deconv, ReLU) are not generally invertible; even fractional-strided convolutions are not bijections and ReLU has flat regions. Please either (i) adopt truly invertible architectures (e.g., NICE/RealNVP/Glow-style flows) to empirically validate Theorem 9 or (ii) formalize an “approximate reversibility” argument with error bounds.
- Experiments are inconclusive: μ-ReLU, theoretically preferred, often underperforms ReLU; Table 2 differences appear within error bars. Please explain the discrepancy (e.g., weak admissibility of ReLU, training dynamics, mismatch to reversibility) and add comparisons to WGAN and Hinge/LSGAN losses. For the discriminator link, compare against established heuristics and loss choices from Salimans et al. (Ref. [34]) and later strong baselines (e.g., spectral norm + hinge), not just sigmoid/no-link.
- Scope beyond χ-exponentials: Are there extensions when P is outside the deformed exponential family but Q is inside, or vice versa? Can the identity be turned into upper/lower bounds that still yield useful training criteria in misspecified settings?

Quality:
- The theoretical development appears careful and builds on known constructs (χ-logarithms, escorts, Bregman). However, several proofs are deferred; given the centrality of Theorems 5–7 and 9, more intuition, special cases, and sanity checks would improve confidence.
- Experimental support is too limited to substantiate design recommendations.

Clarity:
- The paper is dense. Please add intuitive explanations and figures for deformations/signatures/escorts, including concrete χ examples (e.g., Tsallis), their induced activations, and what “escort” means in sampling terms.
- Unify equation numbering and clearly state implementability assumptions (availability of densities vs samples).
- Provide a step-by-step recipe: given an f and an activation v, how to obtain χ and Ψ used in practice.

Originality:
- High on the theory side: a general variational identity linking f-divergences to parameter Bregman divergences via escorts, and a novel factorization result for deep generators.

Significance:
- Potentially impactful for theory of GANs and information geometry. Practical significance is currently limited by strong assumptions and lack of compelling empirical gains.

Specific questions and requests:
1) Justify the χ-exponential family assumption: When and why should natural image distributions or standard GAN generator families be well-approximated by χ-exponentials? Any empirical diagnostics?
2) Parameter non-identifiability: How does Eq. 11 reconcile with weight symmetries/permutations in deep nets that break uniqueness of ϑ? Does D_C(θ‖ϑ) remain meaningful under such reparameterizations?
3) Implementability: Can J(Q) and Z be bounded/estimated from samples? Provide tractable upper bounds for J(Q) (referenced as Theorem/Corollary 8) and discuss how decreasing the bound via controlling Z informs activation or architecture choice.
4) Activation design: Explain why μ-ReLU underperforms ReLU in some settings despite theory. Does “weak admissibility” suffice? Include ablations on invertibility and depth/width.
5) Discriminator link: Compare Matsushita vs sigmoid/hinge/identity across datasets and architectures, and against prior heuristics (Ref. [34]), reporting Fréchet Inception Distance and precision/recall where applicable.
6) Expected utility view: Please expand, including the precise utility/risk measures and how they reproduce the variational objectives.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant and original theory; bridges information geometry and GANs.
- Clear conceptual guidance on the role of activations and discriminator links.
- May inspire future algorithmic developments (e.g., escort-aware generators, invertible architectures).

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong assumptions with limited empirical validation.
- Unclear practical benefits; key identity not turned into an implementable training procedure.
- Experiments inconclusive; recommended design choices not consistently superior.

Overall:
An interesting theoretical contribution with promising ideas on activations and discriminator compositeness, but currently not substantial or convincing enough for GAN practitioners. Strengthening empirical validation, clarifying implementability, and relaxing assumptions would significantly improve the paper.
