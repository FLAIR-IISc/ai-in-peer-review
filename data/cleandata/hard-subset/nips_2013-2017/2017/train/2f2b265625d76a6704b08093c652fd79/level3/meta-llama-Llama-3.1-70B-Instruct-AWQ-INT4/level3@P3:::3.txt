This paper provides an interesting theoretical framework for understanding Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) through the lens of information geometry. The authors address several key questions about GANs, including the link between the GAN solution and parameter convergence, what generator fits at convergence, and choosing discriminator output activation for compositeness.

The paper uses a deformed exponential distributions framework to derive theoretical results about GANs. The authors present a variational generalization connecting f-divergences between distributions to Bregman divergences between parameters. They show that under a reversibility assumption, deep generators factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions.

The paper suggests that careful choices of generator hidden activations and discriminator output activation may improve GANs. The authors also offer an alternative GAN interpretation via expected utility theory. However, the reviewer finds the theory interesting but questions its practical relevance and usefulness in its current form.

One major concern is that many results assume data and model lie in the deformed exponential family, which needs justification and exploration of whether the results extend beyond this assumption. Another concern is the parameter-space f-GAN identity (Eq.7), which requires clarification of its practical benefits, implementability, and experimental comparison to distribution-based optimization.

The authors underuse the RHS of Eq.7 and must explain why convergence in divergence might not imply parameter convergence with a realistic example. The practical implication about output activation gf is interesting but needs experimental comparison to prior heuristics (Ref. [34]).

The experiments are inconclusive, as μ-ReLU (theoretically preferred) performs worse than ReLU. The authors should explain this and discuss reversibility in DCGAN and comparison to WGAN. The results in Table 2 appear within error bars, providing insufficient empirical support for theoretical claims.

Some minor suggestions include providing more intuition on deformations, signatures, and escorts for readers unfamiliar with deformed exponential densities. The authors should also compare among the upper bounds for J(Q) in Theorem 8, implications for activation choice, and explain why decreasing the bound with escort normalization Z is beneficial.

Overall, the theoretical insights in this paper are interesting but currently not substantial or convincing enough for GAN practitioners. The authors need to address the concerns mentioned above and provide more empirical evidence to support their claims.
