

# Review of "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

This paper addresses fundamental theoretical questions about GANs, specifically exploring the connection between f-divergence minimization and parameter convergence in the generator. It builds on Nowozin et al.'s (NIPS 2016) generalization of GANs to all f-divergences by introducing a variational information geometric identity that relates f-divergences to Bregman divergences via deformed exponential families. The authors show that for distributions in this family, the f-GAN game can be equivalently formulated as a geometric optimization problem, with the generator converging to a parameter that minimizes a Bregman divergence.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing a variational identity (Corollary 8) that connects f-divergences to Bregman divergences in the context of GANs. This provides a principled understanding of generator convergence in parameter space, which is missing in prior work. The results about deep generators factorizing escorts of deformed exponential families (Theorem 9) are mathematically interesting and suggest a theoretical basis for why certain activation functions work well in practice. The paper also completes the supervised game picture by showing how proper composite losses (Theorem 12) relate to the f-GAN framework, providing a more principled way to choose the discriminator's output activation function.

**Weaknesses:**
The major weakness is the strong assumption that both data and generator distributions lie within deformed exponential families. The paper provides no justification for why this assumption holds in realistic applications, nor does it address whether results extend beyond this narrow class of distributions. The experiments (Section 6) fail to convincingly demonstrate the practical value of the theoretical insights. For example, the theoretically preferred μ-ReLU activation performs worse than standard ReLU in most cases, and the authors do not adequately explain this contradiction. The paper also fails to provide a clear practical benefit of the parameter-space convergence (Theorem 8) over the more commonly used divergence-based convergence. The authors underuse the right-hand side of their key equation (Eq.7), and do not provide a concrete example where parameter convergence is practically useful but divergence convergence is not.

**Quality:** The theoretical results are technically sound and well-supported by the mathematical derivations. However, the paper lacks a discussion of the practical limitations of its assumptions, which weakens the overall contribution. The experimental results do not provide sufficient empirical evidence to support the theoretical claims, as the performance differences are often within error bars.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and mathematically precise, but the exposition of deformed exponential families and escort distributions may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with these concepts. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of the key mathematical constructs, particularly the role of the signature function χ and the escort distribution.

**Originality:** The connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences via deformed exponential families is novel in the GAN context. While related work exists in information geometry (Amari, 2000), the paper successfully adapts these concepts to the GAN framework in a way that provides new theoretical insights.

**Significance:** The theoretical insights are interesting but currently lack clear practical significance for GAN practitioners. The paper does not convincingly demonstrate how the proposed framework can improve current GAN implementations. The experimental results are inconclusive and fail to show meaningful performance gains over existing approaches. The paper also doesn't adequately address how the results might generalize beyond the restrictive deformed exponential family assumption.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Provides a novel theoretical connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences in the GAN context
- Offers a principled understanding of generator convergence in parameter space
- Completes the supervised GAN game picture with proper composite losses
- Theoretical results about activation functions could inform future GAN design

*Con:*
- Strong assumption of deformed exponential family membership lacks justification
- Experimental results fail to demonstrate practical benefits
- Parameter-space convergence claim lacks clear practical utility
- Theoretical framework doesn't address how to handle realistic data outside the assumption
- Inconclusive experiments (μ-ReLU performs worse than ReLU)

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution to the GAN literature but currently falls short of providing convincing practical insights for the NIPS community. The theoretical results are sound but are built on assumptions that are unlikely to hold in realistic GAN applications. The experimental results fail to validate the theoretical claims, and the paper does not sufficiently explain why the parameter-space convergence is practically useful. While the ideas are promising, the current version lacks sufficient evidence to demonstrate their value to practitioners.

The authors should address the following before acceptance:
1. Justify why real-world data distributions might lie within deformed exponential families
2. Provide stronger experimental evidence demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed framework
3. Clarify the practical significance of parameter-space convergence compared to divergence-based convergence
4. Address the performance gap between μ-ReLU and ReLU in experiments

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution remains largely theoretical without clear pathways to practical implementation. I recommend rejection, but with encouragement to address these concerns in a future version.
