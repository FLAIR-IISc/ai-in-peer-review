

# Review: "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell"

This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the understanding of GANs by connecting them to information geometry through deformed exponential families. The authors build on Nowozin et al.'s f-GAN framework [30], extending it with a variational identity that relates f-divergences to Bregman divergences. Their key insight is that for distributions in deformed exponential families (a superset of exponential families), the f-GAN objective can be expressed as a geometric optimization problem between parameters, rather than just between distributions.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is Theorem 7 and Corollary 8, which establish the "variational information-geometric f-GAN identity" (Eq. 11). This identity provides a parameter-space perspective on GAN convergence, showing that solving the f-GAN game guarantees convergence in parameter space when distributions lie in the deformed exponential family. The authors further show that deep generators can factorize escorts of deformed exponential distributions under a reversibility assumption, with popular activation functions (ReLU, ELU, Softplus) satisfying the conditions for this factorization.

The paper also completes the supervised GAN game picture by showing how the discriminator's output activation function relates to proper composite losses [32], providing a principled interpretation for the "link function" left arbitrary in [30].

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences is novel and elegant, addressing a gap in the GAN literature regarding parameter-space convergence.
- The deformed exponential framework provides a unifying perspective for understanding various GAN variants.
- The paper offers a principled approach to generator activation design and discriminator output activation, with experimental validation showing potential improvements.
- The mathematical treatment is rigorous and well-structured, with comprehensive proofs in the supplement.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Major concern: The deformed exponential family assumption lacks justification.** The paper's key results (Theorem 9, Corollary 8, and the entire theoretical framework) require data and model distributions to lie within deformed exponential families, which is a strong restriction. The authors provide no evidence that real-world data (like images in MNIST or LSUN) actually lie in this family. Without this, the theoretical convergence guarantees are largely irrelevant for practical GAN applications. The paper should either provide empirical evidence that real data approximately satisfies this condition or explicitly discuss the limitations of the results beyond this family.

- **Major concern: The practical value of the parameter-space identity (Eq. 11) is unclear.** The paper presents Eq. 11 as a key theoretical contribution but fails to clarify its practical benefits. How does this identity help with implementation or optimization in real GANs? The authors claim it "guarantees convergence in parameter space," but they don't explain how this differs from or improves upon existing convergence guarantees (e.g., for WGAN [6]). A detailed comparison with distribution-based optimization approaches would be valuable.

- **Experimental limitations:** The experiments in Section 6 are inconclusive. The theoretically preferred μ-ReLU activation (with μ < 1) consistently performs worse than ReLU (μ = 1) in most settings, contradicting the theoretical claims. The authors do not adequately explain this discrepancy or discuss whether the reversibility assumption holds in standard architectures like DCGAN. The results in Table 2 appear within error bars, providing insufficient empirical support for the theoretical claims. The authors should conduct additional experiments comparing their approach to prior work (e.g., [34]) and provide a thorough analysis of the μ-ReLU results.

- **Theoretical gap:** The paper states that "solving the f-GAN game can guarantee convergence in the parameter space" but doesn't address the critical issue that convergence in divergence (which the f-GAN objective approximates) does not necessarily imply convergence in parameter space. The authors should provide a concrete example where this disconnect occurs in practice.

**Minor concerns:**
- The paper would benefit from more intuition about deformations, signatures, and escorts for readers unfamiliar with deformed exponential densities.
- The authors underuse the RHS of Eq. 7 (the parameter-space identity) and don't fully explain why convergence in divergence might not imply parameter convergence.
- The comparison among the upper bounds for J(Q) in Theorem 8 is unclear, and the authors should explain why decreasing this bound with escort normalization Z is beneficial.

**Overall assessment:**
The paper presents interesting theoretical insights that could potentially inform future GAN design. The connection between information geometry and GANs is novel and well-executed mathematically. However, the results are currently too restricted to deformed exponential families to be practically relevant for most GAN applications. The experimental validation is insufficient to support the theoretical claims, and the paper fails to adequately address the disconnect between theoretical guarantees and practical GAN performance.

While the theoretical contribution is significant for information geometry and the f-GAN framework, the paper falls short of providing substantial or convincing insights for GAN practitioners. The key theoretical results require assumptions that are unlikely to hold for real-world data, and the experimental results contradict the central theoretical claim about activation functions. Without stronger empirical evidence or a more general theoretical framework, the practical impact of this work remains unclear.

**Recommendation:** Weak reject. The paper makes an interesting theoretical contribution but fails to demonstrate its practical relevance or provide sufficient evidence to support its central claims. The authors should either extend their analysis to more general distribution classes or provide stronger empirical justification for their assumptions before the results can be considered compelling for the GAN community.
