- Novel perspective on GAN theory addressing GAN solution vs parameter convergence, architecture choices, and output activation compositeness.  
- Main contribution: a variational generalization linking f-divergences between distributions to Bregman divergences between parameters via deformed exponential distributions.  
- Under a reversibility assumption, deep generators can factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions, with implications for GAN design.  
- Practical recommendations on generator hidden activations and discriminator output activation functions.  
- Alternative interpretation of GANs via expected utility theory.  
- Major concern: results rely on the data/model being in the deformed exponential family; unclear generalizability beyond that assumption.  
- Questionable practical utility/implementability of the parameter-space f-GAN identity (Eq. 7); authors should clarify real-world use.  
- Critique that the right-hand side of Eq. 7 is underused; request for a realistic example where divergence convergence does not imply parameter convergence.  
- Request experimental comparison of output-activation results to prior heuristics (Ref. [34]) and explanation why μ-ReLU performs worse than ReLU.  
- Experiments are inconclusive; reviewer asks for more extensive empirical evaluation and discussion (e.g., reversibility of DCGAN, comparison to WGAN).  
- Minor request: provide more intuition on deformations, signatures, and escorts for readers unfamiliar with deformed exponential densities.  
- Minor request: compare upper bounds for J(Q) in Theorem 8, discuss activation-choice implications, and explain why decreasing the bound with escort normalization Z is beneficial.  
- Overall judgment: interesting theoretical insights but not yet substantial or convincing enough for practitioners in current form.  
- Scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 6/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel perspective, variational generalization, practical suggestions.  
- Arguments for rejection: restrictive assumptions, insufficient experimental support, and questionable practical relevance.
