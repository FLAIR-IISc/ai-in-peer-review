

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a novel information-geometric (IG) framework for f-GANs, extending Nowozin et al.'s work [30] by connecting f-divergences to Bregman divergences through deformed exponential families. The core insight is the variational information-geometric identity (Corollary 8), which shows that f-GAN optimization can be interpreted as geometric parameter-space optimization: f-GAN(P, Q) = D(θ‖ϑ) + Penalty(Q), where D is a Bregman divergence and θ, ϑ are natural parameters of deformed exponential families. This identity is a major theoretical advance, as it provides a rigorous foundation for understanding generator convergence in f-GANs, which Nowozin et al. left unaddressed.

The paper's strongest contribution is Theorem 8 (the variational information-geometric identity), which elegantly bridges information theory and information geometry. It demonstrates that f-GANs can be viewed as optimizing Bregman divergences between natural parameters, rather than just f-divergences between distributions. This insight has practical implications: it explains why deep architectures work well in f-GANs by showing that standard activation functions (ReLU, ELU, Softplus) satisfy a sufficient condition for factorizing escort densities of deformed exponential families. Theorem 9 provides a constructive proof that deep networks can efficiently represent complex deformed exponential families, with the factorization complexity being O(L·d²) versus Ω(L²·d²) for shallow networks.

The paper also makes valuable contributions to understanding the supervised game in f-GANs. Theorem 12 clarifies the role of the discriminator's link function in proper composite losses, showing how it should be chosen to ensure the loss is proper. This resolves an ambiguity in Nowozin et al.'s formulation regarding the choice of the link function gf.

However, the paper suffers from significant organizational and presentation issues that diminish its impact. The IG framework's utility is not clearly articulated early in the paper, making it difficult for readers to grasp why this perspective matters. The connection between the IG view and practical f-GAN design could be stronger—Section 5 on proper losses doesn't clearly explain how to choose f or design f-GANs, and the experiments (Section 6) are largely disconnected from the main theoretical claims. The activation function experiments don't require the IG framework to interpret their results, and the WGAN experiments distract from the paper's focus on f-GANs.

Several technical points require clarification. The claim that φ_l in Eq. (12) are "deep sufficient statistics" (Section 4) is questionable, as Eq. (13) doesn't match the form of sufficient statistics in deformed exponential families. The utility-theory paragraph (lines 257-269) is tangential and should be moved to an appendix. The paper would benefit from moving Fig. 1 (IT-IG connections) from the appendix to the main text for better flow.

Quality-wise, the proofs appear technically sound (the reviewer verified most, except Theorem 8), and the paper makes a genuine theoretical contribution to understanding GANs. The results advance the state of the art by providing a rigorous geometric foundation for f-GANs, which has implications for understanding convergence behavior and designing better architectures. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the paper's dense presentation and the fact that the practical guidance it offers (activation function design) is not clearly tied to the IG framework.

Originality is strong—the connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences via deformed exponential families appears novel, and the paper significantly extends Nowozin et al.'s work. The paper also demonstrates that deformed exponential families are a natural framework for understanding GANs, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

Clarity is the paper's main weakness. The theoretical exposition is dense, and the connection to practical applications is not sufficiently emphasized. The paper would benefit from a clearer statement of the IG framework's utility early in the introduction, and a more focused presentation that better connects the theoretical results to practical f-GAN design.

Overall, this paper makes an important theoretical contribution to understanding f-GANs through information geometry. While the paper's presentation needs significant improvement to fully realize its potential impact, the core insights are valuable and well-supported. The theoretical results advance the field by providing a rigorous geometric perspective on f-GAN convergence and generator design. Given the importance of understanding GANs' theoretical foundations, I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the paper would benefit from improved clarity and organization. The reviewers should note that the paper is currently too technical for many readers, and a clearer exposition would make this valuable contribution more accessible to the broader community.
