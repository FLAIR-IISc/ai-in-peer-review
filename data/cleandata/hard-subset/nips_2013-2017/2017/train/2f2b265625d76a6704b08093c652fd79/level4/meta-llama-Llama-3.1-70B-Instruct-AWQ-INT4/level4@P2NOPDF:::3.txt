The authors identify several intriguing GAN-related questions and attempt to address them within the framework of deformed exponential distributions. These questions include the extent to which solving the GAN problem implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator is actually fitting when convergence occurs, and how to choose the output activation function of the discriminator to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function.

The authors present several theoretical results, including a variational generalization of a known theorem that relates an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between the parameters of such distributions. This theorem provides an interesting connection between the information-theoretic viewpoint of measuring dissimilarity between probability distributions and the information-geometric perspective of measuring dissimilarity between the corresponding parameters. They also show that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Furthermore, their theoretical investigation suggests that a careful choice of the hidden activation functions of the generator and the output activation function of the discriminator could potentially improve GANs. Additionally, they briefly mention an alternative interpretation of the GAN game in the context of expected utility theory.

While I find the authors' theoretical results interesting, I have several concerns regarding the practical relevance and usefulness of their results in their current form.

Questions and Concerns:

Firstly, many of the authors' theorems and resulting insights hold for the specific case where the data and model distributions P and Q are members of the deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption and elaborate on whether it is met in practice? Could similar results, such as Theorems 4 and 6, be derived without this assumption?

One of the authors' main contributions, the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq.(7), relates the well-known variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. Can the authors explain the benefits of this parameter-based point of view? Is it possible to implement GANs in terms of this parameter-based optimization, and what would be the advantages? I would have liked to see experimental results comparing the two approaches to optimizing GANs. The authors do not seem to utilize the right-hand side of this identity, except to assert that solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space (provided the residual J(Q) is small). Can the authors provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence formulation over distributions does not imply convergence in parameter space?

As an interesting practical implication of their theoretical investigation, the authors show that the choice of the output activation function gf matters for the GAN loss to have the desirable proper compositeness property. I found this result particularly interesting and would have liked to see how their theoretically derived gf compares experimentally against the heuristic choice provided in Ref. [34].

Consequences for Deep Learning and Experimental Results:

Under the assumption that the generator network is reversible, the authors show that there exists an activation function v such that the generator's hidden layers factor exactly as escorts for the deformed exponential family. In Table 2 (left), the authors compare several generator architectures against different choices of activation functions (that may or may not comply with this escort factorization). However, the plot for the GAN MLP indicates that the "theoretically superior" μ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU (limiting case μ → 1). From their analysis, I would expect the invertible μ-ReLU (satisfying the reversibility assumption) to perform better than the non-invertible baseline (not satisfying the reversibility assumption). Can the authors explain why the μ-ReLU performs worse? Can they comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption and how these results compare against the Wasserstein GAN, which is not based on f-divergences (and for which I do not expect their theoretical analysis to hold)? Finally, as far as I can tell, their results on whether one activation (Table 2 center) or link function (Table 2 right) performs better than the others are all within error bars. I do not think this provides sufficient support for their theoretical results to be relevant in practice.

Minor Concerns:

Many of the authors' results are derived within the framework of deformed exponential densities. As the general ML community might not yet be familiar with this, I would welcome it if the authors could provide more intuition about what a deformation (or signature) and an escort are.

In Theorem 8, the authors derive several upper bounds for J(Q). Can they elaborate on how these bounds compare against each other and what the implications are for choosing activation functions? They also mention that this upper bound on J(Q) is decreasing with the normalization parameter of the escort Z. Can they explain why this is beneficial and how we can leverage this?

In light of these comments, I believe the insights gained from the theoretical analysis are not substantial enough from the point of view of a GAN practitioner.
