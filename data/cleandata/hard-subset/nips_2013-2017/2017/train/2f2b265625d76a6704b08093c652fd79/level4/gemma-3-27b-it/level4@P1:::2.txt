This paper proposes an information geometric (IG) view of the f-GAN algorithm. It first demonstrates that f-GAN convergence can be understood through a one-to-one mapping between f-divergence, chi-divergence, and a Bregman divergence result. It then discusses a principled way to implement f-GAN, and finally, presents a factorization result for deep neural network representations, alongside a discussion of activation function choices linked to the chi (or f) function. While I did not meticulously check every detail in the appendix, the proofs appear correct (with the exception of Theorem 8, which I did not have time to fully review).

I find this paper to be very dense and contain numerous novel results potentially of significant interest to machine learning and information theory. I am particularly impressed by the exposition of Theorem 6, which illuminates the information geometric aspects of f-GAN. Therefore, I recommend accepting this paper. 

However, I believe the paper’s density and technicality may make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the connections between information theory and information geometry – specifically, the fundamental relationship where the KL divergence for an exponential family maps to a Bregman divergence, and concepts from Fenchel duality. Many deep learning engineers, who represent a core audience for GAN papers, might fall into this category. To enhance clarity, I suggest the following:

1. Consider moving Figure 1 from the appendix to the main text. It effectively illustrates the connections between information theory and information geometry within the f-GAN context.
2.  It would be helpful to explicitly state the benefits of considering an information geometric view early in the paper. As I understand it, this perspective allows us to leverage the geometry of the parameter space to analyze optimization behavior. This point is currently made briefly on page 7, which feels somewhat late in the presentation.
3. The connection between Section 5 and the core f-GAN optimization results isn't entirely clear to me. It seems to demonstrate the representational power of deep neural networks using deformed exponential families, but doesn’t directly relate back to how this informs the f-GAN game itself. While the one-to-one correspondence between *v*, *f*, and *χ* is interesting, it doesn't immediately translate into practical guidance for designing f-GANs – for instance, which f-divergence to choose, or how to best leverage a given activation function within an f-GAN objective.
4.  I’m unclear why φ<sub>l</sub> can be viewed as "deep sufficient statistics" in Theorem 6. Equation (13) doesn’t appear to conform to the standard form of a deformed exponential family.
5.  Consider relocating lines 257-269. The discussion of utility theory seems somewhat tangential to the central information geometric view, and removing this paragraph (perhaps retaining it in the appendix) could free up space to more clearly explain the core results.
6.  I find the experiments somewhat disconnected from the main claims of the paper. For example, the results in section 5 alone could support the discussion of (A) – a reader doesn’t necessarily need to understand the IG view of f-GAN to apply the suggested activation functions. Similarly, section 4 is not strongly tied to the IG view. The WGAN results, in particular, could be distracting, as the paper focuses primarily on f-GANs. I spent some time locating the clarifying statement on line 227-228 regarding WGAN.

In summary, while this paper presents numerous valuable results and dense derivations, the material feels less than optimally organized. It resembles a consolidation of results from multiple investigations into a single 8-page NIPS submission. While I support acceptance, I believe the paper would benefit significantly from editing to enhance clarity and coherence.
