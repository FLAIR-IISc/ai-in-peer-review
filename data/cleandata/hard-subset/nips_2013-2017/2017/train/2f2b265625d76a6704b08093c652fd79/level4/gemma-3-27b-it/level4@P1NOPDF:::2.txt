Thank you for an interesting read.

This paper presents an information-geometric (IG) perspective on the f-GAN algorithm. It first demonstrates that f-GAN converges in parameter space by leveraging the one-to-one mapping between f-divergences and chi-divergences, along with a result concerning Bregman divergences. The paper then discusses a principled implementation of f-GAN and, crucially, provides a factorization result for deep neural network representations, linking this to the choice of activation functions via their one-to-one mapping to the chi (or f) function. While I didn’t verify every detail in the appendix, the proofs appear correct, with the exception of Theorem 8, which I didn’t have time to examine fully.

I find this paper to be highly insightful and densely packed with new results potentially valuable to both machine learning and information theory. I am particularly impressed by the exposition of Theorem 4, which clearly articulates the IG foundation of f-GAN. Therefore, I recommend acceptance. 

However, I believe the paper’s complexity could pose a challenge for readers less familiar with the connections between information theory and information geometry – specifically, the mapping of Kullback-Leibler divergence for an exponential family to Bregman divergence and the concepts of Fenchel duality. Many deep learning practitioners, who form the primary audience for GAN papers, may fall into this category. To improve clarity, I suggest the following:

1. Consider moving Figure 1 from the appendix to the main text. It effectively illustrates the relationships between information theory and information geometry within the f-GAN context.
2. It would be helpful to explicitly state the benefits of adopting an IG view at the beginning of the paper. My understanding is that this perspective allows the use of parameter space geometry to analyze optimization behavior. While this is briefly mentioned on page 7, it feels somewhat late in the presentation.
3. The connection between Section 5 and f-GAN isn’t immediately clear. It appears to utilize the deformed exponential family solely to characterize the distributions representable by a deep neural network, rather than as a direct consequence of the f-GAN optimization process. While I agree that v, f, and chi have a one-to-one correspondence, the paper doesn't explain how this result can inform the design of the f-GAN game – for instance, which f-divergence to select, or, given an activation function, which f-GAN objective will perform best in terms of convergence.
4. I’m unsure why the phi_l in Theorem 6 are considered “deep sufficient statistics.” Equation (13) doesn’t seem to conform to the structure of a deformed exponential family.
5. Consider relocating lines 257-269 to an earlier, more relevant section. The discussion of utility theory feels tangential to the core IG view and could be moved to the appendix to conserve space for explaining the central results.
6. The experiments feel somewhat disconnected from the main claims. For example, the results described in point (A) appear to rely only on Section 5, implying that the IG view of f-GAN isn't essential for applying these new activation functions. Similarly, point (B) only requires understanding Section 4, which is not tightly linked to the IG analysis. The WGAN results, in particular, could be distracting and potentially confusing, as the paper primarily focuses on f-GANs. I had to actively search for the mention of WGAN on lines 227-228.

In summary, while this paper offers valuable results and rigorous derivations, the presentation feels somewhat disjointed. It reads as a consolidation of results from multiple investigations into an 8-page NIPS submission. Despite this, I support acceptance, but believe the paper would benefit significantly from editing to enhance clarity and coherence.
