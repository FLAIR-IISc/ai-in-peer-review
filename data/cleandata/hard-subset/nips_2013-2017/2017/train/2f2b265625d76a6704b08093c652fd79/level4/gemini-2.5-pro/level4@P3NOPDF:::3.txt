This paper investigates several fundamental questions regarding Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), such as the relationship between convergence in the GAN objective and convergence in parameter space, the nature of the distribution the generator fits, and how to select the discriminator's output activation function to ensure desirable properties like proper compositeness of the loss. The authors address these questions using the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions.

The primary theoretical contributions are as follows:
1.  A variational generalization of a theorem that connects an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between their parameters. This result forges a link between the information-theoretic view of distributional dissimilarity and the information-geometric perspective of parameter-space dissimilarity.
2.  A demonstration that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as "escorts" of deformed exponential distributions.
3.  Theoretical guidance suggesting that a principled choice of the generator's hidden activation functions and the discriminator's output activation function could improve GAN performance.
4.  A brief alternative interpretation of the GAN framework within the context of expected utility theory.

Overall, the paper presents several interesting theoretical results derived from an information-geometric perspective. However, I have significant concerns regarding the practical relevance, the justification of key assumptions, and the empirical validation of these findings in their current form.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Justification of the Deformed Exponential Family Assumption:** A central concern is the reliance on the assumption that the data and model distributions, P and Q, are members of the deformed exponential family. This assumption underpins many of the key theorems and insights. The authors should justify this assumption by discussing its validity for real-world data distributions and clarifying whether key results, such as Theorems 4 & 6, could be derived under more general conditions. Without this justification, the applicability of the entire framework to practical GAN scenarios remains in question.

2.  **Practical Utility of the Information-Geometric Identity:** One of the paper's main contributions is the information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7), which relates the standard variational f-divergence optimization over distributions to an optimization problem over parameters. The practical utility of this parameter-space perspective is unclear. Could this formulation lead to a new, perhaps more stable, optimization algorithm for GANs? If so, what would be the benefits? A direct experimental comparison between the standard distributional optimization and this proposed parameter-based optimization is notably absent.

    Furthermore, the paper uses this identity to argue that convergence in the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space (if the residual J(Q) is small). However, the significance of this implication is not well-motivated. The authors should provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence objective *does not* lead to parameter convergence, thereby highlighting the importance of their result.

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation for Discriminator Activations:** A compelling practical implication of the theory is the guidance on choosing the discriminator's output activation function (`gf`) to ensure the loss is properly composite. This is a potentially valuable contribution. However, the paper misses a crucial opportunity to validate this finding experimentally by comparing the theoretically derived `gf` against the heuristic choice proposed in the original f-GAN paper [34]. Such an experiment would have provided direct evidence for the practical utility of the theoretical analysis.

4.  **Weak and Contradictory Experimental Results:** The experimental results provided do not offer compelling support for the paper's theoretical claims.
    *   The theory suggests that a reversible generator architecture is desirable. Yet, in Table 2, the proposed invertible μ-ReLU activation (designed to satisfy the reversibility assumption) performs worse than the standard non-invertible ReLU (its limiting case). This result appears to contradict the theoretical motivation. The authors should provide an explanation for this discrepancy.
    *   It is unclear whether the DCGAN architecture used in the experiments satisfies the reversibility assumption, which is a prerequisite for much of the analysis. A comparison with non-f-divergence-based methods like Wasserstein GANs would also be valuable to contextualize the scope of the proposed theory.
    *   Finally, the performance differences reported for various activation and link functions in Table 2 appear to be within the error bars, making it difficult to conclude that the theoretically-motivated choices offer any significant practical advantage.

**Minor Concerns**

*   **Clarity and Intuition:** The paper relies heavily on concepts from deformed exponential densities (e.g., "deformation," "escort"), which may be unfamiliar to the broader machine learning community. Including a more intuitive explanation of these concepts would significantly improve the paper's accessibility.
*   **Interpretation of Bounds in Theorem 8:** In Theorem 8, several upper bounds for J(Q) are derived. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on how these bounds compare, their practical implications for selecting activation functions, and a more detailed explanation of why a bound that decreases with the normalization parameter Z is beneficial and how this can be practically leveraged.

**Conclusion**

In summary, while the theoretical direction is intriguing, the current manuscript does not sufficiently bridge the gap between its information-geometric analysis and practical applications for GAN practitioners. The core assumptions are not fully justified, and the experimental evidence is not strong enough to substantiate the theoretical claims. I believe the paper's insights are not yet substantial enough to be of immediate use to the deep learning community.
