The authors investigate several key questions surrounding Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), including the relationship between GAN convergence and parameter space convergence, the objective function the generator learns to fit, and the optimal choice of discriminator output activation for loss function compositeness. They approach these questions using the framework of deformed exponential distributions, leading to several theoretical results.

Specifically, they present a variational generalization of a theorem linking f-divergences to Bregman divergences, establishing a connection between information-theoretic and information-geometric perspectives on distribution dissimilarity. They demonstrate that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. This investigation suggests that careful selection of generator hidden activations and discriminator output activations could enhance GAN performance, and they briefly explore an interpretation of the GAN game through the lens of expected utility theory.

Overall, the paper presents interesting theoretical contributions. However, I have several concerns regarding the practical applicability of these results in their current form.

My primary question is regarding the assumption that data and model distributions P and Q belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors should justify this assumption, detailing its validity in practice and discussing whether similar results – such as Theorems 4 & 6 – could be obtained without it.

Furthermore, the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq.(7) relates variational f-divergence to a parameter-based optimization problem. The authors should clarify the advantages of this parameter-based perspective. Is it possible to implement GANs using this approach, and what benefits would it offer? I would have greatly appreciated experimental validation comparing these two optimization approaches. Surprisingly, the authors primarily use the right-hand side of this identity to assert a link between the GAN game and parameter space convergence when the residual J(Q) is small, an implication that isn’t immediately obvious.  Could they provide a scenario where convergence in f-divergence does *not* imply parameter space convergence?

The authors highlight the importance of the discriminator output activation function, *gf*, for achieving proper loss function compositeness. I found this result compelling and would have liked to see a comparative experimental analysis of their theoretically derived *gf* against the heuristic choice in Ref. [34].

Regarding the reversibility assumption, the authors show that a generator network can be factored with a specific activation function as escorts for deformed exponential distributions.  However, Table 2 demonstrates that the theoretically superior μ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU. Given the reversibility assumption, I expected the invertible μ-ReLU to outperform the non-invertible baseline.  An explanation for this discrepancy is needed.  Furthermore, can the authors comment on the reversibility of the DCGAN architecture and how their results compare to those from Wasserstein GANs, which are not based on f-divergences? Finally, the differences in performance observed in Tables 2 (center and right) largely fall within the margin of error, providing insufficient evidence to support the practical relevance of their theoretical findings.

Minor points: Given that the broader machine learning community may be unfamiliar with deformed exponential densities, providing more intuitive explanations of concepts like deformation (or signature) and escorts would be beneficial.  Additionally, in Theorem 8, the authors derive upper bounds for J(Q). A comparison of these bounds and a discussion of their implications for activation function selection would be helpful.  Elaboration on why the upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort normalization parameter Z, and how this can be leveraged, is also warranted.

In conclusion, while the theoretical analysis offers some insights, I believe they are currently not substantial enough to significantly impact the practice of GAN development.
