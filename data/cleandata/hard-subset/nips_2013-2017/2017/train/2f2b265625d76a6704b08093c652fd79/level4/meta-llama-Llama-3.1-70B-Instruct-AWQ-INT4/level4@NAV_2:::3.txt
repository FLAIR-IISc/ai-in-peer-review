The authors investigate several interesting GAN-related questions, such as the extent to which solving the GAN problem implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator is actually fitting when convergence occurs, and how to choose the output activation function of the discriminator to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function. They address these questions within the framework of deformed exponential distributions, deriving several theoretical results, including a variational generalization of a known theorem that relates an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between the parameters of such distributions.

This theorem provides an interesting connection between the information-theoretic viewpoint of measuring dissimilarity between probability distributions and the information-geometric perspective of measuring dissimilarity between the corresponding parameters. They also show that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their theoretical investigation suggests that a careful choice of the hidden activation functions of the generator and a proper selection of the output activation function of the discriminator could potentially help to further improve GANs.

However, I have several concerns regarding the practical relevance and usefulness of their results in the present form. Firstly, many of their theorems and resulting insights hold for the specific case that the data and model distributions P and Q are members of the deformed exponential family. The authors should justify this assumption, elaborate on whether it is met in practice, and explain whether similar results could be derived without this assumption.

The information-geometric f-GAN identity relates the well-known variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. However, it is unclear what we gain from this parameter-based point of view, whether it is possible to implement GANs in terms of this parameter-based optimization, and what the benefits would be. Experimental results comparing the two approaches to optimization of GANs would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the authors do not make use of the right-hand side of this identity, other than to assert that solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space (provided the residual J(Q) is small). However, it is not obvious why this implication is not straightforward, and a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence formulation over distributions does not imply convergence in parameter space would be helpful.

The authors show that the choice of the output activation function gf matters for the GAN loss to have the desirable proper compositeness property. However, experimental results comparing their theoretically derived gf against the heuristic choice provided in Ref. [34] would be valuable.

Under the assumption that the generator network is reversible, the authors show that there exists an activation function v such that the generator's hidden layers factor exactly as escorts for the deformed exponential family. However, the experimental results in Table 2 (left) indicate that the "theoretically superior" μ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU (limiting case μ → 1). The authors should explain why the μ-ReLU performs worse and comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption.

Additionally, the results on whether one activation (Table 2 center) or link function (Table 2 right) performs better than the others are all within error bars, which does not provide sufficient support for their theoretical results to be relevant in practice.

Finally, the authors should provide more intuition as to what a deformation (or signature) and an escort is, as the general ML community might not yet be familiar with these concepts. They should also elaborate on the implications of the upper bounds for J(Q) derived in Theorem 8 and how these bounds compare against each other.

In light of these comments, I believe the insights gained from the theoretical analysis are not substantial enough from the point of view of a GAN practitioner. The authors should address these concerns and provide more practical relevance and usefulness to their results.

Strengths:
- The authors investigate several interesting GAN-related questions and provide a theoretical background to understand the unsupervised part of the game.
- They derive several theoretical results, including a variational generalization of a known theorem that relates an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between the parameters of such distributions.
- They show that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions.

Weaknesses:
- Many of their theorems and resulting insights hold for the specific case that the data and model distributions P and Q are members of the deformed exponential family, which may not be met in practice.
- The authors do not provide sufficient experimental results to support their theoretical claims, and the results they do provide are often within error bars.
- The authors do not provide enough intuition as to what a deformation (or signature) and an escort is, which may make their results less accessible to the general ML community.

Acceptance:
I would not accept this paper in its current form. The authors need to address the concerns mentioned above and provide more practical relevance and usefulness to their results.
