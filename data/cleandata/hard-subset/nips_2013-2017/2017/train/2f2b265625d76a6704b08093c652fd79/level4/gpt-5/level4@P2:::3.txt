Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies GANs through the lens of information geometry and deformed exponential families. Building on f-GAN (Nowozin et al.) and the classical identity linking KL divergence between exponential-family distributions to a Bregman divergence between their natural parameters, the authors:
- Provide a variational generalization that connects an f-divergence between distributions to a Bregman divergence between their parameters within deformed exponential families, framed to be amenable to the f-GAN setup.
- Show that, under a reversibility assumption on the generator, deep networks factorize as escorts of deformed exponential distributions, which links standard deep architectures to this broader statistical family.
- Clarify the role of the discriminator’s output activation via proper composite losses, arguing that the output link should be the composition of f′ with a proper link, and suggesting practical guidance for choosing activation functions in both generator and discriminator.

These results extend the f-GAN variational formulation to an information-geometric identity and propose architectural implications. They also briefly note an interpretation via expected utility.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge: The main identity relating variational f-divergence objectives to parameter-space Bregman divergences is elegant and strengthens ties between information theory (distribution-level divergences) and information geometry (parameter-level divergences).
- Architectural insights: The escort factorization result and sufficient conditions on activations offer principled guidance for designing generator activations and the discriminator’s link function.
- Clarity on proper composite losses: The analysis of the discriminator’s output activation gf, with a principled derivation from proper composite losses, is practically relevant and improves on the somewhat ad hoc choices in prior implementations.

Questions and concerns
- Assumption of deformed exponential families: Many key results (e.g., Theorems 4 and 6 in the paper’s numbering) require P and Q to lie in a deformed exponential family. Please justify when this assumption holds in practice, and whether analogous results can be derived without it. How restrictive is this modeling class for real data and typical generators?
- Value of the parameter-space view: The information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7 in the paper) maps distribution-level optimization to an optimization over parameters. What concrete advantages does this confer? Can one implement GAN training directly via the parameter-side objective, and would it improve optimization stability, sample quality, or convergence speed? An empirical comparison of distribution-level versus parameter-level optimization would be very informative.
- Convergence in parameters vs distributions: The paper leverages the right-hand side of the identity mostly to argue that solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space (modulo the residual J(Q)). Why is this nontrivial? Can you provide a realistic example where convergence in the f-divergence between distributions does not imply convergence of parameters, and explain how your identity resolves this?
- Proper composite losses and gf: The derivation that gf should be the composition of f′ with the link is compelling. It would strengthen the paper to compare this theoretically derived gf against the heuristic choice in f-GAN empirically, across identical architectures and datasets.
- Reversibility and activations: The escort factorization requires a reversible generator. Does the DCGAN variant used meet reversibility in practice (e.g., invertible convolutions and activations)? In Table 2 (left), μ-ReLU—motivated by the theory—sometimes underperforms standard ReLU (e.g., GAN MLP). Why might the theoretically favorable (invertible) μ-ReLU not outperform the non-invertible baseline? Please discuss confounders (e.g., optimization dynamics, regularization, initialization).
- Scope with respect to WGAN: Since WGAN is not based on f-divergences, how should we interpret your experimental comparisons under WGAN training? Are any of the theoretical guarantees expected to carry over, or are these purely empirical tests of activation/link choices?
- Strength of empirical evidence: Several plots suggest differences within error bars, making it hard to conclude that the theoretically motivated activations or links yield consistent gains. Additional runs, stronger metrics (e.g., FID, IS), and ablations isolating reversibility and escort-factorization compliance would make the empirical case more convincing.

Minor comments
- Intuition for deformed families and escorts: Given that the broader ML audience may be unfamiliar with deformed exponential families, more intuition would help. Briefly explain what a “signature” (deformation) is, how escorts reweight densities, and why these constructs arise naturally in your variational identities.
- Bounds on J(Q) (Theorem 8 in the paper): Please compare the proposed upper bounds empirically or at least qualitatively. How do these bounds guide the choice of activations? You state that the bound decreases with the escort normalization Z—why is this desirable, and how can one exploit this in architecture or training (e.g., via activation/link choices that influence Z)?

Evaluation by NIPS criteria
- Quality: The theoretical development is interesting and appears sound, but several practical assumptions (deformed families, reversibility) limit immediate applicability. The empirical validation is suggestive but not decisive.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written, yet additional intuition for deformed families/escorts and a more explicit discussion of how the identity informs practice would improve accessibility.
- Originality: The variational information-geometric identity and the escort factorization for deep reversible nets are novel and nontrivial extensions of prior results.
- Significance: Potentially significant if the assumptions can be relaxed or shown to hold in practice, and if the parameter-space perspective leads to improved training. The current experiments do not yet demonstrate strong practical gains.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually unifies f-GANs with information geometry via a clean variational identity.
- Offers principled guidance on discriminator links and generator activations.
- Opens avenues for parameter-space analyses of GAN convergence.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on deformed exponential families and reversibility; unclear practical prevalence.
- Limited use of the parameter-space side of the identity in experiments; lack of direct empirical comparison.
- Empirical improvements are modest and often within error bars.

Overall, the theoretical contributions are appealing, but stronger empirical validation and a clearer demonstration of practical benefits would substantially improve the paper’s impact for GAN practitioners.
