Thank you for an interesting read.

This paper proposes an information-geometric (IG) perspective on the f-GAN algorithm. It first shows that f-GAN converges in parameter space by leveraging a one-to-one correspondence between f-divergences and chi-divergences together with a Bregman divergence result. It then discusses an appropriate way to implement f-GAN. Finally, the main text presents a factorization result for deep neural network representations and examines the choice of activation functions, highlighting a one-to-one mapping to the chi (or f) function. I did not check every detail in the appendix, but the proofs appear correct (with the exception of Thm. 8, which I did not have time to read before the deadline).

The paper is dense and contains many new results of potential interest to both machine learning and information theory. I am particularly impressed by the exposition of Thm. 4, which clearly explains the IG aspects of f-GAN. Overall, I view this as a clear accept.

That said, the paper will be challenging for readers who lack background in the connections between information theory (IT) and information geometry—at least the basic facts that KL divergence for exponential families can be mapped to a Bregman divergence, and the role of Fenchel duality. Many deep learning practitioners—the primary audience for a GAN paper—may fall into this category. To improve clarity and accessibility, I suggest:

1. I like Fig. 1 in the appendix, which succinctly illustrates the connections between IT and IG in the f-GAN context. Consider moving it to the main text.

2. It would help to state early on why the IG view is useful. My understanding is that geometry in parameter space provides tools to analyze optimization behavior. You mention this briefly on page 7, but that feels too late.

3. I am not entirely clear on how Section 5 connects to f-GAN. It seems to use the deformed exponential family to describe distributions representable by deep neural networks, rather than to derive consequences of f-GAN optimization. While I agree that v, f, and chi correspond one-to-one, the paper does not explain how this insight informs the design of the f-GAN game—for example, which f-divergence to select, or, given a specific activation function, which f-GAN objective should be preferred (e.g., for convergence properties).

4. For Thm. 6, why can the φ_l be viewed as “deep sufficient statistics”? Equation (13) does not appear to be in the form of a deformed exponential family.

5. As noted, it may help to relocate lines 257–269 to a different part of the paper. The utility theory discussion also seems tangential to the IG perspective; consider moving that paragraph to the appendix to free space for explaining the main results.

6. The experiments feel only loosely tied to the paper’s main claims. For example, point (A) can be discussed solely from Section 5; the IG perspective is not required to adopt the proposed activation functions. Similarly, for (B) one mainly needs Section 4, which is not closely tied to the IG view of f-GAN. The WGAN results, in particular, are potentially distracting and confusing in a paper primarily about f-GANs; I had to search for the brief mention of WGAN (lines 227–228).

In summary, the paper offers many useful results and dense derivations, but the presentation is not as clear and coherent as it could be. It currently reads like a compressed collection of contributions spanning multiple papers fit into an 8-page NIPS submission. While I am supportive of acceptance, I strongly recommend editing for organization, clearer signposting of the main claims, and tighter integration between the IG perspective and the practical guidance on f-GAN design and implementation.
