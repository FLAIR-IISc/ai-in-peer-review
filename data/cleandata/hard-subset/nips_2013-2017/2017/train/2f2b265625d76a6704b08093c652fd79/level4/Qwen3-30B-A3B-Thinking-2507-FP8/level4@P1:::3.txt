

The authors address key theoretical questions in GANs, specifically regarding what convergence in parameter space means when solving the GAN problem, what the generator actually models upon convergence, and how to choose the discriminator's output activation function to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function.

The paper makes several theoretical contributions within the framework of deformed exponential distributions. The authors present a variational generalization (amenable to f-GAN formulation) of a known theorem that relates an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between their parameters. This establishes an important connection between information-theoretic and information-geometric perspectives. They also show that under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be represented as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their theoretical investigation suggests that careful selection of generator activation functions and discriminator output activation functions could potentially improve GAN performance.

Overall, the authors present interesting theoretical results, but I have concerns about their practical relevance in the current form.

First, many theorems and insights rely on the assumption that data and model distributions P and Q belong to the deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption? Do they believe it's generally met in practice, and could similar results be derived without it?

The authors' main contribution, the information-geometric f-GAN identity (Equation 7), relates variational f-divergence to information-geometric optimization over parameters. Can the authors explain what practical benefits this parameter-based perspective offers? Is it possible to implement GANs using this parameter-based optimization, and if so, what are the benefits? I would have appreciated experimental comparisons between the two optimization approaches. It's surprising that the authors don't make significant use of the right-hand side of this identity beyond stating that solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space (when J(Q) is small). Why isn't this convergence implication obvious? Could the authors provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence formulation does not imply convergence in parameter space?

The authors' finding about the importance of the discriminator's output activation function gf for proper compositeness is particularly compelling. I would have appreciated an experimental comparison between the theoretically derived gf (as the composition of f' and the link function) and the heuristic choice used in [30].

Regarding the experimental results: Under the reversibility assumption, the authors claim that μ-ReLU (which is invertible) should perform better than ReLU (which is not). However, in Table 2 (left), μ-ReLU actually performs worse than the ReLU baseline for the GAN MLP architecture. Given that μ-ReLU satisfies the reversibility assumption while ReLU does not, this result contradicts their theoretical expectations. Could the authors explain this discrepancy? Additionally, could they comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption, and how their results compare against Wasserstein GAN, which is not based on f-divergences?

Finally, the experimental results in Tables 2 (center and right) show that performance differences between activation functions and link functions are largely within error bars. These results do not provide strong empirical support for the practical relevance of the authors' theoretical findings.

Many of the authors' results rely on the framework of deformed exponential distributions, which may be unfamiliar to the broader machine learning community. I would appreciate more intuitive explanations of key concepts like deformation (or signature) and escort.

In Theorem 8, the authors derive several upper bounds for J(Q). How do these bounds compare to each other, and what are their implications for choosing activation functions? The authors note that the upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort normalization parameter Z. Could they elaborate on why this is beneficial and how this can be leveraged in practice?

In light of these concerns, I believe the practical insights gained from this theoretical analysis are not sufficiently substantial for GAN practitioners. While the theoretical contributions are interesting, the paper would benefit from stronger empirical validation, clearer justification of assumptions, and more concrete guidance for practitioners.
