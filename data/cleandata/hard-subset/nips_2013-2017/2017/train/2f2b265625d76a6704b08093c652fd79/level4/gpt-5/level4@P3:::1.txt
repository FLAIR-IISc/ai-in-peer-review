Summary and relation to prior work
This paper revisits f-GANs through an information-geometric lens. Building on (i) the variational characterization of f-divergences (Nowozin et al., 2016), (ii) the identity relating KL divergence between members of the same exponential family to a Bregman divergence between their natural parameters (Amari; Nielsen), and (iii) deformed (χ-)exponential families (Naudts; Amari et al.), the authors derive a variational information-geometric identity (vig-f-GAN). In brief: under mild conditions, an f-divergence If(P‖Q) can be represented as a KLχ divergence; for χ-exponential families, a KLχ term equals a Bregman divergence DC(θ‖ϑ) between natural parameters plus a penalty depending only on Q; and this KLχ admits a variational form analogous to the f-GAN objective. Consequently, solving the variational game amounts (up to a penalty) to minimizing a Bregman divergence between generator and target parameters, providing a geometric interpretation of f-GAN training.

The paper also (a) clarifies the discriminator’s “output activation” in f-GAN as a proper composite loss link function, yielding a principled choice for the mapping gf rather than an arbitrary constraint; and (b) shows that standard deep generators can factorize escorts of deformed exponential families under a sufficient condition on activation functions, which is satisfied (exactly or in the limit) by popular activations such as ELU, Softplus, and ReLU.

Experiments on MNIST and LSUN investigate two design levers suggested by the theory: generator activations and discriminator link functions.

Quality
- The theoretical development is technically interesting and, if correct, provides a unifying view that connects variational f-divergences to Bregman geometry for deformed exponential families. The proofs are deferred to the supplement; key assumptions include bounded subgradients on the density ratio range (Theorem 5), existence/normalization of escorts, and especially invertibility of all generator layers (Theorem 9).
- The invertibility requirement merits deeper discussion. While the paper suggests this can hold for DCGAN-like architectures, typical strided convolutions and ReLUs are not bijections on their full domains. Please clarify the precise architectural constraints (e.g., dimension matching, strictly monotone activations, invertible convolutions) and how they were satisfied in the experiments. If experiments use non-invertible components, discuss how the conclusions of Theorem 9 are expected to degrade.
- The link between theory and experiments is currently weak. The empirical studies vary activations and links, but they do not test the central geometric predictions (e.g., convergence in parameter space via DC(θ‖ϑ), or the factorization/escort structure). Consider adding synthetic experiments with known χ-exponential families where ground-truth parameters are available, reporting both the variational objective and the corresponding Bregman distance and penalty J(Q). This would directly validate Corollary 8.
- The boundedness condition in Theorem 5 and the x-dependent χ in Theorem 7 are powerful but abstract. Including concrete special cases (e.g., f = KL, reverse-KL, JS; χ(z) = z) with explicit T* and χ constructions would greatly aid understanding and verify that the variational form reduces to known results.

Clarity
- The paper is densely written with many layers of generality. Readers would benefit from:
  - A running simple example (e.g., 1D Gaussian exponential family; then a Tsallis/χ example) illustrating Theorems 5–7 and Corollary 8.
  - An “intuition box” for the penalty J(Q): what does it measure, when is it small, how does it behave outside exponential families?
  - A figure summarizing the mapping between f, χ, the escort Q̃, the discriminator link Ψ, and the generator parameters ϑ.
  - Clearer signposting of when χ depends on x and how that impacts the discriminator parameterization.
- Minor wording/grammar:
  - p.7, l.258: “For eq. (7), is means when C …” → “For eq. (7), this means that when C …”
  - Ensure consistent notation for escorts (Q̃), domains (R++), and dual χ-logarithms (χ•).
  - Define all symbols at first use (e.g., Z for escort normalization, Hout, χ•).

Originality
- The synthesis is novel: a constructive bridge from general f-divergences to KLχ, then to Bregman geometry for deformed exponential families, with a variational characterization aligned with the GAN game. The proper composite loss perspective for the discriminator link is a useful clarification in the f-GAN framework. The activation–χ correspondence and escort factorization for deep nets are also original and potentially impactful.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: offers a principled lens for generator/discriminator design and a geometric notion of convergence. Practical significance hinges on how restrictive the assumptions are and whether the geometric insights translate to measurable gains. Current experiments provide preliminary evidence but are not yet conclusive. Strengthening the empirical link (as suggested above) would materially increase impact.

Experiments
- Report quantitative metrics (e.g., FID/IS where appropriate for 2017 standards) alongside visuals. Indicate variance over multiple runs and training stability.
- Explicitly state whether the experimental architectures satisfy the invertibility conditions; if not, add ablations with invertible flows (e.g., NICE/RealNVP-style components) to test Theorem 9 more faithfully.
- For the discriminator link, compare the theoretically prescribed Ψ (from Theorem 12) against sigmoid and Matsushita on the same f; clarify whether you also adjusted the loss accordingly.

Pros
- Unifies f-GANs with information geometry through a clear variational identity.
- Clarifies the role of proper composite losses and link functions in the discriminator.
- Connects generator activations to χ-exponential families, suggesting principled architecture choices.
- Constructive proofs (mapping between v and χ).

Cons
- Heavy generality obscures intuition; key assumptions (especially invertibility) need practical justification.
- Experiments do not directly validate the geometric claims; limited datasets/metrics.
- Some notation and exposition issues impede readability.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The theoretical contribution is elegant and potentially influential, but the paper would benefit from clearer exposition and stronger empirical validation aligned with the theory. Addressing the clarity points, adding concrete special cases, and including experiments that test the geometric predictions would significantly strengthen the submission.
