This paper poses several compelling questions regarding Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). These include the extent to which solving the GAN optimization problem implies convergence in parameter space, the nature of the distribution being fitted by the generator at convergence, and the optimal choice of the discriminator's output activation function to ensure a properly composite loss.

The authors address these questions using the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions, from which they derive several theoretical results. First, they present a variational generalization of a known theorem, compatible with the f-GAN framework, which connects the f-divergence between distributions to the Bregman divergence between their corresponding parameters. This result establishes a valuable link between the information-theoretic view of distributional dissimilarity and the information-geometric perspective of parametric dissimilarity. Second, they demonstrate that under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as "escorts" of deformed exponential distributions. Furthermore, their theoretical investigation suggests that GAN performance can be improved by a careful selection of the generator's hidden activations and the discriminator's output activation. The paper also briefly mentions an alternative interpretation of the GAN game in the context of expected utility theory.

Overall, while the paper presents several interesting theoretical contributions, I have significant concerns about the practical relevance and immediate utility of these results.

**Questions & Concerns:**

A central concern is that many of the theoretical results, including Theorems 4 and 6, hinge on the assumption that the data and model distributions (P and Q) belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors should justify this assumption by discussing its validity in practical scenarios. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to know if these key results can be derived without this restrictive assumption.

The paper's information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7), a main contribution, connects the standard variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. However, the practical benefit of this parameter-based perspective is unclear. Could GANs be implemented using this parametric optimization, and if so, what would be the advantages? An experimental comparison between the standard distributional optimization and this proposed parametric optimization is notably absent. The authors appear to use this identity primarily to assert that convergence in distribution space implies convergence in parameter space (assuming a small residual J(Q)), but do not otherwise leverage the parametric side of the equation. This implication seems intuitive; could the authors provide a realistic scenario where convergence in distribution space *does not* imply convergence in parameter space to highlight the significance of their result?

The paper derives an interesting practical result regarding the discriminator's output activation function, `gf`, showing its importance for achieving a properly composite loss. This finding is particularly compelling. However, the paper misses a crucial opportunity to experimentally validate it by comparing the theoretically-derived `gf` (a composition of f' and the link function) against the heuristic choice proposed in Ref. [34].

Regarding the experimental results, the theory suggests that for a reversible generator, there exists an activation function `v` that allows its hidden layers to factor as escorts for the deformed exponential family. However, the experimental results in Table 2 (left) appear to contradict this. For the GAN MLP, the "theoretically superior" μ-ReLU activation performs worse than the baseline ReLU (μ → 1). This is counter-intuitive, as one would expect the invertible μ-ReLU, which satisfies the reversibility assumption, to outperform the non-invertible baseline. An explanation for this discrepancy is needed. Additionally, do the DCGAN architectures tested satisfy the reversibility assumption? A comment on how these results relate to the Wasserstein GAN (which is not based on f-divergences and thus may fall outside the scope of this theory) would also be welcome. Finally, the results comparing different activation and link functions (Table 2, center and right) appear to be within error bars, making them statistically inconclusive. This lack of significant empirical evidence weakens the claim that the theoretical findings are practically relevant.

**Minor Concerns:**

The paper relies heavily on the framework of deformed exponential densities. Since this may be unfamiliar to the broader machine learning community, the manuscript would be improved by providing more intuition for key concepts such as "deformation," "signature," and "escort."

Regarding Theorem 8, which provides upper bounds on J(Q), it is unclear how these bounds compare to one another. What are the practical implications of these different bounds for selecting activation functions? The authors also state that the upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort's normalization parameter Z. Please elaborate on why this is a desirable property and how it can be practically leveraged.

In summary, while the paper's theoretical analysis is interesting, the connection to practice is not well-established. The insights provided, in their current form, may not be substantial enough to guide GAN practitioners.
