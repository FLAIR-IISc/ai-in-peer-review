

The authors address several intriguing questions regarding GANs, including the extent to which solving the GAN optimization implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator actually fits upon convergence, and how to select the discriminator’s output activation function to ensure the loss function’s proper compositeness. They frame their analysis within the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions, deriving several key theoretical results.  

Notably, they present a variational generalization of a known theorem linking an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between parameters. This theorem establishes a meaningful connection between the information-theoretic view of distribution dissimilarity and the information-geometric perspective of parameter dissimilarity. Under a reversibility assumption, they demonstrate that deep generative networks factor into "escorts" of deformed exponential distributions. Their analysis further suggests that careful selection of generator hidden-layer activations and discriminator output activations could enhance GAN performance, and they briefly propose an expected utility interpretation of the GAN game.  

While the theoretical contributions are interesting, I question the practical relevance of these results in their current form.  

**Key concerns:**  
First, many theorems and insights rely on the assumption that data and model distributions \(P\) and \(Q\) belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors must justify this assumption: Is it empirically valid? Could similar results (e.g., Theorems 4 and 6) be derived without it?  

Second, their main contribution—the information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7)—relates the variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. What specific advantages does this parameter-centric perspective offer? Can GANs be implemented using this approach, and what benefits would it yield? Experimental comparisons between standard f-GAN optimization and this parameter-based method would clarify this. Surprisingly, the authors only use the right-hand side of Eq. 7 to assert that GAN convergence implies parameter-space convergence (when residual \(J(Q)\) is small). Why is this implication non-obvious? Please provide a concrete scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence formulation *fails* to imply convergence in parameter space.  

Third, the authors derive a theoretically motivated output activation function \(g_f\) (Section 2.4, Ref. [34]) for proper compositeness. This is a compelling result, but I would have appreciated an empirical comparison between \(g_f\) (as the composition of \(f'\) and the link function) and the heuristic choice in Ref. [34].  

**Practical implications and experimental results:**  
Under the reversibility assumption, the authors prove that a specific activation function \(v\) allows generator hidden layers to factor exactly as escorts for the deformed exponential family. However, Table 2 (left) shows that for the GAN MLP, the "theoretically superior" \(\mu\)-ReLU performs *worse* than the baseline ReLU (limiting case \(\mu \to 1\)). Given their analysis, I expected \(\mu\)-ReLU (satisfying reversibility) to outperform non-invertible ReLU. Why does it underperform? Additionally, does the DCGAN architecture satisfy the reversibility assumption? How do these results compare to Wasserstein GANs (which avoid f-divergences and thus fall outside the authors’ theoretical scope)? Finally, the performance differences in Table 2 (center and right) lie within error margins, offering insufficient evidence for the practical relevance of their theoretical claims.  

**Minor concerns:**  
- The framework relies on deformed exponential densities, which may be unfamiliar to many ML practitioners. Providing intuitive explanations for "deformation" (or "signature") and "escort" would greatly enhance accessibility.  
- Theorem 8 derives multiple upper bounds for \(J(Q)\). How do these bounds compare, and what do they imply for activation function selection? The authors note that the bound decreases with the escort normalization parameter \(Z\); why is this beneficial, and how can it be leveraged in practice?  

In conclusion, while the theoretical analysis is mathematically sound, the insights do not yet provide substantial value for GAN practitioners. The reliance on restrictive assumptions, lack of empirical validation for key claims, and unclear practical benefits weaken the paper’s contribution. To strengthen their case, the authors should address these concerns with rigorous justification, broader empirical validation, and clearer articulation of practical implications.
