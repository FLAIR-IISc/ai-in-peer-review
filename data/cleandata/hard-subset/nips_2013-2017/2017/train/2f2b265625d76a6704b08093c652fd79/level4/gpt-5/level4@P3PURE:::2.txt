Thank you for an interesting read.

Summary and contributions:
- The paper proposes an information-geometric (IG) view of the f-GAN algorithm. It first shows that f-GAN converges in parameter space using the one-to-one mapping between f-divergences and chi-divergences together with a Bregman divergence result. It also discusses proper implementation details for f-GAN. Finally, it provides a factorization result for deep neural network representations and discusses the choice of activation functions, highlighting a one-to-one mapping to the chi (or f) function.
- I did not check every detail in the appendix, but the proofs appear correct, with the exception of Theorem 8, which I did not have time to read before the deadline.

Evaluation:
- The paper is dense and contains many new results that will interest both the machine learning and information theory communities. I am particularly impressed by the exposition of Theorem 4, which clearly articulates the IG perspective for f-GAN. Overall, I consider this a clear accept.

Clarity and readability:
- Despite the strength of the results, the paper may be difficult to follow for readers not already familiar with the connections between information theory (IT) and information geometry (IG)—for example, the mapping of KL divergence for exponential families to Bregman divergence, and the use of Fenchel duality. Many deep learning practitioners—the primary audience of a GAN paper—may fall into this category.

Suggestions for improving clarity and coherence:
1. Consider moving Figure 1 from the appendix to the main text, as it succinctly summarizes the IT–IG connections in the f-GAN context.
2. Provide an upfront statement explaining why the IG view is helpful. My understanding is that it enables analysis of optimization behavior via geometry in parameter space. You briefly mention this on page 7, which feels too late; surfacing it earlier would better frame the contributions.
3. Clarify how Section 5 connects to f-GAN. As written, it appears to use the deformed exponential family to characterize the distributions representable by deep neural networks, rather than presenting a consequence of f-GAN optimization. While I accept the one-to-one correspondence between v, f, and chi, it remains unclear how this informs the design of the f-GAN game—for instance, which f-divergence to choose, or, given an activation function, which f-GAN objective is preferable for convergence.
4. Explain why, in Theorem 6, the functions φ_l can be viewed as “deep sufficient statistics.” Equation (13) does not seem to be in the form of a deformed exponential family, so the justification for that terminology is not obvious.
5. It may help to move lines 257–269 elsewhere. Additionally, the paragraph on utility theory does not seem directly related to the IG view; consider removing it from the main text (keeping it in the appendix) to free space for clearer exposition of the core results.
6. The experiments do not feel tightly aligned with the main claims. For (A), the activation-function results can be motivated solely by Section 5, without invoking the IG perspective. For (B), one mainly needs Section 4, which again is not closely tied to the IG view of f-GAN. The WGAN results, in particular, are distracting and somewhat confusing for a paper centered on f-GANs; I even had to search for the brief mention on lines 227–228 to understand the relevance.

In summary, this paper offers valuable results and dense, well-developed derivations, but the presentation could be substantially clearer. At times it reads like multiple papers compressed into an 8-page NIPS submission. While I support acceptance, I recommend revising for focus and coherence to make the claims and their connections more explicit.
