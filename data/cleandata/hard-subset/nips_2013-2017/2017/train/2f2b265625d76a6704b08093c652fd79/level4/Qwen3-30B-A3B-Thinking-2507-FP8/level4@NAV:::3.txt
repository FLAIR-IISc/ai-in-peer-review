

The authors identify several compelling GAN-related questions, including the extent to which solving the GAN problem implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator actually fits upon convergence, and (from a network architectural perspective) how to select the discriminator's output activation function to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function.  

The authors address these questions within the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions, deriving key theoretical results:  
- A variational generalization (amenable to f-GAN formulation) of a theorem linking an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between parameters, establishing a connection between information-theoretic and information-geometric perspectives on dissimilarity.  
- Under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions.  
- Theoretical insights suggest that careful choice of generator hidden activation functions and discriminator output activation functions could improve GAN performance.  
- An alternative interpretation of the GAN game within expected utility theory is briefly mentioned.  

Overall, the authors present interesting theoretical contributions, though I have significant concerns regarding their practical relevance in the current form.  

**Questions & concerns:**  
1. Theorems and insights rely on the assumption that data and model distributions \(P\) and \(Q\) belong to the deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption in practice? Do similar results (e.g., Theorems 4 & 6) hold without it?  
2. The information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7) relates variational f-divergence over distributions to parameter-based optimization. What practical gains does this parameter perspective offer? Can GANs be implemented using this approach, and what benefits would it confer? Experimental comparisons between distribution-based and parameter-based optimization are notably absent. Why is the right-hand side of Eq. 7 unused beyond asserting that small residual \(J(Q)\) implies parameter-space convergence? Why is this implication non-obvious? Provide a realistic scenario where distribution-space convergence does not imply parameter-space convergence.  
3. The authors demonstrate that the discriminator's output activation \(g_f\) (Section 2.4, Ref. [34]) must satisfy a specific form for loss compositeness. This is particularly intriguing. How does their theoretically derived \(g_f\) (as \(f' \circ \text{link function}\)) compare experimentally against the heuristic choice in Ref. [34]?  
4. Under the reversibility assumption, the generator's hidden layers factor as escorts for deformed exponentials. Table 2 (left) shows that the theoretically superior \(\mu\)-ReLU performs worse than baseline ReLU (limit \(\mu \to 1\)) for GAN MLP. Given the reversibility assumption, why does \(\mu\)-ReLU underperform? Does DCGAN satisfy reversibility, and how do these results compare to Wasserstein GAN (which lacks f-divergence foundation)? Additionally, Table 2 (center/right) shows no statistically significant performance differences across activation/link functions—all within error bars—providing insufficient evidence for practical relevance.  

**Minor concerns:**  
- The general ML community may lack familiarity with deformed exponential densities. Provide intuitive explanations for "deformation (signature)" and "escort."  
- Theorem 8 derives upper bounds for \(J(Q)\). How do these bounds compare, and what implications do they have for activation function selection? The bound's decrease with escort normalization parameter \(Z\) is noted—explain why this is beneficial and how to leverage it.  

In light of these concerns, the theoretical insights do not offer substantial practical value for GAN practitioners.
