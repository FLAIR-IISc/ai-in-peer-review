This paper presents an information geometric (IG) perspective on the f-GAN algorithm. It demonstrates f-GAN convergence in parameter space by leveraging the one-to-one mapping between f-divergence and chi-divergence, alongside a result concerning Bregman divergences. The paper further proposes a refined implementation of f-GAN and provides a factorization result for deep neural network representations, connecting activation function choices to the chi (or f) function. While I did not verify every detail in the appendix, the proofs appear sound, with the exception of Theorem 8, which I was unable to review fully.

I found this paper to be highly substantive and containing numerous potentially valuable results for both machine learning and information theory. I was particularly impressed by the presentation of Theorem 4, which elegantly elucidates the IG foundation of f-GAN. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.

However, the paper’s density and technical depth may pose a barrier to readers less familiar with the interplay between information theory (IT) and information geometry (IG) – specifically, the relationship between KL divergence for exponential families and Bregman divergences, as well as concepts from Fenchel duality. Given that a significant portion of the target audience for a GAN paper comprises deep learning engineers, clarity is paramount. To that end, I suggest the following improvements:

1. Consider relocating Figure 1 from the appendix to the main text, as it effectively illustrates the connections between IT and IG within the f-GAN framework.
2. A clearer articulation of the benefits of adopting an IG view would be helpful, ideally near the beginning of the paper. As I understand it, this perspective allows leveraging the geometry of the parameter space to analyze optimization behavior, a point briefly mentioned on page 7, which felt somewhat late in the presentation.
3. The connection between Section 5 and f-GAN is currently unclear. It appears to present a use of deformed exponential families to characterize the distributions representable by deep neural networks, rather than a direct consequence of f-GAN’s optimization process. While the one-to-one correspondence between v, f, and chi is accepted, the paper does not elaborate on how this result informs f-GAN design – for example, which f-divergence to choose, or which f-GAN objective functions best with a given activation function in terms of convergence properties.
4. The rationale for viewing phi_l as “deep sufficient statistics” in Theorem 6 is unclear, and Equation (13) does not appear to conform to the structure of a deformed exponential family.
5. Consider relocating lines 257-269 to a more relevant section. The discussion of utility theory appears tangential to the core IG view and could potentially be moved to the appendix to conserve space for a more thorough explanation of the primary results.
6. The experiments do not strongly support the central claims of the paper. For example, the application of new activation functions (A) relies primarily on the results of Section 5, and does not necessitate an understanding of the IG perspective on f-GAN. Similarly, (B) depends primarily on Section 4, which is also loosely connected to the IG view. The WGAN results are particularly distracting and potentially confusing, given the paper's focus on f-GANs; I spent considerable effort locating the brief mention of WGAN on lines 227-228. 

In summary, while this paper contains many valuable results and intricate derivations, the presentation lacks a certain clarity. It feels as though results from multiple investigations have been compressed into a single NIPS submission. I support acceptance, but believe the paper would benefit significantly from editing to improve the clarity and coherence of its claims.
