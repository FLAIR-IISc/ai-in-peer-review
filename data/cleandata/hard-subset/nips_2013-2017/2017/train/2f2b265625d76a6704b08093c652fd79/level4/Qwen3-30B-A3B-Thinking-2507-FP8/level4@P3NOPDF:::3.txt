

The authors tackle theoretically intriguing questions about GANs within the framework of deformed exponential distributions, addressing convergence in parameter space, the generator’s objective, and discriminator output activation design. Their key contribution is a variational generalization of an f-divergence/Bregman divergence relationship, revealing a connection between information-theoretic and information-geometric perspectives. They further derive conditions under which deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions, suggesting that careful choices of generator hidden activations and discriminator output activations (e.g., \(g_f = f' \circ \text{link}\)) could enhance GAN performance. While the theoretical results are novel, their practical relevance requires significant justification.

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Theoretical Assumption Justification:** The core results (e.g., Theorems 4, 6) assume data/model distributions \(P\) and \(Q\) belong to the deformed exponential family. This is a strong restriction. The authors must clarify whether this assumption holds in practice (e.g., for real-world image datasets) and whether similar results can be derived without it. Without this, the framework’s applicability to standard GANs remains unclear.  

2. **Practical Value of Parameter-Space Optimization:** The information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7) reinterprets the variational f-divergence minimization as an optimization over parameters. The authors fail to articulate *why* this perspective is useful. Specifically:  
   - What concrete benefits does parameter-space optimization offer over standard distribution-space optimization?  
   - Can this be implemented in practice, and if so, how?  
   - Why is the right-hand side of Eq. 7 unused beyond the convergence-in-parameter-space claim? The assertion that convergence in distribution does *not* imply convergence in parameters requires a realistic counterexample, not just a statement.  

3. **Contradictory Empirical Results:** The theoretical claim that reversible generators (e.g., μ-ReLU) should outperform non-reversible ones (e.g., ReLU) is directly contradicted by Table 2 (left). The μ-ReLU architecture performs *worse* than the baseline ReLU (which corresponds to the limit \(\mu \to 1\)). The authors must explain this discrepancy. Additionally:  
   - Does DCGAN satisfy the reversibility assumption? If not, why does the theoretical framework apply to it?  
   - How do these results compare to Wasserstein GANs (which avoid f-divergences)?  
   - The performance differences across activation/link functions in Table 2 (center/right) fall within error bars, providing insufficient evidence to support the theoretical claims.  

**Minor Concerns:**  
- The framework relies on specialized concepts (e.g., "deformation," "escort"). A brief intuitive explanation for a general ML audience would greatly improve accessibility.  
- Theorem 8 provides upper bounds for \(J(Q)\). The authors should clarify:  
  - Which bound is tightest for practical activation functions?  
  - Why a decreasing bound with escort normalization parameter \(Z\) is beneficial and how it guides activation selection.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper presents elegant theoretical insights but falls short in demonstrating practical utility. The core theoretical assumption lacks empirical grounding, the proposed parameter-space optimization offers no clear implementation path or advantage, and the key empirical claim (reversible generators improve performance) is contradicted by the results. The table data, showing no statistically significant gains from theoretically motivated choices, undermines the practical relevance of the framework. Without addressing these gaps—particularly the disconnect between theory and experiment—the contribution remains limited for GAN practitioners.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Reject**. The theoretical results, while mathematically sound, do not provide actionable insights for building better GANs. The paper would require substantial empirical validation under realistic assumptions (e.g., verifying the deformed exponential family assumption for real data) and concrete implementation examples to justify its significance. As written, the work does not advance the state of the art in a way that would benefit the GAN research community.
