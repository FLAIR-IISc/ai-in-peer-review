- Paper addresses fundamental GAN questions using deformed exponential distributions and offers theoretical results (variational generalization linking f-divergences to Bregman divergences, generator as “escort”, activation suggestions).

- Primary concern: practical relevance and demonstrable impact of the theoretical findings are unclear.

- Strong modeling assumption: both data and model distributions lie in the deformed exponential family—authors must justify prevalence, discuss limitations, and whether key theorems hold without it.

- The information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq.7) is central, but the concrete benefits and implementability of the parameter-based optimization perspective are not articulated.

- Missing empirical comparison between distribution-based and parameter-based GAN optimization to validate claimed advantages.

- Need clarification and a concrete example showing when f-divergence convergence does not imply parameter-space convergence.

- Insight on discriminator activation gf is promising, but requires comparative experiments against heuristic choices (e.g., from [34]).

- Reversibility-based prediction that μ-ReLU should outperform ReLU is contradicted by Table 2; authors must explain whether this reflects experimental issues or invalidates the assumption.

- Applicability to non-reversible architectures (e.g., DCGAN) and comparison to frameworks outside f-divergence (e.g., WGAN) are lacking.

- Reported differences in activation/link function performance seem statistically marginal and fall within error bars, limiting practical support for theoretical claims.

- Exposition could improve: provide intuitive explanations for “deformation”/“signature” and “escort” to aid readers unfamiliar with deformed exponential densities.

- Theorem 8: need comparative analysis of upper bounds on J(Q), practical implications for activation selection, and guidance on leveraging the bound’s decrease with normalization parameter Z.

- Overall verdict: intriguing theory but tenuous practical connection; recommend rejection unless stronger experimental validation and clearer practical benefits are provided.
