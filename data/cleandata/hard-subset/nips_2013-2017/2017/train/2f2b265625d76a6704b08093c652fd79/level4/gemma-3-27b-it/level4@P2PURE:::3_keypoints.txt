- Paper studies GAN questions: link between GAN training and parameter-space convergence, generator’s representation during convergence, and discriminator output activation choice for loss compositeness.
- Uses deformed exponential distributions to derive theoretical results within an information-theoretic framework.
- Main theoretical contributions: a variational generalization relating f-divergences to Bregman divergences in parameter space (information-geometric f-GAN identity).
- Shows that under a reversibility assumption, deep generators can be factored as escorts of deformed exponential families.
- Suggests that careful choice of generator hidden activations and discriminator output activations could improve GAN performance; offers an alternative expected-utility interpretation of GANs.
- Major concern: heavy reliance on the assumption that data and model distributions belong to the deformed exponential family—needs justification and discussion of generality.
- Key missing clarification: practical benefits and implementability of the parameter-based optimization view from the f-GAN identity, and lack of experiments comparing it to standard variational optimization.
- Requests demonstration or example where f-divergence convergence does not imply parameter-space convergence to justify the nontriviality of that implication.
- Interesting practical claim about discriminator output activation affecting loss compositeness, but the theoretically derived activation should be experimentally compared to existing heuristics (e.g., Ref. [34]).
- Experimental issues: μ-ReLU (theoretically invertible) performs worse than baseline ReLU in GAN MLP—needs explanation and investigation of the reversibility assumption in practical architectures like DCGAN.
- Suggests comparing results to WGANs (outside f-divergence theory) and notes performance differences among activations/link functions are within error bars, giving weak empirical support.
- Minor point: deformed exponential densities and concepts like deformation/signature and escorts need more intuition for ML readers.
- Theorem 8 needs clarification: compare its upper bounds for J(Q), explain implications for activation selection, and justify why decreasing the escort normalization Z is advantageous and how to use it.
- Conclusion: theoretical insights are interesting but currently insufficiently practical or convincing for GAN practitioners.
