The authors pose several interesting GAN-related questions: the extent to which solving the GAN objective implies convergence in parameter space; what the generator is actually fitting upon convergence; and, perhaps most practically from an architectural standpoint, how to choose the discriminator’s output activation to ensure the loss has the proper compositeness property.

They investigate these issues within the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions and derive, among other things, the following theoretical results:
- A variational generalization (amenable to an f-GAN formulation) of a known theorem linking an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between the parameters of those distributions. This provides a compelling bridge between the information-theoretic view of discrepancies between probability distributions and the information-geometric view of discrepancies between their parameters.
- Under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions.
- Their analysis suggests that carefully choosing the generator’s hidden activation functions and the discriminator’s output activation could further improve GANs.
- They briefly discuss an alternative interpretation of the GAN game via expected utility theory.

Overall, the theoretical results are interesting, but I have significant concerns about their practical relevance and usefulness in their current form.

Questions and concerns:
- Many theorems and conclusions assume that the data and model distributions P and Q belong to the deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption—i.e., clarify when it holds in practice—and explain whether similar results (e.g., Theorems 4 and 6) can be obtained without it?
- A central contribution is the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq. (7), which relates the standard variational f-divergence over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. What concrete advantages does this parameter-based perspective offer? Is it feasible to implement GANs using this parameter-based optimization, and what would be the benefits? I would have expected experiments comparing these two optimization approaches. Somewhat surprisingly, the paper does not appear to use the right-hand side of the identity beyond asserting that solving the GAN game implies parameter convergence (when the residual J(Q) is small). Why is this implication nontrivial? Can the authors provide a realistic example where convergence in the variational f-divergence over distributions fails to imply convergence in parameter space?
- A particularly practical implication is that the choice of the discriminator’s output activation g_f (see section 2.4 in Ref. [34]) matters to ensure the loss is properly composite. I would have liked to see experiments comparing the theoretically derived g_f (as the composition of f′ and the link function) to the heuristic in Ref. [34].

Consequences for deep learning and experimental results:
- Assuming a reversible generator, the authors show there exists an activation function v such that the generator’s hidden layers factor exactly as escorts for the deformed exponential family. In Table 2 (left), they compare generator architectures under various activation functions (which may or may not satisfy this escort factorization). For the GAN MLP, the “theoretically superior” μ-ReLU actually underperforms the baseline ReLU (the μ → 1 limit). Based on their analysis, I would have expected the invertible μ-ReLU (satisfying the reversibility assumption) to outperform the non-invertible baseline (violating that assumption). Why does μ-ReLU perform worse in practice?
- Can the authors comment on whether DCGAN satisfies the reversibility assumption, and how these findings compare to the Wasserstein GAN, which is not based on f-divergences (and for which their analysis may not apply)?
- Finally, as far as I can tell, the differences in performance across activations (Table 2, center) and link functions (Table 2, right) are within error bars. This does not convincingly support the practical relevance of the theoretical claims.

Minor concerns:
- Because many results rely on deformed exponential densities, it would help the broader ML audience to include more intuition about what a deformation (or signature) is and what an escort is.
- In Theorem 8, the authors provide several upper bounds on J(Q). How do these bounds compare, and what are the implications for choosing activation functions? They also state that this upper bound decreases with the escort’s normalization parameter Z. Why is this desirable, and how can it be exploited in practice?

In light of these points, I do not find the practical impact of the theoretical analysis to be substantial from a GAN practitioner’s perspective.
