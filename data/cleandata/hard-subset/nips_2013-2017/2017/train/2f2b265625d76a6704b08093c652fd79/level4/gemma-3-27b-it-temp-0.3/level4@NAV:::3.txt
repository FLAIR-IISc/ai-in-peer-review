The authors investigate several important questions surrounding Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), including the relationship between GAN convergence and parameter space convergence, the generator’s learned representation upon convergence, and the optimal selection of discriminator output activation functions to ensure loss function compositeness. They approach these questions using the framework of deformed exponential distributions, yielding several theoretical results.

Specifically, they present a variational generalization of a theorem connecting f-divergences between distributions to Bregman divergences between parameters, establishing a link between information-theoretic and information-geometric perspectives on dissimilarity. They further demonstrate that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. This investigation suggests that careful selection of generator hidden activations and discriminator output activations could improve GAN performance, and they briefly explore an alternative interpretation of the GAN game through the lens of expected utility theory.

Overall, the paper presents interesting theoretical contributions. However, I have concerns regarding the practical relevance and applicability of these results in their current form.

My primary questions and concerns are as follows: Many of the theorems and insights rely on the assumption that data and model distributions, P and Q, belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors should justify this assumption, elaborating on its validity in practical scenarios and exploring whether similar results could be obtained without it (e.g., Theorems 4 & 6). 

Their key contribution, the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq.(7), relates the variational f-divergence formulation to a parameter-based optimization problem. The authors should clarify the advantages of this parameter-based perspective and discuss the feasibility and potential benefits of implementing GANs using this approach. I would have appreciated experimental validation comparing optimization using both formulations. Furthermore, the authors largely do not utilize the right-hand side of this identity beyond stating that GAN convergence implies parameter space convergence when the residual J(Q) is small. This implication seems non-trivial and warrants further explanation. Could the authors provide a scenario where variational f-divergence convergence does *not* imply parameter space convergence?

The authors demonstrate the importance of the discriminator output activation function for achieving proper compositeness of the GAN loss. This result is particularly compelling, and I would have liked to see a comparison of their theoretically derived activation function against the heuristic choice presented in Ref. [34] through experimentation.

Regarding consequences for deep learning, the authors show that, assuming generator reversibility, an activation function exists such that the generator’s hidden layers factor as escorts for the deformed exponential family. However, their experimental comparison in Table 2 (left) shows that the theoretically superior μ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU. This contradicts the expectation that the invertible μ-ReLU (satisfying the reversibility assumption) would outperform the non-invertible baseline. The authors should address this discrepancy. Additionally, they should comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption and how these results compare to those obtained with Wasserstein GANs, which are not based on f-divergences and therefore fall outside the scope of their theoretical analysis. Finally, the differences in performance observed across activations and link functions in Tables 2 (center and right) are all within the margin of error, providing insufficient evidence to support the practical relevance of their theoretical findings.

Minor points: Given that the broader machine learning community may be unfamiliar with deformed exponential densities, providing more intuitive explanations of concepts like deformation (or signature) and escorts would be beneficial. In Theorem 8, the authors derive upper bounds for J(Q). A comparison of these bounds and a discussion of their implications for activation function selection would be valuable. They also note that this upper bound decreases with the escort normalization parameter Z; further elaboration on why this is desirable and how it can be leveraged would be appreciated.

In conclusion, while the theoretical analysis yields interesting insights, I believe they are not currently substantial enough to be highly impactful for GAN practitioners.
