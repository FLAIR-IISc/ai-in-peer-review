Thank you for an interesting read.
This paper presents an information-geometric (IG) perspective on the f-GAN algorithm. It first establishes convergence in parameter space by leveraging the one-to-one mapping between f-divergences and chi-divergences together with a Bregman divergence argument. It then discusses a proper way to implement f-GAN. The main text also provides a factorization result for deep neural network representations and analyzes the choice of activation functions, showing a one-to-one correspondence with the chi (or f) function. I did not check every detail in the appendix, but the proofs appear correct, except for Theorem 8, which I did not have time to read before the deadline.
The paper is dense and contains many new results likely to interest both the machine learning and information theory communities. I am particularly impressed by the exposition of Theorem 4, which clearly articulates the IG aspect of f-GAN. Overall, I consider this a clear accept.
That said, the paper may be difficult to follow for readers who are not already familiar with the connections between information theory and information geometry (e.g., the fact that the KL divergence on an exponential family reduces to a Bregman divergence, and the role of Fenchel duality). Many deep learning practitioners—arguably the main audience for a GAN paper—may fall into this category. To improve clarity, I suggest:
1. I like Figure 1 in the appendix, which summarizes the IT–IG connections in the f-GAN context; consider moving it to the main text.
2. It would help to state early on why the IG view is useful. My understanding is that geometry in parameter space can elucidate optimization behavior. You briefly mention this on page 7, which feels too late.
3. I do not fully see how Section 5 connects to f-GAN. It seems to use the deformed exponential family to characterize distributions representable by deep neural nets, rather than presenting a consequence of the f-GAN optimization results. While I agree that v, f, and chi have a one-to-one correspondence, the paper does not explain how this informs the design of the f-GAN game—for example, which f-divergence to choose, or, given an activation function, which f-GAN objective is best in terms of convergence.
4. For Theorem 6, why can the phi_l be viewed as “deep sufficient statistics”? Equation (13) does not appear to be in the form of a deformed exponential family.
5. As mentioned, it might help to relocate lines 257–269. The utility-theory paragraph does not seem directly related to the IG view; consider removing it from the main text (it can remain in the appendix) to free space to explain the main results.
6. The experiments do not seem tightly linked to the paper’s central claims. For (A), Section 5 alone suffices—you do not need the IG view to apply the new activation functions. For (B), one mainly needs Section 4, which is also only loosely connected to the IG view of f-GAN. The WGAN results are particularly distracting and confusing in a paper centered on f-GANs; I even had to search for the brief mention on lines 227–228.
In summary, while the paper provides many useful results and dense derivations, the presentation is not organized in a crystal-clear way. It reads like material compressed from multiple papers into an 8-page NIPS submission. I support acceptance but recommend significant editing to make the claims clearer and more coherent.
