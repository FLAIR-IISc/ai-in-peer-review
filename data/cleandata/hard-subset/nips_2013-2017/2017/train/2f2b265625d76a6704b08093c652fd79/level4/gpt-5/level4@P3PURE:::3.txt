This paper raises several noteworthy GAN-related questions: to what extent does solving the GAN problem imply convergence in parameter space; what the generator is fitting at convergence; and, from an architectural perspective, how to choose the discriminator’s output activation to ensure proper compositeness of the loss.

The authors address these questions within an information-theoretic framework based on deformed exponential distributions and derive several theoretical results. They present a variational generalization (amenable to an f-GAN formulation) of a known theorem relating an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between the parameters of those distributions. This provides an interesting bridge between the information-theoretic view of dissimilarity between distributions and the information-geometric view of dissimilarity between parameters. They also show that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their analysis suggests that careful choices of the generator’s hidden activation functions and the discriminator’s output activation could improve GAN performance. They further note an alternative interpretation of the GAN game in the context of expected utility theory.

Overall, the theoretical results are interesting, but I have several concerns about their practical relevance in the current form.

Questions and concerns:
- Scope of assumptions. Many results are derived under the assumption that the data and model distributions P and Q belong to the deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption—i.e., is it met in practice—and clarify whether similar results (e.g., Theorems 4 and 6) could be established without it?
- Information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7). One of the main contributions is the identity that maps the variational f-divergence over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. What is concretely gained from this parameter-based viewpoint? Is it feasible to implement GAN training directly via this parameter-based optimization, and what are the expected benefits? I would have liked to see experiments comparing distribution-space optimization to this parameter-space approach. In addition, the right-hand side of Eq. 7 is not used beyond asserting that solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space (provided the residual J(Q) is small). Why is this implication not obvious? Can the authors provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence over distributions does not translate into convergence in parameter space?
- Discriminator output activation and proper compositeness. The result that the choice of the discriminator’s output activation gf (see Section 2.4 in Ref. [34]) matters for proper compositeness is practically appealing. It would strengthen the paper to compare the theoretically derived gf (the composition of f′ with the link function) against the heuristic choice in Ref. [34] on standard benchmarks.
- Consequences for deep learning and experiments. Assuming a reversible generator, the authors show there exists an activation v such that the generator’s hidden layers factor exactly as escorts for the deformed exponential family. In Table 2 (left), the GAN MLP results indicate that the “theoretically superior” μ-ReLU actually performs worse than the baseline ReLU (the limiting case μ → 1). Given that the invertible μ-ReLU satisfies the reversibility assumption while the baseline ReLU does not, I would have expected μ-ReLU to perform better. Can the authors explain this discrepancy? Do DCGAN architectures satisfy the reversibility assumption, and how do the theoretical predictions align with results for Wasserstein GANs, which are not based on f-divergences (and thus seemingly outside the scope of the analysis)? Finally, the performance differences for activation functions (Table 2, center) and link functions (Table 2, right) appear to be within error bars. As presented, this does not convincingly support the practical relevance of the theoretical claims.

Minor concerns:
- Accessibility of the framework. Because many results rely on deformed exponential densities, it would help the broader ML community if the paper provided more intuition, including clear explanations of “deformation” (or “signature”) and “escort.”
- Bounds on J(Q) (Theorem 8). The paper provides several upper bounds on J(Q). How do these bounds compare, and what guidance do they offer for choosing activation functions? You also note that the upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort normalization parameter Z. Why is this desirable in practice, and how can practitioners exploit this in model design or training?

Conclusion:
While the paper offers an appealing set of theoretical connections—particularly the link between f-divergences over distributions and Bregman divergences over parameters, the factorization into escorts under reversibility, and guidance on discriminator output activations—the current empirical support is limited, and key assumptions (notably membership in the deformed exponential family and generator reversibility) are not convincingly justified for practical settings. In its present form, the work’s practical impact for GAN practitioners appears limited.
