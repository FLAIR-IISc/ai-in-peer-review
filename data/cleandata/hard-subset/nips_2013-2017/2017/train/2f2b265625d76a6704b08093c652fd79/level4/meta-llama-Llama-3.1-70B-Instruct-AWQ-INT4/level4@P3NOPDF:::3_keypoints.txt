- Paper studies GAN questions: link between solving the GAN objective and parameter-space convergence, generator fit at convergence, and discriminator output activation for proper compositeness, within deformed exponential distributions.
- Main theoretical contribution: variational generalization linking f-divergence between distributions to Bregman divergence between their parameters.
- Shows (under a reversibility assumption) deep generative networks can be factored as escorts of deformed exponential families.
- Argues generator hidden activations and discriminator output activations can be chosen carefully to potentially improve GANs; briefly offers an expected-utility interpretation of the GAN game.
- Concern: many results require P and Q to lie in the deformed exponential family—needs justification whether this holds in practice or can be relaxed.
- Concern: unclear practical benefits of the information-geometric f-GAN identity—authors should explain implementation, advantages, and provide experimental comparisons to distributional optimization.
- Concern: authors only use the identity to claim parameter convergence when residual J(Q) is small; need examples where variational convergence does not imply parameter convergence to motivate this claim.
- Practical implication about output activation is interesting but needs experimental comparison to heuristic choices (Ref. [34]).
- Experiments problematic: μ-ReLU (theoretically motivated) performs worse than ReLU in MLP GANs—authors should explain, clarify whether DCGAN satisfies reversibility, and compare to non-f-divergence methods like WGAN.
- Minor: paper needs more intuition and accessible explanations for deformed exponential densities, deformations/signatures, and escorts for ML readers.
- Minor: Theorem 8 gives multiple upper bounds for J(Q) but lacks comparison between bounds, implications for activation choice, and explanation of why decreasing bound with escort normalization Z is beneficial.
- Overall: theoretical insights are interesting but practical relevance is limited by strong assumptions and unconvincing experimental validation.
