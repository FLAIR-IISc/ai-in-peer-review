This paper proposes an information geometric (IG) view of the f-GAN algorithm. It demonstrates that f-GAN convergence can be understood through the bijective mapping between f-divergence and chi-divergence, leveraging a Bregman divergence result. The authors also discuss a principled approach to implementing f-GAN and present a factorization result for deep neural network representations, alongside a discussion of suitable activation functions linked to the chi (or f) function. While I did not exhaustively verify every detail in the appendix, the proofs appear correct, with the exception of Theorem 8 which I lacked time to fully examine.

I find this paper highly dense and containing numerous novel results potentially valuable to both machine learning and information theory. I am particularly impressed by the exposition of Theorem 6, which illuminates the information geometric aspects of f-GAN. Therefore, I recommend acceptance. 

However, the paper’s complexity may pose a challenge for readers less familiar with the connections between information theory and information geometry—specifically, the foundational relationship between KL divergence for exponential families and Bregman divergence, and the concepts of Fenchel duality. A significant portion of the target audience for a GAN paper, namely deep learning engineers, might fall into this category. I offer the following suggestions to enhance clarity:

1.  Consider moving Figure 1 from the appendix to the main text. It effectively visualizes the connections between IT and IG within the f-GAN context.
2.  A clearer statement articulating the benefits of the IG perspective early in the paper would be helpful. I believe it allows leveraging the parameter space geometry to analyze optimization behavior, a point briefly mentioned on page 7, which felt somewhat late in the presentation.
3.  The connection between Section 5 and f-GAN is not immediately apparent. It appears to focus on the representational capacity of deep neural networks within the framework of deformed exponential families, rather than a direct consequence of f-GAN optimization. While the one-to-one correspondence between v, f, and chi is noted, the implications for f-GAN design—such as optimal f-divergence selection given an activation function or the best f-GAN objective for a given activation—are not fully explored.
4.  Clarify why the phi\_l in Theorem 6 can be interpreted as "deep sufficient statistics." Equation (13) does not explicitly represent a deformed exponential family.
5.  Reconsider the placement of lines 257-269. The discussion surrounding utility theory seems less directly connected to the core IG view, and could potentially be moved to the appendix to streamline the presentation and create space for clarifying the main results.
6.  The experiments feel somewhat disconnected from the paper's central claims. For instance, the results in Section 5 related to activation functions (A) could be presented independently of the IG perspective on f-GAN. Similarly, the discussion in Section 4 is not intrinsically tied to the IG view. The WGAN results, in particular, might be distracting and could be de-emphasized, as the paper primarily focuses on f-GANs. I had to search for the clarifying statement on lines 227-228 regarding WGAN.

In summary, while this paper presents many valuable results and intricate derivations, the material isn't organized in a perfectly lucid manner. It appears to consolidate findings from multiple investigations into a single NIPS submission. Despite this, I support acceptance, but believe the paper would benefit from editing to enhance the clarity and coherence of its claims.
