This paper addresses several important questions regarding Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), including the extent to which solving the GAN optimization problem implies convergence in parameter space, the nature of the distribution the generator fits at convergence, and the principled selection of the discriminator's output activation function to ensure a properly composite loss. The authors tackle these questions using the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions, leading to several theoretical contributions.

First, they introduce a variational generalization of a known theorem, compatible with the f-GAN framework, that connects the f-divergence between distributions to the Bregman divergence between their corresponding parameters. This result forges a valuable link between the information-theoretic view of distributional dissimilarity and the information-geometric perspective of parameter dissimilarity. Second, they demonstrate that under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as "escorts" of deformed exponential distributions. Third, their analysis suggests that specific choices for the generator's hidden activations and the discriminator's output activation can improve GAN performance. Finally, they briefly propose an alternative interpretation of the GAN objective through the lens of expected utility theory.

While the paper presents a series of interesting theoretical results, I have several concerns regarding their practical relevance and the empirical support provided.

### Questions & Concerns

1.  **Justification of the Deformed Exponential Family Assumption:** A significant portion of the theoretical framework, including key theorems and insights, relies on the assumption that the data (P) and model (Q) distributions belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors should provide a stronger justification for this assumption. Is there evidence that this condition is met by real-world data distributions commonly used in GAN literature? Furthermore, could similar results, particularly Theorems 4 and 6, be derived under more general assumptions?

2.  **Practical Utility of the Information-Geometric f-GAN Identity:** The paper's central identity in Eq. (7) elegantly connects the standard f-divergence optimization over distributions to an information-geometric optimization over parameters. However, the practical benefit of this new perspective is not made clear.
    *   Could this parameter-based objective be used directly for training GANs, and if so, what would be the advantages over the standard variational approach? The absence of experiments comparing these two optimization strategies is a missed opportunity.
    *   The authors primarily use this identity to argue that convergence in distribution space implies convergence in parameter space (provided the residual J(Q) is small). This conclusion seems intuitive; could the authors describe a realistic scenario where convergence in the f-divergence would *not* imply convergence in parameter space, thereby highlighting the non-triviality of their result?

3.  **Lack of Empirical Validation for Discriminator Activation:** The theoretical finding that the choice of the discriminator's output activation function (`gf`) is crucial for achieving a properly composite loss is a potentially significant practical contribution. However, this insight is not validated empirically. A comparison between the theoretically-derived `gf` (as a composition of f′ and the link function) and the heuristic choices commonly used (e.g., in Ref. [34]) would have substantially strengthened the paper's practical claims.

4.  **Interpretation and Significance of Experimental Results:** The experimental section raises more questions than it answers.
    *   **Contradictory Performance:** In Table 2 (left), the theoretically-motivated μ-ReLU, which satisfies the paper's reversibility assumption, performs worse than the standard ReLU (its μ → 1 limit), which does not. This appears to contradict the theoretical motivation. Could the authors explain this discrepancy?
    *   **Unstated Assumptions:** Does the DCGAN architecture used in the experiments satisfy the reversibility assumption required by the theory? How should these results be interpreted in relation to methods like Wasserstein GAN, which are not based on f-divergences and for which the theory presumably does not apply?
    *   **Statistical Significance:** The reported performance differences for various activation and link functions in Table 2 (center and right) appear to be within the error bars. As such, these results do not provide compelling evidence that the theoretical recommendations translate into meaningful practical improvements.

### Minor Concerns

*   **Clarity on Terminology:** The paper relies heavily on concepts from deformed exponential densities (e.g., "deformation," "signature," "escort"). Providing more intuitive explanations of these terms would make the work more accessible to the broader machine learning community.
*   **Implications of Bounds in Theorem 8:** The authors derive several upper bounds for the residual J(Q). It is unclear how these bounds relate to one another and what practical guidance they offer for selecting activation functions. Additionally, the paper states that the bound decreases with the escort's normalization parameter Z and that this is beneficial. Further elaboration on why this is desirable and how it can be exploited in practice would be helpful.

In summary, while the paper offers an interesting theoretical lens through which to view GANs, its practical implications are not sufficiently developed or supported by the experimental results. The connection between the sophisticated theoretical framework and concrete, validated improvements for GAN practitioners remains tenuous.
