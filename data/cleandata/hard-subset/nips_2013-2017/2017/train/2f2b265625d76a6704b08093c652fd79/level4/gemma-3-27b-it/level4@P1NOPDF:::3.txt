The authors identify several interesting questions related to Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), such as the relationship between solving the GAN problem and convergence in parameter space, what the generator learns upon convergence, and – perhaps most relevant from a network architectural perspective – how to choose the discriminator’s output activation function to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function.

The authors aim to address these questions within the (information-theoretic) framework of deformed exponential distributions, deriving several theoretical results. They present a variational generalization – amenable to the f-GAN formulation – of a known theorem linking an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between their parameters. This provides an interesting connection between the information-theoretic view of measuring dissimilarity between probability distributions and the information-geometric perspective of measuring dissimilarity between the corresponding parameters. They demonstrate that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as “escorts” of deformed exponential distributions. Their investigation further suggests that careful choices of both generator hidden activation functions and the discriminator’s output activation function could potentially improve GAN performance. They also briefly outline an alternative interpretation of the GAN game in the context of expected utility theory.

Overall, I find that the authors present some interesting theoretical results, but I have several concerns regarding the practical relevance and usefulness of these results in their current form.

**Questions & Concerns:**

Firstly, many of their theorems and resulting insights hold specifically when the data and model distributions, P and Q, are members of the deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption? Specifically, can they elaborate on whether this assumption is met in practice, and explain if similar results (such as Theorems 4 & 6) could be derived without it?

One of the authors’ main contributions, the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq.(7), relates the well-known variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. What is the benefit of this parameter-based viewpoint? Is it possible to implement GANs using this parameter-based optimization, and what advantages would it offer? I would have appreciated experimental results comparing the two approaches to GAN optimization. Surprisingly, the authors don’t seem to utilize the right-hand side of this identity beyond asserting that solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space (given a small residual J(Q)). Why is this implication not immediately obvious? Can the authors provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence formulation over distributions does *not* imply convergence in parameter space?

As an interesting practical consequence of their theoretical work, the authors show that the choice of the discriminator’s output activation function, gf (see section 2.4 in Ref.[34]), is crucial for achieving the desirable property of proper compositeness in the GAN loss. I found this result particularly interesting and would have been keen to see how their theoretically derived gf (as the composition of f′ and the link function) compares experimentally against the heuristic choice in Ref. [34].

**Consequences for Deep Learning and Experimental Results:**

Under the assumption of a reversible generator network, the authors demonstrate the existence of an activation function v such that the generator’s hidden layers exactly factor as escorts for the deformed exponential family. In Table 2 (left), the authors compare various generator architectures with different activation function choices (that may or may not comply with this escort factorization). The plot for the GAN MLP, for example, suggests that the “theoretically superior” μ-ReLU actually performs worse than the baseline ReLU (limiting case μ → 1). Given their analysis, I would expect the invertible μ-ReLU (satisfying the reversibility assumption) to outperform the non-invertible baseline (which does not). Can the authors explain this counterintuitive result? Can they comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption, and how these results compare against those from Wasserstein GANs (which are not based on f-divergences and for which their theoretical analysis might therefore not apply)? Finally, as far as I can tell, the differences in performance between activation functions (Table 2 center) or link functions (Table 2 right) are all within the margin of error. This does not provide sufficient support for their theoretical results having practical relevance.

**Minor Concerns:**

Many of their results are derived within the framework of deformed exponential densities. As the broader machine learning community might not be familiar with this, the authors could provide more intuition regarding concepts such as “deformation” (or “signature”) and “escort.”

In Theorem 8, the authors derive several upper bounds for J(Q). How do these bounds compare to one another, and what are the implications for choosing activation functions? They also note that this upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the normalization parameter Z of the escort. Can they elaborate on why this is beneficial and how we can leverage it?

In light of these comments, I believe the insights gained from the theoretical analysis are not substantial enough to be immediately useful for a GAN practitioner.
