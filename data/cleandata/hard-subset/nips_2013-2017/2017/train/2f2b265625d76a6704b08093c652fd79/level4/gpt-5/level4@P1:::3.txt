Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper revisits the f-GAN framework of Nowozin et al. by bringing information geometry into the picture. The authors work with deformed (χ-)exponential families, a broad generalization of exponential families, and establish a variational identity that links the variational f-divergence objective to a Bregman divergence between natural parameters plus a penalty term involving escorts. Concretely, for P and Q in a χ-exponential family, they derive an identity of the form f-GAN(P, escort(Q)) = D(θ‖ϑ) + Penalty(Q), connecting distribution-level (information-theoretic) and parameter-level (information-geometric) views. They then analyze deep generators under a reversibility assumption, showing that popular architectures can factorize a large class of χ-escort densities with compact representations, and they give sufficient conditions on activation functions that guarantee this factorization. On the discriminator side, they clarify the role of the output activation gf in f-GANs: by invoking proper composite losses, they argue that gf is not arbitrary but should be the composition of f′ and a link function Ψ, and they exhibit alternatives (e.g., Matsushita link). They supplement these results with empirical studies on MNIST and LSUN, exploring generator activations (e.g., μ-ReLU, Softplus) and discriminator links.

Strengths:
- Theoretical contribution: The variational identity bridging f-divergences and Bregman divergences over parameters for χ-exponential families is insightful and generalizes the classic KL–Bregman correspondence beyond regular exponential families. This offers a principled path to reason about GAN convergence in parameter space.
- Architectural guidance: The analysis yields actionable conditions on generator activations (strongly/weakly admissible) and provides a principled account of the discriminator’s output activation via proper composite losses, clarifying the somewhat arbitrary choice in prior f-GAN implementations.
- Breadth: Deformed exponential families substantially extend exponential families, making the framework potentially applicable to richer models than those covered by earlier KL-only identities.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Assumption of χ-exponential families: Many results (e.g., Theorems 5–7, 9, 12) rely on P and Q lying in a χ-exponential family, and on the generator being reversible. Please justify how plausible these assumptions are in practice, and whether analogous statements (such as the core identity and escort factorization) can be established without them. Can you characterize when data distributions encountered in computer vision or NLP are well-approximated by χ-families?
- Value of the parameter-space view: The information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. (11) in the paper) reframes the variational objective as D(θ‖ϑ) + J(Q). What do we gain in practice from optimizing in parameter space? Can one design training objectives that directly minimize D(θ‖ϑ) and compare them empirically against the standard distribution-level variational objective? The current experiments do not exploit the right-hand side beyond the observation that convergence in f-divergence can imply convergence in parameters (up to J(Q)). Please provide realistic scenarios where distributional convergence does not guarantee parameter convergence, and show how your identity resolves the gap.
- Discriminator output activation: I found the proper composite loss perspective compelling. It would be very useful to see experiments comparing your theoretically derived gf = f′∘Ψ against the heuristic choice used in prior f-GAN implementations (Nowozin et al., Sec. 2.4). The current results with the Matsushita link are promising but limited in scope.
- Generator reversibility and activations: Under reversibility, there exists an activation v making hidden layers factor as escorts. Yet the GAN-MLP results show μ-ReLU sometimes underperforms ReLU, and several comparisons appear within error bars. Please explain: (i) does the DCGAN variant used actually satisfy invertibility assumptions (including fractional-strided convolutions)? (ii) why might μ-ReLU underperform despite theoretical advantages? (iii) to what extent should we expect your analysis to carry over to WGAN, which is not an f-divergence method, yet appears in your experiments?
- Practical impact of J(Q): In Theorem 8 you bound J(Q). How tight are these bounds in practice, and how do they inform the choice of activation/signature χ? You note that the bound decreases with the escort normalization Z; why is that beneficial, and how can practitioners control Z via architectural or activation choices?

Clarity:
The paper is dense and mathematically careful, but parts assume familiarity with χ-exponential families, escorts, and signatures. Given the audience, more intuition would help:
- Provide a short, self-contained explanation of deformations/signatures and escorts, with simple examples (e.g., how ReLU/Softplus relate to χ and to escort densities).
- When introducing the proper composite loss view, explicitly connect to the gf choice in f-GAN and summarize how Ψ affects the optimal discriminator and density ratio estimation.

Originality:
The extension from KL/exponential families to f-divergences/χ-families, and the variational identity that aligns with f-GAN, appear novel and timely. The link to proper composite losses in this context is also a useful clarification.

Quality:
The theoretical development is interesting and appears sound at a high level, though several key components are deferred to the supplement. The empirical section provides initial support for the activation and link-function recommendations, but the effects are modest and often within error bars. Stronger, targeted experiments are needed to substantiate practical gains.

Significance:
Conceptually, the work could influence how we think about GAN convergence and architecture design. Practically, its impact is limited in the current form because the assumptions are strong (χ-family membership, reversibility), and the empirical validation is not yet decisive.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical advance connecting f-GANs to information geometry for χ-families.
- Principled guidance on discriminator links via proper composite losses.
- Conditions on generator activations with a constructive path from activations to χ.

Cons for acceptance:
- Strong assumptions with limited empirical justification.
- Lack of experiments leveraging parameter-space optimization or demonstrating clear gains from the identity.
- Mixed empirical results on activations and links; many differences fall within error bars.
- Limited intuition for deformations/escorts for a broad ML audience.

Minor suggestions:
- Clarify theorem/eq. numbering when referenced in text (e.g., the central identity is Eq. (11)).
- Expand the intuition around signatures χ and escorts with diagrams or simple toy examples.
- Discuss how tight the J(Q) bounds are and how they guide activation/χ choices in practice.

Overall, the theoretical contributions are intriguing, but stronger empirical validation and clearer guidance for practitioners are needed to demonstrate practical relevance.
