

The authors identify several compelling GAN-related questions, including the extent to which solving the GAN problem implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator is actually fitting upon convergence, and (from a network architectural perspective) how to select the discriminator's output activation function to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function.  

Within an information-theoretic framework based on deformed exponential distributions, the authors derive several theoretical results. They present a variational generalization (adaptable to f-GAN formulations) of a known theorem linking an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between their parameters. This theorem establishes a meaningful connection between the information-theoretic perspective of measuring distribution dissimilarity and the information-geometric perspective of measuring dissimilarity between parameters. The authors further demonstrate that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their theoretical analysis suggests that careful selection of generator hidden activation functions and proper choice of discriminator output activation functions could enhance GAN performance. They also briefly propose an alternative interpretation of the GAN game through expected utility theory.  

While the theoretical results are intriguing, I have significant concerns regarding their practical relevance and utility in the current form.  

**Key concerns:**  
1.  Many theorems and insights assume data and model distributions \(P\) and \(Q\) belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors must justify this assumption: Does it hold in practice? Could similar results (e.g., Theorems 4 & 6) be derived without it?  
2.  The authors' main contribution, the information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7), relates the variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. What practical advantages does this parameter-based perspective offer? Can GANs be implemented using this optimization, and what benefits would it provide? Experimental comparisons between the two optimization approaches are essential but absent. Notably, the authors do not utilize the right-hand side of Eq. 7 beyond asserting that GAN convergence implies parameter-space convergence (when residual \(J(Q)\) is small). Why is this implication non-obvious? Provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the f-divergence formulation *fails* to imply convergence in parameter space.  
3.  The authors show the discriminator's output activation function \(g_f\) (Section 2.4, Ref. [34]) must be chosen for proper compositeness. This is a particularly valuable insight; I expected a direct experimental comparison between their theoretically derived \(g_f\) (as \(f' \circ\) link function) and the heuristic choice in Ref. [34].  

**Consequences for deep learning and experimental results:**  
Under the reversibility assumption for the generator, the authors prove the existence of an activation \(v\) enabling exact escort factorization for the generator's hidden layers. Table 2 (left) compares architectures with activation functions (complying or not with escort factorization). For the GAN MLP, the theoretically superior μ-ReLU underperforms the baseline ReLU (limiting case \(\mu \to 1\)). Given their analysis, the invertible μ-ReLU should outperform the non-invertible baseline. The authors must explain this discrepancy. Additionally, they should clarify whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption and how their results compare to Wasserstein GAN (which does not rely on f-divergences, making their theoretical analysis inapplicable). Finally, all comparative results in Table 2 (center and right) fall within error bars, providing insufficient evidence for practical relevance.  

**Minor concerns:**  
- The framework relies on deformed exponential densities, which may be unfamiliar to the broader ML community. The authors should provide intuitive explanations of "deformation," "signature," and "escort."  
- Theorem 8 derives upper bounds for \(J(Q)\). How do these bounds compare, and what do they imply for activation function selection? The authors note \(J(Q)\)'s upper bound decreases with the escort normalization parameter \(Z\); elaborate on why this is beneficial and how it can be leveraged.  

In light of these points, the practical insights from the theoretical analysis are insufficient for GAN practitioners. The work requires stronger justification of assumptions, experimental validation of theoretical claims, and clearer articulation of practical utility.
