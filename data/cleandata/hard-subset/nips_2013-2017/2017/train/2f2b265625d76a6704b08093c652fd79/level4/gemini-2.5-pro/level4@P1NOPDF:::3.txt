This paper explores several interesting theoretical questions related to Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors investigate the extent to which solving the GAN objective implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator is actually fitting at convergence, and how to select the discriminator's output activation function to ensure the proper compositeness of the loss function.

To address these questions, the authors employ the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions. Their main theoretical contributions are:
1.  A variational generalization of a known theorem that relates an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between their parameters. This result, amenable to the f-GAN formulation, provides a valuable connection between the information-theoretic view of distributional dissimilarity and the information-geometric view of parameter dissimilarity.
2.  A proof that under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as so-called escorts of deformed exponential distributions.
3.  Theoretical guidance suggesting that a careful choice of the generator's hidden activations and the discriminator's output activation could improve GAN performance.
4.  A brief alternative interpretation of the GAN game within the context of expected utility theory.

Overall, the paper presents some interesting theoretical results. However, I have several concerns regarding their practical relevance and usefulness in their current form.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Assumption of Deformed Exponential Family:** A central concern is that many theorems and insights rely on the assumption that the data and model distributions, P and Q, belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors should justify this assumption, elaborate on whether it is met in practice, and discuss whether key results (such as Theorems 4 & 6) could be derived without it.

2.  **Practical Utility of the Information-Geometric Identity:** One of the paper's main contributions, the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq. (7), relates the standard variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. The practical benefit of this parameter-centric view is not made clear. Is it possible to implement GANs using this parameter-based optimization, and what would be the advantages? The paper would have been much stronger with experimental results comparing the two optimization approaches. Furthermore, the authors do not appear to leverage the right-hand side of this identity beyond asserting that solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space (provided the residual J(Q) is small). It is not obvious why this implication is non-trivial. Could the authors provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence formulation over distributions *does not* imply convergence in parameter space?

3.  **Lack of Experiments for Discriminator Activation:** A key practical implication of the theory is that the choice of the discriminator's output activation function, g_f, is crucial for the GAN loss to have the desirable proper compositeness property. I found this result particularly interesting. However, it is not validated experimentally. I would have liked to see how the theoretically derived g_f (as the composition of f' and the link function) compares against the heuristic choice provided in the f-GAN paper (Ref. [34]).

4.  **Weak Experimental Support:** The experimental results provided do not offer sufficient support for the paper's theoretical claims. For example, under the assumption that the generator network is reversible, the theory suggests that certain activation functions (like μ-ReLU) are preferable. However, in Table 2 (left), the "theoretically superior" μ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU on the GAN MLP task. Given that the invertible μ-ReLU satisfies the reversibility assumption while the non-invertible ReLU does not, this result is counter-intuitive and contradicts the theory's predictions. Could the authors explain this discrepancy? Additionally, can they comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption? A comparison against the Wasserstein GAN, which is not based on f-divergences and for which this analysis is not expected to hold, would also be insightful. Finally, the reported results comparing different activation functions (Table 2, center) and link functions (Table 2, right) all appear to be within error bars. This is not sufficient evidence to support the claim that the theoretical results are relevant in practice.

**Minor Concerns:**

1.  **Clarity of Concepts:** The framework of deformed exponential densities may be unfamiliar to many in the machine learning community. I would encourage the authors to provide more intuition for key concepts like "deformation," "signature," and "escort" to improve the paper's accessibility.

2.  **Bounds in Theorem 8:** In Theorem 8, the authors derive several upper bounds for the residual J(Q). It would be helpful if they discussed how these bounds compare against each other and what their implications are for choosing activation functions. They also mention that this upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort's normalization parameter Z. Could they elaborate on why this is a desirable property and how this insight can be leveraged?

In light of these comments, I believe that while the theoretical analysis is interesting, the insights gained are not yet substantial enough to provide clear guidance for GAN practitioners.
