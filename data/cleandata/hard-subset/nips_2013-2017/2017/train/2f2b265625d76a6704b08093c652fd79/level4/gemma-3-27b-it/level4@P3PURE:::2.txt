Thank you for an interesting read. This paper presents an information geometric (IG) perspective on the f-GAN algorithm. It first demonstrates that f-GAN converges in parameter space, leveraging the one-to-one mapping between f-divergence and chi-divergence, alongside a result concerning Bregman divergences. The authors then discuss a principled implementation of f-GAN and, in the main text, provide a factorization result for deep neural network representations, relating the choice of activation functions to the corresponding chi (or f) function. While I did not verify every detail in the appendix, the proofs appear correct, with the exception of Theorem 8, which I did not have time to fully examine.

I find this paper to be highly dense and containing numerous novel results potentially valuable to both machine learning and information theory. I am particularly impressed by the exposition of Theorem 4, which articulates the IG foundations of f-GAN. Therefore, I recommend this paper for acceptance.

However, I believe the paper’s density and reliance on specialized knowledge may pose a barrier to understanding for readers less familiar with the connections between information theory (IT) and information geometry (IG)—specifically, concepts such as the mapping of KL divergence within an exponential family to Bregman divergence, and Fenchel duality. Many deep learning engineers, who represent a primary audience for GAN papers, may fall into this category. To improve clarity, I suggest the following:

1. Consider moving Figure 1 from the appendix to the main text. It effectively illustrates the connections between IT and IG in the context of f-GAN.
2. Explicitly state the benefits of adopting an IG viewpoint early in the paper. As I understand it, this geometry allows for analysis of optimization behavior in parameter space, a point currently mentioned briefly on page 7, which feels somewhat late.
3. Clarify the connection between Section 5 and f-GAN. Currently, it appears to utilize a deformed exponential family to characterize the distribution representable by a deep neural network, rather than deriving directly from the f-GAN optimization results. While the one-to-one correspondence between v, f, and chi is acknowledged, the paper does not elaborate on how this correspondence informs the design of the f-GAN game—for example, guiding the selection of an appropriate f-divergence or suggesting optimal objective functions for given activation functions.
4. Explain the reasoning behind viewing phi_l as “deep sufficient statistics” in Theorem 6. Equation (13) does not appear to conform to the structure of a deformed exponential family.
5. Reconsider the placement of lines 257-269. Furthermore, the discussion on utility theory appears tangential to the core IG perspective and could potentially be moved to the appendix to create space for a more detailed explanation of the main results.
6. The experiments do not appear to directly support the central claims of the paper. For instance, the results from Section 5 concerning activation functions can be discussed independently of the IG view of f-GAN. Similarly, the insights from Section 4 regarding convergence are also largely independent. The inclusion of WGAN results may be distracting and confusing, given the paper’s primary focus on f-GANs. I had to actively search for the relevant discussion of WGAN (lines 227-228).

In summary, while the paper presents valuable results and detailed derivations, the organization feels somewhat disjointed. It reads as a compilation of results from multiple investigations condensed into an 8-page NIPS submission. While I support acceptance, I believe the paper would benefit significantly from editing to enhance clarity and coherence of its claims.
