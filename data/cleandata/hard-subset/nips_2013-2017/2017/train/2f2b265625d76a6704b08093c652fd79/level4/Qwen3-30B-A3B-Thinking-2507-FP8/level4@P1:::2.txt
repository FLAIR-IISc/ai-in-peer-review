

This paper presents an information geometric framework for understanding f-GANs, establishing a connection between the f-divergence formulation of the GAN objective and Bregman divergences through deformed exponential families. The authors demonstrate that for distributions within a broad class of deformed exponential families (a superset of regular exponential families), the f-GAN objective can be equivalently expressed as a geometric optimization problem. This provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for understanding generator convergence in parameter space, which has been an open question in the GAN literature. The authors further show that standard deep neural network architectures can efficiently factorize these deformed exponential families, establishing a one-to-one correspondence between activation functions and the signature of the deformed exponential family. The paper also completes the supervised game picture by incorporating proper composite losses, clarifying the role of the link function in the discriminator.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a novel variational information-geometric identity (Corollary 8) that bridges f-divergences with Bregman divergences through deformed exponential families. This identity reveals that solving the f-GAN game guarantees convergence in parameter space, addressing a key limitation in Nowozin et al.'s original formulation. Second, the authors prove that deep neural networks can factorize escorts of deformed exponential families with compact architectures, showing that the total dimension of sufficient statistics is linear in the number of layers rather than quadratic as in shallow networks. Third, they establish a theoretical foundation for selecting activation functions in generators and link functions in discriminators, demonstrating that popular activations like ReLU, ELU, and Softplus satisfy the necessary conditions.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical contributions are novel and significant, extending the information geometric perspective of GANs beyond previous work. The connection between deep architectures and deformed exponential families provides a principled explanation for why deep networks work well in GANs. The experimental results (Section 6) support the theoretical claims, showing that carefully chosen activation functions and link functions can improve performance on standard benchmarks like MNIST and LSUN. The paper is technically sound, with well-constructed proofs and a clear theoretical narrative.

However, the paper's presentation could be improved. The dense theoretical content makes it challenging for readers unfamiliar with information geometry. The connection between the information geometric framework and practical GAN implementation is not sufficiently emphasized early in the paper. While the authors mention that the geometric perspective can guide activation function design, they do not explicitly state how this framework helps practitioners choose between different f-divergences or how to select optimal parameters for a given architecture. The experimental section would be strengthened by explicitly linking the results to the theoretical claims rather than presenting them as separate findings.

The paper would benefit from several specific revisions. First, the relationship between the information geometric framework and practical GAN implementation should be clearly articulated in the introduction, not buried in Section 7. Second, the connection between Section 5 (proper composite losses) and the main information geometric framework could be made more explicit. The current presentation makes it appear as a separate contribution rather than a natural consequence of the geometric perspective. Third, Figure 1 from the appendix would be more effective in the main text to illustrate the connections between information theory and information geometry.

The experiments, while relevant to the paper's claims, are not sufficiently tied to the main theoretical contribution. For instance, the results in Section 6A about varying activation functions could be framed as direct applications of the theoretical conditions established in Lemma 11, rather than presented as separate experimental findings. Similarly, the comparison of link functions in Section 6B should explicitly connect to the theoretical insights from Theorem 12.

The paper's main weakness is its organization. The technical content is excellent, but the presentation buries the key insights within dense mathematical derivations. A more structured approach would help readers grasp the significance of the results more quickly. The authors should consider moving the conceptual overview of the information geometric perspective to the introduction and using the supplementary material for the more technical proofs.

Despite these organizational challenges, the paper makes important theoretical contributions that advance our understanding of GANs. The connection between deep architectures and deformed exponential families provides a principled explanation for the empirical success of GANs, and the theoretical framework offers new avenues for designing better GAN architectures. The paper's technical quality is excellent, with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important gap in the GAN literature. While the presentation could be improved, the theoretical contributions are substantial and would be valuable to the machine learning community. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in understanding GANs from an information geometric perspective.

Given the paper's technical quality, novelty, and significance, I recommend acceptance. The authors should address the presentation issues in the camera-ready version to make the paper more accessible to a broader audience. The theoretical framework established here has the potential to influence future work on GANs and generative modeling more broadly.
