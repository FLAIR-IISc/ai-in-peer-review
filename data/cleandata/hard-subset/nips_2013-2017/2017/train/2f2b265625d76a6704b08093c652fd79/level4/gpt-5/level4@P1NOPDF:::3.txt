The authors identify several interesting GAN-related questions: to what extent solving the GAN objective implies convergence in parameter space; what the generator is actually fitting when convergence occurs; and, perhaps most relevant architecturally, how to choose the discriminator’s output activation so the loss is a proper composite. They study these questions within the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions and derive, among other results: (i) a variational generalization (amenable to the f-GAN formulation) of a known theorem relating an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between parameters—thus connecting information-theoretic dissimilarities between distributions with information-geometric dissimilarities between parameters; (ii) a factorization result showing that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be expressed as escorts of deformed exponential distributions; (iii) guidance suggesting that careful choices of generator hidden activations and the discriminator output activation could improve GANs; and (iv) an alternative interpretation of the GAN game in terms of expected utility theory.

Overall, the paper presents interesting theoretical insights, but I have significant concerns about their practical relevance and usefulness in the current form.

Questions and concerns:
- Scope of assumptions. Many results apply only when the data and model distributions P and Q lie in the deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption—specifically, how often is it met in practice? Can similar results (e.g., Theorems 4 and 6) be derived without this restriction, or at least for broader classes of models?
- Information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7). One of the main contributions links the variational f-divergence over distributions to an information-geometric optimization over parameters. What do we gain in practice from this parameter-based viewpoint? Is it possible to implement GAN training directly via this parameter-space optimization, and what are the concrete benefits? I would have expected empirical comparisons between the standard variational f-divergence optimization and its parameter-space counterpart. Somewhat surprisingly, the right-hand side of the identity appears to be used only to claim that solving the GAN game implies parameter convergence (assuming the residual J(Q) is small). Why is this implication not obvious from the variational formulation? Can the authors provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence over distributions does not entail convergence in parameter space?
- Discriminator output activation gf. A particularly interesting practical implication is that the choice of gf (see Section 2.4 in Ref. [34]) matters to ensure that the GAN loss is a proper composite. I would have liked to see experiments comparing the theoretically derived gf (the composition of f′ and the link function) against the heuristic choice in Ref. [34].

Consequences for deep learning and experimental results:
- Under the assumption that the generator is reversible, the authors show that there exists an activation v such that the generator’s hidden layers factor exactly as escorts of the deformed exponential family. In Table 2 (left), they compare several generator architectures under different activations, some of which satisfy this escort factorization. For the GAN MLP, the “theoretically superior” μ-ReLU actually performs worse than the baseline ReLU (the limiting case μ → 1). Based on the analysis, I would have expected the invertible μ-ReLU (which satisfies the reversibility assumption) to outperform the non-invertible baseline (which violates it). Can the authors explain why μ-ReLU underperforms?
- Can the authors comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption? How should we interpret the results in light of this assumption? Also, how do these findings compare against the Wasserstein GAN, which is not based on f-divergences and to which the presented theory may not apply?
- Finally, as far as I can tell, the observed differences in performance across activations (Table 2, center) and link functions (Table 2, right) are within error bars. This does not provide sufficient empirical support that the theoretical recommendations yield practical gains.

Minor concerns:
- Exposition of deformed exponential densities. Since much of the analysis hinges on deformed exponential densities, it would be helpful to provide more intuition for the broader ML audience—for example, clear explanations of what a deformation (or signature) is and what an escort distribution represents.
- Theorem 8 and bounds on J(Q). The authors derive several upper bounds on J(Q). How do these bounds compare in tightness, and what are the implications for choosing activation functions in practice? The paper notes that the bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort’s normalization parameter Z. Why is this desirable, and how can practitioners leverage this in model design or training?

In light of these comments, I do not find the theoretical insights, as currently presented and evaluated, to be substantial enough from the perspective of a GAN practitioner.
