

The authors address several theoretically intriguing questions about GANs, including the relationship between solving the GAN problem and convergence in parameter space, the nature of the generator's target during convergence, and the selection of discriminator output activations to ensure loss compositeness. Within the framework of deformed exponential distributions, they derive key theoretical results, including a variational generalization of an f-divergence/Bregman divergence theorem that bridges information-theoretic and information-geometric perspectives. They further establish that under reversibility, deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions, suggesting that careful choice of generator hidden activations and discriminator output activations could improve GAN performance. An alternative interpretation via expected utility theory is also noted.

While the theoretical contributions are intellectually stimulating, the practical relevance of these results in their current form is questionable. The following concerns warrant clarification:

1.  **Restrictive Assumption Justification:** The core theorems (e.g., Theorems 4 & 6) assume data and model distributions \(P\) and \(Q\) belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors must justify this assumption's practical validity. Is this family sufficiently general to encompass real-world data distributions? Crucially, they should address whether similar results can be derived without this assumption, as its necessity significantly limits applicability.

2.  **Value of the Parameter-Based Optimization View:** The central "information-geometric f-GAN identity" (Eq. 7) reinterprets the variational f-divergence minimization as an optimization over parameters. The authors must clarify *what is gained* from this perspective. Can this view enable novel GAN implementations? If so, what are the specific benefits over standard f-GAN training? The absence of experimental comparisons between the distribution-based and parameter-based optimization approaches is a significant omission. Furthermore, the claim that GAN convergence implies parameter convergence (under small residual \(J(Q)\)) requires justification. Why is this not immediately obvious? A concrete counterexample where distribution convergence *fails* to imply parameter convergence would strengthen the argument.

3.  **Contradictory Experimental Findings:** The theoretical prediction that reversible generator activations (like \(\mu\)-ReLU) should outperform non-reversible ones (like ReLU) is contradicted by Table 2 (left). The "theoretically superior" \(\mu\)-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU. The authors must explain this discrepancy. Does the reversibility assumption hold for the MLP architecture tested? Additionally, the DCGAN architecture's compatibility with the reversibility assumption needs comment. How do these results compare to Wasserstein GAN (WGAN), which operates outside the f-divergence framework (and thus falls outside the authors' theoretical scope)? Finally, the comparative results in Table 2 (center and right) show no statistically significant differences within error bars, undermining the claim that specific activations or link functions offer practical advantages.

**Minor Concerns:**
*   The paper assumes familiarity with deformed exponential families, a niche concept. Providing intuitive explanations for "deformation," "signature," and "escort" would greatly enhance accessibility for the broader ML audience.
*   Theorem 8 derives upper bounds for \(J(Q)\). The authors should clarify the comparative tightness of these bounds and their practical implications for activation function selection. The statement that \(J(Q)\) decreases with the escort normalization parameter \(Z\) needs elaboration: why is this beneficial, and how can it be leveraged in practice?

**Overall Assessment:**
The theoretical work presents an interesting connection between information geometry and GANs. However, the reliance on the restrictive deformed exponential family assumption, the lack of clear practical implementation pathways or experimental validation for the core claims (especially the activation function results), and the contradictory empirical findings significantly diminish the contribution's impact for GAN practitioners. The insights, as presented, do not provide sufficient actionable guidance or demonstrate tangible improvements over existing methods. The paper would require substantial revisions—particularly justifying the core assumptions, demonstrating practical implementation benefits, and resolving the experimental contradictions—to be considered a meaningful contribution to the GAN literature. As it stands, the theoretical novelty does not translate into substantial practical value.
