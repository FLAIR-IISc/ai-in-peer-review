This paper addresses several important theoretical questions regarding Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). The authors investigate the extent to which solving the f-GAN objective implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator converges to, and how to select the discriminator's output activation function to ensure the loss function is properly composite. To address these questions, the authors employ the information-geometric framework of deformed exponential families. Their main theoretical contributions include:

*   A variational generalization of a known theorem that connects f-divergences between distributions to Bregman divergences between their corresponding parameters. This result is amenable to the f-GAN formulation and establishes a valuable link between the information-theoretic and information-geometric views of GANs.
*   A proof that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be shown to factor as "escorts" of deformed exponential families.
*   Theoretical guidance suggesting that principled choices for the generator's hidden activation functions and the discriminator's output activation function could lead to improved GAN performance.

Overall, the paper presents a set of interesting theoretical results. However, I have several concerns regarding the practical relevance and immediate usefulness of these findings as they are currently presented.

**Questions & Concerns:**

Many of the paper's key theorems and insights rely on the assumption that the data and model distributions, P and Q, are members of a deformed exponential family. Could the authors justify this assumption? Specifically, is it likely to be met in practice for real-world data distributions? Furthermore, could similar results (e.g., the information-geometric identity) be derived without this strong assumption?

A central contribution is the "variational information-geometric f-GAN identity" (Corollary 8, Eq. 11), which connects the standard variational f-divergence objective to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. What is the practical benefit of this parameter-based perspective? Could GANs be optimized directly in this parameter space, and if so, what advantages would that offer? It would have been compelling to see an experimental comparison between the standard distributional optimization and this proposed parametric optimization. Surprisingly, the paper does not seem to leverage the right-hand side of this identity, other than to argue that solving the f-GAN game implies convergence in parameter space (assuming the penalty J(Q) is small). Why is this implication not already obvious? It would be helpful if the authors could provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the distributional sense (i.e., vanishing f-divergence) does not imply convergence in parameter space.

The paper makes an interesting practical point regarding the choice of the discriminator's output activation function, `gf`, discussed in Nowozin et al. [30]. The authors show this choice is critical for the loss to be properly composite. This is a compelling result. However, the experiments do not seem to directly compare the theoretically-derived `gf` (as a composition of f' and a link function) against the more heuristic choices used in the original f-GAN paper. Such a comparison would have provided strong support for this part of the theory.

**Experimental Results:** The experiments test some consequences of the theory, particularly regarding the choice of activation and link functions. However, the results are not fully convincing. Under the reversibility assumption, the theory connects certain activation functions to the escort factorization. The results in Figure 1 (left) for the GAN MLP architecture show that µ-ReLU (which is invertible and should fit the theory) often performs worse than the standard, non-invertible ReLU. This seems to contradict the theoretical motivation. Can the authors explain this discrepancy? Relatedly, do the specific DCGAN architectures used in the experiments satisfy the reversibility assumption? Furthermore, some experiments use the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN), but the paper's theory is developed for f-divergences. It is unclear how the theoretical results are expected to apply to WGANs. Finally, across all experiments in Figure 1, the performance differences between the various activation and link functions appear to be minor and likely within the bounds of experimental noise. The current results do not provide strong evidence that the proposed theoretically-motivated choices lead to practical improvements.

**Minor Concerns:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper relies heavily on the framework of deformed exponential families. Since this may be unfamiliar to much of the machine learning community, it would be very helpful if the authors could provide more intuition for key concepts like "deformations" (signatures) and "escorts".
*   **Penalty Term J(Q):** The paper discusses the penalty term J(Q) and its bounds. To better understand its role, could the authors compare the different bounds against each other and explain their implications for selecting activation functions? They also state that a bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort's normalization constant Z. Could they elaborate on why this is beneficial and how this insight could be leveraged in practice?

In light of these comments, while the theoretical direction is interesting, I believe the practical insights for a GAN practitioner are not yet substantial enough in the paper's current form.
