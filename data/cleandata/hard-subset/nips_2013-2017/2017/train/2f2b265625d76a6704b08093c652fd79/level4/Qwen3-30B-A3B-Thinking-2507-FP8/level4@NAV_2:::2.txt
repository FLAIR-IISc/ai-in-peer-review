

This paper provides an information geometric (IG) perspective on f-GANs, establishing a connection between f-divergences and Bregman divergences through deformed exponential families. The authors demonstrate that f-GAN converges in parameter space by leveraging a variational generalization of the well-known identity relating KL divergence to Bregman divergence for exponential families. They extend this to show that deep generator architectures can effectively factorize escorts of deformed exponential families, revealing a one-to-one correspondence between activation functions and the chi-function used in the divergence formulation. The paper also completes the supervised game picture by characterizing the optimal discriminator's link function as a proper composite loss.

The paper makes several novel contributions: (1) a variational information-geometric identity for f-GANs (Theorem 7 and Corollary 8), (2) a characterization of deep architectures as factorizing escorts of deformed exponential families (Theorem 9), and (3) a proper composite loss perspective for the supervised game (Theorem 12). These results build upon Nowozin et al.'s foundational work on f-GANs while extending the theoretical understanding of GANs beyond the exponential family framework. The connection to information geometry is particularly valuable as it provides a geometric interpretation of the generator's convergence properties, which is missing in the original f-GAN formulation.

The technical quality is strong, with most proofs appearing sound and well-justified. The results extend the theoretical foundation of GANs by connecting them to information geometry and statistical physics, which is a significant advancement over previous work. The paper demonstrates how the IG perspective provides practical insights for designing better GAN architectures through principled activation function selection and discriminator link function design.

The clarity of the paper could be improved in several ways. The connection between the IG perspective and practical GAN implementation is not sufficiently emphasized early in the paper, which is important since the main audience includes deep learning practitioners who may not be familiar with information geometry. The paper would benefit from a clearer statement about why this IG perspective is useful for GAN practitioners at the beginning. Section 5, which discusses the proper composite loss framework, is somewhat disconnected from the main IG narrative and could be better integrated. The explanation of how the deep network layers relate to "deep sufficient statistics" (Theorem 9) is not sufficiently clear in the main text. The experiments, while interesting, could be more tightly connected to the theoretical contributions, as the activation function experiments in Section 6(A) could be understood without the IG perspective, and the WGAN experiments in Section 6(B) are somewhat distracting given the paper's focus on f-GANs.

The paper is highly original in connecting f-GANs to information geometry through deformed exponential families, which represents a significant extension beyond the exponential family framework used in previous information-geometric GAN work. This work clearly differentiates itself from recent NIPS papers on GANs, particularly those focusing on Wasserstein distances or alternative divergence formulations.

The significance of this work is substantial as it provides both theoretical insights and practical guidance for GAN design. The connection between activation functions and the chi-function offers a principled way to select activation functions for generator networks, and the proper composite loss framework provides a new perspective on discriminator design. These insights have the potential to influence future GAN research and development.

Strengths: Novel theoretical connection between f-GANs and information geometry, strong technical foundation, practical insights for GAN practitioners, significant extension of previous work on f-GANs.

Weaknesses: Lack of clear motivation for why the IG perspective is useful to GAN practitioners early in the paper, some sections (particularly Section 5) could be better integrated with the main narrative, experiments could be more tightly connected to theoretical contributions, and the paper is quite dense for a general machine learning audience.

This paper makes an important contribution to the theoretical understanding of GANs by connecting them to information geometry through deformed exponential families. While the paper is technically sound and makes significant theoretical advances, it would benefit from improved organization and clearer connections between the theoretical results and practical GAN implementation. The results are important enough to warrant acceptance, but the authors should address the clarity issues before publication.
