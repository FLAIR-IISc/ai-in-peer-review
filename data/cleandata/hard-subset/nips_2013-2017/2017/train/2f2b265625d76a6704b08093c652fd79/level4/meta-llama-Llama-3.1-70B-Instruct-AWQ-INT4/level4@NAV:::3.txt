The authors explore several intriguing GAN-related questions, including the extent to which solving the GAN problem implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator is fitting upon convergence, and how to choose the discriminator's output activation function to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function.

Within the framework of deformed exponential distributions, the authors derive several theoretical results, including a variational generalization of a known theorem that relates f-divergence between distributions to Bregman divergence between parameters. This theorem establishes an interesting connection between the information-theoretic and information-geometric perspectives on measuring dissimilarity between probability distributions and their corresponding parameters. They demonstrate that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factorized into escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their investigation suggests that careful selection of the generator's hidden activation functions and the discriminator's output activation function could potentially improve GANs. They also touch upon an alternative interpretation of the GAN game in the context of expected utility theory.

Overall, the authors present some interesting theoretical results. However, I have several concerns regarding the practical relevance and usefulness of their results in their current form.

Questions and Concerns:

To begin with, many of the authors' theorems and insights rely on the assumption that the data and model distributions P and Q belong to the deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption, explaining whether it is met in practice and discussing the possibility of deriving similar results without this assumption?

One of the authors' main contributions, the information-geometric f-GAN identity, relates the variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. Can the authors explain the benefits of this parameter-based perspective and discuss the potential implementation of GANs using this approach? I would have liked to see experimental results comparing the two optimization approaches for GANs. It is surprising that the authors do not utilize the right-hand side of this identity beyond asserting that solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space. Can the authors provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence formulation over distributions does not imply convergence in parameter space?

The authors' theoretical investigation has an interesting practical implication: the choice of the output activation function affects the compositeness property of the GAN loss. I found this result particularly interesting and would have liked to see a comparison of their theoretically derived activation function against the heuristic choice provided in Ref. [34].

Consequences for deep learning and experimental results: Under the assumption of a reversible generator network, the authors demonstrate that there exists an activation function that allows the generator's hidden layers to factor exactly as escorts for the deformed exponential family. However, the experimental results in Table 2 (left) show that the "theoretically superior" μ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU. Can the authors explain this discrepancy and comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption? How do these results compare against the Wasserstein GAN, which is not based on f-divergences? Finally, the results in Table 2 (center and right) do not provide sufficient evidence to support the practical relevance of their theoretical results.

Minor concerns:

Many of the authors' results rely on the framework of deformed exponential densities, which may be unfamiliar to the general ML community. Providing more intuition about deformations, signatures, and escorts would be helpful.

In Theorem 8, the authors derive upper bounds for J(Q). Can they compare these bounds and discuss the implications for choosing activation functions? They also mention that the upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the normalization parameter of the escort Z. Can they elaborate on the benefits of this property and how it can be leveraged?

In light of these comments, I believe that the insights gained from the theoretical analysis are not substantial enough from the perspective of a GAN practitioner.
