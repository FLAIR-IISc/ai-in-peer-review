

The authors address several key GAN-related questions, including whether solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator actually converges to during training, and how to select the discriminator's output activation function to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function. They frame their analysis within the theoretical framework of deformed exponential families, presenting a variational generalization of the relationship between f-divergences and Bregman divergences. This theorem establishes an important connection between information-theoretic and information-geometric perspectives on GANs. The authors further demonstrate that under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions, suggesting that specific activation function choices could enhance GAN performance. They also provide a theoretical justification for the discriminator's output activation function within the proper composite loss framework.

Strengths: The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by connecting f-GANs to information geometry, providing a novel perspective on the GAN game. The derivation of the variational information-geometric identity (Corollary 8) is technically sound and represents a meaningful extension of previous work. The connection between activation functions and escort factorization (Theorem 9 and Lemma 11) offers potential practical insights for GAN architecture design.

Weaknesses: The paper's main limitation is the restrictive assumption that both data and model distributions belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors fail to justify why this assumption is reasonable in practical applications or demonstrate how to verify it for real-world data. The theoretical results are not translated into concrete practical benefits, as the experimental comparisons (Table 2) show minimal improvements within error bars. The paper also doesn't explain how to implement the parameter-based optimization perspective in practice or demonstrate tangible advantages over existing approaches.

Specific concerns: The authors claim that their theoretical results imply convergence in parameter space, but fail to explain why this is non-obvious or provide a scenario where variational convergence doesn't imply parameter convergence. The experimental results contradict the theoretical expectations - the theoretically justified μ-ReLU activation performs worse than ReLU in the GAN MLP architecture, despite the reversibility assumption. The paper doesn't clarify whether DCGAN satisfies the reversibility assumption or how results compare to WGAN, which doesn't use f-divergences. The paper assumes familiarity with deformed exponential families, but doesn't provide sufficient intuition for this technical concept. The bound on J(Q) (Theorem 8) is not adequately explained or compared to other bounds.

Pro arguments: The paper establishes a novel theoretical connection between f-GANs and information geometry, offers a principled approach to activation function selection, and provides a proper composite loss framework for the GAN game.

Con arguments: The restrictive distributional assumption limits practical applicability, the theoretical results don't translate to clear practical benefits as demonstrated by the experiments, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why the theoretical insights matter to GAN practitioners.
