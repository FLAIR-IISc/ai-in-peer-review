Review- The paper surfaces several noteworthy GAN-related questions, including the degree to which solving the GAN objective ensures convergence in parameter space, what the generator is actually modeling when convergence is achieved, and—perhaps most relevant for architecture design—how to select the discriminator’s output activation so that the loss function is properly composite.
The authors tackle these issues within an information-theoretic framework based on deformed exponential distributions and derive, among other contributions, the following theoretical results:
They provide a variational generalization (compatible with the f-GAN framework) of a known theorem linking an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between their parameters. This result establishes an interesting bridge between the information-theoretic perspective of comparing distributions and the information-geometric perspective of comparing parameters. They further show that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their analysis additionally indicates that an appropriate choice of the generator’s hidden activations and a principled selection of the discriminator’s output activation can potentially improve GAN performance. They also briefly offer an alternative view of the GAN game through the lens of expected utility theory.
Overall, I think the paper contains several interesting theoretical insights; however, I have significant reservations about their current practical relevance and utility.

Questions & concerns:
To start, many theorems and conclusions are conditioned on the assumption that the data and model distributions P and Q belong to a deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption—i.e., discuss how often it holds in practice—and clarify whether analogous results (e.g., Theorems 4 and 6) could be obtained without it?
A central contribution, the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq. (7), connects the standard variational f-divergence objective over distributions to an information-geometric optimization over parameters. What do we gain from this parameter-space formulation? Is it feasible to implement GAN training directly via this parameter-based optimization, and what concrete benefits would it offer? I would have expected empirical comparisons between the distribution-level and parameter-level optimization approaches. Somewhat surprisingly, the manuscript does not actually exploit the right-hand side of the identity beyond asserting that solving the GAN game implies parameter convergence (assuming the residual J(Q) is small). Why is this implication nontrivial? Can the authors provide a realistic example where convergence in the variational f-divergence over distributions does not imply convergence in parameter space?
A practically relevant implication of the theory is that the discriminator’s output activation gf (see Section 2.4 of Ref. [34]) matters to ensure proper compositeness of the GAN loss. I found this particularly compelling and would have liked to see experiments comparing the theoretically motivated gf (as f′ composed with the link function) with the heuristic choice in Ref. [34].
Consequences for deep learning and experimental results: Assuming the generator is reversible, the authors show there exists an activation v such that the generator’s hidden layers factor exactly as escorts for the deformed exponential family. In Table 2 (left), several generator architectures are compared across activation choices that may or may not satisfy this escort factorization. For the GAN MLP, the plot suggests that the “theoretically preferable” μ-ReLU actually underperforms the baseline ReLU (the limiting case μ → 1). Based on the analysis, I would have expected the invertible μ-ReLU (which satisfies the reversibility assumption) to outperform the non-invertible baseline (which violates it). Why does μ-ReLU perform worse in practice? Also, does the DCGAN architecture meet the reversibility assumption, and how do these findings relate to Wasserstein GANs, which are not grounded in f-divergences (and thus appear outside the scope of the presented theory)? Finally, as far as I can tell, the differences among activations (Table 2 center) and link functions (Table 2 right) lie within error bars, which does not convincingly support the theory’s practical impact.

Minor concerns:
Much of the development relies on deformed exponential densities. Since this framework may be unfamiliar to much of the ML community, more intuition about what a deformation (or signature) is and what an escort represents would be helpful.
In Theorem 8, several upper bounds for J(Q) are derived. How do these bounds compare, and what guidance do they offer for selecting activation functions? The paper also notes that the upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort normalization constant Z. Could the authors elaborate on why this is beneficial and how it can be exploited in practice?
Given the above, I do not find the current practical implications sufficiently compelling from a GAN practitioner’s standpoint.
