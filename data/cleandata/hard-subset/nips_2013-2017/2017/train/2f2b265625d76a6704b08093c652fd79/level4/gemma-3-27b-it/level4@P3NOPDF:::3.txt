The authors tackle several fundamental questions surrounding Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), including the relationship between GAN convergence and parameter space convergence, the generator’s learned representation, and crucially, the selection of discriminator activation functions to ensure a well-behaved loss landscape. They approach these questions using the framework of deformed exponential distributions, deriving theoretical results such as a variational generalization of a theorem linking f-divergences to Bregman divergences, demonstrating that deep generative networks can be viewed as “escorts” of deformed exponential distributions under a reversibility assumption, and suggesting activation function choices for improved GAN training. While the presented theory is interesting, my primary concern lies with the practical relevance and demonstrable impact of these findings.

My first question concerns the strong assumption that both the data and model distributions belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors should justify this assumption, elaborating on its prevalence in real-world datasets and explaining whether comparable results, like Theorems 4 and 6, could be established without this constraint. A discussion of the limitations imposed by this assumption is critical.

A central contribution is the information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7), which bridges variational f-divergence optimization over distributions with parameter-based optimization. However, the authors need to clearly articulate the benefits of this parameter-based perspective. Specifically, is it possible to *implement* GANs utilizing this parameter-based optimization approach, and what tangible advantages would it offer? The absence of experimental comparisons between distribution-based and parameter-based GAN optimization is a significant omission. Furthermore, beyond stating that solving the GAN game implies parameter space convergence when J(Q) is small, the authors should elaborate on why this implication isn’t self-evident. A concrete example illustrating a scenario where f-divergence convergence does *not* translate to parameter convergence would significantly strengthen this point.

The authors correctly identify the importance of the discriminator’s output activation function, *gf*, for ensuring proper compositeness of the GAN loss. This is a potentially valuable insight. I would have greatly appreciated a comparative experimental evaluation of their theoretically derived *gf* (composition of f’ and the link function) against the heuristic choice found in the cited reference [34].

Regarding the reversibility assumption and the resulting generator factorization, the experimental results (Table 2) are perplexing. The authors demonstrate that under the reversibility assumption, the generator’s hidden layers should factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions, predicting superior performance for μ-ReLU compared to ReLU. However, the results in Table 2 (left) indicate the opposite – μ-ReLU performs *worse* than ReLU. A detailed explanation for this discrepancy is essential. Do the authors believe the observed performance difference is due to limitations in the experimental setup, or does it invalidate the core assumption? Furthermore, how do these results apply to architectures like DCGAN, which may not strictly adhere to the reversibility assumption? A comparison with Wasserstein GAN, which operates outside the f-divergence framework, would also be informative. Finally, the statistical significance of the observed differences in activation function and link function performance (Tables 2 center and right) appears marginal, falling within error bars and providing limited practical support for the theoretical predictions.

Several minor points could enhance the paper’s clarity. Given that deformed exponential densities may be unfamiliar to a broad machine learning audience, providing more intuitive explanations of concepts like “deformation” (or “signature”) and “escort” would be beneficial. In Theorem 8, the authors derive upper bounds for J(Q). A comparative analysis of these bounds and a discussion of their practical implications for activation function selection would be valuable. Further elaboration on why the decreasing trend of the upper bound on J(Q) with the normalization parameter Z is advantageous, and how to effectively leverage this observation, would also be appreciated.

In conclusion, while the authors present intriguing theoretical results, the connection to practical GAN training remains tenuous. The insights gained from the theoretical analysis, in its current form, do not appear substantial enough to significantly impact the work of a GAN practitioner. I recommend rejection, but would encourage resubmission if the authors can address the concerns raised, particularly with stronger experimental validation and a clearer articulation of the practical benefits of their theoretical framework.
