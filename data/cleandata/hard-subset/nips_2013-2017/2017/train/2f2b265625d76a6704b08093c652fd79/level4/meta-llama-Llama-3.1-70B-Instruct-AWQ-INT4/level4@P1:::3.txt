The authors identify several interesting GAN-related questions, such as to what extent solving the GAN problem implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator is actually fitting when convergence occurs, and how to choose the output activation function of the discriminator to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function.

The authors set out to address these questions within the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions, from which they derive several theoretical results. They present a variational generalization of a known theorem that relates an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between the parameters of such distributions. This theorem provides an interesting connection between the information-theoretic viewpoint of measuring dissimilarity between probability distributions and the information-geometric perspective of measuring dissimilarity between the corresponding parameters.

The authors also show that under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their theoretical investigation suggests that a careful choice of the hidden activation functions of the generator, as well as a proper selection of the output activation function of the discriminator, could potentially help to further improve GANs. They also briefly mention an alternative interpretation of the GAN game in the context of expected utility theory.

However, I have several concerns regarding the practical relevance and usefulness of their results in the present form. Firstly, many of their theorems and resulting insights hold for the specific case that the data and model distributions P and Q are members of the deformed exponential family. The authors should justify this assumption and elaborate on whether it is met in practice. Furthermore, they should explain whether similar results could be derived without this assumption.

Another concern is the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq.(11), which relates the well-known variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. The authors should explain what we gain from this parameter-based point of view and whether it is possible to implement GANs in terms of this parameter-based optimization. They should also discuss the benefits of this approach and provide experimental results comparing the two approaches to optimization of GANs.

The authors' experimental results are somewhat underwhelming, and I would have liked to see more substantial support for their theoretical results. For instance, the comparison of different activation functions in the generator and different link functions in the discriminator shows results that are mostly within error bars. The authors should provide more insight into why their theoretical analysis does not seem to hold in practice.

Minor concerns include the lack of intuition provided for the general ML community regarding deformed exponential densities, deformations, and escorts. The authors should provide more explanation and examples to help readers understand these concepts. Additionally, they should elaborate on the implications of their upper bounds for J(Q) and how these bounds compare against each other.

In light of these comments, I believe the insights gained from the theoretical analysis are not substantial enough from the point of view of a GAN practitioner. The authors should address these concerns and provide more practical relevance and usefulness to their results.

Strengths:

* The authors present some interesting theoretical results that provide new insights into the GAN framework.
* They derive a variational generalization of a known theorem that relates f-divergence to Bregman divergence.
* They show that deep generative networks can factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions under a reversibility assumption.

Weaknesses:

* Many results are derived within the framework of deformed exponential densities, which may not be familiar to the general ML community.
* The authors do not provide sufficient justification for the assumption that the data and model distributions are members of the deformed exponential family.
* Experimental results are somewhat underwhelming and do not provide substantial support for the theoretical results.
* The authors do not provide enough insight into why their theoretical analysis does not seem to hold in practice.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The authors present some interesting theoretical results that provide new insights into the GAN framework.
* Their work has the potential to improve our understanding of GANs and provide new avenues for research.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The authors do not provide sufficient justification for their assumptions and do not address the practical relevance and usefulness of their results.
* Experimental results are somewhat underwhelming and do not provide substantial support for the theoretical results.
* The authors do not provide enough insight into why their theoretical analysis does not seem to hold in practice.

Recommendation:

* The authors should address the concerns mentioned above and provide more practical relevance and usefulness to their results.
* They should provide more insight into why their theoretical analysis does not seem to hold in practice and how to improve the experimental results.
* The authors should consider revising their paper to provide more intuition and explanation for the general ML community regarding deformed exponential densities, deformations, and escorts.
