This paper presents an interesting and dense information geometric (IG) view of the f-GAN algorithm. The authors first establish that f-GAN converges in parameter space by leveraging the one-to-one mapping between f-divergence and chi-divergence, in conjunction with a result on Bregman divergence. The paper then discusses a proper implementation of f-GAN. The main text also provides a factorization result for deep neural network representations and discusses the choice of activation functions that have a one-to-one mapping to the chi (or f) function. While I did not check every detail in the appendix, the proofs I reviewed appear to be correct (with the exception of Theorem 8, which I did not have time to read before the deadline).

This paper contains numerous novel results that will likely be of interest to both the machine learning and information theory communities. The exposition of Theorem 4, which details the information geometric perspective of f-GAN, is particularly impressive. For these reasons, I recommend this paper for acceptance.

However, my main concern is that the paper may be inaccessible to readers who are not well-versed in the connections between information theory and information geometry (e.g., the relationship between KL divergence and Bregman divergence for exponential families, or Fenchel duality). As many deep learning practitioners—the primary audience for a GAN paper—may fall into this category, I believe the paper's clarity could be significantly improved.

My suggestions for improving the paper are as follows:

1.  I suggest moving Figure 1 from the appendix to the main text. It provides a valuable illustration of the connections between IT and IG in the context of f-GANs and would help orient the reader.

2.  The motivation for adopting an IG perspective should be stated more clearly and much earlier in the paper. The key insight that this view allows for analyzing optimization behavior in parameter space is a crucial selling point, but it is not mentioned until page 7, which is too late to frame the paper's contributions effectively.

3.  The link between Section 5 and the main f-GAN analysis is unclear. While the paper establishes a one-to-one correspondence between the activation function `v`, the f-divergence `f`, and the chi-function `chi`, it does not elaborate on how this theoretical connection can be used to improve the design of the f-GAN framework. For instance, it is not explained how this result could guide the choice of an optimal f-divergence for a given network architecture, or vice versa.

4.  I was unconvinced by the characterization of `phi_l` in Theorem 6 as "deep sufficient statistics." It is not immediately obvious how equation (13) conforms to the structure of a deformed exponential family, which would be needed to justify this terminology.

5.  Regarding the paper's structure, the motivating discussion on lines 257-269 would be more effective if moved to the introduction. Additionally, the paragraph on utility theory seems tangential to the core IG argument. Removing it from the main text (while perhaps retaining it in the appendix) would free up valuable space to elaborate on the central results.

6.  The experiments feel disconnected from the paper's main theoretical contributions. The results on new activation functions (A) seem to follow directly from Section 5 and do not depend on the broader IG framework for f-GANs. Similarly, the results in (B) appear to rely only on Section 4 and are not closely tied to the IG view. The inclusion of WGAN results is particularly distracting in a paper focused on f-GANs; I had to search the text to find the brief justification on lines 227-228.

In summary, while this paper offers many valuable results and dense derivations, its presentation could be substantially improved. The current structure gives the impression of several distinct research threads being combined into a single submission, rather than presenting a single, coherent narrative. Therefore, while I am supportive of acceptance, I strongly encourage the authors to revise the paper to improve the clarity and cohesion of their arguments.
