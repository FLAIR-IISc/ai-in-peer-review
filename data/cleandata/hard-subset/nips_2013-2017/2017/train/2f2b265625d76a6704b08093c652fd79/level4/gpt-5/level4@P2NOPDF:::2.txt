Thank you for an interesting and thought-provoking paper.

This work develops an information-geometric (IG) perspective on f-GAN. It first establishes convergence in parameter space via a one-to-one correspondence between f-divergences and chi-divergences together with a Bregman divergence argument. It then discusses practical implementation choices for f-GAN. Finally, the main text presents a factorization of deep neural network representations and analyzes activation functions that are in bijection with the chi (or f) function. I did not verify every detail in the appendix; apart from Theorem 8, which I could not read before the deadline, the proofs appear correct.

Overall assessment: the paper is dense and contains several novel results of potential interest to both machine learning and information theory. I was particularly impressed by the exposition around Theorem 4, which clearly articulates the IG side of the f-GAN story. I recommend acceptance.

That said, the paper may be hard to follow for readers not already familiar with the links between information theory (IT) and information geometry (IG)—for example, the mapping from KL on exponential families to Bregman divergences and the role of Fenchel duality. Many deep learning practitioners (a core audience for GAN work) may lack this background. To improve clarity and accessibility, I suggest:

1) Consider moving Appendix Figure 1, which nicely summarizes the IT–IG connections in the f-GAN context, into the main text.

2) State early why the IG viewpoint is beneficial. My understanding is that it enables reasoning about optimization behavior via parameter-space geometry. You mention this on page 7, which feels too late; introducing this motivation in the opening sections would better frame the contributions.

3) Clarify how Section 5 connects to f-GAN training. As written, it seems to use the deformed exponential family to characterize distributions representable by deep networks, which is not an immediate consequence of the f-GAN optimization analysis. While the one-to-one mapping among v, f, and chi is clear, the paper does not explain how this guides the design of the f-GAN game—for instance, how to choose an f-divergence given a desired activation (or vice versa), or which combinations yield better convergence.

4) For Theorem 6, please justify why the φ_l can be interpreted as “deep sufficient statistics.” Equation (13) does not obviously match the canonical form of a deformed exponential family; bridging this gap would help.

5) The material around lines 257–269 would be better placed elsewhere. The utility-theoretic discussion appears tangential to the IG perspective; consider moving it to the appendix and using the space to further explain the core results.

6) The experiments feel only loosely tied to the main theoretical claims. Point (A) appears to follow from Section 5 alone, without requiring the IG view, and point (B) depends mainly on Section 4, which is also not tightly linked to the IG analysis. The inclusion of WGAN results is distracting given the paper’s focus on f-GANs; moreover, the brief WGAN note at lines 227–228 is easy to miss.

In summary, the paper offers valuable results and careful derivations, but the presentation is not as cohesive as it could be. It reads like multiple papers compressed into an eight-page NeurIPS submission. I remain supportive of acceptance, but recommend editing to streamline the narrative, foreground the IG motivation, and make the claims and their empirical support more directly connected.
