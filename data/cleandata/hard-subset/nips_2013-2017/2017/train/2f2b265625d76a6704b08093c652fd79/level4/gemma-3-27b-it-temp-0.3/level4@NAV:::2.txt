Thank you for this interesting and insightful work.

This paper presents an information geometric (IG) perspective on the f-GAN algorithm, demonstrating convergence in parameter space through the established 1-1 mapping between f-divergence and chi-divergence, leveraging a result concerning Bregman divergences. The authors further detail a principled implementation of f-GAN and provide a factorization result for deep neural network representations, linking activation function choices to the chi (or f) function. While I did not exhaustively verify all details within the appendix, the proofs appear sound, with the exception of Theorem 8, which I was unable to fully review due to time constraints.

I found this paper to be highly substantive, containing numerous novel results with potential significance for both machine learning and information theory. I was particularly impressed by the clear exposition of Theorem 4, which effectively articulates the IG foundation of f-GAN. Overall, I recommend acceptance.

However, the paper’s density and technical depth may present challenges for readers less familiar with the connections between information theory and information geometry – specifically, the relationship between KL divergence for exponential families and Bregman divergences, as well as concepts from Fenchel duality. Given that a primary audience for GAN papers consists of deep learning practitioners, improving accessibility is crucial. To that end, I suggest the following:

1. Consider relocating Figure 1 from the appendix to the main text, as it effectively illustrates the key connections between IT and IG within the f-GAN framework.
2. Explicitly state the benefits of adopting an IG viewpoint early in the paper. As I understand it, this approach allows for the analysis of optimization behavior through the geometry of the parameter space. While this is briefly mentioned on page 7, a more prominent introduction would be beneficial.
3. Clarify the connection between Section 5 and f-GAN. Currently, it appears to utilize the deformed exponential family solely to characterize the distributions representable by deep neural networks, rather than as a direct consequence of f-GAN optimization. While the 1-1 correspondence between v, f, and chi is acknowledged, the paper does not elaborate on how this result informs f-GAN design – for example, guiding the selection of f-divergences or identifying optimal objectives given a specific activation function.
4. Elaborate on the justification for viewing phi_l as “deep sufficient statistics” in Theorem 6. Equation (13) does not immediately appear to conform to the structure of a deformed exponential family.
5. Reconsider the placement of lines 257-269. Furthermore, the discussion of utility theory appears somewhat tangential to the core IG perspective and could potentially be moved to the appendix to create space for a more detailed explanation of the main results.
6. The experimental results feel somewhat disconnected from the central claims of the paper. For example, the application of new activation functions (A) does not necessarily require understanding the IG view of f-GAN, and the results concerning Section 5 suffice. Similarly, (B) relies primarily on Section 4, which is not intrinsically linked to the IG perspective. The WGAN results, in particular, could be distracting and confusing, given the paper’s focus on f-GANs; I had to actively search for the relevant context on lines 227-228.

In summary, this paper presents valuable results and rigorous derivations. However, the organization feels somewhat disjointed, resembling a consolidation of multiple research threads into a single NIPS submission. While I support acceptance, I believe that focused editing to enhance clarity and coherence would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.
