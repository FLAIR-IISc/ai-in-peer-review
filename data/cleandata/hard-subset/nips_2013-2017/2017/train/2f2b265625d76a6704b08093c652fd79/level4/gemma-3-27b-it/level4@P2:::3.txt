The authors tackle several intriguing questions surrounding Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), including the convergence properties of the GAN game, the function learned by the generator, and the optimal design of discriminator activation functions to ensure proper loss composition. They frame their investigation within the context of deformed exponential distributions, deriving theoretical results that connect f-divergence minimization to information-geometric optimization over parameters. Specifically, they present a variational generalization of a theorem linking f-divergences and Bregman divergences, demonstrate that deep generative networks, under a reversibility assumption, can be viewed as factorizations of escorts of deformed exponential distributions, and suggest that careful choices of generator activation functions and discriminator output functions can improve GAN performance.

Overall, the paper presents interesting theoretical connections, but I have several concerns regarding the practical relevance and impact of these results in their current form.

**Questions & Concerns:**

A central assumption throughout much of the work is that data and model distributions, *P* and *Q*, belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors should rigorously justify this assumption. Is it commonly met in practical applications? If not, can similar results (e.g., Theorems 4 & 6) be derived without this constraint, or at least under weaker assumptions?

The authors introduce an information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 11) that relates the variational f-divergence formulation to a parameter-based optimization problem. What tangible benefits does this parameter-based perspective offer? Is it possible to implement GANs directly using this formulation, and if so, what advantages would accrue? Experimental comparison of these two optimization approaches would be highly valuable. Furthermore, the authors acknowledge convergence in the variational f-divergence does not necessarily imply convergence in parameter space without a small residual J(Q). A concrete example illustrating a scenario where this occurs would strengthen the argument.

The authors identify the importance of the discriminator output activation function *gf* (Section 2.4 of Nowozin et al. [30]) for proper loss composition. Their theoretically derived *gf*, as the composition of *f’* and the link function, is an interesting result. Experimental comparisons between this theoretically optimal choice and the heuristic selection from Nowozin et al. [30] are needed to demonstrate its practical effectiveness.

**Consequences for Deep Learning and Experimental Results:**

Assuming network reversibility, the authors demonstrate the existence of an activation function *v* that allows the generator’s hidden layers to factor as escorts of deformed exponential families. However, the experimental results (Table 2, left) are not entirely convincing. For instance, in the GAN MLP case, the theoretically favored μ-ReLU performs *worse* than the baseline ReLU. Given their analysis, one would expect the invertible μ-ReLU (satisfying the reversibility assumption) to outperform the non-invertible ReLU. Can the authors explain this counterintuitive outcome? 

It would also be useful to clarify whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption and to compare these results with those obtained using Wasserstein GANs, which are not based on f-divergences and for which their theoretical framework might not directly apply. The reported experimental differences between activation functions and link functions (Table 2, center and right) appear to fall within the margin of error, providing limited support for the theoretical results’ practical relevance.

**Minor Concerns:**

The paper frequently references deformed exponential densities. To broaden its accessibility, the authors could provide more intuitive explanations of concepts like "deformation" (or "signature") and "escort" for readers unfamiliar with these terms.

Theorem 8 presents several upper bounds for J(Q). A comparison of these bounds and a discussion of their implications for activation function selection would be beneficial. The authors also mention that this upper bound decreases with the escort’s normalization parameter *Z*. Further elaboration on why this is desirable and how it can be leveraged would enhance understanding.

In conclusion, while the paper offers intriguing theoretical insights, the lack of strong experimental validation and the reliance on specific distributional assumptions limit its immediate impact from the perspective of a GAN practitioner.
