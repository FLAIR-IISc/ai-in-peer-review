The authors pose several pertinent GAN-related questions: to what extent solving the GAN game entails convergence in parameter space; what object the generator actually learns upon convergence; and, of particular architectural relevance, how to select the discriminator’s output activation so the loss is a proper composite loss.

They tackle these issues within the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential (χ-exponential) families and derive, among other things, the following contributions:
- A variational generalization (compatible with the f-GAN formulation) of the classical identity linking an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between their natural parameters. This connects an information-theoretic measure over densities with an information-geometric measure over parameters.
- Under a reversibility assumption, they show deep generators factorize as “escorts” of deformed exponential families.
- Their analysis suggests design guidance: judicious choices of generator hidden activations and of the discriminator’s output activation (to ensure proper compositeness) can improve GAN training.
- They briefly allude to an alternative interpretation of the GAN game via expected utility theory.

Overall, the paper offers interesting theoretical insights, but I have several concerns about their practical import in the current form.

Questions & concerns:
- Scope of the χ-exponential assumption: Many key theorems and conclusions apply when both data and model distributions P and Q lie in a deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this modeling assumption in practice? Are there realistic settings where it holds, and can similar results (e.g., Theorems 4 and 6 in the paper) be derived without relying on this assumption?
- Value of the parameter-space view: The main information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. (7) in the paper) maps the standard variational f-divergence objective over distributions to a parameter-space Bregman optimization. What concrete advantages does this parameter-based viewpoint yield? Can one implement GAN training directly using this parameter-space objective, and what benefits would that confer? An empirical comparison of distribution-level versus parameter-level optimization would have been compelling. As presented, the right-hand side of the identity is largely used only to assert that solving the GAN game implies parameter convergence (assuming J(Q) is small). Why is this implication nontrivial? Can the authors provide a realistic case where convergence in the variational f-divergence over distributions does not entail convergence in parameter space?
- Discriminator output activation and proper compositeness: A key practical takeaway is that the choice of gf (cf. Sec. 2.4 of Ref. [34]) is not arbitrary if one wants a proper composite loss. This is a valuable point. However, I would have liked to see experiments comparing the theoretically prescribed gf (composition of f′ with the link) against the heuristic in Ref. [34].
- Consequences for deep learning and experiments: Assuming generator reversibility, the authors prove the existence of an activation v such that hidden layers factor exactly as escorts of a deformed exponential family. In Table 2 (left), however, for GAN MLP the “theoretically motivated” μ-ReLU underperforms the baseline ReLU (the μ → 1 limit). Given that invertible μ-ReLU satisfies the reversibility assumption while ReLU generally does not, why does μ-ReLU perform worse here? Does the DCGAN configuration used in experiments satisfy the reversibility assumption, and how should we interpret results vis-à-vis Wasserstein GAN, which is not an f-divergence method (and thus outside the stated theory)? Finally, most observed differences between activations (Table 2 center) and link functions (Table 2 right) appear within error bars, which weakens empirical support for the claimed practical implications.

Minor concerns:
- Intuition for deformed exponentials and escorts: Since many results rely on χ-exponential families, additional intuition would help the broader ML audience—for example, what a “deformation/signature” χ and an escort are, and how they relate to familiar exponential-family concepts.
- Bounds on J(Q): In Theorem 8, several upper bounds on J(Q) are presented. How do these compare in tightness, and what do they imply for choosing activation functions in practice? The text notes that the upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort’s normalization Z—why is this desirable, and how can practitioners exploit this when designing architectures or activations?

In light of the above, while the theory is elegant, I find the practical impact for GAN practitioners insufficiently substantiated at present.
