### Summary

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of f-GANs through the lens of information geometry. Building on the work of Nowozin et al., which generalized the GAN framework to all f-divergences, the authors aim to answer several key questions: whether solving the f-GAN game implies convergence in the generator's parameter space, how to characterize the distribution the generator is fitting, and how these insights might inform network architecture design.

The core of the paper is a novel variational identity that connects the information-theoretic f-GAN objective to an information-geometric one. Specifically, the authors show that for distributions within a broad class known as "deformed exponential families," the variational f-divergence objective is equivalent to a Bregman divergence between the distributions' natural parameters, plus a penalty term. This result provides a direct link between minimizing a divergence between distributions and optimizing a geometric distance in a parameter manifold.

From this central theorem, the authors derive several consequences for deep learning. They demonstrate that, under a reversibility assumption, standard deep generator architectures can be shown to factorize a large number of "escort" distributions from this family, with certain activation functions (like ELU, Softplus, and variants of ReLU) being particularly well-suited for this task. The paper also re-frames the discriminator's objective in the context of proper composite losses, providing a principled method for choosing its final output activation function, a choice described as "somewhat arbitrary" in prior work.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Theoretical Depth:** The paper tackles fundamental and challenging theoretical questions about the behavior of GANs. The primary contribution—the "variational information-geometric f-GAN identity" (Corollary 8)—is an elegant and novel result that bridges the information-theoretic formulation of f-GANs with the information-geometric perspective. This provides a new and potentially powerful lens for analyzing GANs.

2.  **Clarification of Prior Work:** The analysis in Section 5 provides a clear and principled explanation for the choice of the discriminator's output activation function (`gf` in Nowozin et al.). By connecting it to the theory of proper composite losses and link functions, the authors resolve an ambiguity in the original f-GAN paper, which is a valuable contribution.

3.  **Architectural Insights:** The theoretical connection between generator activation functions and the factorization of deformed exponential families (Theorem 9 and Lemma 11) is intriguing. It suggests a principled, rather than purely empirical, basis for selecting activation functions in generative models.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

Despite its theoretical elegance, the paper's practical significance and the real-world applicability of its results are not yet clearly demonstrated. My main concerns are as follows:

1.  **The Deformed Exponential Family Assumption:** The central results of the paper, including the main identity in Corollary 8, rely on the assumption that the true data distribution (P) and the generator's distribution (Q) are both members of the same deformed exponential family. This is a strong assumption. Can the authors provide more justification for why this is a reasonable model for the complex, high-dimensional data distributions (e.g., natural images) that GANs are typically applied to? Do the key insights, such as convergence in parameter space, hold if this assumption is violated?

2.  **Practical Utility of the Geometric Viewpoint:** The paper establishes a beautiful equivalence between the variational objective and a Bregman divergence over parameters. However, the practical benefit of this new perspective is not explored. For instance:
    *   Could the right-hand side of the identity in Eq. (11) be used to formulate a new, perhaps more stable, optimization algorithm for GANs? An experimental comparison between optimizing the standard variational objective and a new parameter-space objective would have been a powerful demonstration of the work's utility.
    *   As it stands, the main takeaway from the identity seems to be that minimizing the f-divergence implies parameter-space convergence (if J(Q) is small). However, one might expect that for a well-specified and identifiable parametric model Qθ, convergence of distributions Qθ → P would inherently imply convergence of parameters θ → θ*. Could the authors clarify this point and provide a scenario where distribution convergence would *not* imply parameter convergence, thus highlighting the importance of their result?

3.  **Weak Experimental Support:** The connection between the theory and the experimental results feels tenuous. The experiments are intended to validate the paper's architectural suggestions, but the results are inconclusive.
    *   The experiments comparing different activation functions (Figure 1, left & center) do not show a clear winner. In the GAN MLP case, the theoretically-motivated µ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU. The authors should discuss potential reasons for this discrepancy, especially since the non-invertible ReLU does not satisfy the reversibility assumption of Theorem 9.
    *   Could the authors comment on whether the DCGAN architecture used in the experiments satisfies the reversibility assumption? Furthermore, since the Wasserstein distance is not an f-divergence in the Csiszár sense, how does the presented theory apply to the WGAN results?
    *   Most of the reported performance differences across different activation and link functions appear to be within the error bars, making it difficult to conclude that the theoretically-motivated choices offer a tangible and statistically significant benefit in practice.

### Minor Points

*   **Clarity for a Broader Audience:** The paper relies heavily on concepts from information geometry (e.g., deformed exponential families, signatures, escorts) that may be unfamiliar to a significant portion of the NIPS audience. While definitions are provided, adding more intuition behind these concepts would greatly improve the paper's accessibility and impact.
*   **Interpretation of J(Q):** In Theorem 8, the authors provide upper bounds on the penalty term J(Q). It would be helpful if they could offer more interpretation of these bounds. For example, how do they guide the choice of activation function, and why is it beneficial for J(Q) to decrease with the escort's normalization constant Z?

### Conclusion

This paper presents a set of interesting and elegant theoretical results that connect f-GANs to the rich field of information geometry. The work is original and provides a novel perspective on GAN training. However, in its current form, the paper falls short of demonstrating that these theoretical insights translate into tangible benefits for GAN practitioners. The strong underlying assumptions are not fully justified for real-world applications, and the experimental results are not strong enough to validate the practical utility of the derived architectural principles.
