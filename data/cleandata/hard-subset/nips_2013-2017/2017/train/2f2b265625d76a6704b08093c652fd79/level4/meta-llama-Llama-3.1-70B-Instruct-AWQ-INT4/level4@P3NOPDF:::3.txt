This paper explores several interesting questions related to Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), including the relationship between solving the GAN problem and convergence in parameter space, the nature of the generator's fit when convergence occurs, and the choice of output activation function for the discriminator to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function. The authors address these questions within the framework of deformed exponential distributions, yielding several theoretical results.

One of the key contributions of the paper is a variational generalization of a known theorem that relates f-divergence between distributions to Bregman divergence between the parameters of these distributions. This theorem provides an interesting connection between the information-theoretic viewpoint of measuring dissimilarity between probability distributions and the information-geometric perspective of measuring dissimilarity between the corresponding parameters. The authors also show that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their theoretical investigation suggests that careful selection of the hidden activation functions of the generator and the output activation function of the discriminator could potentially improve GANs. Furthermore, they briefly mention an alternative interpretation of the GAN game in the context of expected utility theory.

While the authors present some interesting theoretical results, I have several concerns regarding the practical relevance and usefulness of their findings in their current form. Firstly, many of the theorems and resulting insights hold for the specific case where the data and model distributions P and Q are members of the deformed exponential family. It would be beneficial for the authors to justify this assumption, elaborating on whether it is met in practice and explaining whether similar results could be derived without this assumption.

Another main contribution of the paper is the information-geometric f-GAN identity, which relates the variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. However, it is unclear what benefits this parameter-based point of view provides. The authors should explain whether it is possible to implement GANs in terms of this parameter-based optimization and what advantages this approach would offer. Experimental results comparing the two approaches to optimizing GANs would be valuable in this regard. Notably, the authors do not utilize the right-hand side of this identity, except to assert that solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space (provided the residual J(Q) is small). It would be helpful to understand why this implication is not obvious and to see a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational f-divergence formulation over distributions does not imply convergence in parameter space.

One of the practical implications of the authors' theoretical investigation is that the choice of output activation function matters for the GAN loss to have the desirable proper compositeness property. This result is particularly interesting, and it would be beneficial to see how the theoretically derived activation function compares experimentally against the heuristic choice provided in Ref. [34].

Under the assumption that the generator network is reversible, the authors show that there exists an activation function v such that the generator's hidden layers factor exactly as escorts for the deformed exponential family. However, the experimental results in Table 2 are not entirely convincing. For instance, the plot for the GAN MLP indicates that the "theoretically superior" μ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU (limiting case μ → 1). I would expect the invertible μ-ReLU (satisfying the reversibility assumption) to perform better than the non-invertible baseline (not satisfying the reversibility assumption). The authors should explain why the μ-ReLU performs worse and comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption. Additionally, it would be helpful to compare these results against the Wasserstein GAN, which is not based on f-divergences and therefore may not be subject to the authors' theoretical analysis.

Minor concerns include the need for more intuition regarding deformed exponential densities, as this framework may be unfamiliar to the general machine learning community. The authors should provide more explanation of what a deformation (or signature) and an escort are. Furthermore, in Theorem 8, the authors derive several upper bounds for J(Q). It would be beneficial to understand how these bounds compare against each other and what implications they have for choosing activation functions. The authors also mention that this upper bound on J(Q) is decreasing with the normalization parameter of the escort Z, but it is unclear why this is a good thing and how it can be leveraged.

In light of these comments, I believe that the insights gained from the theoretical analysis are not substantial enough from the point of view of a GAN practitioner. While the authors present some interesting results, the practical relevance and usefulness of their findings are limited by the assumptions made and the lack of convincing experimental results.
