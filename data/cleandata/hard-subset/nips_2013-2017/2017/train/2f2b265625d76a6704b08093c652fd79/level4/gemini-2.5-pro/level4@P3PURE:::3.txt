The authors identify several interesting questions related to Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), including the extent to which solving the GAN objective implies convergence in parameter space, what distribution the generator fits at convergence, and, from a practical architectural standpoint, how to select the discriminator's output activation to ensure the loss function is properly composite.

To address these questions, the authors employ the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions. From this framework, they derive several theoretical results:
1.  They present a variational generalization of a theorem (amenable to the f-GAN formulation) that connects an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between the parameters of those distributions. This result establishes a valuable link between the information-theoretic view of distribution dissimilarity and the information-geometric perspective of parameter dissimilarity.
2.  They show that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as "escorts" of deformed exponential distributions.
3.  Their theoretical investigation leads to practical suggestions, such as the careful selection of hidden activation functions in the generator and the output activation of the discriminator, to potentially improve GAN performance.
4.  They briefly mention an alternative interpretation of the GAN game from the perspective of expected utility theory.

Overall, while the authors present several interesting theoretical results, I have concerns regarding their practical relevance and usefulness in their current form.

**Questions and Concerns:**

A central concern is that many of the theorems and insights, such as Theorems 4 and 6, rely on the assumption that the data and model distributions (P and Q) belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors should justify this assumption. Is it met in practical scenarios? Could similar results be derived without it?

A main contribution is the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq. (7), which connects the variational f-divergence over distributions to an information-geometric optimization over parameters. What is the practical gain from this parameter-based perspective? Could GANs be implemented using this parameter-based optimization, and if so, what would be the benefits? The paper would be strengthened by an experimental comparison between the standard distributional optimization and this proposed parameter-based approach. Surprisingly, the authors seem to primarily use the right-hand side of this identity only to assert that solving the GAN game implies parameter-space convergence (if the residual J(Q) is small). Why is this implication not considered obvious? Could the authors provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the variational formulation does not imply convergence in parameter space?

The authors show that the choice of the discriminator's output activation function, g_f, is crucial for ensuring the GAN loss has the desirable property of being properly composite. This is a particularly interesting practical implication. However, the paper lacks an experimental comparison between the theoretically derived g_f (the composition of f′ and the link function) and the heuristic choice from prior work (Ref. [34]). Such an experiment would be a valuable validation of the theory.

Regarding the consequences for deep learning and the experimental results:
*   The authors show that under a reversibility assumption, a generator's hidden layers can be factored as escorts using a specific activation function, v. However, the results in Table 2 (left) for the GAN MLP seem to contradict the theory's prediction. The "theoretically superior" invertible μ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline non-invertible ReLU (the limiting case μ → 1). Why does the model that satisfies the theoretical assumption perform worse?
*   Can the authors comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption? Furthermore, how do these results compare to the Wasserstein GAN, which is not based on f-divergences and for which this theoretical analysis is not expected to hold?
*   Finally, the experimental results presented in Table 2 (center and right) for different activation and link functions appear to be within error bars of each other. This does not provide sufficient evidence that the theoretical findings are practically relevant.

**Minor Concerns:**
*   **Clarity of terminology:** The paper's framework relies heavily on deformed exponential densities. Since this may be unfamiliar to the broader machine learning community, the authors should provide more intuition for key concepts like "deformation," "signature," and "escort."
*   **Interpretation of Theorem 8:** The authors derive several upper bounds for J(Q) in Theorem 8. It is unclear how these bounds compare to one another and what their implications are for choosing activation functions. Additionally, they state that the upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort's normalization parameter Z. Could they elaborate on why this is beneficial and how this insight can be practically leveraged?

In light of these comments, I believe the insights from the theoretical analysis are not yet substantial enough to be compelling for a GAN practitioner.
