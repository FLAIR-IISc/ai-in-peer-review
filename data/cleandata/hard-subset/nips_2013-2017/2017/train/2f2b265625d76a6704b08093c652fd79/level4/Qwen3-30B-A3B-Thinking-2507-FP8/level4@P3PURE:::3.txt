

The authors identify several intriguing GAN-related questions, including the extent to which solving the GAN problem implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator actually fits upon convergence, and (most relevant from an architectural perspective) how to choose the discriminator's output activation function to ensure proper compositional properties of the loss function.  

Within the framework of deformed exponential distributions, the authors derive several theoretical results addressing these questions. They present a variational generalization (amenable to f-GAN formulation) of a known theorem linking an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between their parameters. This establishes a meaningful connection between the information-theoretic view of distribution dissimilarity and the information-geometric perspective of parameter dissimilarity. Under a reversibility assumption, the authors show that deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their analysis further suggests that careful selection of generator hidden activation functions and the discriminator's output activation function could improve GAN performance. An alternative interpretation of the GAN game within expected utility theory is also briefly mentioned.  

While the theoretical results are interesting, I have significant concerns regarding their practical relevance and utility in the current form.  

**Key concerns:**  
1. **Assumption of deformed exponential family membership:** Many theorems and insights rely on the assumption that data and model distributions \(P\) and \(Q\) belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors must justify this assumption: do these distributions typically satisfy it in practice? Can similar results (e.g., Theorems 4 & 6) be derived without this constraint?  

2. **Practical utility of the information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7):** The authors establish a connection between the variational f-divergence formulation and parameter-based optimization. However, they do not clarify what is gained from this perspective. Can GANs be implemented using this parameter-based optimization? If so, what are the concrete benefits? Crucially, the authors do not utilize the right-hand side of Eq. (7) beyond asserting that solving the GAN game implies parameter-space convergence (when residual \(J(Q)\) is small). Why is this implication non-obvious? Please provide a realistic scenario where convergence in the f-divergence formulation does not imply convergence in parameter space.  

3. **Experimental validation of output activation choice:** The authors demonstrate that the discriminator's output activation \(g_f\) (defined as the composition of \(f'\) and the link function) is critical for the loss's compositional property. This is a compelling theoretical insight, but the authors omit a direct experimental comparison between their theoretically derived \(g_f\) and the heuristic choice in Ref. [34]. Such a comparison is essential to validate the practical relevance of this finding.  

4. **Contradiction in Table 2 results:** Under the reversibility assumption, the authors claim that an activation function \(v\) exists enabling exact escort factorization for generator hidden layers. Table 2 (left) shows that for the GAN MLP, the theoretically superior \(\mu\)-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU (\(\mu \to 1\)). This contradicts the expectation that the invertible \(\mu\)-ReLU (satisfying reversibility) should outperform the non-invertible baseline. The authors must explain this discrepancy. Additionally, they should clarify whether DCGAN satisfies the reversibility assumption and how their results compare to Wasserstein GAN (which avoids f-divergences and thus falls outside the scope of their analysis). Finally, the performance differences across activation functions (Table 2, center) and link functions (Table 2, right) all lie within statistical error margins, providing insufficient evidence to support the practical relevance of their theoretical claims.  

**Minor concerns:**  
- The framework of deformed exponential densities is unfamiliar to many ML practitioners. The authors should provide intuitive explanations of "deformation," "signature," and "escort" to enhance accessibility.  
- In Theorem 8, the authors derive multiple upper bounds for \(J(Q)\). How do these bounds compare, and what are their implications for activation function selection? The claim that the bound decreases with the escort normalization parameter \(Z\) requires elaboration: why is this beneficial, and how can it be leveraged in practice?  

**Conclusion:**  
While the theoretical contributions are mathematically sound, the insights do not yet provide substantial practical value for GAN practitioners. The lack of empirical validation, unresolved contradictions in experimental results, and over-reliance on restrictive assumptions significantly limit the paper's impact. The authors must address these concerns to strengthen their case.
