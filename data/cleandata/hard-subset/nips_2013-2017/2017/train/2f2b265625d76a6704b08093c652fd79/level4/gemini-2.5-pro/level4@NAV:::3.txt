This work poses several pertinent questions within the domain of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). These include the degree to which solving the GAN optimization objective translates to convergence in the parameter space, the nature of the distribution the generator ultimately models upon convergence, and, with particular relevance to network architecture, the selection of the discriminator's output activation to guarantee the desirable property of proper compositeness for the loss function.

To address these questions, the authors employ an information-theoretic framework based on deformed exponential distributions, leading to several theoretical contributions: First, they introduce a variational generalization of a known theorem, compatible with the f-GAN framework, which establishes a link between the f-divergence of two distributions and the Bregman divergence of their corresponding parameters. This result forges a compelling connection between the information-theoretic perspective (measuring dissimilarity between distributions) and the information-geometric perspective (measuring dissimilarity between parameters). Second, they demonstrate that, assuming reversibility, deep generative networks can be factored as "escorts" of deformed exponential distributions. Furthermore, their theoretical analysis suggests that meticulous selection of the generator's hidden activations and the discriminator's output activation could lead to performance improvements in GANs. The paper also briefly touches upon an alternative framing of the GAN objective through the lens of expected utility theory.

In summary, while the paper presents a set of intriguing theoretical findings, I have several reservations concerning their practical applicability and utility in their current form.

**Questions & Concerns:**

*   A foundational concern is that many of the core theorems and insights are predicated on the assumption that both the data (P) and model (Q) distributions belong to the deformed exponential family. The authors should provide a justification for this assumption, clarifying its validity in practical scenarios. Furthermore, could analogous results (e.g., Theorems 4 & 6) be obtained if this assumption were relaxed?

*   The paper's information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7) connects the standard variational f-divergence formulation (over distributions) with an information-geometric optimization problem (over parameters). What is the practical advantage of adopting this parameter-centric perspective? Could GAN training be implemented using this parameter-based optimization, and if so, what benefits would it offer? An experimental comparison between these two optimization paradigms would have been highly valuable. It is also notable that the authors seem to leverage the right-hand side of this identity primarily to claim that solving the GAN objective implies parameter space convergence (assuming a small residual J(Q)). Why is this implication not considered trivial? The authors should provide a realistic example where convergence in the distributional f-divergence sense does not lead to convergence in the parameter space.

*   A practical implication of the theory is that the choice of the discriminator's output activation function, gf (as detailed in section 2.4 of Ref. [34]), is crucial for achieving the proper compositeness property in the loss. This is a particularly interesting finding. It would have been compelling to see an experimental comparison of the theoretically-derived gf (as a composition of f′ and the link function) against the heuristic choice proposed in reference [34].

*   Regarding the deep learning implications, the authors demonstrate that under a reversibility assumption, an activation function v exists that allows the generator to be factored as escorts. However, the experimental results appear to contradict the theoretical motivation. For instance, in Table 2 (left), the supposedly "theoretically superior" μ-ReLU activation performs worse than the baseline ReLU (which corresponds to the limiting case μ → 1). Given that the invertible μ-ReLU satisfies the reversibility assumption while the standard ReLU does not, this outcome is counterintuitive. Can the authors provide an explanation for this discrepancy? Additionally, does the DCGAN architecture adhere to the reversibility assumption? How do these findings relate to architectures like the Wasserstein GAN, for which the f-divergence framework is not directly applicable? Finally, the reported results comparing different activation functions (Table 2, center) and link functions (Table 2, right) seem to fall within the margin of error. As such, the experiments do not provide convincing evidence for the practical relevance of the theoretical claims.

**Minor Concerns:**

*   The work is heavily reliant on the framework of deformed exponential densities. Since this may be unfamiliar to a broad machine learning audience, it would be beneficial for the authors to provide more intuitive explanations for key concepts such as "deformation," "signature," and "escort."

*   Theorem 8 introduces several upper bounds on the residual term J(Q). A comparison of these bounds is needed, along with a discussion of their practical implications for selecting activation functions. The authors also state that this upper bound decreases with the escort's normalization parameter Z; they should elaborate on why this is a desirable property and how it can be exploited in practice.

Considering these points, I find that the practical insights offered by the theoretical analysis, from the perspective of a GAN practitioner, are not yet substantial enough.
