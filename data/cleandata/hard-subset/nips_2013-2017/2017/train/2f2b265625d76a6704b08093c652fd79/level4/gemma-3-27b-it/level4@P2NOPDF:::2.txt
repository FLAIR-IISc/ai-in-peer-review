This paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful information geometric (IG) perspective on the f-GAN algorithm. The authors establish convergence of f-GAN in parameter space by leveraging the bijective mapping between f-divergences and chi-divergences, alongside a result concerning Bregman divergences. They also address practical implementation details and provide a factorization result for deep neural network representations, linking this to the choice of activation functions through their relationship to chi (or f) functions. While I haven’t verified every detail in the appendix, the proofs appear sound, with the exception of Theorem 8, which I was unable to examine closely due to time constraints.

Overall, I believe this paper contains a significant number of novel results with potential implications for both machine learning and information theory. I was particularly impressed by the clear exposition of Theorem 4, which elegantly presents the IG foundation of f-GAN. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.

However, the paper’s density and technical nature may present a significant barrier to readers less familiar with the connections between information theory (IT) and information geometry (IG). Many deep learning practitioners, a key audience for GAN research, may lack this background, particularly regarding concepts like the relationship between KL divergence for exponential families and Bregman divergences, and the basics of Fenchel duality. To enhance accessibility, I suggest the following revisions:

1.  The diagram in Appendix Figure 1, illustrating the connections between IT and IG within the context of f-GAN, should be moved to the main text. This visual aid would greatly benefit readers.

2.  The motivation for adopting an IG perspective should be stated more explicitly at the beginning of the paper. The core benefit, as I understand it, is leveraging the geometry of the parameter space to analyze optimization behavior. While this is briefly mentioned on page 7, it feels too late in the presentation.

3.  The connection between Section 5 and the f-GAN framework is unclear. It appears to apply the deformed exponential family to describe the distributions representable by a deep neural network, but this doesn’t seem to directly *follow* from the f-GAN optimization results. While the 1-1 correspondence between v, f, and chi is acknowledged, the paper doesn't articulate how this insight informs f-GAN design – for example, suggesting which f-divergence to choose, or how to select an appropriate f-GAN objective given a particular activation function to optimize convergence.

4.  The claim that phi_l in Theorem 6 represents "deep sufficient statistics" requires further justification. Equation (13) does not immediately appear to conform to the structure of a deformed exponential family.

5.  Consider relocating lines 257-269 to a more relevant section. The discussion of utility theory feels somewhat tangential to the core IG perspective and could potentially be moved to the appendix to create space for clarifying the primary results.

6.  The experiments feel weakly connected to the central claims of the paper. For example, the applications described in (A) – utilizing the new activation functions – can be justified solely by the results in Section 5, without requiring the IG perspective on f-GAN. Similarly, (B) relies primarily on Section 4, again with limited connection to the broader IG view. The inclusion of WGAN results could be distracting and confusing, given the paper’s focus on f-GANs; I had to search specifically for the brief mention of WGAN on lines 227-228.

In summary, this paper presents valuable and intricate results, but the material feels somewhat disjointed. It reads as a collection of findings from multiple studies compressed into a single NIPS submission. While I support acceptance, I believe the paper would significantly benefit from editing to improve clarity, coherence, and streamline the presentation of its core arguments.
