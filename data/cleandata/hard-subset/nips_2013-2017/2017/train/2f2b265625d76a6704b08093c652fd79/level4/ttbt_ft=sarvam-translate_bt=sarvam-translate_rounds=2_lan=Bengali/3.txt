The authors have identified several interesting GAN-related questions, such as whether a GAN problem leads to convergence in the parameter space, what the generator actually fits if convergence occurs, and how the output activation function of the discriminator should be chosen to ensure the correct construction of the loss function (perhaps most relevant from a network architecture perspective).

The authors aim to answer these questions within the framework of the distorted exponential distribution (information theory), from which they have derived the following theoretical results, among others:

They presented a variation of a known theorem (suitable for the f-GAN formulation) that relates an f-divergence in the distribution to a corresponding Bregman divergence in the parameters of this type of distribution. This theorem establishes an interesting connection between the information-theoretical perspective of measuring the discrepancy in probability distributions and the information-geometric perspective of measuring the discrepancy in the corresponding parameters. They showed that under the assumption of reversibility, deep generative networks act like an escort for the distorted exponential distribution. Their theoretical investigation further suggests that a careful choice of the generator's hidden activation functions and the correct selection of the discriminator's output activation function can potentially help improve GANs. They also briefly mentioned an alternative explanation of the GAN game in the context of expected utility theory.

Overall, I believe that the authors have presented some interesting theoretical results, but I have several concerns about the practical relevance and usefulness of their results in their current form.

Questions and Concerns:

Firstly, many of their theorems and resulting insights are only applicable in specific cases where the data and model distribution are members of the distorted exponential family P and Q. Can the authors support this assumption, that is, explain in detail whether it has actually been fulfilled, and explain whether similar results (such as theorems 4 and 6) can be obtained without this assumption?

One of the author's main contributions, the information-geometric f-GAN identity in the equation (7), relates the well-known variable f-divergence formulation of the distribution to an information-geometric optimization problem on the parameters. Can the authors explain what we get from this parameter-based perspective? Is it possible to implement GANs in terms of this parameter-based optimization, and what are its advantages? I would really like to see experimental results comparing two methods of optimizing GANs. Surprisingly, the authors do not use the right-hand side of this identity, only mentioning that a GAN game leads to convergence in the parameter space if the residual J(Q) is small. And why is this underlying meaning not clear? Can the authors provide a realistic scenario where the variable f-divergence formulation on the distribution does not imply convergence in the parameter space?

As an interesting practical implication of their theoretical investigation, the authors showed that the choice of the output activation function GF (see section 2.4 of reference [34]) is important for the desired correct construction of the GAN loss. I found this result particularly interesting and would certainly like to see how their theoretically derived GF (as a combination of the f′ and link function) experimentally compares with the heuristic choice given in reference [34].

Consequences for Deep Learning and Experimental Results: Under the assumption that the generator network is invertible, the authors showed that there is an activation function v such that the hidden layers of the generator act like an escort for the distorted exponential family. In Table 2 (left), the authors compared several generator architectures against different choices of activation functions (which may or may not correspond to this escort factorization). For example, the plot of the GAN MLP indicates that the "theoretically improved" μ- ReLU actually performs worse than the baseline ReLU (μ → 1 in the limiting case). From their analysis, I would certainly expect that the invertible μ- ReLU (which satisfies the reversibility assumption) would perform better than the non-invertible baseline (which does not satisfy the reversibility assumption). Can the authors explain why μ- ReLU performs poorly? Do they satisfy the DCGAN architecture reversibility assumption, and how do these results compare with the Wasserstein GAN, which is not based on f-divergence (and for which I do not expect their theoretical analysis to be correct)? Finally, as far as I can tell, their results include error bars on whether an activation (Table 2 center) or link function (Table 2 right) performs better than the others. I do not think this provides enough support for their theoretical results that are relevant in practice.

Small Concerns:

Many of their results arise from the structure of the distorted exponential density. Since the general ML community may not yet be familiar with it, I would welcome if the authors could provide more insight into what the distortion (or signature) and escort are.

In Theorem 8, the authors have created several upper bounds for J(Q). What are the implications for how these bounds compare with each other and what activation function should be used? They also mention that the normalization of these upper bounds of J(Q) with the parameter Z of the escort decreases. Can they explain a little more why this is good and how we can use it?

In light of these comments, I believe that the insights derived from the theoretical analysis are not sufficiently important from the GAN practitioner's point of view.
