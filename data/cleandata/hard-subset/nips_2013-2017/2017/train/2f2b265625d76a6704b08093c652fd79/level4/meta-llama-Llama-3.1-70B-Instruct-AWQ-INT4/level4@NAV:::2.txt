I appreciate the opportunity to review this paper, which presents an intriguing information geometric (IG) perspective on the f-GAN algorithm.

The authors demonstrate that f-GAN converges in parameter space by leveraging the 1-1 mapping between f-divergence and chi-divergence, as well as a Bregman divergence result. Furthermore, they discuss a suitable implementation of f-GAN and derive a factorization result for the deep neural network representation, highlighting the 1-1 mapping between the choice of activation functions and the chi (or f) function. While I did not thoroughly verify every detail in the appendix, the proofs appear to be correct, with the exception of Theorem 8, which I did not have time to review prior to submission.

This paper is dense and rich in new results that could be of significant interest to the machine learning and information theory communities. The exposition of Theorem 4, in particular, effectively communicates the IG aspect of the story for f-GAN. As a result, I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

However, I also believe that this paper may be challenging for readers without a strong background in the connections between information theory (IT) and information geometry (IG), particularly those familiar with the simplest mappings, such as KL for an exponential family, and Bregman divergence. As the primary audience for a GAN paper consists of deep learning engineers, I suggest the following revisions to enhance clarity:

1. Consider relocating Figure 1 from the appendix to the main text, as it effectively illustrates the connections between IT and IG in the context of f-GAN.
2. Provide a clear statement at the beginning of the paper explaining the benefits of considering the IG view. My understanding is that this perspective enables the use of geometry in the parameter space to discuss optimization behavior, which is briefly mentioned on page 7, but could be introduced earlier.
3. Clarify the connection between Section 5 and f-GAN. While the deformed exponential family is used to explain the distribution that a deep neural network can represent, it appears to be unrelated to the f-GAN optimization results. Although the 1-1 correspondence between v, f, and chi is established, it is unclear how this result can inform the design of the f-GAN game, such as selecting an f-divergence or determining the optimal f-GAN objective given an activation function.
4. Explain why phi_l can be viewed as "deep sufficient statistics" in Theorem 6, as equation (13) does not appear to be in the form of a deformed exponential family.
5. Consider relocating lines 257-269 to a different section or removing the utility theory part, as it seems unrelated to the IG view and could be relegated to the appendix to free up space for explaining the main results.
6. The experiments appear to be tangential to the primary claims made in the paper. For example, the results from Section 5 can be discussed independently of the IG view of f-GAN, and the WGAN results may be distracting and confusing, as they are not directly related to the main topic of f-GANs.

In summary, while this paper presents valuable results and derivations, the material could be organized more clearly. The paper appears to be a condensed version of multiple papers, which may obscure the main claims and coherence. Although I support acceptance, I recommend editing the paper to clarify the claims and improve their coherence.
