Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies GANs through the lens of information geometry. Building on Nowozin et al.’s f-GAN framework [30], the authors develop a variational identity that links f-divergences between distributions to Bregman divergences between parameters when the data and model lie in deformed (χ-)exponential families. Concretely, they introduce KLχ divergences and prove a “variational information-geometric” identity equating a variational f-GAN objective to a Bregman divergence between natural parameters plus a penalty term J(Q). This generalizes the well-known KL/exponential-family result that KL(P‖Q) equals a Bregman divergence in parameter space. They further analyze deep generators and show that, under a reversibility assumption, a deep network can factorize the generator density as an escort of products of χ-exponential families; this yields sufficient conditions on generator activations (e.g., ELU, Softplus; ReLU in a limiting sense) to realize such factorization. On the discriminator side, they clarify that the “output activation” (the gf in [30]) is not arbitrary if one desires a proper composite loss; they connect gf to the composition of f′ with a link function, and derive an explicit proper composite loss that aligns with their information-geometric identity. The experiments explore (i) generator activations and (ii) discriminator link functions on MNIST and LSUN, with DCGAN and MLP backbones, comparing standard and alternative choices.

Quality
The theoretical developments appear careful and technically sound, with proofs deferred to the supplement. The extension from KL/exponential families to f-divergences/deformed exponential families via escorts is elegant, and the variational identity is a noteworthy synthesis of information-theoretic and information-geometric views. However, several central results hinge on strong assumptions: (a) P and Q belonging to deformed exponential families; (b) network reversibility to obtain closed-form generator densities; and (c) mild boundedness conditions needed to equate f-divergences with KLχ. The paper’s empirical section does not yet convincingly demonstrate practical benefits of the theory; performance differences are small and often within error bars, and there is no direct comparison of optimizing the variational distributional form versus the parameter-space objective suggested by the identity.

Clarity
The paper is dense but mostly well-organized. Given that deformed exponential families and escorts are not widely familiar in the ML community, additional intuition and simple examples would help. The role of the normalization Z in escorts and its effect on J(Q) could be explained more clearly. The treatment of the discriminator’s output activation as a proper composite link is insightful; a more explicit mapping from f to gf and Ψ, with concrete examples beyond the sigmoid, would improve readability.

Originality
The paper offers a novel bridge between f-GAN variational training and parameter-space optimization for a broad class of families (deformed exponentials), extending classical KL/Bregman identities. The factorization result relating deep networks and escorts via activation design, and the proper composite loss perspective on gf, are original and interesting.

Significance
The theoretical insights could influence how we think about GAN objectives and architecture choices. Yet the practical impact remains unclear: the deformed-exponential-family assumption and reversibility are restrictive in practice, and the experiments provide limited evidence of measurable gains. Without stronger empirical validation or concrete algorithms that leverage the parameter-space identity, the contribution risks being viewed as primarily conceptual.

Strengths
- Elegant variational identity linking f-divergences and Bregman divergences for deformed exponential families, generalizing classical results.
- Clear conceptual connection between generator activations and escort factorization; sufficient conditions covering common activations.
- Clarification of the discriminator’s output activation via proper composite losses and explicit links Ψ.
- Potential to inform more principled GAN architecture and loss design.

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on the deformed exponential family and reversibility assumptions, with limited justification for real-world GANs.
- Lack of empirical comparison between distributional optimization and the parameter-space objective implied by the identity.
- Experimental gains are small and often within error bars; some results contradict expectations from the theory (e.g., μ-ReLU vs ReLU).
- Limited intuition and practical guidance about escorts, signatures, and the behavior of J(Q).

Questions and requests for clarification
- Assumption scope: To what extent do real-world P and Q (as learned by standard generators) lie in deformed exponential families? Can the authors provide diagnostics, examples, or robustness results when this assumption is violated? Are Theorems 4 and 6 (and the key identity) adaptable without this assumption?
- Parameter-space optimization: Beyond showing that the variational and geometric views coincide, what do we gain practically from the parameter-based objective? Can one implement training directly in parameter space (e.g., minimizing the Bregman term plus J(Q)) and does it help optimization or stability? A comparison would strengthen the paper.
- Convergence: The identity suggests that minimizing the variational objective implies parameter convergence (modulo J(Q)). Why is the converse not obvious, and can the authors provide a realistic example where variational convergence (in distribution) does not imply parameter convergence?
- Discriminator link: Please compare the theoretically derived gf (composition of f′ and Ψ) against the heuristic choice in [30], both qualitatively and quantitatively. This seems one of the most actionable implications.
- Reversibility and activations: DCGAN is claimed to be invertible under certain choices. Do the experimental DCGANs satisfy these conditions (e.g., strictly invertible convolutions, activation functions)? How do the observations change when the reversibility assumption is enforced or violated? Why does μ-ReLU sometimes underperform ReLU if μ-ReLU better matches the theory?
- Applicability beyond f-GAN: What should we expect for Wasserstein GANs, where the objective is not an f-divergence? Are any of the activation/link insights expected to transfer?
- J(Q) bounds and Z: Theorem 8 gives upper bounds for J(Q). How do these bounds compare numerically, and what do they recommend regarding activation choices? Why does decreasing J(Q) with larger escort normalization Z help in practice, and can this be tuned?

Pros for acceptance
- Theoretical novelty and unification of f-divergence variational training with parameter-space geometry.
- Fresh perspective on the role of activations (generator) and links (discriminator) grounded in proper composite losses and escorts.
- Potential to inspire new, better-motivated GAN designs.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical assumptions (deformed exponential family, reversibility) may rarely hold and are not well validated.
- Limited and inconclusive experiments; no direct empirical test of the parameter-space objective.
- Insufficient intuition and guidance for practitioners on when and how to apply the theory.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper offers elegant and potentially impactful theory, but the current empirical support and practical validation are insufficient for NIPS. Strengthening the experimental section (especially testing the parameter-space objective and the derived gf/Ψ choices), providing clearer intuition for deformed exponential families and escorts, and discussing robustness to assumption violations would considerably improve the submission.
