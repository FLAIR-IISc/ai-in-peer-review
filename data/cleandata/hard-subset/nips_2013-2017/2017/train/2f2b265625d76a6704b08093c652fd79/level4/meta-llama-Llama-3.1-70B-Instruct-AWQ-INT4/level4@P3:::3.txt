The paper "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell" presents a theoretical analysis of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) within the framework of deformed exponential distributions. The authors derive several interesting results, including a variational generalization of a known theorem that relates an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between the parameters of such distributions. They also show that under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Furthermore, their theoretical investigation suggests that a careful choice of the hidden activation functions of the generator as well as a proper selection of the output activation function of the discriminator could potentially help to further improve GANs.

The authors' main contribution is the information-geometric f-GAN identity, which relates the well-known variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. This identity provides a new perspective on the GAN game, highlighting the connection between the information-theoretic view point of measuring dissimilarity between probability distributions and the information-geometric perspective of measuring dissimilarity between the corresponding parameters.

However, the practical relevance and usefulness of the authors' results in their present form are questionable. Many of their theorems and resulting insights hold for the specific case that the data and model distributions P and Q are members of the deformed exponential family. It is unclear whether this assumption is met in practice, and whether similar results could be derived without this assumption.

The authors' experimental results are also not entirely convincing. While they show that the choice of the output activation function gf matters in order for the GAN loss to have the desirable proper compositeness property, their comparison of different activation functions and link functions does not provide sufficient support for their theoretical results to be relevant in practice. In particular, the results on whether one activation or link function performs better than the others are all within error bars.

In conclusion, while the authors present some interesting theoretical results, their practical relevance and usefulness are limited by the assumptions made and the lack of convincing experimental results. To strengthen their contribution, the authors should provide more intuition about the deformed exponential densities, clarify the implications of their results for GAN practitioners, and provide more comprehensive experimental results that demonstrate the benefits of their theoretical analysis.
