This paper studies GANs through the lens of deformed exponential families and information geometry. The authors pose several fundamental questions (e.g., when and how convergence in the GAN objective implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator fits at convergence, and how to choose the discriminator’s output activation to ensure proper compositeness of the loss), and provide a suite of theoretical results:

- A variational generalization (compatible with f-GAN) that relates an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between parameters, thereby connecting information-theoretic and information-geometric views.
- Under a reversibility assumption, a factorization of deep generative networks as escorts of deformed exponential distributions.
- Architectural implications for the generator’s hidden activations and the discriminator’s output activation (via proper composite losses), plus an alternative view of the GAN game through expected utility.

I find several of these connections insightful and potentially impactful. However, I have significant concerns about the practical relevance and empirical support of the claims in their current form.

Major questions and concerns:

1) Scope and assumptions. Many results rely on P and Q belonging to the deformed exponential family. Please justify when this assumption is met in practical deep generative modeling. Are common generator-induced distributions (after pushing forward simple priors through neural nets) well-approximated in this family? Can Theorems 4 and 6, or their qualitative consequences, be extended beyond this assumption, or at least stated with clear approximation guarantees?

2) Information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7). The identity links the standard variational f-divergence optimization over distributions to an optimization over parameters via a Bregman divergence (plus a residual J(Q)). What do we gain algorithmically or statistically from this parameter-space view? Could one implement a GAN that directly minimizes the parameter-space objective (e.g., by estimating or controlling J(Q))? Please provide experiments comparing standard f-GAN training to a parameter-based procedure suggested by the right-hand side of Eq. 7. The paper appears to use the identity mainly to assert that solving the GAN game implies parameter convergence when J(Q) is small. Why is that implication nontrivial? Provide a realistic scenario (e.g., non-identifiability, non-invertible maps, model misspecification) where convergence in the distributional f-divergence does not imply convergence in parameter space, and show how your residual term clarifies the gap.

3) Discriminator output activation and proper compositeness. The result that the discriminator’s output activation gf should be the composition of f′ with the link function to ensure proper composite losses is practically appealing. Please add experiments that directly compare this theoretically derived gf to the heuristic choice in Ref. [34], across multiple datasets, architectures, and f-divergences, with calibration and stability metrics (e.g., variance of gradients, discriminator overfitting) to support the claim.

4) Reversibility, escorts, and empirical findings. Under reversibility, the generator’s hidden layers factor as escorts. In Table 2 (left), μ-ReLU (invertible, thus matching the reversibility assumption) performs worse than ReLU (non-invertible). This is at odds with the theory’s suggestion. Please explain this discrepancy. Possibilities include: optimization issues (conditioning, initialization, step sizes), mismatch between the assumed deformation and the network’s effective distributional family, numerical stability or saturation of μ-ReLU, or stronger implicit regularization from non-invertibility. Also clarify whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies reversibility in practice (e.g., presence of downsampling/upsampling, zeroing nonlinearities, and non-bijective layers), and how this affects the escort factorization prediction. Finally, how do your conclusions relate to Wasserstein GANs, which are not f-divergence-based? A comparative study would delineate the scope of your theory.

5) Statistical significance and power. In Table 2 (center/right), differences among activations and link functions appear to fall within error bars. This weakens the practical case for the proposed choices. Please provide larger-scale experiments, more runs, confidence intervals, and tests of effect size to establish whether any improvements are robust and meaningful.

6) Theorem 8: bounds on J(Q). You provide several upper bounds on J(Q). How do these compare in tightness under realistic training conditions? What concrete guidance do they give for choosing activations and links? You note that the bound decreases with the escort normalization Z; please elaborate on the mechanism, why this is desirable (e.g., tighter parameter–distribution alignment), and how to leverage it in practice (e.g., through architectural constraints, normalization, or temperature-like parameters).

Minor requests for clarity:

- Many results are framed in terms of deformed exponential densities. Please add intuitive explanations and simple examples of deformations (signatures) and escorts, and relate them to well-known families or to common neural network activations. A short primer would make the paper accessible to a broader ML audience.
- Provide empirical diagnostics that connect the theory to practice: track parameter-space Bregman distances during training; estimate or bound J(Q) (even approximately) to validate its relevance; test sensitivity to departures from reversibility (e.g., controlled injectivity/bijectivity ablations); and probe whether the generator’s layers empirically behave like escorts (e.g., via Jacobian spectra or invertibility checks).

Overall evaluation:

The paper offers elegant theoretical connections between f-divergences, Bregman divergences, and the structure of deep generative models, with intriguing architectural implications. However, the current empirical evidence does not convincingly demonstrate practical benefits, and key assumptions and mechanisms require further justification and validation. Strengthening the empirical section (particularly around Eq. 7’s parameter-space optimization, discriminator output activations tailored by f, and the reversibility/escort predictions), clarifying the generality beyond deformed exponential families, and providing intuitive exposition would substantially improve the paper’s impact for GAN practitioners.
