- Paper asks foundational GAN questions: does solving the GAN game imply parameter convergence, what does the generator fit at convergence, and how should the discriminator output activation be chosen.

- Framework: uses deformed exponential distributions and derives a variational generalization linking f-divergences to Bregman divergences between parameters.

- Provides a bridge between distribution-space (information-theoretic) and parameter-space (information-geometric) views.

- Under a reversibility assumption, shows deep generators factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions and suggests specific hidden/discriminator activations.

- Briefly connects the GAN game to expected utility theory.

- Overall: interesting theoretical contributions but concerns about practical relevance.

- Main modeling concern: many results assume P and Q lie in the deformed exponential family—need justification of this assumption or weaker conditions.

- Key identity (Eq. 7) links variational f-divergence to a parameter-space objective—reviewer asks what practical gains this perspective yields (stability, sample efficiency, diagnostics) and requests experiments optimizing the parameter-side objective.

- Request for concrete examples where convergence in variational f-divergence does not imply parameter convergence; questions why the implication is nontrivial in practice.

- Practical implication: discriminator output activation g_f is theoretically derived—reviewer asks for empirical comparison between the derived g_f and prior heuristics on standard benchmarks.

- Empirical concern: μ-ReLU (theoretically motivated) underperforms standard ReLU in experiments; requests clarification on confounders (optimization, hyperparameters, regularization) and whether DCGAN meets reversibility.

- Scope concern: how do results apply to WGANs that do not optimize an f-divergence?

- Statistical robustness: many reported comparisons lie within error bars, weakening empirical support—request more robust statistics and variance estimates.

- Residual J(Q) is central to bridging spaces—ask how J(Q) behaves in practice, how to estimate/control it, and whether it correlates with FID/IS/precision/recall.

- Minor requests: add intuitive, self-contained explanations of “deformation/signature” and “escort” with simple examples for accessibility.

- Minor requests: compare tightness of Theorem 8 bounds on J(Q), explain the role of escort normalization Z, how Z interacts with network design, and whether it can be tuned.

- Minor requests: clarify conditions for reversibility in common architectures (pooling, normalization, dropout) and propose practical workarounds if full reversibility is infeasible.

- Minor requests: provide reproducibility details—training protocols, hyperparameter sweeps, and variance across random seeds for activation/link-function experiments.

- Conclusion: theory draws intriguing links between f-divergences and parameter geometry and proposes principled activation choices, but empirical evidence is limited and core assumptions need practical justification; stronger experiments and implementation guidance required.
