The paper raises several foundational questions about GANs: to what extent solving the GAN game implies convergence in parameter space; what the generator is actually fitting at convergence; and, from an architectural perspective, how to choose the discriminator’s output activation to ensure proper compositeness of the loss.

Within an information-theoretic framework based on deformed exponential distributions, the authors derive several results. They provide a variational generalization (compatible with the f-GAN framework) of a known theorem linking an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between the parameters of those distributions. This offers a useful bridge between the information-theoretic view (dissimilarity between distributions) and the information-geometric view (dissimilarity between parameters). Under a reversibility assumption, they further show that deep generators factor as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their analysis suggests that carefully choosing generator hidden activations and the discriminator’s output activation can improve GANs. They also briefly connect the GAN game to expected utility theory.

Overall, these are interesting theoretical contributions. However, I have concerns about their practical relevance in the current form.

Questions and concerns:
- Many results rely on P and Q belonging to the deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption in realistic settings? Are common data/model pairs well approximated by this family? Can similar results (e.g., Theorems 4 and 6) be established without it, or under weaker conditions?
- A central contribution, the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq. (7), relates the variational f-divergence over distributions to an information-geometric optimization over parameters. What is gained by this parameter-space perspective in practice? Can GANs be implemented to optimize the parameter-side objective directly, and what advantages would that yield (e.g., stability, sample efficiency, convergence diagnostics)? I would have expected experiments comparing distribution-space vs parameter-space optimization. Moreover, beyond asserting that solving the GAN game implies parameter convergence when the residual J(Q) is small, the right-hand side appears underused. Why is this implication nontrivial? Can the authors provide a realistic scenario where convergence of the variational f-divergence does not imply convergence in parameter space?
- A valuable practical implication is the role of the discriminator’s output activation g_f (see Sec. 2.4 of Ref. [34]) in ensuring proper compositeness of the GAN loss. It would be compelling to compare the theoretically derived g_f (via f′ composed with the link function) against the heuristic in Ref. [34] on standard benchmarks, to quantify the benefit of theory-guided choices.
- Consequences for deep learning and experiments: Assuming generator reversibility, the authors show the existence of an activation v such that hidden layers factor as escorts for the deformed exponential family. In Table 2 (left), however, the “theoretically superior” μ-ReLU underperforms standard ReLU (the μ → 1 limit) in the GAN MLP. If invertibility and alignment with the escort factorization are advantageous, why does μ-ReLU do worse? Please clarify potential confounders (e.g., optimization difficulty, hyperparameter sensitivity, regularization effects). Does the DCGAN architecture meet the reversibility assumption? How should we interpret these results for WGANs, which do not optimize an f-divergence (and thus may fall outside the scope of the theory)? Finally, many comparisons in Table 2 (center/right) appear within error bars, which weakens the empirical support for the theoretical prescriptions.
- The residual J(Q) plays a key role in bridging distribution-space and parameter-space convergence. Can the authors comment on how J(Q) behaves in practical training regimes, how it can be estimated or controlled, and whether it correlates with commonly used diagnostics (FID, IS, precision/recall)?

Minor comments:
- Much of the analysis is framed in terms of deformed exponential densities. Since these concepts are not yet standard in the broader ML community, additional intuition would help. For example, a short, self-contained explanation of “deformation/signature” and “escort” with simple examples would improve accessibility.
- In Theorem 8, several upper bounds for J(Q) are provided. How do these bounds compare in tightness across typical regimes, and what guidance do they offer for choosing activation functions? The manuscript notes that the bound decreases with the escort normalization parameter Z; please elaborate on why this is beneficial, how Z interacts with network design, and whether Z can be tuned or indirectly controlled during training.
- Clarify the precise conditions under which the reversibility assumption holds for common architectures (e.g., presence of noninvertible layers like pooling, normalization, or dropout), and discuss practical workarounds if strict reversibility is not feasible.
- For reproducibility and fair comparison, please detail training protocols, hyperparameter sweeps, and variance across random seeds for the activation/link-function experiments.

Conclusion:
The paper offers intriguing theoretical links between f-divergences and parameter-space geometry, and highlights principled choices for activations and discriminator outputs. However, the current empirical evidence is limited, the key modeling assumption (membership in the deformed exponential family) lacks practical justification, and the parameter-space perspective is not sufficiently validated experimentally. Strengthening the empirical comparisons (especially for g_f and activation choices), clarifying the scope and necessity of the assumptions, and providing concrete implementation guidance would substantially increase the practical impact for GAN practitioners.
