The authors have identified several interesting GAN-related questions, such as whether convergence occurs in the parameter space when a GAN problem is solved, what the generator actually fits when convergence occurs, and (perhaps most relevant from a network architecture perspective) how the output activation function of the discriminator should be chosen to ensure the correct composition of the loss function.

The authors aim to answer these questions within the framework of a deformed exponential distribution (information theoretic), from which they derived the following theoretical results, among others:

They presented a variational generalization of a known theorem (suitable for the f-GAN formulation) that relates an f-divergence within the distribution to a corresponding Bregman divergence within the parameters of such a distribution. This theorem establishes an interesting connection between the information-theoretical perspective of measuring the discrepancy within the probability distribution and the information-geometric perspective of measuring the discrepancy within the corresponding parameters. They showed that under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks act as an escort for the deformed exponential distribution. Their theoretical investigation further suggests that a careful choice of the generator's hidden activation functions and the correct selection of the discriminator's output activation function can potentially help improve GANs. They also briefly mentioned an alternative explanation of the GAN game in the context of expected utility theory.

Overall, I believe the authors have presented some interesting theoretical results, but I have several concerns about the practical relevance and usefulness of their results in their current form.

Questions and Concerns:

Firstly, many of their theorems and resulting insights are applicable only to specific cases where the data and model distributions P and Q are members of the deformed exponential family. Can the authors support this assumption, i.e., explain in detail whether it is actually fulfilled, and explain whether similar results (such as theorems 4 and 6) can be obtained without this assumption?

One of the author's main contributions, the information-geometric f-GAN identity in equation (7), relates the well-known variational f-divergence formulation of the distribution to an information-geometric optimization problem on the parameters. Can the authors explain what we get from this parameter-based perspective? Is it possible to implement GANs in terms of this parameter-based optimization, and what are its advantages? I would really like to see experimental results comparing the two methods of optimizing GANs. Surprisingly, the authors do not use the right-hand side of this identity, only stating that convergence occurs in the parameter space when a GAN game is solved (if the residual J(Q) is small). And why is this underlying meaning not clear? Can the authors provide a realistic scenario where the variational f-divergence formulation on the distribution does not imply convergence in the parameter space?

As an interesting practical implication of their theoretical investigation, the authors showed that the choice of the output activation function gf (see section 2.4 of reference [34]) is important for the GAN loss to have the desired correct composition property. I found this result particularly interesting and would certainly have liked to see how their theoretically derived gf (as a combination of f′ and the link function) compared experimentally to the heuristic choice given in reference [34].

Consequences for Deep Learning and Experimental Results: Under the assumption that the generator network is reversible, the authors showed that there is an activation function v such that the hidden layers of the generator act as an escort for the deformed exponential family. In table 2 (left), the authors compared several generator architectures against different choices of activation functions (which may or may not correspond to this escort factorization). For example, the plot of the GAN MLP indicates that the "theoretically improved" μ-ReLU actually performs worse than the baseline ReLU (for the limiting case μ → 1). From their analysis, I would certainly expect the invertible μ-ReLU (which satisfies the reversibility assumption) to perform better than the non-invertible baseline (which does not satisfy the reversibility assumption). Can the authors explain why μ-ReLU performs poorly? Do they satisfy the DCGAN architecture reversibility assumption, and how do these results compare to the Wasserstein GAN, which is not based on f-divergence (and for which I do not expect their theoretical analysis to be correct)? Finally, as far as I can tell, their results are within the error bars on whether an activation (table 2 center) or link function (table 2 right) performs better than the others. I do not think this provides enough support for their theoretical results to be relevant in the practical field.

Minor Concerns:

Many of their results arise from within the framework of deformed exponential density. Since the general ML community may not yet be familiar with it, I would welcome it if the authors could provide more insight into what the distortion (or signature) and escort are.

In theorem 8, the authors have created several upper bounds for J(Q). How do these bounds compare to each other, and what are the implications for what activation function should be used? They also mention that the normalization of these upper bounds of J(Q) decreases with the parameter Z of the escort. Can they explain a little more about why this is good and how we can use it?

In light of these comments, I believe the insights derived from the theoretical analysis are not sufficiently important from the GAN practitioner's point of view.
