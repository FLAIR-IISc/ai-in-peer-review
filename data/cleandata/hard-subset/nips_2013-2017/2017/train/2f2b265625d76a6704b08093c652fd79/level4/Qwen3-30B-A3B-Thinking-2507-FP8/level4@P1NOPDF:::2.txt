

This paper presents an interesting contribution by introducing an information geometric (IG) perspective on the f-GAN algorithm. The authors demonstrate that f-GAN converges in parameter space through a one-to-one correspondence between f-divergence and chi-squared divergence, supported by a Bregman divergence result. They further develop a practical implementation framework for f-GAN and provide a factorization result for deep neural network representations, linking activation functions to the chi (or f) function via a one-to-one correspondence. While I have not verified every detail in the appendix, the proofs (excluding Theorem 8, which I was unable to verify due to time constraints) appear correct.  

The paper is dense with novel results that would interest researchers in machine learning and information theory. I particularly appreciate the exposition of Theorem 4, which effectively frames the IG perspective for f-GAN. Overall, this constitutes a strong case for acceptance. However, the technical depth may hinder accessibility for practitioners unfamiliar with the connections between information theory (IT) and information geometry (IG), such as the mapping of KL divergence for exponential families to Bregman divergences or Fenchel duality. Given that deep learning engineers form a primary audience for GAN papers, the following suggestions would enhance clarity:  

1. **Figure 1 (Appendix)**: This figure effectively summarizes IT-IG connections in the f-GAN context. Consider moving it to the main text to provide immediate context.  
2. **Motivation for IG View**: The introduction should explicitly state why the IG perspective is useful—e.g., to analyze optimization behavior in parameter space. The current mention on page 7 is too late.  
3. **Section 5 Relevance**: The connection between Section 5 (deformed exponential families for neural network representations) and f-GAN remains unclear. The section explains how activation functions relate to f-divergences but does not address how this informs f-GAN design (e.g., which f-divergence to choose or how activation functions affect convergence).  
4. **Theorem 6 Interpretation**: The claim that φₗ acts as "deep sufficient statistics" requires clarification. Equation (13) does not visibly align with the deformed exponential family form.  
5. **Utility Theory Discussion**: The utility theory paragraph (lines 257–269) appears tangential to the IG framework. It could be moved to the appendix to free space for clarifying core contributions.  
6. **Experimental Relevance**: The experiments do not directly support the paper’s central claims. Results in Section 5 (activation functions) could be discussed without the IG framework, and Section 4’s content (e.g., WGAN results on line 227–228) distracts from the f-GAN focus. The WGAN results, while interesting, are peripheral and require extra effort to locate.  

In summary, while the paper offers valuable theoretical insights and rigorous derivations, its current organization lacks clarity. The manuscript integrates findings from multiple research threads into an 8-page format, resulting in a presentation that feels crowded rather than cohesive. I strongly recommend acceptance but emphasize that substantial editing is needed to sharpen the narrative, explicitly link sections to the f-GAN framework, and prioritize accessibility for the broader ML community. The core contributions are compelling, but their impact would be significantly amplified with a more focused and reader-friendly structure.
