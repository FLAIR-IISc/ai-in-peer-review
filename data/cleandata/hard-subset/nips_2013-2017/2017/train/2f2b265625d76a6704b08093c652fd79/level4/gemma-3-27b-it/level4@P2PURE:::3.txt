The authors identify several interesting questions related to Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), including the connection between GAN training and parameter space convergence, the generator’s learned representation during convergence, and—most relevant from a network architectural standpoint—how to choose the discriminator’s output activation function to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function.
The authors attempt to address these questions within the information-theoretic framework of deformed exponential distributions, deriving several theoretical results. These include a variational generalization, applicable to f-GAN formulations, of a theorem relating f-divergences between distributions to corresponding Bregman divergences between parameters. This provides a connection between the information-theoretic view of distribution dissimilarity and the information-geometric perspective on parameter dissimilarity. They further demonstrate that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. Their investigation suggests that careful selection of generator hidden activation functions and discriminator output activation functions could improve GAN performance, and they briefly present an alternative interpretation of the GAN game through the lens of expected utility theory.
Overall, the authors present interesting theoretical results, but I have concerns regarding their practical relevance and usefulness in their current form.
Questions & concerns:
First, many of their theorems and insights rely on the assumption that the data and model distributions, P and Q, are members of the deformed exponential family. Can the authors justify this assumption, elaborating on its prevalence in practice and explaining whether similar results (such as Theorems 4 & 6) could be obtained without it?
A key contribution, the information-geometric f-GAN identity in Eq.(7), connects the variational f-divergence formulation with a parameter-based optimization problem. What benefits does this parameter-based view offer? Is it possible to implement GANs using this approach, and what would be the advantages? I would have appreciated experimental results comparing optimization using both approaches. Surprisingly, the authors do not extensively utilize the right-hand side of this identity beyond stating that solving the GAN game implies parameter space convergence (when the residual J(Q) is small). Why is this implication not self-evident? Can the authors provide a scenario where f-divergence convergence does *not* imply parameter space convergence?
Regarding a practical implication, the authors demonstrate that the choice of discriminator output activation function impacts the compositeness properties of the GAN loss. I found this result particularly interesting, and would have liked to see a comparison of their theoretically derived activation function (the composition of f′ and the link function) against the heuristic choice in Ref. [34] through experimentation.
Concerning deep learning and experimental results: Assuming generator reversibility, the authors show the existence of an activation function enabling generator hidden layers to factor exactly as escorts for the deformed exponential family. In Table 2 (left), they compare generator architectures with different activation functions, some complying with this escort factorization. However, the plot for the GAN MLP indicates that the theoretically superior μ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU (corresponding to the μ → 1 limit). Given their analysis, I would expect the invertible μ-ReLU (satisfying reversibility) to outperform the non-invertible baseline. Why does μ-ReLU perform worse? Can they comment on whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption and how these results compare to those from Wasserstein GANs, which are not based on f-divergences (and therefore are outside the scope of their theoretical analysis)? Finally, the differences in performance between activations (Table 2 center) or link functions (Table 2 right) appear to be within error bars. This provides insufficient evidence to support the practical relevance of their theoretical results.
Minor concerns:
Many results are presented within the framework of deformed exponential densities. Given that this area may be unfamiliar to the broader ML community, providing more intuition regarding concepts like deformation (or signature) and escorts would be beneficial.
In Theorem 8, the authors derive upper bounds for J(Q). How do these bounds compare to each other, and what implications do they have for activation function selection? They also state that this upper bound decreases with the escort’s normalization parameter Z. Could they elaborate on why this is advantageous and how we might leverage it?
In conclusion, I believe the insights gained from the theoretical analysis are not substantial enough to significantly impact the work of a GAN practitioner.
