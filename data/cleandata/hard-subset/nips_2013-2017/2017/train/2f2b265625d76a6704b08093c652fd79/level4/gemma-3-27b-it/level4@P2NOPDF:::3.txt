The authors tackle several important questions regarding Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), including the relationship between GAN convergence and parameter space convergence, the generator’s learned representation, and the crucial role of discriminator activation functions in ensuring a well-defined loss function. They investigate these questions using the framework of deformed exponential distributions, leading to several theoretical results. These include a variational generalization of a theorem connecting f-divergences to Bregman divergences, demonstrating a link between information theory and information geometry. They further show that, under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can be factored as “escorts” of deformed exponential distributions, and suggest that careful selection of generator activation functions and discriminator output activations could improve GAN performance. Finally, they offer an alternative interpretation of the GAN game through the lens of expected utility theory.

Overall, the paper presents interesting theoretical contributions, but I have significant concerns about their practical impact in the current form.

My primary questions revolve around the strong assumption that data and model distributions belong to the deformed exponential family. The theorems and insights presented hinge on this assumption; therefore, the authors need to justify its validity. Specifically, they should elaborate on how often this condition is met in practice and whether similar results can be derived without it, particularly for Theorems 4 and 6.

A key contribution is the information-geometric f-GAN identity (Eq. 7), which bridges the gap between variational f-divergence formulations and parameter-based optimization. However, the authors haven’t clearly articulated the benefits of this parameter-based perspective. Can GANs be practically implemented using this approach? What advantages would it offer? I believe the paper would be substantially strengthened by experimental results comparing optimization via f-divergence minimization and parameter-based optimization. Furthermore, the authors largely overlook the right-hand side of this identity, beyond stating it implies convergence in parameter space when the residual J(Q) is small. This implication feels non-trivial and requires further explanation. Can the authors provide a scenario where minimizing the f-divergence does *not* lead to convergence in parameter space?

The authors correctly identify the importance of the discriminator’s output activation function for achieving proper compositeness of the GAN loss. This result, derived theoretically, is particularly appealing. I would have greatly appreciated a direct experimental comparison between their proposed activation function (derived as a composition of f′ and the link function) and the heuristic choice from the original GAN paper [34].

Regarding the consequences for deep learning, the authors demonstrate that, assuming generator reversibility, hidden layers can be factored as escorts of deformed exponential distributions. However, the experimental results (Table 2 left) present a puzzle: the theoretically superior μ-ReLU appears to perform *worse* than the baseline ReLU. Given the analysis, one would expect the invertible μ-ReLU to outperform the non-invertible ReLU. An explanation for this counterintuitive outcome is crucial. Additionally, the authors should discuss whether the DCGAN architecture satisfies the reversibility assumption and how their results compare to those from Wasserstein GANs, which are not based on f-divergences and therefore fall outside the scope of their theoretical framework. Finally, the results presented in Tables 2 (center and right) show no statistically significant differences between activation and link function performance; this lacks sufficient evidence to support the practical relevance of their theoretical findings.

Minor points: The paper relies heavily on the framework of deformed exponential densities. To improve accessibility for a broader machine learning audience, the authors should provide more intuitive explanations of concepts like “deformation” (or “signature”) and “escort.” Regarding Theorem 8, the derived upper bounds for J(Q) need further contextualization. How do these bounds compare, and what are the implications for activation function selection? Furthermore, the authors state that the upper bound on J(Q) decreases with the escort’s normalization parameter Z, which they claim is beneficial. Elaborating on *why* this is a positive outcome and how it can be leveraged would be helpful.

In conclusion, while the theoretical analysis offers potentially interesting insights, the practical implications for GAN practitioners are currently not substantial enough to warrant a strong recommendation. Further investigation and, crucially, more compelling experimental validation are needed.
