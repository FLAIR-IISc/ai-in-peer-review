The paper "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell" by Nock et al. presents an interesting theoretical framework for understanding the behavior of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) in the context of deformed exponential distributions. The authors identify several key questions related to GANs, such as the extent to which solving the GAN problem implies convergence in parameter space, what the generator is actually fitting when convergence occurs, and how to choose the output activation function of the discriminator to ensure proper compositeness of the loss function.

The authors derive several theoretical results, including a variational generalization of a known theorem that relates an f-divergence between distributions to a corresponding Bregman divergence between the parameters of such distributions. This theorem provides an interesting connection between the information-theoretic viewpoint of measuring dissimilarity between probability distributions and the information-geometric perspective of measuring dissimilarity between the corresponding parameters.

The authors also show that under a reversibility assumption, deep generative networks can factor as "escorts" of deformed exponential distributions. Their theoretical investigation suggests that a careful choice of the hidden activation functions of the generator and a proper selection of the output activation function of the discriminator could potentially help to further improve GANs.

However, I have several concerns regarding the practical relevance and usefulness of these results in their present form. Firstly, many of the theorems and resulting insights hold for the specific case that the data and model distributions P and Q are members of the deformed exponential family. It is unclear whether this assumption is met in practice, and whether similar results could be derived without this assumption.

Secondly, the authors' main contribution, the information-geometric f-GAN identity, relates the well-known variational f-divergence formulation over distributions to an information-geometric optimization problem over parameters. However, it is unclear what benefits this parameter-based point of view provides, and whether it is possible to implement GANs in terms of this parameter-based optimization.

Thirdly, the authors' experimental results are not entirely convincing. For instance, their comparison of different activation functions for the generator suggests that the "theoretically superior" μ-ReLU performs worse than the baseline ReLU, which is counterintuitive. Additionally, their results on the choice of link function for the discriminator are not significantly different from the baseline, which raises questions about the practical relevance of their theoretical analysis.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their results. However, the paper could benefit from more intuition and explanations for the general machine learning community, who may not be familiar with the framework of deformed exponential densities.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but some of the proofs and derivations could be more detailed and explicit. Additionally, some of the notation and terminology could be more clearly defined and explained.

In terms of originality, the paper presents some interesting and novel theoretical results, but the practical relevance and usefulness of these results are unclear.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to contribute to a deeper understanding of GANs and their behavior, but more work is needed to demonstrate the practical relevance and usefulness of the results.

Overall, I would rate the paper as follows:

* Quality: 8/10
* Clarity: 8/10
* Originality: 7/10
* Significance: 7/10

I would recommend the paper for publication, but with the condition that the authors address some of the concerns and questions raised above, and provide more intuition and explanations for the general machine learning community.
