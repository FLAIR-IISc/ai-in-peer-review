**Summary of the Main Ideas**

The paper "f-GANs in an Information Geometric Nutshell" presents a theoretical framework for understanding the supervised game of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) using information geometry. The authors extend the GAN principle to all f-divergences and provide a variational information geometric identity that connects the f-GAN game to a geometric optimization problem. They also show that deep architectures can be powerful at modeling complex distributions by factorizing deformed exponential families. The paper provides a complete proper loss picture of the supervised GAN game and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach through experiments.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon the work of Nowozin et al. [30] who showed that the GAN principle can be extended to all f-divergences. The authors also draw inspiration from information geometry and the work of Amari [2, 4, 7, 9, 35] on the relationship between f-divergences and Bregman divergences.

**Strengths**

1. **Theoretical contributions**: The paper provides a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of GANs and their connection to information geometry.
2. **Deep architectures**: The authors show that deep architectures can be powerful at modeling complex distributions by factorizing deformed exponential families.
3. **Complete proper loss picture**: The paper provides a complete proper loss picture of the supervised GAN game, which sheds light on the connection between the value of the optimal discriminator and the density ratio between the generator and discriminator distributions.
4. **Experimental validation**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach through experiments on MNIST and LSUN datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Technical complexity**: The paper requires a strong background in information geometry and convex analysis, which may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
2. **Assumptions**: The authors make several assumptions, such as the invertibility of the activation functions, which may not always hold in practice.
3. **Limited experimental evaluation**: The experimental evaluation is limited to two datasets and two architectures, which may not be representative of all possible scenarios.

**Originality**

The paper presents a novel theoretical framework for understanding GANs using information geometry. The authors provide a variational information geometric identity that connects the f-GAN game to a geometric optimization problem, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Significance**

The paper has significant implications for the field of GANs and deep learning. The authors' theoretical contributions and experimental validation provide a deeper understanding of the supervised game of GANs and the power of deep architectures in modeling complex distributions.
