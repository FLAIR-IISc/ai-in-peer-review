This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, demonstrated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea is to model high-order correlations between different modalities (image, question, and answer) by computing separate "potentials" for each modality (unary), pairs of modalities (pairwise), and all three modalities (ternary). These potentials are then linearly combined and passed through a softmax to generate attention distributions for each modality. The resulting attended feature vectors are fed into a decision module, which uses Multimodal Compact Bilinear/Trilinear (MCB/MCT) pooling to predict the final answer. This approach extends prior work on co-attention (e.g., Lu et al., 2015) and bilinear pooling (e.g., Fukui et al., 2016) by explicitly formulating and combining attentions derived from interactions of different orders. The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The primary contribution is the explicit modeling of high-order interactions within the attention mechanism itself. While prior work has combined modalities before applying attention (e.g., MCB) or used co-attention between two modalities, this paper proposes a structured way to compute and combine unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. This is a conceptually interesting and powerful idea. The strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art on a very competitive benchmark, demonstrate the significance and effectiveness of this approach.

2.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper includes a solid experimental evaluation. The quantitative results are strong and compared against relevant baselines. The qualitative analysis is particularly insightful, with attention visualizations that effectively illustrate the contribution of different potentials. For example, the visualizations in Figure 5 compellingly show how unary attention focuses on salient objects, while pairwise attention refines the focus based on the question, leading to a more accurate final combined attention. This suggests the performance gains are not merely from increased model capacity but from a genuinely better attention mechanism.

3.  **Interesting Qualitative Findings:** The visible shift between the unary, pairwise, and final combined attention maps is a notable strength. It provides good intuition for how the model works and supports the central claims of the paper. This is a strong piece of evidence for the model's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's main weakness is its writing and organization, which is often jumbled and makes the method difficult to fully grasp. The exposition could be significantly improved.
    *   The connection to probabilistic models and mean-field inference is stated as motivation but is never formalized. The "potentials" are computed by neural networks, and the "mean-field iteration" is a single linear combination followed by a softmax. This connection feels tenuous. The paper would be stronger if this framing were either rigorously justified with a clear probabilistic model or softened to be purely motivational.
    *   Figure 2 is presented at an awkward level of abstraction, mixing high-level concepts with specific layer types, which makes the overall architecture hard to follow. A simplified diagram focusing on how the raw modalities are used to generate unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, which are then combined to produce the final attended vectors, would be much clearer.
    *   A significant oversight is that Figure 3 is never referenced in the main text, leaving the reader to guess its purpose. This should be corrected.

2.  **Originality Context:** While the core idea is novel, the paper could do a better job of positioning its contributions relative to prior work. The building blocks for computing potentials (1x1 convolutions, similarity matrices) and the decision-making module (MCB/MCT) are heavily inspired by or directly taken from previous work [7, 15]. The key novelty lies in the *architecture* for combining these components into a multi-order potential system. The authors should clarify that their contribution is this specific formulation, which provides a principled way to extend co-attention and MCB-like ideas to different interaction orders.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Equation (1) introduces learnable parameters (α, β, γ) to weigh the different order potentials. What values do these parameters converge to during training? It would be highly insightful to know if different modalities learn to rely more heavily on certain orders of potentials (e.g., does image attention rely more on pairwise potentials, while question attention relies more on unary?). This would add significant weight to the paper's core claims.

2.  The paper presents results for a 2-modality (image-question) model that underperforms previous methods like MCB [7]. Given the architectural similarities, could the authors provide some intuition for this performance gap? Is it related to differences in the embedding dimensions, or does it suggest that the decision module (e.g., the 2-layer MCB) is critical and interacts in a specific way with the attention mechanism?

3.  In Section 4.2 and 4.3, you mention comparing a single MCT unit against a 2-layer MCB setup for decision making and report validation results. Could you elaborate on these findings? The choice of decision module seems important, and a more detailed comparison or ablation study would strengthen the paper.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and powerful method for high-order attention that achieves excellent results. The qualitative analysis provides good evidence that the model is learning a meaningful attention distribution. However, the paper is significantly held back by poor clarity and presentation. If the authors can substantially revise the writing to better explain their model, clarify its connection to probabilistic methods, and more precisely situate its novelty, it would be a very strong contribution.
