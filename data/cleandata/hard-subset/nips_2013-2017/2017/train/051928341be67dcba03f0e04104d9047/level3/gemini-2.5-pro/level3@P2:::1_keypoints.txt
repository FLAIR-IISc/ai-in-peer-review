- Proposes attention via learned unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answers, marginalized and linearly combined to produce attentions and achieve SOTA on VQA.  
- Novel and principled formulation: modeling attention as a learned combination of different-order potentials for interpretable, extensible multimodal interactions.  
- Strong empirical performance: achieves state-of-the-art results on the competitive VQA benchmark, supporting higher-order correlations.  
- Thorough evaluation: extensive quantitative comparisons, ablations showing 3-modality > 2-modality, and insightful qualitative visualizations.  
- Compelling qualitative analysis: visuals show pairwise potentials refining unary attention and final attention focusing on semantically relevant regions.  
- Clarity and presentation issues: writing is dense and organization confusing, hindering understanding of model details and contributions.  
- Probabilistic framing mismatch: mean-field/potentials motivation is not formally connected to the deterministic neural model and should be formalized or softened.  
- Figure 2 is overly detailed and presented at a confusing abstraction level; a simplified diagram focusing on core flow is needed.  
- Positioning and novelty unclear: pairwise resembles Lu et al. (2016) co-attention and decision module follows Fukui et al. (2016); authors should clarify how their framework generalizes or departs from prior work.  
- Learned potential weights: request to report learned α, β, γ to show which modalities rely on which potential orders.  
- 2-modality baseline underperforms prior work: ask for intuition whether this is due to lower embedding dims, decision module differences, or other hyperparameters.  
- Decision module question: why does cascaded pairwise (2-layer MCB) outperform a direct three-way MCT unit for VQA—explain expressivity differences.  
- Suggest visualizing marginalized ternary attention maps alone to demonstrate the unique contribution of third-order correlations.
