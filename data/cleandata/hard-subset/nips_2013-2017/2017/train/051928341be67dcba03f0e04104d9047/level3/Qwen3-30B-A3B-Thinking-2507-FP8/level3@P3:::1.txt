

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors introduce a high-order attention model that learns correlations between multiple data modalities through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The model extends previous work on attention mechanisms in VQA (such as Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25]) by explicitly modeling higher-order interactions between visual, textual, and answer modalities. The authors frame their approach as a probabilistic model with mean-field inference to combine these potentials, achieving state-of-the-art performance on VQA.

The paper's strengths are significant. The proposed high-order attention mechanism achieves strong empirical results on VQA, with attention maps that provide meaningful interpretability. The thorough qualitative analysis (Fig. 5-8) demonstrates that the model's improvements come from better attention mechanisms rather than simply increased model capacity, as the authors show attention patterns that correlate with the question content. The quantitative results on VQA test-dev and test-std datasets are compelling, with the model achieving state-of-the-art performance while having fewer parameters than competing approaches.

The paper is particularly interesting in its demonstration of how the attention shifts between unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials as the model processes the input. The attention maps in Fig. 5 provide a compelling visualization of how the model combines different attention types to focus on relevant regions. I would have found the paper even stronger with marginalized trinary attention maps (as requested in the key points) to better understand how the model integrates all three modalities.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. The writing and presentation are confusing and poorly organized, making it difficult to follow the technical contributions. The connection to probabilistic modeling is only motivational rather than formalized, with the authors failing to provide a clear probabilistic interpretation of their approach. Figure 2, which attempts to illustrate the system architecture, is at an awkward abstraction level and would be clearer if simplified to emphasize the potential generation process. Additionally, Figure 3 (illustrating k-order attention) is never referenced in the text, which is a significant oversight.

The perceived novelty of the work is limited. The architecture essentially extends Multimodal Compact Bilinear Pooling (MCB) ideas to higher-order interactions, but the paper doesn't clearly distinguish how this differs from previous work. The authors claim their approach is generally applicable to any number of modalities, but fail to adequately demonstrate this beyond the VQA application.

Several critical questions remain unanswered. The paper doesn't reveal what values were learned for the linear coefficients (αi, βi, γi) combining marginalized potentials—do different modalities favor different potential orders? The 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise model underperforms MCB despite similar architecture, yet the paper provides no intuition or explanation for this counterintuitive result. The authors mention a sequential MCB vs single MCT decision heads comparison but provide no results or observations.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with strong empirical results, but the presentation undermines the technical contribution. The clarity is poor due to confusing writing and presentation issues. The originality is limited, as the approach builds directly on MCB and related work without sufficiently differentiating itself. The significance is moderate, as the attention mechanism is a useful contribution to VQA, but the lack of clear novelty and weak presentation diminish its impact.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a novel attention mechanism that achieves strong empirical performance on VQA, its value is significantly diminished by poor writing, unclear presentation, and limited perceived novelty. The authors should clarify their probabilistic motivation, improve the organization of the paper, and provide more detailed analysis of their model's behavior.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Strong empirical results on VQA
- Thorough qualitative and quantitative evaluation
- Interesting attention shift between unary/pairwise/ternary potentials
- Attention maps provide meaningful interpretability

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Poor writing and presentation
- Limited novelty (extends MCB ideas without clear differentiation)
- Probabilistic connection is motivational but not formalized
- Critical questions unanswered (linear coefficients, 2-modality performance)
- Figure 3 never referenced in text

The paper has potential but requires significant revision to clarify its contributions and improve presentation before it can be considered for acceptance.
