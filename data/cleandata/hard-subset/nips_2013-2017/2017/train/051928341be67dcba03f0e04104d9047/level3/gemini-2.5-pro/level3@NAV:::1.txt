This paper presents a high-order attention model for multimodal tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness on the Visual Question Answering (VQA) benchmark. The central idea is to extend existing co-attention mechanisms by explicitly computing and combining unary (single-modality), pairwise, and trinary potentials to generate attention distributions over the image, question, and candidate answers. This approach builds on prior work in multimodal pooling, such as Multimodal Compact Bilinear pooling (MCB), by generalizing interaction modeling to different orders. The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the standard VQA dataset.

The paper’s key strength is its strong empirical performance, supported by a novel and interesting method. The experiments are thorough, with solid quantitative comparisons and insightful qualitative visualizations. The attention maps are particularly compelling, as they suggest the model’s improvements stem from a more refined attention mechanism rather than simply increased capacity. The visualized shift from unary/pairwise attentions to the final combined attention provides valuable insight into the model's reasoning.

However, the paper is significantly weakened by poor clarity and presentation. The writing is jumbled and difficult to follow, which obscures the technical details. For example, Figure 2 is presented at an awkward level of abstraction, and Figure 3 is never referenced in the text. The novelty also appears limited; the architecture can be framed as a direct extension of MCB-style concepts to different orders, and the authors should better clarify their core conceptual contribution. Furthermore, the probabilistic connection is purely motivational. The use of terms like "potentials" and "mean-field inference" is not formally justified, and this framing should either be mathematically grounded or softened.

To improve the manuscript, the authors should address the following questions:
1. What were the learned values for the linear coefficients that combine the potentials? Do different modalities favor different potential orders?
2. Why does the "2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise" model underperform MCB, given their architectural similarities?
3. The paper mentions comparing sequential MCB vs. a single MCT decision head but provides no results. What was observed?

In summary, this work introduces an effective model with impressive results, but it requires a major revision to improve its clarity, better position its contributions, and temper its claims before it is ready for publication.
