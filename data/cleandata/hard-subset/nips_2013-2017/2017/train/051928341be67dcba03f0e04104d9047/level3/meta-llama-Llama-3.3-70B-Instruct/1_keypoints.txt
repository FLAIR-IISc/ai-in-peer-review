- Paper analyzes Bitcoin P2P anonymity pre- and post-2015 by modeling the network as a graph and evaluating deanonymization by an eavesdropper under trickle and diffusion protocols.
- Thorough qualitative and quantitative analysis, including simulations on regular trees and a real Bitcoin network snapshot.
- Results indicate both trickle and diffusion have poor anonymity with similar detection probabilities.
- Attention maps demonstrate improved attention mechanisms and a notable shift from unary/pairwise attentions to the final attention.
- Technical soundness: theoretical analysis and experiments support the claims; authors provide careful and honest evaluation.
- Paper addresses an important, difficult problem and yields unique conclusions on existing data.
- Writing and presentation are weak and sometimes confusing, needing clearer exposition.
- Novelty is limited: the architecture mainly reiterates prior model intuition with added complexity.
- The probabilistic connection is not well formalized and requires clarification.
- Figure 2 is overly abstract and should be simplified to emphasize unary/pairwise/trinary potential generation.
- Figure 3 is not referenced in the text and its relevance is unclear.
- Reviewer requests results/insight on learned linear coefficients and why the 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise model underperforms.
- Discussion of sequential MCB versus a single MCT decision head is interesting but lacks empirical results.
- Strengths summarized: interesting method, thorough experiments, and significant findings.
- Weaknesses summarized: limited originality, weak writing/presentation, and unclear figures/discussions.
- Acceptance arguments: thorough anonymity analysis, demonstrable poor anonymity for both protocols, and improved handling of a hard problem.
- Rejection arguments: limited architectural novelty, poor presentation, unclear probabilistic linkage, and ambiguous figures.
