- Proposes a novel probabilistic high-order attention mechanism combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials for multimodal tasks (image, question, answer).
- Uses a single mean-field inference step to convert combined potentials into attention distributions and fuses attended features for answer prediction.
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art results on VQA test-dev and test-std, with ablation showing benefit from the ternary (third-order) potential.
- Technical formulation is sound and extends prior co-attention and bilinear pooling methods into a structured, extensible framework.
- 2-modality variant (unary + pairwise) underperforms existing two-modality methods (e.g., MCB, MLB), raising questions about that component’s advantage.
- Performance gains may be driven mainly by including the answer modality, making it unclear if the probabilistic formulation itself is superior.
- Evaluation lacks thoroughness: need controlled comparisons (e.g., same embedding dimensions) to de-confound architecture vs. capacity effects.
- Failure analysis is limited to a single example; broader analysis of when and why high-order attention fails is missing.
- “Attention over answers” concept is unclear and under-explained, especially given the discrete, large answer vocabulary in VQA.
- Paper lacks visualizations or detailed explanation of how answer-attention distributions are computed and used.
- Minor clarity issues and typos (e.g., “mechanisms” vs. “mechanism”, inconsistent reporting of validation vs. test splits).
- Originality: explicitly modeling and learning weights for unary/pairwise/ternary potentials is a novel and general idea.
- Would benefit from explicit comparison to prior works that also use answers as input (e.g., Jabri et al., 2016) and discussion of conceptual differences.
- Significance: framework is generalizable to k modalities and could influence future multimodal work (e.g., video QA with subtitles).
- Positive arguments for acceptance: principled framework, SOTA results, clear writing, and ablation supporting ternary potential usefulness.
- Negative arguments for rejection: unclear contribution of 2-modality model, poor motivation/explanation of answer-attention, missing comparisons to relevant prior work, limited failure analysis.
- Key experimental clarifications requested: detailed mechanism for answer attention, controlled comparison isolating feature dimensions, and consistency of reported evaluation splits.
- Request for conceptual clarification on differences from Jabri et al. (2016) and whether gains remain when framing as simpler classification baselines.
