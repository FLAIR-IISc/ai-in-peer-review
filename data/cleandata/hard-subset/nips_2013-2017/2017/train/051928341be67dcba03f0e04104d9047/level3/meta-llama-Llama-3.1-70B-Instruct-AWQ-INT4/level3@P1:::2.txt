This paper proposes a novel, generic multimodal attention formulation for Visual Question Answering (VQA) tasks. The authors extend existing attention mechanisms by incorporating unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image, question, and answer modalities. The key idea is to model high-order correlations between these modalities, which is demonstrated to be effective in improving the performance of VQA systems.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed attention mechanism, which is a significant strength. The authors also provide a thorough ablation study, demonstrating the contribution of the ternary potential to the overall performance of the model. Furthermore, the paper presents state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, which is a notable achievement.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas that require clarification. Firstly, the concept of "attention over answers" is unclear, particularly since most answers are single tokens, and no visualizations of answer attention are provided. It would be beneficial to provide more insight into how the attention mechanism operates on the answer modality.

Secondly, the main improvement in performance appears to be driven by the ternary potentials. Without these, the model does not outperform existing 2-modality methods, except for HieCoAtt. This raises questions about the effectiveness of the proposed attention mechanism in the absence of ternary potentials.

Additionally, the authors should compare their model to prior methods that use answers as inputs, such as Jabri et al.'s Revisiting VQA Baselines. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the benefits and limitations of the proposed attention mechanism.

Furthermore, the paper lacks an analysis of failure cases and model failure modes. Providing such an analysis would help to identify areas where the model can be improved and provide a more complete understanding of its performance.

Lastly, there are some minor errors and typos in the paper, including a reporting discrepancy (L38 "mechanism"→"mechanisms"; L237 claims validation but Table 1 shows test-dev/test-std).

In the post-rebuttal, the authors report that attention maps correlate better with human maps, but comparisons to state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods, such as MCB, are unclear. Additionally, the authors' "MCB vs. MCT" numbers appear inconsistent/swapped (68.4% vs 69.4%) and conflict with the MCB value (68.6%) in Table 1.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of VQA, with a generic multimodal attention formulation that could generalize beyond a single task. However, addressing the mentioned weaknesses and providing more insight into the attention mechanism would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive understanding of its contributions.
