

This paper proposes a novel high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), extending previous work on attention models by incorporating unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to model multi-modal interactions. The authors position their work as a probabilistic extension of MCB (Multimodal Compact Bilinear Pooling) to higher-order interactions, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on VQA datasets with a relatively simple architecture.

The strengths of the paper are notable. The method achieves strong empirical results, with thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluations showing that high-order attention significantly improves performance over previous approaches. The attention maps effectively illustrate how the model shifts from unary to pairwise to ternary attention, suggesting the improvements stem from better attention modeling rather than increased capacity. The architecture is generally applicable to multi-modal tasks beyond VQA, which is a significant contribution.

However, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses. The writing is confusing and disorganized, making it difficult to follow the technical contributions. The perceived novelty is limited, as the architecture primarily extends MCB ideas to higher orders without sufficiently clarifying how it differs from prior work. The probabilistic connection is presented as motivational but lacks formalization, requiring either rigorous mathematical development or softened language. Figure 2 is at an inappropriate abstraction level, while Figure 3 is never referenced in the text. The paper also fails to address several key questions: the learned coefficients for linear combinations of potentials, why the 2-modality Unary+Pairwise model underperforms MCB despite similar architecture, and the lack of results comparing sequential MCB vs. single MCT decision heads.

The paper demonstrates quality through its thorough experimental evaluation, but the unclear presentation and limited novelty undermine its contribution. The significance is moderately high for the VQA community, but the lack of clear differentiation from prior work on multi-modal attention mechanisms reduces its impact. The paper would benefit from clearer exposition, formalization of the probabilistic claims, and addressing the unresolved questions about model design choices. While the results are promising, the paper's current form lacks the clarity and novelty expected for NIPS.
