

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors introduce a generic formulation that models unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer modalities. The key insight is that high-order correlations between these modalities can better direct attention to relevant elements, leading to improved performance. The paper builds upon existing attention mechanisms for VQA (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) but extends them to incorporate higher-order interactions rather than focusing solely on pairwise relationships.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it presents a unified probabilistic framework for attention that can be extended to any number of modalities, offering a more general approach than previous work. Second, the authors provide an ablation study demonstrating that ternary potentials (involving all three modalities) significantly improve performance over pairwise-only models. Third, the paper is clearly written with well-structured explanations and helpful visualizations of attention patterns.

The paper's main strength is its generic multimodal attention formulation, which could potentially generalize beyond VQA to other multimodal tasks. The ablation study convincingly shows that the ternary potential component is crucial for the performance gains observed. The authors also demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the VQA benchmark (69.4% on test-dev), which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. The most significant issue is the unclear interpretation of "attention over answers" given that most answers are single tokens. The paper claims to provide attention over answers but fails to visualize these attention maps, making it difficult to understand how this component functions. Additionally, the main performance improvement appears to be driven almost entirely by the ternary potential component. Without this component, the model does not outperform existing two-modality methods (except HieCoAtt), which requires clarification. The authors should have compared their approach to models that explicitly use answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al. [9]) since their ternary input seems critical.

The paper also lacks analysis of failure cases and model failure modes, which would have provided valuable insights into the limitations of their approach. Furthermore, there are minor errors including a typo on line 38 ("mechanism"→"mechanisms") and a reporting discrepancy on line 237 where validation results are claimed but Table 1 shows test-dev/test-std results.

Post-rebuttal, the authors addressed some concerns by reporting that their attention maps correlate better with human attention maps, but they failed to provide clear comparisons to state-of-the-art attention methods like MCB. Additionally, the "MCB vs. MCT" numbers in their response appear inconsistent with the values reported in Table 1 (68.4% vs 69.4% for MCT, conflicting with the MCB value of 68.6%).

Regarding the review criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims, though the main improvement appears to be driven by a single component (ternary potential) without sufficient explanation of why this component is necessary.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written with good organization and helpful illustrations.
- Originality: The high-order attention formulation represents a meaningful extension to existing work, though the core idea of incorporating multiple modalities has been explored before.
- Significance: The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA, advancing the field demonstrably. The generic framework could inspire future work on multimodal tasks.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and well-executed approach to multimodal attention that achieves strong results on VQA. While the ternary potential component appears to be the main driver of performance gains, the generic nature of the framework and its clear presentation make this a valuable contribution. The concerns about answer attention interpretation and the lack of comparison to answer-inclusive baselines are significant but do not undermine the core technical contribution. The paper's clear writing and strong empirical results outweigh its weaknesses.

I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the concerns about answer attention and provide clearer comparisons to relevant prior work in future revisions. The generic framework and state-of-the-art performance on VQA make this a strong contribution to the field.
