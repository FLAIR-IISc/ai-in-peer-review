- Proposes a high-order attention mechanism for VQA to learn correlations across visual input, question, and answer using mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities.  
- Claims state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, demonstrating effectiveness of higher-order attention.  
- Extends prior attention and bilinear pooling work to k-th order correlations, enabling direct reasoning over combinations of modalities.  
- Builds on compact bilinear pooling (MCB) and extends it to higher-order interactions via MCT.  
- Thorough empirical evaluation with comparisons to established baselines.  
- Qualitative attention visualizations (Fig. 5–7) support the claim that higher-order correlations improve decision-making.  
- Explores different decision-making modules (MCB vs. MCT), but experimental outcomes are not fully reported.  
- Writing is dense and convoluted, hindering comprehension of the technical details.  
- The probabilistic motivation feels under-developed and not formally connected to a true probabilistic model; wording should be tempered or formalized.  
- Figure 2 is overly abstract and fails to communicate core model components; Figure 3 is unreferenced and effectively useless.  
- Novelty/perceived contribution versus existing bilinear and multi-modal methods is insufficiently articulated.  
- Missing details: learned linear coefficients (α, β, γ) and whether modalities favor different potential orders.  
- No reported results comparing sequential MCB decision module versus single MCT head despite claims of experimentation.  
- Unexplained underperformance of the unary+pairwise (2-modality) model relative to MCB needs investigation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: SOTA results, novel extension to attention mechanisms, compelling qualitative evidence, and thorough empirical comparisons.  
- Arguments for rejection: unclear writing, weakly emphasized novelty, underdeveloped probabilistic framing, and unclear/unused figures.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on improving clarity, formalizing or softening probabilistic claims, clarifying novelty, and fixing figure presentation and references.
