This paper introduces a novel high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to capture correlations between multiple data modalities. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, attributing the success to more effectively directing attention to relevant elements in each modality. While the empirical results are strong and the approach shows promise, the paper suffers from weaknesses in clarity and presentation, hindering a full assessment of its novelty.

The work builds upon the recent surge of attention mechanisms in multi-modal tasks like VQA, image captioning, and machine comprehension. Existing approaches, such as those by Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], often focus on pairwise interactions or lack explicit modeling of multi-modal relationships. This paper addresses these limitations by proposing an attention mechanism that learns correlations of arbitrary order (unary, pairwise, ternary, etc.) between modalities. The core idea revolves around representing attention as a probabilistic model based on high-order potentials, using mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities for attention weights. This allows for a flexible extension to any number of data modalities.

The strengths of this work are clear. The achieved state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset is a significant accomplishment, demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the proposed attention mechanism. The thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons to existing methods and ablation studies (though some results are only mentioned without complete presentation – see questions below), provides strong evidence supporting the authors’ claims.  The visualization of attention maps, particularly the observation of a shift between unary/pairwise attentions and the final combined attention, is insightful and suggests the method learns meaningful representations. The qualitative examples further illustrate the model’s ability to focus on relevant image regions and question words.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The writing is often convoluted and difficult to follow. The introduction, while outlining the motivation, lacks a concise summary of the contribution. The description of the attention mechanism, while detailed, is unnecessarily complex and could benefit from simplification.  Figure 2, for example, is at an abstract level that obscures the core components, and should be revised to explicitly show the unary, pairwise, and ternary potential generation steps. Furthermore, Figure 3 is introduced but never referenced in the text, rendering it ineffective.

The claimed novelty of the approach is also somewhat unclear. While the extension of bilinear pooling techniques like MCB [7] to higher orders is a contribution, it feels incremental. The authors should more clearly articulate *how* their approach differs significantly from existing work.  The probabilistic connection, framed as a motivation for using potentials, feels weak. The authors either need to formalize this probabilistic interpretation or tone down the language.

Several questions arise that, if addressed, would strengthen the paper. First, what values were learned for the linear coefficients (αi, βi, γi) that combine the marginalized potentials? Do different modalities consistently favor different potential orders, offering insight into the learned representations? Second, the paper mentions a 2-modalities model utilizing unary and pairwise attention, but it underperforms the MCB baseline despite a similar architecture.  What is the intuition for this result, and how does it relate to the decision computation modules used? Finally, the authors mention testing different decision heads (sequential MCB vs. single MCT) but provide no quantitative results.  What observations were made from these experiments?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   State-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark.
*   Novel extension of existing attention mechanisms to higher-order correlations.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation and insightful visualizations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak and convoluted writing style, hindering clarity.
*   Incremental novelty – the connection to existing work (e.g., MCB) requires stronger clarification.
*   The probabilistic motivation lacks formalization.
*   Missing experimental details and results related to the decision making components.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**.  The empirical results are strong and the idea is promising, but significant improvements to the clarity of the presentation, a stronger articulation of the novelty, and addressing the raised questions are crucial. I believe the authors have the potential to produce a very strong paper with a focused revision addressing these points.  A major revision is recommended.
