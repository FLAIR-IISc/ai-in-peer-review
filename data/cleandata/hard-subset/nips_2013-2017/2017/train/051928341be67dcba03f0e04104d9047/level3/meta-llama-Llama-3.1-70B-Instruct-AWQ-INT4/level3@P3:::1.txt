The paper "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering" proposes a novel attention mechanism that learns high-order correlations between various data modalities, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset. The authors provide a thorough qualitative and quantitative evaluation of their approach, demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing complex relationships between visual and textual inputs.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of a high-order attention model that can handle multiple data modalities. The authors show that their approach can be easily extended to any number of modalities, making it a versatile and powerful tool for multimodal learning tasks. The use of mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities is also a notable aspect of the paper, as it allows for efficient computation of attention weights.

One of the strengths of the paper is its thorough evaluation of the proposed approach. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the attention maps, demonstrating how the high-order attention model captures complex relationships between the input modalities. The qualitative evaluation is also impressive, with the authors showing that their approach can attend to reasonable pixel and question locations, even in cases where existing approaches fail.

However, the paper's writing and presentation are weak points. The text is often jumbled and confusing, making it difficult to follow the authors' reasoning. The notation is also inconsistent, with different symbols used to represent the same quantities in different parts of the paper. The figures are not well-explained, and some of them (e.g., Figure 2) are at an awkward abstraction level.

Another weakness of the paper is the limited perceived novelty of the approach. While the authors claim that their high-order attention model is a significant departure from existing approaches, it is not entirely clear how their work differs from previous contributions. The authors should clarify the contributions of their work and provide a more detailed comparison with existing approaches.

The probabilistic connection between the attention model and the decision-making component is also not formalized, which is a weakness of the paper. The authors should either formalize this connection or soften their language to avoid giving the impression that their approach is more theoretically grounded than it actually is.

In terms of originality, the paper's approach is not entirely new, as it builds on existing work on attention mechanisms and multimodal learning. However, the authors' extension of these ideas to high-order correlations between multiple modalities is a significant contribution.

The significance of the paper's results is high, as it demonstrates the effectiveness of high-order attention models in capturing complex relationships between visual and textual inputs. The authors' approach has the potential to be applied to a wide range of multimodal learning tasks, and the results presented in the paper are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in the field.

In conclusion, while the paper has some weaknesses in terms of writing and presentation, its technical contributions are significant, and its results are impressive. With some revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field of multimodal learning.
