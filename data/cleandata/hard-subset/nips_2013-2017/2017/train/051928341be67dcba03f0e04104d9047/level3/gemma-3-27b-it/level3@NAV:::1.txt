## Review of High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

This paper proposes a novel high-order attention mechanism for Visual Question Answering (VQA) motivated by the idea that modeling correlations between multiple modalities (visual, textual, and answer choices) can improve performance. The core idea is to learn unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials representing the importance of individual features and their interactions, and then use mean-field inference to derive attention weights. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, suggesting the benefits of modeling higher-order correlations.

The approach is interesting and builds upon recent advances in attention mechanisms like MCB and MCT [7, 15]. The quantitative results are strong, demonstrating performance competitive with existing methods. The authors also provide a thorough set of experiments, including ablation studies and qualitative visualizations of the attention maps, which support their claims. Notably, the attention maps suggest that the performance gains stem from improved attention targeting, rather than simply increasing model capacity. The shift in attention focus observed between unary/pairwise and final attention is a particularly interesting observation, suggesting a hierarchical refinement of attention. 

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in clarity and presentation. The writing is often jumbled and difficult to follow. While the authors introduce the idea of high-order potentials, the connection to probabilistic modeling feels more like motivation than a formal contribution, and should be either formalized or toned down. Figure 2 is overly abstract and could be simplified to better illustrate the generation of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. Furthermore, Figure 3 is never referenced in the text, rendering it effectively useless.

The perceived novelty of the architecture is also limited; it essentially extends MCB ideas to higher-order interactions. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of its specific contributions beyond this extension. Several questions remain unanswered. What were the learned values for the linear coefficients used to combine the marginalized potentials? Do different modalities indeed favor different potential orders? Why does the 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise model underperform MCB despite a similar architecture? Finally, the authors mention experimenting with sequential MCB vs single MCT decision heads, but provide no results – what did they observe?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical performance, achieving state-of-the-art results.
*   Novel application of high-order correlations to attention mechanisms.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation with both quantitative and qualitative analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Weak and confusing writing significantly hinders understanding.
*   Limited novelty – primarily an extension of existing MCB ideas.
*   Lack of formalization of the probabilistic connection.
*   Unexplained figures and unanswered questions.



**Overall:** While the core idea and results are promising, the paper’s current state requires substantial revision to improve clarity and fully justify its contributions. I recommend rejection with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the concerns outlined above.
