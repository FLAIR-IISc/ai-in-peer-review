- Proposes a generic multimodal attention framework modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image, question, and answer for VQA with mean-field inference and compact bilinear/trilinear pooling for fusion.  
- Main contribution is casting attention as a high-order probabilistic model that naturally extends to three (or more) modalities and explicitly models third-order interactions.  
- Method relates to structured/graphical attention prior work but is tailored to multimodal VQA with explicit ternary potentials.  
- Empirically, inclusion of ternary potentials provides the majority of performance gains and yields strong three-modality results on VQA.  
- Model is technically sound and the ablation study cleanly demonstrates the importance of the ternary potential.  
- Two-modality empirical comparisons are unconvincing: without ternary terms the method does not beat several strong 2-modality baselines (except HieCoAtt).  
- An apples-to-apples comparison (matching embedding dimensions and training) is required to fairly assess 2-modality performance.  
- The concept of “attention over answers” is unclear given most answers are single tokens, and no visualizations or analysis of answer attention (PA) are provided.  
- Failure modes are only briefly shown with one figure; a more systematic failure analysis is missing.  
- Paper is clearly written and well organized, with minor issues (e.g., “mechanism”→“mechanisms”, and reporting validation vs. test-dev/test-std inconsistencies).  
- Originality: presents a novel, unified high-order attention formulation that generalizes beyond a single task.  
- Significance: strong three-modality SOTA and a generic framework are valuable and could influence future multimodal work if verified with stronger baselines.  
- Post-rebuttal concerns: human attention correlation results are appreciated but comparisons to prior SOTA (e.g., MCB) remain unclear.  
- Reporting discrepancy: MCB vs. MCT numbers appear inconsistent/swapped (68.4% vs 69.4%) and conflict with Table 1’s MCB (68.6%); reconciliation is requested.  
- Pros summarized: generic extensible formulation, clear writing, solid ablation showing ternary contributions, and strong 3-modality results.  
- Cons summarized: weak 2-modality comparisons needing controlled baselines, unclear/unsupported answer-attention concept with no visualizations, limited failure analysis, and minor typos/reporting discrepancies.  
- Recommendation: lean accept contingent on clarifying 2-modality baselines, visualizing/analyzing answer attention, reconciling MCB/MCT numbers, and expanding failure analysis.
