This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to learn correlations between different data modalities – visual input, question, and answer. The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms often lack the ability to capture these higher-order relationships and propose a model based on learning potentials of varying order, utilizing mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities. They demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, suggesting the effectiveness of their approach.

The work builds upon a rich history of attention mechanisms in both image and text processing, reviewed extensively in Section 2. Previous VQA approaches often focus on pairwise interactions [15, 25], or utilize stacked attention [27] or dynamic memory networks [24]. More recent work explores bilinear models [13] and probabilistic approaches [14]. The key contribution here lies in extending these concepts to *k*-th order correlations, allowing the model to directly reason about interactions between any combination of modalities.  This differs from methods like Lu et al. [15] which primarily focus on two modalities and struggle with generalization to higher order interactions.  The paper also builds on concepts from compact bilinear pooling [7], extending it to higher order interactions through the use of MCT.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The proposed high-order attention mechanism demonstrably improves performance on the challenging VQA task, achieving state-of-the-art results. The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation, comparing their approach against established baselines.  The qualitative results, with visualization of attention maps (Fig. 5-7), are particularly insightful. The observed shift in attention focus from unary to pairwise and finally to combined attention supports the claim that higher-order correlations contribute meaningfully to the decision-making process.  The exploration of different decision-making modules, specifically MCB and MCT, is a welcome addition. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses, primarily related to clarity and presentation.  The writing is often dense and convoluted, making it difficult to follow the technical details. For instance, the motivation for the probabilistic framework, while intuitively appealing, feels under-developed; the connection to a true probabilistic model isn't formalized, and the language used to describe it should be tempered.  Figure 2 is overly abstract and does not effectively communicate the core components of the model – a simplified diagram focusing on the unary, pairwise, and ternary potential generation would be more effective. Similarly, Figure 3 is introduced but never referenced in the text, rendering it essentially useless. 

The perceived novelty of the approach also warrants further discussion. While extending MCB to higher orders is a valid contribution, the paper needs to more clearly articulate how this differs significantly from existing bilinear pooling methods and the broader landscape of multi-modal interaction modeling. The paper would benefit from a more focused discussion of the architectural differences and the specific advantages of the proposed *k*-order attention mechanism. 

Several questions remain unanswered. What values were learned for the linear coefficients (α, β, γ) that combine the marginalized potentials? Do different modalities consistently favor different potential orders? Understanding these coefficients could offer valuable insights into the learned relationships between modalities. The authors mention experimenting with different decision-making modules (sequential MCB vs. a single MCT head) but provide no results – clarifying the outcomes of these experiments would be beneficial. Finally, the underperformance of the 2-modalities unary+pairwise model compared to MCB, despite the similar architecture, requires further investigation and explanation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark.
*   Introduces a novel extension to existing attention mechanisms.
*   Provides compelling qualitative results demonstrating the benefits of higher-order attention.
*   Thorough empirical evaluation with comparisons to relevant baselines.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The writing is unclear and convoluted, hindering comprehension.
*   The perceived novelty of the approach is not adequately emphasized.
*   The probabilistic connection feels underdeveloped and lacks formalization.
*   Key figures are either unclear or not referenced in the text.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses in presentation, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of VQA and multi-modal reasoning. The high-order attention mechanism demonstrates promising results and offers a potentially fruitful direction for future research. However, significant revisions addressing the clarity and novelty concerns are necessary. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, simplifying the writing, clarifying the novelty of the approach, formalizing or softening the probabilistic language, and improving the clarity and integration of the figures are crucial for maximizing the impact of this work.
