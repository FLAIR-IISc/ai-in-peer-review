

This paper proposes a novel high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA), which models correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms often fail to explicitly consider multiple modalities, instead focusing on pairwise interactions or single-modality representations. Their approach models high-order correlations (up to ternary) and uses mean-field inference to obtain marginal attention probabilities, which are then used to create attended representations for decision making.

The work is well-positioned within the NIPS literature, building upon recent advances in attention mechanisms for VQA (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25], Fukui et al. [7]). While existing approaches like MCB (Multimodal Compact Bilinear Pooling) have been successful for pairwise interactions, the authors identify a gap in modeling higher-order correlations between three or more modalities. Their approach extends the idea of attention mechanisms to a more general probabilistic framework that can be applied to any number of modalities.

The paper's strengths are significant. First, the high-order attention mechanism is genuinely novel and generalizable beyond VQA, potentially applicable to other multimodal tasks. Second, the ablation study thoroughly demonstrates the contribution of ternary potentials, showing that their inclusion leads to significant performance improvements over pairwise-only models (64.57% vs 63.82% on test-dev). Third, the paper is exceptionally well-written with clear mathematical formulations, illustrative figures, and a logical organization that makes the technical content accessible.

However, several weaknesses warrant consideration. The most significant concern is the unclear conceptualization of "attention over answers" given that most answers are single tokens (not sequences), and the paper provides no visualizations of answer attention. This undermines the paper's claim of a comprehensive multimodal attention framework. Second, the paper's main performance gains appear to be driven almost entirely by the ternary potential component; without it, the model does not outperform existing two-modality approaches (except HieCoAtt), which requires stronger justification. Third, the paper should have compared more thoroughly with prior work that incorporates answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al. [9], "Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines"), as the authors' ternary approach essentially uses answers as a third modality.

The paper contains minor technical issues: a reporting discrepancy (Table 1 shows test-dev/test-std results but L237 claims validation performance), a typo ("mechanism" vs "mechanisms" in L38), and inconsistent MCB/MCT performance numbers (68.4% vs 69.4% vs 68.6% in Table 1). The lack of failure case analysis limits the understanding of the model's limitations, particularly for cases where attention is identical across different questions (Fig. 8).

The significance of this work is moderate to high. While the approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA, the broader impact depends on whether the high-order attention framework can be successfully generalized to other multimodal tasks. The paper's strength lies in its elegant probabilistic formulation of attention, which could inspire future work in multimodal learning. The VQA results are impressive (64.57% on test-dev), but the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons to recent SOTA methods, particularly those that use answers as inputs.

In terms of quality, the technical content is sound with appropriate ablation studies, but the justification for the ternary potential's necessity is not fully convincing without stronger comparison to two-modality baselines. The clarity is excellent, making the complex attention mechanism accessible to readers.

For acceptance considerations, I note the post-rebuttal clarification that the model's attention maps correlate better with human attention, which is promising. However, the inconsistent MCB/MCT performance numbers (68.4% vs 69.4% vs 68.6%) remain concerning. The authors' claim that their model is "relatively simple" (40 million parameters vs 70+ million for [7]) is valid but doesn't fully address the core concern about the two-modality baseline performance.

Pro: Novel high-order attention framework with strong VQA results (SOTA), clear writing, thorough ablation study, well-motivated theoretical approach.

Con: Unclear "attention over answers" concept, main gains driven by ternary potentials without sufficient justification for two-modality comparison, insufficient comparison to answer-inclusive baselines, minor technical errors, lack of failure analysis.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the attention mechanism literature with a well-motivated framework for high-order multimodal correlations. While the VQA results are impressive, the paper would benefit from addressing the concerns about the two-modality baseline and the unclear answer attention component. Given the paper's strong technical content, clear writing, and state-of-the-art performance, I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors should address these concerns in the camera-ready version. The generic framework has the potential to influence future multimodal learning research beyond VQA.
