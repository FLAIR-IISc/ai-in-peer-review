## Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel, generally applicable high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, demonstrated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea is to model unary, pairwise, and, crucially, ternary potentials between image, question, and answer representations to refine attention. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, with the improvement largely attributed to the inclusion of these ternary potentials. 

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and technical details of the proposed approach. The modular design, allowing for easy extension to different modalities, is a significant strength, representing a potentially impactful contribution. The ablation study convincingly demonstrates the contribution of the ternary potential, adding credibility to the claims.

However, several weaknesses remain. The concept of “attention over answers” is unclear, as most VQA answers are single tokens. Visualizations of this attention would greatly clarify this aspect. While the authors demonstrate improved performance *with* ternary potentials, the model *without* them doesn't substantially outperform existing 2-modality methods (except for HieCoAtt). The paper needs to better address why this is the case and clarify whether the gains are truly driven by the ternary potentials or by a strong baseline incorporating them. 

Furthermore, the paper neglects comparison to prior work that *explicitly* incorporates the answer into the attention mechanism (e.g., Jabri et al., 2017). Given the emphasis on the ternary input, such a comparison is crucial. A more detailed analysis of failure cases and common error types is also lacking, hindering our understanding of the model’s limitations. Finally, there are minor errors (e.g., L38) and a reporting discrepancy (L237 vs. Table 1) that need to be addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Generic and potentially widely applicable multimodal attention framework.
*   Quantitative SOTA results on VQA with the ternary potential.
*   Clear writing and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Unclear explanation and lack of visualization of "attention over answers."
*   Limited gains without the ternary potential and lack of comparison to similar models.
*   Missing failure case analysis.
*   Minor errors and reporting inconsistencies.

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment:** I lean towards acceptance despite the unsatisfactory response to the 2-modality comparison concerns. The generic framework and the quantitative 3-modality SOTA gains are compelling. However, the authors' reporting of attention map correlations with human maps lacks clarity regarding comparison with SOTA models like MCB. The numerical inconsistency regarding MCB vs. MCT also raises concerns, requiring a final check from the area chairs. Overall, the paper represents a valuable contribution, albeit with some lingering questions.
