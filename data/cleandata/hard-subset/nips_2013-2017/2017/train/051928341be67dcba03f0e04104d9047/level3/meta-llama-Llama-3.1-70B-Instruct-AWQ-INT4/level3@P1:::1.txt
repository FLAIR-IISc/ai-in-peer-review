This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that learns high-order correlations between different data modalities. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the VQA dataset, achieving state-of-the-art performance.

The proposed attention mechanism is based on a probabilistic model that captures high-order correlations between the visual input, question, and answer. The model uses a mean-field inference approach to compute marginal probabilities for each modality, which are then used to compute the attention weights. The authors also propose a tensor sketch algorithm to reduce the dimensionality of the attention weights, allowing for efficient computation.

The paper provides a thorough qualitative and quantitative evaluation of the proposed approach, including comparisons with existing state-of-the-art methods. The results suggest that the proposed approach achieves better performance than existing methods, particularly in terms of attention accuracy.

One of the strengths of the paper is the novel attention mechanism, which achieves good empirical performance. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including qualitative and quantitative experiments. The paper also highlights the importance of high-order attention models, which is a notable shift from existing approaches.

However, there are several weaknesses in the paper. The writing and presentation are jumbled and need improvement, making it difficult to follow the authors' arguments. The paper also lacks clarity on the novelty of the approach, with the architecture extending existing MCB ideas to higher/lower orders. The probabilistic connection is only motivational and not formalized, which may be confusing for readers. Additionally, some figures, such as Figure 2, are at an awkward abstraction level and should be simplified to emphasize unary/pairwise/trinary potential generation.

In terms of questions, it would be interesting to see the marginalized trinary attention maps to better understand the attention mechanism. Additionally, the authors mention sequential MCB vs single MCT decision heads but provide no results, which would be useful to understand the trade-offs between these approaches.

Overall, the paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset. However, the writing and presentation need improvement, and the authors should clarify the novelty of their approach and provide more results on the trade-offs between different decision heads.
