This paper proposes a high-order attention model for Visual Question Answering (VQA), formulating attention as interactions between image, question, and answer modalities using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The key contribution lies in demonstrating that incorporating these ternary potentials leads to state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

The work builds upon the recent trend of attention mechanisms in multimodal tasks (Bahdanau et al., 2014; Lu et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2016), specifically addressing the limitation of existing methods that often handle modalities in an ad-hoc or task-specific manner. The authors present a generic framework applicable to any number of modalities, and their probabilistic formulation based on mean-field inference is a notable design choice. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the proposed approach and experimental setup. The ablation study effectively highlights the impact of the ternary potentials.

However, several weaknesses and points of clarification remain. A significant concern is the interpretation of “attention over answers.” Given that most answers in the VQA dataset are single tokens, it’s unclear what this attention mechanism is actually focusing on. The lack of visualizations of answer attention further exacerbates this issue. While the authors describe the mechanism mathematically, a visual explanation would significantly improve understanding.

Furthermore, the substantial performance improvement appears heavily reliant on the inclusion of ternary potentials. Without them, the model does not outperform existing 2-modality methods (except for HieCoAtt), suggesting that the core framework itself isn’t necessarily superior. This necessitates a clearer discussion of the contributions *beyond* the ternary potentials. Why is this specific formulation of high-order potentials effective, and what advantages does it offer over simpler approaches?

A crucial missing comparison is to models that directly incorporate answers as input (Jabri et al., 2016). Given that the ternary potential seems critical to the performance gains, it's essential to benchmark against approaches explicitly designed to leverage answer information.  This would help establish whether the benefit stems from the high-order attention or simply from including the answer modality.

The paper also lacks a thorough analysis of failure cases and failure modes. Understanding *when* and *why* the model fails would provide valuable insights for future work. Showing examples of images and questions where the model performs poorly, along with a qualitative analysis of the attention maps, could reveal limitations and areas for improvement.

Minor issues include typographical errors (e.g., L38 “mechanism” should be “mechanisms”) and a reporting discrepancy (L237 claims validation results, but Table 1 presents test-dev/test-std results). These should be corrected for clarity.

Post-rebuttal, the reviewer leans towards acceptance primarily due to the generic framework and the quantitative gains achieved in 3-modality SOTA performance.  However, the unsatisfactory response regarding comparisons to 2-modality methods remains a concern. The authors claim improved correlation with human attention maps, but the comparisons to SOTA models like MCB are unclear.  Additionally, the reported numbers for MCB vs. MCT (68.4% vs 69.4%) appear inconsistent and conflict with the MCB value (68.6%) presented in Table 1.  This discrepancy needs resolution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and generic multimodal attention framework.
*   State-of-the-art performance on VQA with the inclusion of ternary potentials.
*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Ablation study demonstrating the importance of ternary potentials.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Unclear interpretation of “attention over answers”.
*   Performance gains heavily reliant on ternary potentials; core framework's advantage unclear.
*   Lack of comparison to models utilizing answers as inputs.
*   Insufficient analysis of failure cases.
*   Minor errors and reporting discrepancies.
*   Inconsistent results reported post-rebuttal.
