This paper proposes a generic multimodal attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image, question, and answer in VQA, with mean-field inference producing marginal attentions and compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) for decision fusion. Relative to prior co-attention methods (e.g., Lu et al.) and bilinear pooling (Fukui et al.), the contribution is to cast attention as a high-order probabilistic model that trivially extends to three modalities and, in principle, to more. The formulation is related to structured/graphical attention ideas (e.g., Kim et al., Structured Attention) but is tailored to multimodal VQA with explicit third-order interactions. Quantitatively, the inclusion of ternary potentials yields the bulk of the reported gains and establishes strong 3-modality results on VQA.

Quality: The model is technically sound and the ablation cleanly demonstrates the importance of the ternary potential. However, the empirical story for 2-modality settings is less convincing: without ternary terms, the method does not surpass several strong 2-modality baselines (except HieCoAtt), and the paper attributes this to a lower embedding dimension. An apples-to-apples comparison (same dims/training) is needed. It is also unclear what “attention over answers” means when most answers are single tokens; no visualizations or analysis of PA are provided. Failure modes are only briefly touched (one figure); a systematic analysis is missing.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. Minor issues: L38 “mechanism”→“mechanisms”; L237 states validation results while Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std.

Originality: A novel, unified high-order attention formulation that is easy to generalize beyond a single task.

Significance: The 3-modality SOTA and generic framework are valuable; if verified with stronger baselines, this can influence future multimodal work.

Post-rebuttal: I appreciate the additional human attention correlation results, but comparisons to SOTA (e.g., MCB) remain unclear. Also, the “MCB vs. MCT” numbers (68.4% vs 69.4%) appear inconsistent/swapped and conflict with Table 1’s MCB (68.6%). Please reconcile.

Pros:
- Generic, extensible multimodal attention formulation
- Clear writing; solid ablation showing ternary contributions
- Strong 3-modality results

Cons:
- Weak 2-modality comparisons; need controlled baselines
- Unclear/unsupported “answer attention” concept; no visualizations
- Limited failure analysis
- Minor typos/reporting discrepancies

Recommendation: Lean accept, contingent on clarifying 2-modality baselines, visualizing/analyzing answer attention, reconciling MCB/MCT numbers, and expanding failure analysis.
