Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning, instantiated on VQA. The core idea is to model attention as a set of potentials defined over one, two, or three modalities (unary, pairwise, ternary). Unary potentials capture the importance of individual elements (image regions, question words, candidate answers). Pairwise potentials are computed from similarity matrices between modalities (e.g., image–question), and ternary potentials from a correlation tensor over image–question–answer. A single mean-field-like update combines marginalized potentials via a learned linear mixture, followed by a softmax to produce attention distributions over each modality. The attended features are then fused using compact polynomial sketching: either Multimodal Compact Bilinear pooling (MCB) (possibly stacked) or its trilinear extension (MCT). On VQA multiple-choice, the method attains strong accuracy with fewer parameters than some baselines, and the qualitative attention maps show sensible shifts from generic unary saliency to question- and answer-conditioned foci.

This work connects to co-attention and bilinear pooling for VQA (e.g., Lu et al. 2016; Fukui et al. 2016; Kim et al. 2017 MLB; Nam et al. 2016; Yang et al. 2016; Xiong et al. 2016; Xu & Saenko 2016). It differs by explicitly defining and combining high-order cross-modal potentials and by using a simple linear mixture to weight their contributions per modality prior to attention normalization. For decision fusion it adopts the tensor sketch machinery of MCB/MCT common in recent VQA systems.

Quality
- Soundness: The definitions of unary, pairwise similarity matrices, and ternary correlation tensors are clear, and the attention distributions are obtained by softmax of a learned mixture of marginalized potentials. The “mean-field inference” claim is motivational; as written it corresponds to a single linear combination + softmax, not an iterative inference schedule or a principled CRF objective. Either formalize the probabilistic model (variables, factors, energy, variational objective, fixed-point updates) or rephrase to avoid overclaiming.
- Empirics: Results on VQA test-dev/test-std (multiple choice) are strong, with competitive or SOTA numbers using fewer parameters. The qualitative analyses are thorough and insightful, showing how pairwise and combined attentions differ from unary. Ablations include 2- vs 3-modality and MCT vs stacked MCB decision heads; however, more systematic ablations would strengthen the case (see suggestions).
- Efficiency: Computing C3 across image patches × words × answers can be large; practicality hinges on multiple-choice with na ≈ 18. Please clarify computation/memory cost and scaling if na is large (e.g., open-ended) or for longer questions, and whether any factorization/sparsification is used.

Clarity
The paper is readable but the presentation is muddled in places.
- The probabilistic framing is not developed; notation around potentials, marginalization, and “mean-field” could be streamlined.
- Figure 2 sits at an awkward abstraction level; simplifying it to foreground the unary/pairwise/ternary potential computation and the linear-mixture attention would help.
- Figure 3 should be explicitly referenced and walked through in Sec. 3.2; currently it is easy to lose track of what is convolved where.
- Several minor writing issues (typos, repeated words, inconsistent notation) detract from clarity. A careful editorial pass is recommended.

Originality
Moderate. High-order cross-modal interactions are not new in fusion (MCB/MCT, MLB, MUTAN), and joint/co-attention has been explored. The novelty here is to instantiate high-order interactions at the attention level with explicit potentials per order and per modality, and to learn how much each order contributes via simple linear combinations. The idea is neat and practical, but the conceptual distance from existing bilinear/trilinear fusion and co-attention is not large; the paper would benefit from a crisper positioning.

Significance
High. The framework is general and potentially useful beyond VQA. The empirical gains and qualitative insights suggest the approach will be of interest to the community, and the modularity makes it easy to extend to additional modalities. With stronger ablations and clearer framing, this could be an influential attention architecture.

Questions for the authors
- Learned coefficients: Please report the learned α, β, γ (means/variances over validation) and discuss how different modalities weight unary/pairwise/ternary terms. Do these weights vary by question type?
- 2-modality underperforming MCB: You attribute this to lower embedding dimension. Can you control for dimension and capacity, and disentangle attention quality from the decision head’s expressiveness?
- MCT vs stacked MCB: You mention both; please provide a comprehensive comparison (VGG vs ResNet backbones, val/test, multiple seeds), including accuracy, parameters, FLOPs, and latency.
- Ternary attention visualization: Please include the marginalized ternary potentials θV,Q,A(·) per modality alongside unary and pairwise maps to substantiate how A influences attention.
- Open-ended VQA: Do results hold in the open-ended setting? If the ternary attention requires candidate answers as input, this limits applicability. Please discuss.
- Training stability: What is the variance across runs? Any ensembling? Early stopping criteria?

Suggestions for improvement
- Either formalize the CRF/mean-field view (and show benefit of more than one iteration) or present the method as a learned linear mixture of potentials without probabilistic claims.
- Provide stronger ablations: remove each potential order; vary the linear mixture (fixed vs learned; per-modality vs shared); control embedding sizes; compare against MLB/MUTAN decision heads under your attention.
- Report learned coefficients and per-question-type performance to illuminate when high-order attention helps.
- Clean up and simplify figures; ensure Figure 3 is referenced and explained. Move some engineering details (tensor sketch, hyperparameters) to an appendix and add a complexity analysis.
- Consider reporting attention–human alignment metrics (e.g., VQA-HAT, Visual7W pointing) to bolster interpretability claims.

Pros
- Clear, modular high-order attention mechanism with strong VQA results and fewer parameters.
- Thorough qualitative analyses; attention maps are convincing and task-dependent.
- General, extensible framework for multiple modalities.

Cons
- Probabilistic motivation is not formalized; risk of overclaiming.
- Incremental novelty relative to co-attention and bilinear/trilinear fusion literature.
- Presentation issues (figures, notation, missing details on learned weights and ablations).
- Limited scope of evaluation (primarily multiple-choice; limited analysis of decision head interactions).
