

This paper proposes high-order attention mechanisms (unary, pairwise, ternary) for visual question answering (VQA), extending the multimodal compact bilinear pooling (MCB) framework to model higher-order correlations between modalities. The authors claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA by learning attention weights through mean-field inference over high-order potentials, with qualitative attention maps suggesting improved interpretability. The work builds on Fukui et al. (NIPS 2016) and Lu et al. (NIPS 2016), but extends their bilinear pooling to ternary interactions.

**Strengths**  
The empirical results are compelling: the proposed model achieves 64.57% accuracy on VQA test-dev (vs. 63.82% for MCT), demonstrating that high-order attention improves performance over pairwise methods. The qualitative analysis (Figs. 5–7) effectively illustrates how attention shifts with question context, showing that pairwise potentials correlate with question words (e.g., "glasses" → glass regions), while combined attention adapts to semantic nuance. The thorough ablation (e.g., 3-modality vs. 2-modality models) and comparison to MCB/MCT (Sec. 4.3) provide strong evidence that the gains stem from better attention modeling rather than model size.

**Weaknesses**  
*Novelty and Formalization*: The core contribution is incremental. MCB (Fukui et al., NIPS 2016) already handles pairwise interactions via tensor sketching; extending it to ternary interactions via MCT (Pham & Pagh, SIGKDD 2013) is a natural step, not a fundamental advance. The probabilistic framing (Section 3.2) is underdeveloped—unary/pairwise potentials are treated as "features" rather than formalized probabilistic terms, and the mean-field approximation lacks theoretical justification. The claim that "high-order correlations effectively direct attention" is not substantiated by analysis of learned coefficients (e.g., α_i, β_i in Eq. 1), which would clarify why image attention relies more on pairwise terms.  

*Clarity and Presentation*: The writing is confusing. Figure 3 (illustrating potential modules) is never referenced in the text, creating disorientation. Figure 2 (system architecture) is overly abstract and fails to highlight the key innovation (potential generation). Critical questions remain unanswered:  
- Why does the 2-modality model (unary + pairwise) underperform MCB despite similar architecture? (Section 4.3 notes this but offers no explanation.)  
- The paper claims to extend MCB to "any number of modalities" but provides no results for >3 modalities or analysis of how potential order affects performance.  
- The comparison between sequential MCB and single MCT decision heads (mentioned in Sec. 4.2) lacks results.  
- Marginalized ternary attention maps (requested by reviewers) are not shown, limiting interpretability of the 3-modality model.

*Significance*: While the VQA results are strong, the methodological contribution is modest. The paper does not advance the *theory* of attention mechanisms—instead, it repackages existing techniques (tensor sketching, MCB) with minor extensions. The attention maps are insightful, but this alone does not justify the novelty claim. The work is more of a "good engineering application" of prior ideas than a significant theoretical or algorithmic advance expected at NIPS.

**Recommendation**  
The paper has strong empirical results and valuable qualitative insights, but the novelty is overstated, and the presentation undermines its contribution. The core idea (extending MCB to ternary interactions) is not sufficiently differentiated from Fukui et al. (NIPS 2016) or Pham & Pagh (SIGKDD 2013). To strengthen the case, the authors should:  
1) Formalize the probabilistic interpretation of potentials (not just "motivate" it),  
2) Clarify the relationship to prior work (e.g., "Unlike MCB, we model ternary dependencies via..."),  
3) Address the unanswered questions (coefficients, 2-modality performance),  
4) Simplify Figure 2 and reference Figure 3 in text.  

As written, the paper is technically sound but lacks the conceptual novelty required for NIPS. It would be better suited for a venue emphasizing applied VQA (e.g., CVPR). I recommend **reject**.
