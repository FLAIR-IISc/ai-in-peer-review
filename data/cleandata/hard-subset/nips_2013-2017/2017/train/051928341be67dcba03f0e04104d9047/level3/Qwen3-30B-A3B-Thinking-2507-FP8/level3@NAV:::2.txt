

This paper proposes a novel multimodal attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that incorporates unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer modalities. The authors demonstrate that high-order correlations, particularly ternary potentials, significantly improve VQA performance. The work builds on existing attention mechanisms like Lu et al.'s (2016) and Fukui et al.'s (2016) approaches but extends them to a more general framework applicable to any number of modalities.

The paper's strengths include its clear presentation of a generic framework that could potentially generalize beyond VQA, a thorough ablation study demonstrating the contribution of ternary potentials, and well-organized writing. The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on VQA benchmarks, showing the effectiveness of their high-order attention mechanism.

However, several weaknesses require attention. The concept of "attention over answers" remains unclear since most answers are single tokens, and the paper provides no visualizations of answer attention. More critically, the main performance gains appear solely attributable to ternary potentials; without them, the model fails to outperform existing two-modality methods (except HieCoAtt), necessitating clearer justification. The authors should compare with Jabri et al.'s (2016) work that uses answers as inputs, as their ternary approach seems critically dependent on this input. The paper also lacks failure case analysis and model failure mode examination. Minor issues include a reporting discrepancy (L237 claims validation but Table 1 shows test-dev/test-std) and inconsistent MCB vs. MCT numbers that conflict with Table 1 values.

Post-rebuttal, the authors' claim that their attention maps correlate better with human attention lacks comparison to SOTA methods like MCB. While the generic framework and 3-modality SOTA gains are compelling, the insufficient clarification regarding 2-modality performance remains concerning. Despite these issues, the paper's contribution to multimodal attention mechanisms is significant.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to multimodal learning with a well-motivated framework. The technical quality is strong, and the significance is demonstrated through state-of-the-art results. While the weaknesses require careful consideration, the paper's strengths and potential for broader application make it suitable for acceptance.
