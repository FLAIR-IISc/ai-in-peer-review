This paper proposes a novel high-order attention model for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The central idea is to formulate attention as a probabilistic model that combines unary potentials (over a single modality), pairwise potentials (correlating image and question), and, most importantly, a new ternary potential that models the joint correlation between the image, question, and answer modalities. This work extends prior co-attention mechanisms (e.g., Lu et al., 2016), which are typically limited to pairwise interactions, by explicitly modeling a three-way dependency to improve VQA performance.

The primary strengths of this paper are its novelty and clear presentation. The formulation of a generic multimodal attention framework that can incorporate k-order correlations is a significant conceptual contribution, with potential applications beyond VQA. The paper is well-written, and the authors effectively demonstrate the value of their core idea through an ablation study that shows the ternary potential is crucial for the model's performance gains.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed. First, the concept of "attention over answers" is poorly defined. Given that most VQA answers are single words, what this attention signifies is unclear, and the submission lacks visualizations to help interpret it. Second, the performance gain appears almost entirely dependent on the ternary potential. Without it, the authors' 2-modality model does not outperform existing methods, suggesting the core improvement may not be the framework itself but rather the inclusion of the answer modality as an input. This makes a comparison to prior work that also uses answers as input (e.g., Jabri et al., 2016) essential, yet it is missing. The paper would also benefit from an analysis of failure cases to better understand the model's limitations. Minor issues include typos ("mechanism" should be plural on L38) and a discrepancy between the text (L237 claims validation results) and Table 1 (which shows test-dev/test-std).

In conclusion, while the claims regarding the 2-modality baseline are not fully convincing, the generic nature of the proposed framework and the state-of-the-art results achieved with the 3-modality model are significant contributions. The novelty of the high-order attention formulation and its demonstrated empirical success make a compelling case for acceptance, provided the authors clarify the noted ambiguities and inconsistencies in the final version.
