This paper proposes a high-order attention model for Visual Question Answering (VQA), aiming to capture richer interactions between visual and textual modalities. The core idea – leveraging unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to guide attention – is interesting and yields strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset. However, the presentation is significantly hampered by weak writing and a lack of clarity regarding the novelty of the approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Model & Strong Performance:** The proposed model demonstrably achieves good performance on VQA, establishing a new state-of-the-art. This is a significant empirical contribution.
* **Thorough Experiments:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing the proposed method against several baselines, and ablating different components (MCT vs MCB). Both quantitative and qualitative results are provided.
* **Interesting High-Order Attention Concept:** The shift from unary/pairwise attentions to the final combined attention, visualized in the qualitative results, hints at the potential of higher-order correlations to refine attention mechanisms. The observation that performance improves through better attention rather than increased model capacity is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Poor Writing & Presentation:** The writing is frequently jumbled and difficult to follow. The flow of ideas is disrupted by repetitive phrasing and a lack of clear transitions. This hinders understanding of the core concepts.
* **Limited Perceived Novelty:** The model builds upon existing ideas from Multimodal Compact Bilinear Pooling (MCB) and its extensions. While extending these to higher orders is a logical step, the paper doesn't sufficiently articulate *what* makes this extension a significant contribution beyond a straightforward generalization. The contribution needs to be more clearly positioned relative to prior work.
* **Weakly Formalized Probabilistic Connection:** The authors motivate the model with a probabilistic interpretation based on potentials and mean-field inference. However, this connection remains largely motivational, lacking a rigorous formalization. The authors should either develop a more formal treatment or tone down the probabilistic language.
* **Awkward Figure 2:** Figure 2 is overly abstract and doesn’t effectively convey the generation of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. A simplified diagram focusing explicitly on how each potential is computed would be far more informative.
* **Unreferenced Figure 3:** Figure 3 is presented but never referenced or discussed in the main text, rendering it useless and suggesting incompleteness.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* **Linear Coefficient Values:** What specific values were learned for the linear coefficients (α, β, γ) combining the marginalized potentials (Eq. 1)? Do different modalities (image, question, answer) consistently favor different potential orders?
* **2-Modal Unary+Pairwise Underperformance:** Why does the 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise model underperform the MCB baseline despite having a similar architecture? Is there any intuition regarding this, or is it potentially related to the decision computation modules (MCB vs. MCT)?
* **Sequential MCB Results:** The authors mention exploring sequential MCB vs. a single MCT decision head but provide no experimental results. What observations were made during this exploration, and why were the results not included in the paper?

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound method with strong empirical results. The idea of leveraging high-order potentials for attention is intriguing. However, the paper is significantly hampered by its poor writing and lack of clarity regarding the novelty of the approach. The unreferenced figure and weakly formalized probabilistic connection further detract from the overall quality. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* State-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark.
* Thorough experimental evaluation.
* Interesting direction for attention mechanism research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Weak and confusing writing style.
* Limited perceived novelty – the contribution isn't clearly articulated.
* Unsubstantiated probabilistic claims.
* Incomplete presentation (unreferenced figure).



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The core idea is promising, and the experimental results are strong. However, significant improvements to the writing, clarity, and justification of novelty are required before this paper would be suitable for NIPS. Specifically, the authors should focus on clarifying the contributions relative to existing MCB work, providing a more formal treatment of the probabilistic aspects, simplifying Figure 2, and incorporating a discussion of Figure 3. A thorough rewrite to improve clarity and flow is also essential.
