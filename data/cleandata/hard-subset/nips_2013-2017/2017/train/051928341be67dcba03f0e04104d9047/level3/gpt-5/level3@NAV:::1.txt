Summary: This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for VQA that models unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over image, question, and answer modalities, and then computes attention distributions via a single mean-field-like linear combination of marginalized potentials with learnable coefficients. The attended vectors are fused with compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) for decision making. The approach is related to hierarchical co-attention, bilinear pooling (MCB), and structured attention, but differs by explicitly constructing and marginalizing k-order potentials and learning how to weight them. Empirically, the model attains strong results on VQA with fewer parameters than recent baselines; qualitative maps suggest the gains stem from sharper attention rather than capacity.

Quality: The empirical evaluation is thorough (quantitative SOTA, qualitative analyses, comparisons of MCB vs MCT). However, the probabilistic framing remains largely motivational: the “mean-field” update is a single learned linear blend without a clear underlying energy, variational objective, or convergence analysis. Please formalize the probabilistic model (potentials, partition function, inference objective) or soften that language. Controlled ablations are needed: show the learned linear coefficients (α, β, γ) and their variability across modalities and question types; provide apples-to-apples comparisons to MCB with matched embedding dimensionality and capacity. Include marginalized ternary attention maps.

Clarity: The writing and organization are often confusing. Figure 2 sits at an awkward abstraction level; simplify it to foreground unary/pairwise/ternary potential generation and marginalization. Figure 3 is never (or insufficiently) referenced; integrate it when introducing each module. There are minor notation slips and redundancies; a careful edit would help reproducibility.

Originality: The combination of explicit k-order potentials and learned marginal weighting is interesting, but the architectural novelty feels incremental relative to MCB and co-attention; please sharpen the contributions beyond “extending MCB to higher order” and clarify differences to structured attention and dual attention.

Significance: Results are competitive and the interpretability of shifting unary/pairwise vs final attention is compelling; the method could be useful for broader multimodal tasks.

Questions/requests:
- What values were learned for α, β, γ? Do different modalities favor different orders?
- Why does the 2-modal Unary+Pairwise model underperform MCB despite similar architecture—capacity, normalization, or decision head differences?
- You mention sequential MCB vs a single MCT head; please report results and discuss observed trade-offs.
- Please show marginalized trinary attention maps and per-question-type analyses.

Pros:
- Strong empirical results with fewer parameters
- Thorough qualitative/quantitative evaluation
- Clear interpretability via attention shifts

Cons:
- Probabilistic connection not formalized
- Writing/presentation issues; figures need revision
- Limited perceived novelty without clearer conceptual differentiation
