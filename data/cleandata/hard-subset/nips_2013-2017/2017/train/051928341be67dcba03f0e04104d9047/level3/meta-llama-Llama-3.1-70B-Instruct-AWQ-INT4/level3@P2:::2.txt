This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) tasks, which learns high-order correlations between various data modalities. The authors present a generic multimodal attention formulation that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image, question, and answer. The proposed approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and achieves state-of-the-art performance.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. The use of high-order potentials is a key contribution of the paper, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through an ablation study. The paper also presents a thorough analysis of related work, which helps to situate the proposed approach within the broader context of VQA research.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its generic multimodal attention formulation, which could generalize beyond a single task. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the VQA task, but the formulation could potentially be applied to other tasks that involve multiple data modalities.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One of the main concerns is that the paper does not provide a clear explanation of what "attention over answers" means, given that most answers are single tokens. The authors also do not provide visualizations of answer attention, which would help to clarify this aspect of their approach.

Another concern is that the main improvement in the paper appears to be driven by the ternary potentials, and without them, the model does not outperform existing 2-modality methods (except HieCoAtt). This raises questions about the contribution of the ternary potentials and whether they are truly necessary for achieving state-of-the-art performance.

Additionally, the paper does not compare to prior models that use answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., Revisiting VQA Baselines), which seems critical given the use of ternary inputs. The paper also lacks analysis of failure cases and model failure modes, which would provide a more complete understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

Finally, there are some minor errors and typos in the paper, and there is a reporting discrepancy (L38 "mechanism"→"mechanisms"; L237 claims validation but Table 1 shows test-dev/test-std).

Overall, the paper presents a novel and effective approach to VQA, but there are some areas that require further clarification and analysis. Despite these concerns, the reviewer leans towards accepting the paper due to its generic framework and quantitative 3-modality SOTA gains.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to VQA that achieves state-of-the-art performance.
* The generic multimodal attention formulation could generalize beyond a single task.
* The paper provides a thorough analysis of related work and situates the proposed approach within the broader context of VQA research.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper does not provide a clear explanation of what "attention over answers" means, given that most answers are single tokens.
* The main improvement in the paper appears to be driven by the ternary potentials, and without them, the model does not outperform existing 2-modality methods (except HieCoAtt).
* The paper does not compare to prior models that use answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., Revisiting VQA Baselines).
* The paper lacks analysis of failure cases and model failure modes.

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the concerns mentioned above.
