- Proposes a generic multimodal attention framework modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) with a single mean-field iteration and MCB/MCT fusion.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA multiple-choice; ablations indicate ternary (V–Q–A) interactions drive the gains.  
- Novelty: explicit high-order potential decomposition and mean-field marginalization; conceptually extensible to k modalities and related but distinct from structured attention.  
- Strength: clean, generic formulation that decomposes interactions; clear exposition, diagrams, and qualitative attention visualizations.  
- Strength: strong quantitative VQA MC performance with compact model and informative ablations on ternary contributions and fusion choices.  
- Weakness: “attention over answers” (PA) is under-specified, not visualized, and hard to interpret for short/single-token answers.  
- Weakness: main empirical benefit appears to come from conditioning on answers; the 2-modality variant does not beat several strong 2-modality baselines.  
- Weakness: missing direct comparisons to prior answer-conditioned baselines (e.g., Jabri et al.) under identical settings.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of failure modes and lack of systematic error breakdown by question type, answer length/frequency, etc.  
- Weakness: reporting inconsistencies, minor typos, and confusion about evaluation splits (val vs. test-dev/test-std).  
- Weakness: no discussion of computational/time/memory costs of the ternary tensor C3 and scaling with nq, nv, na or any low-rank/sparsity approximations.  
- Quality: technically sound and pragmatic; claims supported for 3-modality case but weaker empirical story for 2-modality and missing efficiency/hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.  
- Clarity: generally well written, but needs clarification on PA, efficient computation/storage of C3, and consistent reporting of splits/metrics.  
- Originality: moderate — decomposition into learnable unary/pairwise/ternary potentials plus mean-field is a useful new perspective.  
- Significance: potentially high for VQA and other multimodal tasks, but impact depends on demonstrating benefits without answer conditioning and providing more comprehensive comparisons.  
- Key author requests: clarify how PA is defined/learned and visualize it; detail how C3 is computed/stored and its costs; add direct comparisons to answer-conditioned baselines; report human-attention correlation protocol/metric; reconcile MCB vs. MCT numbers.  
- Minor typos/examples: “mechanism”→“mechanisms” (L38); mismatch between claimed validation and Table 1’s test-dev/test-std (L237); standardize “threeway” vs. “ternary”; small grammatical slips.  
- Pros for acceptance: general, extensible framework with clear probabilistic view, informative ablations, and 3-modality SOTA on VQA MC.  
- Cons for acceptance: heavy reliance on answer-conditioning for gains, missing relevant baselines, limited efficiency/failure analysis, and reporting inconsistencies.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors clarified some points but did not resolve 2-modality comparison or baseline issues; human-attention and MCB/MCT reporting remain unclear; reviewer still leans to accept.  
- Overall recommendation: 6 (weak accept); confidence 3/5.
