## Review of “High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering”

This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism based on high-order correlations between data modalities, demonstrated on the Visual Question Answering (VQA) task. The core idea – modeling interactions beyond pairwise relationships – is interesting and builds upon recent work in attention mechanisms like MCB and related bilinear pooling approaches (Lu et al., Fukui et al.). The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, suggesting the effectiveness of their approach. The thorough experimental evaluation, including ablation studies and qualitative visualizations, is a significant strength. The shift in attention focus observed between unary/pairwise and the final attention layer is particularly noteworthy and hints at a hierarchical reasoning process. 

However, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The writing is often jumbled and difficult to follow, with concepts introduced without sufficient explanation. For example, the motivation stemming from a probabilistic interpretation of attention feels tacked on and lacks formalization. The authors should either rigorously formalize this connection or tone down the probabilistic language. Figure 2 is overly abstract and doesn’t effectively convey the generation of unary, pairwise, and trinary potentials. A simplified diagram focusing on these potential generation steps would be much more helpful. Furthermore, Figure 3 is referenced nowhere in the text, rendering it useless. 

While the novelty of extending MCB ideas to higher orders is present, the contribution isn’t clearly articulated. The paper needs to better position itself within the existing literature and explicitly highlight what differentiates this work beyond simply increasing the order of attention. The authors mention sequential MCB vs. single MCT decision heads but provide no results – this is a missed opportunity for valuable insight. 

I have several questions: What specific values were learned for the linear coefficients combining the marginalized potentials? Do different modalities demonstrably favor different potential orders? Why does the 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise model underperform MCB despite a similar architecture? Is there any intuition relating this to the decision computation modules? Finally, what observations were made regarding the sequential MCB vs. single MCT decision heads?

**Arguments for acceptance:** Strong empirical performance, novel approach to attention modeling, thorough experimentation.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Weak and confusing writing, limited perceived novelty in contribution, lack of formalization of probabilistic motivation, underutilized figures.

**Overall:** I believe this paper has potential, but requires substantial revisions to address the clarity and presentation issues. The core idea is promising, and the results are strong, but the paper as it stands is difficult to fully appreciate. I recommend rejection with encouragement to resubmit after significant revisions.
