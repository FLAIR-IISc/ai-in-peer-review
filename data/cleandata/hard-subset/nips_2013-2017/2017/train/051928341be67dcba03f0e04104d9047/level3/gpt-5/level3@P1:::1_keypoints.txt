- Proposes high-order attention for multimodal VQA using unary/pairwise/ternary potentials combined via a learned linear mixture and softmax to produce marginal attentions.  
- Builds pairwise via Corr2 similarity matrix and ternary via Corr3 tensor; fuses attended vectors with compact tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to avoid outer-product blowup.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art on multiple-choice VQA with fewer parameters than strong baselines.  
- Conceptually clean: separates potential estimation from marginalization, providing a general template that can scale to more modalities.  
- Strong empirical results, thorough training details, and useful ablations comparing fusion methods (MCB vs MCT).  
- Informative qualitative analyses show attention progression from unary → pairwise → final sharpening, suggesting gains from improved attention.  
- Practical implementation uses standard CNN/LSTM embeddings, 1×1 convs for potentials, and sketching-based fusion.  
- Novelty is incremental over prior co-attention and bilinear pooling; main additions are explicit ternary potential and learned linear combination (mean-field framing).  
- Probabilistic “mean-field” interpretation is only motivational and not derived; needs formalization or removal.  
- Notation and tensor/weight shapes are unclear; manuscript would benefit from a schematic isolating potential generators and marginalizations.  
- Figures (esp. Fig.2 and Fig.3) need simplification, clearer references, and consistent notation; Corr2/Corr3 and dimensions should be defined at first use.  
- Computational complexity and memory implications of the ternary tensor C3 are not sufficiently discussed; batching/approximation strategies need clarity.  
- Results limited to multiple-choice VQA; authors should clarify open-ended VQA or other datasets (e.g., Visual7W) to support generality.  
- Method is technically sound as a neural architecture but the mean-field claim must be derived or reframed as heuristic learned mixing.  
- Writing and structure need major improvement: streamline notation, reorder method section to “potential construction → marginalization → decision fusion,” and move details to appendix.  
- Originality is moderate: systematizes hinted-at high-order attention/trilinear pooling ideas into potentials and demonstrates practical gains.  
- Significance: empirical gains on a major benchmark and modular design with interpretable attention visualizations are valuable.  
- Request clarification on learned mixing coefficients (α, β, γ) and whether different modalities weight potential orders systematically; propose per-question-type breakdown.  
- Ask for marginalized ternary attention visualizations per modality to validate three-way interactions add value.  
- Request controlled study explaining why 2-modal Unary+Pairwise underperforms MCB and whether decision fusion differences cause the gap.  
- Ask for consistent comparisons between sequential stacked MCB and single MCT across datasets and clear recommendations for when to use each.  
- Ask how C3 is computed/stored in practice, whether marginalization weights are factorized, and measured training/inference overhead of ternary potentials.  
- Suggest formalizing or softening the probabilistic framing, tightening contribution statement, and adding ablations (remove potentials, fixed vs learned mixing, per-category results, backbone comparisons).  
- Recommend improving figures, cross-references, and including success/failure cases showing when ternary attention helps or hurts.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong benchmark results, compact general design, and qualitative evidence for higher-order attention usefulness.  
- Arguments against acceptance: modest novelty, unsupported probabilistic interpretation, and presentation/writing issues that hinder clarity.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying contributions, tightening exposition, and either formalizing or tempering the probabilistic claims.
