## Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a generic high-order attention mechanism for multimodal data, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea is to move beyond pairwise interactions between modalities (image, question, answer) and incorporate ternary potentials to capture higher-order correlations. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, attributing the gains to the inclusion of these ternary potentials. 

The work builds upon the growing body of research utilizing attention mechanisms in multimodal tasks, referencing relevant work in image and textual attention (Lu et al., Xu et al., Fukui et al.) and highlighting the limitations of existing approaches in explicitly modeling multi-way interactions. The proposed framework is a valuable contribution as it offers a generalized approach to multimodal attention, potentially applicable beyond VQA. The ablation study convincingly demonstrates the contribution of the ternary potentials to performance, and the paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methodology. 

However, several weaknesses remain. The notion of “attention over answers” is unclear, particularly given that most answers are single tokens. The lack of visualizations illustrating where the model focuses its attention *within* the answer space hinders understanding. While the authors show the improvement from adding ternary potentials, the model *without* them doesn’t significantly outperform existing 2-modality methods (except HieCoAtt), raising questions about the baseline performance and the necessity of the ternary component for substantial gains. 

Crucially, the paper doesn’t adequately compare to prior work that *explicitly incorporates answers as input* (e.g., Jabri et al., 2017), which seems essential given the reported importance of the ternary input. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the claim of novelty. Furthermore, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of failure cases and the types of questions/images where the model struggles. This would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach. Minor errors and inconsistencies (e.g., L38, L237) also detract from the overall polish.

Post-rebuttal, the authors’ claim of improved correlation with human attention maps is unconvincing without clearer comparisons to SOTA methods like MCB. The inconsistency in the reported MCB vs. MCT numbers and the discrepancy with Table 1’s MCB value are concerning and require clarification. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The generic framework for multimodal attention and the quantitative SOTA gains on VQA are significant.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** The lack of strong 2-modality performance, insufficient comparison to relevant prior work, and the unresolved inconsistencies raise concerns.

**Overall:** Despite the remaining concerns, I lean towards acceptance due to the potential of the proposed framework and the demonstrated improvements in 3-modality VQA. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper.
