- Proposes a high-order attention model (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) for VQA that achieves state-of-the-art performance.  
- Presents thorough experiments, multiple baselines, and component ablations with both quantitative and qualitative results.  
- The high-order attention concept is interesting and appears to improve performance by producing better attention rather than merely increasing model capacity.

- Writing and presentation are poor, with jumbled phrasing and weak flow that hinder understanding.  
- Limited perceived novelty: extension of MCB-style methods to higher orders is not clearly positioned as a significant contribution.  
- The probabilistic motivation (potentials and mean-field inference) is weakly formalized and remains largely motivational.  
- Figure 2 is overly abstract and fails to clearly show how unary/pairwise/ternary potentials are computed.  
- Figure 3 is unreferenced in the text and appears incomplete or useless.

- What were the learned values of the linear coefficients (α, β, γ) combining marginalized potentials, and do modalities favor different potential orders?  
- Why does the 2-modal Unary+Pairwise model underperform the MCB baseline despite similar architecture—possible relation to MCB vs MCT decision modules?  
- What were the results and observations for sequential MCB versus a single MCT decision head, and why were they omitted?

- Overall: technically sound method with strong empirical results, but paper quality is undermined by clarity and novelty issues.  
- Arguments for acceptance: state-of-the-art benchmark performance, thorough evaluation, and an interesting direction for attention research.  
- Arguments against acceptance: confusing writing, unclear novelty, unsubstantiated probabilistic claims, and incomplete presentation (unreferenced figure).  
- Recommendation: reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit after improving writing, clarifying novelty, formalizing or toning down probabilistic claims, simplifying Figure 2, addressing Figure 3, and including missing experiment details.
