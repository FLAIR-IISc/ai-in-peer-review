- Proposes high-order attention (unary, pairwise, ternary) for VQA, extending MCB to model ternary interactions and claims SoTA (64.57% on VQA test-dev) via mean-field inference.  
- Empirical strength: improves over MCT/MCB (64.57% vs 63.82%) showing high-order attention yields measurable gains.  
- Qualitative strength: attention maps (Figs. 5–7) show attention shifting with question context and semantic nuance.  
- Experimental strength: thorough ablations and comparisons indicate gains arise from attention modeling rather than model size.  
- Novelty concern: contribution is incremental — natural extension of MCB/MCT rather than a fundamental advance.  
- Formalization issue: probabilistic framing is underdeveloped; unary/pairwise treated as features and mean-field lacks theoretical justification.  
- Missing analysis: no inspection of learned coefficients (α_i, β_i) to support claims about which potentials drive attention.  
- Clarity/presentation problems: Figure 3 is never referenced; Figure 2 is overly abstract and hides the main innovation (potential generation).  
- Unanswered experimental questions: unexplained why 2-modality (unary+pairwise) underperforms MCB; no results for >3 modalities despite claims; no sequential MCB vs single MCT comparison results.  
- Interpretability gap: marginalized ternary attention maps requested by reviewers are not shown, limiting insight into the 3-modality model.  
- Significance concern: methodological contribution is modest and reads as strong engineering/application of prior techniques rather than theoretical or algorithmic novelty.  
- Recommendation: technically sound but overstated novelty; reviewer recommends reject for NIPS and suggests targeting applied VQA venues.  
- Suggested revisions: formalize the probabilistic interpretation, clarify relationship to prior work, analyze learned coefficients and the 2-modality discrepancy, provide >3-modality or sequential vs single-head comparisons, and simplify/reference figures (Fig. 2/3).
