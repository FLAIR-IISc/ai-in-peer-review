This paper introduces a high-order attention model for multimodal tasks, demonstrated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The central idea is to formulate attention as a probabilistic process, generating "potentials" that capture correlations within and between modalities. The model computes unary (single-modality), pairwise (two-modality), and trinary (three-modality) potentials. These are then linearly combined with learned weights and passed through a softmax to produce final attention distributions over image regions, question words, and potential answers. This approach extends prior co-attention mechanisms (e.g., Lu et al. 2015) and bilinear pooling methods (e.g., Fukui et al. 2016) by explicitly modeling higher-order interactions before the final decision-making step. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Performance (Originality/Significance):** The formulation of attention through explicitly computed unary, pairwise, and trinary potentials is a novel and intuitive way to model complex inter-modal dependencies. The model's strong empirical performance, achieving state-of-the-art results on the challenging VQA benchmark, demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach. This suggests that explicitly modeling these higher-order correlations is a promising direction for multimodal reasoning.

2.  **Thorough Experiments (Quality):** The paper provides a solid empirical evaluation, including both quantitative comparisons against strong baselines on standard test sets and extensive qualitative visualizations. The attention maps are particularly insightful, providing compelling evidence for the model's claims.

3.  **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The visualizations in Figure 5 effectively show how the final attention is a non-trivial combination of unary and pairwise signals. The unary attention often highlights salient objects, the pairwise attention correlates question words with relevant image regions, and the final attention synthesizes these to focus on the correct location. This supports the central claim that the performance gains come from a more sophisticated attention mechanism, rather than just increased model capacity. It would be highly interesting to also see visualizations of the marginalized trinary potentials to understand their specific contribution to the final attention maps.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's primary weakness is its clarity. The writing is jumbled, making it difficult to follow the central ideas. The connection between the high-level motivation and the low-level implementation is not clearly articulated. For instance, the motivation from probabilistic models and mean-field inference is not well-connected to the final implementation, which is a straightforward (though effective) weighted sum of learned potential maps. The manuscript would benefit significantly from a major revision focused on improving the organization and flow of ideas.

2.  **Limited Perceived Novelty:** While the high-order formulation is new, the architectural components are direct extensions of prior work, particularly Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) pooling. The paper could do a better job of positioning its contributions. The core novelty appears to be in how attention is *calculated* (via potentials) before being *used* by a decision module (like MCB/MCT). This distinction should be clarified to precisely delineate the paper's contribution from that of Fukui et al. (2016) and others.

3.  **Tenuous Probabilistic Connection:** The paper motivates its model using the language of probabilistic graphical models ("potentials," "mean-field inference"). However, this connection is only motivational and is not formalized. The final attention calculation is a linear combination of features followed by a softmax, which the authors describe as a "single mean-field iteration." This link is weak and potentially misleading. The authors should either formalize this connection with a clear probabilistic model and derivation or soften the language to present the architecture as a neurally-inspired mechanism for combining evidence of different orders.

4.  **Figure Quality and Referencing:** The figures are not used effectively to improve clarity.
    *   Figure 2, which outlines the system architecture, is presented at an awkward level of abstraction. It does little to illuminate the core contribution: the generation and combination of unary, pairwise, and trinary potentials. A simplified diagram emphasizing this specific process would be much more informative.
    *   Figure 3 is critical for understanding how the potentials are generated, yet it appears to be completely unreferenced in the main body of the text. This is a significant oversight that leaves the reader to guess the details of the potential functions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The linear combination of potentials (Equation 1) is a key part of the model. What were the learned values for the coefficients (α, β, γ)? Do these coefficients show any clear patterns? For instance, does image attention learn to rely more heavily on pairwise potentials, while question attention relies more on unary ones? This would provide strong quantitative evidence for the model's flexibility.

2.  In Table 1, the "2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise" model underperforms the MCB model from Fukui et al., despite the text suggesting a similar architecture. The paper attributes this to a lower embedding dimension. Is there any further intuition for this performance gap? Could it be related to differences in the "decision computation modules" or other subtle architectural choices?

3.  The paper mentions comparing a sequential 2-layer MCB with a single MCT decision head for the 3-modality model, finding the former to be superior (64.57% vs 63.82% on val). However, these results are not presented clearly in the main tables. Could you elaborate on what was observed and perhaps include this ablation more formally?

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and effective method for multimodal attention that achieves excellent results. The core idea of modeling higher-order potentials is compelling. However, the paper is significantly hampered by poor writing, a tenuous connection to its probabilistic motivation, and ineffective figures. While the quality of the results and the novelty of the core idea are high, the lack of clarity is a major obstacle. I would strongly encourage the authors to undertake a substantial revision to improve the presentation and better formalize their claims.
