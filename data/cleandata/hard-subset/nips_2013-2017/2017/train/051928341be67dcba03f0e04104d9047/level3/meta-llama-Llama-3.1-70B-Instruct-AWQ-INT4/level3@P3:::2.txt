This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that learns high-order correlations between various data modalities, specifically designed for visual question answering (VQA) tasks. The authors introduce a generic multimodal attention formulation that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image, question, and answer. This approach allows for the modeling of interactions between multiple modalities, which is particularly useful in VQA tasks where questions often require the consideration of both visual and textual information.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed attention mechanism, including the data embedding, attention, and decision-making components. The authors also provide a thorough review of related work on attention mechanisms, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches and the benefits of their proposed method.

One of the strengths of this paper is the generic multimodal attention formulation, which could generalize beyond a single task. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the VQA dataset, achieving state-of-the-art performance. The ablation study also provides valuable insights into the contribution of the ternary potential, showing that it yields improved results.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. Firstly, the concept of "attention over answers" is unclear, given that most answers are single tokens, and no visualizations of answer attention are provided. Additionally, the main improvement appears to be driven by the ternary potentials, and without them, the model does not outperform existing 2-modality methods (except HieCoAtt). This raises questions about the robustness of the model and the importance of the ternary potentials.

Furthermore, the paper lacks analysis of failure cases and model failure modes. The authors only provide a single example of a failure case, where the attention of their approach is identical despite two different input questions. This limited analysis does not provide a comprehensive understanding of the model's limitations and potential biases.

Another weakness is the lack of comparison to prior models that use answers as inputs, such as Jabri et al.'s approach. Since the ternary input seems critical to the proposed method, it would be beneficial to compare the performance of the model to other approaches that also utilize answer inputs.

Finally, there are some minor errors and typos in the paper, such as the reporting discrepancy in Table 1, where the authors claim validation but show test-dev/test-std results.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is also well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow and understand.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image, question, and answer. This approach is a significant departure from existing attention mechanisms, which often focus on pairwise interactions between two modalities. The paper also provides a thorough review of related work, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches and the benefits of the proposed method.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in VQA tasks, where questions often require the consideration of both visual and textual information. The proposed attention mechanism has the potential to advance the state of the art in VQA tasks and provide a more robust and effective approach to modeling multimodal interactions.

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the field of VQA and multimodal attention mechanisms. While there are some weaknesses and limitations, the paper provides a thorough and well-written explanation of the proposed approach, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
