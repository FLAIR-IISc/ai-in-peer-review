- Proposes a novel multimodal attention that models unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, linearly combines them, softmaxes to get attention, and uses MCB/MCT for decision — achieves state-of-the-art on VQA.  
- Strength: Novelty/significance — explicitly modeling high-order interactions in the attention mechanism is conceptually interesting and empirically effective.  
- Strength: Thorough evaluation — strong quantitative results and insightful qualitative attention visualizations support the method beyond mere capacity increases.  
- Strength: Qualitative findings — clear shifts between unary, pairwise, and combined attention maps provide intuition and evidence for the model’s behavior.  
- Weakness: Clarity and presentation — writing and organization are jumbled, making the method hard to follow and needing substantial exposition improvements.  
- Weakness (probabilistic framing) — the mean-field/probabilistic motivation is stated but not formalized; the “potentials” and single linear+softmax step make the connection tenuous.  
- Weakness (figures/diagrams) — Figure 2 mixes abstraction and implementation details confusingly; Figure 3 is never referenced and should be fixed.  
- Weakness (originality context) — building blocks (1x1 convs, similarity matrices, MCB/MCT) are from prior work; the main novelty is the architectural formulation combining multi-order potentials and should be clearly positioned as such.  
- Question 1 — What values do the learned weights (α, β, γ) converge to, and do different modalities rely on different potential orders?  
- Question 2 — Why does the 2-modality (image-question) variant underperform prior MCB methods despite architectural similarities — is it embedding size, decision module, or interaction effects?  
- Question 3 — Please elaborate on comparisons between a single MCT unit and a 2-layer MCB decision module and provide more detailed ablations.  
- Recommendation — Revise the paper to improve exposition, either rigorously justify the probabilistic framing or present it purely as motivation, and better situate the architectural contribution relative to prior work.
