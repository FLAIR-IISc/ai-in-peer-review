This paper proposes a novel and generally applicable high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA). The core idea is to model not just pairwise interactions between modalities (image and question) as is common, but also higher-order correlations, specifically introducing ternary potentials encompassing image, question, and answer. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset using this approach.

The paper builds upon the growing body of work leveraging attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks like VQA, image captioning, and machine comprehension (e.g., [8, 15, 26]). Existing attention models are often criticized for entangled representations and task-specificity. This work addresses these limitations by proposing a more generic formulation based on probabilistic modeling with high-order potentials, and utilizing mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities. The authors thoroughly review related work in both image and textual attention, highlighting the novelty of their approach in explicitly modeling higher-order interactions. Comparisons are made to recent methods like Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The high-order attention mechanism, particularly the inclusion of ternary potentials, represents a significant extension to existing attention formulations. The proposed framework is generic and potentially applicable beyond VQA.
* **Performance:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup. The illustrations (Figs. 2 & 3) are helpful in understanding the model architecture.
* **Ablation Study:** The ablation study clearly demonstrates the contribution of the ternary potentials to the overall performance, establishing their importance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **"Attention over Answers":** The concept of "attention over answers" is somewhat unclear given that the VQA dataset typically uses single-token answers. The paper lacks visualizations or detailed explanations of what this attention actually focuses on.
* **Ternary Potential Dependence:** The main performance gains appear to be driven by the inclusion of ternary potentials.  The authors acknowledge that without them, the model does not outperform existing 2-modality methods (except HieCoAtt), requiring further clarification. It’s important to establish the value added *specifically* by the higher-order modeling, and not simply from an increased model capacity.
* **Missing Comparisons:** The paper does not compare to prior work that explicitly incorporates answers as inputs to the model (e.g., Jabri et al. [9]). Given the significant impact of the ternary potentials, which include the answer modality, a direct comparison to methods already utilizing answers is crucial.
* **Limited Failure Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough analysis of failure cases and the model’s failure modes. Understanding *why* the model fails in certain scenarios would provide valuable insights.
* **Minor Issues:** There are minor errors and inconsistencies in the paper (e.g., L38 "mechanism" should be "mechanisms"; L237 claims validation but Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std). 

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a compelling new approach to multimodal attention with a strong emphasis on higher-order correlations. The achieved state-of-the-art results on VQA are noteworthy. However, the reliance on ternary potentials and the lack of comparison to relevant prior work (Jabri et al. [9]) are concerns. The limited analysis of failure cases also weakens the paper. Despite these weaknesses, the generic nature of the proposed framework and the quantitative gains on the 3-modality VQA task justify a positive recommendation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and generic multimodal attention formulation.
* State-of-the-art performance on VQA.
* Clear presentation and well-structured paper.
* Demonstrable improvement from ternary potentials.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Performance heavily reliant on ternary potentials; limited gains without them.
* Lack of comparison to methods explicitly utilizing answers.
* Insufficient analysis of failure cases.
* Minor errors and inconsistencies.

**Confidence:** 7/10

**Recommendation:** Accept. Despite the identified weaknesses, the novelty and performance improvements make this a valuable contribution to the field. I would encourage the authors to address the concerns regarding the dependency on ternary potentials and the lack of comparison to relevant prior work in a future version.
