- Proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks targeting high-order correlations and achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA.  
- Introduces a probabilistic attention model using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to compute attention probabilities.  
- Compares decision modules, specifically Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) vs Multimodal Compact Trilinear (MCT) pooling for combining attended features.  
- Model is well-motivated and shows strong empirical results, demonstrating the utility of high-order attention mechanisms.  
- Experiments are thorough, including quantitative results and qualitative attention visualizations that aid interpretability.  
- Inclusion of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials is a notable contribution, with 3-modality models outperforming 2-modality baselines.  
- Solid comparisons with existing methods (e.g., [7], [15]) are provided.  
- Paper suffers from clarity and organization issues, making key ideas hard to follow.  
- Probabilistic grounding of the attention mechanism is not formalized rigorously.  
- Novelty is limited—largely extends prior intuitions rather than introducing fundamentally new concepts.  
- Figure 2 lacks sufficient detail to explain the architecture; Figure 3 is not referenced and its purpose is unclear.  
- No explicit trinary attention maps are provided, leaving the experimental analysis incomplete.  
- Related work discussion is insufficiently detailed to clearly distinguish contributions from prior high-order attention methods.  
- Question: how do learned linear coefficients in marginalized potentials vary by modality and influence performance?  
- Question: why does the 2-modality Unary+Pairwise model underperform compared to MCB [7], and what is the intuition?  
- Question: observations on sequential MCB layers versus a single MCT layer for the decision head are missing and would clarify trade-offs.  
- Recommendation: weak accept conditional on addressing clarity, formalizing probabilistic links, and providing more comprehensive evaluations (e.g., trinary attention maps).
