Summary
This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning, instantiated for VQA. The model defines unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over words, image regions, and answers; forms marginalized potentials for each modality; and combines them via learned linear coefficients followed by a softmax (Eq. 1) to obtain modality-specific attention distributions. Decision making uses compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) over attended vectors to predict answers. The authors compare against co-attention and MCB baselines, reporting strong quantitative results on VQA and providing qualitative attention visualizations.

Relative to prior work, the method sits between co-attention (e.g., Lu et al.) and bilinear/trilinear pooling approaches (e.g., Fukui et al., Kim et al.). The paper’s novelty lies in explicitly modeling and linearly combining potentials of multiple orders (unary, pairwise, ternary), and in demonstrating that the learned attention distributions differ meaningfully from unary/pairwise maps, which correlates with improved performance.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architecture is sensible and the empirical results are strong. However, the probabilistic framing (potentials, “mean-field inference”) remains largely motivational. The current implementation reduces to a single linear combination of marginalized potentials followed by softmax; no variational objective or inference derivation is provided. Claims tying the approach to CRFs/mean-field should be formalized or softened.
- Empirical support: Quantitative evaluations on VQA test-dev and test-std are thorough, including baselines, model variants (2- vs 3-modality), and decision modules (MCT vs stacked MCB). Qualitative attention maps are informative. The evidence suggests the gains come from better attention rather than mere capacity: attention visualizations sharpen with higher-order terms while model size stays moderate.
- Reproducibility: Most training and architecture details are provided, but several choices affecting conclusions are under-specified (e.g., learned α/β/γ weights, precise data preprocessing/answer vocabulary mapping, and fair capacity-matched comparisons against MCB).

Clarity
The paper is difficult to follow in places. Section 3 mixes architectural and probabilistic narratives; Figure 2 sits at an awkward abstraction level and obscures the unary/pairwise/ternary potential flow; and Figure 3 is not consistently referenced or integrated into the exposition. The notation around Corr2/Corr3 vs θ·,· and the marginalization directions would benefit from a clearer, modular presentation.

Originality
Conceptually, extending bilinear/trilinear interactions to the attention stage with explicit multi-order potentials is interesting and, to my knowledge, not previously reported in this form. That said, core components (outer-product correlations, MCB/MCT for decision making) are established. The paper’s contribution is primarily architectural and empirical rather than theoretical.

Significance
The model achieves competitive-to-state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters and provides interpretable attention diagnostics. The idea of learning to weigh potential orders per modality could generalize beyond VQA. If the probabilistic claims are clarified and the presentation tightened, the impact could be solid for the multimodal/attention community.

Strengths
- Novel high-order attention formulation that is simple to implement and broadly applicable.
- Strong empirical performance on VQA with a relatively compact model.
- Thorough qualitative and quantitative analysis; attention maps are compelling and suggest improvements stem from better attention allocation rather than capacity.
- Interesting empirical observation: clear shifts between unary/pairwise attentions and the final attention; the framework’s ability to modulate potential orders appears useful.

Weaknesses
- Writing and organization are jumbled; figures and text do not always align (Figure 2 too abstract; Figure 3 insufficiently referenced).
- Limited perceived novelty: architecture largely extends MCB/MCT concepts to attention; the precise conceptual contribution over co-attention and bilinear/trilinear models should be clarified.
- Probabilistic framing (“mean-field,” “potentials”) is motivational only; no formal model or derivation. Either provide a proper probabilistic formulation (e.g., CRF with an explicit energy, variational inference) or soften the language.
- Insufficient discussion of why the 2-modality Unary+Pairwise variant underperforms a strong MCB baseline despite similar structure and computational budget.

Questions for the authors
- What values were learned for α, β, γ in Eq. (1)? Do different modalities systematically favor different orders (e.g., image attention relying on pairwise θV,Q, question attention relying on unary θQ)? Please report statistics (means/variances across runs) and, if possible, per-question-type analyses.
- The 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise model underperforms MCB despite architectural similarities. Beyond lower embedding dimension, what is your intuition? Is there a mismatch between where high-order interactions are modeled (attention vs decision)? Any evidence relating this gap to the choice of decision module?
- You mention sequential/stacked MCB heads vs a single MCT decision head but present only limited numbers. Please provide a complete comparison (dev and test, VGG and ResNet backbones), including training stability, convergence speed, and parameter/compute parity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify contributions: explicitly position the work relative to Lu et al.’s (co-)attention and Fukui et al.’s MCB, and articulate what is new (multi-order attentional potentials, learned modality-specific weighting).
- Either formalize the probabilistic model (define an energy with unary/pairwise/ternary factors, show the mean-field update leading to Eq. (1), discuss independence assumptions) or remove probabilistic terminology.
- Revise figures: simplify Figure 2 to emphasize the pipeline for generating unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and their marginalization; ensure Figure 3 is referenced where each module is introduced; add a diagram for Eq. (1)’s linear combination and softmax.
- Report and visualize marginalized ternary attention maps (per modality) alongside unary/pairwise/final attention to substantiate the claimed “notable shift.”
- Provide the learned α/β/γ values and conduct an ablation varying/removing each potential order per modality.
- Tighten comparisons: capacity-match the 2-modality variant to MCB (d=2048), and include fair ablations isolating backbone, embedding size, and decision module.
- Improve writing in Section 3: unify notation, separate the definitions of Corr2/Corr3 from the marginalizations, and reduce redundancy.

Pros
- Strong results with interpretable attention behavior.
- General, modular attention design over multiple orders and modalities.
- Careful qualitative analysis and relevant baselines.

Cons
- Overstated probabilistic framing without formal backing.
- Presentation issues (figures/text alignment, clarity).
- Incremental relative to existing bilinear/trilinear and co-attention literature unless positioned and formalized more clearly.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The empirical strength and the insight that learned mixtures of attention orders improve VQA are compelling; with clearer exposition and a more rigorous treatment (or tempered claims), this could be a solid contribution.
