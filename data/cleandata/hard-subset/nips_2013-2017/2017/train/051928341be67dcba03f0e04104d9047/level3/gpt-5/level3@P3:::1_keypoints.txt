- Introduces a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal VQA using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials.  
- Forms marginalized potentials per modality and linearly combines them with learned coefficients followed by softmax (Eq. 1) to get attention distributions.  
- Uses compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) over attended vectors for answer prediction.  
- Reports strong quantitative results on VQA with informative qualitative attention visualizations.  
- Novelty: explicit multi-order (unary/pairwise/ternary) attentional potentials and learned modality-specific weighting.  
- Core components (outer-product correlations, MCB/MCT) are established; contribution is mainly architectural/empirical rather than theoretical.  
- Technical soundness: sensible architecture and strong results, but probabilistic framing (potentials/mean-field) is largely motivational and lacks formal derivation.  
- Empirical support: thorough quantitative evaluations, model variants, and attention analyses suggesting gains come from improved attention, not just capacity.  
- Reproducibility: most details given, but key choices (learned α/β/γ, preprocessing, answer mapping, fair baselines) are under-specified.  
- Clarity: paper is difficult to follow in places; figures (especially Fig.2 and Fig.3) and notation (Corr2/Corr3 vs θ) need clearer, modular presentation.  
- Originality: interesting extension of bilinear/trilinear interactions to attention, not previously reported in this exact form.  
- Significance: competitive results with fewer parameters and interpretable diagnostics; broader applicability if presentation and claims are tightened.  
- Strength: simple, broadly applicable high-order attention formulation with strong, compact VQA performance.  
- Strength: compelling qualitative evidence that higher-order terms sharpen attention allocation.  
- Weakness: writing and organization are jumbled; figures and text do not always align.  
- Weakness: limited perceived novelty relative to MCB/MCT and co-attention; clearer positioning needed.  
- Weakness: probabilistic language (CRF/mean-field) is overstated without formal model or derivation.  
- Weakness: unclear why 2-modality Unary+Pairwise underperforms a strong MCB baseline despite similar structure.  
- Key question: What values were learned for α, β, γ across modalities and runs; do modalities favor different orders?  
- Key question: Why does the 2-modality Unary+Pairwise model underperform MCB; is it due to embedding dimension or decision-module placement?  
- Key question: Provide full comparisons of stacked MCB heads vs single MCT head across backbones, training stability, convergence, and compute/parameter parity.  
- Suggestion: Clarify contributions relative to co-attention and MCB and explicitly state what is new.  
- Suggestion: Either formalize the probabilistic model (energy, mean-field derivation) or remove/soften probabilistic terminology.  
- Suggestion: Revise figures to clearly depict potential generation, marginalization, and Eq.(1) combination; reference Fig.3 properly.  
- Suggestion: Report learned α/β/γ, visualize marginalized ternary maps, perform ablations removing each potential order, and capacity-match comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept — empirically compelling but needs clearer exposition and a more rigorous or tempered probabilistic treatment.
