- Proposes a high-order attention model for multimodal tasks that computes unary, pairwise, and trinary potentials, linearly combines them with learned weights, applies softmax, and achieves state-of-the-art on VQA.

- Novelty/performance: Explicit modeling of unary/pairwise/trinary potentials is a novel, intuitive way to capture inter-modal dependencies and yields strong empirical results.

- Experiments: Provides solid quantitative comparisons against strong baselines and extensive qualitative visualizations.

- Qualitative insight: Figure 5 shows final attention is a non-trivial synthesis of unary and pairwise signals (unary highlights objects, pairwise links words to regions); inspecting marginalized trinary potentials would be useful.

- Clarity/presentation: Writing is jumbled and the connection between high-level motivation and low-level implementation is unclear; needs major reorganization.

- Limited perceived novelty: Architectural components largely extend prior work (e.g., MCB); the paper should better position its contribution as a novel attention calculation rather than a wholly new architecture.

- Tenuous probabilistic connection: Use of probabilistic/mean-field language is only motivational and not formalized; the linear weighted-sum + softmax is a weakly supported "single mean-field iteration" claim.

- Figures and referencing: Figure 2 is too abstract to convey the core contribution; Figure 3 appears unreferenced in the text—both reduce clarity and need revision.

- Question: What are the learned coefficients (α, β, γ) in the potential combination, and do they show modality-specific patterns?

- Question: Why does the 2-modality Unary+Pairwise model underperform MCB—is it embedding dimensionality, decision-module differences, or other factors?

- Question: Please elaborate and present the ablation comparing sequential 2-layer MCB vs single MCT decision head for the 3-modality model (reported val scores not clearly shown).

- Overall: The method is interesting and effective with compelling core ideas, but its impact is hampered by poor writing, an overstated probabilistic framing, and ineffective figures—requires substantial revision.
