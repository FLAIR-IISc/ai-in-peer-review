This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, demonstrated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The central idea is to formulate attention as a probabilistic model combining potentials of different orders. The model computes unary potentials for each modality (image, question, answer), pairwise potentials for each pair of modalities, and a third-order (ternary) potential for the joint interaction of all three. These potentials are then linearly combined and converted to attention distributions using a single mean-field inference step. The resulting attended feature vectors are fused for final answer prediction. The authors show that this high-order attention model, particularly the inclusion of ternary potentials involving the answer modality, achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset. This work extends prior co-attention models (e.g., Lu et al., 2016) and bilinear pooling methods (e.g., Fukui et al., 2016) by proposing a more structured and extensible probabilistic framework for capturing interactions across multiple modalities.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound. The formulation of attention using potentials and mean-field inference is a valid and interesting approach. The claims of state-of-the-art performance are well-supported by experimental results on the standard VQA test-dev and test-std benchmarks (Table 1). The ablation study demonstrating the performance gain from the third-order potential is a valuable piece of evidence for the model's design.

However, the evaluation could be more thorough.
1.  The contribution of the novel 2-modality formulation (unary + pairwise potentials) is not clearly established. Table 1 shows that the 2-modality version of this model (63.8%) underperforms several existing 2-modality models like MCB (68.9%) and MLB (68.6%). While the authors mention this might be due to a lower embedding dimension, this makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed probabilistic formulation is superior to existing co-attention mechanisms, or if the performance gain of the 3-modality model is primarily due to the inclusion of the answer as an input, a strategy also explored by others.
2.  The paper presents one failure case (Fig. 8) but lacks a broader analysis of the model's failure modes. Understanding when and why high-order attention fails would strengthen the paper.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The figures, particularly Figure 3 illustrating the potential modules, are helpful for understanding the architecture.

However, a key concept lacks clarity: "attention over answers". In VQA, candidate answers are often single words or short n-grams. It is unclear what it means to compute an attention distribution over the components of an answer set in this context, especially since the answers are mutually exclusive candidates for a classification task. The paper does not provide visualizations or a detailed explanation for this part of the model, which is central to the ternary potential.

There are also minor issues:
*   There are a few typos (e.g., L38 "mechanism" should be "mechanisms").
*   L237 states that MCT vs. 2-layer MCB were evaluated on the 'val' dataset, but Table 1 reports results on 'test-dev' and 'test-std'. This should be clarified for consistency.

**Originality**
The idea of modeling multimodal attention through explicit high-order potentials within a probabilistic framework is novel. While pairwise co-attention has been explored (Lu et al., 2016), the explicit formulation and combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials is a new contribution. The framework's design, which allows for trivial extension to k modalities, is also an original and valuable concept.

However, since the main performance driver appears to be the inclusion of the answer modality, the paper would be stronger if it explicitly compared its approach to other works that also use answers as an input. For instance, Jabri et al. (2016, "Revisiting visual question answering baselines") also use answers but argue for a simpler binary classification framing and question the need for complex attention. A discussion of how this work's findings relate to theirs would better position its contribution.

**Significance**
The paper's results are significant, achieving state-of-the-art on a challenging and popular benchmark. The proposed high-order attention framework is general and could inspire new approaches for other tasks involving three or more modalities (e.g., video question answering with subtitles). The idea of learning the weights of different-order potentials is also an interesting and potentially impactful mechanism for building more flexible multimodal models. Researchers and practitioners in VQA and related fields are likely to build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Proposes a novel, principled, and generalizable framework for multimodal attention using high-order potentials.
2.  Achieves state-of-the-art results on the competitive VQA benchmark.
3.  The paper is clearly written and the core ideas are well-communicated.
4.  The ablation study effectively demonstrates the positive impact of the proposed ternary potential.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  The contribution of the 2-modality version of the model is unclear, as it does not outperform existing 2-modality methods. This obscures whether the main benefit comes from the novel formulation or simply from using the answer as an input.
2.  The concept of "attention over answers" is poorly motivated and explained, despite being a core component of the model's best-performing variant.
3.  The paper lacks a comparison to relevant prior work that also uses answers as input (e.g., Jabri et al., 2016).
4.  The analysis of model behavior, especially its failures, is very limited.

**Questions for Authors:**
1.  Could you please clarify the mechanism and intuition behind "attention over answers"? How is the attention distribution computed and used when the answer space consists of thousands of distinct, often single-word, classes? Visualizations of this attention would be very helpful.
2.  Could you elaborate on the performance of your 2-modality model? Is the performance gap with methods like MCB due solely to feature dimensions, or does it suggest that the pairwise attention formulation is not as effective as existing co-attention methods? A controlled comparison using the same feature dimensions would be necessary to de-confound these factors.
3.  The ablation in Section 4.3 comparing MCT (63.82%) and 2-layer MCB (64.57%) seems to be for the 3-modality model on the validation set. How do these numbers relate to the 2-modality model's result of 63.8% in Table 1? Please clarify the experimental settings for these different results.
4.  How does your work conceptually differ from Jabri et al. (2016), who also feed the answer as an input but frame the problem as binary classification and argue against complex attention mechanisms?
