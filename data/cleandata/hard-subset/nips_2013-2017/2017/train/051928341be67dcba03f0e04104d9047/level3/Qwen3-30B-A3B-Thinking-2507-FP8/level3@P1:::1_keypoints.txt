- Proposes a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal VQA that models unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer.  
- Builds on existing attention methods (Lu et al., 2016; Fukui et al., 2016) by extending to higher-order interactions and offering a probabilistic motivation.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art empirical performance on VQA, outperforming 2-modality baselines (64.57% vs 63.82% on validation).  
- Attention maps and qualitative examples convincingly show the model shifts focus based on question content, supporting attention-driven gains rather than mere capacity increases.  
- Strong experimental design with comparisons between decision units (MCT vs 2-layer MCB) and well-designed ablation showing benefit of 3-modality (high-order) attention.  
- Writing and presentation are confusing in places, particularly the probabilistic interpretation of the attention mechanism.  
- The probabilistic link between "high-order potentials" and the model is motivational only and not formally established, requiring either rigor or softened claims.  
- Unclear contribution statement: architecture is essentially an extension of MCB to higher orders but novelty is not clearly articulated.  
- Several figures are poorly used—Figure 2 is overly abstract and Figure 3 is not referenced—hindering clarity of the proposed innovation.  
- Missing reporting of learned coefficients (αi, βi, γi) for potential combinations, which would clarify the relative importance of potentials.  
- Lack of explanation for why the 2-modality Unary+Pairwise model underperforms MCB despite similar architecture.  
- Mention of sequential MCB vs single MCT decision heads lacks accompanying results or analysis.  
- Overall: empirically strong and promising approach, but contribution and impact are obscured by presentation, unclear novelty, and missing analyses; needs clearer exposition and additional details.
