The paper "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering" proposes a novel attention mechanism that learns high-order correlations between various data modalities. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the task of visual question answering (VQA), achieving state-of-the-art performance on the standard VQA dataset.

The main idea of the paper is to extend the traditional attention mechanism to capture high-order correlations between multiple data modalities. The authors propose a probabilistic model that uses unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to compute attention probabilities. The unary potentials capture the importance of each feature, while the pairwise and ternary potentials capture correlations between two and three modalities, respectively.

The authors evaluate their approach on the VQA real-image test-dev and test-std datasets, achieving state-of-the-art performance. They also provide qualitative results, showing that their attention mechanism attends to reasonable pixel and question locations. The authors also compare their approach to existing methods, such as Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], and show that their approach outperforms these methods.

The paper has several strengths. First, the proposed attention mechanism is novel and achieves good empirical performance. Second, the authors provide thorough qualitative and quantitative experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach. Third, the paper shows a notable shift between unary/pairwise attentions and final attention, which suggests that the attention mechanism is able to capture complex correlations between modalities.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, the writing and presentation are jumbled and need improvement. Second, the probabilistic connection is only motivational and not formalized, which makes it difficult to understand the theoretical foundations of the approach. Third, the architecture extends MCB ideas to higher/lower orders, but the contributions are not clearly clarified. Fourth, Figure 2 is at an awkward abstraction level and should be simplified to emphasize unary/pairwise/trinary potential generation. Fifth, Figure 3 is never referenced in the text.

In terms of novelty, the paper extends the traditional attention mechanism to capture high-order correlations between multiple data modalities. However, the contributions of the paper are not clearly clarified, and it is not clear how the approach differs from existing methods.

The paper is well-organized, but the writing is weak and confusing. The authors should improve the writing and presentation to make the paper more readable. Additionally, the authors should provide more details about the learned values for the linear coefficients combining marginalized potentials and explain why different modalities favor different potential orders.

In terms of significance, the paper is important because it proposes a novel attention mechanism that achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA task. The approach has the potential to be applied to other tasks that require attention mechanisms, such as machine translation and image captioning.

Overall, the paper is a good contribution to the field of visual question answering, but it needs improvement in terms of writing and presentation. The authors should also clarify the contributions of the paper and provide more details about the learned values for the linear coefficients combining marginalized potentials.
