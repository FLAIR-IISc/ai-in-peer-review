- Introduces a high-order attention mechanism for VQA that captures arbitrary-order correlations between modalities and reports state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.
- Represents attention as a probabilistic model with high-order potentials and uses mean-field inference to obtain marginal attention weights, allowing extension to multiple modalities.
- Strong empirical results with thorough experiments, ablation studies, and attention visualizations demonstrating meaningful learned representations.
- Writing is convoluted and presentation lacks clarity, making it hard to assess contributions and novelty.
- Introduction lacks a concise contribution summary; the detailed mechanism description is overly complex and could be simplified.
- Figure 2 is too abstract and should explicitly show unary/pairwise/ternary potential generation; Figure 3 is never referenced.
- Novelty is unclear and may be incremental relative to bilinear pooling approaches like MCB; the differences from prior work need clearer articulation.
- The probabilistic motivation feels weak and either needs formalization or the language should be toned down.
- Missing experimental details: learned values for linear coefficients (αi, βi, γi) that combine marginalized potentials are not reported.
- The 2-modality model (unary + pairwise) underperforms MCB despite similar architecture—intuition and explanation relating to decision modules are lacking.
- Quantitative results and observations for different decision heads (sequential MCB vs. single MCT) are not provided.
- Arguments for acceptance: SOTA benchmark performance, novel higher-order attention extension, thorough evaluation and useful visualizations.
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear writing, incremental novelty, weak probabilistic framing, and missing experimental details.
- Overall assessment: weak accept contingent on major revision to improve clarity, better articulate novelty, and address the raised questions.
