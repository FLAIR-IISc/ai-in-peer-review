- Proposes high-order attention for multimodal VQA using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials combined by a learned linear mixture and compact polynomial sketching (MCB/MCT).
- Builds on co-attention and bilinear/trilinear fusion literature but applies high-order interactions explicitly at the attention level.
- Soundness: unary/pairwise/ternary definitions and softmax attention are clear, but the "mean-field inference" claim is overstated and not formalized.
- Empirics: strong multiple-choice VQA results with fewer parameters and insightful qualitative attention maps.
- Ablations: includes some analyses (2- vs 3-modality, MCT vs stacked MCB) but needs more systematic ablations.
- Efficiency: computing full ternary tensor C3 can be large; practicality currently tied to small na (≈18) in multiple-choice—cost for open-ended or longer questions unclear.
- Clarity: readable overall but probabilistic framing, notation, and presentation (Figures 2 and 3) are muddled and need streamlining.
- Figure issues: Figure 2 abstracts at the wrong level; Figure 3 is not adequately referenced or explained in the text.
- Writing: minor typos, repeated words, and inconsistent notation require editorial cleanup.
- Originality: moderate — novel to apply high-order potentials at attention, but conceptually close to existing bilinear/trilinear fusion and co-attention.
- Significance: high — general, modular framework with potential use beyond VQA and promising empirical/qualitative gains.
- Question: report learned α, β, γ (means/variances) and analyze modality-specific and question-type variations.
- Question: control for embedding dimension to disentangle 2-modality performance drop from decision-head capacity.
- Question: provide comprehensive MCT vs stacked MCB comparisons across backbones, seeds, parameters, FLOPs, and latency.
- Question: visualize marginalized ternary potentials per modality alongside unary and pairwise maps.
- Question: discuss applicability to open-ended VQA since ternary attention needs candidate answers.
- Question: report training stability, variance across runs, ensembling, and early stopping criteria.
- Suggestion: either formalize the CRF/mean-field probabilistic view (with iterative updates) or remove the probabilistic claim.
- Suggestion: stronger ablations — remove specific potential orders, vary mixture (fixed vs learned, per-modality vs shared), and control embedding sizes.
- Suggestion: report learned coefficients and per-question-type performance to show when high-order attention helps.
- Suggestion: simplify and clarify figures; move engineering details (tensor sketch, hyperparameters) to appendix and include complexity analysis.
- Suggestion: consider reporting attention–human alignment metrics (VQA-HAT, Visual7W) to support interpretability claims.
- Pros: clear, modular high-order attention mechanism with strong VQA performance and parameter efficiency.
- Pros: thorough qualitative analyses showing task-dependent attention shifts.
- Pros: general and extensible framework for multiple modalities.
- Cons: probabilistic motivation is not formalized and may overclaim.
- Cons: incremental novelty relative to prior co-attention and bilinear/trilinear fusion work.
- Cons: presentation issues, missing details on learned weights and ablations.
- Cons: evaluation scope limited (mainly multiple-choice) and decision-head interactions not fully analyzed.
