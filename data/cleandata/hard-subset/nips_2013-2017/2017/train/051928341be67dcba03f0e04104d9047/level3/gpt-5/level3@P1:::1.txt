Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning, instantiated on VQA. The core idea is to model unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image, question, and answers) and to obtain marginal attentions via a single “mean-field” style combination: a learned linear mixture of marginalized unary/pairwise/ternary potentials followed by a softmax. Pairwise potentials are built from a learned similarity matrix (corr2) between per-token question features and per-location image features; ternary potentials are built analogously as a corr3 tensor over image, question, and answer features. The attended modality vectors are then fused for decision making using compact tensor sketching (MCB for pairwise, MCT for ternary, or stacked MCB), to avoid the parameter blow-up of explicit outer products. On VQA (multiple-choice), the approach achieves state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters than strong baselines.

The work is related to co-attention and bilinear pooling methods for VQA (e.g., Lu et al. 2016 co-attention; Fukui et al. 2016 MCB; Kim et al. 2017 MLB/Hadamard low-rank bilinear; Nam et al. 2016 dual attention; Kim et al. 2017 structured attention). It generalizes pairwise co-attention by explicitly incorporating higher-order dependencies and by treating attention as combinations of potentials across modalities. The decision module leverages tensor sketching ideas (Pham and Pagh 2013) as in MCB, extended to trilinear pooling.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean unification: decouples potential estimation (unary/pairwise/ternary) from marginalization via a shared linear mixing, providing a general template that could scale to more modalities.
- Strong empirical results on VQA with fewer parameters than certain baselines; thorough reporting of training details and ablations on decision fusion (MCB vs MCT) are appreciated.
- Qualitative analyses are informative: the progression from unary to pairwise to final attention often aligns with intuition (unary attends to salient regions; pairwise focuses on question-relevant regions; final attention sharpens further). This suggests the gains stem from improved attention rather than sheer capacity.
- The proposal is practical: uses standard CNN/LSTM embeddings, simple 1×1 convs for potentials, and sketching-based fusion to control complexity.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement:
- Novelty is somewhat incremental relative to prior co-attention and bilinear pooling work. The main new element is the explicit ternary potential and its linear combination with pairwise/unary ones, plus the “mean-field” framing. Clarify and sharpen the specific contributions beyond “extend MCB-style ideas to higher order” and “combine potentials linearly.”
- The probabilistic interpretation is only motivational. If claiming mean-field inference over a factor graph/CRF, please formalize the underlying energy, factorization, and show how Eq. (1) is obtained as a mean-field update (or, alternatively, soften/remove the probabilistic language and present the combination as a learned gating of potentials).
- Clarity/presentation issues:
  - The manuscript is at times jumbled; notation and shapes (e.g., of weights used for marginalization) are hard to follow. A schematic that isolates the three potential generators and their marginalizations would help.
  - Figure 2 sits at an awkward abstraction level; simplify to emphasize how unary/pairwise/ternary potentials are formed and combined into attentions.
  - Figure 3 is not properly referenced/explained in the text; ensure all figures are called out where relevant, with consistent notation.
  - Please define Corr2/Corr3 and their tensor dimensions where first introduced; explicitly state how marginalization over C3 is implemented efficiently.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity. The ternary correlation tensor C3 is potentially very large. Provide complexity and memory considerations, batching strategy, and any approximations (e.g., separable parameterization of marginalization weights).
- Scope/metrics: Results are reported for multiple-choice VQA; please clarify performance on open-ended VQA (if applicable) and/or Visual7W to support generality.

Quality:
- The method is technically sound as a neural architecture, with solid baselines and training details. However, the “mean-field” claim is not derived; either add a derivation or present the combination as heuristic/learned mixing. Ablations are helpful, though further ones (see questions below) would strengthen the causal story.

Clarity:
- Writing can be improved substantially: streamline notation, ensure equations are consistent, and restructure the method section around “potential construction → marginalization → decision fusion.” Move some design details to an appendix, and ensure all figures are referenced and interpretable standalone.

Originality:
- Moderate. High-order attention and trilinear pooling have been hinted at in prior literature; the paper’s contribution is to systematize them as potentials and demonstrate practical gains.

Significance:
- Empirical gains on a major benchmark and a modular design likely to be adopted. The attention visualizations provide insight, which is valuable for the community.

Questions for the authors:
- Learned mixing coefficients: What values do the learned α, β, γ take? Do different modalities systematically weight different potential orders (e.g., image favoring pairwise with question; answers favoring unary/ternary)? A breakdown by question type (Yes/No, Number, Other) would be informative.
- Ternary attention maps: Please show marginalized ternary attentions over each modality and compare to unary/pairwise to verify the added value of three-way interactions.
- 2-modal Unary+Pairwise vs MCB: Why does your 2-modality variant underperform MCB despite similar capacity? Is the gap attributable to decision fusion (MCB captures multiplicative interactions directly), or to differences in the attention pipeline? A controlled study holding the decision head fixed would help.
- Sequential MCB vs single MCT: You mention both but do not report clear comparative results across datasets; please provide a consistent evaluation (test-dev/test-std), and discuss when stacked MCB is preferable to MCT.
- Efficiency: How is C3 computed/stored in practice? Are the marginalization weights wiv,ia, wiq,ia factorized or learned as 1D kernels? What is the measured training/inference overhead of including ternary potentials?

Suggestions:
- Clarify and, if possible, formalize the probabilistic framing; otherwise reduce overclaims and focus on the empirical utility of mixing potentials.
- Tighten the narrative around contribution: “We propose a general k-order attention potential framework with learned mixing and show that including ternary potentials + compact trilinear/bilinear fusion improves SOTA on VQA with fewer parameters.”
- Add ablations: remove each potential type; vary mixing (fixed vs learned); report per-category results; compare ResNet vs VGG embeddings for fairness.
- Improve figures and cross-references; add examples of success/failure where ternary attention helps/hurts.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong results on a key benchmark with a compact, generally applicable design.
- Clear qualitative evidence that higher-order attention sharpens focus.
- Practical recipe (potentials + sketching) that others can build on.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Modest novelty over existing co-attention/bilinear pooling methods.
- Probabilistic interpretation is not substantiated.
- Writing and presentation issues hinder clarity.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the contributions, tightening the exposition, and either formalizing or tempering the probabilistic claims.
