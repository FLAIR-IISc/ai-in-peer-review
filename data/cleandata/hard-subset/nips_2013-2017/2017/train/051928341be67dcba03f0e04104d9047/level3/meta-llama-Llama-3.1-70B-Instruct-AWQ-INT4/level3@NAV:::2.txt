This paper proposes a novel, generic multimodal attention formulation for Visual Question Answering (VQA) that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image, question, and answer. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their high-order attention mechanism on the VQA dataset, achieving state-of-the-art performance.

Strengths of the paper include its clear writing, well-organized structure, and the generic nature of the proposed attention formulation, which could generalize beyond a single task. The ablation study provides valuable insights into the contribution of the ternary potential, and the results show a significant improvement over existing methods.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement. The concept of "attention over answers" is unclear, especially since most answers are single tokens, and no visualizations of answer attention are provided. The main improvement appears to be driven by the ternary potentials, and without them, the model does not outperform existing 2-modality methods. The authors should clarify the contribution of the ternary potentials and provide a more detailed analysis of the results.

Additionally, the paper lacks a comparison to prior models that use answers as inputs, such as Jabri et al.'s work. The authors should also provide an analysis of failure cases and model failure modes to better understand the limitations of their approach.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. However, the authors could improve the clarity of their writing by providing more detailed explanations of their methods and results.

Regarding originality, the paper proposes a novel attention formulation that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. While the idea of using high-order potentials is not new, the authors' application of this concept to VQA is innovative and contributes to the field.

The significance of the paper lies in its state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset and its potential to generalize to other multimodal tasks. The authors' approach could inspire new research directions in multimodal attention and VQA.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel, generic multimodal attention formulation that achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.
* The ablation study provides valuable insights into the contribution of the ternary potential.
* The paper is well-written, and the structure is clear and easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The concept of "attention over answers" is unclear, and no visualizations of answer attention are provided.
* The main improvement appears to be driven by the ternary potentials, and without them, the model does not outperform existing 2-modality methods.
* The paper lacks a comparison to prior models that use answers as inputs.
* The authors should provide an analysis of failure cases and model failure modes.

Overall, I lean towards accepting the paper due to its generic framework and quantitative 3-modality SOTA gains, despite the unsatisfactory response on 2-modality comparisons.
