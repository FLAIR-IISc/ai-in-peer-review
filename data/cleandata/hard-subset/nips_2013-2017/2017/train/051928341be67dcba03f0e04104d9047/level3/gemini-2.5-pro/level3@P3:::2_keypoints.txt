- Proposes a generic high-order attention framework for multimodal tasks that models attention as probabilistic inference over unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials.  
- Demonstrated on VQA by linearly combining these potentials and using softmax to produce attention vectors fused for classification.  
- Novelty/significance: elegant, principled formulation that generalizes to >3 modalities and explicitly models three-way interactions.  
- Empirical quality: ablation study shows the ternary potential is the primary driver of performance gains.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written with a clear description of architecture, potentials, and experiments.  
- Major weakness: “attention over answers” is poorly motivated and not well explained or visualized, making it hard to interpret.  
- Weakness: 2-modality baseline underperforms prior 2-modality methods, suggesting gains largely come from adding the answer modality.  
- Weakness: missing comparisons to prior work that conditions on answers (e.g., Jabri et al.), so contribution of ternary formulation vs. answer conditioning is unclear.  
- Weakness: lacks deeper error analysis and more failure cases to understand model limitations.  
- Quality issue: multiple minor errors and reporting inconsistencies (typos, dataset split claims, and inconsistent result numbers) undermine confidence in experiments.  
- Post-rebuttal: authors claim better alignment with human attention but provide no quantitative comparison; rebuttal did not satisfactorily address 2-modality concerns.  
- Summary: introduces an interesting, generalizable high-order attention framework with SOTA 3-modality results, but clarity, baseline comparisons, and reporting issues remain; reviewer leans toward acceptance.
