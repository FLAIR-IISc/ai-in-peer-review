

# Review for NIPS 2017

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors introduce a probabilistic framework that models attention through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer modalities. Unlike prior work that primarily focuses on pairwise interactions (e.g., image-question), their approach explicitly models higher-order correlations, particularly the ternary interaction between all three modalities. This builds upon recent attention mechanisms in VQA (Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25], Fukui et al. [7]) but extends them by incorporating a more general probabilistic model for attention that can be applied to any number of modalities.

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach on the standard VQA dataset, achieving state-of-the-art performance. The authors provide a clear theoretical formulation of their attention mechanism and include ablation studies showing the contribution of each potential type.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Framework**: The paper presents a generalizable multimodal attention framework that could potentially be applied beyond VQA to other multimodal tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Strong Ablation Study**: The authors effectively demonstrate the contribution of ternary potentials through ablation studies, showing that the 3-modality model significantly outperforms 2-modality variants.

3. **Clear Writing**: The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and helpful illustrations of the attention mechanisms.

4. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The approach achieves SOTA performance on the VQA benchmark, which demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Unclear "Attention over Answers"**: The paper claims to model attention over answers (A), but most answers are single tokens, making the concept of "attention" over them unclear. The paper provides no visualizations of answer attention, which would have helped clarify this aspect.

2. **Incomplete Comparison with 2-Modality Methods**: The main performance improvement appears to be driven by the ternary potentials. Without them, the model does not outperform existing 2-modality methods (except HieCoAtt), and the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this ternary approach is superior to previous methods that also incorporate answer information (e.g., Jabri et al. [9], "Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines").

3. **Lack of Failure Analysis**: The paper lacks analysis of failure cases and model failure modes, which would have provided valuable insights into the limitations of the approach.

4. **Reporting Inconsistencies**: The paper contains minor errors (e.g., "mechanism" vs. "mechanisms" in L38) and a reporting discrepancy (L237 claims validation results but Table 1 shows test-dev/test-std results). Additionally, the MCB vs. MCT comparison numbers appear inconsistent with Table 1 (68.4% vs. 69.4% vs. 68.6%).

5. **Inadequate Comparison to Answer-Inclusion Methods**: The paper should have compared more directly with prior models that use answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al.), as the ternary input seems critical to their approach.

## Evaluation on Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound framework with a clear probabilistic interpretation. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of ternary potentials. However, the comparison with existing methods is not thorough enough, particularly regarding 2-modality approaches. The lack of failure analysis and inconsistent reporting slightly weaken the quality assessment.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with helpful figures illustrating the attention mechanisms. The mathematical formulation is presented clearly, though the unclear "attention over answers" concept slightly detracts from the clarity.

**Originality**: The core idea of modeling high-order attention potentials is novel for VQA, extending previous work on pairwise attention. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work that incorporates answer information (e.g., Jabri et al.), which limits the perceived originality.

**Significance**: The proposed framework is generalizable beyond VQA, which is significant for the field. The SOTA results on VQA demonstrate practical value. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the fact that the main improvement comes from the ternary potential, which isn't sufficiently justified compared to existing methods that also incorporate answer information.

## Final Recommendation

The paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing a generalizable high-order attention framework that achieves SOTA performance on VQA. The main strength is the novel approach to multimodal attention that can be extended to any number of modalities. While the paper has some weaknesses in comparison to prior work and reporting inconsistencies, these are outweighed by the novelty of the framework and the strong empirical results.

The authors' post-rebuttal clarification about better correlation with human attention maps is helpful, though the MCB vs. MCT comparison remains confusing. The framework's potential for broader application beyond VQA significantly enhances its value.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the reporting inconsistencies and clarify the "attention over answers" concept. The paper's generalizable framework and SOTA results on VQA make it a strong candidate for NIPS 2017.
