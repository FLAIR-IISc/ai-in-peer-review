Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a generic multimodal attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) representations. Attention is treated probabilistically: unary potentials capture the importance of features within a modality; pairwise potentials capture correlations (via learned similarity matrices) between modalities (e.g., V–Q, V–A, Q–A); and a ternary potential captures joint V–Q–A dependencies. A single mean-field iteration linearly combines marginalized potentials per modality followed by a softmax to obtain attention distributions PV, PQ, and PA. The attended vectors are then fused for decision making using compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT). On VQA (multiple-choice), the 3-modality model achieves state-of-the-art performance; ablations indicate that ternary interactions are the main driver of the gains.

Relative to prior VQA attention work, including co-attention (Lu et al., NIPS’16), stacked/hierarchical attention (Yang et al.; Xu et al.), and bilinear pooling (Fukui et al., MCB; Kim et al., low-rank bilinear), this paper’s novelty is an explicit high-order potential view with a unified mechanism that in principle extends to an arbitrary number of modalities. It is loosely related to structured attention (Kim et al., 2017) but differs in its factorization (unary/pairwise/ternary) and mean-field combination. The use of answer inputs relates to “Revisiting VQA Baselines” (Jabri et al.), and the paper should compare directly to such models.

Strengths:
- A clean, generic attention formulation that explicitly decomposes and combines unary, pairwise, and ternary terms; conceptually extensible beyond VQA.
- Strong quantitative results on VQA MC with a relatively compact model; ablations clearly show the value of ternary potentials.
- Clear exposition and diagrams; intuitive qualitative illustrations of image/question attention.
- Sensible design of the decision fusion layer (MCB/MCT) and a fair ablation between the two.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- The notion of “attention over answers” is under-specified. Given that most answers are short and often single tokens, it is unclear how PA is meaningfully learned/used, and no visualizations of answer attention are provided.
- The main empirical gains seem to arise only when the ternary potential (i.e., using answers as inputs) is used. The 2-modality variant does not outperform several strong 2-modality baselines (except HieCoAtt). This raises the question of whether the attention formulation per se is superior, or whether conditioning on answers is the main factor.
- Missing direct comparisons to prior models that also feed answer candidates (e.g., Jabri et al.) under the same splits/settings.
- Limited analysis of failure modes; one qualitative failure is shown, but a systematic error breakdown is missing (by question type, answer length/frequency, etc.).
- Reporting inconsistencies and minor typos (examples below), and confusion about evaluation splits (val vs. test-dev/test-std).
- Computational aspects of ternary correlations (C3) are not discussed: complexity/memory scaling with nq, nv, na, and any approximations beyond 1×1 convs.

Quality:
Overall technically sound. The probabilistic interpretation with a single mean-field step is reasonable and pragmatic. Claims are supported with quantitative SOTA on VQA MC and with ablations showing the contribution of ternary potentials and the MCB/MCT choice. However, the empirical story for the 2-modality case is weaker, and baselines that include answers as inputs are missing. More rigorous analysis of efficiency and sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., d, na) would strengthen the work.

Clarity:
Generally well written and organized. The model description is clear, and the figures are helpful. Some sections need clarification: what PA represents in practice, how C3 is computed efficiently, and consistent reporting of splits/metrics. Minor typos noted below.

Originality:
Moderate. High-order multimodal interactions are known in bilinear pooling, but the explicit decomposition into learnable unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and mean-field marginalization across modalities is a novel and useful perspective. The extension to k modalities is conceptually straightforward.

Significance:
High for VQA and potentially broader multimodal tasks (image captioning, VQG, machine comprehension). The SOTA gains on VQA MC and the generality of the formulation suggest others might build on this. The impact would be stronger with clearer evidence that the approach improves even without answer conditioning and with more comprehensive comparisons.

Questions for the authors:
- Please clarify how “attention over answers” is defined and learned. Are multi-word answers handled as n-grams only? Can you visualize PA or provide statistics (e.g., entropy over choices) and show that it contributes beyond a learned bias toward frequent answers?
- How do you compute/store the ternary tensor C3 in practice? What are the time/memory costs as a function of nq, nv, na? Any low-rank or sparsity tricks used?
- Can you add a direct comparison to Jabri et al. and related answer-conditioned baselines under identical settings?
- You state that attention maps correlate better with human maps; please provide the metric (e.g., rank-correlation, IoU), the protocol, and comparisons to strong baselines like MCB.
- The MCB vs. MCT numbers post-rebuttal appear inconsistent with Table 1 (you report 68.4% vs 69.4%, while Table 1 lists 68.6% for MCB). Please reconcile.

Minor comments and typos:
- L38: “mechanism” → “mechanisms.”
- L237: You mention validation, but Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std; please align the text and the table.
- Various small grammatical slips (e.g., duplicated “and” in [23]; “threeway” vs “ternary” terminology—standardize).

Pros for acceptance:
- Generic, extensible multimodal attention framework with a clear probabilistic formulation.
- Strong SOTA on VQA MC driven by ternary interactions; ablations are informative.
- Clear writing and useful visualizations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on answer-conditioning for gains; 2-modality variant not competitive with strongest baselines.
- Missing comparisons to prior answer-conditioned baselines.
- Limited analysis of efficiency and failure modes; some reporting inconsistencies.

Post-rebuttal update:
The authors clarified several points but did not satisfactorily address the 2-modality comparison or provide a rigorous baseline including answer inputs. They reported improved human-attention correlation, but comparisons to SOTA remain unclear. There is also confusion in the reported MCB vs. MCT numbers. Despite these, I lean to accept based on the generality of the framework and the demonstrated 3-modality SOTA gains.

Overall recommendation: 6 (weak accept)
Confidence: 3/5
