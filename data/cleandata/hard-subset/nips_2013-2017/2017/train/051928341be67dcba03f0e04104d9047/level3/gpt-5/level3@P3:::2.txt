Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a generic multimodal attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions over image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) representations. Potentials from each order are linearly combined and normalized via a single mean-field iteration to yield attentions PV, PQ, and PA. Decision making uses tensor sketching: either Multimodal Compact Trilinear pooling (MCT) over three attended vectors or a more expressive two-layer Multimodal Compact Bilinear pooling (MCB) stack. The approach is conceptually grounded in probabilistic graphical models and high-order potentials, while remaining computationally feasible via sketching.

Relative to prior VQA attention work, the method subsumes unary attention (e.g., SAN), pairwise co-attention over two modalities (e.g., HieCoAtt, MCB), and extends naturally to three modalities. It also connects to structured attention and bilinear pooling methods, but provides a unified formulation that can scale to higher orders. The paper cites and compares to Lu et al. (HieCoAtt) and Fukui et al. (MCB), and discusses work that includes answers as inputs (Jabri et al.). The contribution is well-positioned within the VQA literature and should be applicable to other multimodal tasks.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction of unary/pairwise/ternary potentials, the linear combination with learnable weights, and the single-step mean-field interpretation are sound and clearly specified. The decision modules are standard and well-motivated.
- Empirical support: Quantitative results on VQA MC test-dev/test-std are strong when using the full three-modality model. The ablation indicating the contribution of ternary potentials is convincing and central to the paper’s claims. Qualitative visualizations for image/question attentions are informative.
- Gaps: The 2-modality instantiation underperforms several published 2-modality baselines, with the paper attributing this to reduced embedding dimensionality. This confounds the fairness of comparisons; a like-for-like 2-modality baseline (feature dimension, backbone, and classifier capacity) would strengthen the case. The role of answer-attention is not clearly explained or visualized. Failure modes are only briefly touched upon.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, well-organized, and readable. The probabilistic interpretation, potential definitions, and architectural choices are explained with helpful figures. Minor notational issues and a few reporting inconsistencies remain (see Minor comments).

Originality
The core novelty is a generic high-order attention formulation that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over multiple modalities and yields learned modality-specific attention via a mean-field view. While components (co-attention, bilinear pooling, structured attention, count/tensor sketching) exist, their integration into a unified, scalable high-order attention framework is original and broadly applicable.

Significance
The method advances state of the art in the three-modality VQA setting and provides a reusable abstraction for multimodal attention. If adopted, this could influence follow-on work in tasks like grounded language, visual dialog, and audiovisual QA. The clearest empirical benefit stems from modeling ternary interactions.

Strengths
- Generic multimodal attention formulation that unifies unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials and could generalize beyond VQA.
- Clear exposition and intuitive probabilistic grounding; strong, tidy architecture.
- Solid ablation showing meaningful gains from the ternary potential; three-modality SOTA results.
- Practical decision modules (MCT/MCB) with a careful comparison.

Weaknesses
- Ambiguity around “attention over answers”: most answers are single tokens; what does a distribution over A mean in MC VQA? No visualizations or analyses of PA are provided.
- The main empirical gains seem driven by ternary potentials; the 2-modality version does not outperform strong two-modality baselines (except HieCoAtt). The explanation (lower embedding dim) needs verification via controlled comparisons.
- Missing comparisons to prior models that use answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., Revisiting VQA Baselines), which is particularly important given the criticality of the answer modality here.
- Limited analysis of failure cases and model failure modes; no breakdown by question type or error taxonomy.
- Minor errors/typos and a reporting discrepancy: L38 “mechanism”→“mechanisms”; L237 mentions validation while Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std.

Requests for clarification and additional analysis
1) Answer attention: Please clarify the semantics of PA in multiple-choice VQA. Is it effectively a learned prior over candidates? How does it differ from the final scoring over answers? Provide visualizations or statistics (e.g., entropy over answer candidates, sensitivity to paraphrased or near-duplicate answers, behavior on multi-word answers).
2) Fair 2-modality comparison: Provide a pairwise-only version with the same feature dimensionality/backbone and classifier capacity as leading 2-modality methods (e.g., 2048-d ResNet features and identical MCB head). Report test-dev results and ablate α/β weights to show robustness.
3) Baselines that use answers as inputs: Include comparisons to Jabri et al. and related answer-conditioned baselines under the same protocol; discuss pros/cons vs. your ternary attention.
4) Human-attention correlation: You mention improved correlation with human attention maps; please report the exact metrics (e.g., rank-correlation, KL) and compare against strong baselines such as MCB, controlling for resolution and normalization.
5) Mean-field iterations: You use a single mean-field step; did additional iterations help? Any evidence that learned α/β/γ weights stabilize?
6) Post-rebuttal numbers: The reported MCB vs. MCT results (68.4% vs. 69.4%) appear inconsistent/swapped and conflict with Table 1’s MCB value (68.6%). Please reconcile these figures and specify which backbone/split each refers to.

Minor comments
- Typo: In the attended modalities equation, the last term should define aA (not aV) for answers.
- L38: “mechanism”→“mechanisms.”
- L237: The text mentions validation performance, but Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std; please fix the wording and ensure consistent splits.
- Cite dataset splits and preprocessing choices used in each table; clarify if “train+val” was used to train the final test-std models.
- Consider adding per-question-type breakdowns and a brief error analysis.

Reproducibility
Training details are reasonably specified and the authors indicate a public implementation. Please ensure the repository matches the paper’s final configuration and includes scripts to reproduce all ablations.

Overall evaluation and recommendation
This is a well-written and technically sound paper that presents a general, extensible high-order attention framework with clear benefits in the three-modality VQA setting. While the 2-modality comparisons and the semantics of answer-attention need clarification, the core idea is strong, the empirical gains with ternary potentials are meaningful, and the framework is likely to be useful beyond VQA. Post-rebuttal, I lean toward acceptance given the genericity of the approach and the quantitative state-of-the-art with three modalities, while encouraging the authors to address the requested comparisons and reporting inconsistencies.
