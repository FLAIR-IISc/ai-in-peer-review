This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to model correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer). The core idea is to move beyond pairwise attention and incorporate ternary potentials, allowing the model to capture more complex relationships between the visual input, the question, and the possible answers. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

The work builds upon the growing body of research leveraging attention mechanisms for multi-modal tasks like VQA, image captioning, and machine comprehension (e.g., [8, 15, 26]). Existing attention mechanisms are often criticized for their task-specific design and the ad-hoc manner in which they extract data representations. This paper addresses these limitations by proposing a more general and flexible attention framework based on high-order potentials. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant approaches like those of Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25], and Fukui et al. [7].  The paper also acknowledges more recent work, such as that of Jabri et al. [9], which challenges the necessity of attention mechanisms altogether.

The strengths of this paper lie in its novel and potentially impactful approach to multi-modal attention. The proposed framework is indeed generic and could, in principle, be applied to other tasks involving multiple data sources. The ablation study clearly demonstrates the contribution of the ternary potentials, showing that including interactions between all three modalities leads to improved performance.  The paper is also well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to understand the proposed methodology. The illustrations in Figure 3 are particularly helpful in visualizing the attention mechanisms.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses.  The concept of "attention over answers" is somewhat unclear, as the answers in the VQA dataset are typically single tokens. The lack of visualizations of attention weights focused on the answer options limits understanding of this component. A key finding is the improvement gained by utilizing ternary potentials.  However, it’s not fully clear whether the performance gains are *solely* attributable to including the answer as a third modality. The paper states that the model without the ternary potentials doesn't outperform existing two-modality methods (except HieCoAtt), implying the improvement is strongly tied to the inclusion of answers.  This point requires further clarification; a more rigorous comparison to strong two-modality baselines would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the authors should compare their approach to prior work that explicitly incorporates answers as inputs, such as the approach by Jabri et al. [9].  Given the apparent importance of the answer modality in their framework, a comparison to models already leveraging this input is essential. The paper also lacks a detailed analysis of failure cases. Understanding where the model struggles and *why* would provide valuable insights and directions for future research.  A few minor errors and inconsistencies are present; for example, the phrase "mechanism" is used when "mechanisms" is intended (L38), and there's a discrepancy in the reported results (L237 claims validation, while Table 1 only presents test-dev/test-std results).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and generic high-order attention framework.
*   State-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the methodology.
*   Demonstrated importance of ternary potentials through ablation study.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Unclear explanation of “attention over answers.”
*   Limited analysis of failure cases.
*   Insufficient comparison to prior work that incorporates answers as inputs.
*   Minor errors/inconsistencies in the manuscript.



Overall, this paper presents a promising new approach to multi-modal attention for VQA. While the weaknesses mentioned above should be addressed, the novelty and potential impact of the work warrant serious consideration. Given the reported state-of-the-art results and the generic nature of the proposed framework, I lean towards accepting this paper, contingent on addressing the minor issues raised and clarifying the performance relative to strong two-modality baselines.
