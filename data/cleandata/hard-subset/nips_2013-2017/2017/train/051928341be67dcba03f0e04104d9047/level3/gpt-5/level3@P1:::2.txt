Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a generic multimodal attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary attention potentials over image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) representations. Potentials are combined via a single mean-field iteration (a learned linear combination followed by softmax) to produce attention distributions over each modality. Pairwise potentials are constructed from learned similarity matrices (Corr2), and ternary potentials from learned triple-wise correlations (Corr3). Attended features are fused using compact polynomial pooling (MCB for pairwise; MCT for ternary), enabling scalable decision modules. The method is applied to VQA, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy on the standard datasets with the 3-modality model; ablations indicate a clear gain from the ternary potential.

The work is related to co-attention mechanisms (Lu et al., NIPS’16), bilinear pooling methods (Fukui et al., MCB), structured attention (Kim et al.), dual attention (Nam et al.), multi-hop/stacked attention (Xu; Yang), and language–vision neural module approaches (Andreas et al.). Unlike many prior models that couple features early or alternate attention, this paper treats attention as a multivariate probabilistic model with explicit high-order potentials and provides a straightforward path to more than two modalities. The decision layer leverages tensor sketching (Pham & Pagh) for efficient high-order fusion, consistent with prior successes of MCB in VQA.

Strengths:
- A general, principled formulation of multimodal attention with learnable unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and mean-field inference; conceptually extensible to k modalities.
- Strong empirical results on VQA test-dev/test-std with the 3-modality model; quantitative SOTA with a relatively compact parameter budget.
- Useful ablation demonstrating the contribution of ternary potentials, supporting the central claim that higher-order correlations help.
- Clear exposition of the attention modules, including computationally efficient implementations of Corr2/Corr3 and compact pooling.
- Qualitative visualizations indicating modality-specific attentions and their interaction.

Weaknesses:
- The role of “attention over answers” is under-specified. Most answers are single tokens; no visualizations of answer attention are shown, and its practical utility is unclear beyond serving as an additional input feature.
- The 2-modality variant does not outperform competitive baselines (e.g., MCB-based models) and appears weaker than SOTA except HieCoAtt; the fairness of comparisons is affected by different embedding sizes and design choices. The paper’s core gain seems tightly coupled to including answers as inputs.
- Missing direct comparison to prior models that also condition on candidate answers (e.g., Jabri et al., “Revisiting VQA Baselines”), which is crucial given the importance of the ternary path.
- Limited analysis of failure modes; only a single failure case is shown, with little diagnosis of when high-order attention fails or when answer attention misleads the model.
- Minor issues: typos (“mechanism”→“mechanisms” in L38), possible reporting discrepancy (claiming validation while Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std), and inconsistent MCB vs. MCT numbers post-rebuttal.

Quality:
- Technically sound overall. The probabilistic formulation and mean-field combination are reasonable and well-motivated, and the Corr2/Corr3 constructions are clear. The experimental setup is standard; training details are sufficient. However, fairness of baseline comparisons in the 2-modality setting is not fully controlled (e.g., dimensionality, architecture variants), and the absence of direct answer-conditional baselines leaves a gap.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized; the attention modules and decision units are presented clearly. The paper would benefit from explicit definitions/examples clarifying “answer attention,” more attention map visualizations including the answer dimension, and a clearer separation of validation vs. test reporting. Minor typos noted.

Originality:
- The high-order attention-as-potentials view and its coupling with MCT are novel in this explicit, general form. While related ideas (bilinear/structured attention, co-attention, MCB) exist, unifying unary/pairwise/ternary potentials with mean-field inference and demonstrating their utility for VQA contributes new insight.

Significance:
- The demonstrated SOTA with ternary attention is meaningful. The framework has potential to generalize to other multimodal tasks (e.g., captioning, VQA variants, text–audio–vision). The reliance on answers as inputs for the main gain raises questions about generality, but the method’s formulation is broadly applicable.

Detailed questions and suggestions:
- Please clarify and visualize “answer attention”: How is PA distributed across candidate answers in MC VQA? Do longer, multi-word answers get different treatment? Can you show cases where answer attention meaningfully changes visual or question attention?
- Provide a controlled 2-modality comparison to strong baselines (e.g., MCB with 2048-d features, same data splits/regularization), or at least a sensitivity analysis to embedding size to support the claim that differences are due to dimensionality rather than modeling.
- Include comparisons to models that condition on candidate answers (e.g., Jabri et al.) under the same protocol. How does performance change if candidates are noisy or if the candidate set is large?
- Expand failure analysis: characterize typical errors (counts, colors, attribute vs. relational questions), calibration of attention weights, and cases where high-order potentials hurt.
- Post-rebuttal: The reported MCB vs. MCT numbers (e.g., 68.4% vs. 69.4%) appear swapped and conflict with Table 1 (MCB 68.6%). Please reconcile these numbers and specify datasets/splits/features used. Also quantify the reported improvement in correlation with human attention maps relative to strong baselines (e.g., MCB), including metrics and statistical significance.

Minor comments:
- Fix typos and labeling inconsistencies; ensure all claims (validation vs. test) match the tables and captions.
- Cite and discuss Kim et al. (bilinear/Hadamard pooling) and other recent strong baselines in the appropriate contexts, emphasizing differences.

Pros for acceptance:
- General and extensible attention framework.
- Clear empirical gains with 3-modality high-order attention on a prominent benchmark.
- Solid technical contribution with practical implementation details.

Cons for acceptance:
- SOTA depends on including answers as inputs; weaker 2-modality performance and limited fair comparisons.
- Limited analysis/visualization of answer attention and failure modes.
- Some reporting inconsistencies.

Overall recommendation:
Leaning accept. Despite concerns about the 2-modality baseline and incomplete comparisons to answer-conditional models, the paper presents a principled, general attention formulation with demonstrated SOTA in the 3-modality setting and plausible impact beyond VQA. Addressing the comparison and reporting issues in the final version would strengthen the contribution.

Reviewer confidence: medium.
