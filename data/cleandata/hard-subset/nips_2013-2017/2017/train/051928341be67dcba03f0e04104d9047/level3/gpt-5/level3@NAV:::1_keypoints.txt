- Proposes high-order attention modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image, question, and answer, marginalized via a learned mean-field–like linear blend.  
- Attended vectors fused with compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) and achieve strong VQA results with fewer parameters.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough (quantitative SOTA, qualitative analyses, MCB vs MCT comparisons).  
- Probabilistic framing is largely motivational and lacks a formal energy/partition/inference objective or convergence analysis—formalize or soften that language.  
- Need controlled ablations: report learned coefficients (α, β, γ) and their variability across modalities and question types.  
- Require apples-to-apples comparisons to MCB with matched embedding dimensionality and capacity to isolate gains.  
- Include marginalized ternary attention maps and per-question-type analyses.  
- Clarity issues: writing/organization confusing; Figure 2 is at the wrong abstraction level and Figure 3 is insufficiently referenced.  
- Minor notation slips and redundancies impair reproducibility; a careful edit is needed.  
- Originality is interesting but feels incremental relative to MCB and co-attention; clarify differences from structured/dual attention and sharpen claimed contributions.  
- Significance: competitive results and interpretable shifts between unary/pairwise/final attention suggest broader multimodal usefulness.  
- Specific reviewer questions: what were learned α,β,γ values and modality differences; why does Unary+Pairwise underperform MCB; report sequential MCB vs single MCT head trade-offs.  
- Pros: strong empirical results with fewer parameters, thorough evaluation, interpretable attention behavior.  
- Cons: unformalized probabilistic connection, presentation/figure issues, and limited perceived novelty without clearer conceptual distinction.
