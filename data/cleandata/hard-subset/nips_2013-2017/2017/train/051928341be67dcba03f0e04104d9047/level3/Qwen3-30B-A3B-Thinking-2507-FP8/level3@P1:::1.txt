

This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically visual question answering (VQA), which models correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The authors build upon existing attention mechanisms like Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016) but extend them to higher-order interactions, arguing that their probabilistic approach better captures the joint dependencies between modalities. The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA, with thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluation.

The paper's main strength lies in its empirical results. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over previous methods, with their 3-modality model outperforming 2-modality baselines by a clear margin (64.57% vs 63.82% on the validation set). The attention maps in Figure 5 effectively illustrate how the model shifts attention based on question content, providing strong evidence that the performance gains stem from improved attention mechanisms rather than larger model capacity. The qualitative analysis of attention maps (e.g., focusing on the laptop for "battery-operated device" questions) is particularly compelling and well-executed.

The paper also makes a strong case for the value of high-order attention through its comprehensive experimental design. The authors compare multiple decision-making units (MCT vs. 2-layer MCB) and demonstrate the superiority of their approach. The ablation study showing the importance of high-order attention (3-modality vs. 2-modality) is well-designed and clearly supports their claims.

However, the paper suffers from significant presentation issues that undermine its contribution. The writing is confusing in places, particularly in the probabilistic interpretation of the attention mechanism. The connection between the proposed "high-order potentials" and the probabilistic model is only motivational and not formally established. The authors should either provide a more rigorous probabilistic foundation or soften the language to avoid overclaiming the theoretical contribution.

The most problematic aspect is the paper's unclear contribution statement. The architecture is essentially an extension of existing MCB (Fukui et al., 2016) to higher orders, but this is not clearly articulated. The paper claims novelty in "learning high-order correlations," yet the implementation is largely a straightforward extension of the MCB framework to ternary interactions. The authors should clarify exactly what makes their approach novel compared to existing work.

Several figures are poorly utilized. Figure 2 presents an overly abstract system architecture that doesn't effectively convey the key innovation. The paper would benefit from simplifying this figure to emphasize the generation of unary/pairwise/trinary potentials. Figure 3, which illustrates the potential generation process, is never referenced in the text, creating a disconnect between the figures and the narrative.

The paper raises several important questions that remain unanswered. The authors should report the learned coefficients for the linear combination of potentials (αi, βi, γi) to show which potential types are most important for different modalities. Additionally, the 2-modality Unary+Pairwise model underperforms MCB despite similar architecture; the authors should provide intuition for this discrepancy. The mention of sequential MCB vs. single MCT decision heads without results is also problematic.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these issues. Clarifying the contribution relative to existing MCB work, formalizing the probabilistic connection (or softening the claim), improving the writing and figure presentation, and providing more detailed analysis of the learned coefficients would greatly enhance the paper's impact.

Overall, this is an interesting approach with strong empirical results that demonstrates the value of high-order attention in VQA. The core idea has merit, but the current presentation obscures the contribution. With clearer exposition and more precise contribution statement, this could be a valuable addition to the literature. As it stands, the paper's potential is somewhat undermined by its presentation issues, though the empirical results are compelling and the qualitative analysis is particularly strong. The authors should focus on clarifying their contribution and improving the writing to better highlight the novelty of their approach.
