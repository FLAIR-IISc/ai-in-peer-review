This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) intended to capture correlations between different data modalities (image, question, answer). The core idea is to extend standard attention mechanisms to consider not just pairwise interactions, but also higher-order correlations via potentials and mean-field inference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

The paper is well-structured and builds logically from motivation to method and results. It effectively connects the proposed approach to existing work in attention mechanisms for both image and text processing, providing a comprehensive overview of relevant literature (Sections 2). The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of existing attention mechanisms (lack of explicit multi-modality modeling, task-specificity) and positions the proposed method as a general solution. The mathematical formulation of the high-order attention model (Section 3) is clear, though dense, and the explanations of unary, pairwise and ternary potentials are helpful. The experimental setup (Section 4) is described in sufficient detail and the results show a clear improvement over existing methods. The inclusion of qualitative results (Figs. 5-8) strengthens the paper by providing insight into the attention mechanism's behavior.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of extending attention to explicitly model high-order correlations between modalities is novel and addresses a clear limitation of existing attention mechanisms. The use of potentials and mean-field inference is a creative approach.
* **Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset is a significant accomplishment and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Generality:** The authors emphasize the general applicability of the proposed attention mechanism beyond VQA, and the framework seems amenable to extension to other multi-modal tasks.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The related work section is extensive and provides a good overview of the landscape of attention mechanisms.
* **Clear Explanation:** Despite the complexity of the method, the paper does a good job of explaining the key concepts and mathematical formulations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Justification:** While the mathematical formulation is clear, the benefits of explicitly modeling third-order correlations (compared to, say, a carefully engineered pairwise interaction) are not fully justified. The paper doesn't deeply explore the impact of *which* order correlations are most important. A more thorough ablation study examining the performance with only unary, pairwise, and then ternary potentials would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning but doesn’t provide extensive details on hyperparameter sensitivity. Given the complexity of the model, it’s likely sensitive to hyperparameter choices.  More discussion on this would improve the robustness of the findings.
* **Failure Case Analysis:** The single failure case highlighted (Fig. 8) is a bit superficial. A more in-depth analysis of failure modes and potential reasons for the model’s shortcomings would be valuable.  Why does it fixate on the umbrella?
* **Limited Ablation:** The ablation study comparing MCT and 2-layer MCB is limited in scope.  Further ablation analyzing the impact of different components of the model (e.g., different embedding architectures, dropout rates) would strengthen the findings.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper acknowledges the computational cost but doesn't provide a detailed analysis or comparison to existing methods. This is an important consideration for potential adopters.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper proposes a novel and theoretically sound approach to attention mechanisms with demonstrably strong empirical performance on a challenging benchmark.
* The method addresses a clear limitation of existing attention mechanisms and has the potential to be applicable to a wide range of multi-modal tasks.
* The paper is well-written and provides a thorough review of related work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The benefits of explicitly modeling higher-order correlations are not fully justified, and the impact of different order correlations is not thoroughly explored.
* The lack of a detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and failure modes weakens the robustness of the findings.
* The computational cost of the proposed method is a potential concern.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is **Strong Accept**.  The novelty and strong empirical performance of the proposed high-order attention mechanism outweigh the weaknesses. While addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper, the current contribution is significant and warrants acceptance to NIPS. The approach has the potential to influence future research in attention mechanisms and multi-modal learning. The authors present a well-reasoned and clearly articulated contribution that addresses an important problem in the field.
