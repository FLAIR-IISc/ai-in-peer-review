This paper proposes a multimodal attention framework that explicitly models unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) representations, and obtains marginal attentions via a mean-field-inspired softmax combination of these potentials. For decision fusion, it uses tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to approximate high-order outer products of attended features. On VQA (multiple-choice), the model achieves state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters than prior systems. The work is positioned against co-attention (Lu et al., NIPS’16), stacked attention (Yang et al., 2016), DMN (Xiong et al., 2016), bilinear pooling variants (Fukui et al., 2016; Kim et al., ICLR’17), and structured attention (Kim et al., 2017), arguing that prior models either entangle modalities or are limited to pairwise interactions, whereas the proposed formulation cleanly generalizes to higher-order correlations and any number of modalities.

Quality: The methodology is sound and clearly specified, with concrete potential definitions and a practical fusion strategy. The empirical section shows meaningful gains, especially when moving from 2- to 3-modality attention. However, the treatment of mean-field is shallow (a single learned linear combination); it would help to justify why multiple MF iterations are unnecessary. The computational and memory costs of building C3 over nv×nq×na are not analyzed; a complexity/runtime table would bolster claims of scalability. Comparisons sometimes vary embeddings/backbones (e.g., d=512 vs 2048), confounding fairness. Ablations isolating the contribution of each potential (remove ternary, remove pairwise, etc.) are missing.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written with precise notation and helpful figures. A brief derivation connecting Eq. (1) to mean-field and an explicit complexity analysis would improve readability. Some typos remain.

Originality: Modeling attention as a high-order potential-based probabilistic model and performing MF-style marginalization is a fresh and principled perspective relative to prior co-attention and bilinear pooling work. The integration with MCT/MCB is sensible.

Significance: Results are competitive and the formulation is likely reusable beyond VQA. That said, evaluation is limited to VQA v1 multiple-choice; testing on open-ended and VQA v2 (to mitigate language priors) would strengthen claims.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled high-order attention formulation with strong results
- Simple, extensible design; good qualitative visualizations
- Demonstrates benefit of 3-modality reasoning

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited evaluation scope (no VQA v2, no open-ended)
- Missing ablations and runtime analysis for ternary potentials
- Fairness of comparisons due to differing embedding dimensions/backbones
- Mean-field connection not fully justified beyond one iteration
