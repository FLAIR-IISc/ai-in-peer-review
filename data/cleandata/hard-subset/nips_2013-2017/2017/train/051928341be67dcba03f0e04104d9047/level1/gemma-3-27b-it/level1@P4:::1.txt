The paper proposes a novel high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA). The core idea is to model interactions between different data modalities (image, question, answer) at various orders – unary, pairwise, and ternary – using probabilistic potentials and mean-field inference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

This work builds upon the growing body of research utilizing attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks. Previous works, such as Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25], have explored joint attention over two modalities. However, they often lack the explicit modeling of higher-order correlations. Kim et al. [13, 14] also explore probabilistic modeling of attention, but their approach doesn't explicitly extend to arbitrary numbers of modalities as easily. The paper explicitly addresses these limitations by proposing a generalizable high-order attention framework. The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of relevant approaches, demonstrating the authors' awareness of the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The high-order attention mechanism is a novel approach to modeling interactions between multiple modalities. Extending attention to higher orders is a conceptually valuable contribution.
* **Generality:** The proposed framework is designed to be applicable to any number of data modalities, making it potentially useful beyond VQA.
* **State-of-the-art performance:** The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
* **Technical Soundness:** The use of probabilistic potentials and mean-field inference provides a theoretically sound foundation for the attention mechanism. The description of the different potential types (unary, pairwise, ternary) is clear.
* **Detailed Explanation:** The paper provides a fairly detailed explanation of the technical aspects of the proposed approach, including the data embedding and decision-making components.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** While theoretically sound, the proposed model is relatively complex, involving multiple components (unary potentials, pairwise potentials, ternary potentials, tensor sketching). This complexity could make it difficult to implement and debug.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimension, dropout rates, learning rate) as indicated in section 4.3, but a more thorough ablation study would be valuable to understand the impact of each parameter.
* **Ablation Study:** The ablation study comparing MCT and 2-layer MCB is limited. A more comprehensive analysis of the different components of the model (e.g., the contribution of ternary potentials vs. pairwise potentials) would provide further insight.
* **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** While the provided qualitative examples are helpful, they are limited. Expanding the qualitative analysis to include more examples, and perhaps visualizing the attention weights in a more intuitive way, would be beneficial.
* **Failure Case Discussion:** The single failure case highlighted is interesting but needs more in-depth analysis. Understanding why the model fails in certain scenarios is crucial for future improvement.
* **Implementation Details:** More implementation details could be helpful for reproducibility. For example, the specific pre-trained CNN used for image embedding is not fully specified.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to multimodal attention.
* The proposed high-order attention mechanism achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark dataset.
* The work has the potential to impact a broader range of multimodal learning tasks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The model is complex and may be difficult to implement and tune.
* The ablation study is limited, making it difficult to fully assess the contribution of each component.
* The qualitative analysis could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to multimodal attention. The proposed high-order attention mechanism is a valuable contribution to the field. While the model is complex and further analysis is needed, the achieved results and the potential impact of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly expanding the ablation study and providing more detailed implementation details.
