## Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism leveraging high-order correlations between data modalities, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea is to move beyond pairwise interactions (common in existing attention models like [15, 25]) to model higher-order relationships between visual features, question embeddings, and even answer choices. The authors argue this allows for more nuanced attention focusing, leading to improved performance. They implement this using a mean-field inference approach to approximate marginal probabilities and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon the established success of attention mechanisms in multimodal tasks like VQA [6, 26]. It correctly identifies limitations in existing approaches, particularly the often ad-hoc and task-specific nature of attention design. The proposed high-order attention mechanism is a logical extension, offering a more general and potentially expressive approach. The use of probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference is a sound theoretical foundation. The experimental results are compelling, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, and the ablation studies comparing different decision-making units (MCT vs. MCB) are insightful. The qualitative examples provided in Figures 5-8 help to understand the behavior of the proposed attention mechanism.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is presented, the explanation of the mean-field inference process could be more detailed. The connection between the learned parameters (αi, βi, γi) and the actual learned correlations isn't fully elucidated. The ablation studies, while present, could be more extensive, for example, exploring the impact of different orders of correlation (e.g., 4th order). The failure case analysis (Fig. 8) highlights a limitation of the model – a tendency to focus on salient but irrelevant features – and a deeper discussion of this issue would be beneficial. The writing, while generally clear, is occasionally dense and could benefit from streamlining.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to attention modeling.
*   State-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark (VQA).
*   Clear identification of limitations in existing attention mechanisms.
*   Insightful ablation studies.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited explanation of the mean-field inference process.
*   Ablation studies could be more comprehensive.
*   Failure case analysis lacks depth.
*   Writing could be more concise.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The proposed high-order attention mechanism is a promising direction for multimodal reasoning, and the empirical results are strong. While some aspects could be improved, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity and completeness concerns.
