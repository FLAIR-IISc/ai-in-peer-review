- Proposes a unified high-order attention mechanism for multimodal reasoning, modeling attentions as marginals from unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials and using MCB/MCT fusion for VQA.  
- Implements unary scores via linear layers, pairwise via similarity matrices marginalized to modality potentials, and ternary via an inner-product tensor marginalized similarly.  

- Strength: Provides a clear, principled decomposition into unary/pairwise/ternary terms that generalizes to more modalities.  
- Strength: Achieves strong multiple-choice VQA performance with relatively few parameters and compares MCT vs stacked MCB in ablation.  
- Strength: Qualitative visualizations show pairwise/ternary terms meaningfully shift attention.  
- Strength: Uses compact bilinear/trilinear pooling to avoid explosive parameter counts.

- Weakness: Probabilistic/mean-field inference claims are underspecified and not formally justified; no explicit joint distribution or iterative inference.  
- Weakness: Computational cost and memory implications of the ternary term C3 are unclear; no complexity analysis or implementation detail on explicit vs implicit computation.  
- Weakness: Ambiguity about marginalization weight parametrization (shared vs index-specific) raises parameter count and generalization concerns.  
- Weakness: Evaluation scope limited—results only on multiple-choice VQA with unclear baseline comparability and no open-ended or other datasets.  
- Weakness: Insufficient ablations to isolate gains from unary/pairwise/ternary terms and limited analysis of learned weightings (α/β/γ).

- Quality: Method is a sound attention/fusion architecture but theoretical claims about probabilistic modeling are unsupported and experimental reporting is incomplete (missing tables, unclear fairness).  
- Quality: Qualitative results and a failure case are helpful, but numerical validation and computational cost reporting are lacking.

- Clarity: Paper is mostly readable but needs a precise joint model or removal of mean-field framing.  
- Clarity: Must state whether C3 is constructed explicitly and provide time/memory complexity in terms of nv, nq, na, d.  
- Clarity: Clarify shapes and sharing of marginalization weights and report total parameter counts.  
- Clarity: Include full quantitative results, standard deviations, and ensure baselines use identical backbones/modalities.

- Originality: Offers a fresh decomposition perspective but largely combines established components (similarity matrices, bilinear pooling, compact sketching) rather than introducing a wholly new paradigm.

- Significance: Potentially practical and adoptable if computational issues are resolved and gains generalize beyond the reported setting; ternary attention is particularly relevant for multiple-choice VQA.

- Suggestions: Formalize the probabilistic model or reframe the method without mean-field claims.  
- Suggestions: Provide complexity/memory analysis and explicit vs implicit implementation details for C2/C3.  
- Suggestions: Report systematic ablations (unary vs +pairwise vs +ternary), with/without answers, and analyze α/β/γ weights.  
- Suggestions: Clarify evaluation protocol regarding answer-input usage, add open-ended results and other datasets.  
- Suggestions: Quantitatively assess attention quality (e.g., correlation with human attention maps).  
- Suggestions: Discuss impact of embedding dimensionality (e.g., 512 vs 2048) for fair comparisons.

- Pros for acceptance: Strong empirical performance, modular and extensible design, and useful qualitative insights with compact sketching fusion.  
- Cons for acceptance: Weak probabilistic justification, incomplete reporting and baseline fairness, unclear computational tractability for ternary attention, and limited evaluation breadth.

- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept—promising and practical approach but needs clearer modeling, stronger ablations, and more thorough evaluation to fully substantiate claims.
