

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (visual, textual, and answer) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors extend standard attention mechanisms by introducing unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials that capture correlations between different modalities. They argue that these higher-order correlations better direct attention to relevant elements across modalities, which they demonstrate through state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset.

The paper builds on recent advances in attention mechanisms for VQA, particularly Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016), but extends them to model high-order interactions. The approach differs from most prior work that typically considers pairwise interactions between two modalities (e.g., image and question), and it generalizes to any number of modalities. The authors present a probabilistic framework using mean-field inference to combine these high-order potentials into attention distributions, which are then used to compute attended features for decision making.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA (64.57% on test-dev using 2-layer MCB), demonstrating the practical value of high-order attention. The ablation study clearly shows the benefit of incorporating third-order attention (3-modality model) over 2-modality approaches.

2. The method is well-motivated and the mathematical formulation is clear. The authors provide a thorough description of how unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials are computed, with helpful visualizations (Fig. 3) explaining the architecture.

3. The qualitative analysis (Figs. 5-7) effectively demonstrates how the attention mechanism works, showing that the model correctly focuses on relevant visual regions based on the question. The comparison with existing approaches (Fig. 7) provides convincing evidence of improved attention quality.

4. The paper is well-written with clear organization, sufficient implementation details for reproducibility, and appropriate citations to related work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The novelty claim is somewhat overstated. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from Kim et al. (2017) "Structured Attention Networks," which also models high-order interactions using a similar probabilistic framework. The authors should explicitly compare their approach to this recent work, as it represents the closest related contribution.

2. The theoretical justification for why high-order attention is necessary is weak. While the empirical results are strong, the paper doesn't adequately explain why higher-order correlations provide benefits beyond what pairwise attention could achieve. The authors should address whether the improvement comes from the high-order modeling itself or simply from a more expressive architecture.

3. The computational complexity of the approach isn't adequately addressed. Ternary potentials (k=3) require O(n^3) operations, which could become prohibitive for larger modalities or datasets. The paper doesn't discuss this limitation or how it scales to even higher-order interactions.

4. The comparison with the most relevant recent work (e.g., dual attention networks by Nam et al., 2016) is limited. The authors should have included a more thorough comparison with these approaches that also model interactions between modalities.

5. The failure analysis (Fig. 8) is limited to a single example, and the paper doesn't discuss whether this is a common failure mode or how it might be addressed.

**Significance and Overall Assessment:**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the VQA literature by demonstrating that modeling high-order correlations between modalities can lead to improved performance. While the approach is relatively simple and the results are strong, the novelty is somewhat incremental compared to recent work on structured attention. The method's general applicability to any number of modalities is a positive aspect, but the paper doesn't sufficiently explore this potential beyond VQA.

The paper meets the technical quality bar with sound methodology and comprehensive experiments. The clarity is excellent, and the significance is demonstrated through strong empirical results. However, the novelty is not as strong as the authors claim, particularly in relation to Kim et al. (2017).

**Recommendation:**

Accept. While the novelty claim is somewhat overstated, the paper makes a solid contribution to the field with strong empirical results and a well-executed method. The authors have demonstrated that high-order attention mechanisms can improve VQA performance, and the approach is generally applicable to other multimodal tasks. The paper provides valuable insights into how attention mechanisms work in VQA, and the results are clearly presented. With minor revisions to better contextualize the work against Kim et al. (2017) and to strengthen the theoretical justification, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS 2017.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on standard VQA benchmark
- Clear, well-organized presentation with strong experimental validation
- Provides useful insights into attention mechanisms through qualitative analysis
- Generalizable approach to any number of data modalities

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Novelty is somewhat incremental compared to recent structured attention work
- Theoretical justification for high-order attention is weak
- Limited discussion of computational complexity
- Comparison with closest related work (Kim et al., 2017) is insufficient
