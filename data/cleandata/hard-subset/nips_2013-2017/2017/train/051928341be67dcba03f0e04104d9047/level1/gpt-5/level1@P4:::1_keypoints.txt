- Proposes a multimodal attention framework modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions among image (V), question (Q), and answers (A) using potentials and a learned softmax mixture, fused via MCB/MCT tensor-sketching.
- Distinguishes itself from prior co-attention and bilinear pooling by explicitly framing attention as high-order potentials and including the answer modality (ternary attention), relating to but differing from structured attention.
- Clear, modular formulation with learnable per-modality weights, easily extensible to more modalities.
- Solid engineering: pairwise similarity matrices, a 3-way correlation tensor for ternary interactions, and practical fusion via tensor sketching to control parameter growth.
- Empirical results show competitive or state-of-the-art performance on multiple-choice VQA with fewer parameters and interpretable attention visualizations.
- Ablation studies compare MCT vs two-layer MCB and two- vs three-modality settings, suggesting benefits from higher-order modeling.
- Probabilistic/mean-field justification is underdeveloped: no explicit energy/factor graph, derivation of updates, or conditions validating the linear-mixture update.
- Computational costs of constructing ternary correlations (C3) are not analyzed; runtime/memory/scalability concerns remain unaddressed and tensor sketching is used only for fusion.
- Evaluation scope is limited to multiple-choice VQA v1.0; open-ended VQA and generalization to other datasets/tasks are not shown, leaving unclear whether gains stem from using the answer modality.
- Ablations on potential orders are limited; more granular experiments (e.g., removing ternary while keeping pairwise or varying mixture weights) are needed to identify drivers of improvement.
- Notation and implementation details lack clarity (e.g., 1×1 conv definitions, tensor shapes, reductions, training objectives), with minor typos and formatting issues.
- Overall quality: model is plausible and empirically supported but would benefit from fuller result tables, statistical rigor, and stronger theoretical grounding for the mean-field claim.
- Clarity: paper is readable with helpful figures, but the probabilistic framing is not rigorously connected to updates and experimental protocol details are incomplete.
- Originality: moderate — systematic inclusion of ternary attention and learned k-order mixtures is novel, while extensions like MCT are incremental.
- Significance: moderate — of interest to multimodal attention researchers but limited by evaluation focus and lack of computational analysis.
- Suggestion: provide a concrete probabilistic model (e.g., CRF), derive mean-field updates, and justify the learned mixture coefficients.
- Suggestion: analyze computational and memory complexity of C3 and propose scalable approximations (low-rank factorization or sketching for attention).
- Suggestion: expand evaluation to open-ended VQA and newer datasets (e.g., VQA v2.0) to test generality beyond multiple-choice settings.
- Suggestion: add targeted ablations isolating unary/pairwise/ternary contributions, multi-iteration vs single-step updates, and the effect of including the answer modality.
- Suggestion: clarify implementation and tensor shapes, provide complete quantitative tables with standard deviations, and consider releasing code.
- Arguments for acceptance: presents a clear, general high-order multimodal attention framework, shows competitive results with fewer parameters, and is conceptually simple and extensible.
- Arguments against acceptance: mean-field claim lacks rigorous justification, evaluation and ablations are limited, computational trade-offs for ternary attention are not addressed, and novelty is incremental.
