Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a general attention framework that models high-order correlations across modalities via potentials and mean-field inference. Given embeddings for image regions V, question words Q, and (for multiple-choice VQA) candidate answers A, the model defines unary potentials (per modality), pairwise potentials (e.g., correlations between image and question), and ternary potentials (joint correlations among all three). Attention distributions over each modality are obtained by a learned linear combination of the respective unary, marginalized pairwise, and marginalized ternary potentials followed by a softmax (interpreted as a single mean-field iteration). Attended modality vectors feed a decision module that uses Tensor Sketch approximations: Multimodal Compact Bilinear pooling (MCB) and its trilinear extension (MCT). The method is evaluated on VQA, with both qualitative attention visualizations and quantitative results on the VQA test-dev/test-std multiple-choice setting. The authors report state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters than prior work.

This work builds on a large body of attention for VQA, including hierarchical and co-attention (Lu et al., NIPS’16), stacked attention (Yang et al., 2016), dynamic memory networks (Xiong et al., 2016), and bilinear pooling (Fukui et al., MCB; Kim et al., MLB/Hadamard, ICLR’17). Compared to co-attention methods that typically fuse two modalities, the paper explicitly formulates attention as a probabilistic model with high-order potentials and extends to three modalities by incorporating candidate answers. It also leverages compact trilinear pooling for decision making.

Quality:
- Soundness: The architecture is clearly specified, with consistent training details and code availability. The unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” are computed via learned linear projections and dot products, with learned marginalization weights. However, the probabilistic framing and “mean-field” claim is somewhat superficial: the method boils down to learning a weighted sum of potentials followed by softmax, with a single “iteration.” There is no explicit energy function over all variables nor evidence of iterative inference improving results.
- Empirical support: The paper reports strong test-dev/test-std results in the multiple-choice setting and compares to Lu et al. (co-attention) and MCB. Ablations show 3-modality > 2-modality and 2-layer MCB > MCT in their setup. The qualitative analyses are helpful and show question-dependent attention. That said, the quantitative section lacks tables in the submission and does not report open-ended VQA or VQA v2, limiting conclusions about generality. Fairness of comparisons is weakened by using a lower embedding dimension (512 vs. 2048) for their 2-modality baseline.
- Efficiency/complexity: Computing ternary correlations C3 is potentially expensive (O(nq·nv·na·d)). This is tractable for multiple-choice na≈18 but would be prohibitive for na≈3000; the paper should clarify that ternary attention is used only for MC with per-question candidates, and report memory/runtime of the C3 operations.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and well organized, with clear module descriptions and equations. Implementation details (optimizers, dropouts, dims) are provided and code is available. Some notation/typos should be fixed: aV is used twice (for image and answer) in Eq. defining attended vectors; “and and” appears; smax should be defined once. It would help to explicitly state when A denotes the 18 candidates versus the full answer vocabulary and how training differs between MC and open-ended.

Originality:
The main novelty is the explicit high-order attention formulation across multiple modalities using correlation tensors and the integration of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials with learnable mixing weights. Prior VQA work predominantly handles two modalities; while using answers as inputs for MC has been explored (e.g., Jabri et al.), modeling ternary attention and combining it with pairwise/unary components is a distinct contribution. On the decision side, MCT seems a natural extension of MCB. Related contemporary bilinear/factorized fusion methods such as MLB (Kim et al., ICLR’17), MFB (Yu et al., CVPR’17), and MUTAN (Ben-Younes et al., 2017) should be discussed and compared.

Significance:
If the reported gains indeed reach SOTA on standard VQA benchmarks with fewer parameters, the approach is impactful. The formulation is general and could apply beyond VQA to other multimodal tasks (e.g., captioning with attributes, text+audio+vision). The ternary attention idea is particularly relevant for multiple-choice VQA. However, the focus on MC (the easier setting) and absence of VQA v2 or open-ended results reduce the perceived breadth. The probabilistic claim is more of an interpretation than a substantive algorithmic advance.

Pros:
- Clear, modular formulation of multi-modal attention with unary/pairwise/ternary potentials.
- Empirically strong results on VQA MC with fewer parameters; useful qualitative analyses.
- General framework that can, in principle, scale to k modalities; simple to implement.
- Inclusion of compact trilinear pooling and ablations between MCT and stacked MCB.

Cons:
- “Mean-field” framing is shallow; no iterative inference or principled energy modeling.
- Computational cost of ternary attention not analyzed; unclear handling for open-ended VQA.
- Comparisons omit strong contemporaries (MLB, MFB, MUTAN); 2-modality baseline underpowered (512-d vs. 2048-d).
- Limited evaluation scope (no VQA v2; emphasis on MC only).
- Minor clarity issues and typos; some ambiguity in answer embedding/training protocol.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide comprehensive quantitative tables (test-dev/test-std) and include open-ended and VQA v2 results. Report gains per question type (Y/N, number, other).
- Add fair comparisons against MLB/MFB/MUTAN with matched visual features and dimensions.
- Clarify computational complexity and memory for C3; report runtime and GPU memory. Explicitly state that ternary attention is computed over the 18 MC choices only.
- Strengthen the probabilistic story: either add iterative mean-field updates and show benefits, or present the method as a learned mixture of potentials without probabilistic claims.
- Ablate the contribution of each potential (unary only, +pairwise, +ternary), and the learned mixing weights. Analyze robustness to embedding dimension and number of glimpses.
- Evaluate without using answers as inputs (open-ended) to demonstrate broader utility.
- Fix notation/typos and unify the description of answer embeddings across settings.

Overall recommendation:
Borderline accept leaning to accept. The high-order attention idea is intuitive and practically effective for multiple-choice VQA, with a clean formulation and promising results. Strengthening comparisons, clarifying complexity, and broadening evaluation would make the contribution more compelling.
