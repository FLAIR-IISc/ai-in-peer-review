- Proposes a high-order attention mechanism for VQA that models unary, pairwise, and ternary correlations via potentials and mean-field inference.  
- Extends standard attention to capture multi-modal interactions between image, question, and answer.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset with qualitative examples illustrating attention behavior.  
- Paper is well-structured, with a clear motivation and logical progression from background to method and results.  
- Related work section is thorough and situates the contribution within existing attention literature.  
- Mathematical formulation of the model is clear though dense; unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials are well explained.  
- Novelty: explicitly modeling high-order correlations is an innovative contribution addressing limitations of current attention methods.  
- Performance: empirical results show substantial improvement, supporting the method’s effectiveness.  
- Generality: framework appears extendable to other multi-modal tasks beyond VQA.  
- Explanation quality: despite complexity, key concepts and derivations are communicated clearly.  
- Complexity & justification: paper does not fully justify the added benefit of third-order correlations over carefully designed pairwise models.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: limited discussion and analysis of tuning and sensitivity given model complexity.  
- Failure analysis: only one superficial failure case is shown; deeper analysis of failure modes is lacking.  
- Ablation: ablation studies are limited in scope and miss analysis of several model components and settings.  
- Computational cost: acknowledged but not quantified or compared against baselines, which is important for adoption.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, theoretically sound approach with strong empirical results and good writing support acceptance.  
- Rejection concerns: insufficient justification for higher-order benefits, weak hyperparameter/failure analyses, and unclear computational overhead.  
- Overall assessment: reviewer recommends Strong Accept, citing novelty and empirical strength despite noted weaknesses.
