

# Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (visual, textual, and answer) for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors frame attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities. The approach extends beyond traditional pairwise attention mechanisms (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25]) by explicitly modeling interactions between three modalities. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, achieving 64.57% accuracy on the validation set using a 2-layer MCB unit with the high-order attention mechanism.

The paper relates well to prior work in attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks. It correctly identifies that existing approaches (e.g., [15, 25, 7]) primarily focus on pairwise interactions between two modalities, while their method extends to higher-order correlations. The authors appropriately cite relevant work on image and textual attention mechanisms, as well as recent multimodal approaches like MCB [7] and bilinear models [13].

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear probabilistic framework for attention, with well-defined unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The mean-field inference approach is theoretically grounded and effectively combines different attention sources.

2. **Experimental Rigor:** The authors conduct thorough experiments comparing their approach to multiple baselines (Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]). The ablation studies demonstrate the importance of high-order attention (3-modality vs. 2-modality models) and the effectiveness of their decision-making units (MCT vs. 2-layer MCB).

3. **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-organized with clear figures (Fig. 3-8) that effectively illustrate the attention mechanism and qualitative results. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the experimental setup is adequately described.

4. **Significant Results:** The approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA with a relatively simple model (40 million parameters vs. 70+ million in [7]), demonstrating that high-order attention mechanisms can improve performance without significantly increasing model complexity.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While the authors propose high-order attention, they don't sufficiently explain *why* higher-order correlations are beneficial for VQA. The paper lacks theoretical analysis of how high-order interactions specifically improve performance compared to pairwise interactions.

2. **Marginal Improvement:** The performance gain over the best baseline (64.57% vs. 63.82% on val set) is modest. The paper doesn't convincingly argue that this marginal improvement justifies the more complex model architecture.

3. **Overlap with Bilinear Methods:** The approach bears significant similarity to existing bilinear methods (e.g., [7, 13, 11]). The paper would have been stronger with a clearer distinction between their probabilistic model and previous bilinear approaches.

4. **Limited Generalization:** The paper only evaluates on VQA, without demonstrating the general applicability of high-order attention to other multimodal tasks. The claim that the approach is "generally applicable to any number of data modalities" isn't sufficiently supported.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel probabilistic framework for attention mechanisms
- State-of-the-art results on VQA with a relatively simple model
- Clear theoretical foundation and thorough experimental validation
- Well-written with effective visualizations

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Marginal performance improvement over existing methods
- Insufficient theoretical justification for high-order attention
- Limited generalizability beyond VQA
- Overlap with recent bilinear methods that isn't sufficiently differentiated

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound and well-executed approach to attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks, with clear state-of-the-art results on VQA. The high-order attention model is a natural extension of existing work and provides a useful perspective on attention mechanisms. However, the marginal performance gains and limited theoretical justification for why high-order attention is superior to pairwise attention weaken the paper's contribution.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more thorough theoretical analysis of why high-order correlations improve performance
2. A clearer distinction from existing bilinear methods
3. Evaluation on additional multimodal tasks to demonstrate broader applicability
4. A more compelling argument for the significance of the marginal performance gains

Given the modest improvement over existing methods and the lack of strong theoretical justification, I recommend borderline acceptance. The paper is a solid technical contribution that advances the state of the art in VQA, but its significance is somewhat limited by the marginal gains and lack of broader applicability. With the suggested revisions addressing the theoretical justification and clearer differentiation from prior work, this could be a strong NIPS paper. As it stands, it's a good paper that makes a modest contribution to the field.
