- Summary: Introduces a novel high-order attention mechanism modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials combined via mean-field inference for VQA, achieving state-of-the-art results.  
- Quality: Technically sound with clear mathematical formulations, thorough ablations, reproducible implementation, and appropriate baselines.  
- Quality caveat: The link between high-order correlations and specific performance gains could be more explicitly explained.  
- Clarity: Well-organized and clearly written with effective figures and consistent notation.  
- Clarity caveat: Practical/computational aspects of implementing ternary potentials need clearer explanation; MCT vs MCB advantages could be expanded.  
- Originality: Novel formalization of high-order multimodal attention that generalizes beyond existing pairwise methods.  
- Originality caveat: Could cite and compare with some more recent 2017 VQA work (e.g., probabilistic attention).  
- Significance: Substantial — achieves SOTA on VQA with a simpler, smaller model and is extensible to other multimodal tasks.  
- Strength: Novel high-order attention framework generalizing pairwise attention.  
- Strength: Clear theoretical formulation using mean-field inference.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results (SOTA) with a relatively small model.  
- Strength: Comprehensive ablation studies demonstrating the value of 3-modality attention.  
- Strength: Good visualizations that aid interpretability.  
- Weakness: Limited theoretical justification for why high-order correlations improve performance.  
- Weakness: Failure case shown (Fig. 8) but not deeply analyzed.  
- Weakness: Limited discussion of computational complexity for higher-order potentials.  
- Weakness: Incomplete comparisons to some recent VQA approaches.  
- Weakness: Does not explore extensions beyond three modalities in experiments.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept — well-motivated, validated, and significant contribution suitable for NIPS 2017.  
- Suggested improvements: Strengthen theoretical motivation for high-order effects and provide deeper analysis of failure cases and computational costs.
