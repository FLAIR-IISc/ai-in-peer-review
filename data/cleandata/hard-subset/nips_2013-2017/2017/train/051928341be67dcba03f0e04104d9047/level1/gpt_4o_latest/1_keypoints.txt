- Proposes a novel high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks (VQA), modeling higher-order correlations between modalities.  
- Introduces a probabilistic framework with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials and uses mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities.  
- Produces succinct, task-relevant representations and explicitly models second- and third-order interactions.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA while using fewer parameters (40M vs. 70M in prior work).  
- Incorporates a third modality (answers) which improves performance over two-modality models.  
- Provides qualitative visualizations of attention maps that support interpretability.  
- Technical novelty: generalizes beyond pairwise interactions, making the approach extensible to >2 modalities.  
- Probabilistic framework: principled and interpretable alternative to heuristic attention methods.  
- Efficiency: competitive performance with substantially fewer parameters.  
- Clarity: paper is dense and hard to follow; potentials and mean-field sections need clearer explanations for non-experts.  
- Comparative baselines: lacks detailed ablation studies isolating unary vs. pairwise vs. ternary contributions.  
- Failure cases: limited analysis of errors (e.g., inability to distinguish some questions) and causes.  
- Generality: experiments are limited to VQA; applicability to other multimodal tasks is not demonstrated.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, efficient, extensible, and empirically strong contribution.  
- Against acceptance: presentation clarity, limited broader validation, and insufficient failure analysis leave open robustness questions.  
- Recommendation: accept, contingent on improved clarity, deeper failure analysis, and broader experimental validation in the final version.
