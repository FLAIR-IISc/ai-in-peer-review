- Proposes a novel multimodal attention mechanism for VQA that models high-order (unary, pairwise, ternary) correlations between image, question, and answer.  
- Defines unary potentials via 1x1 convolutions to capture importance of individual features within each modality.  
- Computes pairwise potentials from similarity matrices formed by outer products of embedded features between two modalities.  
- Introduces ternary potentials using a 3rd-order correlation tensor to capture three-way interactions among image, question, and answer.  
- Final attention for each modality is a learnable linear combination of unary, marginalized pairwise, and marginalized ternary potentials followed by softmax.  
- Frames the attention computation as a single iteration of mean-field inference, though implementation is a single feed-forward weighted sum of precomputed potentials.  
- Uses attended feature vectors combined with Multimodal Compact Bilinear/Trilinear pooling for final classification.  
- Empirically achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA v1 dataset, validating the approach.  
- Paper is technically sound, components are well-defined, and experimental details aid reproducibility.  
- Writing and organization are clear, and diagrams effectively illustrate unary/pairwise/ternary concepts.  
- The mean-field inference framing is overstated and not sufficiently justified, risking mild mischaracterization.  
- Primary originality is the explicit, learnable combination of multi-order potentials and extension of co-attention to three modalities.  
- Significance lies in a generalizable framework for modeling higher-order multimodal interactions that could inspire further research.  
- Scalability concerns: correlation tensors grow exponentially with order, raising memory and computation issues not adequately discussed.  
- Limited justification/exploration of specific mathematical forms for correlation functions and marginalization choices.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel framework, strong empirical results, generalizability, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak mean-field framing, unaddressed scalability, and limited motivation for chosen potential formulations.
