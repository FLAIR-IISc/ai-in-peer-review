

# Peer Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism that learns high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (visual, textual, and answer) for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors propose modeling attention through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, which are combined via mean-field inference to produce attention weights. This approach differs from previous work (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) that primarily focuses on pairwise correlations between two modalities. The authors demonstrate their method on the VQA dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results with a relatively simple architecture. The paper builds on recent advances in attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks while providing a more general framework that can be extended to any number of modalities.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear mathematical formulations of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The experimental setup is well-designed, with thorough ablation studies showing the importance of high-order attention (e.g., the 3-modality model significantly outperforms a 2-modality baseline). The authors properly acknowledge limitations, including a failure case (Fig. 8) where attention fails to distinguish between different questions. The implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility. The theoretical foundation is solid, though the connection between the high-order correlations and the specific improvements in performance could be more explicitly explained. The paper includes appropriate baselines and comparisons to relevant work, making the claims well-supported.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to methodology to results. The figures (particularly Fig. 3, 4, 5, and 7) effectively illustrate the attention mechanisms and their visualizations. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined. However, the relationship between the theoretical formulation and the practical implementation could be clarified further, especially regarding the computational aspects of the ternary potentials. The description of the decision-making component using tensor sketch algorithms (MCB/MCT) is clear, though the advantages of MCT over MCB might benefit from more detailed explanation.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution to the attention mechanism literature by formalizing high-order correlations as a general framework for multimodal attention. While pairwise attention mechanisms have been explored (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25]), the authors' extension to higher-order correlations is novel and well-justified. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work by emphasizing the generalizability of their approach to any number of modalities, which is not feasible with existing methods. The authors adequately reference relevant work, though they might have included more recent 2017 VQA papers (e.g., [14] on probabilistic attention). The high-order attention concept represents a meaningful extension beyond current state-of-the-art approaches.

## Significance
The paper's significance is substantial, as it demonstrates that high-order attention correlations lead to state-of-the-art performance on VQA with a relatively simple model (40M parameters vs. 70M+ in some competitors). This approach could be extended to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA, potentially advancing the field's understanding of how to effectively model interactions between multiple data sources. The paper provides a new perspective on attention mechanisms that could inspire future work in multimodal learning. The practical impact is evident from the performance gains on a standard benchmark, and the simplicity of the approach makes it accessible for broader adoption.

## Strengths
1. Novel high-order attention framework that generalizes beyond pairwise attention
2. Clear theoretical formulation with mean-field inference
3. State-of-the-art performance on VQA with a relatively simple model
4. Comprehensive ablation studies showing the value of high-order attention
5. Good visualization of attention patterns that provides interpretability

## Weaknesses
1. Limited theoretical justification for why high-order correlations improve performance
2. Failure case (Fig. 8) is mentioned but not deeply analyzed
3. Limited discussion of computational complexity for higher-order potentials
4. Comparison to some recent VQA approaches (e.g., [14], [13]) could be more thorough
5. The paper doesn't explore extensions to more than three modalities

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of multimodal learning by introducing a novel high-order attention mechanism. The approach is theoretically grounded, well-validated through experiments, and achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the literature on attention mechanisms. While the theoretical justification for high-order correlations could be strengthened, the empirical results are compelling and the method is generalizable to other multimodal tasks.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel high-order attention framework that extends beyond existing pairwise approaches
- State-of-the-art performance on standard VQA benchmark
- Clear theoretical formulation and comprehensive experimental validation
- Generalizable approach applicable to any number of modalities
- Well-written with good visualizations of attention patterns

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical justification for why high-order attention outperforms pairwise attention
- Failure case analysis is superficial
- Some recent VQA approaches not adequately compared

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS 2017. I recommend acceptance. The authors should consider strengthening the theoretical discussion of high-order correlations and providing a more detailed analysis of the failure case in the final version.
