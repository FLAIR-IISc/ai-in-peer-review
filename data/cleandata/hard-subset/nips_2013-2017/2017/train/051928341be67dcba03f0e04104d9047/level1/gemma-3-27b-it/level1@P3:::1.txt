This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA). The core idea is to model correlations between different modalities (image, question, answer) up to a higher order than typically done in existing attention mechanisms, using potentials and mean-field inference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

The paper builds upon the growing body of work employing attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks, particularly VQA. Existing attention mechanisms, as noted by the authors, often treat modalities in an entangled manner and are task-specific. This work aims to address these limitations with a more general and expressive attention mechanism. The related work section provides a reasonably comprehensive overview, touching upon relevant approaches like joint attention (Lu et al., 2015; Fukui et al., 2017), stacked attention (Yang et al., 2016), and bilinear models (Kim et al., 2016). The authors correctly highlight the novelty of their approach in handling high-order correlations and its potential for generalization to multiple modalities. The connection to probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference is a strong aspect.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly modeling high-order correlations between modalities using potentials and mean-field inference is a novel contribution.  It goes beyond pairwise interactions commonly found in attention mechanisms.
* **Generality:** The proposed mechanism is presented as generally applicable to multiple modalities, unlike many existing attention models designed specifically for two inputs.
* **State-of-the-art Results:**  Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset is a significant achievement and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The probabilistic formulation with mean-field inference provides a solid theoretical foundation and justifies the approach.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained clearly, even the more technical aspects of mean-field inference.  The figures, particularly Fig. 3, effectively illustrate the attention process.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention using tensor sketching (Pham & Pagh, 2012) to address the computational cost of higher-order tensors, the complexity of the proposed model, especially for a large number of modalities, remains a concern.  A more detailed analysis of the computational overhead would be beneficial. The paper lacks specific runtime numbers, and only mentions 40 hours of training.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The model likely has several hyperparameters (learning rate, dropout rates, embedding dimensions, coefficients α, β, γ, etc.) that could significantly impact performance. The paper doesn't provide enough detail on how these hyperparameters were tuned, and the sensitivity to these parameters remains unclear.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies, comparing MCT and 2-layer MCB, are limited. More comprehensive ablation studies are needed to evaluate the contribution of each component of the proposed model (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) and justify the complexity.  For example, how much does adding the ternary potentials *actually* improve performance compared to just using pairwise potentials?
* **Failure Case Analysis:**  While the authors present a failure case, it’s a single example. A more thorough analysis of failure cases, identifying common patterns and limitations, would strengthen the paper. The umbrella example points towards a lack of fine-grained reasoning.
* **Comparison with Strong Baselines:** While comparisons with Lu et al. (2015) and Fukui et al. (2017) are made, these baselines are not necessarily the *strongest* currently available. Comparisons with more recent and sophisticated VQA models (e.g., those incorporating transformers or more advanced multi-modal fusion techniques) would be more compelling.
* **Embedding dimension:** The authors admit using a lower embedding dimension than other state-of-the-art approaches. The improvement achieved by the attention mechanism might be partially masked by this design choice.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to multimodal attention.
* The achieved state-of-the-art results on VQA demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity of the model are significant concerns.
* The lack of comprehensive ablation studies and a thorough analysis of failure cases weakens the claims of superiority.
* The comparison with baselines could be stronger.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of multimodal attention. While the computational complexity and lack of extensive ablation studies are legitimate concerns, the novelty of the approach and the impressive experimental results make it worthy of acceptance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should: 1) provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity; 2) conduct more comprehensive ablation studies; 3) provide more details on hyperparameter tuning; and 4) consider comparing with stronger baseline models.
