Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a unified “high-order attention” mechanism for multimodal reasoning, instantiated for VQA. The key idea is to treat attentions over each modality (image regions, question tokens, and optionally candidate answers) as marginal probabilities in a model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. Concretely, the authors compute (i) unary scores per modality via 1×1 conv (i.e., linear layers plus tanh), (ii) pairwise correlations via a similarity matrix C2=QWq(VWv)ᵀ and marginalize it along one dimension to produce modality-specific potentials, and (iii) ternary correlations via an inner-product tensor C3 over QWq, VWv, AWa, marginalized to produce modality-specific ternary potentials. A learned linear combination of these potentials per modality is passed through softmax to yield attention distributions. Attended vectors are then fused for prediction using Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) pooling or its trilinear extension (MCT), with an empirical preference for a two-stage MCB that increases expressivity.

This work connects to a large VQA literature on attention and multimodal fusion, including co-attention (Lu et al.), MCB (Fukui et al.), MLB/low-rank bilinear pooling (Kim et al.), stacked/dynamic attention (Yang et al., Xiong et al.), and structured/dual attention (Kim et al., Nam et al.). The main claimed novelty is a probabilistic view that cleanly separates and combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, and that is in principle extensible to more modalities. It also revisits using answers as inputs for multiple-choice VQA (cf. Jabri et al.), but here integrated into both attention and fusion via ternary interactions.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: A clear decomposition into unary, pairwise, and ternary terms provides a principled lens on multimodal attention and a straightforward way to extend beyond two modalities.
- Empirical results: On VQA multiple-choice, the model achieves strong performance (claimed state-of-the-art at submission time) with fewer parameters than some competitors. The ablation comparing MCT vs stacked MCB is informative.
- Qualitative analyses: Visualizations illustrate how pairwise/ternary terms shift attention appropriately given different questions/answer sets.
- Practical design: The use of compact bilinear/trilinear pooling avoids the parameter blow-up typical for explicit tensor fusion.

Weaknesses
- Probabilistic/inference claims are underspecified: The “mean-field inference” interpretation is not fully justified. No explicit joint distribution is defined; the “single-iteration mean-field” reduces to a learned linear combination of potentials followed by softmax without iterative message passing.
- Computational clarity of high-order terms: The ternary correlation C3 and its marginalizations could be computationally prohibitive unless na is small (e.g., only MC candidates). The paper does not provide complexity or memory analysis or clarify whether C3 is formed explicitly or computed implicitly.
- Ambiguity in marginalization parameters: The weights used to marginalize C2/C3 (wiv, wiq, wiv,ia, etc.) are not clearly defined (shared vs index-specific), raising concerns about parameter counts and generalization.
- Comparability and scope of evaluation: The headline results are on multiple-choice VQA with answer choices as inputs; it is unclear whether baselines do the same, and there are no open-ended results. Only one dataset is reported; other multimodal QA datasets (e.g., Visual7W, COCO-QA) or VQA open-ended would strengthen the evidence.
- Limited ablations: The gains attributed to high-order attention would be more convincing with systematic ablations (unary only, +pairwise, +ternary), and with analysis of the learned α/β/γ weights to substantiate the claim that modalities rely differently on potential orders.

Quality
The method is technically sound as an attention/fusion architecture, and the training setup is standard. However, theoretical claims about probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference are not supported by a formal model or derivations; the attention equations are better viewed as learned potential combinations rather than approximate inference in a well-defined graphical model. Experimental validation appears promising but is incomplete: key numerical results are deferred to a missing table; fairness of comparisons (use of answer choices) should be established; and computational costs of ternary terms should be reported. Qualitative visualizations are helpful, and a failure case is discussed.

Clarity
The paper is mostly readable, but several definitions need tightening:
- Precisely define the joint model (if any) and the role of “mean-field”; or remove the probabilistic inference claim and present this as a deterministic potential-combination mechanism.
- Specify whether C3 is constructed explicitly, and give time/memory complexity in terms of nv, nq, na, d; clarify that, for MC, na equals the number of choices, not the full answer vocabulary.
- Clarify the shapes and sharing of marginalization weights (e.g., wiv vs wiq vs wiv,ia), and report total parameter counts for these layers.
- Include complete quantitative results and standard deviations across runs; detail whether all baselines used identical backbones and input modalities (e.g., answer choices).

Originality
The decomposition into unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and the attempt to interpret attentions as marginal probabilities offer a fresh perspective relative to prior co-attention/bilinear models. That said, the underlying building blocks (similarity matrices, bilinear pooling, compact sketching) are established, and the approach is a well-crafted combination rather than a fundamentally new modeling paradigm. Extensibility to more modalities is a plus.

Significance
If the computational issues are resolved and the empirical gains hold across datasets and settings (including open-ended), this approach could be adopted by practitioners as a flexible way to incorporate higher-order interactions. The explicit ternary attention over (image, question, answers) is particularly relevant for multiple-choice VQA. The work advances practical multimodal attention design, though its broader theoretical impact is modest.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please formalize the probabilistic model or reframe the approach without mean-field claims.
- Provide complexity/memory analysis for C2/C3 and implementation details (explicit vs implicit computation).
- Report ablations: unary vs +pairwise vs +ternary; with/without answer-input; effects of α/β/γ; MCT vs stacked MCB across architectures.
- Clarify evaluation protocol: whether baselines use answer choices as inputs; report open-ended results and additional datasets if possible.
- Analyze attention quality quantitatively (e.g., correlation with human attention maps as in Das et al.).
- Discuss the impact of embedding dimensionality (512 vs 2048) to ensure fair comparisons.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance with a compact, generalizable architecture.
- Clear, modular design that disentangles and combines different correlation orders.
- Useful qualitative insights and practical fusion via compact sketching.

Cons for acceptance
- Probabilistic framing is not rigorously supported.
- Incomplete reporting and unclear fairness of comparisons.
- Computational tractability of ternary attention insufficiently justified.
- Limited breadth of evaluation and ablation.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a practical and potentially impactful architecture for high-order multimodal attention with competitive results, but would benefit from clearer modeling, stronger ablations, and more thorough evaluation to substantiate its claims.
