Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a general multi-modal attention mechanism that models high-order correlations across modalities via learned “potentials.” Specifically, it defines unary potentials over each modality (image, question, answer), pairwise potentials over pairs (e.g., image–question), and a ternary potential over all three (image–question–answer). Attention distributions are computed by a softmax over a learned linear combination of these potentials—interpreted as a single mean-field iteration—to produce marginal attentions per modality. The attended modality vectors are then combined for decision making using compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) to approximate outer products. The method is evaluated on VQA (multiple-choice), showing improvements over strong baselines, with qualitative visualizations illustrating how pairwise/ternary terms sharpen attention conditioned on the question and candidate answers.

Relative to prior work: co-attention mechanisms for two modalities are well-studied (e.g., Lu et al. 2016; Fukui et al. 2016 using MCB; Xu et al. 2016 multi-hop; Yang et al. 2016 stacked attention; Kim et al. 2017 low-rank bilinear pooling; Nam et al. 2016 dual attention). This paper’s novelty lies in (1) explicitly formulating attention as a sum of learned higher-order potentials (unary/pairwise/ternary) with modality-marginalized effects, and (2) extending beyond pairwise co-attention to include a three-way (V,Q,A) term. The use of MCB/MCT for decision fusion follows prior work; the main contribution is the attention formulation and its demonstrated value.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The modules are clearly defined and implementable. Unary potentials are 1×1 convs (linear projections with nonlinearity). Pairwise potentials use similarity matrices C2 from projected features; ternary uses C3 from triple inner products. Attention distributions are obtained by a softmax over a weighted sum of marginalized potentials. While the paper refers to “mean-field inference,” the probabilistic formulation is informal: no explicit joint energy over all variables is specified, and the “mean-field” amounts to one softmax over a linear combination of potentials with learned mixture weights. This is pragmatic but not theoretically rigorous.
- Empirical support: The paper reports state-of-the-art performance on VQA multiple-choice and shows that adding the third modality improves over a 2-modality variant. Training details, hyperparameters, and parameter counts are provided, and ablations between MCT vs two-layer MCB are reported. However, deeper ablations would strengthen the case (e.g., incremental contributions of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials, and the learned mixture weights’ roles).
- Completeness: The work is a complete system with code referenced. The evaluation is limited to VQA multiple-choice; open-ended VQA and other datasets/tasks are not explored.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The explanation of potential construction and modality-marginalization is helpful, and qualitative visualizations support intuition.
- There are some notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., using aV twice for image and answer; missing definitions for convolutional weights in marginalization; occasional ambiguity in tensor shapes). Providing explicit tensor dimensions when marginalizing C2/C3 and clarifying the learned 1×1 conv weights used for marginalization would improve reproducibility. The “mean-field” terminology could be toned down or formalized with an explicit energy.

Originality:
- Moderate. Pairwise co-attention and bilinear pooling are established; the main novelty is the explicit higher-order (ternary) attention via correlation tensors and its clean decomposition into unary/pairwise/ternary components with learned mixture weights. The claim of easy extensibility to more modalities is conceptually sound but unverified empirically, and scaling concerns for higher-order tensors are not discussed in depth.

Significance:
- The paper demonstrates improved performance with fewer parameters compared to prior MCB-based systems, and the qualitative analyses suggest better question-conditioned localization. These are of interest to the VQA/attention community.
- Scope is limited to VQA MC; broader applicability (other datasets, open-ended answers, or different multimodal tasks) is asserted but not shown. The use of answer candidates as an input modality can be effective in MC but is less applicable to open-ended settings; the paper acknowledges related critiques and presents a 2-modality variant, though its performance lags.

Pros:
- Clear, modular attention formulation unifying unary, pairwise, and ternary terms.
- Empirical gains on standard VQA MC benchmarks with fewer parameters than some baselines.
- Good qualitative visualizations illustrating the role of higher-order correlations.
- Practical decision fusion via MCB/MCT and a competitive, reproducible system.

Cons:
- “Probabilistic”/“mean-field” framing is heuristic; lacks a precise joint energy/inference derivation or iterative updates.
- Limited ablations isolating contributions of each potential; no analysis of learned mixture weights α,β,γ.
- Evaluation restricted to VQA MC; no open-ended results or tests on other multimodal tasks.
- Comparisons to some contemporaneous strong bilinear variants (e.g., MLB/MFB) are not fully presented; decision fusion section suggests they were tried, but comprehensive quantitative comparisons are missing.
- Scalability/generalization to >3 modalities is claimed but untested; computational cost for ternary correlations is not analyzed in detail.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a formal energy-based model and, if feasible, iterative mean-field updates to substantiate the probabilistic claims; alternatively, reframe without probabilistic terminology.
- Add ablations: (i) unary only, (ii) +pairwise, (iii) +ternary; report deltas. Analyze learned α/β/γ weights across modalities.
- Include open-ended VQA results and/or another multimodal benchmark to demonstrate generality.
- Expand quantitative comparisons to contemporaneous bilinear/low-rank variants (e.g., MLB/MFB) under matched settings.
- Clarify tensor shapes and marginalization mechanics; fix notation/typos.

Overall assessment:
- Quality: good empirically, moderate theoretical grounding.
- Clarity: good, with minor issues.
- Originality: moderate—new combination and explicit ternary attention.
- Significance: good within VQA; broader impact would benefit from additional experiments.

On balance, the paper presents a useful and effective attention formulation with solid empirical results and clear practical value for VQA. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening clarity and ablations.
