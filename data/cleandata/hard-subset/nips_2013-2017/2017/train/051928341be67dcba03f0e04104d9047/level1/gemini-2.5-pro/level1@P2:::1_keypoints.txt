- Proposes a novel attention mechanism treating attention as probabilistic inference over unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials for multi-modal tasks (demonstrated on VQA).  
- Computes unary (within-modality), pairwise (between two modalities), and ternary (image, question, answer) potentials, linearly combines them with learnable weights, and applies softmax as a single mean-field–like step.  
- Fuses attended features using Multimodal Compact Bilinear/Trilinear Pooling for final decision-making.  
- Builds on prior attention and bilinear pooling VQA work but distinguishes itself by explicitly modeling and learning to combine different orders of interaction.  
- Novelty: inclusion of the answer modality in a third-order attention potential is a key original contribution.  
- Quality: technically solid with rigorous quantitative results showing state-of-the-art performance on VQA test-dev and test-standard.  
- Ablation: 3-modality model significantly outperforms 2-modality variant, supporting the core claim about higher-order correlations.  
- Qualitative analysis: includes attention visualizations and a failure-case analysis, demonstrating careful evaluation.  
- Major theoretical concern: calling the linear-weighted softmax step a mean-field inference iteration is tenuous and lacks formal probabilistic justification.  
- Major practical concern: ternary potential tensor C3 scales as nv * nq * na (na=3000), suggesting a potentially severe computational bottleneck not addressed.  
- Reproducibility issue: weights w_iv, w_iq, w_iv,ia are introduced without definition—unclear if learnable or fixed—critical for implementation.  
- Reproducibility issue: description of the "2-layer MCB unit combination" is too brief and confusing (claimed four-term feature form), hindering reimplementation.  
- Clarity: overall writing and diagrams are good, but Sections 3.2.2 and 3.2.3 lack crucial methodological details.  
- Significance: contributions are broadly applicable beyond VQA (e.g., multi-modal tasks with three+ streams) and likely influential.  
- Arguments for acceptance: principled extensible framework, SOTA results, strong experiments and ablations, high potential impact.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing/unclear implementation details, weak theoretical framing of mean-field claim, and unaddressed computational costs.  
- Recommendation: accept, contingent on revisions to clarify Section 3.2 and 4.2, define the w weights, detail the MCB setup, and discuss computational tractability.
