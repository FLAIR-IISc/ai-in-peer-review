Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a multimodal attention framework that explicitly models unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions among modalities (image V, question Q, and answers A) for visual question answering (VQA). Attentions over each modality are produced by treating attention as a probabilistic model with “potentials”: unary potentials over features within a modality, pairwise potentials capturing cross-modal similarity (e.g., Q–V), and a ternary potential capturing joint interactions among all three modalities (Q–V–A). The final attention over each modality is obtained by a softmax over a learned linear combination of its unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, which the authors motivate as a single mean-field iteration. The attended modality vectors are then fused for decision making using Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) pooling or a trilinear extension (MCT), relying on tensor sketching to approximate outer products efficiently.

Relative to prior VQA attention mechanisms—co-attention (Lu et al., NIPS 2016), bilinear/low-rank pooling (Kim et al., ICLR 2017), stacked attention (Yang et al., 2016), dynamic memory (Xiong et al., 2016), and MCB (Fukui et al., 2016)—the core distinction is (i) an explicit high-order potential view that unifies unary, pairwise, and ternary attentions, and (ii) the use of ternary attention that includes the answer options as a modality, particularly pertinent for the multiple-choice setting. The work also relates to structured attention (Kim et al., 2017) but differs by instantiating concrete high-order correlation tensors and a learned mixture of potentials.

Strengths:
- Clear, modular formulation of attention as a mixture of k-order potentials with learnable weights per modality. The architecture is easy to extend to additional modalities.
- Sound engineering: pairwise correlations via similarity matrices; ternary interactions via a 3-way correlation tensor; and practical fusion using tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to control parameter growth.
- Empirical results on VQA (multiple-choice) indicate competitive or state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters than some baselines, and qualitative visualizations demonstrate interpretable attention behavior.
- Ablations compare MCT vs two-layer MCB and two- vs three-modality settings, suggesting benefits from modeling higher-order interactions.

Weaknesses:
- Probabilistic justification is underdeveloped. The method is described as performing mean-field inference, but an explicit energy/factor graph (with factors for unary/pairwise/ternary potentials), a derivation of the update equations, and conditions under which the proposed linear combination corresponds to a mean-field step are not provided. The learnable scalar combination with softmax appears heuristic rather than a principled inference update.
- Computational aspects of ternary correlations are not fully addressed. Constructing C3 over nv×nq×na with d-dimensional inner products can be expensive; the paper does not analyze runtime/memory or propose approximations for attention computation (tensor sketch is only used for decision fusion).
- Scope of evaluation is limited. Results focus on the multiple-choice version of VQA v1.0; open-ended evaluation is not reported. It remains unclear whether gains are due to modeling the answer modality (which is unavailable in open-ended) or the high-order attention itself.
- Limited ablations on potential orders. While two- vs three-modality is compared, more granular studies (e.g., removing ternary potential while keeping pairwise, or varying the α/β/γ mixtures) would clarify which components drive improvements.
- Some clarity issues in notation and implementation details: definitions of the 1×1 “conv along a dimension” with weights wiv/wiq, the precise shapes of correlation tensors and reductions, and the training objective for multiple-choice vs open-ended classification could be better specified. There are minor typos and formatting glitches.

Quality:
The model is technically plausible and implemented with standard components (1×1 convs, similarity matrices/tensors, tensor sketching). The main claims—improved attention via high-order correlations and competitive VQA performance—are supported by quantitative results and qualitative examples, though the empirical section would benefit from fuller tables and statistical rigor. The lack of a formal derivation for the mean-field claim weakens the theoretical grounding. Efficiency considerations for ternary attention are insufficiently discussed.

Clarity:
Overall readable and well-organized, with intuitive figures illustrating unary/pairwise/ternary attentions and decision fusion. However, the probabilistic framing is not rigorously connected to the presented updates, and some notation is ambiguous. The experimental description includes many hyperparameters but omits precise evaluation protocol details and complete result tables.

Originality:
Moderate. Pairwise co-attention and bilinear pooling are well-studied; the novelty lies in systematically incorporating ternary attention (including the answer modality) and unifying attention computation as a learned mixture of k-order potentials. Extending MCB to MCT is incremental but practical. The notion of high-order attention is a reasonable and timely extension.

Significance:
Moderate. The approach is likely of interest to researchers building multimodal attention models and may spur further work on higher-order interactions. However, its practical impact is constrained by evaluation focused on multiple-choice VQA and limited analysis of computational trade-offs. Demonstrating benefits on open-ended VQA or other multimodal tasks would strengthen significance.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a concrete probabilistic model (e.g., a CRF over modality indices) with an energy function, derive the mean-field updates, and justify the learned mixture coefficients.
- Analyze computational and memory complexity of computing C3 and discuss scalability (e.g., low-rank factorization or sketching for attention computation).
- Expand evaluations to open-ended VQA and, if possible, more recent datasets (e.g., VQA v2.0) to test generality beyond multiple-choice and sensitivity to answer priors.
- Add ablations isolating unary/pairwise/ternary contributions, multi-iteration vs single-step updates, and the effect of including answer modality vs not.
- Clarify implementation details for pairwise/ternary reductions and provide complete quantitative tables and standard deviations; consider releasing code.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear and general framework for high-order multimodal attention.
- Competitive performance with fewer parameters; informative visualizations.
- Conceptually simple, extensible to more modalities.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Probabilistic “mean-field” claim not rigorously justified.
- Limited evaluation scope (primarily multiple-choice VQA v1.0) and modest ablations.
- Computational considerations for ternary attention not fully addressed.
- Novelty is incremental relative to existing co-attention and bilinear pooling methods.
