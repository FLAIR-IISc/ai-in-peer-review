- Proposes multimodal attention modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image (V), question (Q), and answer (A), with MF-inspired softmax marginalization.  
- Uses tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to approximate high-order outer products for decision fusion.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA (multiple-choice) with fewer parameters than prior systems.  
- Positions work relative to co-attention, stacked attention, DMN, bilinear pooling, and structured attention, claiming cleaner generalization to higher-order correlations.  
- Methodology is sound, clearly specified, and provides concrete potential definitions and a practical fusion strategy.  
- Empirical results show meaningful gains, especially when moving from 2- to 3-modality attention.  
- Treatment of mean-field is shallow (single learned linear combination); needs justification for skipping multiple MF iterations.  
- No analysis of computational/memory costs for building C3 over nv×nq×na; a complexity/runtime table is missing.  
- Comparisons sometimes use different embeddings/backbones (e.g., d=512 vs 2048), which may confound fairness.  
- Missing ablation studies isolating each potential’s contribution (e.g., remove ternary or pairwise).  
- Paper is generally well written with precise notation and helpful figures, though some typos remain.  
- A brief derivation connecting Eq.(1) to mean-field and an explicit complexity analysis would improve clarity.  
- Modeling attention as high-order potential-based and MF-style marginalization is original and principled.  
- Integration with MCT/MCB for fusion is sensible and practical.  
- Results are significant and likely reusable beyond VQA, but evaluation is limited to VQA v1 multiple-choice; testing on VQA v2 and open-ended tasks is needed.  
- Pros: clear principled formulation, simple extensible design, good qualitative visualizations, demonstrates 3-modality benefit.  
- Cons: limited evaluation scope, missing ablations and runtime/complexity analysis for ternary potentials, fairness concerns in comparisons, and insufficient mean-field justification.
