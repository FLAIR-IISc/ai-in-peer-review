- Proposes a general attention framework modelling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image regions, question words, and candidate answers, with attention as a weighted sum of marginalized potentials followed by softmax.  
- Uses a single mean-field–style iteration to obtain attentions and feeds attended vectors to a decision module using MCB and a trilinear MCT extension.  
- Evaluated on VQA multiple-choice (qualitative attention visualizations and quantitative test-dev/test-std), reporting state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters and code released.  
- Builds on prior VQA attention and bilinear pooling work, extending co-attention from two modalities to explicit ternary modelling including answer candidates.  
- Architecture and training details are clearly specified and reproducible, with implemented potentials as learned linear projections and dot products.  
- The “mean-field” probabilistic framing is superficial: method reduces to learned weighted sums plus softmax with no explicit energy or iterative inference evidence.  
- Empirically strong on multiple-choice VQA with ablations showing benefit of three modalities and comparisons to co-attention/MCB; qualitative analyses show meaningful question-dependent attention.  
- Quantitative evaluation is incomplete: submission lacks tables, omits open-ended VQA and VQA v2, and uses a weaker 2-modality baseline embedding (512 vs. 2048) weakening fairness.  
- Computing full ternary correlations C3 is potentially expensive (O(nq·nv·na·d)) and tractable only for small na (e.g., ~18 MC choices); memory/runtime not reported.  
- Paper is generally well written and organized with implementation specifics, but contains notation/typo issues (e.g., duplicate aV, “and and”, smax) and ambiguities about answer embedding usage.  
- Main novelty is explicit high-order/ternary attention across modalities with learnable mixing of unary/pairwise/ternary potentials; MCT is a natural MCB extension.  
- Related fusion methods (MLB, MFB, MUTAN) are not sufficiently discussed or compared; prior use of answers as inputs for MC has precedent.  
- If validated on broader benchmarks, the approach is impactful and generalizable to other multimodal tasks; ternary attention is especially relevant for multiple-choice VQA.  
- Limitations: focus on the easier MC setting, lack of VQA v2/open-ended results, shallow probabilistic claim, and unanalysed computational costs for ternary attention.  
- Pros: clear modular formulation, strong MC results with fewer parameters, simple implementability, useful qualitative analyses, and inclusion of compact trilinear pooling.  
- Cons: weak mean-field justification, missing complexity/runtime analysis, incomplete comparisons to contemporaries, limited evaluation scope, and minor clarity issues.  
- Suggested improvements: add full quantitative tables, include open-ended and VQA v2 results, compare fairly to MLB/MFB/MUTAN, report C3 runtime/memory and clarify MC-only use, and either implement iterative mean-field or drop the probabilistic claim.  
- Additional suggestions: ablate unary/pairwise/ternary contributions and mixing weights, analyze robustness to embedding dims and glimpses, clarify answer-embedding/training protocols, and fix notation/typos.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept leaning to accept — intuitive and practically effective for MC VQA but needs stronger comparisons, complexity clarification, and broader evaluation to be fully compelling.
