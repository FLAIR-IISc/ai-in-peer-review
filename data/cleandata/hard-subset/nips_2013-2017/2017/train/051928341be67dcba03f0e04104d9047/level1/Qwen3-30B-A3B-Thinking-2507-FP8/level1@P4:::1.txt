

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

The paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations (beyond pairwise) between multiple data modalities (visual, textual, etc.) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors formulate attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to obtain marginal attention probabilities. They argue that previous attention mechanisms (e.g., [15, 25]) primarily focus on pairwise interactions between two modalities and are often task-specific, while their approach provides a general framework applicable to any number of modalities. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, achieving 64.57% accuracy on the test-standard dataset using a 3-modality model.

The paper builds on recent NIPS work on attention mechanisms for VQA, particularly Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], which use pairwise attention. The authors clearly distinguish their approach by modeling higher-order correlations explicitly. They also relate their work to related NIPS papers on attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks, such as [11, 13, 14, 19].

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical soundness:** The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the high-order attention mechanism with well-defined unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The mean-field inference approach is theoretically sound and well-explained. The experimental design is thorough, including both quantitative results (Table 1) and qualitative attention visualizations (Fig. 5-8).

2. **State-of-the-art results:** The model achieves 64.57% accuracy on the VQA test-standard dataset, outperforming previous methods including Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7] despite having fewer parameters (40M vs. 70M). The authors provide a convincing argument that the high-order attention mechanism contributes to this performance gain.

3. **Clarity and organization:** The paper is well-structured with clear figures (Fig. 3-4) that effectively illustrate the attention mechanisms. The mathematical formulations are accessible, and the implementation details (Section 4.1-4.2) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

4. **Novelty:** The high-order attention concept represents a meaningful extension beyond existing pairwise attention mechanisms. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from previous work, particularly in its ability to model interactions across more than two modalities without requiring task-specific engineering.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper does not provide a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials). It's unclear how much each potential contributes to the final performance, making it difficult to assess the necessity of the high-order components.

2. **Limited analysis of higher-order extensions:** While the authors claim the approach can be trivially extended to any number of modalities, they don't demonstrate this beyond ternary (three modalities). A study showing the performance impact of increasing the order beyond ternary would strengthen their claim.

3. **Computational complexity analysis:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of their approach compared to previous methods, particularly regarding the tensor operations for higher-order potentials. This is important for understanding the practical applicability of their method.

4. **Incomplete comparison of decision units:** While the paper compares MCT and 2-layer MCB, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why 2-layer MCB outperforms MCT. A more thorough comparison of the two decision-making approaches would be valuable.

**Overall assessment:**

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks. The high-order attention concept is novel and well-motivated, and the state-of-the-art results on VQA provide strong empirical support. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to the NIPS community.

The main weaknesses are the lack of comprehensive ablation studies and limited analysis of higher-order extensions beyond ternary potentials. However, these do not undermine the core contribution, which is well-justified and demonstrated with strong results.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel high-order attention mechanism that explicitly models correlations across multiple modalities
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA with fewer parameters than competing methods
- Clear theoretical formulation with well-explained probabilistic model
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation with both quantitative and qualitative results
- Well-motivated by clear distinctions from previous work

*Con:*
- Insufficient ablation studies to isolate contributions of different attention components
- Limited exploration of extending the approach beyond ternary potentials
- Missing computational complexity analysis for higher-order operations
- Incomplete comparison of decision units (MCT vs. 2-layer MCB)

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of multimodal learning by extending attention mechanisms to model high-order correlations. While some aspects of the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, the core technical contribution is sound and impactful. The work is clearly within NIPS scope as it addresses a fundamental problem in multimodal learning with novel methodological contributions. The paper advances the state of the art in VQA and provides a general framework applicable to other multimodal tasks. I recommend acceptance.
