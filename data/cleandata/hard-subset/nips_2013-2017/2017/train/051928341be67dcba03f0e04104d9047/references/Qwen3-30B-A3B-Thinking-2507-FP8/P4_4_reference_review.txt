

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

## Summary of Main Ideas
This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., visual, textual, and answer modalities) for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors extend existing attention mechanisms, which typically focus on pairwise interactions between two modalities (e.g., image and question), to model k-th order correlations between k modalities. Their approach represents attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and higher-order potentials, using mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities. The method is applied to VQA, where the authors claim state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset. The paper relates to previous NIPS work on attention mechanisms, particularly Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016), which focus on pairwise attention for VQA, but argues their approach generalizes to any number of modalities.

## Quality
The technical approach is generally sound, with a clear probabilistic formulation of high-order attention. The implementation details are well-described, and the experimental setup follows standard practices for VQA. However, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis of the mean-field approximation used in the model. The authors claim state-of-the-art performance, but the results (Table 1) show only modest improvements over existing methods (e.g., 0.75% improvement with MCT vs. 2-layer MCB on test-dev). The paper does not provide statistical significance tests for these improvements, which weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations in their approach, such as the failure case shown in Figure 8.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures (Figures 3-8) that effectively illustrate the attention mechanisms. The mathematical notation is generally clear, though the transition from the probabilistic model to the implementation details could be more explicit. The paper adequately explains the proposed method and its application to VQA. The qualitative results (Figures 5-7) provide valuable insights into how the attention mechanism works, demonstrating that the model attends to relevant regions based on the question asked.

## Originality
The paper's main claim is that it extends attention mechanisms to model high-order correlations between multiple modalities, rather than limiting to pairwise interactions. While this is a natural extension of existing work, the novelty is somewhat limited. Previous NIPS papers have explored bilinear models for multimodal interactions (e.g., Kim et al., 2016; Nam et al., 2016), and the proposed approach bears similarities to these methods. The paper fails to sufficiently distinguish its approach from these existing techniques, particularly regarding the distinction between "high-order correlations" and bilinear models. The authors cite relevant work but do not adequately compare their method to the most similar approaches in the literature.

## Significance
The significance of the work is limited by the modest performance improvements over existing methods and the lack of demonstration of broader applicability beyond VQA. The paper claims to achieve "state-of-the-art" performance, but the improvements are marginal (e.g., 0.75% on test-dev), and the paper does not provide sufficient context about how these results compare to other recent VQA methods. The approach's generalizability to any number of modalities is a useful property, but the paper does not demonstrate this in any other task beyond VQA. Without evidence of significant performance gains or broader applicability, the contribution's significance is questionable.

## Strengths
1. Clear articulation of the limitation of existing attention mechanisms (pairwise interactions only).
2. Generalizable approach to any number of modalities, which is a useful property.
3. Competitive results on VQA with modest improvements over existing methods.
4. Excellent qualitative analysis of attention patterns, demonstrating how the model attends to relevant regions based on the question.

## Weaknesses
1. Limited novelty - the approach is a straightforward extension of pairwise attention, with insufficient distinction from existing bilinear models.
2. Modest performance improvements over baselines, with no statistical significance testing.
3. Insufficient comparison to related work, particularly bilinear models (e.g., Kim et al., 2016).
4. Lack of theoretical analysis of the mean-field inference approximation.
5. Overstated claims of "state-of-the-art" performance without sufficient context.

## Recommendation
The paper presents a technically sound extension of attention mechanisms to higher-order correlations, with competitive results on VQA. However, the novelty is limited, the improvements over existing methods are modest, and the significance of the contribution is questionable. While the approach is generalizable to any number of modalities, the paper fails to demonstrate sufficient advantages over existing methods to justify the proposed extension. The authors should more clearly distinguish their approach from similar bilinear models in the literature and provide stronger justification for why higher-order correlations are necessary for VQA.

Pro: Clear articulation of problem, generalizable approach, competitive results, good qualitative analysis.
Con: Limited novelty, modest improvements, insufficient comparison to related work, lack of theoretical analysis.

Overall, while the paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field, it does not represent a significant advance over existing work and falls short of the threshold for acceptance at NIPS.
