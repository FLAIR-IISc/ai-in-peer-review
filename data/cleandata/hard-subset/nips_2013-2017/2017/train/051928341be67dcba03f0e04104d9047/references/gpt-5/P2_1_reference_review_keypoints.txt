- Proposes a general high-order attention framework modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and candidate answers, combined via a learned linear mixture and softmax to produce attention.  
- Implements pairwise correlations with learned linear projections and inner products, and ternary correlations via a third-order tensor of inner products.  
- Interprets the softmax over marginalized potentials as a single mean-field iteration, though the derivation is informal.  
- Uses compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) via tensor sketching to fuse attended features for decision making.  
- Claims state-of-the-art results on VQA v1 multiple-choice with fewer parameters than prior work and shows qualitative attention visualizations.  
- Positions work relative to co-attention, MCB, bilinear/low-rank pooling, structured attention, and dual attention, claiming novelty in explicit higher-order dependency modeling.  
- Technical constructions (unary/pairwise/ternary) are reasonable and implementable, but the “mean-field” justification lacks a formal CRF/variational derivation.  
- Empirical support includes training details and code link, but key quantitative results (test-dev/test-std numbers, confidence intervals) and thorough comparisons are missing.  
- Ablation analysis is limited: no isolated tests for unary/pairwise/ternary contributions, no analysis of learned mixing weights, and unclear runtime/memory costs of C3.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-motivated, but contains notation typos/ambiguities and needs formal mean-field derivation and clearer experimental reporting (features, stds, seeds).  
- Originality is moderate: unifying higher-order attention potentials and extending compact pooling to three modalities is incremental but meaningful, with practical extensibility limits due to combinatorial growth.  
- Significance depends on fair SOTA claims; higher-order attention and MCT are promising but validation is narrow (only VQA v1 MC).  
- Strengths: clear general formulation, practical tensor-sketching engineering, qualitative visualizations, and parameter-efficient competitive results.  
- Weaknesses: informal probabilistic framing, incomplete quantitative details and ablations, scalability concerns beyond three modalities, and minor reproducibility/notation issues.  
- Key questions for authors include reporting exact accuracies with error bars, clarifying baseline inputs for MC, ablations removing potentials or fixing weights, memory/runtime costs of C3, and experiments with multiple mean-field iterations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a clean high-order attention mechanism, implementable end-to-end, and potentially strong empirical utility.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient empirical rigor and fairness checks, rhetorical mean-field claim without derivation, limited evaluation scope and ablation depth.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept — interesting idea with merit but needs clearer derivations, stronger quantitative evidence, and more comprehensive ablations.
