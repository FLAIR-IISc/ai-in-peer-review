

# Peer Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors introduce unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture correlations between modalities, using mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities. They apply this to VQA, achieving state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks. The approach extends existing work on multimodal attention (particularly Fukui et al. [7] and Lu et al. [15]) by providing a probabilistic framework that can handle any number of modalities through high-order potentials.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound methodology with clear mathematical formalization. The experimental setup is thorough, with appropriate baselines (Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7]) and comprehensive evaluation on both test-dev and test-std datasets. The authors provide both quantitative results (showing marginal improvements over state-of-the-art) and qualitative analysis of attention visualizations. The model's efficiency (40M parameters vs. 70M+ in some baselines) is well-documented.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with logical flow from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. Key concepts like unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials are clearly illustrated with Figure 3. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the implementation details (data embedding, decision making) are sufficiently described for replication. The qualitative analysis in Figures 5-8 effectively demonstrates the attention behavior.

**Significance:** The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA with a relatively simple model architecture, demonstrating that high-order attention correlations can improve performance without requiring massive model complexity. The probabilistic interpretation of attention provides a principled framework that could generalize beyond VQA.

## Weaknesses

**Originality:** While the paper claims novelty in modeling "high-order correlations," the approach is quite similar to existing multimodal attention work. The ternary attention module is directly inspired by Fukui et al.'s MCT (Multimodal Compact Trilinear Pooling), which the authors cite but don't sufficiently differentiate from. The paper doesn't adequately establish how their high-order attention differs from existing work that also models multiple modalities. The claim that "our model develops a probabilistic model based on high order potentials" appears to be a rephrasing of existing approaches rather than a fundamentally new contribution. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of their probabilistic framework with existing attention mechanisms.

**Quality:** The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance but the improvement over previous work is marginal (64.57% vs. 63.82% for MCT vs. 2-layer MCB). The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why high-order attention is fundamentally better than simpler approaches. The 2-modality baseline's inferior performance is attributed to lower embedding dimension (512 vs. 2048), which suggests the performance gap might be due to implementation details rather than the core attention mechanism.

**Clarity:** The relationship between the proposed high-order attention model and existing multimodal pooling techniques (MCB, MCT) needs clarification. The paper claims their approach "permits trivial extension to any number of modalities" but provides no evidence of such extension beyond three modalities. The tensor sketch implementation details could be more thoroughly explained for reproducibility.

**Significance:** While the results are competitive, the paper doesn't sufficiently argue why high-order attention is necessary for VQA. The qualitative analysis shows attention correlates with question words, but doesn't convincingly demonstrate that high-order attention provides unique benefits beyond pairwise attention. The failure case in Figure 8 (identical attention for different questions) suggests limitations that aren't adequately addressed.

## Recommendation

**Pro Acceptance:** The paper presents a well-structured, technically sound approach to attention mechanisms with good experimental validation. The model achieves competitive results with a relatively small parameter count. The probabilistic interpretation of attention offers a principled framework that could be valuable for future work.

**Con Acceptance:** The novelty is questionable as the approach closely follows existing multimodal attention work. The performance improvements are marginal, and the paper doesn't convincingly establish why high-order attention is fundamentally better than simpler alternatives. The connection to prior work (particularly Fukui et al. [7]) needs stronger differentiation.

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid paper that makes a modest contribution to the field of multimodal attention mechanisms. While the results are competitive, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how their approach fundamentally differs from existing work, particularly the MCB/MCT framework they cite. The marginal performance improvements don't fully justify the claim of "state-of-the-art" without clearer evidence of the necessity of high-order attention.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but the novelty is somewhat overstated. To strengthen the paper, the authors should more clearly articulate the theoretical and practical advantages of their high-order attention framework compared to existing methods, rather than simply extending the MCB/MCT approach with a probabilistic interpretation. They should also provide evidence of extending their model to more than three modalities to fully demonstrate their claim of "easy extension to any number of data modalities."

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the paper would benefit from addressing these concerns in revision. The contribution is meaningful but not groundbreaking, and the novelty claim needs to be tempered with a more precise comparison to existing work.
