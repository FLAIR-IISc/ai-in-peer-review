

# Peer Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that extends beyond pairwise attention to model high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer). The authors formulate attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to compute marginal probabilities. They claim state-of-the-art performance on VQA datasets by combining these high-order attention mechanisms with tensor sketching techniques (MCT/MCB) for decision making.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Probabilistic Formulation**: The paper provides a well-structured probabilistic framework for attention mechanisms, which is more principled than ad-hoc attention approaches. The mean-field inference approach is a reasonable choice for approximating high-order correlations.

2. **Effective Visualization**: The attention visualizations (Figs. 5-7) effectively demonstrate how the model focuses on relevant image regions and question words, providing valuable interpretability. The comparison with existing methods shows reasonable attention patterns.

3. **Theoretical Foundation**: The approach is grounded in probabilistic modeling with well-defined unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, which is a more principled approach than some recent ad-hoc attention mechanisms.

## Weaknesses

1. **Marginal Performance Gains**: The reported improvement over state-of-the-art (64.57% vs. 63.82% on val set) is extremely marginal in the VQA literature, where even 0.5% improvements are considered significant. The paper fails to convincingly justify the added complexity of high-order attention with this minimal gain.

2. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: Crucially, the paper provides no ablation studies to demonstrate that the higher-order terms (beyond pairwise) actually contribute meaningfully to performance. Without these, it's impossible to determine whether the ternary potential is beneficial or merely adding noise.

3. **Inadequate Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper compares to Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], but fails to benchmark against more recent state-of-the-art VQA models (e.g., those using transformers or more sophisticated attention mechanisms). The cited baselines are somewhat outdated for 2017.

4. **Theoretical Justification Missing**: The paper lacks any theoretical argument for why higher-order correlations would be beneficial for VQA. In fact, the authors acknowledge that "it is hard to extend" Lu et al.'s approach to more than two modalities, yet they don't demonstrate why their approach is better suited for this task.

5. **Computational Complexity Ignored**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of implementing ternary potentials, which would scale cubically with the number of features. For image features (196 patches), the ternary potential would require processing 196³ ≈ 7.5 million interactions, which is substantially more expensive than pairwise attention.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of attention mechanisms to higher-order correlations, the marginal performance gains and lack of evidence demonstrating the necessity of high-order attention undermine the contribution. The authors claim their approach "is easy to extend to any number of data modalities," but provide no evidence beyond the ternary case, and the computational cost of such extension is not discussed.

The paper would benefit significantly from:
1. Ablation studies showing the contribution of each attention term
2. Comparison to more recent state-of-the-art VQA models
3. Theoretical justification for why higher-order correlations are beneficial for VQA
4. Analysis of computational complexity and efficiency

## Recommendation

**Weak Reject**

The paper makes a marginal contribution to the VQA literature with a method that is not clearly justified by the performance gains. The theoretical framework is interesting but lacks sufficient empirical validation to support the claim that high-order attention is beneficial for VQA. The marginal improvement (0.75% absolute) does not justify the increased complexity, especially given the lack of ablation studies showing the necessity of the higher-order terms.

The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could demonstrate that higher-order attention provides meaningful improvements beyond pairwise attention, or if they could provide a compelling theoretical argument for why such correlations are important for VQA. Without these, the paper's contribution appears incremental rather than significant.

The authors should consider revising the paper to include more rigorous ablation studies and comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods before resubmitting. The core idea has potential but requires stronger empirical validation to be considered a meaningful contribution to the field.
