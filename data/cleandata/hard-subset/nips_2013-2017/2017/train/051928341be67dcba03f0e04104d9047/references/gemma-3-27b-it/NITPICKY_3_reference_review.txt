This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to address limitations in existing attention models by learning correlations between multiple data modalities. The core idea is to extend attention beyond pairwise interactions to higher-order relationships, modeled using potentials and mean-field inference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

While the presented idea of high-order attention is interesting and potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet accumulating, flaws that ultimately detract from its overall quality. My assessment is heavily influenced by these numerous details, leading me to recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly modeling high-order correlations between modalities in an attention mechanism is a potentially interesting direction.
*   **Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset is a positive indication.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing attention mechanisms and motivate their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Ablation:** While the paper compares to existing approaches, the ablation studies within the proposed method are insufficient. For example, the effect of removing the ternary potentials is not clearly presented, making it difficult to assess their actual contribution. The results in section 4.3 show that the MCB vs. MCT difference is small and doesn't justify the added complexity of MCT.
*   **Implementation Details and Reproducibility:** The description of several components lacks sufficient detail. For instance, the precise architecture of the convolutional layers used in the unary potentials is not fully specified. The exact hyperparameter settings, beyond the learning rate and batch size, are missing (e.g., weight decay, dropout probabilities for each layer), hindering reproducibility. The mention of the 2-layer MCB showing better results begs for a more thorough comparison, perhaps with a parameter sweep.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies and Clarity:** The notation is occasionally unclear and inconsistent. The use of different symbols for the same concepts (e.g., iv for image index, iq for question index) is acceptable, but the subscripting gets confusing. Eq. (1) uses 'smax' which is a non-standard softmax variant and should be clarified, or simply replaced by 'softmax'.
*   **Overly Complex Formulation:** The use of potentials and mean-field inference feels unnecessarily complex for the demonstrated improvement. Simpler alternatives might achieve comparable results with greater clarity and efficiency. The justification for choosing this specific probabilistic formulation is not strong.
*   **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The paper contains repetitive phrasing and unnecessary detail in some sections (e.g., the description of the data embedding). Some of the related work sections read as long lists of papers without providing insightful connections to the proposed method.
*   **Figure Quality and Information:** Several figures (e.g. Fig. 3, Fig. 4) lack sufficient labeling and clarity. The visualization in Fig. 5 and 6 is limited; showing a wider range of examples would strengthen the qualitative evaluation. Fig. 7 comparing with other methods is too small to properly assess differences in attention maps.
* **Minor Errors**: There are numerous minor formatting errors throughout the paper, including inconsistent spacing and punctuation. The bibliography is not uniformly formatted. In the introduction, the citation "[2]" is used without prior definition.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel approach to multimodal attention.
*   State-of-the-art results on VQA.

**Con:**

*   Insufficient ablation studies.
*   Lack of implementation details hindering reproducibility.
*   Overly complex formulation.
*   Numerous minor flaws in writing and presentation.
*   Unclear contribution from the ternary potential.
*   Small performance gain over existing methods doesn’t justify the added complexity.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea behind this paper has merit, the numerous shortcomings in clarity, implementation detail, and experimental analysis outweigh the novelty and performance gains. The paper feels rushed and lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS submission. The accumulation of minor flaws creates an impression of sloppiness and diminishes the perceived significance of the contribution. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the issues raised in this review – particularly focusing on improving clarity, providing detailed implementation specifications, and conducting more comprehensive ablation studies – before resubmitting to a future conference.
