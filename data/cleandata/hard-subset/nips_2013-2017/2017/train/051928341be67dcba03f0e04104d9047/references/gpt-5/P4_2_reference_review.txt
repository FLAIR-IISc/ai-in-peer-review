Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a general high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning, instantiated for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The key idea is to model attention as a set of learnable potentials over modalities: unary (per-modality), pairwise (between two modalities), and ternary (among three modalities: image V, question Q, and answers A). Potentials are computed via simple neural primitives: 1×1 convolutions for unaries and correlation-based similarity matrices/tensors (bilinear for pairs, trilinear for triples) for higher-order interactions. Attention distributions per modality are produced by a learned linear combination of the corresponding potentials followed by a softmax; the paper frames this as one iteration of mean-field inference. Attended features are then fused for decision making using tensor sketching: Multimodal Compact Bilinear pooling (MCB) or its trilinear extension (MCT). The method is evaluated on VQA with test-dev/test-std results claimed to be state-of-the-art at the time, qualitative attention visualizations, and ablations comparing MCB vs MCT and 2- vs 3-modality settings.

This work builds on a line of multimodal attention models for VQA including hierarchical co-attention (Lu et al., NIPS’16), stacked attention (Yang et al., 2016), dynamic memory (Xiong et al., 2016), and the use of bilinear pooling (Fukui et al., 2016; Kim et al., ICLR’17). Related “structured attention” formulations (Kim et al., 2017) connect attention and probabilistic inference. Compared to these, the paper’s novelty lies in explicitly constructing and learning a mixture of attention potentials at multiple orders and extending attention conditioning to three modalities by including answer choices as inputs for multiple-choice VQA, while using tensor sketching to keep fusion tractable.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The architectural components are standard and reasonable: 1×1 convs, bilinear/trilinear correlations, softmax attentions, and MCB/MCT fusion. The training setup and hyperparameters are provided, and a code link is included. However, the probabilistic framing is under-specified. The paper refers to mean-field inference over potentials but does not define a joint distribution or factor graph, nor derive the linear combination-of-potentials plus softmax as a principled approximation; it functions more as a learnable attention scoring module. Clarifying the probabilistic assumptions would strengthen the claim.
- Correctness and completeness of experiments: The evaluation includes test-dev/test-std numbers (table not in the excerpt), some ablations (MCT vs 2-layer MCB), and qualitative analyses. The reported gains when moving from 2- to 3-modality attention are consistent with the method’s premise. Missing ablations include: removing ternary or pairwise potentials while keeping others; learning weights α,β,γ vs fixed weighting; computational cost/memory of C3; effect of different backbones and embedding dimensions; and performance on open-ended VQA (when A is not available as input).
- Reproducibility: Optimizer, learning rate, dropout, dimensionalities, and data preprocessing are documented; public code is provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-organized, with clear high-level motivations, figures illustrating unary/pairwise/ternary modules, and implementation details. There are notable issues to address:
- Notation/typos: aA is mistakenly written as aV in the definition of attended answers; duplicated symbols and inconsistent subscripts (e.g., θQ,V vs θV,Q) can confuse. Shapes of weights wiv, wiq in the marginalization for pairwise/ternary potentials should be made explicit. Several minor grammar and formatting errors appear in text and references.
- Probabilistic interpretation: “Mean-field” claims should be formalized or rephrased as a learnable combination of potentials.
- Complexity: Computing C3 for multiple-choice (na≈18) is feasible, but complexity and memory scaling should be discussed explicitly.

Originality
The paper’s contributions are incremental but nontrivial: an explicit high-order attention decomposition (unary, pairwise, ternary) with learnable mixing, and extending attention to include the answer modality in multiple-choice VQA. While bilinear/trilinear pooling and co-attention existed, jointly learning multi-order attention potentials and conditioning attentions across three modalities is a useful generalization. The connection to structured attention is acknowledged but not developed theoretically.

Significance
For VQA, the approach yields competitive or state-of-the-art results (per the claims) with fewer parameters than some baselines, and the 3-modality setup provides measurable gains. The method is modular and potentially applicable to other multimodal tasks. The lack of broader task validation and limited ablation granularity temper the generality claims, but the empirical improvements on a key benchmark and code release increase practical impact.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, modular design for high-order multimodal attention; straightforward to extend to k modalities.
- Competitive/state-of-the-art VQA results with a comparatively compact model; improvement from 2- to 3-modality.
- Practical decision fusion via MCB/MCT; thorough training details and released code.
- Informative qualitative visualizations linking attention across modalities.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Probabilistic framing (mean-field over potentials) is not rigorously specified; essentially a neural attention scoring module.
- Missing ablations to isolate the contribution of each potential order and learned mixing coefficients.
- Limited evaluation scope (primarily multiple-choice VQA); no open-ended results or other datasets/tasks.
- Notation and clarity issues; minor but numerous typos and incomplete references.
- Computational complexity of ternary correlations not analyzed.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide a precise probabilistic model (factorization, energy, variational objective) or rephrase the method without mean-field claims.
- Clarify shapes and learning of the marginalization weights in pairwise/ternary potentials; ensure consistent notation.
- Detail the computational and memory costs of C2 and C3 for typical nq, nv, na; report training/inference time and peak memory.
- Add ablations: remove ternary or pairwise terms; fix vs learn α/β/γ; vary d and backbones (e.g., ResNet-152 with 2048-d).
- Report open-ended VQA results (without A as input); analyze the benefit of including A in multiple-choice vs not.
- Compare against additional strong bilinear baselines (e.g., MLB/MFB) under matched settings.

Overall assessment
- Quality: reasonable methodology and promising results; theoretical framing needs clarification; more ablations desirable.
- Clarity: good high-level presentation; notation and editing should be improved.
- Originality: moderate; coherent synthesis and extension to ternary attention.
- Significance: solid for VQA; broader impact plausible but unverified in this submission.
