Summary
This paper proposes a generally applicable attention mechanism that models high-order correlations among modalities via learned potentials and a simple mean-field-inspired combination. Concretely, it defines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image (V), question (Q), and answer candidates (A), where pairwise correlations are captured via similarity matrices and ternary via a three-way correlation tensor. Attention distributions over each modality are obtained by a softmax over a learned linear combination of the corresponding marginalized potentials. For decision making, the attended vectors are fused using tensor-sketch–based pooling (MCB/MCT) to avoid the parameter blow-up of explicit outer products. Applied to VQA (multiple-choice), the approach yields state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters, and qualitative visualizations suggest that pairwise and ternary potentials focus attention on task-relevant regions and words.

Relation to prior work
The work situates itself among co-attention and bilinear pooling methods for VQA (e.g., Lu et al. 2016 co-attention; Fukui et al. 2016 MCB; Kim et al. 2017 bilinear/low-rank). The novelty here is to cast attention as a probabilistic model over modalities with explicit high-order potentials and to compute marginal “attentions” via a single mean-field-like step. Unlike alternating or hierarchical co-attention, this formulation extends naturally to k modalities. Incorporating answer candidates as a modality follows Jabri et al. (2016), but here the answer is integrated through high-order attentional dependencies rather than only as classifier input. The decision module relies on known MCB/MCT.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean and general framework that unifies unary, pairwise, and higher-order attentional interactions across modalities, with a simple, learnable combination that resembles one iteration of mean-field. This is an appealing direction beyond bespoke two-modality co-attention.
- Extensibility: The formulation is explicit about how to scale to more modalities (k-th order potentials), which is a valuable perspective for future multimodal tasks.
- Empirical impact: Strong performance on VQA multiple-choice with fewer parameters than baselines; qualitative analyses indicate that the learned pairwise/ternary potentials meaningfully modulate attentions.
- Practicality: Uses tensor sketching to keep the decision stage tractable; code appears to be provided.

Weaknesses and questions
- Limited scope of evaluation: All results are on VQA multiple-choice. The method’s generality would be more convincing with open-ended VQA, and/or another dataset (e.g., Visual7W, VQA v2).
- Ablations are insufficient to isolate the contribution of high-order attention. The paper reports 2-modality vs 3-modality and MCT vs 2-layer MCB, but does not systematically ablate unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials, or the effect of including A specifically in attention versus only in the final classifier. A clear ablation table would greatly strengthen the causal claims.
- “Mean-field” claim is underdeveloped. The paper interprets the softmax over a linear combination of potentials as one mean-field iteration, but no underlying graphical model or variational objective is specified. This is fine pragmatically, but the probabilistic framing would benefit from a brief derivation or at least a precise statement of assumptions.
- Computational considerations: Computing C3 over (nq × nv × na) can be heavy (na up to 3000). The paper does not discuss complexity, memory, or batching strategies. Are ternary potentials computed over all answers or a candidate subset? Any low-rank/sampling tricks? This matters for “trivial extension to any number of modalities.”
- Clarity/typos: Some notation is confusing (e.g., reusing aV for aA in Section 3.2; “convolve along the visual dimension” essentially denotes a learned weighted sum); several grammatical issues; incomplete citations (venues/years). Figures seem helpful but the text should more clearly define shapes and operations.

Quality
The method is technically sound as a modeling contribution and the components (correlation tensors, learned marginalizations, MCB/MCT) are reasonable. However, claims around inference (mean-field) are more heuristic than derived, and the empirical validation, while promising, lacks broader benchmarks and thorough ablations to cleanly attribute the gains to high-order attention.

Clarity
Generally understandable, but could be improved: tighten notation, fix typos, and explicitly specify tensor dimensions, computational costs, and training details for high-order potentials. A small derivation or schematic of the implied factor graph would aid the probabilistic narrative.

Originality
Moderate-to-strong. While built from known pieces (co-attention, bilinear/trilinear interactions, MCB), the explicit high-order attentional potential framework and its extension to three modalities in the attention stage (not only in pooling) is a meaningful step beyond common two-modality designs at the time.

Significance
For VQA and broader multimodal reasoning, the idea to explicitly model and learn high-order attentional dependencies is important and likely to influence subsequent designs (e.g., for tasks with more modalities or structured outputs). Even with some experimental limitations, the conceptual contribution is valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: unary vs pairwise vs ternary; with/without answer-modality attention; effect of α/β/γ weights.
- Evaluate on open-ended VQA and at least one additional dataset (e.g., Visual7W, VQA v2).
- Clarify the probabilistic/inference story or rephrase as a learned potential combination without overclaiming mean-field.
- Discuss complexity and practical tricks for C3; consider compact/sketched correlations also at the attention stage.
- Improve writing/notation; fix typos and reference details.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, general, and bold approach to high-order multimodal attention.
- Demonstrates SOTA on MC-VQA with fewer parameters.
- Clear qualitative evidence of meaningful high-order attentional effects.
- Likely to inspire follow-up work on multi-modality attention.

Cons against acceptance
- Evaluation limited to one setting; insufficient ablations to isolate contributions.
- Inference justification is heuristic; unclear computational scaling.
- Clarity issues.

Overall recommendation
Accept. Despite some experimental and presentation gaps, the central idea—learning and combining high-order attention potentials across modalities with a simple, extensible mechanism—is a novel and promising direction. Given NeurIPS’s interest in forward-looking ideas, I believe the paper merits inclusion if the authors strengthen ablations and clarify the modeling and complexity aspects in the final version.
