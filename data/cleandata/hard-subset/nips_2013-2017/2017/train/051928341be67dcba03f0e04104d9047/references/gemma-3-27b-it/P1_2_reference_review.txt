## Peer Review for "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal data, specifically targeting Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea is to move beyond pairwise interactions between modalities (image and question) and learn *high-order* correlations – interactions between two or even three modalities simultaneously.  The authors frame this as a probabilistic model solved via mean-field inference, allowing for straightforward extension to any number of modalities. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the VQA dataset, achieving state-of-the-art performance. The work builds upon existing attention mechanisms in both vision and NLP, addressing limitations in their ability to capture complex inter-modal relationships.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of high-order attention is a genuinely interesting and potentially impactful contribution.  While bilinear pooling and similar techniques capture interactions, explicitly modeling higher-order correlations as potentials provides a flexible and theoretically grounded approach. This feels like a logical next step in attention mechanism design.
* **Generality:**  The authors explicitly highlight the applicability of their method beyond VQA and to any multimodal task. The framework's design, particularly the mean-field inference, seems well-suited for scaling to more than two or three modalities, a significant advantage.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on VQA is a strong indicator of the method’s effectiveness. The provided quantitative results demonstrate a clear benefit over existing techniques.
* **Clear Presentation of the Mathematical Formulation:** The use of potentials and mean-field inference is explained relatively clearly, and the equations are well-defined.
* **Ablation study:** The comparison between the MCT and 2-layer MCB unit provides valuable insight into the effectiveness of the proposed decision units.
* **Detailed Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive and does a good job of positioning the contribution relative to existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** While visualizations of attention maps are provided, they are somewhat limited. A more detailed analysis comparing the attention distributions of the proposed method to baselines would significantly strengthen the paper.  The failure case presented feels isolated; more such examples would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters (learning rate, embedding dimension, etc.).  It would be helpful to understand how robust the performance is to changes in these settings.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper acknowledges the computational complexity but does not provide a detailed discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the high-order attention mechanism compared to simpler methods. This is an important practical consideration.  The use of tensor sketching mitigates this but a more explicit analysis would be useful.
* **Potential for Overfitting:** Introducing more parameters (through the higher-order potentials) increases the risk of overfitting. The authors use dropout, but a more thorough analysis of generalization performance, potentially with different dataset splits, would be valuable.
* **The benefit of third-order attention over pairwise:** The paper states that the 3-modality model performs better, but doesn’t sufficiently justify the added complexity. How much of the performance gain comes from the answer modality and the ternary potential, versus a well-tuned pairwise model?

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical foundation and compelling experimental results. The method appears complete and the authors are careful in their evaluation, highlighting both strengths and limitations.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, although the mathematical notation can be dense at times. Expanding the explanations of the mean-field inference process might improve readability.
* **Originality:** The high-order attention mechanism represents a novel approach to multimodal reasoning. The framing as a probabilistic model with mean-field inference is a clever and effective technique.
* **Significance:** This work has the potential to be significant, as it offers a more expressive and flexible way to model interactions between modalities. The state-of-the-art VQA results suggest that the approach is promising.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

*  Novel and technically sound approach to multimodal attention.
*  Strong empirical results on a challenging benchmark.
*  Clear and well-organized presentation.
*  Potential for broad applicability beyond VQA.

**Against:**

*  Limited qualitative analysis.
*  Lack of discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and computational cost.
*  Insufficient justification for the benefit of third-order attention.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to multimodal attention. The empirical results are compelling, and the theoretical foundation is solid. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly providing a more detailed qualitative analysis, discussing computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity, and clarifying the benefit of third-order attention – would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I believe that with these revisions, this work would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference.
