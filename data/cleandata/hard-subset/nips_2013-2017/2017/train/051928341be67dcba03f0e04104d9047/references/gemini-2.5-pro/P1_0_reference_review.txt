### Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multi-modal tasks, specifically demonstrated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The central idea is to move beyond standard unary (self-attention) or pairwise (co-attention) models by explicitly modeling high-order correlations between modalities. The authors frame their approach as a probabilistic model where attention distributions are derived from a combination of "potentials": unary potentials reflecting the importance of features within a single modality (image, question, answer), pairwise potentials capturing correlations between two modalities (e.g., image-question), and a novel third-order (ternary) potential that models the joint interaction of all three. These potentials are calculated and then marginalized to contribute to the final attention score for each element in each modality via a learned linear combination. The final attended feature vectors are then fused for decision-making using Multimodal Compact Pooling. The authors show that this approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, and argue that modeling these higher-order interactions is key to this performance gain.

This work builds upon prior co-attention mechanisms (e.g., Lu et al., 2016) and efficient multi-modal fusion techniques (e.g., Fukui et al., 2016). Its primary contribution is the formalization and implementation of a tractable third-order attention mechanism, extending the complexity of interactions that can be explicitly modeled.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The core contribution—explicitly modeling third-order (and, in principle, higher-order) correlations—is a novel and intuitive step forward for multi-modal attention. The framing of the problem in terms of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials is clean, principled, and provides a generalizable framework that could be applied to other tasks involving more than two modalities.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The model achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA benchmark. The quantitative evaluation in Table 1 clearly demonstrates the method's effectiveness. Crucially, the authors show that their 3-modality model significantly outperforms their own 2-modality implementation, providing strong evidence for the central claim that modeling higher-order correlations is beneficial.

3.  **Thorough Qualitative Analysis:** The paper provides good qualitative visualizations of the attention maps (Figures 5, 6, 7). These figures help build intuition for how the different potentials contribute and how the final attention focuses on relevant regions. The inclusion of a failure case analysis (Figure 8) is a sign of careful and honest evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity on Technical Details and Computational Cost:** The paper's primary weakness lies in its lack of clarity regarding the implementation and computational feasibility of the ternary potential. The third-order correlation tensor, `C3`, would have dimensions `nq x nv x na`. Given that `na` (number of answers) is 3000, this is a very large tensor (`~12 x 196 x 3000`), making its explicit construction and storage computationally prohibitive. The paper does not explain how this is handled. While the marginalization sums suggest it might be computed implicitly, this is a critical detail that is completely omitted.

2.  **Missing Details in Potential Calculation:** In the descriptions of the pairwise and ternary potentials (Sections 3.2.2 and 3.2.3), the marginalization is written as a weighted sum (e.g., `sum(w_iv * C2_iv,iq)`). The paper never defines these weights (`w`). Are they learned parameters? Are they uniform (i.e., a simple average)? This ambiguity makes the method difficult to reproduce and leaves a core component of the model underspecified.

3.  **Potentially Misleading Terminology:** The paper repeatedly refers to its method as performing "mean-field inference." While the potential-based formulation is inspired by graphical models, the actual implementation—a single-step, weighted linear combination of potentials followed by a softmax—is a significant simplification. It does not involve the iterative updates characteristic of true mean-field variational inference. This terminology could be seen as an overstatement of the theoretical grounding and should be clarified or toned down.

4.  **Limited Discussion on Scalability:** The paper proposes a framework for "k-th order correlations" but only demonstrates it for k=3. The computational and memory complexity appears to grow exponentially with the order `k`. A discussion of these scalability limitations would make the paper more complete.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Clarity:** Please provide a detailed explanation of how the ternary potential `θ_V,Q,A` is computed without explicitly instantiating the full `C3` tensor. This is the most significant unclear point in the paper.
*   **Reproducibility:** Please clarify the nature of the weights `w` used in the marginalization steps for pairwise and ternary potentials. Are they parameters of the model? If so, how are they learned?
*   **Quality:** Could you provide a more rigorous justification for the "mean-field inference" claim, or consider rephrasing it to more accurately reflect the single-step weighted combination that is implemented? For example, "a single-step approximation inspired by mean-field methods."
*   **Significance:** While the VQA task is a good testbed, have you considered how this framework would apply to other multi-modal tasks, perhaps where the modalities are more symmetric (e.g., video, audio, and text)?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and significant idea of modeling high-order correlations in attention.
*   The conceptual framework is elegant and generalizable.
*   Achieves state-of-the-art results on a competitive benchmark, demonstrating the practical value of the idea.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Critical implementation details regarding the computation of the core contribution (the ternary potential) are missing, raising concerns about technical soundness and reproducibility.
*   Key parts of the formulation (the marginalization weights `w`) are left undefined.
*   The theoretical claims ("mean-field inference") are not fully substantiated by the described method.

**Recommendation:**

Weak Accept.

The core idea of this paper is strong, novel, and backed by excellent results. It represents a valuable contribution to the field of multi-modal learning. However, the paper is marred by a critical lack of clarity on the computational aspects of its main contribution, the ternary potential. This omission makes it difficult to fully assess the technical soundness and reproducibility of the work. I recommend acceptance on the condition that the authors can adequately address these clarity issues in the final version. The required clarifications are substantial but seem addressable without new experiments.
