Summary
The paper proposes a high-order multimodal attention framework for VQA that explicitly models unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) modalities. Pairwise correlations are implemented via learned bilinear projections and inner products (C2), and ternary correlations via a triple inner product tensor (C3). The “attentions” are computed by linearly combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials with learnable weights followed by a softmax, which the authors interpret as a single mean-field iteration. Attended features are then combined using Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) or Trilinear (MCT) pooling for decision making. On VQA (multiple choice), the paper claims state-of-the-art performance and shows qualitative visualizations.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against co-attention (Lu et al.), bilinear pooling (MCB/MLB), and structured/dual attention. Its distinctive claim is an explicit high-order (up to ternary) potential formulation across modalities with a (single-step) mean-field interpretation and the ability to trivially extend to k modalities. The decision module builds directly on MCB and the tensor sketching literature. While pairwise joint attention and bilinear/trilinear fusion are well-studied, the explicit construction of unary+pairwise+ternary attention “potentials” and their learned combination is a useful perspective, though the probabilistic justification is only loosely argued.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The construction of unary/pairwise/ternary correlation features is standard and reasonable. However, the “mean-field inference” claim is not substantiated: no underlying graphical model/energy is defined for which the proposed updates provably correspond to MF updates; the learned mixture weights (α,β,γ) further decouple the update from variational inference. As presented, the attention computation is a learned linear combination of correlation-derived features followed by softmax, which is fine, but the probabilistic interpretation is tenuous.
- Complexity and feasibility: Building the C3 tensor scales as O(nv·nq·na). This is only feasible in the multiple-choice setting (na≈18) but seems intractable for open-ended classification if na≈3000 is used as input; the paper should clearly state that ternary attention is only used in MC and quantify memory/time. Batch size 300 and C3 with na>100 would be challenging; more details are needed.
- Empirics: The experimental section lacks essential quantitative details. The paper repeatedly claims “state-of-the-art” on VQA test-dev/test-std, but the actual accuracy numbers and comparisons are missing in this version (Tab. 1 is referenced but not shown). One reported comparison (val: MCT 63.82% vs 2-layer MCB 64.57%) is helpful but insufficient. There is no ablation isolating the effect of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials, nor an analysis of the learned mixture weights, nor multiple MF iterations. Fairness of comparisons is unclear (e.g., the authors use d=512 features whereas some baselines use 2048; they acknowledge this affects 2-modality performance). Runtime/memory are not reported. Overall, the empirical evidence does not yet fully support the claims.
- Reproducibility: Many training details are given, and a code link is promised, which would aid reproducibility. However, in a double-blind venue, a non-anonymized code link is problematic.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but several notational and editorial issues detract:
  - Ambiguity in “1×1 convolution” on C2/C3 followed by summations with weights wiv/wiq; better to explicitly define the learned projection/marginalization operators and their tensor shapes.
  - The answer attention equation accidentally reuses aV for aA.
  - Figures and some references are difficult to follow in this text; ensure all tables/figures are present and legible, and all baselines are precisely named.
  - The “mean-field inference” terminology should be either justified by a concrete model/derivation or rephrased.

Originality
- Moderate. Modeling joint attention via bilinear/trilinear correlations is known; MCB/MCT and co-attention are established. The primary novelty is the explicit high-order potential decomposition and the learned mixture of unary/pairwise/ternary attentions under a (loose) MF viewpoint, plus using answer-attention for MC in a unified framework. The approach is conceptually clean and extensible, but the step from pairwise to ternary is incremental relative to MCB/MCT-style methods, and the paper does not demonstrate k>3.

Significance
- Potentially meaningful for VQA if the claimed SOTA with fewer parameters is confirmed and if the high-order attention consistently improves over strong pairwise co-attention baselines. The generality claim (applicable to any number of modalities) is appealing but unproven beyond 3 modalities and may be limited by cubic scaling. Without full quantitative tables and ablations, the broader impact is hard to gauge.

Pros
- Clear motivation for modeling higher-order cross-modal interactions.
- Simple, modular architecture that unifies unary/pairwise/ternary attentions.
- Practical integration with compact bilinear/trilinear pooling.
- Qualitative visualizations suggest reasonable, question-specific attention shifts.
- Training details provided; parameter efficiency claimed.

Cons
- Missing key quantitative results and fair, apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Probabilistic/mean-field framing is not rigorously derived.
- Computational/memory costs of ternary attention not analyzed; applicability beyond MC unclear.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses; no study of k>3.
- Notational clarity issues and minor errors.

Questions for authors
1. Please provide the exact test-dev/test-std numbers (overall and by answer type) and a full comparison table vs Lu et al., MCB/MLB, and other recent baselines with matched visual features and training splits.
2. Clarify whether ternary attention is only used for MC (na≈18). If so, what is the open-ended performance with only V–Q attention and how does the model compare?
3. Provide time/memory benchmarks for pairwise vs ternary attention, and discuss scalability to larger na or additional modalities.
4. Either provide a concrete graphical model and MF derivation for Eq. (1) or reframe the method without the MF claim.
5. Include ablations: removing ternary potentials; varying α/β/γ; multiple MF iterations; effect of d and visual backbone (ResNet-152).

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is sensible and likely useful, but the submission, as written, lacks the quantitative evidence and clarity needed to support a NeurIPS acceptance. With complete results, stronger ablations, and clarified modeling, this could become a solid contribution.
