Summary
This paper proposes a multimodal attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, and answer A) for VQA. Pairwise correlations are computed via similarity matrices (inner products in learned embedding spaces), and ternary correlations via third-order correlation tensors. The different orders are linearly combined (with learnable weights) and passed through a softmax to produce marginal attention distributions per modality; the authors interpret this as one mean-field iteration in a probabilistic model. The attended vectors are then fused via Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) or Trilinear (MCT) pooling (tensor sketch) to make predictions. On VQA v1.0 multiple-choice, the method reports state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters than some prior work; qualitative visualizations suggest modality-dependent attention behavior.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on and positions itself against co-attention (Lu et al., 2016), stacked attention (Yang et al., 2016), dynamic memory networks (Xiong et al., 2016), and bilinear pooling approaches (Fukui et al., 2016; Kim et al., 2017). It is closest to structured or joint attention formulations and bilinear/trilinear pooling, claiming novelty in (i) defining and learning high-order attention potentials over multiple modalities and (ii) using a simple mean-field-like mixture of potentials with learnable combination coefficients, including a third modality (answers) as input for MC VQA.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: The decomposition into unary/pairwise/ternary potentials provides a neat, modular view of multimodal attention and highlights interactions across modalities.
- Generalization to more than two modalities: Formalism makes it straightforward to add an additional modality (answers), and the attention visualization across modalities is interesting.
- Practicality: Uses tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to handle high-order fusion in decision making, and provides training details and a code link.
- Empirical performance: Reports strong multiple-choice VQA results and conducts qualitative analysis. Some ablation (MCT vs 2-layer MCB) is provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Probabilistic modeling and “mean-field” claim: The paper calls the linear mixture followed by softmax a “mean-field iteration” but does not specify a joint distribution/energy, factorization, or derive the update equations. As presented, the combination appears to be a learned weighted sum rather than principled variational inference. This undermines the stated probabilistic interpretation.
- Computational feasibility of ternary potentials: For na=3000 answers, constructing C3 with dimensions (nq × nv × na) and then marginalizing is prohibitive in memory and compute. The paper does not explain how C3 is implemented efficiently, whether it is approximated, sparsified (e.g., top-K answers), or factorized. Without a complexity analysis or implementation details, the practicality of the ternary attention is unclear.
- Fairness of comparisons: The strongest numbers are on the multiple-choice setting where the method uses answers as an input modality. Many baselines do not use answer choices at inference, and standard MC protocols often score choices using a model trained for open-ended answers. The paper should compare under identical settings (with/without A as input) and include open-ended results.
- Limited and uneven ablations: While MCB vs MCT is compared, there is no systematic ablation on the contributions of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials (e.g., removing θV,Q,A, or setting α/β/γ per-term to zero), nor an analysis of the learned weights (do attentions actually depend on higher-order terms?). Also missing are analyses of the number of attention “glimpses” or iterative updates.
- Clarity and notation issues: Several typos/notation inconsistencies (e.g., “aV = sum over na” appears to be aA; repeated “aV” definition; smax arguments; unclear whether wiv, wiq, wiq,ia are shared parameters or position-specific). Figures are referenced but not fully explained; the description of “convolution with 1×1 kernel along a dimension” is essentially a learned weighted sum—this should be stated more clearly.
- Significance beyond VQA v1 MC: Evaluation is confined to VQA v1 multiple-choice. Given the number of prior methods on VQA v1 open-ended and VQA v2, broader validation would strengthen the paper and demonstrate generality.
- Claims of scalability to “any number of modalities”: While conceptually straightforward, high-order potentials scale combinatorially; without low-rank/factorized constructions or sampling, this is not computationally scalable.

Quality
Technically sound at the level of constructing and training attention modules and using MCB/MCT; however, the probabilistic framing is not rigorously justified, and key efficiency aspects of ternary attention are unspecified. Empirical evaluation is promising but incomplete and potentially unfair due to the use of answer choices as input.

Clarity
Readable and generally well organized, but the modeling details need more precision (definitions of potentials, parameter sharing, complexity). Correct the notation typos and clarify implementation of C3 and the “convolutions” along dimensions. A detailed algorithmic description, shapes, and memory footprints would help reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. Pairwise attention via similarity matrices and bilinear pooling are well known; the paper’s main novelty is elevating this to a unified high-order potential view and including answer-aware attention. Similar ideas in structured attention exist; a clearer comparison to Structured Attention Networks (and to MLB/MFB families) would help delineate novelty.

Significance
Potentially useful for multimodal problems needing tri-modal interactions; however, the current experimental scope (VQA v1 MC only) and lack of rigorous analysis limit impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a clear derivation of the mean-field update from a defined joint energy, or refrain from the probabilistic claim and present it as a learned mixture of potentials.
- Detail the computation and memory complexity of θV,Q,A and how C3 is implemented efficiently. Consider factorized/low-rank approximations or top-K candidate pruning, and report runtime/memory.
- Add open-ended VQA results and comparisons where baselines are matched on features and training protocol. Include an ablation removing A as input and quantify the gain from ternary potentials.
- Perform ablations on unary/pairwise/ternary terms and report learned α/β/γ to confirm which orders matter.
- Fix notation/typos and more explicitly describe the parameterization of the “convolution” weights along dimensions.

Pros
- Clear modular attention design; easy conceptual extension to tri-modal attention
- Strong empirical performance on VQA MC; good qualitative visualizations
- Practical use of MCB/MCT for high-order fusion

Cons
- Unclear and potentially infeasible ternary attention computation
- Incomplete and potentially unfair experimental comparisons
- Probabilistic/mean-field framing not rigorously supported
- Limited analysis and ablations; scope restricted to VQA v1 MC

Recommendation: Weak reject
Confidence: High
