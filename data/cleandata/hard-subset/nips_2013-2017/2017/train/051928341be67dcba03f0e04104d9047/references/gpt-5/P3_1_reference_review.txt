Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a “high-order” attention framework for multimodal learning, instantiated for VQA. The key idea is to treat attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, answers A). Unary potentials are obtained via 1×1 convs (i.e., per-token MLPs). Pairwise potentials are computed from a learned similarity matrix C2 = QWq(VWv)ᵀ; marginalized along one dimension to yield modality-specific scores. Ternary potentials are defined via a triple-correlation tensor C3 with entries corr3(q,v,a)=∑l ql vl al, marginalized to produce per-modality scores. The final attention distributions result from a softmax over a learned linear combination of the relevant potentials (Eq. 1), interpreted as a single mean-field iteration. Attended vectors are combined for decision making via Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) pooling or its trilinear extension (MCT), relying on tensor sketching to avoid explicit outer products. The paper claims state-of-the-art performance on VQA (mainly multiple-choice) with qualitative visualizations.

This work connects to co-attention (Lu et al. 2016), bilinear/multimodal pooling (MCB: Fukui et al. 2016; MLB/MFB: Kim et al. 2017), structured attention (Kim et al. 2017), and models that condition on answer candidates (Jabri et al. 2016). The contribution is to explicitly define and combine k-order attention “potentials” across modalities and to incorporate ternary interactions at the attention stage, not only in the decision module.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for modeling explicit cross-modal interactions at the attention stage; the framework is conceptually general (extendable beyond two modalities).
- Simple, modular computations for unary and pairwise potentials; compatible with standard CNN/LSTM embeddings and downstream pooling.
- Qualitative visualizations suggest the pairwise/ternary components meaningfully steer attention conditioned on the question/answers.
- Empirical exploration of MCT vs two-layer MCB and a three-modality variant, with some gains over a two-modality baseline.

Weaknesses and technical concerns (quality/clarity):
- Probabilistic formulation is underspecified. “Mean-field inference” reduces to a weighted sum of potentials followed by softmax. No energy function, variational objective, factorization, or derivation of update equations is provided. As written, this is a log-linear attention model with learnable mixture weights, not a principled mean-field algorithm. Clarify the underlying graphical model and show how Eq. (1) follows from a variational update.
- Computational feasibility of ternary potentials is unclear. The tensor C3 has size nq×nv×na; with na up to 3000 and nv≈196, explicit construction is intractable in memory/time. The paper does not specify how C3 or its marginals are computed without instantiating the full tensor (e.g., via separable low-rank factorizations, associative contractions, or sketching). Provide exact algorithms, complexity, and memory analyses, and ablations/time profiles.
- Evaluation is incomplete and potentially not comparable. Most results discussed are on VQA multiple-choice; many strong baselines do not use answer candidates as inputs at test time. Using A as an input modality can make the task easier. Fair comparisons require:
  - Reporting both open-ended and multiple-choice results with standard VQA accuracy protocol on test-dev/test-std, including Yes/No, Number, Other breakdowns.
  - Comparing against baselines that also condition on candidate answers, or removing A to measure pure VQ performance.
- The quantitative claims of “state-of-the-art” are not substantiated in the text provided: no full results table is shown, only an ablation (63.82 vs 64.57 on val) with VGG features. At the time, strong bilinear/multimodal baselines (MCB/MLB/MFB/MUTAN) reported higher numbers; these should be included with identical backbones and feature dims.
- Novelty over prior co-attention/bilinear attention is incremental. Pairwise attention via similarity matrices and their marginals is standard; the main novelty is the ternary attention involving answers as input. Prior work has explored answer-conditioned models; the paper should more clearly differentiate and quantify what is gained by adding ternary attention at the attention stage versus only at the fusion/decision stage (e.g., MCB/MCT).
- Several clarity and presentation issues: missing/incorrect formulas (duplicate “aV” in Eq. for answers), undefined pooling weights (wiv, wiq in pairwise/ternary pooling), inconsistent notation, missing figure/table references and numbers, and sparse training details (e.g., how α,β,γ are initialized/regularized; number of mean-field iterations beyond 1).

Originality:
- The general “higher-order potentials for attention” framing is interesting, but the operational mechanisms (linear transforms, inner products, softmax) are close to existing co-attention and bilinear pooling work. The primary new element is the explicit ternary attention term. Without a rigorous probabilistic derivation or an efficient novel algorithm for high-order marginalization, the originality is moderate.

Significance:
- If the ternary attention can be computed efficiently and consistently improves VQA beyond bilinear/co-attention baselines (under fair settings), the idea could influence multimodal attention design. As presented, evidence is insufficient: limited quantitative gains, no open-ended results, and unclear computational practicality.

Questions for authors:
1. Provide the formal probabilistic model (variables, factors, energy), the variational objective, and the derivation of Eq. (1). What is the role of α,β,γ (are they shared across positions/modalities)? Any constraints?
2. How do you compute θV,Q,A without materializing C3? Give algorithmic steps, FLOPs, and memory for typical nq,nv,na. Do you use low-rank (CP/Tucker) decompositions or sketching at the attention stage?
3. Are comparisons to MCB/MLB/MFB/MUTAN done with identical visual features (ResNet-152), same answer vocab, and same training data (train+val)? Report open-ended and multiple-choice on test-dev/test-std with per-type breakdowns.
4. Ablations: remove ternary potentials; remove pairwise; vary number of mean-field iterations; set α,β,γ to fixed values; analyze effect sizes and attention quality.
5. How is na defined for multiple choice vs open-ended? When candidates are 18 per example, how do you embed A and compute θA,· consistently?

Pros:
- Conceptually appealing attempt to model higher-order cross-modal interactions in attention.
- Simple modules; compatible with standard backbones and tensor sketching for fusion.
- Qualitative visualizations are informative.

Cons:
- Probabilistic/mean-field claims are not rigorously grounded.
- Unclear and likely prohibitive complexity for ternary attention; missing algorithmic details.
- Incomplete and possibly unfair evaluation; SOTA claims not substantiated.
- Incremental novelty relative to strong contemporaneous bilinear/co-attention work.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is promising, but the paper needs a rigorous formulation, clear efficient algorithms for high-order attention, and comprehensive, fair empirical validation.

Confidence: High.
