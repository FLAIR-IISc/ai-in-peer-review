- Proposes extending attention to unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials with mean-field inference for VQA.  
- Uses tensor sketching for efficient computation and standard techniques; technically sound model design.  
- Claims state-of-the-art results but improvement is modest (64.57% vs. 63.82%) and lacks statistical significance testing.  
- No thorough ablation study to isolate contributions of unary/pairwise/ternary potentials versus the decision module.  
- The ternary potential appears functionally equivalent to existing MCT (trilinear pooling), weakening novelty.  
- Paper does not clearly explain how its probabilistic approach differs from prior bilinear/multimodal pooling work.  
- Well-written and organized with clear figures and implementation details for reproducibility.  
- Ambiguous distinction from prior bilinear attention approaches reduces clarity on the contribution.  
- Contribution is incremental, combining existing ideas (probabilistic modeling, tensor sketching) rather than introducing a new concept.  
- Modest quantitative gains limit significance; unclear that high-order attention is the causal factor for improvement.  
- Qualitative results are helpful but insufficient to demonstrate how high-order attention improves answering capability.  
- Lacks exploration of broader applications beyond VQA to establish wider impact.  
- Pros: clear exposition, logical extension of attention mechanisms, modest performance improvement.  
- Cons: overstated novelty, small gains without significance tests, missing ablations, superficial comparison to MCB/MCT.  
- Recommendation: Weak rejection; requires clearer differentiation from prior work, ablation studies, and stronger evidence for claimed benefits.
