

# Peer Review: "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal learning, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea is to model high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer) using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The authors claim their approach is "generally applicable" to any number of modalities and demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset. They extend existing bilinear attention mechanisms by adding higher-order interaction terms and use mean-field inference to compute attention weights.

The paper builds on the growing body of work on attention mechanisms in multimodal learning, particularly referencing Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7]. The authors position their work as addressing the limitation of "present day attention mechanisms" that "are often geared towards a specific form of input and therefore hand-crafted for a particular task."

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Statement**: The paper correctly identifies the need for more expressive attention mechanisms that can handle multiple data modalities effectively.

2. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The authors achieve competitive results on VQA, which is a strong point. The paper demonstrates that their 3-modality model outperforms a 2-modality version, suggesting the value of modeling higher-order interactions.

3. **Qualitative Analysis**: The qualitative results (Fig. 5-8) provide useful insights into how the attention mechanism works, showing that the model attends to relevant regions in images for different questions.

4. **Generalization Claim**: The paper correctly argues that their approach is extendable to any number of modalities, which is a valid point of differentiation from some prior work.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Overstated Novelty
The paper's core contribution is overstated. The "high-order" attention mechanism is essentially a straightforward extension of existing bilinear attention mechanisms, not a fundamentally new approach. The authors claim to "learn high-order correlations between various data modalities," but their implementation only goes up to ternary (3-way) interactions, not general k-order. More critically, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from existing bilinear attention works:

- Fukui et al. [7] already introduced Multimodal Compact Bilinear Pooling (MCB) for pairwise interactions, which is conceptually similar to the authors' pairwise potential.
- The authors' "ternary" mechanism is essentially a trilinear extension of MCB, which is a natural progression rather than a novel contribution.
- The paper references Kim et al. [13] on bilinear models but doesn't adequately explain how their approach differs from this work.

The paper claims "attention mechanism with several potentials haven't been discussed in detail yet," but this is inaccurate. Bilinear models for multimodal interaction have been extensively studied, and the paper doesn't provide a clear theoretical justification for why their specific formulation of high-order potentials is necessary or superior.

### 2. Insufficient Ablation Studies
The paper claims significant improvements from their high-order attention mechanism, but the ablation studies are insufficient to support this claim:

- They compare a 3-modality model to a 2-modality model, but don't isolate the effect of the high-order attention from other changes (e.g., the use of answer embedding).
- The improvement from MCT (63.82%) to 2-layer MCB (64.57%) is marginal (0.75%), which is not convincing evidence of the value of high-order attention.
- There's no comparison to the most recent VQA approaches (e.g., the 2017 VQA challenge winners), making it difficult to assess true state-of-the-art performance.

### 3. Theoretical and Methodological Concerns
- The paper describes their approach as "a probabilistic model, based on high order potentials and performs mean-field inference," but provides no justification for why mean-field is appropriate here. Mean-field inference is typically used for approximating posterior distributions in graphical models, but the paper doesn't present a clear probabilistic model with an objective to optimize.
- The linear combination of potentials (Equation 1) with learnable weights αi, βi, γi is essentially a way to weight different attention sources, but this is not fundamentally different from other approaches that combine multiple attention mechanisms.
- The paper claims to "learn high-order correlations," but the ternary potential is simply a trilinear interaction of the form d∑l=1 qlvlal, which is a standard trilinear form rather than a novel correlation measure.

### 4. Experimental Limitations
- The paper uses a relatively simple model architecture (40 million parameters) compared to other state-of-the-art VQA models (which often use 70+ million parameters), making it difficult to attribute performance gains to the attention mechanism itself.
- The embedding dimension (d=512) is lower than standard in the field (which often uses d=2048), yet they claim their model achieves better results with fewer parameters. This comparison is misleading without proper ablation on embedding dimension.
- The qualitative results (Fig. 7) don't convincingly demonstrate the advantage of high-order attention over existing methods. For instance, in the first row, the attention appears similar to existing approaches.

## Specific Comments on Related Work

The paper's discussion of related work is problematic in several places:

- It incorrectly claims that "Lu et al. [15] suggest an alternating model, that directly combines the features of the modalities before attending. Additionally, they suggested a parallel model which uses a similarity matrix to map features for one modality to the other. It is hard to extend this approach to more than two modalities." In reality, Lu et al. [15] use a bilinear model that can be extended to more modalities with some effort, and the paper doesn't provide evidence that their approach is actually easier to extend.

- The paper references Kim et al. [14] on "multi-variate probabilistic model" but doesn't explain how their approach differs from this work, which is particularly relevant to their probabilistic formulation.

- The paper mentions "Jabri et al. [9] propose a model where answers are also used as inputs" but doesn't acknowledge that this is a key component of their own model (they use answer embedding as a third modality).

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a modest contribution to the VQA literature by extending existing bilinear attention mechanisms to handle three modalities. However, the novelty is overstated, and the experimental results don't convincingly demonstrate the value of high-order attention over simpler pairwise approaches. The marginal performance gains (0.75% on the validation set) do not justify the claimed significance of the work.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but it fails to make a compelling case for why high-order attention is necessary or superior to existing approaches. The "high-order" aspect is largely a superficial extension of existing techniques rather than a fundamental new insight.

## Recommendation

**Reject**

### Justification for Rejection:

1. **Novelty**: The core contribution is not sufficiently novel. The extension from pairwise to ternary attention is a straightforward technical extension rather than a significant conceptual advance. The paper fails to clearly differentiate its approach from existing bilinear attention mechanisms.

2. **Significance**: The performance gains are marginal and not convincingly attributed to the high-order attention component. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the high-order attention is necessary for the performance gains.

3. **Ablation Studies**: The paper lacks sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the high-order attention mechanism from other components of the model.

4. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper doesn't compare to the most recent VQA approaches, making it difficult to assess true performance.

5. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper claims to have a probabilistic model but doesn't provide sufficient theoretical grounding for their approach.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it:
- Provided a clearer theoretical justification for why high-order attention is necessary
- Conducted more thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the high-order attention mechanism
- Made a more accurate comparison to existing work (particularly Fukui et al. [7] and Kim et al. [13])
- Demonstrated more significant performance gains from the high-order attention

Without these improvements, the paper does not make a sufficiently novel contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Conduct a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of high-order attention from other components of the model.

2. Compare to the most recent VQA approaches (e.g., 2017 VQA challenge winners) to establish true state-of-the-art performance.

3. Provide a more detailed theoretical justification for the high-order attention mechanism, including why mean-field inference is appropriate.

4. Clarify the relationship between the proposed approach and existing bilinear attention mechanisms, particularly [7] and [13].

5. Consider whether the marginal performance gains justify the complexity of the high-order attention mechanism, or if simpler approaches could achieve comparable results.

6. Address the connection to Kim et al. [14] more thoroughly, as it's directly relevant to the probabilistic formulation.

Overall, while the paper is well-written and demonstrates a technically sound approach, the novelty and significance of the work are insufficient for NIPS acceptance.
