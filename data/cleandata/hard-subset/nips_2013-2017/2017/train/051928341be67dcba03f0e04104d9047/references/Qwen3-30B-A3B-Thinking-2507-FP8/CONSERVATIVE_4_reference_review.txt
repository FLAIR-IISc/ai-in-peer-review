

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a "high-order attention model" for visual question answering that extends existing attention mechanisms to model higher-order correlations between visual, textual, and answer modalities. The authors formalize attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to compute attention weights. They claim to achieve state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset by incorporating these high-order interactions.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds upon existing attention mechanisms for VQA, particularly the work of Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016), which use pairwise attention. The authors claim their "high-order" approach is novel, but the core concept appears to be a straightforward extension of existing attention frameworks rather than a fundamentally new method. The approach bears resemblance to the bilinear attention mechanisms in prior work, with the addition of ternary potentials. The paper's claims of novelty are somewhat overstated, as the authors acknowledge similar approaches in related work (e.g., Kim et al. 2017, Nam et al. 2016) but don't clearly differentiate their contribution.

## Strengths

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to results. The mathematical formulation of the high-order attention model is technically sound, and the authors provide sufficient detail about the architecture for reproduction. The qualitative analysis of attention visualization (Fig. 5-7) is compelling and demonstrates that the model attends to relevant regions in images for specific questions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Marginal Performance Gains**: The reported improvements over baselines are minimal. The best result (64.57% on VQA test-dev) is only slightly better than the MCT baseline (63.82%), and the comparison to Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016) is limited to two methods. The paper fails to include more recent state-of-the-art VQA approaches (e.g., from the 2017 VQA Challenge or papers like Han et al. 2017) for a fair comparison.

2. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The comparison to a 2-modality model with a lower embedding dimension (512 vs. 2048) is not a fair comparison. The authors admit this but don't provide a proper ablation study showing the contribution of high-order attention versus embedding dimension. A proper comparison would use identical embedding dimensions across all models.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials). Without this, it's impossible to determine whether the high-order attention actually provides meaningful improvements beyond simply adding more parameters.

4. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance, but the gains are marginal and the comparison is limited. The paper also overstates the novelty of the approach, as the high-order attention concept is a natural extension of existing pairwise attention mechanisms.

5. **Limited Failure Analysis**: The paper shows one failure case (Fig. 8) but doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of failure modes or limitations of the approach.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a technically sound approach, the marginal performance improvements, limited baselines, and overstated claims fail to demonstrate sufficient novelty or significance for NIPS. The "high-order attention" concept is not sufficiently different from existing work to justify its presentation as a major innovation. The paper would benefit from more rigorous comparisons, ablation studies, and a clearer demonstration of why high-order attention is necessary for VQA.

The authors should consider addressing the following points before resubmission:
1. Include more recent and relevant baselines for comparison
2. Perform proper ablation studies to isolate the contribution of high-order attention
3. Address the fair comparison issue with identical embedding dimensions
4. Provide a more nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field of attention mechanisms, but it doesn't meet NIPS' standards for significant methodological advancement or compelling empirical evidence. The approach appears to be a reasonable extension of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new direction that merits publication at NIPS.
