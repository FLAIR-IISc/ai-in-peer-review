Summary
This paper proposes “high-order attention” for multimodal learning, instantiated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The key idea is to model attention as a probabilistic mechanism with unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, answers A) and to obtain attention distributions via a softmax over a learned linear combination of these potentials—interpreted as a single mean-field iteration. Pairwise potentials use similarity (inner products) between embedded modalities; ternary potentials use triple correlations. The attended representations are then fused with compact bilinear or trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) for classification. The method is evaluated on VQA (multiple-choice), with claimed state-of-the-art performance, visualizations, and ablations comparing MCB vs MCT. The approach relates to co-attention models (Lu et al., NIPS’16), bilinear pooling for VQA (Fukui et al., 2016), and structured attention (Kim et al., 2017), but extends to higher-order interactions across three modalities and frames attention as a factorized potential model.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for modeling higher-order cross-modal dependencies; unified framework that includes unary, pairwise, and ternary correlations.
- Elegant use of tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to avoid exponential parameter growth in decision fusion.
- Empirical improvements on VQA multiple-choice with relatively few parameters and reasonable training budget; code link provided.
- Good qualitative visualizations illustrating how pairwise/ternary potentials shift attention conditioned on the question and candidate answers.
- Simple architecture that can, in principle, generalize to more modalities.

Weaknesses and questions
- “Probabilistic/mean-field” interpretation is mostly rhetorical: the update used is a learned linear combination of potentials followed by softmax. No explicit energy/function factorization, variational objective, or derivation of the update is given. No iterative inference or convergence analysis; hence, the connection to mean-field is weak.
- Complexity/feasibility of the ternary potential C3 is not fully addressed. If na is the number of output classes (3k), computing C3 is infeasible; if na is the 18 multiple-choice options per instance, it’s tractable. The text conflates these two notions (na as top-3k outputs vs per-question 18 choices), making the setting unclear.
- Limited evaluation scope. Results are only reported for multiple-choice VQA; no open-ended evaluation, no results on additional datasets (e.g., Visual7W), and no comparison to strong contemporary open-ended baselines. The SOTA claim may be limited to the MC setting and may not be directly comparable to methods that do not use answer choices as input.
- Ablation analysis is thin. There’s no quantitative breakdown of the contribution of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials to attention quality or accuracy; no study of learned α/β/γ weights; no comparison to a simpler two-modality attention with answer-aware fusion only at the decision stage.
- Some clarity issues and typos: e.g., the attended answer vector is denoted as aV in Eq. (2) (should be aA); “convolution with 1×1” on a similarity matrix needs clearer specification; definition and shape of weights w in pairwise/ternary marginalizations are not described; the notational overload for C2/C3 derivations makes reproducibility harder.
- Decision fusion: the “2-layer MCB” scheme is under-specified; it reportedly enables self-interactions (image with itself), but architectural specifics are missing. Reporting only a small val-set gain (0.75%) without statistical significance leaves the conclusion tentative.
- Fairness of comparisons: the 2-modality baseline uses a much lower embedding dimension than some prior work; results could be confounded by capacity.

Quality
Technically, the model components (unary, pairwise similarities, triple correlations, MCB/MCT fusion) are sound and standard; the novelty lies in integrating them into a single attention framework with learned weighting. However, the probabilistic justification is not rigorous, and key computational details (especially for ternary attention) need clarification. Empirical support shows improvements on VQA-MC with qualitative analyses, but broader and deeper ablations are needed to isolate what matters.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable with a clear high-level story, but several definitions are ambiguous or under-specified (na, w weights, shapes, and exact operations in pairwise/ternary marginalizations). Minor typos and notation issues hinder exact reproduction. Figures are helpful; adding explicit tensor shapes and pseudo-code for C2/C3 construction and marginalization would improve clarity.

Originality
Moderate. Co-attention and bilinear fusion are established; structured attention is known. This work’s novelty is to (a) explicitly include higher-order (ternary) potentials in attention over multiple modalities and (b) learn to combine potentials via a shared mechanism. Extending attention to include answers as an input in MC VQA has precedents (Jabri et al., 2016), but the paper positions itself as a unified, extensible framework.

Significance
Moderate. If the gains are robust and reproducible, practitioners may adopt the higher-order attention formulation or its ternary variant for tasks with three modalities. However, the impact is constrained by the evaluation scope (MC only) and limited ablation. Stronger evidence on open-ended VQA and additional datasets would raise significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually appealing generalization of attention to higher-order cross-modal interactions.
- Empirical gains on a standard benchmark with visualizations and public code.
- Leverages compact polynomial kernels effectively for decision fusion.

Cons for acceptance
- Probabilistic/mean-field framing is not substantiated by a formal derivation or iterative inference.
- Ambiguities around computational complexity and precise implementation of ternary attention.
- Narrow evaluation and limited ablation make it hard to attribute improvements to specific components.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a formal energy-based model and derive the mean-field updates (or rephrase the method as learned potential weighting without invoking MF).
- Clarify na and the scope of ternary attention: explicitly state that for MC na=18 per instance; detail how C3 is computed and marginalized (include tensor shapes, memory/compute costs).
- Add ablations: remove ternary potentials; remove pairwise potentials; vary α/β/γ; compare answer-aware attention vs answer-aware fusion only; test multi-iteration updates.
- Evaluate on open-ended VQA (without answer inputs) and at least one additional dataset (e.g., Visual7W), and compare to strong baselines not using answer choices.
- Detail the “2-layer MCB” architecture and report statistical significance across runs.
- Fix typos and improve notation consistency; define the convolution/marginalization weights w explicitly.

Minor issues
- Eq. for attended modalities: aA is mislabeled as aV.
- Some references lack full venue details; ensure proper citations.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the modeling/inference story and providing stronger ablations. Confidence: medium.
