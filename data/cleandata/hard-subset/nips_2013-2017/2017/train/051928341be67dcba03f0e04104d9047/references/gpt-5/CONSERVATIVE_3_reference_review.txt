Summary
The paper proposes a “high-order attention” framework for multimodal tasks, instantiated on VQA. Attention is cast as a set of learnable unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over words, image regions, and answers. Pairwise correlations are computed via bilinear similarity (after linear projections) to produce a similarity matrix C2; ternary correlations use a trilinear inner product tensor C3. Marginal attentions for each modality are formed by a learned linear combination of its unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials,” followed by softmax (described as a single-iteration mean-field step). The attended vectors are fed to a classifier that uses compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) for decision making. On VQA (multiple-choice), the method claims state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters and provides qualitative visualizations.

Relation to prior work
The framing relates to co-attention (e.g., Lu et al., 2016), bilinear pooling for multimodal fusion (e.g., Fukui et al., MCB; Kim et al., MLB), and structured/dual attention work. The main technical elements—bilinear similarities for attention, multimodal pooling via count sketch, and combining attentions across modalities—are established in the VQA literature around 2016–2017. The paper’s contribution is to package these in a “high-order potential” view and to add a ternary attention over question–image–answer in the multiple-choice setting.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for modeling cross-modal interactions beyond pairwise, and a simple mechanism to incorporate ternary dependencies.
- Qualitative visualizations suggest the model focuses on question-relevant regions and words.
- Uses compact bilinear/trilinear pooling to keep classifier parameter count manageable.
- Claims competitive performance with fewer parameters; code link is provided, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- The “probabilistic/mean-field” characterization is superficial. There is no explicit probabilistic model (e.g., a defined joint distribution with potentials), no derivation of mean-field updates, and the final attention is just a learned linear mixture of hand-designed score terms followed by softmax. Calling this “mean-field inference” is misleading.
- Empirical evidence is limited. Reported numbers are sparse; there is no comprehensive table in the paper text, no standard breakdowns by question type (Yes/No, Number, Other), no variance/error bars, and no open-ended VQA results. Comparisons are primarily to Lu et al. and MCB; contemporary bilinear baselines (e.g., MLB/MFB) are not thoroughly covered.
- Ablations are weak. The paper does not quantify the gain from unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials, nor the sensitivity to the α/β/γ mixing parameters. The sole ablation is MCT vs two-layer MCB on val, with a small gap.
- Fairness of baselines is questionable. The two-modality baseline is handicapped by a smaller embedding dimension (512 vs 2048 in prior work), making comparisons ambiguous. It is unclear whether image features (VGG vs ResNet) are matched across baselines.
- The use of answers as inputs is only feasible for multiple choice. For open-ended VQA—a primary setting—ternary attention is undefined. The paper does not demonstrate that the approach helps beyond the multiple-choice regime.
- Computational aspects are glossed over. Computing C3 scales as O(nq nv na d); although na≈18 for MC VQA, the approach will not scale to more modalities or larger candidate sets. The claim that the model is “trivially extendable to any number of modalities” is not supported computationally for the attention stage (only the classifier benefits from sketching).

Clarity
- Several typos and inconsistencies (e.g., repeated or incorrect symbols, aV reused for answer attention aggregation, missing indices). The definitions of marginalization over C2/C3 with 1×1 “conv” are unclear; explicit shapes and axes would help.
- Figures are heavily referenced but not sufficiently described in the text; some figure captions are fragmentary.
- Experimental setup omits important details (exact nq, preprocessing, whether ResNet is used in the best model, training seeds, early stopping).

Originality
- Pairwise co-attention and bilinear/trilinear interactions are established; the novelty is mainly an organizational view plus adding ternary attention over answers for MC VQA. The “high-order potential” framing does not translate into a new inference algorithm or new estimator. Overall, incremental.

Significance
- Without stronger, fair comparisons (including MLB/MFB, stronger co-attention variants) and results on open-ended VQA or VQA v2.0, it is hard to assess broad impact. The multiple-choice improvements, if any, appear modest and could plausibly come from increased capacity over answers rather than the proposed inference view.

Requested clarifications and additional experiments
- Provide a precise probabilistic formulation (if any) and derive the mean-field updates; otherwise, rewrite to avoid probabilistic claims.
- Full quantitative comparison on test-dev and test-std with: MCB, MLB, MFB (and same visual features and embedding sizes), including per-type breakdown and open-ended setting. Include standard metrics and report training variance.
- Ablate unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and the α/β/γ mixtures. Show the benefit of ternary attention beyond trivial gains from seeing the candidate answers.
- Report results on VQA v2.0 and/or Visual7W multiple-choice to test generality.
- Analyze computational/memory cost of C3, and discuss scalability. If using approximations, detail them.
- Fix notation errors, provide exact tensor shapes, and clarify the “convolution” used for marginalizing C2/C3.

Minor comments
- Several references are incomplete; please provide full bibliographic info.
- Please state nq and padding/truncation policy; clarify whether ResNet features were used in the best model.
- The claim “trivial extension to any number of modalities” should be qualified given the cubic (and higher) scaling for attention potentials.

Overall rating: 5 (marginally below acceptance)
Confidence: 4/5

Rationale
The idea of jointly modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions is sensible, and the implementation is straightforward. However, the paper over-claims on “probabilistic inference,” the novelty over established co-attention/bilinear methods is limited, and the evaluation is not rigorous enough (fair baselines, breadth, and ablations) to justify acceptance at NeurIPS. Strengthening the empirical section, clarifying the methodology, and demonstrating benefits beyond multiple-choice would significantly improve the case.
