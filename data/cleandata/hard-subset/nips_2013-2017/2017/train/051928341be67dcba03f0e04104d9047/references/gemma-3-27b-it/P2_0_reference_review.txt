## Review of High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel, generally applicable attention mechanism based on high-order correlations between data modalities. The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms often suffer from ad-hoc representations and task-specificity. Their approach learns k-th order correlations to direct attention to relevant elements across modalities (e.g., image, question, answer). They demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on the VQA task, achieving state-of-the-art performance. The core idea involves modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials and employing mean-field inference to obtain attention probabilities.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly modeling *high-order* correlations in attention is a valuable contribution. While attention mechanisms are prevalent, systematically extending them to capture interactions beyond pairwise relationships hasn't been extensively explored, particularly with the proposed probabilistic formulation.
* **Generality:** The authors convincingly argue for the general applicability of their approach beyond VQA. The framework is designed to handle any number of modalities, which is a significant advantage.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on VQA is a substantial achievement and provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The comparison to established methods like Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7] is well-motivated.
* **Detailed Technical Explanation:** The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the proposed attention mechanism, including the formulation of potentials, mean-field inference, and the implementation details.  Figures 3 and 4 are particularly helpful for understanding the architecture.
* **Ablation Study:** The comparison of MCT and 2-layer MCB shows that the decision-making component is also important.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The introduction of higher-order potentials and mean-field inference adds significant complexity to the model. While the authors explain it well, the increased complexity might hinder reproducibility and practical adoption. The computational cost associated with higher-order correlations is not fully discussed.
* **Limited Ablation:** While an ablation regarding decision units is performed, further ablation studies exploring the contribution of the ternary potentials specifically would be valuable. It's not immediately clear how much the improvement stems from the pairwise vs. ternary interactions.
* **Failure Case Analysis:**  The single failure case illustrated (Fig. 8) highlights a potential issue – the model can get fixated on certain visual features irrespective of the question. A more comprehensive analysis of failure modes would strengthen the paper.
* **Embedding Dimensionality:** The authors acknowledge that their 512-dimensional embeddings are lower than those used in some competing methods, and suggest that increased dimensionality could improve performance. This admission, while honest, points to a potential limitation.
* **Clarity of Notation:** The notation, while generally clear, becomes somewhat dense with the introduction of multiple potential types (θV, θQ, θA, θV,Q etc.).  A concise table summarizing the notation could improve readability.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the experimental results are well-supported. However, the computational complexity of the approach needs further scrutiny.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations are thorough, but as mentioned above, the notation could be streamlined.  The visualizations (Figs. 5, 6, 7) are very helpful.
* **Originality:** The use of high-order potentials within an attention mechanism is novel and represents a clear advance over existing approaches.  The authors properly situate their work within the existing literature.
* **Significance:**  The results are important and demonstrate the potential for improving performance on VQA and potentially other multimodal tasks.  The generality of the approach is a significant strength.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* State-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark.
* Novel and well-motivated approach to attention mechanisms.
* Clear and detailed technical explanation.
* Potential for broader impact beyond VQA.

**Against:**

* Increased complexity compared to existing methods.
* Limited ablation study of individual components.
* Need for more in-depth failure mode analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of attention mechanisms and multimodal learning. The novelty of the approach, combined with the strong empirical results, makes it worthy of acceptance. While the complexity and potential computational cost are concerns, the potential benefits outweigh these drawbacks.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the suggestions regarding ablation studies and failure case analysis. The authors should also consider streamlining the notation for improved readability.
