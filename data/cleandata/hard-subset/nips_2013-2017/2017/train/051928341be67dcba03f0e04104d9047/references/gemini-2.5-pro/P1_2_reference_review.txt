**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, framed as a probabilistic model that learns and combines high-order correlations between different data modalities. The core idea is to compute "potentials" of varying orders: unary potentials (importance of features within a single modality), pairwise potentials (correlations between two modalities), and ternary potentials (correlations among three modalities). For the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA), these potentials are calculated for the image, question, and answer modalities. The final attention distribution for each modality is derived from a learnable linear combination of its relevant potentials, followed by a softmax operation, which the authors liken to a single iteration of mean-field inference. The resulting attended feature vectors are then fused for final classification using Multimodal Compact Bilinear/Trilinear Pooling. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by achieving state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset.

This work builds upon prior co-attention and multimodal pooling methods (e.g., Lu et al., 2016; Fukui et al., 2016). While pairwise (co-attention) mechanisms are common, this paper's primary contribution is the formalization of this process into a system of multi-order potentials and its extension to a third-order (ternary) potential that explicitly models the interaction between the image, question, and candidate answers to derive attention.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

*   **Novelty and Elegance of the Framework:** The conceptual framing of attention through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials is clean, intuitive, and novel. It provides a principled way to combine different attention signals. The extension to third-order attention that incorporates the answer modality directly into the attention calculation is a significant and well-motivated contribution to VQA.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper reports state-of-the-art results on the VQA test-dev and test-std benchmarks at the time of publication. This provides strong evidence that the proposed high-order attention mechanism is not just conceptually interesting but also practically effective. The comparison between their 2-modality and 3-modality models clearly demonstrates the benefit of incorporating the ternary answer-based potential.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The core ideas are explained clearly, and Figure 3 provides an excellent visual aid for understanding how the different orders of potentials are computed. The overall architecture is logical and well-described.
*   **Good Qualitative Analysis:** The authors provide insightful visualizations of the attention maps (Fig. 5, 6, 7), comparing unary, pairwise, and combined attention. This helps build intuition for how the model works. The inclusion of a failure case (Fig. 8) demonstrates a careful and honest evaluation of the model's limitations.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / suggestions for improvement)**

*   **Overstated Connection to Probabilistic Inference:** The claim that the model performs "mean-field inference" is a stretch. Equation (1) shows a weighted linear combination of scores (potentials) followed by a softmax. While this can be viewed as an energy-based model where the softmax approximates a Gibbs distribution, calling it mean-field inference is misleading, as it lacks the iterative message-passing nature characteristic of the algorithm. It would be more accurate to describe it as a learnable fusion mechanism for different attention scores, motivated by concepts from graphical models.
*   **Scalability Concerns are Not Addressed:** The paper repeatedly claims that the model can be "trivially" or "easily" extended to any number of modalities. This is a significant overstatement. The computational and memory complexity of forming the correlation tensor (C_k) grows exponentially with the order *k*. A 4th-order tensor, for example, would be computationally prohibitive for typical feature dimensions. The paper should acknowledge this severe limitation on scalability rather than presenting it as a key advantage.
*   **Lack of Clarity on Minor but Important Details:**
    *   In the calculation of pairwise and ternary potentials (Sec 3.2.2, 3.2.3), the marginalization step involves weights (`w_iv`, `w_iq`, etc.). It is unclear whether these are learnable parameters (e.g., from a 1x1 convolution, which seems likely) or a fixed function. This detail is crucial for reproducibility.
    *   The "2-layer MCB" decision unit is described as more expressive but its implementation is not entirely clear from the text. A small diagram or equation showing how the three attended vectors (aV, aQ, aA) are combined would improve clarity and reproducibility.
*   **Potentially Misleading Claim on Model Simplicity:** The conclusion states that the work demonstrates "state-of-the-art performance using relatively simple models." While the core potential-based idea is conceptually simple, the overall VQA system, which includes a deep CNN, LSTMs, and large MCB units (8192 dimensions), is quite complex and has 40 million parameters. This phrasing could be toned down to be more accurate.

**Review Summary**

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and effective contribution to the field of attention mechanisms, particularly for multimodal tasks like VQA. The core idea of combining multi-order potentials is elegant, and the results are compelling. The main strengths are the novelty of the 3-modality attention framework and the state-of-the-art empirical results that validate the approach. The weaknesses are primarily related to the overstatement of the connection to formal probabilistic inference and the unaddressed issue of computational scalability for orders greater than three. These weaknesses, however, do not undermine the core contribution for the 3-modality case presented. The paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance.
