

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors formulate attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to obtain attention probabilities. Their approach extends beyond existing pairwise attention mechanisms (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) by explicitly modeling higher-order interactions between modalities. The authors demonstrate their method on VQA, achieving state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-structured approach with clear mathematical formulation. The use of mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities is theoretically grounded, and the implementation details are sufficiently thorough. The experiments are well-designed, including ablation studies that show the importance of high-order attention.

2. **Empirical Results:** The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA (64.57% on test-dev) with a relatively small model (40 million parameters), outperforming more complex baselines. The qualitative results (Figs. 5-8) effectively demonstrate how the attention mechanism focuses on relevant visual and textual features.

3. **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The figures (especially Figs. 3, 4, and 5) effectively illustrate the key concepts of unary, pairwise, and ternary attention. The comparison to existing work in Section 2 is thorough and well-referenced.

4. **Practical Contribution:** The model is relatively simple to implement and computationally efficient compared to state-of-the-art methods. The authors provide clear implementation details and demonstrate that their approach can be applied to other multimodal tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Justification:** While the paper presents a probabilistic formulation, it lacks a strong theoretical argument for why high-order attention is necessary. The performance gain over pairwise attention is modest (0.5-1% on test-dev), and the paper doesn't convincingly explain why higher-order interactions provide such benefits. A more thorough theoretical analysis would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper cites Kim et al. [13] (Hadamard Product for Low-rank Bilinear Pooling) but doesn't adequately contrast their approach with this recent work on high-order interactions. The authors claim their method "is easy to extend to any number of data modalities" compared to "bilinear models," but this claim isn't sufficiently supported by a direct comparison.

3. **Ablation Study:** The ablation study (Table 1) shows that the 3-modality model outperforms the 2-modality model, but the authors don't provide a detailed analysis of the specific contributions of each potential type (unary, pairwise, ternary). It would be valuable to see which components contribute most to the performance gain.

4. **Significance of Results:** While the state-of-the-art performance is notable, the absolute improvement over existing methods is modest (around 0.5-1%). The paper would benefit from a more compelling argument about why this level of improvement is significant for the field, especially given the complexity of the proposed approach compared to simpler baselines.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of multimodal learning with a clear and well-executed approach to high-order attention. The empirical results are strong, and the model is relatively simple and efficient. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough theoretical justification for high-order attention, a more comprehensive comparison with recent work on high-order interactions, and a deeper analysis of the ablation results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel approach to modeling attention as high-order correlations between modalities
- State-of-the-art performance on VQA with a relatively small model
- Clear presentation and good experimental validation
- Potential applicability to other multimodal tasks

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Modest performance gain over existing methods (0.5-1%)
- Limited theoretical justification for high-order attention
- Inadequate contrast with recent work on high-order interactions
- Some aspects of the probabilistic model (e.g., Eq. 1) could be better explained

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add a theoretical section explaining why high-order attention is beneficial for multimodal tasks.
2. Conduct a more thorough ablation study to identify which components of the model contribute most to performance.
3. Directly compare with Kim et al. [13] (and other recent high-order interaction work) to better position the contribution.
4. Discuss potential limitations and failure cases in more depth (e.g., the failure case shown in Fig. 8).
5. Clarify the relationship between the probabilistic model and the attention mechanism, especially regarding the linear combination of potentials in Eq. 1.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, but would benefit from addressing the theoretical and comparative weaknesses before acceptance. The technical quality is strong, but the significance could be enhanced with the suggested improvements. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
