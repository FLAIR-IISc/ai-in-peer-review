

# Peer Review: "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms for VQA (e.g., [15, 25, 26]) are limited to pairwise interactions between modalities (image and question), whereas their approach models higher-order correlations (up to third-order: image, question, and answer). The key technical contribution is a probabilistic model that defines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture correlations across modalities, followed by mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities. The authors claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA while being simpler than previous methods.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively articulates the limitation of existing attention mechanisms that focus on pairwise interactions but ignore higher-order correlations between multiple modalities. This is a valid and important gap in the literature.

2. **Well-Structured Methodology**: The paper provides clear mathematical formulations for unary, pairwise, and ternary attention modules (Sections 3.2.1-3.2.3). The visualization in Figure 3 is particularly helpful for understanding the proposed mechanism.

3. **Good Qualitative Results**: The attention visualizations in Figures 5-7 are well-executed and provide intuitive insights into how the model attends to different parts of the image and question. The observation that "the combined result is dependent on the provided question" is compelling.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The use of tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) for decision making is well-motivated and aligns with recent work [7], making the approach computationally feasible.

## Weaknesses and Critical Concerns

### 1. Limited Novelty and Inadequate Distinction from Related Work (Major Weakness)

The paper's core claim of "novel and generally applicable form of attention mechanism" is overstated. Several recent works directly address high-order interactions:

- Kim et al. [14] ("Structured Attention Networks") explicitly proposes a multi-variate probabilistic model for attention, which is nearly identical to the authors' approach. The paper mentions this work but fails to adequately distinguish it from their contribution. The authors should clarify how their mean-field inference approach differs from Kim et al.'s structured attention.

- Nam et al. [19] ("Dual Attention Networks for Multimodal Reasoning") explores multiple attention mechanisms for different modalities, which is conceptually similar to the authors' approach. The paper mentions this work but doesn't adequately contrast it.

- Kim et al. [13] ("Hadamard Product for Low-rank Bilinear Pooling") and Fukui et al. [7] ("Multimodal Compact Bilinear Pooling") both explore higher-order interactions between modalities through bilinear pooling, which is conceptually related to the authors' approach.

The paper's claim that "attention mechanism with several potentials haven't been discussed in detail yet" is not well-supported. A more thorough comparison with these works would strengthen the novelty claim.

### 2. Insufficient Experimental Evaluation (Major Weakness)

The paper claims "state-of-the-art performance" but fails to provide a comprehensive comparison with recent SOTA methods. Key issues:

- Only two baselines are compared (Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7]), despite the existence of numerous recent VQA papers from 2017 (e.g., [11, 12, 16, 18, 20, 27, 28]). The paper should compare with at least 5-6 recent methods.

- The paper doesn't provide an ablation study showing the contribution of the high-order (ternary) attention specifically. It's unclear whether the 3-modality model's improvement over the 2-modality model is due to the high-order attention or simply the additional answer modality.

- The paper claims "despite the fact that our model has only 40 million parameters, while techniques like [7] use over 70 million parameters, we observe state-of-the-art behavior," but doesn't provide a parameter count comparison with other recent methods.

- The paper doesn't include a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the high-order attention mechanism compared to pairwise approaches.

### 3. Methodological Concerns

- **Mean-Field Inference Claim**: The paper states "our model performs mean-field inference" but the approach only uses a single iteration of mean-field (as indicated by "such a linear combination of potentials provides extra flexibility for the model, since it can learn the reliability of the potential from the data. For instance, we observe that question attention relies more on the unary question potential..."). True mean-field inference involves multiple iterations until convergence, which is not demonstrated here.

- **Notation Confusion**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for the ternary potential. In Equation (1), the authors use θV,Q,A(iv), but the ternary potential should be a function of all three modalities (image, question, answer), not just the image index. This is confusing and potentially incorrect.

- **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper doesn't provide any theoretical analysis of why high-order attention should work better than pairwise attention for VQA. The empirical results show improvement, but there's no explanation for why higher-order correlations matter for this task.

### 4. Limited Scope and Generalization

- The paper focuses exclusively on VQA, despite the claim that the approach is "generally applicable." It doesn't demonstrate the approach on other multimodal tasks (e.g., image captioning, visual question generation as mentioned in the introduction).

- The paper doesn't discuss how the approach would scale to more than three modalities, which is a key claim of the method's generality.

## Specific Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Expand Related Work**: Provide a detailed comparison with Kim et al. [14] and Nam et al. [19], explicitly stating how the proposed approach differs from these works. Clarify the relationship between the proposed high-order attention and the bilinear pooling approaches [7, 13].

2. **Conduct Comprehensive Experiments**:
   - Compare with at least 5 recent VQA methods from 2017 (e.g., [11, 12, 16, 18, 20, 27, 28])
   - Include a thorough ablation study showing the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary)
   - Provide parameter counts and computational complexity analysis

3. **Clarify Methodology**:
   - Fix the notation for ternary potential to be consistent
   - Clarify the relationship between the proposed "mean-field inference" and true mean-field
   - Provide a theoretical justification for why high-order attention should work for VQA

4. **Expand Scope**:
   - Demonstrate the approach on at least one additional multimodal task
   - Discuss how the approach would scale to more than three modalities

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound approach to modeling higher-order attention for VQA, but the novelty is limited and the experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the strong claims made. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with related work, especially Kim et al. [14] and Nam et al. [19], and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation against recent SOTA methods.

While the attention visualizations are compelling and the approach is conceptually interesting, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify its claims of novelty and state-of-the-art performance. The authors should address these concerns before the paper could be considered for publication at NIPS.

## Recommendation

**Reject**

The paper has some interesting ideas but fails to adequately demonstrate its novelty or superior performance. The experimental evaluation is insufficient, and the paper doesn't clearly distinguish its contribution from recent related work. With significant revisions addressing the concerns outlined above, the paper could potentially be strengthened to a level suitable for NIPS.
