Summary
The paper proposes a unified “high-order” attention framework for multimodal tasks, instantiated on VQA. It models attention as a set of unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over image regions, question words, and answer candidates. Potentials are computed via learned projections and correlations (bilinear for pairwise and trilinear for ternary); marginal attention distributions over each modality are then obtained by a learned linear combination of the relevant potentials followed by a softmax (interpreted as a single mean-field step). The attended vectors are fused for decision making using compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT). The authors report state-of-the-art results on VQA multiple-choice, show qualitative attention maps, and release code.

Relationship to prior work: This builds on co-attention methods (e.g., Lu et al.) and bilinear pooling (Fukui et al., Kim et al.), and is related to structured attention (Kim et al., “Structured Attention Networks”). The novelty is in explicitly structuring attention as a factorized model with unary/pairwise/ternary potentials across modalities and combining them within a single trainable “mean-field” step, and in extending to three-modality attention (including answers) in VQA.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The potential functions are standard learned projections with inner-product correlations; the combination into attention via a weighted sum plus softmax is reasonable and effective. However, the “mean-field” framing remains heuristic: no clear probabilistic model (energy, factor graph) or derivation of the update equations is given, and only a single iteration is used. This undermines the probabilistic claim.
- Empirical support: The paper claims state-of-the-art on VQA MC with fewer parameters and provides ablations (2- vs 3-modality, MCT vs 2-layer MCB) and qualitative visualizations. However, key quantitative details (exact numbers in Tab. 1) are not visible in the text provided. Ablations isolating the contribution of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials are missing; it is thus hard to assess where gains originate. Evaluation is limited to VQA multiple-choice; no open-ended results or other datasets (e.g., VQA v2, Visual7W) are reported.
- Efficiency/complexity: Computing full ternary correlations C3 over nv × nq × na appears intractable unless na is small. In MC VQA, na=18 per question, making it plausible, but the paper should explicitly state this and analyze complexity. As written, using a global answer vocabulary (top-3000) conflicts with tractability. Practical implementation details (memory use, batching, whether C3 is materialized or computed on the fly) are not provided.
- Reproducibility: Many training details are given and code is released, which is a plus. Some notational and specification gaps remain (see Clarity).

Clarity
- The paper is generally understandable with clear high-level motivation and figures. However, there are several issues:
  - Inconsistent notation/typos (e.g., aV is defined twice, use of aV for answer vector once; Eq. 3.2.2 uses wiv/wiq weights without precise definition or dimensionality; some equations are hard to parse).
  - The “mean-field” interpretation lacks a formal model; α, β, γ are said to be learnable but it is unclear if they are scalars or vectors per potential, and whether shared across modalities.
  - Ambiguity about answer embedding: na is sometimes the number of candidate answers (MC=18) and elsewhere the size of the answer vocabulary (3000). This impacts both feasibility and interpretation of ternary attention.
  - Some referenced tables/figures are not fully described, and the exact test-dev/test-std numbers are crucial for claims.

Originality
- Modeling attention as a combination of unary/pairwise/ternary potentials across modalities is a clean, unifying perspective, and the extension to include answers as a modality in attention is less common at the time (though Jabri et al. question the need for attention by using answers as inputs). The correlation-based potentials themselves are closely related to prior bilinear/trilinear interactions, and the final decision fusion uses existing MCB/MCT. The probabilistic framing is mostly an interpretation; without a rigorous derivation, the methodological novelty is moderate but still meaningful in structuring and extending co-attention to higher orders.

Significance
- If the reported state-of-the-art on VQA MC holds, this is a useful advancement. The framework is general and potentially applicable to other multimodal tasks. However, the limited evaluation scope (single dataset, MC only), lack of stronger baselines (e.g., MLB/MFB variants contemporaneous to 2017), and absence of thorough ablations dampen the broader impact. The interpretability story is promising but could be strengthened by quantitative analysis (e.g., correlation with human attention).

Strengths
- Clear, modular formulation of attention with unary/pairwise/ternary components; extensible to more modalities.
- Simple, practical implementation using 1×1 convs and correlation tensors.
- Strong reported performance with fewer parameters; qualitative attention visualizations are convincing.
- Code release and reasonable training details.

Weaknesses
- Probabilistic/mean-field justification is superficial; essentially a learned weighted sum of potentials.
- Computational complexity and scaling of ternary potentials not fully addressed; ambiguity about na.
- Evaluation limited to VQA MC; no open-ended, no other datasets; missing key quantitative table in the text.
- Insufficient ablations to isolate the contribution of high-order potentials vs baseline co-attention.
- Notational/clarity issues and typos.

Questions for authors
1. Please provide exact test-dev/test-std numbers (overall and per-answer type) and include open-ended results. How do results compare on VQA v2?
2. How is C3 computed efficiently in practice? Is na=18 (MC choices) at training/inference? What happens in open-ended where na is large?
3. Are α, β, γ scalars per potential, vectors, or functions? Are multiple mean-field iterations beneficial?
4. Can you ablate unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials and report gains? What is the latency/memory footprint?
5. How does your method compare to MLB/MFB (and dual/stacked attention) under the same feature backbone?

Verdict
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying quantitative results and scalability. The structured high-order attention idea is reasonable and appears empirically effective; with clearer exposition and stronger ablations, it would be a solid NeurIPS contribution.

Confidence: Medium.
