Summary
The paper proposes a unified high-order attention framework for multimodal reasoning, instantiated on VQA. It defines attention as probabilistic “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, answers A): unary potentials per modality, pairwise potentials via bilinear correlations (e.g., Q–V), and ternary potentials via trilinear correlations (Q–V–A). Attention distributions are obtained by a learned linear combination of marginalized potentials followed by softmax (described as a single mean-field iteration). The attended modality vectors are then fused for decision making using compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) via tensor sketching. The authors report state-of-the-art results on VQA multiple-choice with fewer parameters than some prior work, provide ablations comparing MCT vs stacked MCB, and show qualitative attention visualizations.

Strengths
- Conceptual generality: A clean decomposition of attention into unary/pairwise/ternary terms, with a mechanism that in principle extends to k modalities.
- Ternary attention: Explicit modeling of three-way correlations among image, question, and candidate answers is relatively underexplored and is well-motivated in the multiple-choice VQA setting.
- Practicality: The approach leverages efficient bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) and remains computationally feasible by restricting A to the candidate answers.
- Empirical performance: The model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on VQA multiple-choice, with a relatively modest parameter count, and offers informative visualizations that align with intuition.
- Reproducibility: Many implementation details are provided (optimizer, dimensions, dropout, feature extractors), and code is reportedly available.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Probabilistic framing vs actual inference: Calling the softmax over a linear combination of potentials “mean-field inference” is an overstatement. There is no explicit energy function, no derivation of the update equations as variational mean-field, and no iterative refinement. As presented, the mechanism is a learned mixture of features rather than principled approximate inference in a CRF.
- Relation to prior bilinear attention: The pairwise correlation matrices are standard bilinear forms (or inner products after learned projections), and the ternary tensor is a straightforward trilinear extension. Much of the benefit may stem from known bilinear pooling mechanisms plus answer-conditioning, rather than a fundamentally novel inference scheme. The paper should position more clearly w.r.t. MLB (Kim et al., ICLR 2017), co-attention (Lu et al., NIPS 2016), and other bilinear attention works, clarifying what is unique beyond moving from 2-way to 3-way interactions.
- Fairness and scope of evaluation: The strongest gains appear to leverage answers as inputs (A) in the multiple-choice setting, which meaningfully changes the problem (as noted by Jabri et al.). This is a valid VQA setting, but comparisons should clearly separate models that also condition on answer candidates vs those that do not. An open-ended evaluation (3k-way classification without candidate answers at test time) would strengthen claims of generality and significance.
- Limited ablations: There is no systematic disentangling of the contributions of unary, pairwise, and ternary terms, nor an examination of the learned mixture weights (α, β, γ). It would be valuable to report performance with: only unary; unary+pairwise; pairwise only; ternary added; and to quantify where ternary attention helps (e.g., by question type).
- Complexity and scalability: While ternary attention is feasible with 18 candidate answers, the approach does not analyze complexity or memory usage, nor discuss scalability to more candidates or to open-ended settings (where na would be large). Similarly, the “convolutional” marginalization over C3 is effectively a learned sum; the computational and memory costs merit explicit discussion.
- Clarity and notational issues: There are several typos and ambiguous descriptions (e.g., aA mistakenly written as aV; unclear “1×1 conv along the visual dimension” phrasing; shapes of C2/C3 and indices; whether weights for marginalization are shared; MCB “allowing image features to interact with themselves” but the expression repeats Q). Figures are referenced but sometimes not adequately explained in text.
- Experimental reporting: Key quantitative tables are not shown here, but the narrative mentions small gains of MCT vs 2-layer MCB on val and claims SOTA on test-dev/test-std. Standard VQA reporting typically includes Yes/No, Number, and Other breakdowns; these should be reported. Statistical significance or variability would be helpful. Comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., MLB, SAN, DAN) should be comprehensive and matched for visual features and training data.

Quality
Technically sound at the implementation level, but the probabilistic justification is thin. The ternary attention is a straightforward trilinear correlation with learned marginalization, and the decision module uses known tensor sketching. Claims of generality are plausible; claims of principled mean-field inference are not substantiated.

Clarity
Readable and structured, but needs tighter notation, corrections of typos, and a more precise explanation of computational steps and shapes. The probabilistic section should either provide a derivation or drop the mean-field terminology.

Originality
Moderate. The main novelty is explicit ternary attention over V–Q–A and its integration with high-order fusion. Pairwise components and the compact pooling are established. The extension from bilinear to trilinear is conceptually incremental but practically useful in MC VQA.

Significance
Potentially useful for multimodal tasks with more than two modalities, especially when candidate outputs can be conditioned upon. The empirical SOTA (if confirmed) is noteworthy, though its generality beyond MC VQA remains to be shown.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide a proper probabilistic model (energy, factorization), derive the mean-field updates, and/or rephrase the method without invoking mean-field.
- Add ablations isolating unary/pairwise/ternary contributions and report learned mixture weights to validate where each potential helps.
- Report open-ended VQA results without answer candidates, or justify the focus on MC with a fair comparison set including MC methods that also condition on answers.
- Include complexity analysis (time/memory) for pairwise and ternary potentials; discuss scalability and potential approximations for larger na.
- Clarify implementation details: shapes of C2/C3; whether marginalization weights are shared across positions; details of the FC stack used after MCB/MCT.
- Fix typos and ambiguous notation (e.g., aA vs aV, self-interaction explanation).
- Report per-type accuracies and ensure matched settings against baselines (features, splits, training data).

Reasons to accept
- Clear, general framework that unifies unary/pairwise/ternary attention.
- Demonstrated gains on VQA multiple-choice with fewer parameters and good qualitative analyses.
- Practical contribution: integrating ternary attention with compact trilinear pooling.

Reasons to reject
- Limited novelty beyond straightforward trilinear extension and known compact pooling; overstated probabilistic framing.
- Evaluation centered on multiple-choice with answer conditioning, with limited ablations and unclear fairness to baselines.
- Clarity issues and missing theoretical/computational analysis.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. With strengthened theoretical framing (or a tempered claim), more comprehensive ablations, and clearer, fair evaluations (possibly including open-ended), this would be a solid contribution. As is, it offers a useful engineering advance with promising results but falls short on rigor and comparative analysis. Confidence: high.
