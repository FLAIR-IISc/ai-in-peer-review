Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a unified, higher-order attention framework for multimodal reasoning, instantiated on VQA. The core idea is to view attention as marginal distributions derived from learnable potentials defined over modalities: unary (per-modality saliency), pairwise (correlations between two modalities), and ternary (three-way correlations among image, question, and answers). Potentials are computed via simple 1×1 convs (i.e., linear projections) and inner-product correlations: C2 for pairs and C3 for triples. The model then linearly combines marginalized potentials with learnable weights, followed by a softmax (one “mean-field” iteration) to yield attentions over each modality. Attended vectors are fused for decision making via Tensor Sketching: MCB (pairwise) or its trilinear extension (MCT), avoiding the parameter blow-up of explicit outer products.

Relative to prior co-attention and bilinear pooling works (e.g., Lu et al. 2016; Fukui et al. 2016; Kim et al. MLB/low-rank bilinear), this paper’s novelty is to explicitly formulate attention with higher-order potentials and to show the practical benefit of including answer as an input (ternary attention) for multiple-choice VQA. The approach is conceptually closer to a factor-graph/CRF-like view (structured/mean-field attention, cf. Kim et al. “Structured Attention Networks”), but here tailored to multimodal attention with arbitrary order k. Empirically, the method achieves strong results on VQA (multiple-choice) with qualitative visualizations that align image and text attentions.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and generality: A clear, modular formulation of k-th order attention potentials that, in principle, extends to any number of modalities. This is a bold, unifying perspective likely to inspire further work.
- Practical effectiveness: Reported state-of-the-art on VQA multiple-choice with fewer parameters than some baselines. Qualitative examples convincingly show question-conditioned shifts in attention and sensible answer-side attentions in the 3-modality setting.
- Sensible engineering: Uses Tensor Sketching (MCB/MCT) to keep decision-stage complexity manageable while still modeling high-order interactions; learnable mixing weights (α,β,γ) for robustness to noisy potentials.
- Interpretability: The decomposition into unary/pairwise/ternary attentions provides diagnostic insight unavailable in most monolithic co-attention models.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Mean-field inference claim: The “probabilistic” framing is largely a metaphor; attention is obtained by a single linear combination of potentials and a softmax. There is no explicit probabilistic model (e.g., normalized joint with factors) or derivation for the combination coefficients. This weakens the theoretical grounding.
- Scalability of ternary potentials: C3 is O(d·nq·nv·na). This is feasible for multiple-choice (na ≈ 18) but seems prohibitive for open-ended settings (na in the thousands). The paper should clarify whether ternary attention is only used for MC, and if so, temper claims of generality, or present strategies for approximating C3 in open-ended VQA.
- Evaluation scope and fairness: 
  - Results focus on multiple-choice VQA v1.0; open-ended and/or VQA v2.0 are absent. Given the claimed generality, showing open-ended performance (even without answer-input) would strengthen the case.
  - The 2-modality baseline uses d=512 vs. 2048 in some competitors, acknowledged to underperform. Stronger, controlled ablations (same d, same backbones) are needed to isolate gains from higher-order attention vs. capacity.
  - Limited ablations: No systematic study of the contribution of unary vs. pairwise vs. ternary terms, learned weights, or multiple mean-field iterations.
- Reporting and presentation issues: The exact numbers for test-dev/test-std SOTA are not clearly stated in the text provided; reliance on an omitted Table 1 makes it hard to verify the claim. Several typos and notation errors (e.g., repeated aV where aA is intended), and incomplete references.

Quality
Technically sound as a modeling contribution; implementation details are clear enough to reproduce. However, the probabilistic justification is light, and empirical validation could be broader (open-ended VQA, stronger ablations, fairer baselines). Still, the approach works in practice and is thoughtfully engineered.

Clarity
Generally understandable with useful figures, but the paper would benefit from proof-reading and tighter notation. Please fix typos, clarify complexity of C3, and explicitly state all main numbers (test-dev/test-std) in the main text.

Originality
High. While bilinear/co-attention and tensor sketching are known, the explicit higher-order potential view of attention across k modalities, with learnable aggregation, is a novel and promising conceptual advance circa 2017.

Significance
Moderate-to-high. The framework is general and could influence subsequent multimodal attention designs. Demonstrated gains on VQA MC are encouraging, though stronger breadth of evaluation would increase impact.

Questions for the authors
- How is C3 computed efficiently? What are runtime/memory numbers for ternary attention on typical VQA settings? Is ternary attention used only for MC (na≈18)? If so, can you discuss applicability to open-ended VQA and possible approximations?
- Can you provide controlled ablations quantifying the marginal gains of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials; learned mixing weights; and additional mean-field iterations?
- Please report exact test-dev and test-std accuracies (overall and per-type) for your best model(s), and include fair capacity-matched baselines (same d, same CNN).
- Have you evaluated on VQA v2.0 or Visual7W? Any observations on robustness to dataset biases?

Minor comments and typos
- Several notation slips (e.g., duplicated aV in the attended answer equation). Please correct.
- “and and” duplicates; some references lack full bibliographic info; unify naming of methods (MCB/MCT).
- Clarify “convolution with 1×1 along dimension” vs. learned weighted sums in Eqs. for θV,Q and θV,Q,A.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, general, and interpretable formulation of higher-order multimodal attention.
- Empirical gains on VQA MC with efficient decision-stage pooling.
- Clear potential to catalyze further research on k-modality attention.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited evaluation scope (MC only; no open-ended/v2.0); ablations and fairness issues.
- Weak theoretical grounding for the “mean-field” claim.
- Scalability of ternary potentials not fully addressed.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). Novel and promising direction with solid empirical evidence on MC VQA; I’m willing to overlook some evaluation gaps given the conceptual contribution. Confidence: 3/5.
