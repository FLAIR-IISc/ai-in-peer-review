Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a high-order attention framework for multimodal reasoning, instantiated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea is to model attention as a probabilistic mechanism with (i) unary potentials over each modality (image, question, answer), (ii) pairwise potentials capturing correlations between any two modalities via learned linear embeddings and similarities, and (iii) a ternary potential capturing joint interactions among all three. The marginal attentions over each modality are obtained by a softmax over a learned linear combination of these potentials, interpreted as a single mean-field iteration. The attended features are then fused for decision making via Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) pooling or its trilinear extension (MCT), following tensor sketching to avoid the parameter blow-up of explicit outer products. Experiments on VQA (multiple-choice setting) show competitive or state-of-the-art results, with qualitative visualizations illustrating how pairwise and ternary terms modulate attention.

This builds on and generalizes co-attention and bilinear pooling ideas prevalent in VQA: co-attention between image and text (e.g., Lu et al., NIPS’16), stacked/hierarchical attention (Yang et al., 2016), MCB pooling for pairwise fusion (Fukui et al., 2016), low-rank bilinear variants (Kim et al., 2017), dual attention (Nam et al., 2017), and structured/CRF-inspired attention (Kim et al., 2017). The novelty here is an explicit high-order potential view with a ternary correlation tensor and mean-field-inspired marginalization, plus empirical evidence that using answer candidates as an additional modality helps in multiple-choice VQA.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Framing attention as a combination of unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and using a mean-field interpretation provides a coherent, extensible perspective on multimodal attention.
- Higher-order interactions: The ternary correlation term and the ability to incorporate answer candidates as an input are natural for multiple-choice VQA and lead to empirical gains over a 2-modality baseline.
- Practical design: The use of 1×1 convolutions, learned linear combinations of potentials, and tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) keeps parameter counts moderate and allows high-order fusion.
- Empirical results: On VQA multiple-choice, the model is competitive with strong baselines, with qualitative attention maps that are plausible and question-specific.
- Clarity of architectural components: The paper clearly separates embedding, attention, and decision making, with ablations comparing MCT vs two-stage MCB.

Weaknesses and assessment by review criteria

Quality
- Probabilistic formalization is underspecified. The “mean-field” interpretation is appealing but not formalized: no explicit energy function/factor graph, no derivation that shows the proposed linear combination corresponds to a principled variational update, and only a single iteration is used. This weakens the theoretical claim.
- Computational complexity of ternary correlations is unclear. For multiple-choice VQA the answer set per question is small (18), but the paper also mentions a 3000-answer output space. It is not explicit which answer set is used inside the attention module and how the C3 tensor is computed efficiently. A complexity/memory analysis and implementation details are needed.
- Evaluation breadth is limited. Results focus on multiple-choice VQA; there is no evaluation on open-ended VQA or other datasets (e.g., VQA v2, CLEVR, TDIUC) to test generality and robustness. Statistical significance is not reported.
- Ablations are partial. There is no clear quantification of the incremental contribution of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials, the learned mixing weights (α, β, γ), or multiple mean-field iterations. Also missing: comparisons to alternative pairwise/ternary fusion methods beyond MCB/MCT (e.g., MLB/MFB/MFH).
- Reliance on answer-as-input limits applicability. While appropriate for multiple-choice, using answer candidates as a modality is inapplicable to open-ended settings and risks conflating modeling of candidate scoring with genuine reasoning.

Clarity
- Several notational and editorial issues: weights w_iv, w_iq, w_iv,ia in the marginalization steps are not fully specified (shapes/parameterization); some typos and duplicated phrases; “smax” is used without early definition. Figure references are somewhat dense, and some equations would benefit from clearer indexing.
- Ambiguity about the answer representation used in attention (18 candidates vs top-3000 vocabulary) needs clarification.

Originality
- The high-order potential framing and ternary attention are a meaningful extension of co-attention, but overlap conceptually with structured attention and multilinear pooling literature. The decision module largely adopts existing tensor sketching. Overall, the contribution feels incremental but nontrivial.

Significance
- For multiple-choice VQA, the approach appears to modestly advance the state of the art with fewer parameters. However, broader impact is limited by the focus on the MC setting and by the lack of evaluation on more diagnostic datasets. The theoretical perspective could be influential if strengthened and generalized.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concrete probabilistic model: define the joint distribution/energy over attention variables, derive the mean-field update, and justify the learned linear mixing. Consider multi-iteration mean-field and report effects.
- Clarify computational aspects: specify na used in the ternary attention at train/test, memory/time costs, and any factorization tricks (e.g., low-rank or CP/Tucker decompositions) to scale C3 beyond MC VQA.
- Expand ablations: remove/replace each potential type; vary α/β/γ; compare single vs multiple MF iterations; evaluate with and without the answer modality; test MCB/MCT vs MLB/MFB/MFH under matched features.
- Strengthen baselines and datasets: include open-ended VQA, VQA v2, and a compositional dataset (CLEVR/TDIUC) to assess reasoning benefits of higher-order attention. Control for CNN/LSTM backbones and parameter counts.
- Improve clarity and notation; add proofs/derivations to the supplement; detail training/inference for MC vs open-ended settings.
- Explore region-level features (e.g., object proposals) to improve spatial grounding and efficiency.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, unified high-order attention formulation with plausible mean-field interpretation.
- Demonstrated gains on VQA MC with fewer parameters and informative visualizations.
- Practical and extensible design likely of interest to multimodal learning researchers.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical grounding of the “mean-field” claim.
- Limited evaluation scope (MC VQA only), missing stronger baselines and datasets.
- Unclear computational scaling and some ambiguities in implementation details.
- Incremental novelty relative to existing co-/dual attention and bilinear pooling methods.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea of high-order multimodal attention is promising and the empirical results are encouraging, but the paper needs a stronger and clearer probabilistic formulation, more comprehensive ablations, and broader, more rigorous evaluation to meet the NeurIPS bar.
