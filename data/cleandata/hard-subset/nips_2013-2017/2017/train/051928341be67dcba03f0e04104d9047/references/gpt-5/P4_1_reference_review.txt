Summary
The paper proposes a general high-order attention framework for multimodal learning, instantiated on visual question answering (VQA). Attention is modeled via potentials over modalities: unary potentials over each modality (image V, question Q, and answers A), pairwise potentials capturing cross-modal interactions (e.g., V–Q, V–A, Q–A) through similarity matrices, and ternary potentials capturing three-way interactions via a correlation tensor. The final attention distributions over each modality are computed by a softmax over a learned linear combination of the corresponding unary, pairwise-marginalized, and ternary-marginalized potentials. The attended modality vectors are fused for decision making using Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) or Trilinear (MCT) pooling. On the VQA dataset (multiple-choice setting), the method reports state-of-the-art or competitive results, with qualitative analyses showing question-conditional attention behavior.

Relation to prior work: The approach unifies and extends co-attention mechanisms (e.g., Lu et al., NIPS 2016) by explicitly modeling higher-order interactions across modalities. It also builds on multimodal pooling methods (Fukui et al., MCB) to control parameter growth in decision fusion, and is thematically related to structured attention (Kim et al., 2017) and bilinear/low-rank pooling methods (Kim et al., ICLR 2017). The novelty is to treat attention potentials as explicit k-th order correlations and to combine them in a single framework that, in principle, extends to any number of modalities.

Strengths
- Clear modular decomposition. The paper separates embedding, attention, and decision components, and presents consistent notation and implementation details for V, Q, A.
- Generality of the attention formulation. Defining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials in a common template is conceptually appealing and directly extends co-attention to higher orders.
- Empirical performance. The method achieves strong results on VQA multiple-choice with fewer parameters than some prior work, and shows that adding the third modality (answers) improves over two-modality variants.
- Practical fusion design. Using MCB/MCT to fuse attended features and their higher-order interactions is sensible and empirically validated; the two-layer MCB variant outperforms MCT in the reported setting.
- Qualitative analyses. Attention visualizations demonstrate that pairwise and combined potentials focus on question-relevant image regions, providing interpretability.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Probabilistic framing is underspecified. The paper claims mean-field inference over a probabilistic model with high-order potentials but does not define the joint distribution or energy explicitly, nor derive the mean-field updates. The final attention is a learned linear combination of potentials followed by softmax, with no iterative inference. This weakens the “mean-field” claim and blurs the connection to structured attention methods.
- Computational complexity of ternary potentials. The ternary tensor C3 has size O(nq nv na), which is prohibitive when na is large (e.g., 3000 answers). The paper computes marginalized signals via learned summations over indices, but it is not explained how C3 is formed or avoided in memory/compute. An efficient factorization or on-the-fly computation (e.g., via CP/TT formats) should be described and analyzed.
- Scope of evaluation. Results are reported for the multiple-choice setting (with answers as inputs), which is easier and less widely adopted than open-ended VQA. Comparability to works that do not condition on answer choices is limited; the paper cites Jabri et al. but does not quantify the gap or provide open-ended results.
- Limited ablations. There is no systematic study of the contribution of unary vs. pairwise vs. ternary potentials, the learned mixing weights (α, β, γ), or the effect of removing answer-conditioned attention. An ablation would clarify where the gains come from.
- Incomplete comparison and reporting. The quantitative tables are not shown here, but the text references “state-of-the-art” without detailed baselines or statistical significance. Runtime/memory, training stability, and sensitivity to hyperparameters are not discussed.
- Clarity and notation. Some operations (e.g., “convolution along the visual dimension using a 1×1 kernel” for aggregating correlation matrices) are unconventional and could be spelled out algebraically. Several references lack venue and year details.

Quality
- The attention construction via projected (bi/tri)linear correlations is technically plausible and consistent with prior bilinear co-attention literature. However, the probabilistic interpretation and mean-field claim are not rigorously substantiated, and the computational treatment of ternary interactions is not analyzed. Empirical results are positive but would benefit from stronger ablations and broader benchmarks.

Clarity
- Generally clear high-level presentation, with helpful figures and implementation details for embeddings and training. Missing or ambiguous parts include the exact computation/memory strategy for C3, the definition of the full probabilistic model, and explicit equations for the “convolutions” on C2/C3.

Originality
- The framing of attention as a sum of learned unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over modalities is a useful and, at the time, relatively novel extension of co-attention. The use of MCT is an extension of prior MCB work. Novelty is moderate: many components build on established ideas (bilinear pooling, co-attention), but the unified higher-order attention perspective is a contribution.

Significance
- If computational issues are addressed, the approach could be of interest beyond VQA (e.g., video–audio–text). Within VQA, gains over two-modality methods appear meaningful, but reliance on multiple-choice settings and inclusion of answers as inputs limit generality. The work could stimulate follow-up on efficient higher-order attention.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a formal energy-based model with factors and derive the mean-field updates; clarify why a single “iteration” suffices and whether multiple iterations help.
- Detail the computational strategy for C3 (and general k-way) potentials, with complexity and memory analyses; consider tensor factorizations to avoid explicit construction.
- Add ablations quantifying the impact of each potential order, the mixing weights, and the inclusion of answer-conditioned terms; report open-ended VQA results.
- Expand comparisons to recent co-/dual-attention and bilinear pooling approaches with matched settings; report variance over runs and runtime/memory.
- Improve clarity of the “convolution” aggregation and fix/reference formatting.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and general high-order attention formulation.
- Demonstrated improvements over two-modality baselines; competitive results with fewer parameters.
- Practical fusion design and interpretable qualitative behavior.

Arguments against acceptance
- Probabilistic/mean-field claims not formally supported.
- Unclear computational feasibility/scalability of ternary attention in realistic settings.
- Evaluation limited to multiple-choice VQA with answer inputs; limited ablations and comparisons.

Overall
A promising and potentially impactful formulation of higher-order attention across modalities, with encouraging empirical results. The paper would be substantially strengthened by a rigorous probabilistic derivation, a clear computational treatment of ternary interactions, and broader/ablation-heavy evaluation, especially in open-ended VQA.
