- Proposes a unified high-order attention for multimodal reasoning using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over modalities (V, Q, A) with marginalization and softmax-based attention.
- Implements fusion via Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) and a proposed Multimodal Compact Trilinear (MCT) pooling; reports strong multiple-choice VQA results with fewer parameters.
- Places work relative to co-attention, stacked/dual attention, and bilinear fusion literature; frames novelty as explicit k-ary potentials and trilinear pooling.
- Strength: clear modularization into unary/pairwise/ternary building blocks using simple linear projections and inner products.
- Strength: formulation is extensible to more modalities.
- Strength: uses tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to avoid exponential decision-layer blow-up.
- Strength: convincing qualitative visualizations showing higher-order terms improve attention.
- Strength: competitive empirical performance claimed and code availability promised.
- Weakness: probabilistic/mean-field framing is unsubstantiated—no global energy, factor graph, partition function, or MF derivation and iterations discussed.
- Weakness: ternary tensor C3 scalability unclear and potentially intractable; ambiguous whether na is per-question MC set (18) or global top-3k answers.
- Weakness: notational and implementation ambiguities for marginalization weights (wiv, wiq, wiv,ia) and projection matrices (Wq, Wv, Wa).
- Weakness: evaluation limited—mainly multiple-choice results; open-ended VQA, per-type accuracy, and quantitative attention metrics are missing.
- Weakness: insufficient ablations isolating unary/pairwise/ternary contributions and the effect of including answers as a modality.
- Weakness: limited comparison to strong bilinear baselines (MLB, MUTAN) and unclear fairness of experimental settings.
- Weakness: attention evaluation lacks quantitative correlation with human attention benchmarks (e.g., HAT).
- Quality: technically sound at the operator level but probabilistic framing and ternary computational feasibility need more analysis.
- Clarity: generally readable with helpful figures, but key definitions (weights, shapes, na) and “mean-field” terminology are ambiguous.
- Originality: moderate — useful re-framing into high-order potentials but underlying operators are close to existing bilinear/trilinear methods.
- Significance: potentially useful if claims hold under fair, complete evaluation; impact limited by current missing analyses and scalability concerns.
- Key questions for authors: provide explicit probabilistic model and MF derivation; clarify na and C3 implementation/memory; specify parameterization of marginalization weights; add systematic ablations and OE results; compare to MLB/MUTAN; quantify attention quality and backbone sensitivity.
- Pros summary: modular high-order attention, extensible to more modalities, practical sketching-based fusion, strong qualitative results, plausible gains with fewer parameters.
- Cons summary: unsupported probabilistic claims, unclear ternary scalability, incomplete empirical validation, ambiguous implementation details.
- Recommendation: borderline reject pending camera-ready responses addressing probabilistic derivation, ternary scalability, and thorough, fair comparisons (including OE VQA and ablations).
