

# Peer Review: "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer) for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms either extract abstract representations in an ad-hoc manner or are tailored to specific tasks, and they introduce a probabilistic framework that captures unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to model correlations between modalities. The approach uses mean-field inference to compute marginal probabilities and leverages tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) for efficient decision making. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset with a relatively simple model (40M parameters), showing that high-order attention significantly improves performance over two-modality models.

The paper builds on the recent trend of attention mechanisms in multimodal tasks, particularly drawing from Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25] for two-modality attention, and Fukui et al. [7] for multimodal pooling. The authors position their work as a generalization that extends naturally to any number of modalities, which is a significant advantage over prior work.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear mathematical formulations of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials.
- The experimental setup is thorough, including both quantitative results on standard VQA benchmarks and qualitative analysis of attention mechanisms.
- The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the failure case in Fig. 8) and provide a detailed comparison with relevant baselines.
- The implementation details (hyperparameters, training setup, etc.) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.
- The 3-modality model achieves better results than the 2-modality model, demonstrating the importance of high-order correlations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper claims novelty in "high-order attention" but the specific implementation using tensor sketch (MCB/MCT) has significant overlap with Fukui et al. [7], which the authors cite but don't sufficiently differentiate from. The paper states "In contrast to the latter two models our approach is easy to extend to any number of data modalities," but MCB/MCT is already designed for multiple modalities.
- The explanation of ternary potentials (Section 3.2.3) is somewhat unclear. The authors define a 3-way correlation but don't adequately explain how this differs from simply composing pairwise potentials or why a ternary potential is necessary.
- The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies on the contribution of each potential type (unary, pairwise, ternary). It's unclear how much each component contributes to the final performance.
- The comparison with Lu et al. [15] is not entirely fair, as the authors don't use the same data embeddings or model architecture for a direct comparison.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized with clear sections and logical flow.
- The figures (particularly Figs. 3, 4, and 7) effectively illustrate the key concepts of the attention mechanism.
- The mathematical formulations are clearly presented with appropriate notation.
- The experimental results are well-presented with detailed tables and qualitative examples.

**Weaknesses:**
- The explanation of the ternary potential computation is confusing. The authors define (C3)i,j,k as corr3((QWq):,i, (VWv):,j, (AWa):,k), but then the marginalization process (θV,Q,A(iq)=tanh(∑_iv,ia wiv,ia(C3)iq,iv,ia)) is not clearly motivated.
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the mean-field inference works in practice. The authors state "such a linear combination of potentials provides extra flexibility," but don't explain how the parameters αi, βi, γi are learned or how they contribute to the model's performance.
- The explanation of the MCT unit (Fig. 4) is somewhat confusing. It's unclear why MCT is "approximately sampling from the outer product space" compared to MCB.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of modeling high-order correlations between modalities is novel in the context of attention mechanisms for VQA.
- The authors successfully generalize attention mechanisms to any number of modalities, which is a significant advantage over previous two-modality approaches.
- The probabilistic interpretation of attention using potentials is a fresh perspective that provides interpretability.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty is somewhat overstated. The use of tensor sketch (MCB/MCT) for multimodal fusion was already introduced in Fukui et al. [7] and extended to trilinear pooling in the same paper (though not explicitly called MCT). The authors cite this work but don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from it.
- The paper doesn't clearly explain how their "high-order attention" differs from the "stacked attention" approaches in Yang et al. [27] or the "multi-hop attention" in Xu et al. [25]. These approaches also model higher-order interactions, though not explicitly through a probabilistic framework.
- The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with the work by Kim et al. [14] (Structured Attention Networks), which also models multi-variate probabilistic dependencies.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA with a relatively simple model (40M parameters vs. 70M+ for some baselines), demonstrating that high-order attention is valuable without requiring massive model complexity.
- The model's interpretability (through attention visualization) provides insights into how the model processes multimodal inputs.
- The framework is generalizable to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA, which broadens its potential impact.
- The paper demonstrates that high-order attention correlations significantly improve performance over two-modality models, which is a meaningful contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of the high-order attention mechanism. While the authors claim efficiency through tensor sketching, it's unclear if the high-order mechanism is actually more efficient than the two-modality approaches they compare to.
- The paper doesn't explore whether the high-order attention mechanism is necessary for other tasks beyond VQA, limiting the broader significance of the approach.
- The performance gain (64.57% vs. 63.82% on the val set) is modest compared to the state-of-the-art, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this small improvement is worth the complexity of the high-order mechanism.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a compelling idea for modeling high-order correlations in multimodal attention, with solid experimental results demonstrating its effectiveness for VQA. The technical approach is sound, and the model achieves state-of-the-art results with a relatively simple architecture. However, the novelty is somewhat overstated, as the specific implementation (using MCB/MCT) builds directly on prior work by Fukui et al. [7]. The paper would be stronger with more thorough ablation studies, clearer explanations of the ternary potential mechanism, and a more precise differentiation from related work.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multimodal attention mechanisms, and the results are convincing. The main issues to address are:
1. Better differentiation from Fukui et al. [7] and other related work (particularly regarding the novelty of the tensor sketch implementation)
2. Clarification of the ternary potential mechanism and its necessity
3. More detailed ablation studies on the contribution of each potential type
4. More precise comparison with relevant baselines (e.g., using the same data embeddings for fair comparison)

The paper is well-written and addresses an important problem in multimodal learning. With the suggested revisions, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

## Pro/Con Checklist

**Pro (Arguments for Acceptance):**
- Novel probabilistic framework for high-order attention
- State-of-the-art results on VQA with a relatively simple model
- Clear qualitative analysis of attention mechanisms
- Generalizable to any number of modalities
- Well-organized and well-written

**Con (Arguments for Rejection):**
- Overstated novelty (significant overlap with Fukui et al. [7])
- Inadequate explanation of ternary potentials
- Limited ablation studies on the contribution of different potential types
- Modest performance gain over state-of-the-art
- Unfair comparison with some baselines (e.g., Lu et al. [15])

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 3.2.3, provide a clearer explanation of why ternary potentials are necessary and how they differ from composing pairwise potentials. Consider adding a small example to illustrate.

2. In the Related Work section, more thoroughly compare with Fukui et al. [7] and Kim et al. [14], explaining precisely how the current approach differs and why it's a meaningful extension.

3. Add ablation studies showing the performance impact of including unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials separately (e.g., removing ternary potentials and seeing the performance drop).

4. In Section 4.3, provide a more direct comparison with Lu et al. [15] using the same data embeddings and model architecture for a fairer evaluation.

5. Clarify the computational cost of the high-order attention mechanism compared to two-modality approaches. Is it actually more efficient in practice, or does the high-order mechanism require more computation despite the tensor sketch?

These revisions would strengthen the paper's contribution and make the novelty more clearly established.
