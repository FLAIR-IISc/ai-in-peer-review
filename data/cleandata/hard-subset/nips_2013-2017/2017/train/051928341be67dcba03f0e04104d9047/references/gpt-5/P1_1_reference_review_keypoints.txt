- Proposes a general high-order attention framework modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary "potentials" over modalities (V, Q, A) for multimodal learning, applied to multiple-choice VQA.
- Attention distributions are produced by a softmax over a learned linear combination of unary/pairwise/ternary terms; attended vectors are fused with MCB/MCT for answer prediction.
- Claims extensibility to arbitrary modality counts and interpretability via attention maps; reports SOTA on VQA multiple-choice with fewer parameters.

Strengths:
- Presents a clear, modular recipe for constructing unary/pairwise/ternary attention using simple projections and inner products.
- Offers a unifying view that explicitly models k-way interactions beyond pairwise co-attention.
- Effective engineering: tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to control cost, sensible training details, and promised code release.
- Learned weighting of different order potentials (α/β/γ) allows adaptive reliance on modalities.
- Qualitative attention visualizations appear sensible and supportive.

Weaknesses:
- Probabilistic/mean-field justification is loose and lacks a formal derivation from a defined energy.
- Computational cost of ternary terms is not analyzed; naive C3 construction scales as O(nq·nv·na·d) and may be infeasible for large na or open-ended VQA.
- Experimental scope is narrow: only multiple-choice VQA reported; no open-ended results or per-type breakdowns (Yes/No, Number, Other).
- Missing comparisons to strong bilinear baselines (MLB, MFB/MFH) and recent co-attention variants.
- Limited ablations: no isolated study of unary vs pairwise vs ternary, no α/β/γ analysis, no multi-iteration MF evaluation, no test of removing answer-as-input.
- Clarity issues and typos (mis-typed variables, unclear convolution shapes, unspecified marginalization parameter forms).
- Using answer choices as an input modality changes the task formulation, complicating fair comparisons to open-ended VQA.

Quality:
- Modules are standard neural components arranged coherently; mean-field claim reads more metaphorical than derived.
- Experimental evidence is suggestive but incomplete—missing full tables, per-category metrics, confidence intervals, and strong baselines.

Clarity:
- Generally readable and well organized but needs precise definitions for marginalization weights, convolution shapes, and exact parameter shapes.
- Should either derive the energy/MF updates leading to Eq.(1) or remove/soften probabilistic language.
- Fix notational inconsistencies and report full evaluation details (dataset versions, metrics, variance).

Originality:
- Moderate: builds on established co-attention and bilinear pooling work; main novelty is explicit k-th order potential construction and inclusion of answer modality.

Significance:
- Potentially useful, reusable design for multimodal attention if gains generalize beyond MC VQA; current impact limited by narrow experiments and missing ablations.

Requested revisions / suggestions:
- Provide a formal energy function and derive MF updates yielding Eq.(1), or reframe without probabilistic claims.
- Provide complexity/runtime/memory analysis for C3 and typical nq, nv, na values; propose approximations for larger na or more modalities.
- Run full ablations (remove unary/pairwise/ternary, vary α/β/γ, test multiple MF iterations, A-as-input vs not) and compare MCT vs stacked MCB under equal capacity.
- Compare to MLB/MFB/MFH and more recent co-attention baselines; report open-ended VQA and per-type scores with error bars.
- Clarify backbone features (VGG vs ResNet) and ensure fair apples-to-apples baselines; release code and pretrained models; report inference speed and memory.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, general, and interpretable high-order attention construction with promising empirical results and modular design.

Cons for acceptance:
- Weak theoretical grounding for the mean-field interpretation, narrow experimental evaluation, unclear ternary scalability, and several clarity/notation issues.

Recommendation:
- Weak accept contingent on stronger empirical comparisons, fuller ablations, clearer probabilistic framing, and addressing scalability/clarity concerns.
