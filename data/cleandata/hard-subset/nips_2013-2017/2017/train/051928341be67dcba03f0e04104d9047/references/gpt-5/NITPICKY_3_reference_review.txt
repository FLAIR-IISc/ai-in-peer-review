Summary:
The paper proposes “high-order attention” for multimodal reasoning, instantiated for VQA with three modalities: image (V), question (Q), and candidate answers (A). The model defines unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modality elements, where pairwise/ternary terms are correlation (inner-product) tensors computed after learned linear projections. Attention for each modality is produced by a softmax over a learned linear combination of the modality’s unary, pairwise-marginalized, and ternary-marginalized potentials. The attended vectors are then fused for decision making using MCB/MCT (tensor sketching) to avoid the explosion in parameters. Experiments on VQA (multiple-choice) are claimed to yield state-of-the-art performance; qualitative attention maps are shown.

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple, modular attention formulation that unifies unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions and is, in principle, extensible to k modalities.
- Clear intuition: pairwise and ternary correlation tensors encourage attentions that co-vary across modalities; quantitative gain of 3-modality over 2-modality is plausible and interesting for MC-VQA.
- Uses efficient compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) to mitigate parameter growth; cites appropriate prior.
- Some qualitative examples suggest the model can focus on question-relevant regions and correlate with answer candidates.

Weaknesses:
Major issues
- Double-blind violation: the paper includes an acknowledgments section and a public GitHub URL revealing the authors’ identities. This alone requires rejection under NeurIPS policy.
- Overstated “mean-field inference”: the method computes attention via a single softmax over a learned linear combination of potentials. There is no explicit probabilistic model or energy, no factor graph, and no iterative mean-field updates. Calling this “mean-field” is misleading without a derivation.
- Insufficient, inconsistent experimental reporting: “state-of-the-art” is claimed but the core table (Tab. 1) is missing; only two val-set numbers (63.82 vs 64.57) are given. No standard VQA test-dev/test-std accuracies (overall and per-type), no confidence intervals, and no statistical analysis. Comparisons to strong recent baselines are sparse; ablations isolating unary/pairwise/ternary contributions are absent.
- Ambiguity/inconsistency about using answers as inputs: A is defined both as a large vocabulary embedding (top-3000) and as per-question candidates (na=18). The text alternates between open-ended (classification over 3000 answers) and MC evaluation (highest-scoring among 18 provided). It is unclear which setting is primary, how A is constructed per example, and how results are computed. This undermines reproducibility and interpretation.

Technical/methodological issues
- Complexity: Constructing C3 ∈ Rnq×nv×na and marginalizing it is O(nq nv na) per example. The paper does not analyze or justify runtime/memory costs, nor describe practical tricks to make ternary attention efficient at training/inference time.
- Pairwise/ternary marginalization details are underspecified. For θV,Q(iq) = tanh(∑iv wiv C2iv,iq), are weights wiv shared across questions? If so, that ignores per-word variability; if not, how are they computed? Likewise for ternary wiv,ia.
- The decision-fusion discussion claims “allowing image features to interact with themselves,” yet the example monomial ((aV)i · (aQ)j · (aQ)k · (aA)t) repeats aQ, not aV. This raises concerns about correctness/clarity of the described architecture.

Clarity, notation, and formatting problems (numerous)
- Notational inconsistencies: vectors as rows vs columns; QWq ∈ Rnq×d yet using column indexing (QWq):,i while i is a word index (row). aA is mis-typed as aV. γ4 term uses θV,Q.A (period). smax undefined until later. Mixed use of T and >. Use of “Hereby,” nonstandard.
- Figures/captions: extraneous strings (“Pairwise Potentialconv conv Corr2…”), references to Fig. 1/2/5/7/8/Tab.1 not provided. This impedes understanding.
- Typos and grammar: “and and,” “importance of each feature…,” “the ones discussed in Sec. 3.3,” inconsistent capitalization.
- References: Many incomplete (missing venue/year/pages), some are arXiv only without timestamps; more recent/related bilinear/trilinear fusion works are under-cited relative to the claims.

Quality:
Methodologically plausible but underspecified and with misleading probabilistic framing. Experimental support for claimed SOTA is insufficient; key tables are missing; evaluation setup is unclear.

Clarity:
Currently below NeurIPS standards due to numerous notation errors, missing figures/tables, and ambiguous descriptions.

Originality:
The idea of high-order co-attention is a natural extension of existing bilinear/co-attention approaches; novelty is incremental. Without a principled inference treatment, the “probabilistic” framing is essentially a learned mixture of correlation-derived features.

Significance:
Potentially useful as a modular co-attention variant, but current empirical evidence is too limited to establish significance or robustness, and the double-blind violation precludes consideration.

Questions for the authors:
- Provide the full probabilistic model/energy and a correct mean-field derivation, or remove that claim and present the method as a learned linear fusion of potentials.
- Precisely define how θV,Q and θV,Q,A are computed: shapes, parameter sharing, how w⋅ are produced, and whether they depend on the current indices.
- Clarify the evaluation protocol: MC vs open-ended, how A is formed per example, and how training objectives differ across settings. Report standard VQA metrics on test-dev/test-std with per-type breakdowns and comprehensive baselines.
- Analyze computational/memory complexity and provide wall-clock and peak memory, including for C3.
- Provide ablations: unary only; +pairwise; +ternary; 2-modality vs 3-modality; MCB vs MCT; different embedding dims; with/without answer-as-input.
- Fix anonymization: remove acknowledgments and public URLs for the review phase.

Pros:
- Simple, extensible co-attention framework.
- Sensible use of compact bilinear/trilinear pooling.
- Qualitative examples suggest meaningful cross-modal correlations.

Cons:
- Double-blind violation.
- Overstated “mean-field” claim without derivation.
- Missing key results and ablations; unclear evaluation setup.
- Numerous clarity/notation/formatting issues and inconsistencies.

Recommendation: Reject (in current form). Confidence: High.
