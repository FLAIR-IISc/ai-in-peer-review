Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a general attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions among modalities (image V, question Q, and candidate answers A) via learnable “potentials.” Unary potentials score each modality independently; pairwise potentials are built from similarity matrices (projected inner products) and then marginalize over the complementary modality; ternary potentials extend this to a 3-way correlation tensor C3 and marginalize over the other two modalities. The final attention distributions for each modality are obtained by a learned linear combination of the corresponding unary, pairwise, and ternary “marginalized” potentials followed by a softmax (interpreted as one mean-field update). The attended feature vectors are fed to the decision module, which uses Multimodal Compact Bilinear or Trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT; tensor sketching) to efficiently combine outer-product features across modalities. The method is evaluated on VQA (multiple-choice format), claims state-of-the-art accuracy, and provides qualitative analyses; code is released.

This work builds on and aims to generalize co-attention/bilinear approaches for VQA (e.g., Lu et al. NIPS’16; Fukui et al. 2016) by (i) explicitly modeling high-order modality interactions at the attention level (not only in the decision stage) and (ii) providing a “probabilistic” perspective with learnable reliabilities for different potential orders. It is related to structured attention (Kim et al., 2017) and recent bilinear/low-rank pooling methods (Kim et al., ICLR’17), but claims easier extensibility to >2 modalities.

Strengths:
- Clear modular proposal that unifies unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions under one attention mechanism; conceptually generalizable to more modalities.
- Practical instantiation using simple projections, inner products, and 1×1 convolutions; integrates smoothly with strong decision-stage pooling (MCB/MCT).
- Empirical results on VQA multiple-choice with both quantitative and qualitative analyses; release of code improves reproducibility.
- Sensible insights in the write-up (e.g., different learned weights for image vs. question attention; qualitative examples showing question-dependent shifts).

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty relative to existing co-attention/bilinear pooling frameworks. Pairwise attention via similarity matrices and higher-order interactions via outer products/sketching are well explored; the main addition is the joint linear combination of potentials (with a “mean-field” interpretation) and inclusion of ternary potentials over answers.
- The probabilistic justification is underdeveloped. No explicit factor graph or energy is specified, and “mean-field” reduces to a single linear combination + softmax without iterative inference or an objective connecting potentials to likelihoods. As such, the approach reads more as a flexible parametric fusion than principled inference.
- Evaluation scope and fairness. The strongest results are on the multiple-choice setting (where using candidate answers as input gives an advantage); there are no results on the open-ended setting, and comparisons to prior work that do not use answer inputs are not apples-to-apples. The 2-modality baseline is handicapped with smaller embeddings (d=512 vs. 2048 used in prior work), and the paper acknowledges this, weakening comparative claims.
- Missing ablations. There is no systematic study of the contribution of unary vs. pairwise vs. ternary potentials, no analysis of the learned reliability weights (α,β,γ), no multiple mean-field iterations, and only a small MCT vs. 2-layer MCB comparison on val.
- Efficiency/complexity is not addressed. Constructing C3 scales with nq×nv×na×d; this is feasible in MC VQA (18 candidates) but the paper does not discuss memory/time, whether C3 is formed explicitly, or architectural tricks used to keep it tractable. Practicality for >3 modalities or larger candidate spaces is unclear.
- Clarity issues and typos: repeated variable names (aV used twice), occasional notation mismatches, missing details about the weights wiv, wiq, wiv,ia (shapes and how the “convolutions” are applied), and references with incomplete bibliographic info.

Quality:
- The method is technically sound as a neural architecture; however, the probabilistic framing and mean-field claim are not substantiated by a formal derivation or experiments (e.g., iterative updates).
- Experimental methodology is incomplete for a NeurIPS-level claim: limited ablations, reliance on MC setting, and potentially unfair baselines. Training details are provided; code availability is a plus.

Clarity:
- The high-level idea is understandable, but some notation and figure references are confusing. The paper would benefit from explicit tensor shapes, clear definitions of the convolution/marginalization operators, and a precise probabilistic formulation (if that is a key claim).

Originality:
- Moderate. The idea of combining unary/pairwise/ternary attentions is natural; similarity-based pairwise attention and high-order pooling are not new. The main novelty lies in assembling these components into a single learnable attention module with per-potential reliabilities and extending attention over candidate answers.

Significance:
- Potentially useful in settings with more than two modalities or when exploiting candidate answers is acceptable. However, given the limited and somewhat unfair evaluation and small empirical gains reported in the excerpt (e.g., 63.82 vs. 64.57 on val), the impact is uncertain.

Suggestions for improvement:
1) Provide a formal probabilistic model: define the joint over attentions with explicit energy terms, derive mean-field updates, and explore multiple iterations. Otherwise, present the method as a deterministic parametric combination, not as probabilistic inference.
2) Expand evaluation: include open-ended VQA (and/or VQA v2), and report comparisons that do not use answer inputs to ensure fairness. Add Visual7W or other benchmarks.
3) Ablations: turn off unary/pairwise/ternary components; report learned α,β,γ; compare one vs. multiple mean-field iterations; vary embedding dimensions to match prior baselines; explore region-level features (e.g., object proposals) for image attention.
4) Efficiency analysis: report memory and runtime; clarify whether C3 is computed explicitly; discuss scalability to more modalities or larger na.
5) Improve clarity/notation: fix typos, variable name collisions, and provide tensor shapes for potentials and convolution weights (e.g., wiv, wiq, wiv,ia).
6) Quantify qualitative claims: compare attention maps to human attention (e.g., Das et al., 2016) and measure grounding.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually simple, general framework for multi-modal attention.
- Integrates with effective high-order pooling at decision stage.
- Code release and qualitative analyses.
- Potentially competitive performance on VQA MC.

Cons for acceptance:
- Weak novelty vs. established co-/bi-/tri-linear attention approaches.
- Probabilistic/mean-field framing lacks rigor.
- Evaluation limited to MC, with fairness concerns and sparse ablations.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues in methodological details.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and likely useful, but the current version needs a stronger, fairer empirical case, a clearer and more rigorous formulation (or a reframing as an engineering contribution), and more thorough ablations and efficiency analysis.
