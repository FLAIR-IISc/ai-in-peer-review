Summary
The paper proposes a general, probabilistic formulation of multimodal attention that incorporates unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, and answer A), and computes attention distributions via a single mean-field–like update (linear combination of potentials followed by a softmax). Pairwise potentials are computed via bilinear correlations between projected features (similarity matrices), and ternary potentials via trilinear correlations (a 3D tensor). The method marginalizes these higher-order interactions to produce per-modality attention distributions. For decision making, the authors use Tensor Sketch–based pooling (MCB for pairwise, MCT for trilinear) to combine attended vectors and classify. They evaluate on VQA (multiple-choice) and report state-of-the-art accuracy with fewer parameters than prior work (e.g., MCB), provide ablations (MCT vs 2-layer MCB), and qualitative attention visualizations.

Relation to prior work
The framework connects to co-attention models (Lu et al. 2016), bilinear pooling and low-rank bilinear models (Fukui et al. 2016; Kim et al. 2017), stacked attention and memory networks (Yang et al. 2016; Xiong et al. 2016), and structured attention (Kim et al. 2017). The novelty is the explicit formulation of attention as a combination of learned unary/pairwise/ternary potentials with learnable mixture weights, and the use of trilinear correlations to jointly attend over three modalities including the candidate answers (as also explored in different form in Jabri et al. 2016). Using MCT for 3-way fusion is a direct, reasonable extension of MCB.

Strengths
- Clear, general framework for k-order multimodal attention, with an interpretable decomposition into unary/pairwise/ternary components and learned mixture weights that can reflect reliability of each source.
- Incorporates correlations between all three modalities (V, Q, A), which is particularly natural for multiple-choice VQA and shown empirically to help.
- Solid engineering: detailed embeddings, training recipe, and code release; ablations between MCT and a more expressive two-stage MCB; qualitative analyses of per-modality attentions and comparisons to baselines.
- Competitive to state-of-the-art with fewer parameters; training time and hyperparameters given.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Probabilistic/mean-field claim is under-justified. The “mean-field” update reduces to a learned linear combination of potentials plus softmax; no explicit energy or factor graph is presented, and no iterative inference is performed. This risks overselling the probabilistic aspect; the method is essentially a learned mixture of attention signals.
- Computational/memory complexity of ternary potentials is not addressed. The C3 tensor scales as O(nv nq na). For MC VQA with na ≈ 18, this is feasible; for open-ended VQA with thousands of answers, it is not. The current approach appears constrained to multiple-choice settings; this limitation should be made explicit and discussed.
- Empirical evaluation is limited. Key numbers (test-dev/test-std) are not shown here; comparisons appear to include MCB and co-attention, but stronger/more recent bilinear baselines (MLB/MFB; MUTAN) and VQA v2 are not reported. Ablations that isolate the contributions of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials (turning them on/off) are missing.
- The two-layer MCB “more expressive” variant is somewhat ad hoc (e.g., repeated self-interactions) and could use clearer motivation and analysis.
- Some clarity/typos: duplicated notation for aV and aA; occasional grammatical issues; figures referenced but not fully self-contained.

Quality
Technically sound as a deep learning architecture; the potential blocks are standard (1×1 convs/linear projections, bilinear/trilinear correlations), and the count-sketch–based fusion is appropriate. Claims of state-of-the-art performance and parameter efficiency are plausible, but would benefit from more comprehensive baselines and ablations. The probabilistic framing needs a more rigorous derivation or toned-down language.

Clarity
Generally readable with a coherent structure, but the notation is heavy and the “mean-field” description is terse. Some equations contain typos (e.g., repeated aV definition). A complexity discussion for C2/C3 and practical computation (especially for ternary potentials) would improve clarity.

Originality
Moderate. The decomposition into unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and inclusion of the answer modality for attention is a useful consolidation and extension of prior ideas. However, the core computational primitives (bilinear/trilinear correlations, MCB/MCT) are known, and the novelty is primarily in organizing them into a unified attention-as-potentials framework with learnable mixture weights.

Significance
Potentially useful for multimodal tasks where multiple candidate inputs exist (e.g., MC VQA). The empirical gains and parameter efficiency are promising, and the framework could inspire extensions to other modalities. However, significance is tempered by limited evaluation scope and the scalability restriction of ternary attention to multiple-choice settings.

Reproducibility
Good. Implementation details, hyperparameters, and a code link are provided. Additional ablations (e.g., removing each potential, varying mixture weights, complexity/runtime) would further aid reproducibility and understanding.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a concrete CRF/factor-graph formulation and derive the mean-field update, or rephrase the method as a learned mixture of attention potentials without probabilistic claims.
- Add complexity and memory analysis for pairwise and especially ternary potentials; explain how C3 is computed efficiently and discuss scalability beyond MC VQA.
- Expand experiments: open-ended VQA, VQA v2, Visual7W; compare against MLB/MFB/MUTAN and strong co-attention baselines under matched visual features (ResNet-152).
- Include ablations isolating unary/pairwise/ternary contributions and the learned mixture weights; report their learned values to support interpretability claims.
- Clarify and motivate the two-layer MCB variant; consider alternatives like gated or residual fusion.
- Fix notational typos and improve figure captions.

Decision
Recommendation: Weak accept (6). The paper presents a clear and fairly general high-order attention framework with solid engineering and promising results on MC VQA. With stronger empirical validation and a clearer treatment of the probabilistic claims and scalability, it would be a solid NeurIPS contribution.

Confidence: 0.7
