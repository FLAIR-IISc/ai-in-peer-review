Summary
This paper proposes a “high-order” attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, instantiated on VQA. The model defines unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, and in the multiple-choice setting, candidate answers A). Pairwise correlations are computed via bilinear forms (C2 = QWq(VWv)ᵀ), and ternary correlations via a trilinear tensor C3 with per-modality marginalizations. The authors then linearly combine unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and apply a softmax per modality, which they describe as a single mean-field iteration to produce attention distributions PV, PQ, and PA. The attended vectors are fed to decision layers based on tensor sketching (MCB for pairwise, MCT for ternary) to avoid explicit outer products. On VQA multiple-choice, the authors claim state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters than prior work, present qualitative attention maps, and argue that adding the third modality improves performance.

Strengths
- Clear high-level goal: unify attention across modalities with k-order terms, tractable via simple potential constructions.
- Practical: integrates with standard CNN/LSTM backbones and known pooling (MCB/MCT).
- Empirically, adding answer-aware ternary attention seems to help in the MC setting; some qualitative visualizations are sensible.
- The approach is general in spirit and could, in principle, extend beyond two modalities.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Methodological clarity and correctness:
- “Mean-field inference” claim is not substantiated. There is no explicit probabilistic model (energy, partition function) or derivation showing that Eq. (1) corresponds to a mean-field update. The current implementation is a linear combination of learned “potentials” followed by softmax; calling this mean-field is misleading without a formal CRF/factor-graph specification and update equations.
- Ternary tensor C3 feasibility is not addressed. Constructing and marginalizing a nq × nv × na tensor is prohibitive unless na is small. For MC VQA, na=18 per question may be manageable, but the paper alternately states using top-3000 answers as outputs; it is unclear whether A is an input modality (MC only) or an output (open-ended). Please clarify when ternary terms are used, their shapes per example, and the memory/time implications.
- Pairwise/ternary “convolutions” reduce to weighted sums with coefficients w_iv, w_iq, w_iv,ia, etc. It is unclear if these are shared kernels (as stated) or index-specific parameters (which would explode parameter count and break translational invariance). Precisely define these weights, their dimensions, sharing scheme, and parameter counts.
- Notation inconsistencies/bugs: aA is defined as aV in the last line of Eq. (attended modalities); “smax” is used but not defined; Wq/Wv are overloaded (Wq1, Wq2 vs Wq); Corr2/Corr3 figure labels leak into text; many equations have mismatched parentheses. These hinder reproducibility.
- The decision module: “2-layer MCB” is insufficiently specified (ordering, normalization, nonlinearity, residual/concat, whether sketches are fixed, etc.), yet it materially affects results.

Experimental evaluation:
- Claims of “state-of-the-art” are not supported. Table 1 is missing, and the paper reports only a single val number (63.82 vs 64.57) without full comparisons. There is no comprehensive comparison to strong contemporaneous baselines (e.g., MLB [13], MFB, MUTAN), ablations on each potential (unary vs pairwise vs ternary), or analysis across answer types.
- Only multiple-choice VQA appears evaluated; open-ended results (the default in many VQA papers) are not reported. This limits generality and comparability.
- Training protocol clarity: The text states training on “train+val” for test-dev/test-std results and also reports validation results when training on “train,” but precise splits per table are not included. Fairness against baselines is unclear. Preprocessing choices (tokenization, vocabulary size for Q, handling OOV, use of pre-trained embeddings) are not sufficiently specified.
- No analysis of computational cost (time/memory) per modality/potential, especially for ternary attention, nor of the effect of nv/nq/na or embedding d.

Writing/formatting:
- Numerous typos and formatting issues: “and and,” “st Conference…,” figure captions inserted into text, incomplete references (venues/years missing/inconsistent), and missing tables/figures referenced in the text. These detract from clarity and raise concerns about thoroughness.

Quality
Conceptually, combining unary/pairwise/ternary interactions is reasonable. However, the probabilistic framing is not technically justified; scalability and parameterization of pairwise/ternary reductions are underspecified; and the empirical section lacks the rigor and breadth needed to support the claimed contributions. The paper is not reproducible as written.

Clarity
High-level ideas are understandable, but critical details (derivations, tensor shapes, parameter counts, exact architectures) and key results are missing or inconsistent. Significant editing is needed.

Originality
Modeling higher-order multimodal interactions for attention is a reasonable extension of existing bilinear pooling/co-attention. The paper’s main novelty is the attempt to define attention “potentials” at multiple orders and combine them, plus using MCT. However, without solid derivation or stronger empirical evidence, the novelty feels incremental relative to prior bilinear/trilinear fusion and co-attention works.

Significance
Potentially useful if rigorously formulated and validated. As it stands, due to reporting gaps and methodological ambiguities, it is hard to assess impact. The community is unlikely to adopt this as-is.

Questions/requests for authors
1) Provide the explicit probabilistic model (energy with unary/pairwise/ternary factors) and derive the mean-field updates that lead to Eq. (1). What independence assumptions are made?
2) Precisely define the “convolutions” used to marginalize C2/C3: kernel sizes, sharing, parameter counts. Are weights tied across positions?
3) Clarify when A is an input modality (MC) vs an output (open-ended). Report open-ended results. If ternary attention is only for MC, state this prominently.
4) Provide full quantitative results (test-dev/test-std), with ablations: unary-only, +pairwise, +ternary; and comparisons to MLB/MFB/MUTAN/MCB baselines under matched backbones (VGG/ResNet).
5) Report runtime/memory and how C3 is computed without materializing the full tensor (if applicable).
6) Fix notation/typos and complete references.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear motivation to model higher-order interactions.
- Simple, potentially general mechanism; qualitative examples are promising.
- Incorporates known efficient pooling (MCB/MCT).

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficiently justified “mean-field” formulation.
- Ambiguity and likely inefficiency in ternary attention; unclear parameterization.
- Missing key results/ablations; weak/limited comparisons.
- Numerous clarity/formatting issues; some incorrect equations.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is interesting, but the paper in its current form has substantial methodological ambiguities, missing or incomplete quantitative evidence, and many clarity/formatting problems. With a rigorous derivation, clearer definitions, comprehensive experiments (including open-ended VQA), and polished presentation, a future version could be competitive.
