## NIPS 2017 Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to attention mechanisms, extending them to higher-order correlations between multiple modalities. The proposed method demonstrates strong performance on the VQA task, achieving state-of-the-art results. While the paper is clearly written and the ideas are generally sound, the presentation of the method feels somewhat dense, and the empirical evaluation, while strong, could benefit from more ablation studies and a deeper analysis of the learned attention patterns. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the limitations of existing attention mechanisms in multi-modal tasks like VQA – specifically, their often ad-hoc and task-specific nature and their inability to explicitly model interactions between modalities. The authors propose a high-order attention model that learns correlations between *k* modalities, using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to represent these interactions. The model leverages mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities, enabling it to scale to an arbitrary number of modalities. 

The paper positions its work within a rich landscape of attention mechanisms for both image and text processing, thoroughly reviewing related work.  It appropriately highlights how their approach differs from previous methods like those of Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25], which either combine modalities directly before attention or focus on pairwise interactions. The comparison to Kim et al. [14] and the bilinear models [13] is also relevant, emphasizing the proposed method's ease of extension to multiple modalities.  The authors do a good job of situating their work within the context of existing VQA approaches and attention literature.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of modeling high-order correlations between modalities via potentials and mean-field inference is genuinely novel and addresses a key limitation of existing attention mechanisms.  This offers a more principled and generalizable approach.
* **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, which is a compelling validation of the approach.
* **Generality:** The proposed framework is designed to be applicable to a wide range of multi-modal tasks beyond VQA, a significant advantage. The authors convincingly argue the scalability to more than three modalities.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and motivates the need for a more sophisticated attention mechanism.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The review of related work is comprehensive and well-organized.
* **Detailed Explanation:** The paper dives into the mathematical formulation of the attention mechanism, providing a detailed explanation of the potentials, mean-field inference, and tensor sketching.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Density:** The presentation of the method is quite dense. While mathematically rigorous, it can be challenging to grasp the core ideas without careful reading.  A more intuitive explanation of the mean-field inference process would be helpful.  Visualizations beyond Fig. 2 would aid understanding.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares to other SOTA methods, a more detailed ablation study of the individual components (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) would strengthen the results.  For instance, how much performance gain is achieved by adding the ternary potential compared to only using pairwise potentials?
* **Qualitative Analysis Could Be Expanded:** The qualitative examples (Figs. 5, 6, 7, 8) are helpful, but could be expanded to provide more insight into the learned attention patterns.  Analyzing failure cases more systematically (beyond the single example) would also be beneficial. The observation in Fig. 8, where the attention remains constant across different questions, is concerning and deserves further investigation.
* **MCB/MCT Choice:** The improvement with the 2-layer MCB over MCT, while reported, lacks a detailed justification.  Why does the more expressive model outperform the theoretically more general one?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimension *d*, learning rate, dropout rates) would be valuable.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates a novel and promising approach to attention mechanisms.
* Achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark task.
* Offers a generalizable framework applicable to multiple modalities.
* Thoroughly reviews related work.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The presentation is complex and can be difficult to follow.
* The empirical evaluation could benefit from more ablation studies and a deeper analysis of the learned attention.
* The qualitative analysis is somewhat limited.

**5. Recommendation:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should:

* **Improve Clarity:** Revise the explanation of the mean-field inference process to make it more intuitive. Consider adding more diagrams to illustrate the key concepts.
* **Conduct Ablation Studies:** Include ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) to the overall performance.
* **Expand Qualitative Analysis:** Provide more in-depth analysis of the learned attention patterns, including a systematic examination of failure cases.
* **Justify MCB Choice:**  Provide a clearer rationale for the superior performance of the 2-layer MCB unit.




Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant impact on the field of multi-modal learning. Addressing the minor weaknesses identified above will further enhance its quality and clarity.
