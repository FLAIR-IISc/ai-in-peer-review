

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that extends beyond pairwise attention to model high-order correlations between image, question, and answer modalities. The authors formalize attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to compute attention weights. They demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on VQA datasets, claiming improvements over prior work by Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016).

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-structured approach with clear mathematical formulations. The extension from pairwise to high-order attention is theoretically grounded, and the implementation details (including the use of tensor sketch for efficient computation) are thorough.

2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA benchmarks, with a 64.57% accuracy on the test-std set (compared to 63.82% for MCT). The quantitative results are convincing, and the ablation studies (comparing 2-modality vs. 3-modality models) demonstrate the importance of high-order attention.

3. **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written with clear figures illustrating the unary, pairwise, and ternary attention modules. The mathematical formulations are accessible, and the architecture is logically presented.

4. **Conceptual Contribution:** The idea of modeling higher-order correlations in attention mechanisms is novel and potentially valuable for multimodal tasks beyond VQA. The authors effectively demonstrate that high-order attention can capture more nuanced relationships between modalities.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** While the paper claims higher-order attention improves performance, it doesn't sufficiently explain *why* this is the case. The ablation studies show improved results with higher-order terms, but the paper lacks deeper analysis of how these terms contribute to the model's decision-making process.

2. **Inadequate Comparison to Recent Work:** The paper compares primarily to Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016), but doesn't benchmark against more recent VQA approaches (e.g., from the 2017 VQA Challenge or papers published after the cited works). This weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance.

3. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials). For example, it doesn't show how much each potential contributes to the final performance, nor does it analyze the optimal combination of these potentials.

4. **Vague Mean-Field Inference Explanation:** The paper mentions "mean-field inference" but doesn't clearly explain how this is implemented in their specific case. This is a significant gap given the paper's probabilistic framing.

5. **Limited Generalization Analysis:** The paper focuses solely on VQA, without exploring whether the high-order attention mechanism could benefit other multimodal tasks. The broader significance of the approach is not fully demonstrated.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a technically sound contribution with strong empirical results, but its claims are somewhat overstated given the lack of comprehensive analysis and comparison to the most recent work. While the core idea of high-order attention is interesting, the paper doesn't sufficiently establish why it's fundamentally better than existing pairwise attention approaches.

**Constructive Suggestions:**
1. Conduct a more thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of each attention component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials).

2. Compare against more recent VQA approaches (e.g., those published in 2017) to better establish the state-of-the-art claim.

3. Provide a clearer explanation of the mean-field inference process and its relationship to the attention mechanism.

4. Include more analysis on *why* high-order attention works better, perhaps by examining failure cases or analyzing attention patterns across different question types.

5. Explore the generalizability of the approach to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA to strengthen the significance claim.

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel extension of attention mechanisms to high-order correlations
- Strong empirical results on VQA benchmarks
- Well-structured technical presentation
- Clear visualization of attention patterns

*Con:*
- Insufficient theoretical justification for high-order attention
- Inadequate comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods
- Lack of comprehensive ablation studies
- Vague explanation of mean-field inference
- Limited demonstration of broader applicability

**Overall:** While the paper presents a technically sound approach with promising results, it falls short in fully establishing the novelty and significance of high-order attention. The contributions are incremental rather than transformative, and the claims of state-of-the-art performance would be more convincing with better comparisons to recent work. I recommend rejection, but with the hope that the authors can address these issues in a future revision. The core idea has merit, but the current paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate its value or provide a compelling theoretical foundation for the approach.
