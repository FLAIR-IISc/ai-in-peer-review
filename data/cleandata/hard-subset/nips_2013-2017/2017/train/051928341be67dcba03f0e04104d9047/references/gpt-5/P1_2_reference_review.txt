Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a general multimodal attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” across modalities and combines them to produce per-modality attention distributions via a softmax. The core idea is to capture higher-order correlations among image regions (V), question words (Q), and candidate answers (A) in VQA. Concretely: (i) unary potentials are obtained by 1×1 convolutions (linear projections + tanh) over each modality; (ii) pairwise potentials are based on a similarity matrix C2 = QWq(VWv)^T and are “marginalized” along one dimension via learned 1×1 convs to produce per-modality scores; (iii) ternary potentials use a third-order correlation tensor C3 across (Q, V, A) with corr3(q,v,a) = Σl qlvlal, again reduced via learned weights. The final attention for each modality is a learned linear combination of its unary, pairwise and ternary terms followed by softmax. The attended vectors are fused for decision making using tensor sketching: MCB for pairwise or MCT (trilinear) for three-way fusion.

This work sits in the VQA lineage of attention/co-attention approaches (e.g., hierarchical co-attention by Lu et al. NIPS’16; stacked attention; dynamic memory networks; bilinear pooling/MCB; dual attention). The novelty here is explicitly formulating and learning higher-order interactions in the attention computation itself (not only in final fusion) and claiming a probabilistic mean-field motivation. The paper reports state-of-the-art multiple-choice VQA results with fewer parameters than some prior MCB-based models, plus qualitative visualizations.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: a unified framework that includes unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions for attention, extensible in principle to k modalities.
- Practical implementation that leverages efficient bilinear/trilinear sketching for decision fusion (MCB/MCT).
- Empirical gains on VQA (multiple-choice) with fewer parameters than some prior SOTA; qualitative attention maps suggest question-dependent focusing.
- Simple modules (1×1 convs, inner products) and end-to-end trainability; reasonable engineering choices (ResNet/VGG features, LSTM-based question encoding) and provided training details.
- The inclusion of answer candidates as an input modality for attention is interesting and distinguishes it from standard co-attention.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Probabilistic/mean-field claim is not rigorously derived. The “mean-field” update (linear combination of potentials + softmax) does not appear to be the expectation of clique potentials under factorized marginals, nor does it incorporate current marginal distributions in the messages. Pairwise/ternary reductions use learned weights rather than expectations under attention distributions. Please either provide a proper graphical model and mean-field derivation (with assumptions) or tone down the probabilistic claims and present this as a learned mixture-of-potentials.
- Computational complexity of ternary attention is unclear. For na=3000 answers, nq≈20, nv=196, computing C3 with corr3 over d=512 naively entails ~nq*nv*na*d ≈ billions of MACs per example and a large memory footprint, which seems incompatible with batch size 300. How is C3 computed efficiently? Are you subsampling answers, factorizing weights, using low-rank tricks, or sharing computations across the fixed answer set? Provide complexity analysis, memory usage, wall-clock times, and ablations on na.
- Limited evaluation: only VQA v1 multiple-choice is reported in the excerpt. Open-ended accuracy (the more common setting) and transfer to other datasets (VQA v2, Visual7W) are absent. Without broader evaluation, it is hard to assess generality beyond the multiple-choice setup where answers are given as inputs.
- Ablations are insufficient. While you show 2-modality vs 3-modality and MCT vs 2-layer MCB for the final fusion, there is no systematic study of: (i) unary-only vs +pairwise vs +ternary in the attention; (ii) effect of the learned combination weights α,β,γ; (iii) impact of using answer as an attention modality; (iv) robustness to feature dimension d.
- Comparison baselines: The paper cites hierarchical co-attention (Lu et al.), MCB (Fukui et al.), dual attention, bilinear pooling, etc., but the numerical comparisons are only briefly mentioned and Table 1 is not visible in the submission excerpt. It would be important to compare to the strongest co-attention and bilinear pooling baselines under matched feature backbones and training protocol, including recent strong baselines (e.g., Kazemi & Elqursh).
- Clarity issues: Some notation glitches (e.g., aV repeated where aA is intended), figure captions are terse, and “convolution along a dimension” for reducing C2/C3 could be more formally described (is this a learned 1D conv or simple weighted sum?). The role of the bias terms α5,β5,γ5 and normalization is not discussed.
- The claim “state-of-the-art” should be supported with a complete, up-to-date comparison and statistical significance if possible.

Quality
Technically sound at the module level, but the probabilistic framing is not substantiated by a derivation; the higher-order attention computation may be computationally heavy without a clear efficiency argument. Empirical support is promising but limited in scope and ablations.

Clarity
Generally readable, but sections 3.2.2–3.2.3 would benefit from precise definitions of the “marginalization” operations, complexity discussion, and correction of typos.

Originality
Moderately novel. Pairwise correlations for attention and bilinear pooling are well-studied; the main novelty is folding ternary interactions into attention and unifying them via a learned mixture. The mean-field framing (if made precise) could add conceptual value.

Significance
Potentially useful if the higher-order attention demonstrably and efficiently improves across datasets and settings. As presented, significance is limited by evaluation breadth and missing complexity analysis.

Questions for the authors
- How do you compute C3 in practice for na=3000 and batch size 300? Please provide algorithmic details, memory estimates, and runtime.
- Can you provide a proper mean-field derivation or clarify that your method is a learned mixture rather than inference in a specified graphical model?
- What are the learned weights α,β,γ across datasets? Do they indicate when higher-order terms matter?
- Do results hold on VQA v2 and in open-ended evaluation?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comprehensive ablations on attention orders and on including/excluding the answer modality.
- Provide complexity/runtime analysis and, if applicable, an efficient low-rank/Sketch-based approximation for C3 analogous to MCT.
- Expand experiments to VQA v2 and open-ended results; include matched baselines.
- Clarify notation and “marginalization” operations; fix typos; improve figure readability.
- Report confidence intervals or multiple runs.

Minor comments
- Correct aV = Σia PA(ia)aia to aA = … in Section 3.2.
- The GitHub link is appreciated; ensure code reproduces reported numbers.

Pros
- Conceptually clear extension to higher-order attention with promising results.
- Simple, modular, end-to-end trainable; qualitative attention maps are sensible.
- Uses efficient sketching for decision fusion.

Cons
- Probabilistic justification is weak/incomplete.
- Potentially expensive ternary attention without clear efficiency solution.
- Limited evaluation and ablations; unclear SOTA comparison.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea of higher-order attention is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current submission needs a rigorous derivation (or re-framing), clear computational treatment of ternary attention, and broader, stronger empirical validation to meet the NeurIPS bar. If these issues are addressed, the work could be a solid contribution.
