

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors propose a probabilistic framework where attention is computed through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials that capture correlations across modalities, rather than the typical pairwise or single-modality attention used in prior work. The approach is grounded in mean-field inference over high-order potentials, allowing the model to learn interactions between any number of modalities.

The paper makes a clear and compelling case for the novelty of their approach. They carefully distinguish their work from prior attention mechanisms, particularly noting that existing methods like Lu et al. (2016) and Xu et al. (2015) focus on pairwise attention, while Fukui et al. (2016) use bilinear pooling. The authors convincingly argue that high-order attention represents a significant conceptual shift from current practices, which typically treat modalities as interacting in limited ways. Their framework is theoretically grounded in probabilistic modeling and offers a natural extension to any number of modalities.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA benchmark with a relatively simple model (40 million parameters). The quantitative results in Table 1 are impressive, particularly given that their model uses a lower embedding dimension (512) compared to prior work (2048). The qualitative analysis in Figures 5-7 effectively shows how the high-order attention mechanism produces more meaningful attention maps than existing approaches, with the ternary attention specifically capturing interactions between image, question, and answer modalities.

The paper's strengths are clear: the core idea of high-order attention is genuinely novel and represents a bold conceptual advancement in attention mechanisms. The framework is well-motivated, technically sound, and effectively demonstrated on a challenging task. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the writing is clear and well-organized.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. First, the authors do not sufficiently differentiate their work from Kim et al. (2017), who "presented a technique that also interprets attention as a multi-variate probabilistic model" (as cited in their related work). The paper needs to clarify how their approach differs from this prior work. Second, the computational complexity of the ternary potential calculation (which requires summing over all combinations of image patches, question words, and answer options) is not adequately addressed, raising questions about scalability. Third, the paper lacks detailed ablation studies to quantify how much each component (unary, pairwise, ternary) contributes to the final performance.

Despite these limitations, the paper's contribution is significant. The high-order attention concept represents a new direction in attention mechanisms that could influence future work across multimodal tasks. The authors have successfully demonstrated the value of this approach through strong empirical results and clear theoretical motivation.

The paper aligns well with NeurIPS's focus on novel and impactful contributions. While the methodology isn't perfect, the novelty of the core concept outweighs the minor methodological shortcomings. The paper doesn't merely incrementally improve existing approaches but instead introduces a fundamentally different way of thinking about attention mechanisms.

This work has the potential to inspire a new line of research on high-order attention mechanisms beyond VQA. The conceptual shift from pairwise to high-order attention is bold and significant, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates clear significance for the field.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Pro-acceptance arguments**:
- Novel conceptual framework for attention mechanisms that models high-order correlations between modalities
- Clear differentiation from prior work, particularly regarding the extension to multiple modalities
- State-of-the-art performance on VQA with a relatively simple model
- Strong theoretical grounding in probabilistic modeling
- Potential to inspire future research beyond VQA

**Con-acceptance arguments**:
- Limited differentiation from Kim et al. (2017) who also proposed a multi-variate probabilistic model
- Insufficient discussion of computational complexity of ternary potentials
- Lack of detailed ablation studies to quantify contribution of each component
- The paper could better clarify how the approach generalizes beyond VQA

The paper's novelty and conceptual contribution are substantial enough to warrant acceptance despite the minor methodological limitations. This represents a significant step forward in understanding how attention mechanisms can model complex interactions between multiple data modalities.
