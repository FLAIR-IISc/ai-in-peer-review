

**Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"**

**Summary of Main Ideas:**
The paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism that models correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., visual, textual, and answer modalities) in a unified probabilistic framework. The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms for visual question answering (VQA) are limited to pairwise interactions between two modalities, while their approach can model higher-order correlations (e.g., ternary correlations between image, question, and answer). They formalize this as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, which they use to compute attention probabilities through mean-field inference. The authors apply this approach to VQA, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

The paper relates to previous work by citing attention mechanisms for VQA (Lu et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2016), multimodal pooling approaches (Fukui et al., 2016; Kim et al., 2017), and structured attention networks (Kim et al., 2017). The authors position their work as extending these approaches to handle higher-order interactions between modalities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-structured technical approach with clear mathematical formulations for the high-order attention mechanism. The authors provide detailed descriptions of how the attention potentials are computed, and their experimental evaluation on VQA is thorough, comparing to strong baselines (Lu et al., 2016; Fukui et al., 2016). The quantitative results show state-of-the-art performance on both test-dev and test-std datasets, and the qualitative analysis provides valuable insights into the attention mechanism.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear explanations of the proposed method. Figures 3 and 4 effectively illustrate the unary, pairwise, and ternary attention modules. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results, including implementation specifics (e.g., embedding dimensions, optimization parameters).

3. **Originality:** The high-order attention framework represents a novel approach to modeling multimodal interactions, extending beyond the pairwise interactions common in existing attention mechanisms. The authors convincingly argue that their approach can be trivially extended to any number of modalities, which is a significant advantage over previous work.

4. **Significance:** The approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA, demonstrating its practical utility. The ability to model higher-order correlations between modalities could be valuable for other multimodal tasks beyond VQA, and the attention visualizations provide interpretability benefits.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** The paper compares to Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016), but doesn't provide detailed comparison to the exact implementations of these methods. The claim that "our model has only 40 million parameters, while techniques like [Fukui et al.] use over 70 million parameters" lacks clarity on whether this parameter difference is due to the high-order attention mechanism or other architectural choices.

2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) to the overall performance. It's unclear how much of the improvement is due to the high-order attention mechanism versus other changes in the model architecture.

3. **Overstated Novelty:** The paper claims that "attention mechanism with several potentials haven't been discussed in detail yet," but Kim et al. (2017) on bilinear models and Nam et al. (2016) on dual attention mechanisms have explored similar concepts. The paper should more clearly differentiate its approach from these existing works.

4. **Failure Case Analysis:** While the paper includes a failure case (Figure 8), it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why the model failed or how the high-order attention mechanism contributed to this failure.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound approach to modeling high-order attention in multimodal tasks, with clear experimental validation showing state-of-the-art performance on VQA. The high-order attention framework is a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal representation learning. While the novelty is somewhat limited compared to recent work on bilinear models, the probabilistic formulation and empirical results make it a significant advancement.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Clear technical contribution to multimodal attention mechanisms
- State-of-the-art results on VQA benchmark
- Well-structured and clearly written paper
- Potential applicability to other multimodal tasks

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Limited novelty compared to recent bilinear model work
- Lack of ablation studies to isolate the contribution of high-order attention
- Insufficient comparison to baseline methods with identical implementation details

**Recommendation:**
The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal attention mechanisms. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the technical soundness, clarity, and strong empirical results make it suitable for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing more detailed comparisons to baselines and conducting ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the high-order attention mechanism.
