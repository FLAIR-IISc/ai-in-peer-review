This paper proposes a “high-order attention” mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to capture correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer). While the motivation for moving beyond pairwise attention is reasonable, and the potential for modeling higher-order interactions is intriguing, the paper falls short of convincing me of its necessity or substantial benefit given the presentation.

The central idea – representing attention as a probabilistic model based on potentials and utilizing mean-field inference – isn’t entirely novel; connections to graphical models and related probabilistic approaches (e.g., Kim et al. [14]) are acknowledged but not deeply explored in terms of advantages or disadvantages. The specific implementation, involving convolutions and pairwise/ternary potentials, feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a strong theoretical justification. Why these specific convolution operations and potential formulations? The paper states the technique is “easy to extend to any number of modalities,” but doesn’t address the computational complexity implications of doing so, which are likely significant, and only demonstrates results with three modalities. 

The empirical evaluation, while claiming state-of-the-art performance, is presented with insufficient detail. The improvement over baselines (Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) is marginal, and the fact that the model requires a substantial amount of training (40 hours) for a modest gain raises concerns. Crucially, the comparison isn’t entirely fair; the authors acknowledge using a smaller embedding dimension (512) compared to the 2048 used in some baseline implementations, which inherently disadvantages their model. A more thorough ablation study, isolating the impact of the higher-order attention components, is needed. Simply stating that the 3-modality model performs better than the 2-modality model doesn’t demonstrate the *value* of the ternary potentials – it could be due to other factors. The qualitative results, while visually appealing, are not particularly compelling. They show the attention focusing on reasonably relevant image regions, but don't offer a clear advantage over existing attention mechanisms. The failure case further underscores a potential weakness in the model’s ability to focus on the correct elements when faced with ambiguous questions.

The related work section is extensive, but often descriptive rather than critical. The authors highlight differences from existing methods, but don’t always convincingly explain *why* their approach is superior. The constant comparison to Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7] feels somewhat limited; a broader exploration of other attention mechanisms (e.g., transformers) would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

*   The motivation for exploring higher-order interactions in multimodal data is valid.
*   The idea of framing attention as a probabilistic model is potentially interesting.
*   The paper presents a complete system for VQA.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The proposed approach lacks a strong theoretical foundation. The design choices feel ad-hoc.
*   The empirical evaluation is insufficient to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the high-order attention mechanism. The reported performance gain is marginal, and the comparison to baselines isn't entirely fair.
*   The computational complexity of extending the model to a large number of modalities is not addressed.
*   The qualitative results are not particularly insightful.
*   The related work section is descriptive but lacks critical analysis.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel approach to attention mechanisms.
*   The results, while modest, are state-of-the-art.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification is weak.
*   The empirical evaluation is insufficient.
*   The practical benefits of the proposed approach are not convincingly demonstrated.
*   The computational cost is potentially high.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper presents an interesting idea, but it needs significantly more theoretical grounding, a more thorough empirical evaluation, and a clearer demonstration of its advantages over existing methods before it would be suitable for NIPS. The authors need to address the concerns regarding computational complexity and provide a more compelling justification for the specific design choices made in their model. A more rigorous ablation study and a fairer comparison to state-of-the-art baselines are essential.
