Summary
This paper proposes a general, probabilistic high-order attention framework for multimodal learning, instantiated on VQA. The core idea is to model attention as a set of learnable potentials over modalities: unary (per-modality), pairwise (across two modalities), and ternary (across three modalities: image, question, and candidate answers). A single mean-field iteration (implemented as a linear combination of marginalized potentials followed by softmax) yields marginal attention distributions over each modality. The attended vectors are then fused for decision making via tensor sketching: MCB for pairwise or a new MCT extension for trilinear pooling, enabling high-order outer-product interactions at tractable cost. On VQA (multiple-choice), the method reports state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters than some baselines, and qualitative visualizations suggest reasonable cross-modal attention behavior.

Relation to prior work
Compared to hierarchical co-attention and bilinear pooling approaches (e.g., Lu et al., Fukui et al.), this work explicitly formulates a unified k-th order attention mechanism with potentials and mean-field inference, and extends compact bilinear pooling to trilinear (MCT). It is related to structured/graphical attention (e.g., Structured Attention Networks), but targets multimodal interactions with explicit pairwise and ternary potentials, and integrates answer choices as a third modality (cf. Jabri et al., who question attention via alternative formulations). The claimed extension to any number of modalities is conceptually straightforward under this framework.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: A clean, general formulation of high-order attention with potentials and mean-field inference, plus an MCT fusion unit. This offers a principled bridge between probabilistic modeling and deep attention mechanisms.
- Practical impact: Demonstrates that incorporating answer candidates as an explicit modality via ternary attention improves VQA MC performance. The approach attains SOTA with fewer parameters.
- Generality: The k-order potential design, with simple marginalization and learned combination weights, is modular and ostensibly extensible beyond VQA.
- Empirical support: Quantitative gains over pairwise baselines and qualitative analyses showing question-dependent visual shifts.

Weaknesses and questions
- Computational clarity for ternary potentials: The paper defines the full C3 tensor but does not analyze its memory/time cost or implementation details. This is crucial: nq×nv×na can be large. Is na restricted to the 18 multiple-choice answers per image at training/inference? How are the conv weights w indexed and shared? Are computations batched and/or approximated?
- Limited evaluation scope: Results are reported only on VQA multiple-choice. No results on open-ended VQA, VQA 2.0, or other multimodal datasets (e.g., Visual7W), which would test generality. No analysis of robustness to distractor answers or domain shifts.
- Ablations: While a 2- vs 3-modality comparison is given, there is no systematic ablation of the contribution of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials, or of the learned mixing weights α, β, γ. Also, mean-field iterations are limited to one; it would be informative to show whether additional iterations help or hurt.
- Decision fusion: MCT is introduced but then a 2-layer MCB is empirically better. This raises questions about when high-order fusion helps in practice; deeper analysis would strengthen the case for the trilinear unit.
- Clarity/typos: Some notation is confusing or inconsistent (e.g., aV appearing twice in Eq. for attended vectors; undefined or ambiguous w indices; occasional grammatical issues). The exact architecture sizes and truncation lengths (nq) are under-specified. The promised quantitative Table 1 is not included in the text provided.
- Comparison breadth: Beyond Lu and Fukui, comparisons to other bilinear/low-rank pooling (MLB, MFB, MUTAN) and dual/stacked attention models are limited, though some are cited.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; the “mean-field” step is effectively a learned linear combination of marginalized potentials plus softmax, which is simple and pragmatic. However, computational and implementation details for ternary correlations are missing, and empirical evaluation breadth is limited. The paper would benefit from stronger ablations and broader baselines.

Clarity
Readable and generally well-organized, with helpful figures and qualitative examples. Nonetheless, several notational ambiguities and typos hinder reproducibility. More explicit descriptions of tensor sizes, memory considerations, and training details for C3 are needed.

Originality
Good. The explicit k-order potential view of attention and its application to tri-modal attention with MCT is a meaningful and novel contribution at the time frame suggested by the citations.

Significance
Moderate to high. The framework could influence future multimodal attention designs, and the SOTA result with fewer parameters is practically valuable. If computational issues around high-order potentials are clarified and generalization shown across tasks, impact would be strong.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a computational analysis of C3 (complexity, memory), describe practical approximations (e.g., limiting na, low-rank projections before correlation), and include wall-clock/runtime comparisons.
- Add ablations: remove ternary, remove specific pairwise terms, vary α/β/γ, and experiment with >1 mean-field iteration.
- Broaden evaluation to open-ended VQA and at least one additional dataset; report exact test-dev/test-std numbers and standard baselines (MLB/MFB/MUTAN, dual attention).
- Clarify notation and fix typos; specify nq, how padding/truncation affects C2/C3, and details of the w parameters.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). The paper introduces a fresh and general high-order attention perspective with promising empirical results and a new trilinear pooling unit. Despite some missing details and limited evaluations, the conceptual novelty and potential to open new directions justify acceptance.

Confidence
3/5.
