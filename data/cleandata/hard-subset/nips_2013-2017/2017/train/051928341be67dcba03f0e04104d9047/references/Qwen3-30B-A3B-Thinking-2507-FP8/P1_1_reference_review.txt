

# Peer Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism called "High-Order Attention Models" that learns high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., image, question, answer) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms in VQA primarily focus on pairwise interactions between modalities and lack explicit modeling of higher-order correlations. Their approach models attention using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, which are combined through mean-field inference to produce attention probabilities. The paper demonstrates that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset while using fewer parameters than competing approaches.

The work builds upon existing attention mechanisms for VQA (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) by introducing a probabilistic framework that can naturally extend to any number of modalities, rather than being limited to two modalities as in most prior work. The authors provide a detailed mathematical formulation of their approach, including how unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials are computed, and demonstrate their effectiveness through experiments on the VQA dataset.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The proposed high-order attention mechanism is logically developed from the limitations of existing approaches, and the mathematical formulation is clear. The experimental evaluation on the VQA dataset is thorough, with quantitative results showing state-of-the-art performance compared to several baselines. The authors provide a reasonable explanation for why their 3-modality model outperforms a 2-modality model.

However, the paper has some limitations in its evaluation:
- There is a lack of ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials). It's unclear how much each component contributes to the overall performance.
- The comparison with recent methods is limited. The paper doesn't compare with the influential "Bottom-Up and Top-Down" attention model by Anderson et al. (2017), which was published at CVPR and has become a strong baseline in VQA.
- The choice of using only the top 3000 most frequent answers (covering 91% of answers in the training set) is not well justified. This could potentially bias the results toward common answers and might not reflect the full challenge of the VQA task.

The paper is relatively complete as a technical contribution, but the authors could have provided more thorough analysis of the model's limitations.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the proposed method. The figures (particularly Figures 3-8) are excellent visual aids that help illustrate the attention mechanisms. The mathematical notation is consistent and clearly defined.

However, there are some areas where clarity could be improved:
- The explanation of the ternary potential computation in Section 3.2.3 is somewhat confusing. The authors could clarify how the C3 tensor is used to obtain the attention for each modality.
- Some implementation details are missing, such as the exact architecture of the LSTM layers used for question embedding and the specific hyperparameters used for the MCB/MCT units.
- The paper mentions that "the 2-modality model achieves inferior performance" compared to their 3-modality model, but doesn't provide sufficient detail on why this is the case or how the 2-modality model was implemented.

## Originality

The core idea of modeling high-order attention correlations is novel compared to most existing VQA approaches, which primarily focus on pairwise interactions. The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior art, particularly noting that their model can be easily extended to any number of modalities, unlike approaches like Lu et al. [15] that are limited to two modalities.

The paper adequately references related work, though it misses some key recent contributions. For instance, the influential "Bottom-Up and Top-Down" attention model by Anderson et al. (2017) is not discussed, which is a significant omission given its impact on the VQA field. The authors also don't compare with the "Multimodal Compact Bilinear Pooling" extension by Fukui et al. (2016), which their own work builds upon.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important limitation in current attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field. The state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset demonstrate the practical value of their approach. The probabilistic framework they propose could potentially be applied to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA, which adds to the significance of their work.

The paper makes a demonstrable advancement in the state of the art for VQA, achieving better results with fewer parameters than competing approaches. The authors also provide qualitative evidence showing that their attention mechanism focuses on relevant regions of the image and question words, which is valuable for interpretability.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths:
1. Clear motivation for modeling high-order attention correlations, which addresses a real limitation in existing VQA approaches.
2. Well-structured paper with good mathematical formulation and clear explanations.
3. State-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset with a relatively small model (40 million parameters).
4. Excellent visualizations of attention mechanisms (Figures 5-8) that provide intuitive understanding.
5. Probabilistic framework that can be extended to any number of modalities.

### Weaknesses:
1. Lack of ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each attention component (unary, pairwise, ternary).
2. Missing comparison with influential recent methods like Anderson et al. (2017) "Bottom-Up and Top-Down" attention.
3. Insufficient justification for using only the top 3000 most frequent answers.
4. Limited explanation of why their 3-modality model outperforms a 2-modality model.
5. Some unclear explanations, particularly in the ternary potential computation section.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper with minor revisions. The paper makes a genuine contribution to the field by introducing a novel high-order attention mechanism that achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA. While the paper has some weaknesses in its evaluation and comparison, these are relatively minor and can be addressed with a few additional experiments and clarifications.

The work is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a meaningful problem in multimodal learning. The proposed approach is potentially applicable to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA, which increases its significance.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add an ablation study showing the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) to the overall performance.

2. Compare with the "Bottom-Up and Top-Down" attention model by Anderson et al. (2017), which is a strong recent baseline for VQA.

3. Justify the choice of using the top 3000 most frequent answers. Explain why this is sufficient for a fair evaluation and how it might affect the results.

4. Provide more detail on the 2-modality model used for comparison, including how it was implemented and why it underperforms.

5. Clarify the explanation of ternary potential computation in Section 3.2.3. Consider adding a small example to illustrate how the C3 tensor is used.

6. Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed approach compared to existing methods.

7. In the related work section, add a brief discussion of Anderson et al. (2017) and other recent influential VQA papers.

With these revisions, the paper would be significantly strengthened and would provide an even more compelling contribution to the field.

## Final Assessment

This paper presents a novel high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks that achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA. While the paper has some minor weaknesses in its evaluation and comparison, these are addressable with relatively small revisions. The contribution is significant, the technical approach is sound, and the paper is well-written. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
