

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (beyond the pairwise attention common in current literature) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors propose a probabilistic framework where attention is computed through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, with mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities. The approach is designed to be extendable to any number of modalities, which contrasts with most existing attention mechanisms that focus on pairwise interactions between two modalities.

The paper builds upon recent work on attention mechanisms for VQA (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]), but makes a clear conceptual departure by explicitly modeling higher-order interactions between modalities. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset with a relatively simple model architecture and fewer parameters than competing methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty:** The core idea of modeling high-order attention correlations is genuinely novel and represents a significant conceptual advance beyond the current literature. While some prior work (e.g., Kim et al. [14]) has touched on multi-variate probabilistic models, this paper provides the first systematic approach to high-order attention that can be easily extended to any number of modalities. This is a bold direction that could influence future research in multimodal learning.

2. **Simplicity and Effectiveness:** The approach is elegantly simple in its formulation, yet achieves strong empirical results. The authors demonstrate that their 3-modality model significantly outperforms a 2-modality baseline, providing concrete evidence for the value of high-order attention. The state-of-the-art results with a relatively small model (40 million parameters) compared to competing approaches (over 70 million) is impressive.

3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed mechanism, including detailed descriptions of the unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The figures effectively illustrate the attention mechanisms.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** While the authors demonstrate that high-order attention improves performance, they don't provide a strong theoretical argument for why higher-order interactions are necessary for VQA. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of why certain VQA questions require modeling interactions between all three modalities (image, question, answer).

2. **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't provide comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials). This makes it difficult to determine how much each part contributes to the final performance.

3. **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't thoroughly address the computational cost of the ternary potential calculation, which involves O(n^3) operations. This could be a significant limitation for practical applications with large inputs.

4. **Superficial Qualitative Analysis:** While the attention visualizations are interesting, the qualitative analysis is somewhat superficial. The paper could benefit from deeper analysis of why the high-order attention mechanism produces better results for specific question types.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

- The core idea of high-order attention represents a significant conceptual advance over current attention mechanisms.
- The approach is general and could be applied to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA.
- The state-of-the-art results demonstrate the practical value of the approach.
- The paper is well-written and clearly explains a novel concept that is potentially transformative for multimodal learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

- The paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for why high-order attention is necessary.
- The computational complexity of the ternary potential calculation is not adequately addressed.
- The ablation studies are limited, making it difficult to fully assess the contribution of each component.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a genuinely novel approach to attention mechanisms that could significantly impact multimodal learning. While the paper has some methodological shortcomings, the novelty and significance of the core idea outweigh these limitations. The authors have made a bold conceptual leap that moves beyond the current paradigm of pairwise attention mechanisms, and their empirical results support the value of this approach.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS. The high-order attention framework represents a promising new direction that could inspire future research in multimodal learning. The paper's simplicity, elegance, and strong empirical results further strengthen its case.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical justification and computational complexity concerns, though these are not essential for acceptance given the paper's significant contribution. The authors could strengthen the paper by adding a brief theoretical discussion of when high-order attention is particularly beneficial and by providing a more detailed analysis of computational complexity.

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by introducing a fundamentally new way of thinking about attention mechanisms, and I believe it will be of significant interest to the NeurIPS community.
