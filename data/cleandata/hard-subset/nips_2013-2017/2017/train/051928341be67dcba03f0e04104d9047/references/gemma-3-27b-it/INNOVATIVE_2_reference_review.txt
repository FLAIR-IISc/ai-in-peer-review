This paper introduces a novel high-order attention mechanism applicable to multi-modal data, demonstrated on the VQA task. The core idea – modeling k-order correlations between modalities – is a significant departure from standard attention approaches that typically focus on pairwise interactions. This is a bold attempt to move beyond simple co-attention and capture more complex relationships within the data. The authors frame the problem as a probabilistic model and use mean-field inference, which provides a principled way to handle the complexity. Achieving state-of-the-art results on VQA further strengthens the claim.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The high-order attention concept is genuinely novel. Existing attention mechanisms largely focus on pairwise relationships, and extending this to higher orders represents a potentially significant advance. The probabilistic formulation and mean-field inference approach are clever and offer a compelling way to manage the increased complexity.
*   **Generality:** The authors explicitly position their approach as generally applicable, not limited to VQA. The framework *should* be adaptable to other multi-modal tasks.
*   **Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset is a strong indicator of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. Figures 3 and 4 are particularly helpful in visualizing the attention mechanisms and tensor sketching.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity & Scalability:** While the authors address the computational issues with tensor sketching, the inherent complexity of high-order interactions could limit scalability to a very large number of modalities or high-dimensional data.  The practical limitations of extending this to *many* modalities aren’t fully explored.
*   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study comparing MCT and the 2-layer MCB is helpful, but more detailed ablation is needed. For example, the contribution of the *ternary* potentials (3-way correlations) isn’t clearly isolated. What is the performance gain solely from adding the third-order attention compared to just pairwise?
*   **Failure Case Analysis:** The identified failure case (Fig. 8) is concerning.  That the attention remains fixed regardless of the question suggests a potential rigidity in the model or a sensitivity to specific data biases. More comprehensive failure case analysis is warranted.
*   **Limited Visualization:** While some attention visualizations are provided, a more thorough qualitative analysis comparing attention maps across different questions and scenes would be beneficial.  Showing examples where the high-order attention *clearly* outperforms simpler methods would be convincing.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't address the sensitivity of the results to the various hyperparameters (e.g., the embedding dimension 'd', learning rate, dropout rates).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the high-order attention mechanism is substantial. This moves beyond incremental improvements in attention modeling.
*   The state-of-the-art results provide strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The authors have provided a clear and reasonably well-supported technical description of their method.
*   The potential for generalization beyond VQA is significant.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The complexity and potential scalability issues require further investigation.
*   The lack of detailed ablation studies makes it difficult to fully assess the contribution of each component of the model.
*   The failure case analysis raises concerns about the robustness of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper presenting a novel and promising approach to multi-modal attention. While there are some weaknesses, the significant novelty and strong empirical results outweigh these concerns. I am willing to overlook the relatively limited ablation study and the lack of extensive failure case analysis given the boldness of the idea.  This paper has the potential to significantly influence future research in multi-modal learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I encourage the authors to address the scalability concerns and provide more detailed ablation studies in future work.
