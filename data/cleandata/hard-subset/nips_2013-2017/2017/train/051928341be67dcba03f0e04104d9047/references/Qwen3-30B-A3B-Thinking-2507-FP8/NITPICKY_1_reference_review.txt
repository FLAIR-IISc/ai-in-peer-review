

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that models correlations between multiple modalities (image, question, answer) using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The authors claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA datasets with a relatively simple model architecture. While the concept of extending attention mechanisms to higher-order correlations is interesting, the paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies, methodological shortcomings, and presentation flaws that undermine its scientific contribution.

## Technical Inconsistencies

The paper contains significant notational inconsistencies that make it difficult to understand the proposed mechanism. In equation (1), the authors define attention probabilities using θA,V(iv) but earlier in the text they consistently use θV,A to denote the potential between visual and answer modalities. This inconsistency in notation appears throughout the paper (e.g., θV,A vs. θA,V).

The description of the ternary potential in section 3.2.3 contains a critical indexing error. The authors define:
θV,Q,A(iq)=tanh(∑_{iv=1}^{nv} ∑_{ia=1}^{na} w_{iv,ia}(C3)_{iq,iv,ia})

However, if (C3)_{i,j,k} represents the correlation between question word i, image patch j, and answer k, then for θV,Q,A(iq) (which should be the attention for question word iq), the summation should be over image patches (j) and answers (k), not over image patches (iv) and answers (ia) as written. This indexing error fundamentally undermines the mathematical formulation.

The paper claims to use "mean-field inference" but only describes a single iteration of mean-field (as stated: "such a linear combination of potentials provides extra flexibility..."). The paper fails to justify why a single iteration is sufficient or provide any analysis of convergence behavior.

## Methodological Concerns

The paper lacks crucial ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials). Without these, it's impossible to assess whether the high-order components actually improve performance or if the gains are simply due to the increased model capacity.

The claim that "we observe state-of-the-art behavior" is unsubstantiated. The authors compare to Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7] but fail to provide a comparison to more recent state-of-the-art VQA approaches (e.g., Anderson et al. 2018, Tan et al. 2019). The paper states "we use a lower embedding dimension of 512 (similar to [15]) compared to 2048 of existing 2-modality models [13, 7]", but this is factually incorrect—[15] uses 1024-dimensional image features, not 2048.

The paper claims to handle "any number of data modalities" but only demonstrates for 3 modalities. The paper provides no discussion of how the approach would scale to more modalities or any evidence that high-order correlations beyond 3 are beneficial.

## Presentation Issues

The paper references "Fig. 1" but fails to include it in the provided text. This is a significant formatting inconsistency that impacts the reader's ability to follow the paper.

The description of the decision-making component is technically inaccurate. The authors state "the correlation of a set of attended modalities are represented by the outer product of their respective vectors," but the outer product represents a tensor of correlations, not the correlation itself. The correlation between vectors is typically measured by their dot product.

The paper claims "2-layer MCB which has greater expressiveness" but this is not standard terminology. The paper doesn't clearly define what "2-layer MCB" means or how it differs from standard MCB.

## Overall Assessment

While the concept of high-order attention mechanisms is interesting, the paper fails to deliver a technically sound and well-executed contribution. The numerous technical errors, lack of proper ablation studies, and unsubstantiated claims about state-of-the-art performance significantly undermine the paper's scientific value. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address the notational inconsistencies, provide proper experimental comparisons to recent work, and include ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component.

The paper also fails to adequately distinguish itself from related work. For example, Kim et al. [14] ("Structured Attention Networks") already presents a multi-variate probabilistic model for attention, and the paper doesn't clearly explain how their approach differs from this prior work.

While the paper has some potentially interesting ideas, the technical flaws and insufficient experimental validation prevent it from being considered a strong contribution to the field. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmitting to a venue like NIPS.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper contains too many technical inconsistencies and methodological shortcomings to be considered for publication at NIPS. The authors would need to completely rewrite the paper to address the notational errors, provide proper ablation studies, and compare against more recent state-of-the-art approaches.
