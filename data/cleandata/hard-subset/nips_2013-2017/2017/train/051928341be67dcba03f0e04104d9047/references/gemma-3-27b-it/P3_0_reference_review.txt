## NIPS 2017 Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to attention mechanisms, extending traditional pairwise attention to higher-order correlations between multiple modalities. The idea is sound, the technical presentation is generally clear, and the empirical results on VQA demonstrate state-of-the-art performance. However, the paper suffers from a somewhat incremental nature, and the justification for the complexity of the third-order attention isn’t fully convincing from the reported results.  I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the limitations of existing attention mechanisms in multimodal tasks like VQA, specifically the ad-hoc extraction of representations and the task-specific nature of designs. The authors propose a high-order attention model capable of learning correlations between *k* data modalities, framing it as a probabilistic model solved with mean-field inference.  They demonstrate its effectiveness on VQA, achieving SOTA results.

The work builds upon a substantial body of literature on attention mechanisms for both images and text (reviewed comprehensively). It positions itself effectively by distinguishing itself from existing pairwise attention methods [15, 25] and bilinear models [13] by offering a more general framework easily extendable to arbitrary numbers of modalities. It also acknowledges related work incorporating probabilistic modeling [14], and dual attention [19] but claims superiority in scalability. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of earlier approaches in explicitly modeling multi-modal interactions.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Generality:** The core idea of extending attention to higher-order correlations is novel and offers a potentially powerful framework for modeling complex interactions in multi-modal data.  The claim of scalability to arbitrary modalities is a strong advantage.
* **Theoretical Framework:**  The formulation of the attention mechanism as a probabilistic model with mean-field inference provides a solid theoretical foundation and justification for the proposed approach.
* **Empirical Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset is a significant achievement and validates the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The review of related work is extensive and demonstrates a clear understanding of the existing literature. The authors clearly position their work within the field.
* **Clear Explanation:** The methodology is explained in a relatively clear and understandable manner, with helpful diagrams (Figs. 2 & 3) illustrating the key concepts.
* **Ablation Studies**: The comparison between MCT and 2-layer MCB units for decision making provides valuable insight into the design choices.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Improvement:** While achieving SOTA is commendable, the performance gain over existing methods (specifically [7]) isn’t dramatic. The gains from incorporating the third-order attention seem marginal based on the reported results.  A more substantial benefit would strengthen the justification for the added complexity.
* **Limited Analysis of Third-Order Attention:** The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of *when* or *why* the third-order attention is beneficial.  More in-depth qualitative examples exploring the impact of the third-order correlations would be valuable. The ablation study only demonstrates the difference between the decision making units, not the impact of different attention orders.
* **Computational Cost:** While scalability is claimed, the computational overhead of the third-order attention, especially with the tensor sketching, isn’t fully discussed. A discussion of the practical limitations (e.g., memory requirements) would be helpful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of different hyperparameters (e.g., dimensionality *d*, learning rate, dropout rates) on performance is not adequately explored.
* **Failure Case Discussion:** The single failure case presented (Fig. 8) is a bit simplistic. More thorough error analysis would reveal the weaknesses of the approach and guide future research.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Eq. (1):** The use of separate learnable parameters (αi, βi, γi) for each potential type seems somewhat arbitrary. Is there a justification for this choice? Could a single set of parameters be used across all potentials, or perhaps a smaller set grouped by modality?
* **Sec 4.1 Data Embedding:**  The choice of 512 as the embedding dimension seems somewhat low compared to recent VQA models. Was this a deliberate design choice to keep the model compact, or were there other constraints?
* **Fig. 7:** While the qualitative comparison is helpful, it would be more convincing if it included the attention maps from the baseline methods side-by-side for direct visual comparison.
* **Regarding MCT vs. 2-layer MCB:**  The authors mention the MCB approach’s greater expressiveness but do not explain precisely *why* this is the case beyond stating it allows image features to interact with themselves. A more detailed explanation would be helpful.




**5. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to multi-modal attention.
* State-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark (VQA).
* Thorough related work and clear presentation.
* Potential for broader applicability beyond VQA.

**Con:**

* Incremental improvement over existing methods.
* Limited analysis of the benefits of third-order attention.
* Potential computational overhead not fully addressed.

**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. I recommend the authors:

* Provide a more in-depth analysis of the third-order attention, including more qualitative examples demonstrating its impact.
* Discuss the computational cost of the approach and its scalability limitations.
* Explore the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters.
* Provide a more comprehensive error analysis, including more detailed failure case studies.
* Consider adding a side-by-side visual comparison with baseline attention maps in Fig. 7.
