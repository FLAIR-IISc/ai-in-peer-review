- Overall assessment: Novel, well-motivated high-order attention model for VQA with strong empirical results; recommend Accept with minor revisions.  
- Problem addressed: Existing attention mechanisms are ad-hoc and fail to explicitly model multi-modal interactions.  
- Proposed method: High-order attention using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials with mean-field inference to obtain marginals.  
- Scalability claim: Framework scales to an arbitrary number of modalities and is extendable beyond three modalities.  
- Relation to prior work: Differentiates from prior methods that combine modalities early or focus only on pairwise interactions; comparison to bilinear and related models is appropriate.  
- Novelty strength: Modeling high-order correlations via potentials and mean-field inference is a genuinely new, principled approach.  
- Empirical strength: Achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA benchmark, validating the approach.  
- Generality strength: Framework applicable to a wide range of multi-modal tasks.  
- Motivation & exposition: Problem motivation is clear and related work is thorough.  
- Mathematical detail: Paper provides detailed mathematical formulation, including tensor sketching.  
- Presentation weakness: Method presentation is dense and can be hard to follow; needs more intuitive explanations and visuals.  
- Ablation weakness: Lacks detailed ablation studies quantifying contributions of unary/pairwise/ternary components.  
- Qualitative analysis weakness: Limited qualitative examples and insufficient systematic analysis of failure cases (notably constant attention across questions in one example).  
- MCB/MCT concern: Improvement of 2-layer MCB over MCT is reported but not well-justified theoretically or empirically.  
- Hyperparameter concern: No discussion of sensitivity to hyperparameters like embedding dimension, learning rate, or dropout.  
- Acceptance arguments for: Novel approach, SOTA results, generalizable framework, thorough literature review.  
- Acceptance arguments against: Complex presentation, incomplete empirical/qualitative analyses.  
- Recommendation for revisions: Clarify mean-field inference intuitively, add diagrams, run ablation studies, expand attention analysis and failure case study, and justify the MCB choice.
