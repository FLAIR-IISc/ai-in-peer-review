Summary
The paper proposes a general high-order attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” across multiple modalities (image, question, and candidate answers) and combines them via a learned linear mixture followed by a softmax to yield attention distributions over each modality. Pairwise correlations are implemented as learned linear projections followed by inner products (similarity matrices), and ternary correlations as a third-order tensor of inner products. The authors interpret the softmax over a linear combination of marginalized potentials as a single mean-field iteration. Attended features are then fused for decision making using compact bilinear or trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) via tensor sketching. On VQA v1 multiple-choice, they claim state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters than prior work such as MCB, and show qualitative attention visualizations indicating question-dependent foci across modalities.

Relation to prior work
The approach is positioned relative to co-attention (Lu et al.), MCB (Fukui et al.), bilinear/low-rank pooling (Kim et al.), structured attention (Kim et al.), and dual attention (Nam et al.). The key claimed novelty is explicit modeling of higher-order dependencies (beyond pairwise) among modalities within an attention-as-potentials view, and a simple recipe that “extends to any number of modalities.” The decision layer uses established tensor sketching (Pham & Pagh) as in MCB, generalized to three modalities (MCT).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The unary and pairwise constructions are standard and reasonable; the ternary construction is a straightforward extension. The “mean-field” interpretation is suggestive but not fully derived: there is no explicit CRF energy, independence assumptions, or variational objective shown, so calling the single softmax step “mean-field inference” is more heuristic than principled. Still, the model is clearly implementable and differentiable end-to-end.
- Empirical support: Training details are provided and a code link is given. However, crucial quantitative details are missing from the text provided: exact test-dev/test-std numbers (and confidence intervals) are referenced but not shown here; only a small ablation (MCT vs two-layer MCB on val) is reported. It is also unclear whether all comparisons are apples-to-apples (e.g., do baselines consume answer choices as inputs for MC like this model does?). The qualitative visualizations are compelling but not substitutes for quantitative analysis.
- Ablations and analysis: Limited. The paper asserts the importance of ternary potentials and shows a 3-modality gain over 2-modality, but there is no thorough ablation isolating the contributions of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials (e.g., removing θV,Q,A or zeroing mixing weights). The learned mixing coefficients (α, β, γ) are not analyzed. Runtime/memory costs of C3 are not discussed; although na is small in MC VQA, higher-order scaling could be problematic for more modalities or larger candidate sets.

Clarity
The paper is generally understandable and well-motivated, with clear high-level figures and intuitions. That said:
- Some notation is inconsistent or contains typos (e.g., repeated aV where aA is intended; missing details about the marginalization weights wiv, wiq, wiq,ia; and overloaded d). The “mean-field” description would benefit from a formal energy and update derivation to justify the linear combination and single-iteration choice.
- The experimental section needs explicit numbers, standard deviations across runs, and clearer statement of which features (VGG vs ResNet) were used for the main result.
- Comparisons: please clarify whether baselines for MC use candidate answer embeddings as an input modality.

Originality
Moderately novel. Prior work has used pairwise co-attention and bilinear pooling extensively; this paper’s contribution is to unify unary/pairwise/ternary potentials within an attention-as-potentials framework and to extend compact pooling to trilinear interactions. The conceptual framing and the ternary attention across image, question, and answers are incremental but meaningful. The claim of easy extensibility to arbitrary numbers of modalities is conceptually true but practically limited by combinatorial growth; discussing this trade-off would strengthen the originality narrative.

Significance
If the claimed SOTA on VQA multiple-choice holds with fair comparisons, the empirical significance is nontrivial. Modeling higher-order correlations within attention is likely to influence subsequent multimodal work, and MCT is a plausible tool for tri-modal fusion. However, the breadth of validation is narrow (only VQA v1 MC). Demonstrating benefits on open-ended VQA, Visual7W, or referring expressions, and stronger ablations, would increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear, general formulation of multi-modal attention potentials (unary/pairwise/ternary) with simple, differentiable implementations.
- Practical engineering: uses tensor sketching to keep tri-modal fusion tractable; shows that two-layer MCB can outperform MCT in practice.
- Qualitative attention visualizations support the intended behavior.
- Claims competitive or better results with fewer parameters.

Weaknesses
- “Mean-field” justification is informal; lacks a proper probabilistic derivation and analysis of convergence/iterations.
- Missing or incomplete quantitative details in the text provided; limited ablations and fairness checks vs baselines.
- Scalability concerns for higher-order potentials beyond three modalities are not addressed.
- Clarity/typos and some notational ambiguities; minor reproducibility gaps (exact numbers, features, random seeds).

Questions for the authors
- Please provide the exact test-dev and test-std accuracies (overall and per question type) for all variants, with error bars across runs.
- Do competing MC baselines also use candidate answers as an input modality? If not, can you include such baselines for a fair comparison?
- Can you report ablations removing ternary or pairwise potentials and/or fixing mixing weights to quantify their contributions?
- What are the memory/runtime costs of computing C3 during training/inference? How does na affect throughput?
- Did you try multiple mean-field iterations or non-linear combinations of potentials?

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a conceptually clean, general mechanism for higher-order multimodal attention.
- Likely practical utility for VQA and related tasks; competitive empirical results with fewer parameters.
- Insightful visualizations and an implementable, end-to-end trainable model.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evidence and analysis are not as thorough as expected for NIPS; unclear fairness of comparisons and missing quantitative details.
- The probabilistic “mean-field” framing is more rhetoric than rigor.
- Limited evaluation scope and ablations; scalability beyond tri-modal MC VQA not demonstrated.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea of high-order attention potentials across modalities is interesting and the reported results suggest merit, but the paper would benefit from clearer derivations, stronger quantitative evidence, and more comprehensive ablations.
