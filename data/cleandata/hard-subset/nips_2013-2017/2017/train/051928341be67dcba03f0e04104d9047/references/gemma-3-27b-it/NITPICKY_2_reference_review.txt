## Review of High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism based on high-order correlations between data modalities, applied to the Visual Question Answering (VQA) task. The core idea is to move beyond pairwise interactions and model k-th order dependencies between modalities (image, question, and answer), using a probabilistic framework and mean-field inference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

While the presented approach demonstrates promising results and addresses a relevant problem in multimodal learning, the paper suffers from a concerning number of minor flaws and a lack of rigorous analysis, leading me to recommend rejection despite the novelty of the core idea. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of directly modeling high-order correlations in an attention mechanism is a valuable contribution. Extending attention beyond pairwise interactions is a logical step and offers potential benefits.
* **State-of-the-art performance:** Achieving SOTA results on VQA, even with a relatively small model size, is a positive indicator.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing attention mechanisms (ad-hoc representations, task specificity) and motivates the proposed approach.
* **Probabilistic Framework:** Framing the attention mechanism within a probabilistic model with mean-field inference is a sound approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper lacks crucial ablation studies. It’s unclear what the contribution of each order of correlation (unary, pairwise, ternary) is to the final performance.  Presenting results for models trained with only unary, only pairwise, and only ternary potentials would be vital to understanding the efficacy of the proposed approach. Simply stating that the 3-modality model performs best isn't sufficient.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters (learning rate, dropout, embedding dimension, α, β, γ parameters in Eq. 1) is not discussed. The parameters chosen seem somewhat arbitrary, and a sensitivity analysis is required.
* **Implementation Details:**  The description of the implementation is insufficient. For example, the convolution operation described in section 3.2.1 isn't standard and could benefit from clarification. Is it a 1x1 convolution applied to each feature independently, or across all features? The details of the tensor sketch algorithm (used in decision making) are glossed over, referring only to previous work. A more detailed explanation, or at least a more informative citation, is needed.
* **Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to existing methods (Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) is limited. The authors mention using similar experimental setup, but don’t specify how the baselines were implemented. Were publicly available implementations used, or were they reimplemented? This is a critical detail. Furthermore, the comparison focuses solely on multiple-choice accuracy.  Reporting results on open-ended VQA would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Qualitative Analysis:** The qualitative analysis (Figs. 5-8) is insufficient. The visualizations lack explanation, and the failure case (Fig. 8) is presented without further discussion. Why does the model fail in this specific instance? What could be done to mitigate this issue?
* **Formatting/Writing Issues:** Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies are present. For example, "k−th order correlations" appears inconsistently. Equations are not numbered, making it difficult to reference them. Section 3.2.3 begins with “To capture the dependencies between all three modalities…” but then describes the calculation of pairwise correlations.
* **Redundancy and Verbosity:** The introduction is overly verbose and contains repetitive statements. The related work section is lengthy and could be more concise.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel approach to multimodal attention.
* State-of-the-art results.

**Con:**

* Lack of ablation studies.
* Insufficient implementation details.
* Limited comparison to baselines.
* Minor flaws in writing and formatting.
* Lack of rigorous hyperparameter analysis.
* Insufficient qualitative analysis and discussion of failure cases. 



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty and promising results, the numerous weaknesses outlined above raise serious concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach. The lack of thorough experimentation and analysis prevents a clear understanding of the contributions of the different components of the model. The minor flaws in writing and formatting contribute to a perception of carelessness. While the idea is intriguing, the paper, as it stands, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS.  I recommend rejection and encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses in a revised version. Specifically, a more comprehensive set of experiments, including ablation studies and hyperparameter analysis, is crucial. A clearer and more detailed description of the implementation and a more thorough comparison to relevant baselines are also essential.
