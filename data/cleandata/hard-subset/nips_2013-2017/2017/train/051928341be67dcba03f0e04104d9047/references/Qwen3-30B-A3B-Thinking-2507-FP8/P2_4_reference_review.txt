

# Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

This paper proposes an extension of attention mechanisms to model high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (visual, textual, etc.) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors introduce unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture interactions between modalities, using mean-field inference to obtain marginal attention probabilities. They claim state-of-the-art performance on VQA datasets using this approach.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound in its approach, presenting a well-structured extension of attention mechanisms to higher-order correlations. The model uses standard techniques like tensor sketching for efficient computation, and the experimental setup is reasonable with appropriate baselines. However, the paper has significant weaknesses in evaluation methodology.

The claimed state-of-the-art performance is modest (64.57% vs. 63.82% for MCT vs. 2-layer MCB on the validation set), yet the paper fails to provide statistical significance tests for these improvements. More critically, the paper lacks a thorough ablation study to determine how much each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) contributes to the final performance. Without this, it's impossible to determine whether the high-order attention is truly responsible for the improvement or if it's simply a consequence of using a different decision module (2-layer MCB).

The authors cite Fukui et al. [7] who introduced MCB (Multimodal Compact Bilinear Pooling) and its extension MCT (Multimodal Compact Trilinear Pooling), yet the paper's "ternary potential" is functionally equivalent to MCT. The paper doesn't adequately explain how their probabilistic approach differs from these existing techniques, which weakens the novelty claim.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The technical details are presented logically, and the figures (particularly Figures 3-8) effectively illustrate the key concepts. The authors provide sufficient implementation details (Torch framework, hyperparameters) for reproducibility.

However, the paper could be clearer about the distinction between their approach and previous work. The authors mention that "all of these approaches model attention as a single network" but fail to explicitly state how their probabilistic model differs from existing bilinear approaches. The description of their "high-order attention" is somewhat ambiguous compared to the existing literature on bilinear models.

## Originality Assessment

The paper's main contribution is extending attention mechanisms to high-order correlations, but this is an incremental rather than a fundamentally novel idea. The approach appears to be a combination of existing techniques (probabilistic modeling, tensor sketching) rather than a new concept. The paper's "ternary potential" is essentially the same as the trilinear pooling used in MCT, which was proposed by Fukui et al. [7].

The authors claim that "attention mechanism with several potentials haven't been discussed in detail yet," but this isn't accurate given the existing literature on bilinear models and multimodal pooling. The paper doesn't adequately address how their probabilistic approach differs from previous work, making the novelty claim questionable.

## Significance Assessment

The paper achieves modest improvements on VQA benchmarks, which is significant for the task. However, the significance is limited by several factors: the small performance gains, the lack of clear evidence that high-order attention is the key innovation (vs. the 2-layer MCB decision module), and the failure to explore broader applications beyond VQA.

The paper doesn't demonstrate how high-order attention specifically improves the model's ability to answer questions, which would strengthen the significance claim. The qualitative analysis in Figures 5-7 is helpful but doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the main claim.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a reasonable extension of attention mechanisms to high-order correlations, but the novelty is overstated and the significance is limited by modest performance gains without proper attribution to existing work. The paper would be significantly stronger with a thorough ablation study, clearer distinction from prior work, and more detailed analysis of why the approach works better.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

**Pros:**
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Logical extension of attention mechanisms
- Modest performance improvements over previous methods

**Cons:**
- Novelty overstated; builds heavily on existing bilinear methods
- Modest performance gains (0.7% on validation set) without statistical significance
- Lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of high-order attention
- Superficial comparison to related work (particularly MCB/MCT)

**Recommendation: Weak Rejection**

This paper makes a small contribution to the field but fails to sufficiently demonstrate its novelty or significance for NeurIPS. The performance improvements are modest and not well-justified, and the paper doesn't adequately distinguish its approach from existing bilinear models. The authors would need to significantly revise the paper to properly acknowledge prior work, conduct thorough ablation studies, and provide stronger evidence for the claimed benefits of high-order attention before it would be suitable for NeurIPS.

The paper's main value lies in its clear exposition of attention mechanisms, but this alone isn't sufficient for acceptance at NeurIPS. The field would benefit more from a paper that clearly demonstrates why high-order attention is necessary for improved performance, rather than simply extending existing techniques with minimal justification.
