

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a "high-order attention" mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that models correlations between image, question, and answer modalities through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The authors claim their approach represents a novel generalization of existing attention mechanisms, which they argue typically handle only pairwise interactions between modalities. The method combines these potentials using a linear combination with learnable weights, followed by a soft-max operation to obtain attention probabilities.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**
The paper builds on existing attention mechanisms for VQA, particularly Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25], which use pairwise interactions between image and question features. The authors extend this to include ternary (three-way) interactions between image, question, and answer features, claiming this represents a significant advance. However, the proposed "high-order" attention is largely a straightforward extension of established pairwise attention mechanisms rather than a fundamentally novel approach. The use of "mean-field inference" is misleading, as the method is a simple linear combination of potentials with learnable weights, not true mean-field inference which would involve iterative updates.

**Strengths:**
The paper provides a clear description of the attention mechanism and demonstrates competitive performance on VQA datasets. The qualitative analysis showing attention patterns for different questions is generally well-executed and provides some useful insights.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Misrepresentation of Methodology:** The paper misuses the term "mean-field inference" to describe a simple linear combination of potentials. True mean-field inference involves iterative updates to approximate marginal probabilities, which is not implemented here. This misrepresentation undermines the paper's technical credibility.

2. **Lack of Novelty:** The core idea is a direct extension of existing pairwise attention mechanisms. The ternary potential is merely a natural extension of pairwise potentials, not a fundamentally new concept. The paper fails to adequately distinguish their approach from existing methods like the bilinear models in [13, 7] or the joint attention in [15].

3. **Insufficient Validation:** The paper compares against Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], but does not benchmark against the strongest recent baselines. For example, the VQA challenge winners in 2017 (e.g., [11]) achieved significantly higher accuracy (around 70% on VQA test-dev), while the authors report only 64.57% with their best model. This suggests their approach may not actually be state-of-the-art.

4. **Inadequate Ablation Studies:** The paper shows that the 3-modality model outperforms a 2-modality model, but fails to demonstrate the specific contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary). Without such ablation, it's impossible to determine whether the ternary potential provides meaningful improvement over pairwise attention.

5. **Overhyped Claims:** The paper claims to "address both issues" with current attention mechanisms, but the proposed solution doesn't clearly solve the stated problems. The "high-order" aspect is overhyped - they only extend to ternary (3-way) interactions, which is not truly "high-order" in the mathematical sense.

6. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides no theoretical argument for why high-order attention should work better than pairwise attention. Without this, the approach appears to be a heuristic extension rather than a principled advance.

**Recommendation:**
This paper does not meet NIPS standards for methodological rigor and novelty. While the approach is technically sound and achieves competitive results, the claimed novelty is overstated, and the paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its main claims. The misrepresentation of "mean-field inference" and the misleading terminology around "high-order" attention are particularly concerning for a venue like NIPS that values precision.

The authors should either significantly revise their claims to accurately reflect the incremental nature of their contribution, or provide stronger evidence of the benefits of high-order attention through more comprehensive ablation studies and comparisons with the most recent and strongest baselines.

The paper would be better suited for a conference with less emphasis on theoretical novelty and more focus on practical applications. For NIPS, the contribution appears too incremental to justify acceptance without substantial revisions addressing the methodological concerns and overstatement of novelty.
