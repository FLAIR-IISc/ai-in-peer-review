

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, particularly visual question answering (VQA), that claims to model high-order correlations between data modalities. The authors present a probabilistic framework using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials with mean-field inference to compute attention weights. The method is evaluated on the VQA dataset, with claims of state-of-the-art performance.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in multimodal learning and provides a clear framework for extending attention mechanisms to higher-order correlations. The motivation for modeling high-order interactions is well-justified, and the experimental results on VQA are promising. The authors make a reasonable effort to compare with relevant baselines (Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7]).

## Weaknesses

**Critical Technical Flaw in Formulation (Major Issue):** The core formulation in equation (1) contains a fundamental notational and conceptual error. The authors define θV,Q,A as a ternary potential capturing dependencies between three modalities, yet they use it as if it were a vector in PV(iv) = ... + α4θV,Q,A(iv) + ... This is inconsistent because θV,Q,A is a 3D tensor, not a vector indexed by iv. The notation suggests a scalar value for each image patch, but the potential should depend on all three modalities simultaneously. This error undermines the proposed "high-order" mechanism and makes the method as described technically incoherent.

**Inconsistent Notation and Terminology:** The paper uses inconsistent indexing throughout. For instance, in section 3.2.2, the pairwise potential is defined as (C2)i,j = corr2((QWq):,i, (VWv):,j), which correctly relates question word i to image patch j. However, in the attention computation, they write θV,Q(iq) = tanh(∑iv wiv (C2)iv,iq), which incorrectly uses (C2)iv,iq (image patch index first, then question word index) when it should be (C2)iq,iv. This indexing confusion appears multiple times, making the method difficult to understand.

**Confusing Description of MCB/MCT:** The paper claims to use "2-layer MCB unit combination" for three modalities, but describes it as employing features of the form ((aV)i · (aQ)j · (aQ)k · (aA)t), which suggests four vectors (not three). This is inconsistent with the three-modalities framework. The description of how the 2-layer MCB combines three modalities is unclear and potentially incorrect.

**Incomplete Baseline Comparison:** The paper compares only to Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], but omits several strong VQA baselines from recent literature (e.g., Anderson et al. "Bottom-up and Top-down" 2017, which is a standard benchmark). The claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is therefore unsubstantiated without these comparisons.

**Technical Ambiguities:** Several key components are ambiguously defined:
- The parameters wiv and wiq in section 3.2.2 are never formally defined.
- The "marginalized" potentials in Figure 3 are not properly explained in the text.
- The explanation of how the ternary potential (C3)i,j,k is computed is technically incorrect; corr3(q,v,a) = ∑l qlvlal is not a standard correlation measure.

**Minor but Significant Formatting Issues:**
- Figure 4 is referenced but not included in the text.
- In section 4.1, the paper states "we embed both the 196 VGG-19 or ResNet features into a d = 512 dimensional space," but VGG-19 features are 512×14×14 (not 196×512), so the embedding is unclear.
- In the references, "Torch7" citation has extraneous commas.
- The paper claims "3-modality" model but then describes a "2-modality model" in the results section without clear explanation.

**Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper states "we perform mean-field inference" but provides no theoretical justification for why this approach is appropriate for the graphical model they've defined. The combination of potentials in equation (1) is presented as a heuristic without theoretical grounding.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses an interesting problem and achieves promising results, the fundamental technical flaw in the core formulation (equation 1) is a critical issue that undermines the entire proposed method. The inconsistent notation and ambiguous descriptions further weaken the paper's technical contribution. The incomplete baseline comparison and several formatting inconsistencies prevent the reader from fully understanding and evaluating the method.

The authors have a good idea but have failed to properly formalize it. The technical errors in the core mechanism would require significant reworking before the method could be validated. Given the stringent standards of NIPS, these issues are substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite the paper's promising experimental results.

This paper is not ready for publication in NIPS in its current form. The authors would need to completely re-derive their attention mechanism with proper notation, provide a clear theoretical justification for their approach, and conduct a more comprehensive comparison with recent VQA baselines before resubmission.
