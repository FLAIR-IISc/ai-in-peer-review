**Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"**

**Summary**
This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, demonstrated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The central idea is to explicitly model correlations between modalities (image, question, and answers) using a framework inspired by probabilistic models. The authors define unary, pairwise, and ternary "potentials" which represent the importance of individual features and their interactions. These potentials are combined in a weighted sum and passed through a softmax to generate attention distributions for each modality. The authors refer to this process as a single iteration of mean-field inference. The resulting attended feature vectors are then fused using Multimodal Compact Bilinear/Trilinear (MCB/MCT) pooling for final answer prediction. The model is claimed to achieve state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The core idea of framing multi-modal attention as a problem of modeling high-order correlations via potentials is elegant and provides a structured way to think about interactions between modalities. This is a potentially more principled approach than some of the more ad-hoc co-attention mechanisms in prior work.
2.  **Generalizability:** The proposed framework is, in principle, extensible to an arbitrary number of `k` modalities by defining `k`-th order potentials, which could be a valuable contribution for future work on more complex multi-modal tasks.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper reports state-of-the-art results on the challenging VQA benchmark, which suggests that the proposed architectural components are effective in practice.

**Weaknesses**
Despite the interesting high-level idea and strong results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its technical presentation, experimental evaluation, and overall clarity. These issues are severe enough to undermine the paper's contribution and reproducibility.

1.  **Lack of Technical Rigor and Clarity:**
    *   **Misleading "Mean-Field Inference" Claim:** The paper repeatedly claims to perform "mean-field inference." However, Equation (1) describes a single, feed-forward step involving a learned linear combination of potential terms followed by a softmax. This is not an iterative inference procedure as the term "mean-field inference" implies. At best, it resembles a single iteration, but framing it as such is misleading and imbues the model with a sense of probabilistic rigor it does not possess. It is fundamentally a standard neural attention architecture with a particular justification.
    *   **Ambiguous Potential Calculation:** The computation of marginalized pairwise and ternary potentials is critically underspecified. In Section 3.2.2, the equation for `θV,Q(iq)` involves weights `w_iv` that are never defined. Are they learned? Are they uniform? The text states this is done by convolving "using a 1x1 dimensional kernel," but this description is vague and does not clearly map to the provided equation. The same ambiguity applies to the ternary potentials. This is a crucial methodological detail that is entirely missing, making the model impossible to reproduce from the paper alone.
    *   **Unclear Decision Module:** The description of the "2-layer MCB unit" in Section 4.2 is confusing. The text claims it permits features of the form `((aV)i · (aQ)j · (aQ)k · (aA)t)`, which is not what a standard composition of two MCB units would compute. A 2-layer MCB would typically compute `z = MCB(MCB(aV, aQ), aA)` or a similar grouping. The description provided is either incorrect or requires significant clarification.

2.  **Poor Presentation and Numerous Errors:**
    *   **Inconsistent Notation and Typos:** The manuscript is riddled with notational errors and typos that demonstrate a lack of care.
        *   In Section 3, the footer for the NIPS conference lists "2017".
        *   In Section 3.2, the equation for the attended answer vector `aA` is mistakenly written as `aV = Σ PA(ia)aia`. The attended image vector equation uses `viv` instead of the correctly indexed `v_iv`.
        *   In Section 3.2.2, the correlation matrix `C2` is defined with `(VWv)>`. It is unclear if `>` denotes a transpose; standard notation (`T`) should be used. The indexing `(C2)i,j` is used initially, then switches to `(C2)iv,iq`.
        *   Figure 3 is inconsistent with the text. The diagram labels outputs `θQ,V(iq)` and `θQ,V(iv)`, while the text and equations use `θV,Q(iq)` and `θV,Q(iv)`.
    *   **Sloppy Referencing:** The bibliography is poorly formatted. Multiple entries are incomplete (e.g., [9], [1], [23]), containing extra commas or missing venue and year information. This is below the expected standard for a top-tier publication.
    *   **Vague Language:** The introduction claims existing methods are "ad-hoc and entangled," yet the proposed model involves its own set of ad-hoc design choices (e.g., the specific sequence of conv-tanh-conv for unary potentials, the learned linear combination of potentials via `α, β, γ` parameters). The claim that the proposed method is fundamentally less "ad-hoc" is not well-substantiated.

3.  **Weak Experimental Analysis:**
    *   **Unfair Baseline Comparison:** The authors dismiss their own 2-modality model's weaker performance by stating they used a lower embedding dimension (512 vs. 2048) than competitors. This invalidates the ablation. To convincingly argue for the benefit of the 3-modality model, a direct comparison should have been made where the 2-modality baseline was configured as strongly as possible (i.e., with a 2048 dimension). As it stands, the contribution of the third modality is entangled with the effect of model capacity.
    *   **Superficial Qualitative Analysis:** The qualitative analysis is anecdotal. The failure case in Fig. 8 is appreciated for its honesty, but the analysis is shallow ("Our system focuses on the colorful umbrella..."). A deeper investigation into *why* the model fails in this manner would have been more insightful.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper presents a promising direction for multi-modal attention and achieves strong results, the execution is deeply flawed. The lack of technical clarity in core components of the method, the misleading claims about the theoretical underpinnings ("mean-field inference"), the weak experimental ablation, and the sheer number of typographical and notational errors make the paper difficult to understand, impossible to reproduce, and ultimately not ready for publication at NIPS. The high-level ideas are interesting, but they are buried under a presentation that lacks the rigor, precision, and care required of a top-tier scientific contribution. The authors should be encouraged to significantly revise the manuscript to address these critical issues.
