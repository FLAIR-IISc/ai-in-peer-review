- Proposes high-order attention (unary, pairwise, ternary) for multimodal VQA using mean-field inference and claims general applicability and SOTA results.  
- Extends bilinear attention mechanisms by adding higher-order interaction terms and uses qualitative examples to illustrate attention behavior.

- Strength: Clear identification of the need for more expressive multi-modal attention mechanisms.  
- Strength: Reports competitive results on VQA and shows 3-modality model outperforms 2-modality.  
- Strength: Useful qualitative visualizations of attention maps.  
- Strength: Argues the method is extendable to more than two modalities.

- Weakness: Novelty is overstated; approach is essentially a natural trilinear extension of existing bilinear methods rather than a fundamentally new idea.  
- Weakness: Claims of general k-order modeling are unsupported—implementation only covers up to ternary interactions.  
- Weakness: Fails to clearly differentiate from prior bilinear/MCB work (e.g., Fukui et al., Kim et al.).

- Weakness: Ablation studies are insufficient and do not isolate the effect of high-order attention from other model changes (e.g., answer embeddings).  
- Weakness: Reported performance gains are marginal (e.g., 0.75% improvement) and not convincingly attributed to high-order terms.  
- Weakness: Lacks comparison to the most recent VQA state-of-the-art methods.

- Weakness: Theoretical/methodological concerns—mean-field inference and the claimed probabilistic model lack justification and a clear probabilistic objective.  
- Weakness: Linear weighting of potentials is not novel and ternary potential reduces to a standard trilinear form.

- Weakness: Experimental limitations—model uses fewer parameters and lower embedding dimension than common baselines, making attribution of gains unclear.  
- Weakness: Qualitative results do not convincingly demonstrate advantages over existing attention methods.

- Weakness: Related work discussion is problematic—mischaracterizes Lu et al., omits clear differentiation from Kim et al., and under-acknowledges prior work using answers as inputs (Jabri et al.).

- Overall assessment: Modest technical contribution that overclaims novelty and fails to convincingly demonstrate superiority over simpler pairwise methods.  
- Recommendation: Reject.

- Justification for rejection: insufficient novelty, marginal/significance of gains unclear, inadequate ablation studies, missing SOTA comparisons, and weak theoretical grounding.

- Suggested improvements: perform thorough ablations isolating high-order attention effects.  
- Suggested improvements: compare against recent VQA challenge winners and stronger baselines.  
- Suggested improvements: provide clear theoretical justification for the probabilistic formulation and use of mean-field inference.  
- Suggested improvements: explicitly clarify differences from Fukui et al. and Kim et al. and address related work omissions.  
- Suggested improvements: re-evaluate model/configuration choices (parameter counts, embedding dims) or control for them in experiments.  
- Suggested improvements: demonstrate more substantial empirical or qualitative advantages to justify added complexity.
