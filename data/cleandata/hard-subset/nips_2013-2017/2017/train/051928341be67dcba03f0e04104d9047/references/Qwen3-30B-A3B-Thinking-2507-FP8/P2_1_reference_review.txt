

# Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism called "High-Order Attention Models" that extends traditional attention mechanisms to model high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., image, question, answer) in Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms primarily focus on single-modality or pairwise interactions but fail to effectively model higher-order correlations between multiple modalities. Their approach models attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, and uses mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities. The method is applied to VQA, where it achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset. The paper provides a comprehensive related work section, detailed mathematical formulation, and both quantitative and qualitative evaluation.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a well-structured approach. The authors provide clear mathematical formulations for unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, and their probabilistic interpretation of attention is theoretically grounded. The experimental setup is well-defined with appropriate baselines (Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7]), and they provide both quantitative results (on test-dev and test-std datasets) and qualitative analysis (visualizing attention maps).

However, the paper has some weaknesses in quality. While they claim state-of-the-art performance, they don't compare against more recent VQA approaches that might have emerged after their submission date (2017). The paper also lacks a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) to the final performance. Additionally, the computational complexity of the ternary potential (which involves a 3D tensor) isn't discussed, which is an important consideration for practical implementation.

## Clarity
The paper is well-written and well-organized. The mathematical formulations are clear and logically presented, with helpful visualizations (Figures 3 and 4) that illustrate the attention mechanisms. The experimental section provides sufficient implementation details (hyperparameters, network architecture, training details) to allow reproduction. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from prior work in the related work section.

One minor weakness is that the paper doesn't provide enough detail on why their 3-modality model performs better than 2-modality models beyond stating it as a fact. A more detailed analysis of the specific interactions between modalities would enhance clarity.

## Originality
The paper presents a novel extension of attention mechanisms to model high-order correlations between multiple data modalities. While attention mechanisms for VQA have been studied extensively, the authors correctly identify that existing approaches primarily focus on pairwise interactions or single-modality attention, and don't explicitly model higher-order correlations. Their probabilistic approach with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials is a meaningful contribution that extends the current state-of-the-art.

The related work section is comprehensive and properly contextualizes their contribution. They clearly differentiate their work from existing approaches, particularly noting that methods like Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25] are limited to pairwise attention and difficult to extend to more than two modalities. The paper demonstrates that their approach can be trivially extended to any number of modalities, which is a significant theoretical contribution.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important problem in multimodal learning - the need for effective attention mechanisms that can model complex interactions between multiple data modalities. VQA is a challenging task that requires understanding of both visual and textual information, and the authors demonstrate that their high-order attention model leads to improved performance.

The significance of their contribution is strengthened by the state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, which shows practical impact. The paper also provides valuable insights into how attention mechanisms can be designed to better capture complex multimodal interactions. The authors' approach could potentially be applied to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA, such as image captioning or visual reasoning.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated extension of attention mechanisms to model high-order correlations
- Clear probabilistic formulation with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials
- State-of-the-art results on VQA dataset
- Comprehensive qualitative analysis with attention visualization
- Generalizable to any number of modalities (theoretically)
- Good organization and clear writing

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited comparison to more recent VQA approaches (post-2017)
- Lack of thorough ablation study to isolate component contributions
- Insufficient discussion of computational complexity of ternary potential
- The claim of "state-of-the-art" performance isn't fully justified without comparing to the most recent methods
- The paper doesn't deeply analyze why their approach works better than baselines

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multimodal learning by extending attention mechanisms to model high-order correlations between data modalities. The technical approach is sound, the results are impressive, and the paper is well-written.

The main weaknesses are the limited comparison to more recent work and the lack of a thorough ablation study. The authors should address these by:
1. Adding a comparison to more recent VQA methods (from 2017-2018)
2. Including a detailed ablation study showing the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials)
3. Discussing the computational complexity of the ternary potential

These revisions would strengthen the paper significantly and better support their claim of state-of-the-art performance.

## Final Assessment
This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of multimodal learning by extending attention mechanisms to model high-order correlations between multiple data modalities. The approach is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on VQA. While the paper has some weaknesses in thoroughness of evaluation, these can be addressed with minor revisions. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the state-of-the-art in a meaningful way and provides a novel theoretical framework with practical applications.

**Overall Rating: Strong Accept**
