

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) based on modeling high-order correlations between visual, textual, and answer modalities. The authors frame their approach as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities. The method is evaluated on VQA datasets and claimed to achieve state-of-the-art results.

The paper builds on existing attention mechanisms for VQA, particularly those of Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016), but claims novelty through its high-order correlation modeling. The authors present a theoretically grounded approach that extends beyond pairwise attention to capture higher-order interactions between modalities. They demonstrate their method on VQA, showing improved performance over two baselines.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The attention mechanism is theoretically sound, and the probabilistic formulation provides a principled way to combine different types of attention. The qualitative visualizations of attention patterns are compelling and help illustrate how the model processes different question types.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising results, the paper suffers from significant methodological shortcomings that undermine its claims of novelty and effectiveness. The most concerning issue is the lack of thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the high-order attention mechanism. The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance but only compares against two baselines (Lu et al. and Fukui et al.), both of which are older methods. The authors do not compare against more recent VQA approaches from 2017 that would provide a more meaningful benchmark.

The paper also fails to provide sufficient evidence that high-order attention is the key factor driving performance gains. The authors report a 3.5% improvement over their 2-modality model, but they don't demonstrate whether this improvement comes from the high-order attention specifically or from other components of their system. For instance, the paper mentions using a lower embedding dimension (512 vs. 2048) compared to other methods, which could explain the performance difference rather than the high-order attention mechanism.

The paper's claim that "all of these approaches model attention as a single network" is inaccurate. Recent work by Kim et al. (2017) and others explicitly models multi-modal attention as a probabilistic model with high-order interactions, directly contradicting the authors' novelty claim. The authors also don't provide theoretical analysis of the benefits of high-order attention, nor do they discuss computational complexity or scalability.

Furthermore, the paper's comparison against baselines is insufficient. The authors only report results for the "test-dev" and "test-std" datasets, but don't provide error bars or statistical significance tests for their improvements. They also don't compare against the most recent state-of-the-art VQA approaches from the 2017 conference, which would be expected for a NIPS submission.

The paper's theoretical claims are overstated. While they present a probabilistic model with mean-field inference, this approach bears significant resemblance to existing work on bilinear pooling (Fukui et al., 2016) and structured attention (Kim et al., 2017). The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from these existing methods in a meaningful way.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents a technically sound approach that is well-executed, but the novelty claims are unsubstantiated and the evidence provided is insufficient to support the authors' claims. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison against a broader set of baselines, including recent state-of-the-art methods from 2017. The authors should conduct ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the high-order attention mechanism and provide theoretical justification for why it improves performance.

Without these critical improvements, the paper fails to demonstrate that its proposed high-order attention mechanism provides a significant advancement over existing approaches. The paper is more of an incremental improvement than a novel contribution, and the evidence provided does not meet NIPS' standards for methodological rigor.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Clear presentation of the method
- Good qualitative visualizations of attention patterns
- Theoretical grounding in probabilistic modeling

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Insufficient comparison against relevant baselines
- Lack of ablation studies to isolate the high-order attention mechanism's contribution
- Overstated novelty claims that contradict existing work
- No theoretical analysis of why high-order attention improves performance
- Incomplete evaluation (lacks statistical significance tests)

Given these concerns, I recommend rejection. The paper makes a marginal contribution to an already crowded area of research, with insufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and effectiveness. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper with more rigorous comparisons, ablation studies, and theoretical analysis to meet NIPS' standards for methodological rigor and scientific contribution.
