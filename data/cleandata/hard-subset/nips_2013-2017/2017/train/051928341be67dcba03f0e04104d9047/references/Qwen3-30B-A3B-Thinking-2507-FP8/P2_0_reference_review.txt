

# Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that learns high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., image, question, answer) for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors formalize attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and higher-order potentials, which they compute using mean-field inference. They demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, with a relatively simple model (40 million parameters) compared to previous methods (70+ million parameters). The paper builds upon existing attention mechanisms but extends them to model correlations beyond pairwise interactions, which they argue is crucial for multimodal tasks.

The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work (notably Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7]), arguing that their method is easily extendable to any number of modalities, unlike previous approaches that are limited to two modalities. They provide comprehensive experimental results on VQA, including both quantitative metrics and qualitative visualizations of attention patterns.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a well-structured probabilistic model. The mean-field inference approach for computing attention probabilities is standard and well-justified. The authors provide sufficient technical details about the model architecture, training procedures, and hyperparameters, making the work reproducible.

The claims are well-supported by both quantitative results (Table 1 shows improvements over baselines on VQA test-dev and test-std datasets) and qualitative analysis (Fig. 5-8). The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the modest improvement over previous state-of-the-art (approximately 0.7% absolute gain) and the fact that their 3-modality model outperforms a 2-modality version, suggesting that high-order attention is beneficial but not always essential.

However, the paper doesn't thoroughly address the computational cost of the high-order attention mechanism. While they mention the ternary potential requires a 3D tensor operation, they don't provide any analysis of how this affects training or inference time compared to pairwise attention models. This is a notable omission for a paper claiming to present a practical approach.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical concepts are explained clearly with helpful visualizations (Fig. 3-4 illustrate the unary, pairwise, and ternary attention modules). The paper follows a logical flow from problem statement to related work, proposed method, experimental evaluation, and conclusion.

The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the figures effectively complement the text. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results. The only minor clarity issue is that the paper doesn't explicitly state why high-order attention is necessary for VQA, which could be better justified.

## Originality

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by formalizing attention as a probabilistic model with high-order potentials. While pairwise attention has been explored in previous work (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25]), the explicit modeling of third-order (and potentially higher) correlations between modalities is novel. The authors properly situate their work in the related work section, clearly distinguishing it from prior attention mechanisms.

The claim that their approach is "generally applicable" to any number of modalities is well-supported by the model's architecture, though the paper only evaluates on VQA (3 modalities). This generality is a significant contribution to the attention literature.

## Significance

The paper advances the state of the art in VQA with a modest but consistent improvement over previous methods. While the absolute performance gain (0.7% on test-dev) is small, it's achieved with a simpler model (40M parameters vs. 70M+ for some competitors), which is practically significant. The high-order attention framework could potentially be applied to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA.

The paper's main contribution is the principled probabilistic framework for multimodal attention, which provides a new perspective on how to model interactions between different data modalities. This conceptual contribution is likely to be valuable for future work in multimodal learning.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clear problem statement and motivation for high-order attention
- Well-structured and well-written paper with excellent visualizations
- Comprehensive related work that properly situates the contribution
- Good experimental evaluation with both quantitative and qualitative results
- The approach is simple yet effective, achieving state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters
- The probabilistic interpretation of attention as a mean-field inference problem is well-motivated

**Weaknesses:**
- The improvement over previous state-of-the-art is relatively modest (0.7% absolute gain)
- The necessity of high-order attention (beyond pairwise) for VQA isn't fully justified; qualitative results (Fig. 5-7) suggest pairwise attention often suffices
- Computational cost of high-order attention isn't thoroughly analyzed
- The paper claims generality to "any number of modalities" but only evaluates on VQA (3 modalities)
- Comparison to recent VQA methods (e.g., 2017 VQA challenge winners) is limited

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contribution is novel and significant within the context of attention mechanisms for multimodal learning. While the performance improvement is modest, it's achieved with a simpler model and provides a principled framework for high-order attention that could inspire future work. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and properly contextualized within the literature.

The main concern is that the performance gain is relatively small, but this is a common challenge in the VQA field where improvements are often incremental. The conceptual contribution of modeling attention through high-order potentials is substantial and likely to be impactful for the community.

The authors should consider addressing the computational cost of their approach and providing more justification for why high-order attention is necessary in VQA (beyond the simple claim that it "improves performance") in a revised version. These are minor issues that could be addressed in a camera-ready version.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of multimodal learning that advances our understanding of attention mechanisms. It meets the quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria for NIPS.
