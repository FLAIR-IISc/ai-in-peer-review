

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., visual, textual) for visual question answering. The authors introduce a probabilistic framework with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture interactions between modalities, extending beyond traditional pairwise attention mechanisms. The approach is applied to VQA, achieving state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset. The paper builds upon related work in attention mechanisms for VQA, particularly Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016), but argues that their high-order approach is more generalizable to multiple modalities.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper presents a well-motivated extension of attention mechanisms to higher-order correlations. The probabilistic framework with mean-field inference to compute marginal probabilities is clearly explained and technically sound. The authors provide a thorough description of how unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials are computed, with helpful visualizations (Fig. 3).

2. **Empirical Results**: The approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA benchmark (64.57% on test-dev), demonstrating the practical value of their high-order attention mechanism. The qualitative analysis (Figs. 5-7) effectively shows how their model attends to relevant regions in images based on questions.

3. **Generalizability**: The paper successfully demonstrates how their approach can be extended to any number of data modalities, which is a significant advantage over existing methods that struggle with more than two modalities.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a theoretical argument for why higher-order correlations are necessary for VQA. The authors claim their approach "learns high-order correlations" but don't convincingly explain why these correlations are more beneficial than pairwise ones for the task. A comparison of model complexity versus performance gains would strengthen this argument.

2. **Related Work Analysis**: The related work section could better differentiate their approach from similar recent work. For instance, the paper mentions Kim et al. (2017) on structured attention networks, but doesn't clarify how their probabilistic approach differs from or improves upon this work. The claim that "attention mechanism with several potentials haven't been discussed in detail yet" needs stronger justification against recent literature.

3. **Computational Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of computational complexity. The authors note that their model has "only 40 million parameters" compared to "over 70 million" in existing methods, but don't discuss inference time or memory requirements. This is important for practical adoption.

4. **Experimental Rigor**: The comparison with baselines is somewhat limited. While they compare against Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016), they don't compare against more recent SOTA methods like the Transformer-based approaches (e.g., Tan & Bansal, 2019) or the multimodal transformer architectures that have since become dominant in VQA. The ablation study comparing MCT versus 2-layer MCB is limited to a single dataset split.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound and well-executed, with solid experimental validation. However, it lacks sufficient theoretical justification for the high-order approach, which weakens the contribution. The model is complete but doesn't fully address the limitations of their approach.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and helpful visualizations. The implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

**Originality**: The high-order attention mechanism is novel in its probabilistic formulation and generalizability to multiple modalities. However, the novelty is somewhat limited by the fact that higher-order interactions have been explored in other contexts (e.g., tensor-based methods), and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from these.

**Significance**: The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA, which is significant. The generalizability to multiple modalities is a valuable contribution. However, the paper doesn't convincingly argue why high-order attention is necessary for VQA beyond empirical gains, limiting the broader impact.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound approach to attention mechanisms with a clear application to VQA. While the empirical results are strong and the generalizability to multiple modalities is promising, the paper would benefit from stronger theoretical justification, more comprehensive comparison with recent SOTA methods, and a clearer differentiation from related work.

**Decision**: Weak Accept (if the authors address the concerns in the revision, particularly regarding theoretical justification and related work).

**Suggestions for Improvement**:

1. Expand the theoretical discussion to better justify why high-order attention is beneficial for VQA, not just empirically but conceptually.

2. Compare against more recent VQA SOTA methods (e.g., Transformer-based approaches) to demonstrate the approach's relevance in current literature.

3. Clarify the relationship between their work and similar approaches like Kim et al. (2017) and the multimodal tensor methods.

4. Provide a more thorough computational analysis (training time, inference time, memory usage) to help practitioners evaluate the practical utility.

5. Consider adding an ablation study showing how much each order of attention (unary, pairwise, ternary) contributes to the final performance.

The paper has strong empirical results and a promising approach, but needs to better articulate the theoretical underpinnings and position its contribution within the broader landscape of attention mechanisms to maximize its impact.
