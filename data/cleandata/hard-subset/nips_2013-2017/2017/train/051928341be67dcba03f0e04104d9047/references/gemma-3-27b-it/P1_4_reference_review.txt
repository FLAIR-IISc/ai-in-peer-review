## Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel, generally applicable attention mechanism that learns high-order correlations between data modalities. The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms often treat modalities in an ad-hoc and entangled manner and lack the ability to model complex interactions. Their approach utilizes unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture these correlations, employing mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities. The effectiveness of the proposed method is demonstrated on the Visual Question Answering (VQA) task, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in attention mechanisms for both image and text, as well as joint attention approaches for multimodal tasks like VQA. It acknowledges related work from Andreas et al. [1], Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25], and Fukui et al. [7], among others. The key novelty lies in extending attention beyond pairwise interactions to encompass higher-order correlations (specifically, up to ternary in this work) in a probabilistic framework, and presenting a readily scalable approach using mean-field inference. This is a natural progression from bilinear models [13] and dual attention mechanisms [19] but aims for greater generality. The comparison to Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7] is particularly relevant as these represent strong baselines in the VQA domain.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly modeling higher-order correlations between modalities via probabilistic potentials is a valuable contribution. This goes beyond simply concatenating or combining features.
*   **Generality:** The framework is presented as being applicable to any number of data modalities, offering a significant advantage over methods tailored to specific pairings.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset is a compelling demonstration of the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper provides a relatively clear explanation of the mathematical formulation, including the use of mean-field inference. The illustrations in Fig. 3 are helpful in understanding the attention mechanisms.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The related work section is extensive and demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature.
* **Analysis of Decision Making Units:** The comparison between MCT and 2-layer MCB is a valuable addition, providing insight into the optimal configuration for this task.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity & Scalability:** While the authors claim scalability, the complexity of modeling and inferring high-order potentials could become a significant bottleneck as the number of modalities increases. While mean-field inference is used, the computational cost is still likely substantial. The parameter count, although reasonable (40M), is still high and the ablation studies could be more detailed.
*   **Limited Visualization Depth:** While visualizations are provided (Fig. 5, 6, 7, 8), they mostly focus on two modalities. Visualizing the ternary attention and its impact on the final decision would be insightful. The failure case (Fig. 8) highlights a weakness but doesn’t provide extensive analysis.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not delve into the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters, such as the embedding dimension (d) or the parameters governing the mean-field inference.
*   **MCB/MCT Reliance:** While the use of MCB/MCT is justified, the performance is heavily reliant on these tensor sketching units. Further investigation into alternative methods for handling high-order correlations in the decision-making stage would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Ablation Studies**: More detailed ablation studies, specifically isolating the contribution of each order of potential (unary, pairwise, ternary) would be highly valuable.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to multimodal attention.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.
*   The work has the potential to impact research in multimodal learning and VQA.

**Against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability of the approach with a growing number of modalities remains a concern.
*   The limited depth of the visualization and analysis hinders a full understanding of the method’s behavior.
*   The reliance on MCB/MCT as a key component might limit the generalizability of the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of multimodal attention. While there are some areas for improvement, particularly regarding scalability analysis and more in-depth visualization, the novelty, clarity, and empirical results make it worthy of acceptance to NIPS. The authors have convincingly demonstrated the benefits of modeling high-order correlations between modalities, and this work opens up new avenues for research in multimodal learning.

**Rating:**  **Accept (Strongly Consider)**

**Confidence:** 8/10
