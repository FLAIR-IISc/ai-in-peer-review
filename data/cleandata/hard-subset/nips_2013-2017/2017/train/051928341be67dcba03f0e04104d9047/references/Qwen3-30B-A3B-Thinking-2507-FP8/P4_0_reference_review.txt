

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., image, question, answer) for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors frame attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and higher-order potentials, using mean-field inference to compute marginal probabilities. This approach differs from prior work (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) that typically models attention as a single network or uses bilinear pooling for pairwise interactions without explicitly modeling higher-order correlations. The paper demonstrates the approach on VQA, achieving state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, with qualitative analysis showing the attention mechanism's interpretability.

## Quality
The technical approach is sound and well-structured. The authors present a clear probabilistic formulation for attention that generalizes beyond pairwise interactions to higher-order correlations. The mean-field inference approach for computing marginal probabilities from high-order potentials is theoretically grounded and well-motivated. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using standard VQA datasets and comparing against relevant baselines (Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]). The paper demonstrates clear performance gains over previous methods, with the 3-modality model outperforming 2-modality variants. The implementation details (hyperparameters, network architecture, training procedure) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The authors acknowledge limitations through a failure case analysis (Fig. 8) showing attention that doesn't adapt to different questions.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulation is presented logically, with helpful schematic illustrations (Figs. 3 and 4) explaining the unary, pairwise, and ternary attention modules. The relation to previous work is well-explained in Section 2, and the paper effectively distinguishes their approach from existing attention mechanisms. The description of the data embedding, attention, and decision-making components (Section 3) flows logically. The qualitative results (Figs. 5-7) effectively illustrate how the attention mechanism works across different questions, enhancing the paper's interpretability. The writing is professional and accessible to readers familiar with attention mechanisms.

## Originality
The paper's main contribution is the formalization of high-order attention models that explicitly capture correlations between multiple data modalities. While some prior work (e.g., [7, 11, 19]) considers interactions between modalities, the authors convincingly argue that their approach is novel in explicitly modeling attention through high-order potentials with mean-field inference, rather than using bilinear pooling or other techniques. The paper correctly identifies the gap in previous work (Section 2) that attention mechanisms are often "hand-crafted for a particular task" and fail to consider multiple modalities explicitly. The extension to any number of modalities is a significant advantage over existing approaches. The authors provide adequate references to related work, though they might have included more recent VQA papers from 2017.

## Significance
The results are significant for the VQA task, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance with a relatively simple model (40 million parameters compared to 70+ million in [7]). The approach advances the state of the art in VQA while providing better interpretability through visualized attention maps. The model's ability to handle multiple modalities with a unified framework could be valuable for other multimodal tasks beyond VQA. The paper provides a clear demonstration of how high-order attention improves performance over pairwise attention, with the 3-modality model significantly outperforming 2-modality variants. The findings suggest that modeling higher-order correlations between modalities is an important direction for future multimodal learning.

## Strengths
- Clear formalization of high-order attention with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials
- Effective comparison with relevant baselines showing state-of-the-art results
- Good qualitative analysis demonstrating the attention mechanism's interpretability
- Well-organized presentation with helpful schematic illustrations
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on standard VQA datasets
- Practical implementation with sufficient detail for reproducibility

## Weaknesses
- The paper could better distinguish their approach from recent work on higher-order interactions (e.g., [13, 19]), which also considers multiple modalities
- The implementation details for the ternary potential computation could be slightly more explicit
- The failure case (Fig. 8) is mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed
- The paper doesn't discuss potential computational overhead of higher-order attention compared to simpler pairwise approaches

## Recommendation
This paper makes a solid technical contribution to attention mechanisms in multimodal learning. The high-order attention model is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear performance improvements over previous approaches on a significant benchmark task. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. While the novelty claim is strong, the paper could be strengthened by more explicitly differentiating from recent work on higher-order interactions. The significance of the work is high, with clear potential for application to other multimodal tasks. I recommend acceptance.
