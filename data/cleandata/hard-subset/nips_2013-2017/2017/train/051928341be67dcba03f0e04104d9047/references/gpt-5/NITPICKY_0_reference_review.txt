Summary
The paper proposes “high-order attention” for multimodal tasks, instantiated on VQA. It models attention as a set of unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over image patches (V), question words (Q), and answers (A). Unary potentials are obtained via 1×1 convolutions (i.e., linear projections + tanh). Pairwise potentials are inner-product similarity matrices C2 between two modalities; ternary potentials are tri-linear correlations C3. For each modality, the final attention is the softmax of a learned linear combination of its unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The attended representations are fused with MCB/MCT (tensor sketch) and passed to an MLP for answer prediction. Experiments on VQA (mostly multiple-choice) are claimed to achieve state-of-the-art.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: leveraging higher-order interactions among modalities, including answers, is sensible in VQA.
- Simple building blocks: inner-product correlations and softmaxed combinations are easy to implement.
- Using tensor sketch for efficient high-order fusion is appropriate and aligns with prior practice (MCB).
- Qualitative visualizations suggest that pairwise/ternary potentials can sharpen attention conditioned on the question/answers.

Major weaknesses (technical/quality)
1) Probabilistic framing and mean-field claims are unsupported. The paper repeatedly refers to potentials and “mean-field inference,” but no energy function, factor graph, or KL objective is specified. The “mean-field” update in Eq. (1) is a learned linear combination followed by softmax, not a derived mean-field fixed point. Without a proper derivation or a precise probabilistic model, the probabilistic interpretation is misleading.

2) Missing/ambiguous definitions and inconsistent notation hinder reproducibility.
- The weights wiv, wiq, wiq,ia used to marginalize C2/C3 are introduced as “convolutions,” but their dimensionality, parameterization, and learning are not defined. Are they shared 1×1 conv kernels per column/row, per modality, or global scalars?
- C2 indexing is inconsistent: defined as QWq(VWv)ᵀ with (i,j) correlation, but later θV,Q(iq) uses (C2)iv,iq (order swapped), and θV,Q(iv) sums over iq with wiq without specifying shapes.
- The same symbol aV is used twice in the attended-modality equations; the third line should be aA.
- The use of Wq, Wv, Wa vs. Wq1/Wq2, etc., is inconsistent across sections.
- smax(·) is not formally defined (over which index; any temperature?).
- “convolve the matrix along the visual dimension using a 1×1 kernel” is not meaningful as written.

3) Computational complexity is not addressed. The ternary correlation tensor C3 has size nq×nv×na. For na=18 (MC setting), this may be feasible; for open-ended na≈3000 (which the paper elsewhere assumes for outputs), this is not. The paper claims trivial extension to k modalities but does not discuss the O(∏i ni d) cost or any low-rank/approximate scheme for computing/marginalizing higher-order tensors.

4) Empirical validation is incomplete and not convincingly supports SOTA claims.
- The key “Tab. 1” with test-dev/test-std numbers is missing. Only one validation comparison is given (MCT 63.82% vs 2-layer MCB 64.57%), with no baselines from the literature in the same setting or standard open-ended metrics.
- No ablation quantifies the contribution of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials, no analysis of learned α/β/γ, and no comparison to strong bilinear/attention baselines under matched settings (e.g., MLB, MFB, MFH).
- The 2-modality model is made intentionally weaker (d=512 vs 2048 in prior work) and then claimed inferior; this is not a fair comparison.
- No runtime/memory measurements, and no analysis of how na affects ternary costs.

5) Violates double-blind policy. A GitHub URL is included that appears to de-anonymize the submission.

Clarity and presentation issues
- Numerous typos and formatting problems: “and and,” “st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...,” figure captions with raw “conv tanh” blocks, broken equations, stray punctuation/spaces, inconsistent hyphenation (k−order), and undefined figure/table references (Tab. 1; Fig. 2–8 not provided here).
- Several reference mismatches/misattributions: e.g., RNNSearch is attributed to Hermann et al. [8] rather than Bahdanau et al. [3]; many entries lack venue/year; some are arXiv only without context.
- The claim “Due to space limitations we omit...” in core methods is not acceptable when the omitted parts are essential for reproducibility.

Originality
- The high-level idea of modeling cross-modal interactions (bilinear co-attention, stacked attention, dual attention) is well studied. Using answers as input was explored by Jabri et al. The main novelty is the explicit ternary correlation tensor and combining modality-specific unary/pairwise/ternary “potentials.” However, without a sound probabilistic derivation or ablations showing clear gains attributable to ternary potentials (not just to using answers), the incremental novelty is unclear.

Significance
- Potentially useful if the ternary attention demonstrably improves VQA and remains scalable, but the current empirical evidence is insufficient. The lack of rigorous comparisons, missing results table, and unclear complexity limit impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting attempt to unify multi-modal attentions via higher-order correlations.
- Practical integration with MCB/MCT; conceptually simple and potentially extensible.
- Qualitative examples indicate meaningful cross-modal focus.

Cons for acceptance
- Probabilistic/mean-field framing is unsubstantiated.
- Key methodological details are missing or inconsistent; notation errors.
- Incomplete/insufficient experiments; missing main results table; unfair baseline.
- Scalability and complexity of ternary attention not addressed.
- Blatant double-blind violation and numerous writing/formatting issues.

Recommendations to authors
- Provide a precise probabilistic model (variables, factors, energy), derive mean-field updates, or drop the probabilistic framing.
- Fully specify all parameters (w· weights), tensor shapes, indexing, and implementation details; fix notation and typos.
- Add thorough ablations (unary vs pairwise vs ternary; with/without answers; α/β/γ analysis), matched baselines, open-ended and MC results, and runtime/memory.
- Discuss scalability to more modalities and large na; consider low-rank/attention-sampling approximations.
- Fix references and ensure strict anonymization.

Recommendation: Reject (promising idea, but currently too many methodological, empirical, and presentation deficiencies; double-blind breach). Confidence: High.
