This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to capture correlations between multiple modalities (image, question, answer). While the idea of explicitly modeling higher-order interactions is intuitively appealing, the presentation lacks the rigor and thorough evaluation expected at NIPS. The paper feels more like a preliminary exploration than a fully-fledged contribution.

The primary weakness lies in the lack of convincing evidence that the proposed “high-order” attention genuinely outperforms simpler, well-established methods. The authors achieve state-of-the-art results, but these are presented without sufficient detail regarding the specific configuration (e.g., embedding dimensions, network architecture beyond the attention mechanism itself) and a comprehensive comparison to strong baselines *with comparable model complexity*. Claiming SOTA while using a lower embedding dimension than previous SOTA approaches (as mentioned in section 4.3) suggests the comparison isn’t fully apples-to-apples. Furthermore, the improvement of 2-modality to 3-modality is shown, but without establishing if 2-modality is competitive.  The described "tensor sketching" units, while attempting to address computational complexity, are also presented without a clear justification for their chosen parameters or a detailed analysis of their impact on performance.

The motivation for high-order attention—addressing "ad-hoc and entangled" representations and task-specific attention—is reasonable. However, the paper doesn't adequately demonstrate *how* the proposed method avoids these pitfalls better than existing attention mechanisms (e.g., bilinear pooling, multi-hop attention). The probabilistic formulation using potentials and mean-field inference is also not novel in itself and the advantages of this specific formulation over others remain unclear.  The discussion of related work is extensive, but occasionally feels like a litany of attention mechanisms without a critical assessment of their limitations in the context of high-order interactions. Specifically, the dismissal of Lu et al. [15]’s approach as difficult to extend to more than two modalities feels somewhat overstated; tensor-based representations readily address this limitation.

The clarity of the paper is also a concern. While the individual components are described, the overall system architecture (Fig. 2) is rather abstract and doesn’t provide a clear understanding of the data flow. The notation is often cumbersome, and the explanation of the mean-field inference process could be more accessible. The qualitative results (Figs. 5-8) are helpful, but their impact is diminished by the lack of a detailed analysis of failure cases and a comparison to the attention maps generated by other methods. The failure case discussed (Fig. 8) highlights a significant limitation—the model can fixate on irrelevant image regions despite different questions, suggesting a lack of robust reasoning.

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of explicitly modeling high-order correlations between modalities is interesting and potentially valuable.
*   The paper provides a comprehensive overview of existing attention mechanisms.
*   The reported state-of-the-art results, if rigorously established, could be a significant contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Insufficiently rigorous comparison to strong baselines with comparable complexity.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of the tensor sketching units and their impact on performance.
*   Limited justification for the chosen probabilistic formulation.
*   Clarity issues in the explanation of the system architecture and mean-field inference.
*   Insufficient analysis of failure cases.
*   The improvement of adding a third modality is shown without comparing against solid 2-modality baselines.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   If the authors can provide a more thorough and convincing comparison to established baselines, demonstrating a statistically significant and practically meaningful improvement.
*   If they can provide a more detailed justification for the design choices made in the tensor sketching units and their impact on performance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The current presentation lacks the rigor and thorough evaluation expected at NIPS.
*   The novelty of the proposed method is incremental, and the benefits are not clearly demonstrated.
*   The clarity issues hinder understanding and reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

This paper presents an interesting idea, but it falls short of the quality standards expected at NIPS. The authors need to significantly strengthen their experimental evaluation, improve the clarity of their presentation, and provide a more compelling justification for their approach. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit to a future conference.
