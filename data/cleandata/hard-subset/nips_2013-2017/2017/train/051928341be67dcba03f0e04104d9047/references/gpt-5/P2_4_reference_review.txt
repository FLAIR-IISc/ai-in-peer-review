Summary
The paper proposes a multimodal attention mechanism that explicitly models unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) modalities. Attention is computed by linearly combining these potentials with learned weights followed by a softmax, which the authors describe as a single mean-field iteration. Pairwise potentials are computed via learned linear projections and inner products (similarity matrix C2), and ternary potentials via a third-order correlation tensor C3. The attended vectors are then fused for decision-making via Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) or Trilinear (MCT) pooling (tensor sketching). On VQA multiple-choice (MC), the method claims state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters, and offers qualitative attention visualizations.

Relation to prior work: The paper sits between co-attention (e.g., Lu et al.) and bilinear pooling (Fukui et al.) lines. The novelty is the unified, explicitly high-order attention over multiple modalities (including answers), and the claim of a probabilistic/mean-field interpretation. It also compares to Jabri et al. who use answers as inputs in VQA-MC.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and generality: Explicit decomposition into unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over modalities is intuitive and potentially extensible to more modalities.
- Empirical promise: On VQA-MC, the approach appears competitive and achieves strong performance with a relatively compact model. The qualitative attention maps are sensible and illustrate the benefit of higher-order terms.
- Practical design: Using tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) for the decision stage is a good engineering choice to avoid the combinatorial blow-up of explicit outer products.
- Relevance: The topic is timely and squarely in scope for NeurIPS (attention, multimodal learning, VQA).

Weaknesses
- Probabilistic justification is thin: The “mean-field” claim is not formalized. There is no clear probabilistic model/energy or variational objective from which Eq. (1) is derived; instead, the method appears to be a learned linear combination of potentials followed by softmax. Without stating the underlying distribution or factor graph and the approximations, the theoretical framing is unconvincing.
- Missing computational analysis for ternary attention: Computing a full C3 over Q×V×A scales as O(d·nq·nv·na). This is tractable for MC (na≈18) but intractable for open-ended classification (na≈3000). The paper should clarify when ternary is used, and how complexity is managed. As written, the “trivial extension to any number of modalities” claim is not supported computationally.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair comparisons: The 2-modality baseline uses smaller embeddings (d=512) than competing methods (often 2048), and the paper attributes its underperformance to this. Strong, apples-to-apples comparisons are needed. The SOTA claim lacks a complete results table in the paper text (numbers vs. prior SOTA for both MC and open-ended settings).
- Ablations are limited: There is no systematic quantification of the contribution of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials, or the learned weights α,β,γ. While MCT vs 2-layer MCB is reported on val, this does not isolate the attention design’s contribution.
- Scope of evaluation: Only VQA is evaluated, despite the generality claim. Even within VQA, open-ended results are not shown, and there is no cross-dataset validation (e.g., Visual7W). No variance or multi-seed reporting.
- Clarity/notation issues and minor errors: Some typos (e.g., duplicate symbol aV in Eq. block), occasional notation overload, and missing details (e.g., exact nq, handling of variable-length answers, when A is used as input vs target).

Quality
- Technically sound at the level of a well-engineered attention variant; however, the probabilistic justification is not rigorous, and key complexity considerations for high-order terms are not addressed. Empirical evaluation is promising but not comprehensive; stronger baselines and ablations are needed to support the core claims.

Clarity
- Generally understandable, but the probabilistic framing and computational footprint of ternary attention need clearer exposition. The paper would benefit from a precise definition of the graphical model/energy, a derivation of the “mean-field” update, and a complexity table. Fix minor notation/typos.

Originality
- Moderate. The idea of modeling pairwise correlations is well-established; extending to ternary attention over V, Q, and A in a unified potential framework is a reasonable and incremental step. Using MCB/MCT for decision-making is known. The novelty is in explicitly tying them together and learning a weighted combination of potentials.

Significance
- Potentially useful for practitioners working on VQA-MC and other multimodal tasks if the high-order attention consistently improves performance. The generality claim is appealing, but the lack of broader evaluations and rigorous analysis tempers impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and practical high-order attention mechanism with good qualitative behavior.
- Competitive VQA-MC performance with fewer parameters.
- Conceptually unifying view that could inspire follow-up work.

Cons for acceptance
- Weak theoretical grounding of the “mean-field” interpretation.
- Insufficient ablations and fairness of comparisons; SOTA claim not fully substantiated in-text.
- Limited evaluation scope (no open-ended results, single dataset).
- Unaddressed computational scaling of ternary potentials beyond MC.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a formal probabilistic model (energy, factors) and derive the mean-field updates; or rephrase the method without overclaiming a variational interpretation.
- Include comprehensive ablations: remove unary/pairwise/ternary terms, vary α/β/γ, and quantify effects on both attention and accuracy.
- Report full quantitative tables for test-dev and test-std (MC and open-ended), with matched visual feature dimensions across baselines.
- Analyze computational cost and memory of C2 and C3; clarify when ternary is used (MC vs open-ended). If necessary, introduce low-rank/Sketching approximations at the attention stage.
- Evaluate on an additional dataset (e.g., Visual7W) and report variance across runs.
- Fix notational typos and improve clarity around answer embeddings and the difference between MC and open-ended pipelines.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a sensible and potentially useful extension of co-attention to higher-order settings and shows promising results, but the theoretical framing is not rigorous and the empirical validation is not yet strong enough (in breadth and fairness) to support a clear accept. With stronger comparisons, clearer derivations, and broader evaluation, this work could merit acceptance.
