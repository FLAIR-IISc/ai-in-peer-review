Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a multimodal attention framework that explicitly models unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions across modalities (image V, question Q, answers A) via “potentials,” and computes marginal attentions per modality by linearly combining these potentials followed by a softmax. Pairwise potentials are built from a learned similarity matrix C2 = QWq(VWv)^T; ternary potentials from a 3-way correlation tensor C3 over V, Q, A. The model claims a mean-field interpretation (one iteration) for producing per-modality attention distributions. The attended features are then combined using tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to perform answer prediction. Experiments are reported on VQA (multiple-choice), with qualitative visualizations and some ablation on MCT vs 2-layer MCB. The authors argue state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters.

Relative to prior work: Co-attention and bilinear pooling have been explored (Lu et al. 2016; Fukui et al. 2016; Kim et al. on bilinear/hadamard pooling; Nam et al. on dual attention; Kim et al. on structured attention). The novelty here is an explicit high-order attention formulation across any number of modalities, including using answer candidates as a third modality in the attention mechanism, and a simple combination rule with learned reliability weights for different potential orders. The decision module leverages known compact bilinear/trilinear pooling.

Quality:
- Soundness: The core idea—explicit high-order potentials for attention—is reasonable and well-motivated. However, the “mean-field” claim is not fully supported. The paper does not define a concrete probabilistic graphical model (energy, partition function, factorization assumptions) from which the linear combination + softmax emerges as a principled single mean-field step. As written, the potentials are neural features and the attention is a learned weighted sum plus softmax; calling this mean-field is potentially misleading without derivation.
- Method details: The construction of pairwise/ternary potentials is clear at a high level (inner products in a shared d-dim space), but several implementation details are underspecified: (i) the “1×1 convolution along a dimension” used to marginalize over C2/C3 is vague; what are the exact tensor shapes and filters? Are w_iv and w_iq shared or position-dependent? (ii) Computational/memory complexity of forming C3 is nontrivial; with na equal to the number of answer candidates per question (18), it may be manageable, but the paper should make this explicit and outline the efficient computation strategy. (iii) The role and learned values of α, β, γ (reliability weights) are not analyzed; showing learned weight magnitudes would support claims about which potentials matter.
- Empirical support: The paper claims state-of-the-art results on VQA multiple-choice with fewer parameters, but the excerpt does not show the actual numbers in Table 1 nor confidence intervals. There is one small ablation (MCT vs 2-layer MCB, ~0.75% diff). Missing are stronger ablations isolating contributions of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials, and 2-modality vs 3-modality under comparable settings, as well as comparisons controlling for CNN backbone (VGG vs ResNet) and embedding dimension. Without these, it is difficult to attribute gains to the high-order attention mechanism rather than to pooling choices or other training details.
- Scope of evaluation: Only VQA multiple-choice is considered. It would strengthen the case to also report open-ended VQA or other datasets (e.g., Visual7W), or to at least justify why the 3-modality mechanism is intrinsically tied to multiple-choice.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and well-organized, with useful figures and qualitative attention maps. However, the probabilistic framing is underspecified (see above). Notation occasionally slips (e.g., repeated symbols, minor typos), and the convolution/marginalization operations over correlation tensors should be described more precisely. A complexity discussion would improve clarity. The code link is a plus for reproducibility.

Originality:
- Moderate. While pairwise co-attention and bilinear pooling are established, explicitly modeling and learning k-th order attention potentials across modalities, including using answers as a conditioning modality in attention, is a meaningful extension. The decision module (MCB/MCT) itself is not novel. The “mean-field” view is intriguing but needs a firmer grounding to be considered a genuine theoretical innovation.

Significance:
- Potentially significant for multimodal reasoning if the reported improvements are robust and attributable to the high-order attention. The framework is conceptually general and could extend to more modalities. However, the empirical evidence provided here is limited (MC VQA only, modest ablation). Broader and more controlled comparisons would make the impact clearer.

Pros:
- Clear, intuitive framework for high-order multimodal attention; easy to extend conceptually.
- Uses compact bilinear/trilinear pooling to keep the decision module scalable.
- Qualitative visualizations are compelling; code availability is helpful.
- Potential for improved performance with fewer parameters.

Cons:
- Probabilistic/mean-field claims not rigorously derived.
- Missing key implementation and complexity details for pairwise/ternary potentials.
- Limited ablations; unclear contribution of ternary vs pairwise components.
- Evaluation limited to VQA multiple-choice; fairness of SOTA claim hard to assess without full table and controlled backbone settings.

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- Provide a formal probabilistic model (energy function) and derive the mean-field updates that lead to Eq. (1), or reframe the method without the mean-field claim.
- Clarify the exact computations for θV,Q and θV,Q,A: tensor shapes, parameter sharing, and how “convolutions along dimensions” are implemented. Include time/memory complexity and how C3 is computed efficiently when na varies.
- Add ablations: remove pairwise, remove ternary, vary α/β/γ (learned vs fixed), and report learned weights to show which potentials drive performance.
- Control for CNN backbone and embedding size across baselines; report both open-ended and MC results if possible.
- Evaluate on another dataset or at least on VQA with different backbones to demonstrate robustness.
- Include quantitative comparisons to structured attention and bilinear pooling baselines under matched settings.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper proposes a simple, general idea—explicit high-order attention potentials across modalities—that is plausible and potentially impactful for multimodal learning. However, it needs a clearer derivation (or a toned-down claim) and stronger empirical isolation of where the gains come from. With revisions addressing clarity and experimental rigor, this could be a solid contribution.
