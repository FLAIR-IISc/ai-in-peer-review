

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper introduces a novel "high-order attention" mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically targeting visual question answering (VQA). The authors propose modeling attention through unary, pairwise, and higher-order potentials that capture correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer). Unlike existing approaches that typically focus on pairwise attention (e.g., [15, 25]), their framework extends to k-th order correlations between k modalities, using a probabilistic model with mean-field inference to obtain marginal attention probabilities. The approach is applied to VQA, where it achieves state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Conceptual Novelty:** The paper's most significant contribution is the conceptual framework of high-order attention. While prior work (e.g., [7, 15]) focuses on pairwise interactions between modalities, the authors successfully extend the attention mechanism to model higher-order correlations in a principled probabilistic manner. This represents a genuine conceptual shift in how attention mechanisms can be designed for multimodal learning.

2. **General Applicability:** The framework is designed to be modular and extendable to any number of data modalities, which is a compelling advantage over existing approaches that are often tailored to specific tasks or modalities.

3. **Clear Exposition:** The paper is well-written with excellent visualizations (Fig. 3-8) that effectively illustrate the unary, pairwise, and ternary attention mechanisms. The mathematical formulation is clear and well-motivated.

4. **Competitive Results:** Despite using a relatively simple architecture (40M parameters vs. 70M+ in some baselines), the approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA benchmarks, with consistent improvements over pairwise attention models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Marginal Performance Gains:** While the approach is novel, the performance improvements over strong baselines are modest (0.7% on validation set with 2-layer MCB vs. MCT). The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of when and why higher-order attention provides significant advantages.

2. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks strong theoretical arguments for why higher-order correlations are necessary or beneficial for VQA. The empirical results show improvement but don't fully explain the underlying mechanisms.

3. **Incomplete Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive ablation study to determine the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) to the final performance.

4. **Comparison to Similar Work:** While the paper cites related work on bilinear models (Kim et al. [13]) and multimodal pooling (Fukui et al. [7]), it could better distinguish how their probabilistic approach differs from these alternatives.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a genuinely novel contribution to the field of multimodal learning through its high-order attention framework. As a reviewer who prioritizes conceptual novelty over marginal performance gains, I find the high-order attention mechanism to be a bold and promising direction that could inspire future work in multimodal learning. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and demonstrates the approach's potential on a challenging benchmark.

While the performance improvements are modest, they are consistent and achieved with a simpler architecture than some competitors. The conceptual contribution appears significant enough to warrant acceptance, particularly given that the authors have demonstrated its effectiveness on a standard benchmark and provided a clear path for extension to other modalities and tasks.

The paper's main weakness is that it doesn't fully establish why higher-order attention is necessary for VQA (beyond showing it works), but this is a common limitation in novel approach papers that prioritize conceptual innovation over theoretical rigor. Given the novelty of the approach and its clear potential for broader impact beyond VQA, I believe this paper deserves acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept (Strong Accept)
