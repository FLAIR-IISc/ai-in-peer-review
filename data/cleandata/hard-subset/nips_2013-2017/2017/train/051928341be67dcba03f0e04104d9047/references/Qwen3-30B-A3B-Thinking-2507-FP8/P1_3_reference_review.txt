

# Peer Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that explicitly models correlations between multiple data modalities (visual, textual, and answer) beyond the typical pairwise interactions. The authors introduce unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture different orders of correlations between modalities, using mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities. They demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, outperforming previous attention-based methods like those of Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016). The paper is well-structured, with clear explanations of the proposed mechanism and thorough experimental validation.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Conceptual Contribution**: The paper's core contribution—explicitly modeling high-order correlations between multiple modalities—represents a meaningful conceptual advance over existing attention mechanisms that typically handle only pairwise interactions. The authors clearly articulate how their approach extends beyond previous work, which is limited to two modalities.

2. **Clear Formulation**: The probabilistic formulation of attention with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials is well-motivated and clearly presented. The mean-field inference approach provides a principled way to combine different types of attention, and the implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the VQA benchmark, with a modest but consistent improvement over previous methods. The authors provide thorough ablation studies showing the importance of high-order attention (comparing 2-modality vs. 3-modality models) and analyze different decision-making units (MCT vs. 2-layer MCB).

4. **Good Practical Considerations**: The authors carefully address computational concerns by using tensor sketching techniques to avoid the exponential parameter growth that would occur with high-order correlations. Their implementation choices (e.g., embedding dimension, dropout rates) are well-justified.

5. **Qualitative Analysis**: The visualizations in Figures 5-7 provide compelling evidence that their attention mechanism focuses on meaningful regions of the image and relevant question words, with the combined attention being question-dependent.

## Weaknesses

1. **Marginal Performance Improvement**: While the paper claims state-of-the-art performance, the improvement over previous methods is relatively small (approximately 0.5-1% on the VQA test-dev set). Given the significance of the contribution, the authors should provide a more thorough analysis of why this small improvement is meaningful and how it translates to real-world impact.

2. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification**: The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of why high-order correlations are beneficial for VQA. A more rigorous discussion of the representational power of high-order attention mechanisms would strengthen the paper's contribution. For instance, how does the ternary potential capture interactions that pairwise potentials cannot?

3. **Limited Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper cites and compares to work from 2016-2017, but misses more recent approaches to high-order attention mechanisms. For example, the paper by Kim et al. (2017) on "Structured Attention Networks" also explores higher-order interactions, which should be discussed in relation to this work.

4. **Sparse Failure Analysis**: While Figure 8 shows a failure case, the paper doesn't adequately analyze why the model failed in this scenario. A deeper investigation into the limitations of their approach would provide valuable insights for future work.

5. **Implementation Details**: Some implementation details could be more thorough. For instance, the paper mentions using VGG-19 and ResNet for image embeddings but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of their performance. The choice of embedding dimension (512 vs. 2048) is mentioned, but the impact on model performance isn't fully explored.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multimodal learning by introducing a novel high-order attention mechanism that explicitly models correlations between multiple data modalities. While the performance improvements are modest, the conceptual advance is significant and the approach is generalizable to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors have carefully evaluated both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach. The marginal performance gain is a limitation, but the conceptual contribution and generalizability of the approach make it a valuable addition to the literature.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. The conceptual novelty of high-order attention mechanisms is significant, and the authors have provided a well-executed study demonstrating the approach's effectiveness. While the performance gains are modest, the paper's contribution to the theoretical understanding of attention mechanisms in multimodal learning is substantial.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Provide a more detailed analysis of why high-order attention is beneficial for VQA, including theoretical justification.

2. Compare the approach more thoroughly to recent work on higher-order interactions (e.g., Kim et al. 2017).

3. Expand the failure analysis in Figure 8 with a deeper investigation into why the model failed in that specific case.

4. Include a more detailed comparison of different image embedding methods (VGG vs. ResNet) and their impact on performance.

5. Consider adding a discussion of how the approach might generalize to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal learning by providing a principled approach to modeling high-order correlations between data modalities, and I believe it will be of interest to the NIPS community.
