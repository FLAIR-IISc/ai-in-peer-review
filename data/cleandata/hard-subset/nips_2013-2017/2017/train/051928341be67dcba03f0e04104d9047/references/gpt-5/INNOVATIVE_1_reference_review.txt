Summary
This paper proposes a unified, high-order attention framework for multimodal reasoning, instantiated on VQA. The key idea is to model attention as a probabilistic system with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over modalities (image V, question Q, answers A), compute correlations via inner products (C2, C3), and obtain marginal attentions via a single mean-field step (linear combination of potentials followed by softmax). The approach is conceptually extensible to k modalities. Decision-making combines attended features using tensor-sketch-based pooling (MCB/MCT) to capture high-order interactions efficiently. On VQA multiple-choice, the method reports state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters than some baselines, and provides qualitative visualizations indicating question- and answer-conditioned attentions.

Relation to prior work
- Co-attention models (e.g., Lu et al. 2016) and bilinear pooling (Fukui et al. 2016) model pairwise interactions between image and question; this paper extends to explicit high-order (ternary) interactions and formulates attention as a potential-based probabilistic model.
- Structured attention (Kim et al. 2017) introduces attention as latent variables with inference; the novelty here is high-order cross-modal potentials and a simple mean-field parameterization over modalities, including answers as inputs for MC VQA.
- The decision pooling stage reuses known MCB/MCT machinery; the contribution is in the attention formulation rather than the pooling.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual framing: attention as a learnable combination of unary, pairwise, and higher-order potentials, with a clear path to k-modality extension. This is a bold, general idea likely to be useful beyond VQA.
- Ternary attention (V-Q-A) is well-motivated for multiple-choice VQA and appears to yield meaningful gains over two-modality baselines.
- Empirically strong: claims state-of-the-art MC VQA performance with substantially fewer parameters than comparable models; qualitative visualizations convincingly show modality-conditioned focus.
- Practical: using one mean-field iteration and 1×1 convolutions keeps the model simple and scalable; MCB/MCT allows high-order feature interactions without explosion in parameters.
- Reproducibility: code link and concrete hyperparameters are provided.

Weaknesses
- Limited ablation and analysis: there is no systematic evaluation of the contribution of each potential (unary vs pairwise vs ternary), nor of the learnable α/β/γ weights. The comparison between 2-modality and 3-modality models confounds embedding dimensionality (512 vs 2048), making it hard to isolate the effect of higher-order attention.
- Probabilistic/inference story is shallow: “mean-field” reduces to a single learned linear combination plus softmax; no exploration of iterative inference or its impact on performance/interpretability.
- Scope of evaluation: only multiple-choice VQA is reported; open-ended accuracy (where answers are not inputs) is not evaluated. Additional datasets (VQA 2.0, Visual7W) would bolster claims of generality.
- Efficiency/complexity: while na is small for MC VQA, computing and marginalizing C3 could be costly; runtime/memory analysis is missing.
- Clarity issues and minor errors: some equations contain typos (e.g., aA incorrectly written as aV in the attended-vector definitions), some notation (weights w⋅ in marginalization) is not fully specified, and references are incomplete.
- Comparisons: beyond Lu et al. and MCB, comparisons to more recent bilinear/compact pooling variants (e.g., low-rank bilinear, MFB/MFH) and strong baselines (e.g., Show, Ask, Attend with tuned settings) are limited for the timeframe.

Quality
The core idea is technically sound and implemented cleanly. Claims are supported by empirical results and consistent qualitative evidence, but the lack of thorough ablations and broader evaluations weakens the case for the specific modeling choices (especially the necessity of ternary potentials vs better pairwise models). The probabilistic framing could be strengthened with more rigorous justification or analysis.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-organized, with helpful figures. However, notation for potential marginalization should be clarified; equations contain a few typos; and references need proper formatting. Providing explicit tensor shapes and computational costs would improve clarity.

Originality
High. Explicitly modeling and combining unary/pairwise/ternary attention potentials across modalities, with a simple mean-field mechanism and demonstrated 3-modality attention, is a notable conceptual step beyond pairwise co-attention. While tensor-sketch pooling itself is not new, its use here complements the new attention design.

Significance
Moderate to high. The framework is general and could influence future multimodal attention designs. Demonstrated gains on VQA MC with fewer parameters are promising. Broader validation (datasets, tasks) and deeper analysis would elevate the impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations removing each potential type and/or zeroing α/β/γ groups; report the learned weights to show which potentials matter.
- Control for embedding dimension when comparing 2- vs 3-modality variants; tune strong pairwise baselines fairly.
- Evaluate on open-ended VQA (and VQA 2.0), and optionally Visual7W; discuss using ternary potentials when answers are not inputs.
- Analyze runtime and memory, especially for C3; discuss scalability to more modalities.
- Explore multiple mean-field iterations or alternative inference schemes.
- Clean up typos/equations; fully specify w⋅ tensors and dimensions; complete references.

Pros
- Conceptually novel and general high-order attention framework.
- Strong empirical performance with fewer parameters.
- Clear qualitative evidence of meaningful, modality-conditioned attention.
- Simple, extensible design; code provided.

Cons
- Limited ablations and scope of evaluation; open-ended VQA missing.
- Probabilistic/inference justification is light; no iterative analysis.
- Some clarity and presentation issues; incomplete comparisons.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline to weak accept). Despite some experimental and clarity gaps, the paper introduces a fresh, general direction for multimodal attention with promising results. The novelty and potential for impact outweigh the current shortcomings, which are addressable. Confidence: medium.
