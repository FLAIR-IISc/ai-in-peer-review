This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to address limitations in existing attention models by learning correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer). The core idea is to move beyond pairwise interactions to consider higher-order potentials, which are then incorporated into a probabilistic model and solved via mean-field inference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a known limitation of existing attention mechanisms – their often ad-hoc and task-specific nature. The concept of high-order correlations feels natural and potentially powerful for multimodal reasoning tasks. The authors correctly position their work within the existing VQA literature, providing a comprehensive overview of related approaches including image and textual attention, as well as joint attention mechanisms. The detailed explanation of the mathematical formulation, including the use of potentials and mean-field inference, is a strength, demonstrating a strong technical foundation.  The ablation studies comparing the different components (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) and the different decision-making units (MCT vs MCB) are valuable for understanding the contribution of each aspect of the proposed method. The experimental results on the VQA dataset are compelling, showcasing improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods. The qualitative results, demonstrating the attention maps, provide further insight into the model’s behavior.

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. The notation is occasionally dense and could benefit from clearer explanations. While the mathematical formulation is detailed, the intuition behind *why* mean-field inference is chosen over other approximation techniques isn't fully articulated.  The paper lacks a thorough discussion of computational complexity. While the authors mention the parameters of their models, they don't analyze the computational cost of the mean-field inference step, which is likely the most expensive part of the process, especially as the number of modalities increases. It's unclear how this scales with more complex data or larger datasets. The comparison to baselines is somewhat limited. While the authors compare to Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], there are other recent VQA models (e.g., those leveraging transformers) that should be included for a more comprehensive assessment. The failure case analysis, while highlighting a limitation, is brief. A more in-depth investigation of failure modes and potential reasons would strengthen the paper. The claim of "trivial extension to any number of modalities" needs to be qualified with a discussion of computational costs and potential diminishing returns as the order of the interactions increases. The role of the answer modality (A) in the attention mechanism, particularly for the ternary potentials, feels somewhat under-explored. Is the answer being used to *guide* the attention over the image and question, or is it simply another input modality? Clarifying this would be beneficial.  Finally, the code is not publicly available, hindering reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   State-of-the-art performance on VQA.
*   Novel and well-motivated approach to multimodal attention.
*   Strong technical foundation with a detailed mathematical formulation.
*   Comprehensive ablation studies.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity is not thoroughly discussed.
*   Limited comparison to recent VQA models.
*   Insufficient analysis of failure modes.
*   Lack of code availability.
*   The intuition for certain design choices is not fully explained (e.g., mean-field inference).



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal attention and VQA. The proposed high-order attention mechanism is a promising approach for capturing complex interactions between data modalities. However, the weaknesses mentioned above prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to address the concerns regarding computational complexity, baseline comparison, failure mode analysis, and code availability. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the research community. Specifically, a section outlining the runtime complexity of the inference process, a comparison with transformer-based VQA models, and publicly released code would be highly valuable.
