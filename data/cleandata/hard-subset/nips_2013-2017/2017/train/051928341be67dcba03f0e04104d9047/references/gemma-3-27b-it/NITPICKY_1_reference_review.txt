This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to address limitations in existing attention approaches by learning correlations between multiple data modalities. While the idea of extending attention to higher-order interactions is conceptually interesting and potentially beneficial, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and lacks sufficient rigor in its presentation, ultimately hindering a positive assessment.

The introduction establishes the motivation adequately, but the phrasing is often imprecise. For instance, the claim that present-day attention mechanisms are "hand-crafted for a particular task" is an oversimplification; many modern attention mechanisms are designed to be task-agnostic, requiring only adaptation of input embeddings. 

The related work section is extensive but poorly organized. While comprehensive, it feels like a laundry list of papers rather than a focused discussion. There is a repetitive pattern of stating limitations of existing work, immediately followed by the claim that the proposed method overcomes them, without sufficient justification. Several citations are missing years (e.g., [6] is cited without a year). More critically, the discussion often lacks depth. The distinction between MCB and MCT is stated but not explained clearly – a reader unfamiliar with these concepts would struggle to understand the improvement. The claim that the proposed approach is “easy to extend to any number of data modalities” needs further substantiation; scalability isn't simply asserted, it needs to be demonstrated or argued.

Section 3, detailing the higher-order attention models, is the core of the paper and also the most problematic. The notation is inconsistent and often unclear. The use of multiple subscripts (e.g., iv, iq, ia) is cumbersome. The equations (1) are poorly motivated. Why a linear combination? Why those specific parameters (αi, βi, γi)? The justification for the soft-max function's role as a "single mean-field iteration" is weak.  The explanation of the convolutional operations within the unary potentials lacks sufficient detail; stating they are equivalent to matrix multiplications doesn't clarify their purpose in the context of the attention mechanism. The motivation for the 1x1 convolutions is also missing. The description of the pairwise and ternary potential calculations is convoluted and lacks clarity. The correlation functions “corr2” and “corr3” appear without adequate explanation; are these simply dot products? The figures (3 & 4) provide limited visual aid, and the labeling is vague. The lack of mathematical rigor makes it difficult to assess the correctness and potential limitations of the proposed approach.

The experimental setup (Section 4) is also flawed. The hyperparameter choices are stated without justification. The choice of RMSProp with specific parameters (α, ε) feels arbitrary. The dropout rates are mentioned but their specific placement and rationale are unclear. The justification for the embedding dimension of 512 is weak, especially when compared to higher dimensions used in competing models.  The claim of achieving state-of-the-art performance is unsubstantiated; the exact numbers are missing for comparison with existing methods, and the table (Tab. 1) is referenced but not included in the provided text. The discussion of the MCB vs MCT comparison lacks statistical significance, citing only a small difference on the validation set. The qualitative results are presented without any quantitative analysis; simply stating that the attention "reacts" to relevant image areas is not sufficient evidence of effectiveness. The failure case (Fig. 8) is acknowledged, but not analyzed – why did the model fail in this instance?

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   **Lack of Rigor:** The paper lacks mathematical precision and sufficient justification for key design choices.
*   **Poor Clarity:** The writing is convoluted, inconsistent, and often unclear.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** Hyperparameter choices and experimental setup are poorly explained.
*   **Missing Results:** Critical experimental results (Tab. 1) are not included.
*   **Weak Justification for Claims:**  Many claims, such as state-of-the-art performance and the benefits of higher-order attention, are not adequately supported.
*   **Organization issues:** Related work section lacks focus and clear connection to the presented method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty of Idea:** The concept of extending attention mechanisms to higher-order correlations is potentially interesting.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The reported state-of-the-art performance, *if accurate*, is a positive aspect (but requires verification).

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting idea, the numerous flaws in presentation, rigor, and experimental detail outweigh the novelty of the approach. This paper, as currently written, is not suitable for acceptance to NIPS. A major revision addressing the points outlined above is required before reconsideration. The paper requires significantly more clarity, mathematical rigor, and comprehensive experimental evaluation.
