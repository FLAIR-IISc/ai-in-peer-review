- Proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order (k-way) correlations between modalities rather than only pairwise interactions.
- Applies the high-order attention framework to VQA (image, question, answer) and reports state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.
- Uses mean-field inference to estimate marginals from high-order potentials, enabling extension to an arbitrary number of modalities.
- Employs tensor sketching to mitigate the computational cost of high-order tensors and improve scalability.
- Positions the work well relative to prior attention methods, differentiating from pairwise attention and bilinear models.
- Connects appropriately to recent probabilistic attention modeling and references tensor sketching literature.
- Strength: clear novelty in explicitly modeling higher-order multimodal interactions within attention.
- Strength: framework is general and potentially applicable to many multimodal tasks beyond VQA.
- Strength: strong quantitative results demonstrating improvement over existing techniques.
- Strength: qualitative visualizations of attention maps help interpret model behavior and show advantages over other attention methods.
- Strength: theoretical grounding by framing attention as a probabilistic model with potentials and mean-field inference.
- Weakness: ablation studies are limited—insufficient analysis of the specific impact of ternary (and higher-order) potentials.
- Weakness: claims about 2-layer MCB vs MCT need clearer justification and architectural explanation.
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity and tuning procedures (learning rates, dropout, etc.).
- Weakness: failure case analysis is sparse; common failure modes and reasons (e.g., fixation on irrelevant objects) are not thoroughly explored.
- Weakness: implementation details (e.g., LSTM architecture for question embedding) are too concise, hindering reproducibility.
- Weakness: comparisons lack more recent stronger VQA baselines; re-running experiments against newer models would strengthen claims.
- Weakness: potential for mean-field over-smoothing is not discussed or mitigated.
- Pro argument: novel, theoretically sound, generalizable, computationally addressed, and empirically strong—merits acceptance.
- Con argument: needs more ablations, hyperparameter and implementation details, stronger baselines, and discussion of inference pitfalls.
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution recommended for Accept with minor revisions to add ablation studies and hyperparameter sensitivity discussion.
