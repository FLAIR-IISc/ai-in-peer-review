Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a high-order attention framework for multimodal reasoning, instantiated for Visual Question Answering (VQA). Attention is modeled as a probabilistic system with unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” defined over modalities (image V, question Q, and, for multiple-choice VQA, candidate answers A). Potentials are combined linearly with learnable weights and normalized by softmax, which the authors frame as a single mean-field iteration yielding marginal attentions PV, PQ, and PA. Unary potentials are 1×1 convs (i.e., linear projections with tanh). Pairwise potentials are computed via correlation matrices (e.g., QWq(VWv)ᵀ), marginalized along the complementary modality to yield per-modality scores. Ternary potentials use a 3-way correlation tensor C3 built by summing triple products across the feature dimension, again marginalized to yield per-modality scores. The attended vectors aV, aQ, aA are then fused for decision making using compact bilinear pooling (MCB) and a trilinear extension (MCT) based on tensor sketching.

Relative to prior work: co-attention and bilinear pooling have been extensively studied (e.g., Lu et al. 2016; Fukui et al. 2016; Kim et al. 2017; Nam et al. 2016). This paper pushes beyond pairwise by explicitly including high-order (3-way) attention couplings and attention over candidate answers (in the multiple-choice setting), which is closer in spirit to Jabri et al. (using answers as inputs), but maintains an attention-centric formulation. The decision fusion builds on MCB and extends it to three modalities via MCT, a straightforward application of tensor sketching for rank-1 trilinear features.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, modular formulation that unifies unary, pairwise, and ternary attentions with learned mixing weights; easy to extend to additional modalities in principle.
- Clear qualitative visualizations showing modality-specific and joint attention behaviors that vary with the question and candidate answers.
- Competitive multiple-choice VQA performance is claimed with fewer parameters, and the ablation between two- and three-modality models suggests value in modeling higher-order interactions.
- Practical use of tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to control the combinatorial explosion in feature dimensions for high-order fusion.

Weaknesses
- Probabilistic framing is superficial: there is no explicit energy or likelihood, no derivation of the mean-field objective, and only one “iteration.” The linear combination plus softmax is better viewed as a learned attention scoring function rather than principled mean-field inference.
- Computational complexity and scalability are under-specified. Computing C3 naively is cubic in sequence lengths and linear in feature dimension. The method may only be tractable because na is small for MC-VQA (≈18), but this is not discussed; it is unclear how the approach scales to open-ended VQA or more modalities.
- Evaluation is limited and somewhat confusing. The paper mixes an MC setting that uses candidate answers as inputs with a standard classification setup using top-3k answers; it is unclear which is used where and how ternary attention is applied. Reported numbers are sparse (e.g., 63.82% vs. 64.57% on val with VGG) and do not convincingly establish state of the art.
- Novelty of MCT is incremental (direct application of tensor sketching to 3-way features). The main novelty rests on including ternary attention potentials and answer attention in MC-VQA; this is useful but relatively narrow.
- Clarity issues and typos: variable misuse (e.g., repeated aV when aA is intended), inconsistent notation (θV,Q.A), and missing details (exact shapes, normalization, and weighting scheme). Some references are incomplete.
- Lack of thorough ablation: no results showing the incremental gain of unary → pairwise → ternary potentials; no runtime or memory profiling; limited comparisons to strong bilinear/low-rank baselines (e.g., MLB, MUTAN, BAN).

Quality
- Technical soundness is mixed. The attention scoring modules themselves are reasonable and implementable, but the probabilistic justification is thin. Efficiency and numerical stability for ternary correlations are not addressed.
- Experimental evidence is not fully sufficient: few quantitative comparisons, unclear test protocol alignment with prior work, and missing open-ended VQA and VQA v2.0 evaluations.

Clarity
- The high-level idea is understandable and figures help, but notation errors and missing implementation details impede reproducibility. The relation between na (18 vs. 3000), and where ternary attention is applied, needs clarification.

Originality
- Moderate. High-order attention potentials and attention over answers in MC-VQA are novel combinations; MCT is a straightforward extension of existing tools.

Significance
- Potentially useful for tasks where multiple modalities interact (e.g., video QA, dialog), but the current evidence focuses on MC-VQA with limited breadth. Broader impact depends on scalability.

Questions for the authors
- How exactly is C3 computed efficiently? Do you instantiate the full tensor or exploit separability/associativity? What are the per-batch memory and FLOP costs for ternary potentials?
- For MC-VQA: is na the number of candidate answers (≈18)? If so, how do you handle the standard open-ended setting where na is large? Do you still use ternary attention there?
- Can you provide a complete ablation isolating the contributions of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, and the learned mixing weights (α, β, γ)?
- How sensitive are results to feature dimension d, CNN backbone, and MCB/MCT output dimension?
- What is the exact SOTA you claim against on test-dev/test-std (numbers and references), and are the protocols matched?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous formulation: define an energy, show how your combination arises from mean-field (or reframe as a learned attention scoring function without probabilistic claims), and consider multiple iterations if tractable.
- Clarify the evaluation protocols and report comprehensive numbers: MC and open-ended VQA (v1 and v2), with standard splits and metrics; include strong baselines (MLB, MUTAN, BAN, DAN, co-attention variants) under matched settings.
- Add efficiency analysis and implementation details for ternary potentials; if you use approximations, describe them; otherwise quantify the cost and discuss scaling to additional modalities.
- Thorough ablations: remove ternary potentials, swap in different fusion modules, vary the mixing weights (or fix them) to assess necessity.
- Improve presentation/notation, fix typos, and provide complete references; release code for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper contains a promising and intuitive idea—explicit high-order attention across modalities—supported by appealing visualizations and some quantitative gains. However, the probabilistic framing is not substantiated, the computational story for ternary attention is unclear, and the empirical evaluation does not convincingly establish significance over strong contemporaneous baselines. Strengthening theory, clarity, scalability, and experiments would make this contribution much more compelling.
