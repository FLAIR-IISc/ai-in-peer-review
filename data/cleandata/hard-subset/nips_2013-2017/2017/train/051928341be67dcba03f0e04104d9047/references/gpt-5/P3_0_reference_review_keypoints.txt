- Proposes multimodal attention modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) for VQA, combined via learnable linear weights and softmax to produce attention.  
- Uses similarity matrices (learned inner products) for pairwise correlations and third-order tensors for ternary correlations, fused with MCB/MCT tensor-sketching for final prediction.  
- Reports state-of-the-art results on VQA v1.0 multiple-choice with fewer parameters than some prior work and provides qualitative attention visualizations and code.  
- Positions itself relative to co-attention, stacked attention, dynamic memory networks, and bilinear pooling; novelty claimed in high-order potentials and including answers as a modality.  
- Strength: conceptual clarity and modular decomposition into unary/pairwise/ternary potentials that cleanly expose multimodal interactions.  
- Strength: formalism easily extends to more than two modalities and produces interesting modality-dependent attention visualizations.  
- Strength: practical use of tensor sketching (MCB/MCT), with training details and code aiding reproducibility.  
- Strength: empirical performance on VQA multiple-choice and some ablation between MCT and 2-layer MCB.  
- Weakness: The “mean-field” probabilistic interpretation is unsupported—no joint energy, factorization, or derivation of update equations are provided.  
- Weakness: Computational and memory feasibility of the ternary tensor C3 (nq × nv × na) is not addressed; no implementation, approximation, or complexity analysis given.  
- Weakness: Comparisons may be unfair—top performance is on multiple-choice using answers as input while many baselines do not use answer choices; open-ended evaluations and matched baselines are missing.  
- Weakness: Ablations are limited and uneven—no systematic study isolating unary vs pairwise vs ternary contributions, no analysis of learned α/β/γ weights, or number of attention glimpses/iterations.  
- Weakness: Notation and clarity issues (typos, inconsistent variable definitions, unclear parameter sharing, vague description of “convolutions” as 1×1 learned sums).  
- Weakness: Evaluation confined to VQA v1 multiple-choice, limiting generality and significance; broader benchmarks (open-ended, VQA v2) are absent.  
- Weakness: Claim of scalability to any number of modalities is questionable without factorized or low-rank schemes, given combinatorial growth of high-order potentials.  
- Quality: Technically sound for attention modules and MCB/MCT use, but probabilistic framing and efficiency of ternary attention are not rigorously justified.  
- Clarity: Paper is readable and organized but needs precise modeling definitions, parameter-sharing details, and explicit computational shapes/memory footprints for reproducibility.  
- Originality: Moderate—extends known pairwise attention and bilinear pooling ideas to a unified high-order view and answer-aware attention, but similar concepts exist in structured attention literature.  
- Significance: Potentially useful for tri-modal problems, but limited experimental scope and missing rigorous analysis reduce impact.  
- Key suggestions: either derive mean-field updates from a defined joint energy or drop probabilistic claims; provide complexity/implementation details for C3 or propose factorized approximations.  
- Key suggestions: add open-ended VQA results and matched-baseline comparisons, ablate unary/pairwise/ternary terms and report learned combination weights, and fix notation/typos with detailed algorithmic descriptions and memory/runtime numbers.  
- Pros summary: modular attention design, strong MC VQA performance, useful qualitative visualizations, practical fusion with MCB/MCT, and code release.  
- Cons summary: unclear/possibly infeasible ternary computation, incomplete and potentially unfair evaluation, unsupported mean-field claim, limited ablations and narrow experimental scope.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject (high reviewer confidence).
