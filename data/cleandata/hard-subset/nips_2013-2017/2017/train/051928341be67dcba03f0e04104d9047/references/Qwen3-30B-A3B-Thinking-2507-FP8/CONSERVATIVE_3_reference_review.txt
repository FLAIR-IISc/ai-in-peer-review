

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a "high-order attention" mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that models correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer) up to third-order. The authors claim their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA by capturing higher-order interactions that existing methods (which typically handle only pairwise interactions) miss.

The technical approach involves a probabilistic attention model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities. The decision-making component uses MCT (Multimodal Compact Trilinear Pooling), an extension of MCB (Multimodal Compact Bilinear Pooling) for three modalities. The authors compare their approach to Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], claiming marginal improvements on VQA datasets.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures illustrating the attention mechanisms.
- The theoretical foundation of using probabilistic potentials for attention is sound and well-explained.
- The qualitative results (Fig. 5-8) demonstrate that the attention mechanism is interpretable and focuses on relevant regions of the image and question.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient validation and comparison:** The paper's claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is unsubstantiated. The authors compare only to two older baselines (Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7] from 2015-2016), while failing to compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods from the 2017 VQA challenge (e.g., the MCB-based models by Xu et al. [25] and others). The reported performance gains are marginal (e.g., 64.57% vs. 63.82% on val dataset), and the paper lacks statistical significance testing to confirm these differences are meaningful.

2. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper fails to provide ablation studies to determine the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) to the final performance. Without this, it's impossible to assess whether high-order attention provides meaningful improvements over simpler pairwise approaches.

3. **Questionable novelty:** The paper's core contribution—modeling higher-order attention—is not truly novel. Kim et al. [14] (2017) already proposed a "structured attention network" that interprets attention as a multi-variate probabilistic model to incorporate structural dependencies. The authors cite this work but fail to clearly differentiate their approach from it. The paper also doesn't adequately address how their method differs from the "dual attention mechanisms" by Nam et al. [19] or the bilinear models by Kim et al. [13].

4. **Misleading parameter count claim:** The authors claim their model has "only 40 million parameters" while "techniques like [Fukui et al.] use over 70 million parameters." This comparison is misleading without context about model architectures and input dimensions. The VGG-based model they describe (with d=512) is likely less expressive than models using higher-dimensional features (e.g., ResNet with d=2048), which would explain the performance gap they observe.

5. **Lack of computational analysis:** The paper doesn't address the computational cost of the high-order attention mechanism compared to pairwise attention. Given the exponential growth of parameters with higher-order interactions, this is a critical omission for practical applications.

**Significance and Impact:**
The marginal performance gains reported don't justify the paper's claim of "state-of-the-art" performance, especially when compared to more recent work. The paper fails to demonstrate why high-order attention is necessary for VQA, as the qualitative results (Fig. 5-8) don't clearly show that third-order interactions provide meaningful improvements over pairwise attention. The authors also don't explore whether their approach generalizes beyond VQA, limiting the broader impact of their work.

**Recommendation:**
This paper falls short of NIPS standards for methodological rigor and evidence. While the attention mechanism is theoretically interesting, the lack of comprehensive comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods, absence of ablation studies, and unsubstantiated claims about performance improvements make it difficult to assess the true contribution of this work. The paper would need to substantially revise its experimental validation to include comparisons to more recent methods, provide ablation studies, and clarify the novelty relative to existing structured attention approaches before it could be considered for acceptance.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Clear theoretical foundation for attention modeling
- Well-organized presentation with helpful visualizations
- Demonstrates interpretability of attention mechanisms

*Con:*
- Insufficient experimental validation against recent state-of-the-art
- Marginal performance gains without statistical significance
- Overstated novelty relative to existing work
- Lack of ablation studies to justify high-order attention
- Misleading comparison of model complexity

Given these concerns, I recommend rejection. The paper presents a technically sound but incrementally improved approach that doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of significance and novelty.
