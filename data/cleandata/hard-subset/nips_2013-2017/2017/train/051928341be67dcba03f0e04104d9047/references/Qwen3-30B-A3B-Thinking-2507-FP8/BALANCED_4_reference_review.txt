

# Review for "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., visual, textual, and answer representations) for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors introduce a probabilistic framework with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture interactions across modalities, followed by mean-field inference to obtain attention probabilities. This approach extends beyond the pairwise attention mechanisms commonly used in existing VQA systems (e.g., [15, 25]) by explicitly modeling higher-order dependencies. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on VQA datasets, showing that their high-order attention mechanism improves upon previous approaches.

The paper relates well to prior work in attention mechanisms for VQA, particularly distinguishing itself from approaches like Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7] by explicitly modeling multi-modal interactions beyond pairwise correlations. It also positions itself relative to recent work on structured attention [14] and bilinear models [13].

## Strengths
1. **Originality**: The high-order attention framework represents a meaningful extension beyond current pairwise attention models. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from existing methods, particularly in its ability to model interactions between three or more modalities.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The probabilistic formulation with mean-field inference is well-justified, and the implementation details (e.g., unary, pairwise, and ternary potential modules) are clearly described. The experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements over baseline approaches.

3. **State-of-the-Art Results**: The model achieves SOTA performance on VQA datasets with relatively modest parameters (40M vs. 70M+ in competing approaches), demonstrating the efficiency and effectiveness of the high-order attention mechanism.

4. **Clarity and Visualization**: The paper is well-organized with clear figures (Fig. 3-8) that effectively illustrate the attention mechanisms and qualitative results. The visualizations of attention patterns provide valuable interpretability.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Generalization Demonstration**: While the authors claim their approach is "generally applicable," they only evaluate it on VQA. The paper lacks experiments demonstrating the method's applicability to other multimodal tasks (e.g., image captioning, visual grounding), which would strengthen the claim of generalizability.

2. **Incomplete Ablation Study**: The paper compares 2-modality vs. 3-modality models but doesn't provide a thorough ablation of the high-order components. It would be valuable to see how much each component (unary, pairwise, ternary) contributes to the final performance, and whether the benefits are consistent across different question types.

3. **Comparison with Recent SOTA**: The paper compares with Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], but doesn't include comparisons with more recent SOTA methods from 2017 (e.g., Kazemi and Elqursh [11], which was published in 2017). This limits the context for the claimed SOTA performance.

4. **Theoretical Justification**: While the paper demonstrates improved performance with high-order attention, it lacks a strong theoretical argument for why higher-order interactions are beneficial for VQA. The authors could better explain why modeling ternary interactions (image, question, answer) is more effective than pairwise interactions.

5. **Scalability Concerns**: The authors claim "trivial extension to any number of modalities," but don't demonstrate how the model scales with increasing modalities (e.g., 4 or more). The computational complexity of the ternary potential module (which involves 3D tensor operations) may become prohibitive with more modalities.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a valuable contribution to attention mechanisms in multimodal learning by introducing a high-order attention framework that achieves SOTA results in VQA. The technical approach is sound, well-motivated, and clearly presented. However, the limited demonstration of generalizability beyond VQA and the incomplete ablation study prevent this from being a strong candidate for NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject**

## Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand Experimental Validation**: Conduct experiments on additional multimodal tasks (e.g., image captioning or visual grounding) to demonstrate the generalizability claim.

2. **Conduct Thorough Ablation Study**: Include a detailed analysis of how each component (unary, pairwise, ternary) contributes to performance, and investigate whether the benefits of high-order attention are consistent across different question types.

3. **Compare with More Recent SOTA**: Update the comparisons to include methods published in 2017 (e.g., Kazemi and Elqursh [11]) to provide a more complete context for the claimed SOTA results.

4. **Strengthen Theoretical Justification**: Provide a more detailed explanation for why high-order attention is beneficial for VQA, potentially through analysis of attention patterns on different question categories.

5. **Address Scalability**: Investigate how the model scales with additional modalities and consider alternative implementations that might reduce computational complexity for higher-order interactions.

While the paper makes a promising contribution, these improvements would strengthen the technical contribution and make the case for generalizability more compelling for the NIPS audience.
