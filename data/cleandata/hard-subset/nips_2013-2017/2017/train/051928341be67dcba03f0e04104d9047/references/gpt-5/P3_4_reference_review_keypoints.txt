- Summary: Proposes a unified high-order attention framework for multimodal reasoning (unary/pairwise/ternary potentials over V, Q, A) with attention as learned marginals and final fusion via compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT); evaluated on VQA multiple-choice.

- Strength: Conceptual generality — clean decomposition into unary, pairwise, and ternary attention terms that can extend to k modalities.

- Strength: Ternary attention — explicitly models three-way Q–V–A correlations, well-motivated for multiple-choice VQA.

- Strength: Practicality — uses efficient MCB/MCT pooling and keeps computation feasible by conditioning on candidate answers.

- Strength: Empirical performance — competitive/SOTA results on VQA multiple-choice with modest parameter count and intuitive visualizations.

- Strength: Reproducibility — many implementation details provided and code reportedly available.

- Weakness: Probabilistic framing is overstated — softmax over linear combination of potentials is not a justified mean-field inference without energy function or derived updates.

- Weakness: Limited novelty vs prior bilinear attention — pairwise forms are standard and ternary is a straightforward trilinear extension; need clearer positioning relative to MLB, co-attention, etc.

- Weakness: Fairness/scope of evaluation — conditioning on answer candidates changes the task; comparisons should separate models that use A from those that do not, and open-ended VQA should be reported.

- Weakness: Limited ablations — missing systematic disentangling of unary/pairwise/ternary contributions and examination of learned mixture weights.

- Weakness: Complexity and scalability concerns — no analysis of time/memory costs or scalability to larger numbers of answer candidates or open-ended settings.

- Weakness: Clarity and notation issues — typos, ambiguous descriptions, unclear tensor shapes, and insufficient figure explanations.

- Weakness: Experimental reporting gaps — per-type accuracies (Yes/No/Number/Other), statistical significance, and matched baselines/features not fully reported.

- Quality: Technically implemented correctly, but probabilistic justification is thin and the method is largely an applied trilinear correlation with learned marginalization.

- Clarity: Readable overall but needs tighter notation, typo fixes, and clearer computational/shape descriptions; avoid or justify mean-field terminology.

- Originality: Moderate — main novelty is explicit ternary V–Q–A attention and integration with compact trilinear pooling; incremental over bilinear methods.

- Significance: Potentially useful for multimodal tasks with conditional outputs; empirical gains on MC VQA are notable but generality beyond MC VQA is unproven.

- Suggestion: Provide a proper probabilistic model or drop the mean-field claim and rephrase the method more accurately.

- Suggestion: Add ablations isolating unary/pairwise/ternary effects and report learned mixture weights and question-type breakdowns.

- Suggestion: Report open-ended VQA results or clearly separate comparisons to other answer-conditioned MC methods.

- Suggestion: Include complexity (time/memory) analysis and discuss scalability or approximations for larger na.

- Suggestion: Clarify implementation details (C2/C3 shapes, weight sharing, FC stack after pooling) and fix notation typos.

- Reasons to accept: Unified, general attention framework; demonstrated gains on MC VQA with fewer parameters; useful integration of ternary attention with compact pooling.

- Reasons to reject: Limited novelty beyond trilinear extension, overstated probabilistic claims, evaluation focused on answer-conditioned MC setting with insufficient ablations and clarity.

- Overall recommendation: Borderline — promising engineering contribution that needs stronger theoretical framing, more comprehensive ablations, and fairer/clearer evaluations; confidence: high.
