This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism intended to capture correlations between multiple data modalities, exemplified through visual question answering (VQA). The core idea – extending attention beyond pairwise interactions to include higher-order relationships – is a reasonable direction to explore, particularly as multimodal reasoning becomes increasingly prevalent. The authors attempt to justify this with the argument that existing attention mechanisms are “hand-crafted” and lack generalizability.

However, despite the appealing high-level concept, the paper suffers from a significant lack of thoroughness and convincing evidence. While the authors claim state-of-the-art performance on VQA, the presentation of results feels incomplete. The gains over existing methods (Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) are marginal, and the comparison is not exhaustive. Given the increased complexity of the proposed model – introducing numerous trainable parameters – a more substantial performance improvement would be expected to justify the architectural choices. The authors acknowledge using a smaller embedding dimension (512 vs. 2048 in other works), and attribute the marginal gain to this, but then state that tuning could improve this, seemingly downplaying the impact of this important design choice. 

The methodological details, while presented, are often obscured by unnecessary complexity. The use of convolutions with kernel size 1x1, re-labeled as “matrix multiplies,” feels like an attempt to obfuscate simple operations. The introduction of “potentials” and mean-field inference, while valid techniques, aren’t adequately motivated in the context of VQA and add a layer of abstraction that doesn’t significantly enhance understanding or performance. The explanation of the tensor sketch units (MCB/MCT) feels cursory, and the benefit of the 2-layer MCB over the simpler MCT is not convincingly demonstrated. The visualization in Fig. 8, illustrating a failure case, is actually quite damning, highlighting a common attentional focus despite differing questions.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive but lacks critical analysis. Simply listing existing attention mechanisms isn't sufficient; a deeper discussion of *why* those mechanisms fail to capture higher-order correlations, and *how* the proposed method demonstrably addresses those limitations, is needed. The claim that existing methods don't "explicitly consider multiple modalities" is misleading – methods like [15, 7] explicitly model interactions *between* modalities, even if not in the same high-order manner. 

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of exploring higher-order attention is potentially valuable.
*   The paper provides a reasonably thorough overview of related work in attention mechanisms.
*   The authors attempt to provide both quantitative and qualitative results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Marginal performance improvement despite increased model complexity.
*   Incomplete experimental comparison; lacks comprehensive baselines and ablation studies.
*   Overly complex methodology with limited justification for specific design choices.
*   Insufficient explanation of key concepts (e.g., tensor sketching).
*   Failure case analysis reveals potential limitations of the approach.
*   The claim of novelty regarding multi-modality is overstated.
*   The choice of a smaller embedding dimension impacting comparison.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

*   **Against Acceptance:** The paper presents an incremental advance with limited practical impact. The increased complexity doesn't translate into a substantial performance gain, and the methodology feels unnecessarily convoluted. The lack of rigorous ablation studies and a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods weakens the claims of superiority.
*   **For Acceptance (with major revisions):** If the authors could significantly improve the performance, conduct a more exhaustive comparison, simplify the methodology, and provide a more compelling justification for the proposed approach, then the paper *might* be reconsidered. Currently, the work feels more like a proof-of-concept than a significant contribution.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. The paper lacks the significance and technical rigor expected of a NIPS publication. While the idea is intriguing, the execution falls short, and the presented results do not convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed high-order attention mechanism. I recommend the authors focus on simplifying the model, conducting more thorough experiments, and clearly articulating the advantages over existing approaches before resubmitting.
