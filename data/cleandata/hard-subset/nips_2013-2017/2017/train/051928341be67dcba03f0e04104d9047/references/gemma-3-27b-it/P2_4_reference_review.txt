## Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism based on high-order correlations between data modalities, aiming to address limitations in existing attention models concerning entangled representations and task-specific designs. The authors propose modeling interactions between *k* modalities using *k*-th order correlations, implemented through a probabilistic model with mean-field inference. They demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on the Visual Question Answering (VQA) task, achieving state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly modeling high-order correlations between modalities within an attention mechanism is a valuable contribution.  It moves beyond pairwise interactions, which are common in existing literature (Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25]), offering increased representational power.
* **Generality:**  The proposed framework is designed to be applicable to any number of data modalities, which is a significant advantage. The authors highlight this extensibility as a key benefit.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper reports state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, providing compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the approach.
* **Clear Explanation of Technical Details:** The paper provides a relatively detailed explanation of the mathematical formulation, including the use of potentials, mean-field inference, and tensor sketching.  The figures (e.g., Fig. 3) are helpful in visualizing the attention mechanisms.
* **Good Comparison to Baselines:** The authors compare their approach to relevant baselines (Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) and demonstrate improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Scalability:**  While the framework is general, the computational complexity of modeling high-order correlations, especially with three or more modalities, is a concern. The reliance on tensor sketching (Pham & Pagh [21]) suggests awareness of this issue, but the performance implications of this approximation are not thoroughly discussed.  The 40-hour training time on a single GPU, even with a relatively small model size (40M parameters), is significant.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While comparisons to existing methods are included, a more thorough ablation study would be beneficial. For example, evaluating the performance of the model with only unary and pairwise potentials, and then adding the ternary potentials, would help quantify the contribution of each component.  The comparison of MCT vs 2-layer MCB is helpful, but more comprehensive ablation would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Qualitative Results – Limited Insight:** While Fig. 5 and Fig. 7 show attention visualizations, they don't provide deep insights into *why* the model is attending to specific regions. More detailed analysis of attention patterns, perhaps with examples of both successful and failed cases, would be valuable.  The discussion of the failure case (Fig. 8) is good but somewhat limited.
* **Embedding Dimensionality:** The authors note that their lower embedding dimension (512) compared to other SOTA models (2048) potentially limits performance.  A more direct comparison with a similarly sized model (e.g., increasing the embedding dimension to 2048) could have helped demonstrate the benefits of their attention mechanism independent of feature dimensionality.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper appears technically sound.  The mathematical formulation is well-defined, and the empirical results support the claims. The use of established techniques like tensor sketching is appropriate.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the work, and the sections flow logically. However, some of the mathematical notation could be simplified or explained more thoroughly.
* **Originality:** The approach of explicitly modeling high-order correlations in attention is novel and represents a meaningful extension of existing work.
* **Significance:** The results are significant, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on a challenging VQA dataset. The potential for applying this approach to other multimodal tasks is also promising.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and well-motivated approach to attention modeling.
* Strong empirical results on a benchmark dataset.
* Clear and detailed technical description.
* Generality to multiple modalities.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity and scalability concerns.
* Limited ablation study.
* Qualitative results lack deep insights.
* Potential limitations due to embedding dimensionality.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS.  The novelty and empirical success of the proposed high-order attention mechanism are significant contributions to the field. While there are some weaknesses, they are not major enough to overshadow the strengths of the work.  Addressing the scalability concerns and providing a more thorough ablation study in future work would further strengthen the contribution. The paper addresses a difficult problem and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
