

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., image, question, answer) for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors introduce unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture different orders of correlations, using mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities. They demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on VQA datasets with a relatively simple architecture.

## Strengths
The paper presents a clear motivation for high-order attention mechanisms in multimodal tasks. The method is conceptually straightforward and extends naturally to any number of modalities. The experimental results on VQA are promising, showing competitive performance with fewer parameters than some prior approaches. The qualitative analysis provides useful insights into how the attention mechanism operates.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Mathematical and Technical Inconsistencies
The paper contains several critical mathematical inconsistencies that undermine the technical soundness of the proposed method:

- In Equation (1), the authors define PV(iv) = smax(α1θV(iv)+α2θV,Q(iv)+α3θA,V(iv)+α4θV,Q,A(iv) + α5). The term θA,V(iv) is inconsistent with the notation used throughout the paper, which consistently uses θV,A for the image-answer potential. This appears to be a typo but creates significant confusion in the mathematical formulation.

- The paper claims to use "mean-field inference" but only describes a single iteration (using smax as a single iteration). The authors do not justify why a single iteration is sufficient or how multiple iterations would change the results. This is a critical omission for a paper claiming to use mean-field inference.

- In Section 3.2.2, the pairwise potential definition contains an undefined variable wiv: "θV,Q(iq) = tanh(∑iv wiv (C2)iv,iq)". The paper never defines wiv, making the equation mathematically incomplete.

- In Section 3.2.3, the ternary potential notation is inconsistent with the definition. The paper defines C3 as (C3)i,j,k = corr3((QWq):,i, (VWv):,j, (AWa):,k), but then uses (C3)iq,iv,ia in the equation, which would require i=iq, j=iv, k=ia. However, the indices don't match the definition's notation.

### 2. Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies
- The paper uses inconsistent notation for the ternary potential: sometimes θV,Q,A, sometimes θV,Q.A (with a dot) in the equations.

- Figure 3 captions use "Threeway" instead of "Ternary" (e.g., "Threeway Potential" vs. "Ternary Potential" used in the text).

- In the references, citation formatting is inconsistent: "In ICCV,, , ," vs. "In NIPS,, , ," (extra commas).

- The paper cites "the VQA dataset [2]" but reference [2] is "VQA: Visual question answering" (a paper), not the dataset itself. The dataset should be cited as [2] as stated, but the reference should correctly identify it as the dataset.

### 3. Experimental and Methodological Concerns
- The paper states "the dimension d of all hidden layers is set to 512" but in the data embedding section, it says "we embed both the 196 VGG-19 or ResNet features into a d = 512 dimensional space." This creates confusion about what d represents in different contexts.

- The paper claims to use "the top 3000 most frequent answers" but doesn't specify whether these answers are selected from the training set only or include validation data. This is critical for reproducibility.

- The optimization description contains a typo: "as well as = 1e−8" should be "as well as ε = 1e−8."

- The paper states "we observe state-of-the-art behavior" but doesn't clearly explain how it compares to the most recent state-of-the-art methods on the VQA leaderboard (e.g., the 2017 VQA leaderboard had methods achieving 68.5% on test-dev, while this paper reports 64.57% with MCB). The claim of "state-of-the-art" is unsubstantiated.

### 4. Theoretical Justification
- The paper claims to "develop a probabilistic model, based on high order potentials and performs mean-field inference" but provides no theoretical justification for why this approach is superior to alternatives. Why linear combination of potentials (Equation 1) rather than other forms of combination?

- The paper claims its approach is "easy to extend to any number of data modalities" but doesn't explain how this extension would work beyond three modalities. This is a critical limitation for the proposed method's generality.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a conceptually interesting approach to multimodal attention, the numerous mathematical inconsistencies, notational errors, and lack of theoretical justification significantly undermine the technical soundness of the work. The paper would require substantial revision to fix these issues before it could be considered for publication. The errors are not merely typographical but affect the mathematical foundation of the proposed method.

I recommend rejection due to the significant technical flaws that prevent the reader from understanding or reproducing the method. The paper's claims about state-of-the-art performance are also not sufficiently supported by the experimental results presented.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:**
- Clear motivation for high-order attention mechanisms
- Simple and extendable architecture
- Competitive results on VQA dataset with fewer parameters
- Useful qualitative analysis of attention patterns

**Con:**
- Critical mathematical inconsistencies in the core formulation
- Inconsistent notation throughout the paper
- Lack of theoretical justification for key methodological choices
- Unsubstantiated claim of "state-of-the-art" performance
- Poorly explained experimental details affecting reproducibility
