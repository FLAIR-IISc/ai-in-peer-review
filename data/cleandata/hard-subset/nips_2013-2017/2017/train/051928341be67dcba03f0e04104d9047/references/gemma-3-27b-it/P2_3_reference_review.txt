## Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism for multimodal data, specifically targeting Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea is to model high-order correlations between modalities (image, question, and answer) using a probabilistic framework and mean-field inference. The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms often handle modalities in an entangled manner and lack generality. They demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, highlighting the benefits of capturing these high-order interactions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a conceptually interesting and technically sound approach to multimodal attention. While the performance gains reported are incremental, the authors convincingly demonstrate the potential of their high-order attention mechanism and provide a compelling justification for its design. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the underlying principles.  However, the presentation could be strengthened with more ablation studies and a more thorough comparison to existing methods.

**Detailed Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound. The use of probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference is well-motivated and the mathematical formulation is clear. The experimental setup is reasonably well-defined. The state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. However, the ablation study comparing the unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials could be more extensive. It's not entirely clear how much each component contributes to the final performance. While the authors acknowledge potential limitations (e.g., the embedding dimension), a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity introduced by higher-order terms would strengthen the analysis.

* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is generally very well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated. The key concepts – unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials – are explained with accompanying diagrams. The equations are well-formatted and easy to follow. The explanations of the data embedding and decision-making components are also clear.

* **Originality (7/10):** While the idea of modeling higher-order correlations is novel, the paper builds upon a significant body of existing work in attention mechanisms. The authors do a reasonable job of differentiating their work from related approaches (Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25], Fukui et al. [7]), particularly highlighting the generality and scalability of their method. However, the improvement over existing methods isn’t revolutionary, so the originality is somewhat incremental.  The use of tensor sketching, though not entirely new in the context of bilinear pooling, is well integrated into their framework.

* **Significance (7/10):** The results are important in the sense that they represent a step forward in VQA performance.  The proposed attention mechanism offers a more general and flexible framework for handling multimodal data, potentially applicable to other tasks beyond VQA. The paper’s focus on capturing high-order interactions is a valuable contribution to the field of attention modeling. However, the improvement in performance over existing state-of-the-art methods is not substantial enough to be considered a major breakthrough. It's hard to gauge the broad impact without further evaluation on other multimodal tasks.



**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The concept of explicitly modeling high-order correlations between modalities is a valuable contribution.
* **Generality:** The proposed framework is designed to be applicable to any number of modalities.
* **State-of-the-Art Performance:** Achieves competitive results on the VQA dataset.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is easy to understand and follow.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference are well-justified.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Improvement:** The performance gains over existing methods are modest.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** A more comprehensive ablation study is needed to evaluate the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials).
* **Computational Complexity:**  The computational cost of higher-order attention isn’t fully discussed.
* **Limited Task Evaluation:** The results are primarily focused on VQA. Evaluation on other multimodal tasks would strengthen the claims of generality.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to multimodal attention.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the underlying principles.
* The approach has the potential to be applied to a wide range of multimodal tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The performance gains over existing methods are not substantial.
* The ablation study is limited.
* The computational complexity of the approach is not fully addressed.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept (with minor revisions).**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should:

*   Expand the ablation study to provide a more detailed analysis of the contribution of each component.
*   Discuss the computational complexity of the approach and potential strategies for mitigating it.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of potential applications beyond VQA.
