Summary and contributions:
The paper proposes a high-order attention framework for multimodal reasoning, instantiated on VQA. The core idea is to treat attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, answers A). Pairwise and ternary interactions are built from inner-product correlations between modality-specific embeddings, forming a similarity matrix C2 and tensor C3. The attentions for each modality are then obtained via a single “mean-field” step: a learned linear combination of the corresponding unary, marginalized pairwise, and marginalized ternary potentials followed by a softmax. For decision fusion, the paper uses compact bilinear (MCB) and trilinear (MCT) pooling (via tensor sketching) to capture high-order correlations while keeping computation tractable. Experiments on VQA report state-of-the-art multiple-choice performance with fewer parameters than some prior work, and include attention visualizations. A Torch implementation is provided.

Relation to prior work:
The approach situates itself among co-attention models (Lu et al.), bilinear/trilinear pooling for fusion (Fukui et al., MCB; Kim et al., MLB; MUTAN not cited), dual/structured attention (Nam et al.; Kim et al., Structured Attention Networks), SAN/stacked attention (Yang et al.), and DMN/iterative memory (Xiong et al.). The paper’s novelty is explicitly modeling higher-order attention potentials across modalities and using a single-step mean-field-style update, and extending co-attention to ternary interactions by incorporating the candidate answers as inputs (as in Jabri et al.’s observation that answers-as-input can be competitive). The combination of high-order attention with tensor sketching is reasonable and timely.

Strengths:
- Conceptual clarity in decomposing attention into unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials,” and showing how each contributes to marginal attentions.
- Ternary attention over (V,Q,A) is a natural extension of co-attention and appears to help MC VQA, where A is given.
- Sensible design choices: 1x1 “convs” as learned linear maps; inner-product correlations; tensor sketching to avoid exponential blow-up in fusion.
- Empirical gains with a smaller parameter budget, and useful qualitative visualizations that demonstrate question-dependent attention changes.
- Reproducibility is aided by code release.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Probabilistic/mean-field claim is underspecified. The current formulation is essentially a learned linear combination of features followed by softmax. No explicit factor graph or variational objective is provided, and only a single “mean-field iteration” is used without derivation of fixed-point updates. As presented, the method reads more as a log-linear attention aggregator than principled mean-field inference.
- Computational complexity of high-order potentials is not analyzed. While ternary C3 seems feasible for MC (na≈18), the stated “trivial extension to any number of modalities” is misleading: building Ck scales as O(d Πm nm), which becomes prohibitive beyond three modalities or when na is large (e.g., open-ended with 3k answers). The paper circumvents exponential cost at the decision stage via sketching, but not at the attention stage.
- Experimental evidence is incomplete. The quantitative table is not shown in the text provided; per-type (Yes/No, Number, Other) breakdowns, open-ended results, and comparisons under matched visual backbones (VGG vs ResNet) are needed. Strong contemporary baselines like MLB/MUTAN/SAN/DAN and stronger single-stream baselines should be included under controlled settings (same features, answer space, data splits).
- Ablations are limited. We need:
  - Contribution of unary/pairwise/ternary potentials separately (disable each term; vary α/β/γ).
  - Multiple “mean-field” iterations vs single step.
  - Effect of normalization of correlations (cosine vs inner product) and learned w weights.
  - Effect of using A as input (3-modality) vs not, on both MC and open-ended.
  - MCT vs 2-layer MCB: the latter’s superior performance is noted; further analysis of why (self-interactions, capacity) would be useful.
- Clarity issues:
  - Notational inconsistencies and typos (e.g., the attended answer vector is mislabeled as aV in Sec. 3.2). Some figure references (e.g., Fig. 3 annotations like “conv conv Corr3”) are hard to parse without explicit layer dimensions and operations.
  - The “convolution along the visual dimension using a 1×1 kernel” is effectively a learned weighted sum; clarifying this will help.
  - It is unclear whether αi/βi/γi are global scalars, vectors, or functions of input; global scalars may be too restrictive.
- Fairness/controls: The paper mentions 40M vs 70M parameters but should carefully control other factors (feature extractor, answer space size, data augmentation).

Quality:
Technically plausible, but the probabilistic framing is weakly justified and the computational story for k>3 is incomplete. The experiments appear promising but require stronger baselines and ablations to isolate where the gains come from.

Clarity:
Generally understandable but needs cleanup in notation, explicit algorithmic descriptions, and complexity analysis. More detailed experimental reporting is required for a reader to reproduce and assess fairness.

Originality:
Moderate. Pairwise co-attention and high-order fusion are known; the ternary attention mechanism that explicitly models V–Q–A interactions and the unified potential-based aggregation provide a useful perspective. The “mean-field” framing feels more like rebranding without rigorous grounding.

Significance:
If the gains over strong baselines hold under controlled settings, the idea of high-order attention potentials could inform future multimodal models. Practical impact may be limited by the scalability of Ck beyond three modalities.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a precise probabilistic model and variational derivation (or drop the mean-field claim and present as learned potential aggregation).
- Add comprehensive ablations (unary/pairwise/ternary; number of iterations; normalization choices) and report per-type metrics on both MC and open-ended VQA.
- Ensure fair, apples-to-apples comparisons to strong baselines (MLB/MUTAN/SAN/DAN) with matched backbones and answer spaces.
- Analyze time/memory complexity for C2/C3 and discuss scalability to more modalities.
- Clarify the role and form of α/β/γ (scalars vs functions), and consider making them context-dependent.
- Fix notational/typo issues and make figures self-contained with operation shapes.

Pros:
- Clear modularization of unary/pairwise/ternary attentions.
- Ternary attention with answers-as-input improves MC VQA.
- Efficient high-order fusion via sketching and good qualitative insights.
- Code available.

Cons:
- Weak probabilistic justification; “mean-field” is not derived.
- Scalability concerns for higher-order attention tensors.
- Missing strong baselines, detailed metrics, and ablations.
- Some clarity and reproducibility issues.

Overall: Promising idea with interesting empirical results, but requires stronger experimental validation and a clearer, more rigorous presentation to meet NeurIPS standards. I lean toward a borderline/weak accept if the missing quantitative details and ablations are present in the full paper; otherwise weak reject.
