Summary
The paper proposes a “high-order attention” framework for multimodal reasoning, instantiated for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The model defines unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over three modalities—image (V), question (Q), and answer candidates (A). Attention distributions for each modality are obtained by a linear combination of the corresponding marginalized potentials followed by a softmax (claimed to correspond to a single mean-field iteration). Attended features are fused with Multimodal Compact Bilinear/Trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) via tensor sketching for decision making. Experiments on VQA (mainly multiple-choice) claim state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters than some prior work, plus qualitative visualizations.

Strengths
- Conceptual framing: casting multimodal attention as combining unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions is intuitive and potentially extensible beyond VQA.
- Practical design: uses simple building blocks (1×1 convs, inner products, MCB/MCT) and integrates answer conditioning for multiple-choice.
- Empirical indications: shows that 3-modality (including answers) can outperform 2-modality in MC-VQA; some ablation on MCB vs MCT.
- Relevance: attention over high-order interactions is timely and useful for multimodal tasks.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Quality/technical soundness
- Probabilistic model unclear: The paper repeatedly refers to “potentials” and “mean-field inference,” but does not define a joint distribution (e.g., CRF factorization, energy, partition function) nor derive the updates. The linear combination with learnable weights plus softmax is not shown to be a principled MF update for any well-specified graphical model. Without a clear probabilistic foundation, the “mean-field” claim is misleading.
- Ternary complexity: The ternary correlation tensor C3 ∈ Rnq×nv×na is intractable when na is large (na=3000 elsewhere). The paper does not present a feasible computational strategy (e.g., low-rank factorization, sparse candidates) except implicitly when na=18 for multiple-choice. If 3rd-order attention is only used for MC with 18 candidates, this should be stated explicitly, along with complexity and memory footprints.
- Notation/definitions inconsistent or incorrect:
  - Eq. (1): “θV,Q.A” vs “θV,Q,A”; inconsistent commas/periods; ambiguities re: which indices are marginalized.
  - The pairwise definitions use (C2)iv,iq but C2 earlier was (i,j) with i=word, j=visual—index mapping is inconsistent.
  - In attended vectors: “aV = …; aQ = …; and aV = …” (last should be aA).
  - Weights wiv, wiq, wiv,ia etc. are introduced but not defined (shapes, parameterization, sharing, initialization).
- Ablations are insufficient: No analysis of the learned α,β,γ weights (do ternary terms actually matter?), no study of multiple MF “iterations,” no removal study of unary/pairwise/ternary contributions, no effect of embedding size d, no per-question-type breakdown.
- Decision module claims: The “2-layer MCB” is only briefly described; it’s unclear how layers are composed, whether additional FC layers are used, and how parameter counts remain comparable to MCT.
- Claims of SOTA lack concrete numbers in the provided text: Table 1 is referenced but not present; exact test-dev/test-std scores, per-type accuracies, and comparisons are missing from the excerpt. Even if present in the full paper, the narrative here makes it hard to verify claims.
- Baselines: Comparisons to strong contemporaneous baselines (e.g., MLB/Mutan/bilinear pooling variants; strong single- or dual-attention models; Show, Ask, Attend and Answer) are not clearly reported. Open-ended VQA results (the more common protocol) appear missing.

Clarity/presentation
- Numerous typos and grammatical issues: “and and”, broken sentences (“st Conference…”), inconsistent variable names, punctuation errors, mismatched indices. These hinder comprehension.
- Figures referenced (Figs. 1–8) are crucial to understanding but not included/legible here; descriptions suggest they mix three-modal and two-modal settings without clear separation.
- Reference errors and incompleteness: Many entries lack full bibliographic details; some attributions are incorrect (e.g., RNNSearch is Bahdanau et al., not Hermann et al.). Consistency (capitalization, venues, years) is poor.
- Important hyperparameters are unspecified (e.g., maximum question length nq, whether ResNet vs VGG is used for final SOTA claims). Parameter count claims (40M vs 70M) are not justified.

Originality
- The notion of pairwise (bilinear) fusion and attention across modalities is well explored (MCB, MLB, co-attention, dual attention, structured attention, etc.). The main novelty appears to be the explicit ternary attention over answers and the “probabilistic” framing. However, without a rigorous probabilistic derivation and given the heavy reliance on known components (inner products, MCB/MCT), the incremental novelty is moderate. Prior work has also explored conditioning on answer candidates for MC-VQA.

Significance
- If substantiated, a clean and effective framework for higher-order attention could be impactful. However, limited and unclear evaluation (MC only; missing numbers in the text; weak baselines; no open-ended) undercuts significance. The generality claim (“trivially extends to any number of modalities”) is not demonstrated beyond k=3, and practical complexity is not addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous probabilistic formulation (factor graph, energies, MF derivation) or remove MF claims and describe the method as a learned mixture of potentials.
- Clarify which components are used for MC vs open-ended; report both settings with standard metrics, per-type breakdowns, and strong baselines.
- Include full ablations: remove unary/pairwise/ternary; vary α,β,γ; number of MF iterations; embedding sizes; with/without answer inputs; MCB vs MCT configurations; runtime/memory.
- Resolve computational concerns for C3 (e.g., low-rank factorization, sampled candidates) and report complexity.
- Fix notation, indexing, and typos; define all parameters (wiv etc.); unify symbols and dimensions.
- Correct and complete references and attributions; add missing related work (e.g., MLB, MUTAN, Tucker, BAN).
- Provide exact scores, training details, and release reproducible code.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem (multimodal attention for VQA).
- Simple, potentially extensible idea of stacking unary/pairwise/ternary terms.
- Some evidence that 3-modality attention helps in MC-VQA.

Cons for acceptance
- Probabilistic/mean-field claims not justified.
- Ternary attention computational feasibility unclear beyond MC.
- Missing/unclear key experimental details and baselines; SOTA claims not verifiable here.
- Numerous notation, formatting, and reference errors; clarity is poor.
- Limited novelty over strong existing bilinear/co-attention methods.

Recommendation
Reject. While the high-level idea is promising, the paper in its current form has significant clarity, correctness, and evaluation shortcomings. Addressing the derivation, computational issues, and experimental rigor—along with a thorough rewrite—could make this a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
