

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., visual, textual, and answer representations) for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors frame attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to obtain marginal attention probabilities. Unlike existing approaches that typically model pairwise interactions (e.g., between image and question), their method systematically extends to k-th order correlations across any number of modalities. The authors demonstrate their approach on VQA, achieving state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks while using a relatively simple model with 40 million parameters.

The paper builds upon previous work in attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks, particularly distinguishing itself from Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], who primarily focus on pairwise attention. While Kim et al. [14] also propose a multi-variate probabilistic model, their approach is limited to specific structural dependencies rather than general high-order attention. The authors clearly position their work as a general extension to any number of modalities, which represents a meaningful advancement over previous attention mechanisms.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with well-defined mathematical formulations for unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The mean-field inference approach is appropriate for the probabilistic model. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods on both test-dev and test-std VQA datasets. The authors provide detailed ablation studies showing the contribution of each component (e.g., comparing 2-modality vs. 3-modality models, MCT vs. 2-layer MCB). The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the failure case shown in Figure 8, demonstrating appropriate honesty. However, the paper could better explain why high-order attention (beyond pairwise) is necessary for VQA, as the theoretical justification for ternary potentials is somewhat underdeveloped compared to the empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures (particularly Figures 3-4) that effectively illustrate the attention mechanism. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The implementation details (Section 4.1-4.2) are sufficiently detailed to allow replication. One minor weakness is that the paper doesn't fully explain how the learnable parameters (αi, βi, γi) are optimized during training, which would aid reproducibility.

**Originality:** The key contribution—extending attention mechanisms to model k-th order correlations across multiple modalities—is novel within the NIPS context. A review of recent NIPS proceedings (2015-2017) shows that while pairwise attention has been widely studied (e.g., [15, 25, 7]), high-order attention mechanisms have not been systematically explored. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work, particularly noting that previous methods either focus on pairwise interactions or are not designed for generalization to more than two modalities. The probabilistic formulation with mean-field inference represents a distinct approach from the bilinear pooling methods used in [7].

**Significance:** The paper makes a meaningful contribution to multimodal learning by providing a simple yet effective mechanism for modeling complex interactions between multiple data modalities. The state-of-the-art results on VQA (64.57% on val set with 2-layer MCB) demonstrate practical significance, and the model's efficiency (40M parameters vs. 70M+ for competitors) suggests potential for broader adoption. The high-order attention framework could be applicable to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA, such as image captioning or visual reasoning, which would further enhance its significance. The qualitative analysis in Figures 5-7 provides valuable insights into how the model processes information, supporting the claim that high-order attention leads to more interpretable and effective decision making.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical foundation for high-order attention mechanisms
- Strong empirical results on VQA, achieving SOTA with a relatively simple model
- Excellent visualizations of attention mechanisms that demonstrate interpretability
- Good differentiation from related work, particularly highlighting the generalizability to any number of modalities
- Comprehensive ablation studies showing the value of each component

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical justification for why high-order attention (beyond pairwise) is necessary for VQA
- The computational complexity of ternary potential calculation (C3 tensor) is not discussed
- The failure case in Figure 8 is noted but not thoroughly analyzed to understand the limitations of the approach
- Some implementation details (e.g., optimization of learnable parameters) could be more explicit

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to the field of multimodal learning. The high-order attention mechanism represents a meaningful advancement over existing attention models, with strong empirical validation on VQA. While some theoretical aspects could be strengthened, the overall contribution is substantial and aligns well with NIPS' focus on high-quality machine learning research. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, offering both theoretical insights and practical improvements for multimodal learning tasks.
