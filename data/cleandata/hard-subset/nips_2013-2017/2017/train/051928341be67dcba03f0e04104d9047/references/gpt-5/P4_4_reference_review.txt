Summary
This paper proposes a unified attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions across modalities in Visual Question Answering (VQA). The key idea is to view attention as a (loosely) probabilistic system with learnable potentials: unary potentials over each modality (image, question, answer), pairwise potentials capturing cross-modal correlations (e.g., question–image), and a ternary potential capturing joint dependencies among image, question, and answer candidates. The model linearly combines marginalized potentials with learnable weights and applies a softmax to obtain attention distributions, which the authors interpret as a single mean-field step. Attended representations are then fused via compact bilinear or trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) for answer prediction. The method is evaluated primarily on VQA multiple-choice, with qualitative visualizations and quantitative comparisons to co-attention and MCB-based baselines. The authors claim state-of-the-art performance for the MC setting using fewer parameters and report that including the third modality (answers) in attention improves results. Code is released.

Relative to prior work, the paper connects to: co-attention [Lu et al.], bilinear pooling for VQA [Fukui et al.], low-rank bilinear pooling [Kim et al., MLB], and structured attention [Kim et al.]. Unlike most prior work that fuses two modalities for attention or decision, this model explicitly defines and combines higher-order potentials and argues the approach is easily extendable to more than two modalities.

Strengths
- Clear modularization: data embeddings, attention (unary/pairwise/ternary), and decision making. The design is conceptually simple and end-to-end trainable.
- Generality: the framework nominally accommodates any number of modalities via k-th order potentials.
- Empirical gains: the three-modality variant improves over the two-modality baseline; compact bilinear/trilinear pooling is used effectively.
- Practicality: training details, feature backbones, and implementation choices are described; code is provided.
- Interpretability: qualitative attention maps across modalities show reasonable behavior and question-dependent shifts.

Weaknesses
- Probabilistic/methodological clarity: The paper calls the combination “mean-field inference,” but no explicit joint distribution or variational objective is specified. The linear combination of potentials with learned α/β/γ and a softmax is not derived from a defined CRF energy or MF updates. As presented, this is a learnable weighted sum of features rather than principled inference.
- Ambiguity in potential parameterization: The pairwise and ternary reductions use learned “convolutions along a dimension” with 1×1 kernels; the exact shapes/parameter counts for wiv, wiq, wiv,ia, etc., are unclear. It is not specified whether these are per-index scalars, vectors, or small networks. This hinders reproducibility and theoretical understanding.
- Computational complexity: The ternary correlation tensor C3 (nq×nv×na) can be large. While MC VQA has na≈18, the paper should explicitly note this and provide complexity and memory analysis, including runtimes and scaling behavior with nv, nq, and na.
- Evaluation scope: Results are limited to multiple-choice VQA; open-ended accuracy is not reported. Comparisons to stronger contemporary baselines (e.g., MLB) under matched backbones are limited. The two-modality baseline is comparatively under-parameterized (d=512 vs. 2048 in some baselines), confounding comparisons.
- Ablations: There is no ablation isolating the contributions of unary, pairwise, and ternary terms (e.g., removing θV,Q,A; varying α/β/γ; multiple MF iterations). The observed improvement could stem from including answer candidates as inputs rather than from the specific high-order potential design.
- Presentation: Minor but pervasive notation issues and typos (e.g., aA mistakenly denoted aV in Eq. for attended answers), inconsistent descriptions of “convolution along the visual dimension,” and some figure/equation references are hard to follow.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The building blocks (dot-product correlations, linear projections, MCB/MCT) are standard. However, the mean-field claim is not substantiated with a formal model or derivation; thus the probabilistic interpretation is not rigorous. The ternary attention is feasible for MC VQA but its complexity and optimization stability are not systematically analyzed. Empirical results suggest the approach works, but the causal role of higher-order potentials is not conclusively established due to limited ablations.
- Experimental support: Training details are thorough, and qualitative visualizations are informative. Quantitative tables are referenced but not fully included in the text; clarity on absolute gains and statistical variation is needed.

Clarity
- The high-level idea is accessible, and the architecture overview is helpful. Several definitions (pairwise/ternary reductions, parameter shapes, precise pooling configurations) should be clarified. Minor typos and notation inconsistencies should be corrected. A concise formalization of the “probabilistic model” would improve clarity.

Originality
- Modeling attention with explicit k-th order potentials across modalities and claiming a mean-field interpretation is a novel framing relative to co-attention and bilinear pooling. The computational primitives overlap with existing pairwise fusion methods (MCB/MLB), but the extension to include answer candidates within the attention mechanism and the general k-order template are meaningful contributions for the 2017 timeframe.

Significance
- For VQA, the idea of directly attending over answer candidates and leveraging higher-order dependencies is practically useful and may influence subsequent multimodal attention designs. Broader impact is limited by the lack of a rigorous inference framework and by evaluation on a single benchmark/setting.

Questions for the authors
1. Please formalize the probabilistic model: What is the joint distribution/energy over attentions? How do Eqs. (1) arise from a mean-field update? Are α/β/γ interpretable under that model?
2. Precisely define the parameterization and shapes of wiv, wiq, wiv,ia in the pairwise/ternary reductions. Are these shared across indices or modality-specific MLPs?
3. Provide computational complexity and memory usage for computing C2 and C3 per batch, including runtimes. Confirm that na is the number of MC choices (≈18) during training/inference.
4. Report open-ended VQA results and add ablations: remove θV,Q,A; vary α/β/γ; test multiple MF iterations; compare to MLB/MFB with matched backbones.
5. Clarify whether the reported “state-of-the-art” compares under identical visual features and answer vocabularies.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, general, and extensible high-order attention framework with positive empirical results.
- Incorporating answer candidates into attention yields tangible gains in MC VQA.
- Released code and detailed training setup aid reproducibility.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of a rigorous probabilistic derivation; “mean-field” terminology appears superficial.
- Ambiguities in parameterization and computational complexity of higher-order terms.
- Limited evaluation scope and ablations; unclear how much the gain stems from the proposed potentials versus decision fusion choices.

Overall, this paper proposes a promising and general multimodal attention formulation with encouraging results, but it would benefit from a clearer formal foundation, stronger ablations, and broader evaluation.
