## NIPS 2017 Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to attention mechanisms, extending them to higher-order correlations between data modalities. The application to VQA demonstrates strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art performance. While the paper is clearly written and the ideas are sound, the presentation feels somewhat dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations and ablations. I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions, primarily focusing on clarity and experimentation.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the limitations of existing attention mechanisms in multimodal tasks like VQA – namely, their ad-hoc feature extraction and task-specificity. The authors propose a high-order attention model that learns correlations between *k* modalities, moving beyond pairwise interactions.  This is framed as a probabilistic model solved via mean-field inference. The approach is demonstrated on VQA, achieving state-of-the-art results.

The paper appropriately situates itself within the existing literature on attention mechanisms for both images and text, highlighting the contributions of Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25] as particularly relevant.  It effectively differentiates itself by offering a more generalizable approach that isn’t limited to two modalities and explicitly models higher-order correlations. The discussion of Kim et al. [14], Nam et al. [19] and bilinear models is also helpful in contextualizing the contribution.  The authors correctly point out the limitations of simply combining modalities before attention (Lu et al.) or ignoring attention on individual modalities (Xu et al.).

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of extending attention to learn higher-order correlations is genuinely novel and addresses a key limitation of existing models. This offers a more principled and flexible approach to multimodal interaction.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset is a significant accomplishment and provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Generality:** The authors convincingly argue for the generality of their approach, claiming it can be applied to any number of modalities.  The probabilistic formulation and mean-field inference provide a solid theoretical foundation for this claim.
* **Thorough Related Work:**  The paper provides a comprehensive review of relevant work in attention mechanisms, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field.
* **Clear System Architecture:**  Fig. 2 provides a helpful overview of the entire system, clearly illustrating the roles of data embedding, attention, and decision making.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity and Clarity:** The mathematical formulation, while correct, is quite dense.  The explanation of mean-field inference and the role of the various potentials (unary, pairwise, ternary) could be made more intuitive with simpler explanations or illustrative examples.  
* **Limited Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares to other state-of-the-art methods, it lacks a comprehensive ablation study to assess the individual contributions of the higher-order terms (pairwise vs. ternary). How much performance gain is attributable to moving from pairwise to ternary attention?  This is critical to understanding the value of the added complexity.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity. Were specific choices (e.g., the dimensionality *d*, learning rate, dropout rates) carefully tuned, or are the results robust to changes in these parameters?
* **Qualitative Results:** While the qualitative examples in Figs. 5-8 are helpful, they don't offer a systematic analysis of the attention maps.  A more rigorous analysis, perhaps comparing attention distributions across different question types or failure cases, would strengthen the qualitative evaluation.
* **Decision Making Unit:** The description of the MCB and MCT units feels somewhat cursory. While referencing the relevant paper [21], a more detailed explanation of how these units address the computational bottleneck of high-order correlations would be beneficial.



**4. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to multimodal attention.
* State-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark.
* Potential for broad applicability across different modalities and tasks.
* Well-written and thoroughly researched.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the method might hinder adoption.
* Limited ablation studies make it difficult to assess the contribution of individual components.
* Lack of discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity.



**5. Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Simplify the Mathematical Notation:** Consider using more intuitive notation and providing more detailed explanations of the equations, especially those related to mean-field inference.
* **Conduct a Thorough Ablation Study:**  Systematically evaluate the performance of the model with only unary potentials, unary + pairwise potentials, and all three levels of potentials.
* **Analyze Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Discuss the impact of different hyperparameters on the model's performance.
* **Provide a More Detailed Explanation of the Tensor Sketching Units:** Elaborate on how MCB and MCT reduce dimensionality and their impact on computational efficiency.
* **Expand the Qualitative Analysis:** Provide a more systematic analysis of the attention maps, comparing them across different questions and failure cases.




**Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of attention mechanisms. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work and increase its impact.**
