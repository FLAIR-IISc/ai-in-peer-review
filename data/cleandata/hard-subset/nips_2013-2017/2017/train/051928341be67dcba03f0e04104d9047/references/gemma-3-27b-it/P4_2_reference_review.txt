This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to model correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer). The core idea is to extend traditional attention mechanisms to capture not just pairwise interactions, but also higher-order relationships, implemented via probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. It positions the work within the context of existing attention mechanisms for both image and textual data, and does a reasonably thorough job of citing relevant work, including recent NIPS publications (e.g., [7, 15]). The proposed approach addresses a valid limitation of existing attention models – the explicit modeling of higher-order interactions between modalities. The use of potentials and mean-field inference provides a principled way to achieve this. The experimental results on the VQA dataset are compelling, demonstrating an improvement over existing methods. The ablation studies comparing the MCT and 2-layer MCB decision units are also valuable. The inclusion of qualitative results visualizing the attention maps adds to the understanding of the model's behavior.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the explanation of the core idea is clear, the mathematical details surrounding the mean-field inference and the specific implementation of the pairwise and ternary potentials could be more accessible. The description of the convolution operations within the potential calculations feels a bit hand-wavy. The authors mention that they used a lower embedding dimension (512) compared to some other state-of-the-art methods (2048), and attribute the initial performance difference to this. However, this feels more like justification than a critical analysis. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between dimensionality and model complexity would be beneficial. The failure case analysis (Fig. 8) highlights a limitation of the model, but the discussion is brief. A deeper exploration of such failure cases and potential remedies would strengthen the work.  The paper relies heavily on established components (CNNs for image embedding, LSTMs for question embedding), and the novelty lies primarily in the attention mechanism itself. While this is a valid contribution, the incremental nature of the advance should be acknowledged more explicitly.  The explanation of *why* high-order attention is beneficial is mostly intuitive; a more theoretical justification, perhaps linking it to representational capacity, would add value. The code is available which supports reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **State-of-the-art performance:** The proposed model achieves strong results on a standard benchmark.
*   **Novelty:** The idea of high-order attention is a natural extension of existing attention mechanisms and addresses a potential limitation.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical details.
*   **Thorough related work:** The paper demonstrates a good understanding of the existing literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Mathematical clarity:** Some of the technical details are not fully explained.
*   **Incremental novelty:** The improvement is incremental, building upon well-established components.
*   **Limited failure analysis:** The discussion of failure cases is superficial.
*   **Lack of theoretical justification:** The benefits of high-order attention are primarily intuitive.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of visual question answering. The proposed high-order attention mechanism is a novel and potentially valuable extension of existing techniques. While the paper has some weaknesses, the strong experimental results and clear presentation outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, focusing on improving the mathematical clarity, providing a more thorough analysis of failure cases, and discussing the theoretical implications of high-order attention.
