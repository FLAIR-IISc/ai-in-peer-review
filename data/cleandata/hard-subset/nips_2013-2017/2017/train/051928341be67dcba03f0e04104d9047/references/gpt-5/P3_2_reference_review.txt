Summary
The paper proposes a unified “high-order” attention mechanism for multimodal reasoning, instantiated on VQA. The core idea is to model attention as marginal distributions over latent variables associated with each modality (image V, question Q, candidate answers A), computed from a set of learnable potentials: unary (per-modality), pairwise (cross-modal similarity matrices), and ternary (a 3-way correlation tensor). The final per-modality attentions are obtained by a softmax over a learned linear combination of the respective marginalized potentials (presented as a single mean-field iteration). The attended modality vectors are then fused for prediction via Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) or the proposed Multimodal Compact Trilinear (MCT) pooling. Experiments on the VQA dataset report state-of-the-art results (particularly in multiple-choice) with fewer parameters than some baselines; qualitative visualizations suggest that higher-order terms steer attention more appropriately.

Relation to prior work
This work sits at the intersection of co-attention (e.g., Lu et al. NIPS’16), stacked attention (Yang et al.), dual/structured attention (Nam et al.; Kim et al.’17), and bilinear pooling methods for multimodal fusion (MCB [Fukui et al.], MLB [Kim et al. ICLR’17], MUTAN). Unlike prior two-modality co-attention, the paper explicitly defines k-ary potentials and claims a probabilistic interpretation with mean-field style inference, and extends the fusion to trilinear pooling (MCT). While tri-modal attention (including answers) for MC VQA is not new conceptually, the explicit formulation and modular decomposition into k-order potentials is an interesting perspective.

Strengths
- Clear modularization of attention into unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials with simple, scalable building blocks (1×1 convs / linear projections and inner products).
- Extensible formulation: in principle generalizes to any number of modalities.
- Practical fusion with tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) avoids exponential blow-up in decision layers.
- Strong qualitative visualizations; the contrast between unary and pairwise/ternary effects is convincing.
- Reported competitive performance with fewer parameters; code availability is promised.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Probabilistic claim and mean-field inference: The paper does not define a global energy/probability model (factors, variables, partition function). Equation (1) is a heuristic linear combination of potentials followed by softmax; calling this “mean-field” without an explicit derivation (from an energy over PV, PQ, PA with corresponding factors) is misleading. There is no discussion of multiple MF iterations, convergence, or how α, β, γ relate to factor weights.
- Ternary potential feasibility: Constructing C3 ∈ Rnq×nv×na is intractable if na is large (e.g., 3k answers) and still heavy even with na=18 candidates. The paper does not analyze complexity or memory, nor clarify whether na denotes the per-question MC set (18) or the global answer space (3k). Section 4.1 and 4.3 contain conflicting statements (top-3k answers vs MC). This is critical to assess scalability.
- Notational/implementation ambiguity: The weights wiv, wiq, wiv,ia used to marginalize C2/C3 are introduced but their parameterization, sharing, and dimensionality are unclear (scalars per location? 1×1 conv filters? learned vs fixed?). Similarly, shapes of Wq, Wv, Wa and whether they are shared across potentials need clarification.
- Limited evaluation scope: Results are presented mainly for multiple-choice; open-ended VQA accuracy (the community’s main metric) is missing, as are per-type accuracies (yes/no, number, other). No comparison to strong bilinear baselines beyond MCB (e.g., MLB, MUTAN) despite citing them; fairness issues (features, answer space, single vs ensemble) are not fully addressed. The paper mentions “state-of-the-art” but the actual numbers and table are not present in the text provided.
- Ablations insufficient: There is no systematic ablation isolating the contributions of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials (e.g., removing θV,Q,A, or varying αi/βi/γi), or the effect of using answers as a third modality (MC vs OE). The only reported ablation compares MCT vs 2-layer MCB.
- Attention evaluation: While qualitative visualizations are nice, quantitative measures (e.g., correlation with human attention [Das et al.]) are not reported. This would strengthen the interpretability claim.
- Theoretical novelty: The potentials reduce to linear projections and inner products; the main novelty is organizing them into a high-order attention “graph” rather than a fundamentally new attention operator. The decision component relies on existing sketching-based pooling.

Quality
Technically sound at the operator level (linear projections, inner/outer products, sketching). However, the probabilistic framing is underdeveloped, and the computational feasibility of the ternary term is not analyzed. Experimental evidence is promising but incomplete (missing OE, missing comparisons/ablations and full tables), making it hard to verify the SOTA claim.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, and the figures help. However, key definitions are ambiguous (w weights, sizes, na), and the “mean-field” language is not justified. The evaluation section needs complete tables, clearer dataset/version descriptions, and precise settings (ResNet variant, nq, preprocessing).

Originality
Moderate. The explicit high-order potential view across modalities and its integration with sketching-based fusion is a useful framing beyond standard co-attention, but the underlying operators (bilinear/trilinear correlations and their marginalizations) are close to existing bilinear attention and co-attention ideas.

Significance
If the claimed gains hold under fair comparisons and on open-ended VQA, the approach would be a useful contribution to multimodal attention design, particularly for tasks involving more than two modalities. As it stands, the impact is limited by missing evaluations and unclear scalability.

Questions for the authors
1) Please provide the explicit probabilistic model (energy/factor graph) and derive Eq. (1) as MF updates. Do you perform more than one MF iteration? What happens if you do?
2) Clarify na: is the ternary potential computed over 18 MC choices per example or the global top-3k vocabulary? Provide memory/time analysis and implementation details for C3.
3) Specify the parameterization of the marginalization weights (wiv, wiq, wiv,ia). Are these learned via 1×1 convs?
4) Provide comprehensive ablations: unary only; +pairwise; +ternary; with/without answer modality; and per-type accuracy.
5) Add comparisons to MLB and MUTAN under matched settings; report open-ended results.
6) Can you quantify attention quality vs human attention (HAT) and study sensitivity to d and feature backbones?

Pros
- Conceptually clean, modular high-order attention formulation
- Extends beyond two modalities; practical decision fusion (MCT/MCB)
- Strong qualitative results; plausible performance gains with fewer params
- Relevance to a central problem (VQA)

Cons
- Probabilistic claims not substantiated; MF framing superficial
- Scalability and implementation of ternary term unclear
- Incomplete empirical validation (OE VQA, strong baselines, ablations)
- Ambiguities and missing details hinder reproducibility

Recommendation
Borderline reject unless the camera-ready addresses the probabilistic derivation, scalability of the ternary potential, and adds thorough, fair comparisons (including open-ended VQA and strong bilinear baselines) with solid ablations. With these additions, I would lean to accept as a solid contribution to multimodal attention.
