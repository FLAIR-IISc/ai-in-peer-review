Summary
This paper proposes a general “high-order attention” framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions among modalities (image V, question Q, and answer A) via potentials and a single mean-field-style update to produce marginal attentions PV, PQ, and PA. Pairwise interactions are computed via correlation matrices C2 between modality embeddings; ternary interactions use a 3-way correlation tensor C3. The final decision combines attended features through compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) via tensor sketching. The method is evaluated on VQA (multiple-choice and classification) and reports state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters than strong baselines. Qualitative visualizations show that the proposed potentials yield interpretable and question-dependent attention.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on attention in VQA (co-attention, stacked/dynamic memory, MCB) and structured attention ideas. Compared to co-attention (e.g., Lu et al.) that alternates or parallels two-modality attention, and bilinear pooling (Fukui et al., Kim et al.), the novelty here is an explicit probabilistic view with high-order (≥3) potentials over modalities and a compact decision layer that can incorporate k-way outer-product statistics without exploding parameters. The authors fairly cite relevant work including MCB and structured attention; the proposed formulation is easier to extend conceptually to more modalities than prior co-attention formulations.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: An elegant and general formulation of attention as a set of learnable unary/pairwise/ternary potentials, with a mean-field-inspired marginalization step. This is a clear step beyond standard two-modality co-attention.
- Practicality: Despite modeling higher-order interactions, the decision stage uses MCB/MCT to control parameter growth; for multiple-choice VQA, the ternary tensor remains tractable.
- Empirical performance: Reports state-of-the-art on VQA with fewer parameters than strong baselines; the ablation showing gains from three-modality over two-modality supports the central claim.
- Interpretability: Visualizations of unary vs pairwise vs combined attention are compelling and help diagnose behavior.
- Generality: The formulation cleanly extends to any number of modalities in principle.

Weaknesses and concerns
- “Mean-field” claim is thin: The inference step is a single softmax over a learned linear combination of potentials. There is no derivation of a consistent probabilistic model or convergence beyond one iteration. This is closer to gated feature fusion than principled variational inference. Clarifying the underlying CRF/energy and the approximation would strengthen the paper.
- Computational complexity of high-order potentials: Computing C3 scales as O(d·nq·nv·na). This is only feasible in MC-VQA when na≈18; it is unclear how the approach scales to open-ended settings if one wanted to use A as input (na≈3000) or to more modalities. The paper claims “trivial extension,” but runtime/memory tradeoffs aren’t quantified.
- Scope of validation: Experiments are limited to VQA. No evidence is provided for generality to other multimodal tasks or for k>3. Ablations on the contribution of each potential (e.g., removing ternary or pairwise terms in Eq. 1) are sparse.
- Fairness of comparisons: Using candidate answers A as an input gives extra signal in MC-VQA. Some baselines may not use answer inputs, potentially inflating relative gains. Please clarify which baselines also condition on candidate answers.
- Clarity/typos: There are a few notational slips (e.g., repeated aV in the attended sums; minor figure text artifacts). Also, details on how na is set in each setting (MC vs open-ended) should be explicit.

Quality
Technically sound at the architectural level, with sensible design choices (1×1 convs, tensor sketching). The probabilistic framing is suggestive but underdeveloped. Empirical support is promising (claimed SOTA, improved with 3-modality), though the paper would benefit from more thorough ablations (weights α/β/γ; effect of C3; number of glimpses; backbone choices) and explicit comparison to answer-conditioned baselines. Runtime/memory reporting for C3 would improve credibility.

Clarity
Generally well organized with clear intuition and figures; some notation glitches and occasional ambiguous descriptions (how A is used across settings). The paper would be reproducible with the provided hyperparameters and code (link given).

Originality
High. Explicit high-order attention potentials and their integration via a light-weight mean-field step and MCT/MCB at decision time are a fresh and bold direction relative to prior co-attention/bilinear models.

Significance
High for VQA and potentially for broader multimodal learning. Even if the probabilistic interpretation is loose, the idea that higher-order attentional dependencies help—and can be made practical—is valuable and likely to inspire follow-up work.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a formal energy function and show that Eq. (1) corresponds to one mean-field iteration; discuss limitations of one-iteration inference and whether more iterations help.
- Quantify compute/memory cost for C3; report per-batch training time, and discuss how to handle open-ended settings and more modalities.
- Include ablations removing θV,Q,A and θV,Q terms, and report the learned α/β/γ weights to support claims about modality reliance.
- Clarify and align baselines regarding whether candidate answers are used as inputs; add a baseline that uses A in attention if not already present.
- Fix typos and notational inconsistencies.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Novel and general high-order attention formulation with clear empirical benefit.
- Strong results with fewer parameters; impactful for the VQA community.
- Compelling qualitative interpretability.
- Bold direction likely to inspire further research.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Probabilistic justification is not fully rigorous; “mean-field” is used loosely.
- Limited experimental breadth and ablation depth; scalability claims not fully substantiated.
- Potential unfairness if baselines do not condition on answer candidates.

Recommendation
Accept (for novelty and promising direction, despite some rough edges).

Confidence
High.
