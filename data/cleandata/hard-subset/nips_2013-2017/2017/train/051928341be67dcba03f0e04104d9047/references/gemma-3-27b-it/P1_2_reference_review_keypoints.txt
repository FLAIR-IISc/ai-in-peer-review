- Proposes a novel high-order attention mechanism for multimodal data, modeling interactions beyond pairwise (including ternary) via potentials and mean-field inference.  
- Applies the method to VQA and reports state-of-the-art performance, demonstrating empirical effectiveness.  
- The approach is general and can scale to more than two or three modalities, enabled by the mean-field inference formulation.  
- The formulation is theoretically grounded and mathematically clear, with well-defined equations for potentials and inference.  
- Includes an ablation study (MCT vs 2-layer MCB) that provides insight into the decision units’ effectiveness.  
- Related work is detailed and positions the contribution clearly relative to existing attention and pooling methods.  
- Limited qualitative analysis: attention visualizations are few and comparisons with baselines are insufficient.  
- No discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity; robustness to settings like learning rate and embedding size is unclear.  
- Computational cost is acknowledged but not analyzed in detail; overhead of high-order attention vs simpler methods is not quantified.  
- Uses tensor sketching to mitigate complexity, but lacks explicit computational/complexity comparisons.  
- Risk of overfitting due to additional parameters from higher-order potentials; only dropout is used without deeper generalization analysis.  
- Insufficient justification for the added complexity of third-order attention — unclear how much gain comes from the answer modality or ternary potential versus well-tuned pairwise models.  
- Paper is technically sound, complete, and careful in evaluation, with clear strengths and limitations noted.  
- Writing and organization are generally good, though the mean-field inference explanation can be expanded for readability.  
- The idea is original and the probabilistic framing is a clever extension of attention mechanisms.  
- The work is potentially significant given its expressiveness and flexible multimodal modeling capability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound, strong empirical results, clear presentation, and broad applicability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited qualitative analysis, missing hyperparameter and computational sensitivity discussion, and weak justification for third-order benefits.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept with Minor Revisions, contingent on adding qualitative analysis, computational/hyperparameter discussion, and clearer justification of third-order gains.
