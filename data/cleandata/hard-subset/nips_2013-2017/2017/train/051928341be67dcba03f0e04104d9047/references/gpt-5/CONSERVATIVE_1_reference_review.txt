Summary
The paper proposes a “high-order” multimodal attention framework for VQA that models unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image regions, question words, and (multiple-choice) answer candidates. Potentials are computed via 1×1 convolutions on embeddings and via inner-product “correlation” operators for pairwise (C2) and ternary (C3) interactions. The authors then form attention distributions by a learned linear combination of these potentials followed by softmax—framed as one iteration of mean-field inference. The attended vectors are fused for prediction using sketch-based pooling (MCB/MCT). The method is evaluated on VQA (test-dev and test-std, multiple choice), with qualitative visualizations and some ablations (MCT vs. two-layer MCB; 2- vs 3-modality).

Relation to prior work
The work is related to co-attention models (e.g., Lu et al. 2016 [15]), bilinear/low-rank pooling approaches (Fukui et al. 2016 [7], Kim et al. 2017 [13]), stacked/dynamic attention (Yang et al. 2016 [27], Xiong et al. 2016 [24]), and structured attention (Kim et al. 2017 [14]). The main claimed novelty is explicit higher-order potentials (including ternary) and a “probabilistic” view with mean-field updates, plus use of MCT for trilinear fusion.

Strengths
- Clear motivation to capture multimodal interactions beyond pairwise, and a consistent architecture for unary/pairwise/ternary potentials.
- Uses established components (1×1 convs, bilinear/trilinear correlations, MCB/MCT), keeping parameter count relatively modest (~40M).
- Qualitative visualizations are informative; failure cases are shown.
- Open-sourcing (not fully clear in anonymized review) would aid reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Probabilistic framing is superficial: there is no explicit energy/factor-graph formulation, no derivation of true mean-field updates, and only a single linear combination + softmax is applied. Calling this “mean-field inference” is misleading.
- Computational and memory complexity for ternary potentials is not addressed. Constructing or even convolving over C3 (nq×nv×na) is potentially prohibitive; no complexity/memory analysis or runtime comparison is provided.
- Experimental validation is limited: only VQA multiple-choice is reported. No open-ended VQA, no Visual7W, and no robustness tests across datasets. Claims of state-of-the-art are not convincingly substantiated in the excerpt (Table 1 not shown; unclear if comparisons are apples-to-apples in features, answer space, and training data).
- Use of answer candidates as an input modality can inflate MC performance and is not consistently adopted by compared baselines. Fairness of comparisons is unclear. The paper does not clearly explain whether A is the per-question candidate set or a global 3k vocabulary during attention.
- Limited ablations: there is no systematic study of the contributions of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials, no analysis of the learned α/β/γ weights, and no comparison to strong bilinear baselines under matched settings (e.g., MLB/MFB/MCB with identical backbones and answer spaces).
- Clarity/notation issues and missing details: shapes of tensors and “convolutions along a dimension” are not precisely defined; learnable weights wiv, wiq, wiq,ia are introduced but not described (are these shared kernels? how parameterized?).
- The decision module deviates from the probabilistic story (two-layer MCB for self-interactions) without principled justification.

Quality
Technical soundness is mixed. The core operators (inner-product correlations, 1×1 convs, sketch-based pooling) are sound and established. However, the probabilistic/mean-field interpretation is not properly derived; without an explicit energy and variational objective, the “inference” step is simply a learned gating/weighting. The computational tractability of ternary potentials is not demonstrated. Empirical evidence is not extensive enough to validate the necessity of truly high-order attention beyond stronger pairwise baselines.

Clarity
Readable at a high level, but important details are missing or ambiguous:
- Exact parameterization and shapes of potentials and the “convolutions” on C2/C3.
- Complexity, memory footprint, and runtime for pairwise/ternary blocks.
- Precise evaluation protocol for MC with answer input; consistency with baselines.
- The table with main numbers is referenced but not present in the excerpt; reported “val” gains (64.57 vs 63.82) are modest.

Originality
The idea of modeling higher-order interactions across modalities is natural, but the implementation largely composes known ingredients (co-attention-like similarities, tensor sketch pooling). Extending to ternary potentials is incremental; “trivial extension to any number of modalities” is asserted but not substantiated experimentally or computationally.

Significance
Given the limited empirical scope and unclear gains over strong pairwise baselines under matched conditions, the impact is uncertain. Without rigorous ablations and broader evaluation, it is hard to conclude that high-order potentials are necessary or superior.

Questions for authors
1) Provide a precise probabilistic model (energy, factors) and derive the mean-field updates you implement. If the current method is not mean-field, please revise the framing.
2) How is C3 computed/stored? What are nv, nq, na at training/testing time; what are the exact time/memory costs per batch; how does this compare to pairwise co-attention?
3) Clarify the answer modality: Are A the per-question candidates or the global 3k vocabulary? If per-question, how do you train? How do you ensure fair comparison to baselines that do not use answer choices as inputs?
4) Provide ablations: remove ternary, remove pairwise, unary-only; report deltas. Also report learned α/β/γ weights.
5) Include strong baselines with identical backbones (visual features, answer vocab, training data) and report both open-ended and MC on standard splits.
6) Explain and justify the two-layer MCB “self-interaction” design; can you integrate it into the probabilistic framework?

Minor/clarity
- Several typos and notation issues (duplicated phrases, mismatched indices). Define all variables (e.g., wiv, wiq, wiq,ia).
- The GitHub link appears non-anonymous.

Pros
- Clear motivation; unified view of multi-order interactions.
- Reasonable qualitative results; modest parameter count.
- Uses established, reproducible components.

Cons
- Weak theoretical grounding of the “mean-field” claim.
- Scalability of ternary attention not addressed.
- Limited, potentially unfair empirical validation; sparse ablations.
- Incremental novelty relative to co-attention/bilinear literature.

Recommendation: Weak reject (4/10). With a stronger, correct probabilistic formulation, thorough complexity analysis, fair and broader experiments, and solid ablations, this line could be impactful.

Confidence: 4/5.
