This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to model correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer). The core idea is to extend standard attention mechanisms to capture not only pairwise interactions but also higher-order correlations, implemented using probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the rapidly growing body of work on attention mechanisms in VQA. It acknowledges and cites key contributions like Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25] which explore joint attention over two modalities.  The key novelty lies in extending these approaches to *k*-order correlations, enabling modeling of interactions between any number of modalities. This is positioned as addressing the limitations of previous methods that often treat modalities in an entangled or task-specific manner.  The paper also connects to related work on bilinear models [13] and probabilistic attention [14], but argues its approach is more generally applicable and scalable to multiple modalities.  The use of tensor sketching (Pham & Pagh [21], Fukui et al. [7]) to handle the dimensionality explosion of higher-order tensors is also a noteworthy contribution building on existing work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of attention mechanisms to higher-order correlations is a valuable contribution.  It provides a more principled way to model complex interactions between modalities, going beyond simple pairwise attention.
* **Generality:** The proposed method is designed to be applicable to any number of modalities, which is a significant advantage over many existing VQA approaches that are tailored to image and question only.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper provides a reasonably clear explanation of the core concepts, including the probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference. The illustrations (Figures 3 & 4) are helpful.
* **Detailed Ablation Studies:** Comparison with MCT and a 2-layer MCB unit demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed decision units.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive, covering a wide range of relevant papers in the field.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity & Implementation Detail:** While the paper explains the general idea, the implementation details of the higher-order potential calculations (Equations 1 & the discussion around C2 & C3) are a bit dense and could benefit from further clarification. A more intuitive explanation of the convolution operations used is needed.
* **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** While some qualitative results are presented (Figures 5, 6, 7, 8), they are not particularly insightful. The comparison to other attention maps is somewhat superficial. More in-depth analysis of *why* the proposed model attends to certain regions would be beneficial.
* **Failure Case Analysis:** The single failure case presented in Fig. 8 doesn't offer much insight. A more systematic analysis of failure modes, and why the high-order attention *fails* in certain scenarios, would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The effect of hyperparameters, particularly the embedding dimension 'd' and the various regularization parameters, isn't thoroughly explored.  A sensitivity analysis would provide more confidence in the robustness of the results.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions the training time (40 hours) but doesn't discuss the computational cost of the higher-order attention mechanism compared to simpler approaches.  Understanding the computational trade-offs is important.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to VQA, addressing a significant limitation of existing methods.
* The empirical results are strong, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance.
* The generality of the method (applicable to multiple modalities) is a key strength.
* The paper is well-written and thoroughly cites related work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The implementation details are somewhat complex and could be clearer.
* The qualitative analysis is limited and doesn't provide deep insights into the model's behavior.
* The computational cost of the proposed method is not fully addressed.
* Some potential failure modes are not explored in depth.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of VQA. The proposed high-order attention mechanism is a novel and promising approach that achieves state-of-the-art results. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding the clarity of implementation details and the depth of the qualitative analysis, these are not severe enough to warrant outright rejection. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should focus on:

1. **Clarifying the Implementation:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of the convolution operations used in the potential calculations.
2. **Enhancing Qualitative Analysis:**  Provide a more in-depth analysis of the attention maps, explaining *why* the model attends to specific regions.  Include more examples and potentially visualize the higher-order correlations.
3. **Addressing Computational Cost:**  Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method and compare it to simpler alternatives.
4. **Expanding Failure Case Analysis:** Conduct a more systematic analysis of failure modes and explore potential reasons for the model's shortcomings.
