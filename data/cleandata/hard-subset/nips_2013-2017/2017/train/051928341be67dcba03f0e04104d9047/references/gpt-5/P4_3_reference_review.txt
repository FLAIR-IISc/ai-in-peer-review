Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a general high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, instantiated for visual question answering (VQA). The core idea is to model attention as a probabilistic module with learnable unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over visual (V), question (Q), and answer (A) modalities. Unary potentials score individual elements within a modality, pairwise potentials use similarity matrices (e.g., between Q and V) to capture cross-modal interactions, and ternary potentials use third-order correlations across V, Q, and A. The final attentions PV, PQ, PA are obtained by a softmax over a learned linear combination of these potentials, which the authors describe as a single mean-field iteration. The attended vectors are then combined via multimodal compact pooling (MCB/MCT) to make predictions. On VQA (multiple-choice), the method aims to achieve state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters than prior work.

The work is positioned relative to co-attention and bilinear pooling techniques: Lu et al. (NIPS’16) model joint attention across image and question; Fukui et al. (MCB, 2016) use compact bilinear pooling for pairwise fusion; Kim et al. (ICLR’17) study bilinear/low-rank pooling (MLB); Kim et al. (2017) and Nam et al. (2016) propose structured and dual attentions. The paper’s contribution is to unify unary, pairwise, and higher-order (ternary) dependencies in a framework that, in principle, extends to more modalities, and to show empirical gains on VQA (particularly when including answer candidates as a modality), building on count-sketch/tensor-sketch methods (Pham & Pagh, SIGKDD’13) to avoid exponential blow-up in the decision module.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The construction of unary and pairwise potentials is standard and implementable. However, the “probabilistic” interpretation and “mean-field” inference are not fully specified. No explicit joint energy/distribution is defined, and the presented updates (a linear weighted sum of potentials followed by softmax) are not derived from mean-field fixed-point equations. This makes the probabilistic framing non-rigorous.
- Practicality/complexity: The ternary potential requires computing third-order correlations C3 over V×Q×A. The paper does not detail how C3 is computed without prohibitive memory/time (nq×nv×na can be very large). If computed explicitly, this is impractical; if computed implicitly/streamed, the algorithmic trick should be clearly described. By contrast, tensor sketch is used later in the decision layer, but not to reduce the cost of C3 in the attention module. This is a significant missing detail.
- Empirical evaluation: The model is evaluated on VQA v1 multiple-choice only. While gains are reported and training efficiency is reasonable, the comparison to strong baselines is limited: open-ended accuracy is not reported; comparisons against MLB/MFB-type models under matched visual backbones/embedding sizes are missing; ablations are partial (2- vs 3-modality), but more granular ablations (remove ternary potentials; remove specific pairwise terms; vary α/β/γ weights) would clarify where improvements arise. Including answer candidates (A) as input should be compared fairly to prior work that also uses A (e.g., Jabri et al., 2016).
- Reproducibility: Many implementation details are provided (optimizer, dimensions, dropout), and code is linked. Some notation errors and incomplete descriptions (e.g., definitions of the convolution weights wiv, wiq, dimensionality/parameter sharing) limit reproducibility without the code.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, with intuitive figures. However:
- The probabilistic model is not formalized; mean-field claim lacks derivation.
- The ternary correlation computation and its complexity are unclear.
- Some notation/typos: duplicated “aV” where aA is intended; inconsistent description of “convolutions” on similarity matrices; some references lack proper venues/pages.
- The role and shape of the marginalization weights (e.g., wiv,ia) need to be specified (shared vs per-index; normalization).

Originality:
The idea of combining unary, pairwise, and higher-order attentions across modalities is a reasonable and, for ternary interactions including answers, relatively less explored direction compared to prior co-attention and bilinear pooling work. The use of tensor sketch for decision fusion is known; applying higher-order correlations within the attention mechanism itself (beyond pairwise similarity matrices) is the main novelty. The contribution would be strengthened by a principled derivation and a more efficient/clear implementation of C3.

Significance:
If the higher-order attention brings consistent improvements, the approach could influence multimodal modeling beyond VQA. However, the current empirical scope (VQA multiple-choice only), modest gains, and unclear computational profile of the ternary attention limit immediate impact. Demonstrating benefits on open-ended VQA and/or additional datasets (e.g., Visual7W) and providing a firm probabilistic derivation would increase significance.

Pros:
- Conceptual unification of unary, pairwise, and ternary attentions across modalities.
- Competitive performance on VQA with fewer parameters; clear engineering details and code.
- Compatible with additional modalities in principle; leverages compact pooling to control parameter growth in the decision stage.

Cons:
- Probabilistic/mean-field framing is not formally justified; updates appear heuristic.
- Ternary potential computation lacks complexity analysis and implementation detail.
- Evaluation limited to multiple-choice VQA; missing comparisons under matched settings to MLB/MFB and to methods using answer inputs.
- Some clarity/notation issues and incomplete references.

Questions for authors:
1) How is C3 computed without materializing the full nq×nv×na tensor? Provide algorithmic details and complexity (time/memory) and how it scales with typical VQA sizes.
2) Can you define the joint energy/distribution over attentions and derive the presented updates as mean-field approximations? If not, consider reframing without probabilistic claims.
3) What are the learned α/β/γ weights? Do they confirm which potentials dominate each modality’s attention?
4) How much of the gain stems from including A as an input? Please provide ablations (V+Q vs V+Q+A) under identical embedding sizes and backbones.
5) Results on open-ended VQA and/or other datasets?

Suggestions:
- Provide a formal probabilistic model and derivation, or remove “mean-field” terminology.
- Detail and, if needed, rework ternary attention with efficient approximations (e.g., low-rank/tri-linear factorization, implicit computation akin to tensor sketch).
- Expand ablations and matched comparisons to MLB/MFB and co-attention baselines; report open-ended metrics.
- Improve notation, fix typos, complete references, and clarify the parameterization of marginalization weights.

Overall: A promising direction with an interesting high-order attention formulation, but the current paper would benefit from a more rigorous derivation and clearer, broader empirical validation.
