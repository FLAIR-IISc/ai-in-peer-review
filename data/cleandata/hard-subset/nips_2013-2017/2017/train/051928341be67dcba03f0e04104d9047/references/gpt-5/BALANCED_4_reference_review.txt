Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a general high-order attention framework for multimodal tasks, instantiated for VQA. The core idea is to model attention over each modality (image V, question Q, and candidate answers A) using a set of learnable potentials: unary (per-modality salience), pairwise (cross-modal correlations, e.g., Q–V), and ternary (joint Q–V–A interactions). Attentions are produced by a softmax over a learned linear combination of these potentials, which the authors interpret as a single iteration of mean-field inference. The attended representations are fused for decision making using compact bilinear or trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) via tensor sketching. On VQA multiple-choice (MC), the method is reported to achieve state-of-the-art results with fewer parameters than some baselines, and the paper provides qualitative visualizations of unary, pairwise, and combined attentions.

This work connects to VQA co-attention and bilinear pooling lines. It goes beyond Lu et al. (2016) co-attention by explicitly constructing and combining unary/pairwise/ternary potentials rather than alternating or parallel attention without an explicit multi-order decomposition. Compared to Fukui et al. (2016) and Kim et al. (2017; MLB), which focus on pairwise bilinear fusion for decision, this paper adds high-order potentials for attention formation and considers answer-aware attention in the MC setting. It is also related to structured/latent attention (e.g., Kim et al., 2017; Rocktäschel et al., 2016), though the probabilistic grounding here is lighter-weight.

Strengths
- Clear modular design (unary, pairwise, ternary) that can, in principle, extend to more modalities.
- Sensible use of tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to handle high-dimensional fusion.
- Empirical gains on VQA MC with a smaller parameter budget than some baselines; reasonable training details provided; code link announced.
- Qualitative analyses showing how pairwise/ternary components shift attention conditioned on the question (and candidates).

Weaknesses
- Probabilistic claims are underdeveloped. The method is described as mean-field inference, but no energy function is specified, and the learned linear mixing of potentials with softmax does not follow from a principled variational derivation. As written, it reads as a heuristic weighted aggregation of potentials.
- Computational and memory complexity of ternary potentials is not addressed. Explicit C3 is O(nq nv na d), which is infeasible with na up to 3000. The paper should explain how ternary potentials are computed without materializing C3, what structural assumptions are made on the weights wiv,ia (e.g., separability), and provide runtime/memory measures.
- Narrow evaluation: only multiple-choice VQA is reported. Many strong works report both open-ended and MC; comparisons restricted to MC limit the generality of claims.
- Fairness of comparison: the 3-modality model consumes answer candidates as inputs. Many baselines score answers without using answer-aware attention to guide V/Q attention. The paper should compare to baselines that also condition on candidate answers under the MC protocol, and/or provide results for the standard image+question scoring setup to isolate contributions of high-order attention from the use of A.
- Ablations are limited. It is unclear how much each potential (unary/pairwise/ternary) contributes; how sensitive results are to the learned mixing coefficients (α,β,γ); and whether the improvements stem primarily from answer-aware conditioning vs. higher-order modeling. Only MCT vs 2-layer MCB is ablated.
- Clarity/notation issues: duplicated symbol for aV in Eq. (attended answers), undefined/underdefined convolutional weighting terms wiv, wiq, wiv,ia, missing dimensionalities/normalizations for Corr2/Corr3, and the lack of a formal description of the “mean-field” update.
- Related work coverage can be expanded, especially on trilinear/multimodal fusion contemporaneous to NeurIPS 2017 (e.g., MUTAN/Tucker fusion), and more thorough discussion of structured attention.

Quality
- Technical soundness is mixed. The architecture components are reasonable and empirically validated; however, the core probabilistic claim is not derived and the computational tractability of ternary terms is not demonstrated. Experimental coverage is limited to MC; stronger, controlled ablations are needed.

Clarity
- The paper is readable and motivates the design with helpful figures, but important details are missing or ambiguous (derivation, complexity, definitions of weights, and a precise loss/objective). Several notation typos should be fixed. Reproducibility would benefit from complete implementation details for ternary potentials.

Originality
- Moderately original. High-order attention over three modalities for attention formation (not just decision fusion) is a useful extension beyond pairwise co-attention. However, the components (correlation tensors, count sketch fusion) are known, and the “probabilistic” framing is not fully realized.

Significance
- Potentially useful if the high-order attention can be shown to scale and generalize. As presented, the impact is limited by evaluation scope (MC only) and missing analysis. Still, the conceptual decomposition may inspire follow-up work.

Pro arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually appealing decomposition into unary/pairwise/ternary potentials.
- Empirical improvements on VQA MC with fewer parameters.
- Good qualitative insights and compatibility with compact high-order fusion.

Con arguments for acceptance
- Unclear theory-to-implementation link (“mean-field” claim), missing energy/objective.
- Unaddressed computational cost and scalability of ternary attention.
- Limited evaluation scope and ablations; fairness of comparisons when using answer-aware attention.
- Clarity issues and incomplete related work.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a formal probabilistic model (energy over attention variables), derive the mean-field update, and justify/learn mixing coefficients under this model.
- Detail efficient computation of ternary potentials: avoid explicit C3 via separability/low-rank factorizations; provide time/memory benchmarks and complexity analysis.
- Expand experiments: open-ended VQA, Visual7W or VQA v2; report both VGG and ResNet backbones; include runtime/parameter counts; and statistical significance where feasible.
- Add ablations: remove ternary terms; pairwise-only; unary-only; fix or drop mixing weights; analyze sensitivity to d and to na; compare 3-modality vs scoring candidates without answer-aware attention.
- Ensure fair baselines that also condition on candidate answers for MC (e.g., answer-conditioned models, binary classifiers).
- Clarify notation (fix aA typo; define all weights; specify Corr2/Corr3 shapes and normalizations) and present the training objective explicitly.
- Broaden related work (e.g., Ben-Younes et al., 2017 MUTAN/Tucker fusion; other contemporaneous trilinear/multimodal attention) and position differences.

Recommendation
- Weak reject. The idea is promising and results are encouraging, but the paper needs a principled derivation, complexity analysis, stronger and fairer experimental evidence, and clearer exposition to meet the NeurIPS bar.

Confidence
- High (4/5): familiar with VQA, attention, and multimodal fusion literature.
