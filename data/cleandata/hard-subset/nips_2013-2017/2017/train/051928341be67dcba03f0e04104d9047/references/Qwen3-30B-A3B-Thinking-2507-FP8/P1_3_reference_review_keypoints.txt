- Summary: Proposes a high-order attention mechanism (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) with mean-field inference to model multimodal correlations for VQA, achieving state-of-the-art results.

- Novelty: Explicitly models high-order correlations between visual, textual, and answer modalities beyond typical pairwise attention.

- Formulation clarity: Probabilistic formulation and mean-field inference are well-motivated and clearly presented, enabling reproducibility.

- Experimental strength: Shows modest but consistent SOTA improvements on VQA with thorough ablation studies comparing 2- vs 3-modality models and decision units.

- Practical considerations: Uses tensor sketching to mitigate parameter explosion and justifies implementation choices (embedding dims, dropout).

- Qualitative analysis: Visualizations indicate attention focuses on meaningful image regions and relevant question words in a question-dependent way.

- Marginal gains: Performance improvement is small (~0.5–1%), needing analysis of practical significance and impact.

- Theoretical gap: Lacks rigorous theoretical justification of why high-order correlations provide additional representational power over pairwise models.

- Related work coverage: Limited comparison to more recent higher-order/structured attention work (e.g., Kim et al. 2017).

- Failure analysis: Insufficient analysis of failure cases beyond a single example; deeper investigation of limitations is needed.

- Implementation details: Some details are missing or underexplored (e.g., VGG vs ResNet comparisons, effect of embedding dimensionality).

- Overall assessment: Conceptually significant and generalizable, technically sound and reproducible, but with modest empirical gains.

- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance at NIPS based on the novelty and solid evaluation.

- Suggested improvements: Add theoretical justification, compare to recent related work, expand failure analysis, detail image-embedding experiments, and discuss generalization to other multimodal tasks.
