The paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering, aiming to address limitations in existing attention models by learning correlations between multiple data modalities. The core idea is to extend attention beyond pairwise interactions to k-th order correlations, modeled through potentials and mean-field inference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

While the concept of high-order attention is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to a lack of rigorous evaluation and incomplete ablation studies. The presentation, despite aiming for clarity, is often convoluted and obscures the actual contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of explicitly modeling high-order correlations in attention mechanisms is a potentially valuable direction.
*   **State-of-the-art Results:** The paper achieves competitive performance on the VQA dataset, suggesting the approach has some merit.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The related work section is extensive, although a bit disorganized.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** There are several inconsistencies in notation. For example, subscripts like 'iv' are used for both image indices and variable names, leading to confusion. The use of α, β, and γ parameters lacks sufficient explanation for their role beyond being “learnable”.
*   **Lack of Rigorous Analysis:** The theoretical justification for using a linear combination of potentials (Eq. 1) is weak. The authors state it “provides extra flexibility”, but this lacks formal justification. Why is this specific linear combination chosen? Is there a theoretical basis?
*   **Implementation Details Missing:** Several crucial implementation details are absent. The choice of activation function (tanh) in the unary potentials is stated without justification. Hyperparameter sensitivity isn't discussed. What batch size was used during training? What optimizer and learning rate were selected?
*   **Ablation Studies Insufficient:** The ablation study comparing MCT and 2-layer MCB is limited to a single dataset (val) and doesn’t explore the impact of varying the dimensionality of the MCB unit, or different convolutional kernel sizes. The claim about the 2-layer MCB is somewhat hand-wavy. The choice of 8192 as a hidden dimension for the tensor sketch seems arbitrary.
*   **Figures Lack Clarity:** Figures 3 and 4 are cluttered and difficult to interpret. The connections between the different components are not clearly indicated. The schematic representation of the potentials in Fig 3 doesn't effectively communicate the mathematical formulation.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim of “trivial extension to any number of modalities” is misleading. While the formalism *allows* it, the computational cost increases exponentially with the number of modalities, making it impractical for a large number of modalities.
*   **Failure Case Analysis:** The identified failure case (Fig. 8) highlights a significant limitation: the model can fixate on irrelevant features even when given different questions. This indicates a lack of robust reasoning.
*   **Vague Language:** Phrases like “state-of-the-art VQA system” and “relatively simple technique” are imprecise and unhelpful.
*   **Redundant Text:** Sections repeat information unnecessarily. For example, the explanation of the data embedding is repeated in multiple sections.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel idea of high-order attention.
*   State-of-the-art results.

**Con:**

*   Numerous implementation details are missing.
*   Insufficient ablation studies.
*   Lack of rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   Notational inconsistencies and poor clarity.
*   Overstated claims.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the core idea and the achieved results, the paper is fundamentally flawed due to its lack of rigor, insufficient detail, and numerous minor errors. The presented work feels incomplete and leaves many important questions unanswered. The claims are not sufficiently substantiated, and the lack of robust ablation studies prevents a fair assessment of the contribution. The numerous minor flaws accumulate to create a significant impediment to understanding and reproducing the reported results.

**Recommendation:** **Reject.**

The authors need to address the aforementioned weaknesses by providing a more rigorous theoretical analysis, conducting comprehensive ablation studies, clarifying the notation, and providing complete implementation details. Without these improvements, the paper does not meet the standards for publication at NIPS. It is a promising idea, but the current presentation falls short of a high-quality scientific contribution.
