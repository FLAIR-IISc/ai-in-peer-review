

# Peer Review: "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (visual, textual, and answer) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors present a probabilistic framework where attention is computed through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, with mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities. The model is applied to VQA, with results showing state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset. The approach is distinct from existing pairwise attention models (e.g., [15, 25]) by explicitly modeling higher-order interactions between modalities. The paper demonstrates that incorporating ternary potentials (modeling interactions between all three modalities: image, question, and answer) leads to improved performance over pairwise attention approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Conceptual Contribution**: The paper identifies a gap in existing attention models (which typically model pairwise interactions) and proposes a principled approach to model higher-order correlations. This is a meaningful extension of attention mechanisms that has not been explored in depth in the literature.

2. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and intuitive illustrations (Fig. 3, 4, 5, 6, 7) that effectively demonstrate the proposed attention mechanisms.

3. **Solid Experimental Validation**: The authors provide thorough quantitative results on standard VQA datasets, comparing against strong baselines (Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]). The performance gains (64.57% on val dataset) are consistent with their claims of state-of-the-art results.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The paper effectively leverages tensor sketching (via MCB and MCT) to handle the computational complexity of high-order correlations, making the approach feasible for practical implementation.

5. **Good Related Work Coverage**: The paper provides a comprehensive survey of attention mechanisms in VQA, clearly distinguishing their approach from existing pairwise attention methods.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Marginal Performance Gains**: While the paper reports state-of-the-art results, the improvement over strong baselines is marginal (e.g., 64.57% vs. 63.82% for MCT vs. 2-layer MCB on val dataset). This raises questions about the practical significance of the contribution. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate that high-order attention is *necessary* for the observed improvement rather than just a small performance boost from additional model capacity.

2. **Lack of Comprehensive Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials). For instance, how much does the ternary potential contribute to the performance gain over the pairwise model? The paper mentions that the 3-modality model outperforms the 2-modality model, but doesn't quantify the contribution of each order of attention.

3. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification**: The authors claim that high-order correlations are beneficial, but provide limited theoretical justification for why higher-order interactions would improve VQA performance. They don't address when high-order attention might be detrimental or how to determine the appropriate order of attention for a given task.

4. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper doesn't compare against the most recent state-of-the-art VQA approaches (e.g., the paper by Tan et al. [22] at NIPS 2017, which achieved 65.5% on VQA test-dev). This omission weakens the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance.

5. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper mentions tensor sketching to overcome computational bottlenecks, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the computational complexity of their approach compared to existing methods. How much slower is their model during training/inference? This is particularly important given their claim of "generally applicable" to any number of modalities.

6. **Implementation Details**: The paper uses a 512-dimensional embedding space, while many recent VQA models (e.g., [7]) use 2048 dimensions. The authors don't adequately justify why they chose this smaller dimensionality, which could impact the model's capacity to learn complex representations. This makes it difficult to fairly compare their results with other methods.

7. **Limited Failure Analysis**: The paper provides only a single failure case (Fig. 8), which is insufficient to understand the limitations of their approach. A more comprehensive analysis of failure modes would strengthen the paper.

## Specific Recommendations for Improvement

1. **Expand Ablation Studies**: Conduct thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each attention order (unary, pairwise, ternary) and demonstrate that high-order attention is indeed the key factor driving performance gains.

2. **Compare Against Recent SOTA**: Include comparisons against the most recent state-of-the-art VQA methods (e.g., Tan et al. [22]) to better position their contribution.

3. **Provide Theoretical Justification**: Add a section discussing when high-order attention would be beneficial (and when it might not be) and why it improves performance on VQA. This could include a discussion of the types of questions where high-order interactions are particularly important.

4. **Include Computational Analysis**: Add a table comparing the computational complexity (training time, memory usage) of their approach with existing methods.

5. **Address Dimensionality Choice**: Justify why they chose a 512-dimensional embedding space instead of the more common 2048 dimensions used in other VQA models.

6. **Expand Failure Analysis**: Provide a more comprehensive analysis of failure cases to help readers understand the limitations of their approach.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically sound extension of attention mechanisms to model high-order correlations between data modalities, which is a meaningful contribution to the literature on multimodal learning. However, the practical significance of the contribution is limited by the marginal performance gains over existing methods and the lack of thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of high-order attention.

While the paper makes a good case for the conceptual novelty of high-order attention, the empirical evidence supporting its practical benefits is not as strong as it could be. The approach is technically sound and well-executed, but the incremental nature of the improvement (compared to existing methods) and the lack of comprehensive analysis of the contribution of high-order attention make it difficult to assess the true significance of the work.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept**

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful conceptual contribution to attention mechanisms. However, the marginal performance gains and lack of thorough analysis of the high-order attention component prevent it from being a strong accept. With the recommended revisions (particularly expanding ablation studies and comparing against recent SOTA), this would be a strong paper. As it stands, it's a marginal accept for NIPS.

## Pro/Con Acceptance

**Pros:**
- Conceptually novel approach to modeling high-order attention
- Well-structured presentation with clear mathematical formulations
- Solid experimental validation on standard VQA benchmarks
- Good comparison against relevant baselines
- Practical implementation using tensor sketching to handle computational complexity

**Cons:**
- Marginal performance gains over existing methods
- Insufficient ablation studies to isolate contribution of high-order attention
- No comparison against the most recent SOTA VQA methods
- Limited theoretical justification for why high-order attention improves performance
- Inadequate discussion of computational complexity and implementation choices

## Final Comments

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of attention mechanisms in multimodal learning. While the performance improvements are modest, the conceptual framework for high-order attention is sound and could inspire future work in this direction. The authors should address the weaknesses identified above, particularly by providing more thorough ablation studies and comparing against the most recent state-of-the-art methods. With these improvements, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
