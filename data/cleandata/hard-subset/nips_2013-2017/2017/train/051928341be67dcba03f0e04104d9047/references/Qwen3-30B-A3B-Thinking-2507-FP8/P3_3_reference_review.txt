

# Peer Review: "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors introduce unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture correlations at different orders, using mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities. They claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA with a relatively simple model (40 million parameters) compared to previous approaches (70+ million parameters).

## Strengths

1. **Novel Conceptual Framework**: The paper offers a clear probabilistic interpretation of attention as a model with high-order potentials, which is a valuable perspective beyond typical pairwise attention mechanisms. The distinction between unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials provides a structured way to model multi-modal interactions.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The model achieves competitive results on VQA, with a 0.75% improvement over MCT (64.57% vs. 63.82% on validation set). The qualitative analysis (Fig. 5-7) effectively demonstrates how attention correlates with question content, providing interpretability.

3. **Clear Methodology**: The mathematical formulation is well-organized, and the architecture (Fig. 3-4) clearly illustrates the proposed mechanism. The implementation details (Section 4.1-4.3) are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Questionable Novelty**: The paper's core contribution—modeling attention across multiple modalities—is not as novel as claimed. The authors cite [7, 15, 25] as prior work, but these papers already explore multi-modal attention. The ternary potential is essentially an extension of existing bilinear models to three modalities, and the probabilistic framework (mean-field inference) is standard in graphical models. The claim that their approach is "easy to extend to any number of modalities" lacks sufficient evidence.

2. **Insufficient Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (unary vs. pairwise vs. ternary potentials). The modest 0.75% improvement over MCT (Table 1) could stem from factors other than the high-order attention mechanism itself, such as better feature representations or hyperparameter tuning.

3. **Misleading Performance Claims**: The paper claims "state-of-the-art" performance but fails to compare against more recent VQA models published after the cited papers (e.g., 2017-2018 works). The parameter count (40M) isn't significantly smaller than competitors (e.g., [7] uses 70M+ parameters), and the training time (40 hours) is comparable to previous works.

4. **Weak Justification for Higher-Order Attention**: The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why ternary attention (involving answer) is necessary. The qualitative examples (Fig. 6) don't clearly show that the answer attention contributes uniquely to the final answer beyond what pairwise attention could achieve. The improvement over 2-modality models might be due to better feature engineering rather than the high-order aspect.

5. **Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method. Ternary potentials require O(n_v × n_q × n_a) operations, which could be prohibitive for large-scale applications, whereas existing bilinear models are more efficient.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents an interesting extension of attention mechanisms to capture higher-order correlations between modalities in VQA. While the technical approach is sound and the results are competitive, the novelty is incremental rather than transformative. The paper would benefit from more thorough ablation studies, stronger justification for the need of high-order attention, and comparison against more recent state-of-the-art VQA models.

The significance of the work is limited by the modest performance gains and lack of clear evidence that the high-order attention mechanism is the key factor driving these gains. The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field of attention mechanisms but doesn't represent a major advance over existing work.

## Recommendation
**Weak Reject**

While the paper is technically sound and presents a reasonable extension to attention mechanisms, the novelty is insufficient to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The contribution appears incremental rather than transformative, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that high-order attention is necessary for VQA. The modest performance gains (0.75% improvement) don't justify the claim of "state-of-the-art" without comparison to more recent methods.

The authors should address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing ablation studies to isolate the contribution of high-order attention and comparing against more recent VQA models to establish true state-of-the-art performance. Without these improvements, the paper's contribution would be difficult to distinguish from existing work in the field.

The paper would be better suited for a conference with a broader scope or a workshop focused on attention mechanisms, rather than NIPS, which prioritizes more significant advances in machine learning.
