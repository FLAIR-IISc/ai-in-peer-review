

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) for visual question answering. The authors introduce unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials with mean-field inference to compute attention probabilities, claiming state-of-the-art performance on VQA datasets.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in multimodal learning and presents a clear framework for extending attention mechanisms beyond pairwise interactions. The conceptual approach of modeling high-order correlations is interesting and potentially valuable for multimodal tasks. The paper is well-organized, with clear descriptions of the components, and the qualitative results in Figures 5-7 provide some intuitive insights into the attention mechanisms.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Inconsistencies
The most significant issue is the fundamental mischaracterization of the inference procedure. The paper claims to use "mean-field inference" (Section 3.2), but Equation (1) simply combines potentials linearly followed by softmax. Mean-field inference involves iterative updates to approximate marginal probabilities, not a single linear combination. This is a critical technical error that undermines the paper's core claim.

The paper contains multiple inconsistent indexing errors in the equations:
- In Equation (1), the authors write "θV,Q,A(iv)" but earlier define θV,Q,A as a ternary potential that should depend on all three modalities (iv, iq, ia), not just image indices.
- In Section 3.2.2, the pairwise potential definitions have inconsistent indexing: "θV,Q(iq) = tanh(∑iv wiv (C2)iv,iq)" should be θQ,V(iq) but the indexing doesn't match the summation over iv.
- In Section 3.2.3, the ternary potential definition has similar indexing issues that make the equations difficult to interpret.

### Methodological Flaws
The paper claims to extend to "any number of data modalities" but only demonstrates unary, pairwise, and ternary cases. The authors provide no explanation of how to handle 4th-order or higher correlations, which is necessary to support their claim of general applicability.

The paper states "we use the top 3000 most frequent answers" (Section 4.3), but the VQA dataset has only 1000 possible answers for multiple-choice questions. This discrepancy needs clarification.

The claim of "state-of-the-art" performance lacks sufficient context. The authors compare to Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7] (both from 2016), while the VQA challenge has seen significant improvements since then (e.g., from the 2017 VQA Challenge). The paper doesn't provide a clear comparison to recent SOTA methods.

### Presentation Issues
The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example, "aV" is used both for the attended image vector and as an index variable (in "aV = ∑iv PV(iv)viv"), which is confusing.

The paper claims to use "VGG features" without specifying whether VGG-19 or VGG-16 is used, which is important as they have different feature dimensions.

The paper states "the MCT setup yields 63.82% where 2-layer MCB yields 64.57%" but doesn't specify which dataset this is on (test-dev or test-std), making it difficult to assess the significance of this small improvement.

### Theoretical Gaps
The paper provides no theoretical justification for why high-order attention would be beneficial, beyond empirical results. The specific form of the potentials (linear combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary terms) is not motivated theoretically.

The paper claims their approach is "easy to extend to any number of data modalities" but provides no evidence or analysis of how the computational complexity scales with the number of modalities.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting conceptual framework for multimodal attention, the technical errors, inconsistent notation, and lack of clear comparison to recent SOTA methods are substantial flaws. The mischaracterization of mean-field inference as a single linear combination is particularly problematic as it undermines the core contribution. The paper also fails to properly address the scope of their claim regarding "any number of modalities."

The paper is not technically sound in key aspects, and the presentation contains numerous inconsistencies that would make it difficult for other researchers to reproduce or build upon the work. The claimed state-of-the-art performance is not sufficiently supported by the comparison to recent literature.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem and contains some valid insights, the technical inaccuracies and methodological flaws are too significant to overlook. The paper would require substantial revisions to address the indexing errors, clarify the mean-field inference claim, provide proper comparisons to recent SOTA methods, and resolve the inconsistencies in the presentation.
