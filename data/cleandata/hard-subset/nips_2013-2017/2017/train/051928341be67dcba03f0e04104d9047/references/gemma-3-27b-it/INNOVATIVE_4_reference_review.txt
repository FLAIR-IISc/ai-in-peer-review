This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering, aiming to model correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer). The core idea—extending attention beyond pairwise interactions to capture higher-order dependencies—is a compelling and novel direction. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art results on VQA, suggesting the approach holds promise.

The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, building from motivation and related work to the proposed method and experimental validation. The explanation of the mathematical formulation, particularly the mean-field inference for obtaining marginal probabilities, is reasonably clear. The inclusion of qualitative examples (Figs. 5, 6, 7, 8) is helpful for understanding how the attention mechanism behaves. The systematic exploration of different decision-making units (MCT vs. 2-layer MCB) demonstrates a thoroughness often lacking in submissions.

However, the paper feels somewhat incomplete in its validation. While SOTA performance is reported, the gains over existing methods, while present, aren't overwhelmingly large given the increased complexity. The ablation studies, while present, could be more extensive. For instance, a clear comparison showing the individual contribution of second-order vs. third-order potentials would be valuable. The sensitivity analysis regarding the embedding dimension (d) could also be improved—the authors acknowledge the lower dimension compared to other SOTA approaches and its potential impact, but don't fully explore the trade-offs.

The failure case illustrated in Fig. 8 is particularly telling. The consistent attention focus despite differing questions points to a potential weakness in the model’s ability to truly differentiate based on query content, suggesting an over-reliance on dominant visual features. This needs further investigation.

Furthermore, while the authors claim ease of extension to *any* number of modalities, the computational cost of higher-order potentials isn't explicitly addressed. The tensor sketching approach mitigates this somewhat, but the scalability limitations with increasing modalities are not thoroughly discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The high-order attention mechanism is a genuinely novel contribution, moving beyond the standard pairwise attention prevalent in VQA. The probabilistic framing and mean-field inference are interesting and provide a principled approach to modeling these interactions.
*   **State-of-the-art performance:** Achieving SOTA results, even modestly, validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and the mathematical details are explained reasonably well.
*   **Thorough experimentation:** The inclusion of multiple comparison baselines and exploration of decision-making units demonstrate a comprehensive evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incremental improvement:** The performance gain is not a dramatic leap over existing methods, and the added complexity of high-order attention might not be fully justified.
*   **Incomplete validation:** Ablation studies could be more extensive, and a more detailed analysis of the model's limitations is needed.
*   **Scalability concerns:** The computational cost of high-order potentials, while addressed with tensor sketching, is not fully discussed.
*   **Failure case:** The example in Fig. 8 raises concerns about the model’s ability to truly attend to question-relevant features.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor flaws and incomplete validation, I lean towards **acceptance**. The novelty of the high-order attention mechanism and the demonstrated SOTA performance outweigh the concerns. This paper introduces a promising new direction for multimodal attention, and the potential for future work building upon this foundation is significant. I would encourage the authors to address the points raised above in a future iteration of the work, particularly strengthening the ablation studies and discussing scalability limitations. The potential impact of this idea is high enough to warrant acceptance even with these current limitations.
