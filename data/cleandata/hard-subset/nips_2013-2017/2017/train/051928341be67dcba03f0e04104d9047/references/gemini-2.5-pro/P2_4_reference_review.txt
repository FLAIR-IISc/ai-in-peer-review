### Paper Review: High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for multi-modal tasks, demonstrated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea is to model attention as a probabilistic process over different data modalities (image, question, and potential answers). The authors introduce a framework based on "potentials" of increasing complexity: unary potentials (importance of features within a single modality), pairwise potentials (correlations between two modalities), and ternary potentials (correlations across all three modalities). These potentials are calculated and then linearly combined with learnable weights to produce final attention distributions for each modality. The authors describe this process as a single mean-field inference step. The attended feature vectors are then fused for final decision-making using Multimodal Compact Bilinear/Trilinear (MCB/MCT) pooling. The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of modeling higher-order interactions.

This work builds upon a rich literature of attention in VQA. It is closely related to co-attention models like Lu et al. (2015), which also explore pairwise interactions between questions and images. It also shares the decision-making module (MCB) with Fukui et al. (2016). The primary distinction and contribution of this work is the formalization of attention as a combination of multi-order potentials in a single, extensible probabilistic framework, and specifically, the novel inclusion of a third-order (ternary) potential that explicitly models the interaction between the image, question, and answer modalities.

---

**Detailed Comments:**

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound for the most part, with strong empirical results backing its central claims. The experimental setup is standard, and the comparisons to relevant baselines are appropriate. The ablation study showing the benefit of the 3-modality model over a 2-modality version effectively supports the main thesis that higher-order attention is beneficial.

However, there are a few points where the technical description raises questions:
1.  **Mean-Field Inference:** The claim of performing "mean-field inference" seems slightly overstated. The model performs a single, feed-forward step where pre-computed potentials are linearly combined and passed through a softmax (Eq. 1). While this can be interpreted as one iteration of a mean-field update, the term typically implies an iterative process to reach a converged distribution. This phrasing could be clarified to more accurately reflect the feed-forward nature of the computation.
2.  **Computational Cost of Ternary Potential:** The calculation of the third-order correlation tensor `C3` appears computationally prohibitive. As described, it is a tensor of size `nq x nv x na`, where each entry is an inner product. For typical values (e.g., nq=20, nv=196, na=3000), this results in a very large intermediate tensor (~12 million entries). The subsequent summations to get `θV,Q,A` would be extremely expensive. The paper provides no details on how this is implemented efficiently or if there are approximations involved. This is a critical omission, as it impacts both the feasibility and reproducibility of the work. Without this clarification, the soundness of the practical implementation is in question.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-articulated, and the structure logically flows from the general framework to the specific application. The figures, particularly Figure 3 illustrating the different potential modules, are very helpful in understanding the architecture.

The main area lacking clarity is the implementation detail mentioned above concerning the ternary potential. A reader would find it very difficult to reproduce this work without making significant assumptions about how to handle the computational and memory costs of `C3`. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of this calculation in the main text or an appendix.

**Originality:**

The paper presents a novel and interesting conceptualization of multi-modal attention. While individual components are not entirely new—pairwise attention via similarity matrices is common (e.g., Lu et al., 2015), and the use of MCB for fusion is borrowed from Fukui et al. (2016)—the key originality lies in the synthesis and extension of these ideas into a unified, high-order framework.

The primary novel contributions are:
1.  The probabilistic formulation of attention as a combination of learnable, multi-order potentials. This provides a principled way to fuse information from different interaction levels.
2.  The introduction of a ternary (third-order) potential that explicitly models the three-way interaction between image, question, and candidate answers. To my knowledge, this is a novel approach in the VQA literature and is shown to provide a significant performance boost.

The work is well-situated within the existing literature, and its differences from prior co-attention and bilinear pooling models are clearly explained.

**Significance:**

The results presented are significant. Achieving state-of-the-art on the competitive VQA benchmark demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method. The model achieves this with fewer parameters than some competing methods (40M vs. 70M for Fukui et al.), suggesting a more efficient architecture.

The conceptual contribution is also significant. The idea of building attention from a hierarchy of potentials is elegant and could inspire new architectures for other multi-modal reasoning tasks beyond VQA. The framework's potential for extension to `k` modalities is an attractive feature for future research.

One potential limitation to its broader significance is the model's reliance on a fixed set of candidate answers to compute the answer-related potentials (`θA`, `θV,A`, etc.). This makes it directly applicable to multiple-choice VQA but less straightforward to adapt to open-ended VQA, where the answer space is effectively infinite. This limitation should be acknowledged and discussed by the authors.

---

**Strengths:**
*   **Novel Framework:** Proposes a principled and extensible framework for high-order attention based on probabilistic potentials.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieves state-of-the-art performance on a challenging and standard VQA benchmark.
*   **Novel Ternary Attention:** The introduction of a third-order potential involving the answer modality is a key novel contribution that is shown to be effective.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written, and the core ideas are communicated effectively through text and figures.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Unclear Computational Feasibility:** The paper completely omits any discussion of the computational and memory costs of the ternary potential, which appear to be prohibitively high as described. This is a major weakness that affects reproducibility and soundness.
*   **Overstated Technical Claim:** The "mean-field inference" framing is slightly misleading for what is a single feed-forward computation.
*   **Limited to Multiple-Choice:** The model's design relies on a pre-defined set of candidate answers, limiting its direct applicability to open-ended generation tasks.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper with a novel idea and excellent results. The main concern is the lack of detail regarding the implementation of the ternary potential, which is a critical part of the contribution. If the authors can clarify that this is computationally feasible (e.g., via a low-rank approximation or other efficient implementation trick not mentioned), I would recommend acceptance. As it stands, the paper makes a strong claim without providing sufficient evidence for its practicality.
