

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) for visual question answering. The authors propose a probabilistic framework with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to obtain marginal attention probabilities. This approach fundamentally differs from prior work that primarily models pairwise interactions (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25]), which typically focus on two modalities rather than modeling higher-order correlations.

The key novelty lies in the conceptual shift toward modeling attention as a multi-order probabilistic model that can systematically incorporate correlations between any number of modalities. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA, which is impressive given the relatively simple architecture (40 million parameters) compared to prior methods (70+ million parameters). The paper's technical formulation is clear, with well-organized figures illustrating the unary, pairwise, and ternary attention mechanisms.

The paper's main strength is its conceptual novelty. While most attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks have focused on pairwise interactions (e.g., image-question, image-answer), this work systematically extends the framework to model interactions between three or more modalities. This represents a meaningful conceptual advancement in attention modeling, as it provides a principled way to incorporate higher-order dependencies that are often present in complex multimodal tasks. The authors properly situate their work within the literature, clearly differentiating their approach from prior attention mechanisms that primarily handle pairwise interactions.

The experimental results on VQA are compelling, demonstrating that high-order attention can improve performance over pairwise attention models. The qualitative analysis (Fig. 5-8) effectively shows how the attention mechanism focuses on relevant regions of the image and question words, providing interpretability that aligns with the task requirements.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses that could be addressed in revision. The improvement over pairwise attention is marginal (63.82% vs. 64.57% on validation set with different MCB implementations), which raises questions about the practical significance of the high-order approach. The paper does not sufficiently explain why high-order attention is necessary for VQA rather than simply using more complex pairwise attention layers or deeper networks. The paper also fails to compare with more recent VQA approaches (e.g., those presented at NIPS 2017), which would strengthen the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance.

The theoretical formulation of the ternary potentials (Section 3.2.3) is somewhat unclear. The paper states that "C3 is a 3D tensor" but doesn't fully explain how the correlations are computed or why the specific formulation (Eq. 3) is appropriate. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the computational complexity of the high-order approach compared to existing methods.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper's conceptual contribution sufficiently novel to warrant acceptance. The high-order attention framework represents a significant conceptual step beyond current attention mechanisms and could inspire future research in multimodal learning. The marginal performance gains are acceptable given the novelty of the approach, and the paper provides a clear path for extension to any number of modalities.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the following points:
1. Provide a clearer explanation of why high-order attention is necessary for VQA, not just why it works
2. Compare more thoroughly with recent VQA approaches (2017 papers)
3. Clarify the mathematical formulation of ternary potentials
4. Discuss the computational complexity of their approach

The paper makes a valuable conceptual contribution that could influence future attention mechanism design. While the empirical gains are modest, the theoretical foundation is strong and the approach is generalizable to other multimodal tasks. This work represents a meaningful step toward more sophisticated attention mechanisms that can model complex interactions between multiple data modalities, which is an important direction for future research in multimodal learning.
