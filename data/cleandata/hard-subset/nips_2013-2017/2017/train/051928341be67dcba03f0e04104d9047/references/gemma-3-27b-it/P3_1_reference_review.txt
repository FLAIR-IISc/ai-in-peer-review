## Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel approach to attention mechanisms by extending them to higher-order correlations between multiple modalities. The idea is conceptually sound and addresses a recognized limitation of existing attention mechanisms – their often task-specific and modality-entangled nature. The authors demonstrate promising results on VQA, achieving state-of-the-art performance. However, the paper suffers from a lack of thorough ablation studies and a somewhat superficial comparison to existing work. While the results are encouraging, the presentation and justification of design choices could be significantly improved. I recommend **weak accept** with major revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper introduces high-order attention models that learn correlations between different data modalities (image, question, answer). It proposes modeling these relationships using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, learned via a mean-field inference approach. The authors apply this framework to the VQA task, demonstrating competitive results compared to existing state-of-the-art methods.

The paper accurately positions itself within the context of existing attention mechanisms for both images and text, citing relevant works like Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25], and Fukui et al. [7].  It correctly points out the limitations of previous approaches in handling multiple modalities effectively and the tendency towards task-specific design.  The authors appropriately highlight the contribution of their work as a more general and flexible attention mechanism. The connection to probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference is a relevant and potentially valuable framing.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of extending attention to higher-order correlations is a significant contribution. It offers a principled way to model complex interactions between multiple data modalities.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented as generally applicable, not just to VQA. The authors highlight the potential for adaptation to other multimodal tasks.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset is a strong indicator of the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing attention mechanisms and motivates the need for a more general and expressive framework.
* **Well-written Introduction:** The introduction provides a good overview of the field and the motivation for the work.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** A major weakness is the absence of thorough ablation studies. It's unclear how much each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) contributes to the overall performance.  Did removing the ternary potentials significantly impact results? What is the benefit of going beyond pairwise interactions?  This is crucial for understanding the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** While the paper compares against a few baselines, the comparison feels somewhat superficial. A more comprehensive comparison against more recent and competitive VQA models is needed.  Why were specific baselines chosen, and what are their strengths/weaknesses relative to the proposed approach?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters. How were the hyperparameters (learning rate, dropout rates, embedding dimension, etc.) chosen?  Are the results robust to different hyperparameter settings?
* **Complexity of Implementation:** The authors claim the approach is easier to extend than alternatives like bilinear models, but the implementation details, particularly concerning the tensor sketching and mean-field inference, seem complex.  A more detailed discussion on the computational cost and scalability would be beneficial.
* **Qualitative Analysis is Limited:** While visualizations are provided, they are somewhat difficult to interpret and don't offer significant insight beyond what one might expect from attention mechanisms. A more in-depth qualitative analysis, possibly with error case analysis, would be valuable.
* **Failure Case Discussion:** The description of the failure case (Fig. 8) is insufficient. More analysis of why the model fails in these scenarios would be beneficial, and it would help guide future work.
* **Minor: Formatting issues:** There are a few instances of awkward phrasing and formatting throughout the paper.



**4. Originality:**

The approach is original in its application of high-order potentials to attention mechanisms. While the individual components (attention, mean-field inference, tensor sketching) are not entirely novel, their combination within this specific framework represents a significant contribution.

**5. Significance:**

If the claims are validated through more rigorous experimentation, this work could have a significant impact on the field of multimodal learning. A more general and expressive attention mechanism has the potential to improve performance across a wide range of tasks.  The ability to capture high-order interactions between modalities could be particularly beneficial in complex scenarios where subtle relationships are crucial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging VQA dataset.
* Presents a novel and potentially powerful approach to attention mechanisms.
* Addresses a recognized limitation of existing attention mechanisms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks thorough ablation studies to justify the complexity of the model.
* Limited and somewhat superficial comparison to existing baselines.
* Implementation details are complex and not fully explained.
* Hyperparameter sensitivity is not discussed.




**Recommendations for Revision:**

* **Conduct extensive ablation studies:** Systematically remove or modify components (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) to assess their individual contributions.
* **Compare against a wider range of baselines:** Include more recent and competitive VQA models in the evaluation.
* **Perform a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis:** Investigate the impact of different hyperparameter settings on performance.
* **Provide a more detailed explanation of the implementation:** Clarify the details of the tensor sketching and mean-field inference process.
* **Improve the qualitative analysis:**  Offer more insightful interpretations of the attention visualizations and analyze error cases.
* **Address the computational complexity:** Discuss the computational cost and scalability of the approach.
* **Strengthen the discussion of the failure case:** Provide a more detailed analysis of why the model fails in specific scenarios.




In conclusion, this paper presents a promising new approach to attention mechanisms. However, the lack of thorough experimentation and analysis prevents me from recommending a strong acceptance at this time. With major revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, this work could become a significant contribution to the field.
