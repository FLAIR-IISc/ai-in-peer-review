This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that aims to model correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer). The core idea is to move beyond pairwise interactions and capture higher-order dependencies using potentials and mean-field inference. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing limitations of existing attention mechanisms that often treat modalities in an ad-hoc manner. The problem setting – capturing high-order interactions in multi-modal data – is certainly relevant and important for cognitive tasks like VQA. The approach, building on probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference, is conceptually sound and offers a principled way to model these interactions. The thorough related work section effectively positions the contribution within the broader landscape of attention mechanisms and highlights the novelty of considering higher-order correlations. The experimental evaluation demonstrates strong results, achieving state-of-the-art performance on VQA. The ablation studies comparing different decision units (MCT vs. 2-layer MCB) and the visualizations of attention maps provide valuable insights.

However, there are several weaknesses. First, the presentation is quite dense and, at times, difficult to follow. While the mathematical formulation is presented, the intuition behind the mean-field inference and the benefits of higher-order potentials aren't always clearly articulated. More intuitive explanations and diagrams would be helpful. Second, the improvements over existing methods, while statistically significant, are not always dramatic. A deeper analysis of *why* the higher-order attention helps would be beneficial. Are specific types of questions or images where the benefits are most pronounced? Third, the failure case highlighted (Fig. 8) reveals a limitation: the model can be misled by spurious correlations. The authors should discuss this issue more explicitly and potentially explore techniques to mitigate it (e.g., regularization, adversarial training). Fourth, the ablation studies are somewhat limited. For example, evaluating the contribution of the ternary potential in isolation (without the pairwise and unary potentials) would provide clearer evidence of its effectiveness. Finally, the computational cost of the proposed approach isn’t thoroughly discussed. Mean-field inference can be expensive, and a detailed analysis of the computational complexity would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper introduces a novel approach to attention mechanisms, moving beyond pairwise interactions to capture higher-order correlations.
*   **Strong performance:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset.
*   **Sound methodology:** The use of probabilistic modeling and mean-field inference provides a principled framework for modeling multi-modal interactions.
*   **Thorough related work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of existing attention mechanisms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Dense presentation:** The paper is difficult to follow in parts, lacking intuitive explanations.
*   **Modest improvements:** The performance gains are not always substantial.
*   **Limited analysis:**  A deeper understanding of *why* the approach works and its limitations is needed.
*   **Computational cost:** The computational complexity is not fully addressed.

**Overall assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The idea of high-order attention is novel and promising, and the results are encouraging. However, the paper needs significant revisions to improve clarity, provide a more in-depth analysis, and address the limitations. I recommend **rejection** with strong encouragement to resubmit after addressing the reviewers' concerns. Specifically, the authors should focus on improving the clarity of the presentation, providing a more detailed analysis of the benefits and limitations of the approach, and addressing the computational cost. Including more extensive ablation studies would also strengthen the paper.
