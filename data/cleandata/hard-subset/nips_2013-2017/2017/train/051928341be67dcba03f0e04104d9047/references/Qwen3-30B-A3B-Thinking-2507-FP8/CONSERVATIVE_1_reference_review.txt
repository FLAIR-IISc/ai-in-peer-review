

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel "high-order attention" mechanism for visual question answering that models correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) at different orders (unary, pairwise, ternary). The authors claim their approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA datasets by effectively capturing high-order dependencies between modalities, in contrast to existing single-modality attention approaches.

The paper makes several claims: (1) their method is the first to explicitly model high-order correlations in attention; (2) their approach is easily extendable to any number of modalities; (3) their method achieves SOTA results with fewer parameters than existing approaches; and (4) their high-order attention provides better interpretability as shown in qualitative results.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a clear and well-organized technical description of their attention mechanism.
- The qualitative results in Figures 5-7 provide some evidence that their attention mechanism focuses on relevant regions in the image and question.
- The implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.
- The paper correctly acknowledges related work, particularly the connection to MCB and MCT techniques.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's primary weakness is its insufficient evidence to support the claimed novelty and performance benefits. The authors' claim that "high-order attention" is novel is unsubstantiated. While the paper cites Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25], it fails to properly contextualize their approach within the broader literature on multimodal attention. In fact, the "high-order" aspect they describe appears to be a straightforward extension of existing bilinear attention mechanisms (e.g., the "outer product" approach in [7]), which has been shown to capture pairwise interactions. The ternary potential they introduce is essentially a trilinear extension of the bilinear model, which has been explored in prior work (e.g., [13] for trilinear models).

The claimed "state-of-the-art" performance is problematic for several reasons. The paper compares against Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7], but these are not the strongest baselines for VQA. The current SOTA on VQA 2.0 as of 2017 (when this paper was submitted) was achieved by models like SAN [12] and LXMERT [14], which the authors fail to compare against. Without these comparisons, the claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is unsubstantiated. The reported improvement (64.57% vs. 63.82% for MCT) is modest (0.75% absolute improvement) and lacks statistical significance testing.

The paper also fails to provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each component. For example, it doesn't show how much pairwise vs. ternary attention contributes to the final performance, nor does it compare against a simpler model that uses only pairwise attention. The claim that their method is "simpler" than existing approaches is questionable given the complexity of their high-order potential formulation.

The theoretical justification for high-order attention is weak. The paper states that "high-order correlations" are important, but provides no evidence that these correlations are necessary for VQA performance. The paper doesn't show that high-order interactions contain information not captured by lower-order ones, which is crucial for justifying the added complexity.

The paper's claim that their model with 40M parameters outperforms models with 70M+ parameters is misleading. The comparison isn't fair because the authors use a lower embedding dimension (512) compared to 2048 in existing models. As the authors themselves note, "higher embedding dimension and proper tuning can improve our 2-modality starting point." This undermines their claim of efficiency.

The qualitative results (Figures 5-7) are promising but don't provide strong evidence for the superiority of their approach. The attention visualizations don't show a clear comparison to how a strong baseline (like [15] or [7]) would have performed on the same examples.

**Recommendation:**
This paper does not meet NIPS's standards for methodological soundness and rigorous validation. The claimed novelty is unsubstantiated, the performance claims are not sufficiently supported by comprehensive comparison against strong baselines, and the theoretical justification for high-order attention is weak. While the approach is technically sound and the implementation is clear, the paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed "high-order" attention provides meaningful improvements over existing methods that would justify its novelty.

The paper would require substantial revision to be acceptable for NIPS. Specifically, it needs: (1) comprehensive comparison against the strongest VQA baselines from the period (including models that use bilinear pooling and beyond), (2) ablation studies demonstrating the contribution of each component, (3) statistical significance testing for performance improvements, and (4) a stronger theoretical justification for why high-order attention is necessary.

As submitted, the paper does not make a sufficient scientific contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The claimed novelty is not supported by evidence, and the improvements over existing methods are modest and not rigorously validated against appropriate baselines. The paper would be better suited for a venue that prioritizes incremental improvements over theoretical novelty.
