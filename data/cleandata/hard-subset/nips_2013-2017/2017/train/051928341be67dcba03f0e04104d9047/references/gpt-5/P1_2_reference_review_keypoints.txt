- Proposes a multimodal attention framework combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to produce per-modality attention distributions for VQA.  
- Core idea: capture higher-order correlations among image regions (V), question words (Q), and candidate answers (A) and fuse attended vectors with tensor sketching (MCB/MCT).  
- Unary potentials via 1×1 convs; pairwise via similarity matrix C2 marginalized with learned 1×1 convs; ternary via third-order tensor C3 reduced by learned weights.  
- Novelty: explicitly formulates higher-order interactions in attention computation (not only final fusion) and claims a mean-field probabilistic motivation.  
- Empirical claim: state-of-the-art multiple-choice VQA results with fewer parameters than some prior MCB-based models plus qualitative attention maps.  
- Strength: unified, extensible framework including unary/pairwise/ternary interactions across modalities.  
- Strength: practical implementation using efficient bilinear/trilinear sketching for decision fusion.  
- Strength: end-to-end trainable with simple modules and provided engineering details.  
- Strength: including answer candidates as an attention modality is an interesting and distinguishing design choice.  
- Weakness: probabilistic/mean-field claim is not rigorously derived and the update rule does not match standard mean-field expectations.  
- Weakness: ternary attention computational complexity is unclear and appears prohibitively expensive (nq*nv*na*d) without described optimizations.  
- Weakness: limited evaluation—only VQA v1 multiple-choice reported; open-ended, VQA v2, and other datasets missing.  
- Weakness: insufficient ablations (e.g., unary vs pairwise vs ternary, learned weight effects, answer-as-modality impact, feature-dimension robustness).  
- Weakness: comparisons to strong baselines under matched settings are incomplete; Table 1 not fully visible.  
- Weakness: clarity issues—notation typos, terse figure captions, and unclear “convolution along a dimension” operations.  
- Quality: technically module-level sound, but probabilistic framing and efficiency claims are not substantiated.  
- Originality: moderately novel—folding ternary interactions into attention is new, but pairwise/bilinear ideas are well-studied.  
- Significance: potentially useful if higher-order attention is shown to be efficient and generalizes across datasets; currently limited by evaluation and complexity analysis.  
- Questions for authors: how is C3 computed efficiently for large na and batch sizes (algorithm, memory, runtime)?  
- Questions for authors: can you provide a proper mean-field derivation or reframe as a learned mixture-of-potentials?  
- Questions for authors: what are the learned α,β,γ weights and do they indicate when higher-order terms matter?  
- Questions for authors: do results hold on VQA v2 and open-ended evaluation?  
- Suggestions: add ablations on attention orders and answer modality, provide complexity/runtime analysis and efficient approximations for C3, and expand experiments to VQA v2/open-ended with matched baselines.  
- Suggestions: clarify notation and marginalization operations, fix typos, improve figures, and report confidence intervals or multiple runs.  
- Minor: correct notation aV → aA in Section 3.2 and ensure GitHub code reproduces reported numbers.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject pending rigorous derivation or reframing, clear computational treatment of ternary attention, and broader empirical validation.
