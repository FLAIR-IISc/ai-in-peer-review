Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a general attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over multiple modalities (image V, question Q, and candidate answer A) to compute attention distributions for each modality. Unary potentials are computed via 1×1 convolutions (linear layers with tanh), pairwise potentials via similarity matrices (e.g., QWq(VWv)ᵀ) aggregated along one dimension, and ternary potentials via a trilinear correlation tensor C3 that captures interactions across all three modalities. The authors then form attention distributions by a learned linear combination of the (marginalized) unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials followed by a softmax, which they interpret as a single mean-field iteration. Attended features are composed with Multimodal Compact Bilinear/Trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) to produce answer scores. They report state-of-the-art results on VQA (multiple-choice), present ablations comparing MCT vs two-layer MCB, and provide qualitative attention visualizations.

Relative to prior work, the paper connects to co-attention over image and question (Lu et al., NIPS’16), bilinear pooling and compact sketching (Fukui et al., MCB; Kim et al., MLB), dual attention (Nam et al.), structured attention (Kim et al.), stacked/multi-hop attention (Yang et al.; Xu & Saenko), and dynamic memory networks (Xiong et al.). The distinctive claim is an explicit high-order potential view that unifies unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions and is purportedly extensible to any number of modalities (including the answer modality as input, as explored in Jabri et al.).

Strengths:
- Clear and general conceptual decomposition of attention into learnable unary, pairwise, and ternary components; easy to see how to plug in additional modalities.
- Empirically, adding the answer modality and higher-order potentials appears to help VQA (especially in multiple-choice), and the qualitative maps are sensible and often question-sensitive.
- Uses compact sketching (MCB/MCT) to keep decision modules tractable; compares MCT vs a two-layer MCB design and provides reasonable training details and implementation notes.
- The framework offers interpretability: different potentials can be visualized to understand contributions of each modality/modal interaction.
- Claimed modest parameter count relative to some baselines.

Weaknesses:
- The “probabilistic” justification and “mean-field inference” are superficial. There is no well-defined energy, factor graph, or variational objective; the final attention is a learned linear mixture of potentials + softmax. Calling this mean-field is misleading without a derivation or iterative updates.
- Limited experimental support: main quantitative table is referenced but not included; results are only for multiple-choice VQA; there is no evaluation on open-ended VQA or VQA v2.0, and no comparisons to strong contemporaneous bilinear/trilinear baselines that also use the answer as input (e.g., Jabri et al.). Reported ablations are minimal (MCT vs two-layer MCB, 2- vs 3-modality); there is no systematic ablation of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials or the learned weights α/β/γ.
- Computational aspects of ternary attention are under-specified. Constructing C3 over nv×nq×na can be prohibitive except in multiple-choice (na≈18). It is unclear how weights w are parameterized and implemented, and whether the approach scales beyond three modalities or to open-ended settings (na≈3000).
- Some clarity/notation issues: typos (duplicated aV where aA is intended), ambiguous dimensions and parameterization of the convolution/aggregation along specific axes, and missing details about “glimpses,” number of attention steps, or whether multi-iteration refinement is possible.
- The performance gains may largely stem from using the answer candidates as an input modality (as noted in Jabri et al.), which is not disentangled from the proposed high-order potentials via a comparable baseline.

Quality:
- Technical soundness is mixed: the attention modules are sensible engineering, but the probabilistic framing is not rigorously developed. Empirical section lacks comprehensive baselines and ablations to firmly attribute gains to high-order potentials. Efficiency/scalability analysis is missing.
- Training details are given, and code is said to be released, which helps reproducibility, but core comparisons and full results are absent in the text provided.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, with helpful figures and qualitative visualizations. However, several notational inconsistencies and missing shapes/complexities impede full understanding. A clearer algorithmic description and precise definitions of all learned weights would help.

Originality:
- The idea of unifying unary/pairwise/ternary attentions across modalities is a reasonable extension of co-attention and bilinear pooling, and positioning attention computation as a combination of potentials is interesting. However, related ideas (co-/dual attention, structured attention, bilinear/trilinear pooling with sketching, using answers as inputs) exist; the novelty lies mostly in the explicit high-order potential decomposition and its use within attention.

Significance:
- If the claimed SOTA holds with fair, thorough comparisons, this would be an incremental but useful contribution to multimodal attention design. The framework could influence future multi-modality tasks. As written, the limited evaluation scope and weak theoretical grounding reduce impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a principled probabilistic formulation: define the joint energy over attention variables, derive mean-field updates, and optionally unroll multiple iterations.
- Include comprehensive ablations: remove/keep unary/pairwise/ternary terms; vary the α/β/γ weights; show effects of including A as input vs not, and compare to answer-conditioned baselines (Jabri et al.).
- Expand evaluation: report open-ended VQA and VQA v2.0; compare against MLB/MFH and more recent co-attention models under matched visual features and answer sets.
- Clarify computational complexity and implementation: how is C3 computed for multiple-choice vs open-ended? Exact shapes and parameter counts for the w weights; runtime/memory comparisons.
- Improve clarity and fix notation/typos; provide pseudocode for attention computation; discuss number of “glimpses” and whether iterative refinement improves performance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually simple, general high-order attention framework with intuitive visualizations.
- Potentially strong empirical performance and practical utility when answer candidates are available.
- Extensible to more modalities and compatible with standard compact pooling.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Probabilistic justification is weak; “mean-field” is a misnomer without derivation.
- Evaluation is limited (only MC VQA) and lacks critical baselines/ablations; unclear attribution of gains.
- Scalability and efficiency of ternary attention are under-specified; clarity issues remain.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and likely useful, but the paper needs a more rigorous formulation and stronger, broader empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards.

Confidence: Moderate.
