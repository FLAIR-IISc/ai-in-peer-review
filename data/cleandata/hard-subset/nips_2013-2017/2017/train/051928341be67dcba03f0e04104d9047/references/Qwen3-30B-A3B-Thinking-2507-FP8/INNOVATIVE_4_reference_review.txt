

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper introduces a novel high-order attention framework that models correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) at various orders (unary, pairwise, ternary, etc.) for visual question answering (VQA). The key insight is that current attention mechanisms primarily model pairwise interactions between modalities, while the proposed approach explicitly captures higher-order correlations through a probabilistic model with high-order potentials. The authors develop a mean-field inference approach to compute attention probabilities from these potentials, enabling a unified framework for any number of modalities.

The paper makes a strong case for the novelty of their approach. While previous work like Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25] model pairwise attention between image and question, they don't explicitly consider higher-order correlations. The authors correctly identify this limitation and propose a framework that can trivially extend to any number of modalities. Their approach is fundamentally different from existing methods that use outer products or concatenation to combine modalities.

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach on VQA, achieving state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset. The qualitative analysis (Fig. 5-7) effectively shows how the high-order attention mechanisms produce more interpretable attention maps that better align with the question content. The ablation study (Table 1) clearly shows the importance of high-order attention, with the 3-modality model significantly outperforming a 2-modality baseline.

Strengths:
1. **Novel conceptual contribution**: The high-order attention framework represents a genuinely new direction in attention modeling, moving beyond pairwise interactions to capture richer correlations between multiple modalities. This is a significant conceptual leap that could influence future work in multimodal learning.
2. **Strong empirical results**: The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA with a relatively simple architecture, demonstrating the practical value of the proposed approach.
3. **Clear motivation**: The paper effectively identifies limitations in current attention mechanisms and positions their work as a solution to these limitations.
4. **Good presentation**: The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes helpful visualizations of the attention mechanisms.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't provide a thorough comparison with all recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., the paper by Kazemi and Elqursh [11] is mentioned but not compared to in detail).
2. The decision-making component uses MCB/MCT pooling, which has been used in previous work (Fukui et al. [7]), though the paper does correctly note this.
3. The paper doesn't explore the potential limitations of high-order attention (e.g., whether higher-order potentials beyond ternary become less meaningful or computationally prohibitive).

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find the paper's conceptual contribution to be sufficiently groundbreaking to outweigh these minor weaknesses. The high-order attention framework represents a significant new direction in attention modeling that could be applied to various multimodal tasks beyond VQA. The paper's claim that "high-order attention is important" is well-supported by their experimental results.

While the paper doesn't push the state-of-the-art by a massive margin (e.g., 2-3% improvement), it achieves this with a more conceptually elegant approach that could inspire future work. The paper's value lies in its novel perspective on attention modeling rather than incremental improvements to existing techniques.

The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and demonstrates the value of the proposed framework through both quantitative and qualitative results. The authors properly situate their work within the existing literature and acknowledge related approaches.

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by introducing a novel conceptual framework for attention modeling that has the potential to influence future research in multimodal learning. The high-order attention approach represents a significant conceptual advancement over current attention mechanisms, which is exactly the kind of bold, novel idea that NIPS should prioritize.

Recommendation: Accept
