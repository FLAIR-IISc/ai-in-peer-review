### Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

**Summary of the paper:**
This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism for multi-modal tasks, demonstrated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The central thesis is to move beyond conventional attention mechanisms by explicitly modeling high-order correlations between different data modalities. The authors propose a probabilistic framework where attention is computed based on unary, pairwise, and ternary "potentials." These potentials represent the importance of individual features (unary), the correlations between pairs of modalities (e.g., image-question), and the interactions among three modalities (image-question-answer). The final attention distributions are derived via a single-step mean-field approximation, which combines these potentials in a learned, linear fashion. The resulting attended feature vectors are then fused for final classification using Multimodal Compact Bilinear/Trilinear pooling. The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper lies in its conceptual novelty and the boldness of its approach. It reframes the problem of multi-modal attention from a purely architectural challenge to one of probabilistic modeling of high-order interactions. This is a significant and welcome departure from the incremental improvements often seen in this space.

1.  **A Principled and Generalizable Framework:** The idea of modeling attention through k-th order potentials is elegant and powerful. It provides a clear, interpretable, and theoretically extensible framework for integrating an arbitrary number of modalities. While prior work on co-attention (e.g., Lu et al., 2016) explored pairwise interactions, this paper's formulation is more general and principled. This move towards a more structured understanding of multi-modal fusion is a substantial contribution that could inspire a new line of research.

2.  **Inclusion of the Answer Modality in Attention:** The decision to treat the potential answers as a third modality and incorporate them directly into the attention mechanism via ternary potentials is a particularly novel and insightful idea. Most VQA models only use the answers at the final classification stage. By allowing the answer candidates to influence the attention on the image and question, the model can actively seek evidence that supports or refutes specific hypotheses. This is a clever mechanism that directly contributes to the model's strong performance, as evidenced by the reported gap between their 2-modality and 3-modality models.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The novel concept is not merely a theoretical proposition; it is backed by state-of-the-art results on a competitive benchmark. Achieving top performance while using a model with fewer parameters than some competing methods (e.g., Fukui et al., 2016) underscores the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed attention mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**
While the core idea is compelling, there are aspects of the methodology and evaluation that could be strengthened. However, these are minor in the context of the paper's main contribution.

1.  **Loose "Mean-Field Inference" Analogy:** The paper frames the calculation of attention probabilities as "mean-field inference." While this provides a nice probabilistic intuition, the implementation is a single, feed-forward step involving a learned linear combination of potentials followed by a softmax. Calling this mean-field inference is a slight overstatement, as it omits the iterative nature of the actual algorithm. This is more of a terminological critique than a fundamental flaw, as the implemented mechanism is clear and effective.

2.  **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper would be more impactful with a more thorough ablation analysis. While the comparison between the 2-modality and 3-modality models is crucial, it would be highly beneficial to understand the relative importance of the different potential terms. For instance, an analysis of the learned weights (α, β, γ) in Equation (1) could reveal which potentials the model learns to rely on for different modalities. A quantitative breakdown of the performance contribution from unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials would provide deeper insight into why the model works.

3.  **Unexplored Details in Decision Making:** The finding that a 2-layer MCB unit outperforms the conceptually more direct MCT unit for fusing the three modalities is an interesting empirical result. However, the paper offers little intuition for this beyond a brief mention of "greater expressiveness." A more detailed explanation or analysis of this architectural choice would be valuable.

**Recommendation:**
I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It introduces a bold, novel, and highly significant idea: a principled framework for high-order attention in multi-modal learning. The conceptual leap from architecturally-defined co-attention to a probabilistic model of multi-way correlations is precisely the kind of forward-thinking research that NIPS should champion. The paper’s main contribution—the high-order potential framework—is a powerful concept that is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. The strong empirical results validate the approach. The identified weaknesses, such as the need for more detailed ablations and a slightly loose analogy to mean-field inference, are minor and do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. This work prioritizes a significant new direction over methodological perfection, and in doing so, pushes the field forward in a meaningful way.
