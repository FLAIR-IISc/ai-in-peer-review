

# Peer Review: "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism that models high-order correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., image, question, answer) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors propose a probabilistic framework where attention is computed using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture interactions between different modalities. The approach generalizes beyond the typical pairwise attention mechanisms used in previous work, which were limited to two modalities. The authors demonstrate that their high-order attention model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset. The paper is well-structured, with thorough related work, clear methodology, and comprehensive experimental results.

The work relates to previous NIPS papers on attention mechanisms, particularly Lu et al. (NIPS 2016) on hierarchical question-image co-attention and Fukui et al. (ICLR 2016) on multimodal compact bilinear pooling. The authors correctly identify that most prior attention mechanisms for VQA are limited to pairwise interactions between two modalities, which motivates their high-order approach.

## Strengths

1. **Novel contribution**: The paper makes a clear and meaningful contribution by generalizing attention mechanisms beyond pairwise interactions to higher-order correlations between multiple modalities. This is a significant extension to the current state of the art, which has largely focused on pairwise attention.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA benchmarks, outperforming previous approaches with a relatively simple model. The quantitative results on both test-dev and test-std datasets are convincing.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors provide both quantitative results and qualitative analysis of attention visualizations, showing how the model focuses on relevant regions of images and words in questions. The comparison with prior work (Lu et al. and Fukui et al.) is well-executed.

4. **Clear methodology**: The paper presents a well-organized and technically sound approach. The probabilistic framework with mean-field inference is clearly explained, and the implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

5. **Practical significance**: The approach demonstrates that high-order attention mechanisms can improve performance on multimodal tasks, which has implications for other tasks involving multiple data modalities beyond VQA.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: While the paper provides empirical evidence that high-order attention improves performance, it doesn't sufficiently explain *why* high-order interactions matter. A more detailed analysis of the specific scenarios where high-order correlations provide advantages would strengthen the paper.

2. **Limited ablation study**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to show the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) to the final performance. It's unclear how much each component contributes to the state-of-the-art results.

3. **Computational complexity concerns**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the high-order attention mechanism, particularly for higher-order interactions. For k-th order attention, the complexity grows exponentially with k, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.

4. **Limited generalization beyond 3 modalities**: While the paper claims the approach is generalizable to any number of modalities, it only demonstrates the method with 3 modalities (image, question, answer). A discussion of practical limitations when extending to more than 3 modalities would be valuable.

5. **Comparison with recent work**: The paper mentions "structured attention networks" (Kim et al., 2017) and "dual attention mechanisms" (Nam et al., 2016) but doesn't thoroughly compare with these approaches. A more comprehensive comparison with recent high-order attention models would strengthen the paper's position in the literature.

## Quality Assessment
The paper is technically sound and well-supported by empirical results. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is appropriate for the task. The authors provide a thorough comparison with relevant baselines and demonstrate state-of-the-art results. The paper appears to be a complete piece of work rather than a position paper. However, the lack of ablation studies and theoretical justification for high-order attention is a notable weakness.

## Clarity Assessment
The paper is well-written and well-organized. The figures (particularly Figures 3 and 4) effectively illustrate the proposed attention mechanisms. The methodology section is clear and detailed enough for an expert to reproduce the work. The only minor issue is that the explanation of the ternary potential computation in Section 3.2.3 could be slightly clearer.

## Originality Assessment
The paper makes a novel contribution by extending attention mechanisms to model high-order correlations between multiple modalities. While there has been work on attention mechanisms for VQA (Lu et al., 2016; Fukui et al., 2016), the paper correctly identifies that most prior work is limited to pairwise attention. The generalization to k-th order attention represents a meaningful extension of the current state of the art. The paper adequately references related work and clearly distinguishes its approach from previous contributions.

## Significance Assessment
The results are significant for the field of multimodal learning, as they demonstrate that high-order attention mechanisms can improve performance on VQA. The approach could be applicable to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA, such as image captioning or visual reasoning. The paper advances the state of the art on VQA in a demonstrable way, with clear quantitative improvements over prior work. While the specific application is VQA, the high-order attention framework has potential to influence broader research in multimodal learning.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of attention mechanisms for multimodal learning, with strong empirical results and a well-motivated approach. The weaknesses identified are important but not critical to the paper's main contribution, and they can be addressed with relatively minor revisions.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add an ablation study showing the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) to the final performance.

2. Include a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the high-order attention mechanism, particularly for higher-order interactions.

3. Provide a more thorough comparison with recent high-order attention models, such as Kim et al. (2017) and Nam et al. (2016).

4. Add a brief theoretical discussion explaining why high-order interactions might be beneficial for VQA, beyond the empirical results.

5. Clarify the explanation of the ternary potential computation in Section 3.2.3 to make it more accessible.

6. Discuss the practical limitations of extending the approach to more than 3 modalities, which is claimed but not demonstrated.

## Final Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multimodal learning. The high-order attention model represents a significant extension to existing attention mechanisms, and the empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over prior work. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in multimodal understanding. With the suggested revisions, this paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions.
