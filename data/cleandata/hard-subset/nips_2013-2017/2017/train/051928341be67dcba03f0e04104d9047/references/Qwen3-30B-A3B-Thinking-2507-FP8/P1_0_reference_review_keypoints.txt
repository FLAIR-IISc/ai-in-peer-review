- Proposes a high-order attention mechanism modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials in a probabilistic framework for VQA using mean-field inference.  
- Uses tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) for efficient multimodal fusion and achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA with a relatively small (40M) model.  
- Positions the method as a generalization of two-modality attention to any number of modalities, citing Lu et al., Xu et al., and Fukui et al.  
- Strength: clear mathematical formulation of potentials and technically sound approach.  
- Strength: thorough experimental setup with quantitative results and qualitative attention visualizations.  
- Strength: implementation details (hyperparameters, training) are provided for reproducibility.  
- Strength: 3-modality model outperforms 2-modality baseline, demonstrating the value of high-order correlations.  
- Weakness: claimed novelty is overstated due to significant overlap with Fukui et al.’s tensor sketching and trilinear pooling.  
- Weakness: explanation and motivation for ternary potentials are unclear and not well distinguished from composing pairwise potentials.  
- Weakness: insufficient ablation studies isolating the impact of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials.  
- Weakness: comparisons with Lu et al. are not fully fair because of different embeddings and architectures.  
- Clarity: paper is well-organized, with effective figures and clear mathematical notation.  
- Clarity: the ternary potential marginalization and how mean-field parameters (α, β, γ) are learned need clearer explanation.  
- Clarity: MCT unit description and why it “approximately samples the outer product space” is confusing.  
- Originality: modeling high-order correlations and the probabilistic interpretation of attention is a novel and interpretable contribution.  
- Originality caveat: prior work (Fukui et al., stacked/multi-hop attention, structured attention) already addresses multimodal interactions, and differentiation is insufficient.  
- Significance: achieves competitive SOTA with a smaller model and provides interpretable attention maps.  
- Significance concern: computational cost of high-order attention is not clearly addressed and performance gains are modest.  
- Overall assessment: solid and convincing results but novelty and necessity of components are not fully justified; paper merits acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Required revisions: better differentiate from Fukui et al., clarify and motivate ternary potentials, add ablation studies for each potential type, provide fairer baseline comparisons, and clarify computational cost.  
- Pro checklist summary: novel probabilistic high-order attention, SOTA with simpler model, clear qualitative analysis, generalizable approach, well-written.  
- Con checklist summary: overlap with prior work, unclear ternary formulation, limited ablations, modest gains, unfair baseline comparisons.  
- Specific improvement suggestions: add a clearer explanation/example for ternary potentials, expand related-work comparisons (Fukui et al., Kim et al.), include ablations removing each potential, run fair comparisons using same embeddings, and quantify computational overhead.
