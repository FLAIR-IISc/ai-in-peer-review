- Proposes a general attention framework combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer to compute attention distributions and uses MCB/MCT for scored answers.  
- Connects to prior work on co-attention, bilinear/trilinear pooling, structured attention, and answer-conditioned VQA, claiming novelty in explicit high-order potential decomposition.  

- Strength: Clear, general decomposition into learnable unary/pairwise/ternary components that can incorporate extra modalities.  
- Strength: Empirical gains when including answer modality and higher-order terms on multiple-choice VQA; qualitative attention maps are sensible.  
- Strength: Uses compact sketching (MCB/MCT) to keep computations tractable and provides implementation/training details; interpretable potentials.  
- Strength: Claimed modest parameter count relative to some baselines.  

- Weakness: Probabilistic/“mean-field” justification is superficial and misleading absent a defined energy/function or iterative derivation.  
- Weakness: Limited experiments—main table missing, only multiple-choice VQA evaluated, no VQA v2.0/open-ended results, and missing comparisons to answer-conditioned baselines (e.g., Jabri et al.).  
- Weakness: Sparse ablations—no systematic study isolating unary vs pairwise vs ternary contributions or effects of learned α/β/γ weights.  
- Weakness: Computational/implementation details of ternary C3 and weight parameterization are under-specified; scalability beyond small na is unclear.  
- Weakness: Notational/clarity issues (typos, ambiguous tensor shapes, missing details on glimpses and iterative attention).  
- Weakness: Performance gains may primarily come from using answer candidates as input rather than from high-order potentials.  

- Quality: Engineering sensible but probabilistic framing not rigorous; empirical evidence insufficient to firmly attribute gains to high-order potentials; reproducibility aided by code release.  
- Clarity: Generally readable with helpful figures, but hampered by notation inconsistencies and missing precise definitions/pseudocode.  
- Originality: Reasonable extension of existing co-/dual-attention and bilinear pooling ideas; novelty mainly in explicit unification of high-order potentials.  
- Significance: Potentially useful incremental contribution if SOTA claims hold with fair comparisons; current limited evaluation and weak theory reduce impact.  

- Suggested improvements: provide principled probabilistic formulation and derived mean-field updates or iterative unrolling.  
- Suggested improvements: run comprehensive ablations (unary/pairwise/ternary, α/β/γ, answer-as-input vs not) and compare to relevant baselines (MLB/MFH, Jabri et al.).  
- Suggested improvements: expand evaluation to open-ended VQA and VQA v2.0, report runtime/memory and parameterization details for C3 and w.  
- Suggested improvements: fix notation/typos, give pseudocode, and clarify number of glimpses and iterative refinement possibilities.  

- Arguments for acceptance: simple, general, interpretable high-order attention framework with promising empirical behavior when answers are available.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weak theoretical justification, limited experimental scope, missing baselines/ablations, and unclear scalability.  

- Recommendation: Weak reject—promising idea but needs stronger theoretical grounding and broader, fairer empirical validation.  
- Confidence: Moderate.
