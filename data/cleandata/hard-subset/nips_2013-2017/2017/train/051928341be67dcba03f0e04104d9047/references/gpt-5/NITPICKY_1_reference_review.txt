Summary
The paper proposes a “high-order attention” framework for multimodal fusion, targeting VQA. It defines unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, answers A) and claims to perform mean-field inference by linearly combining marginalized potentials followed by a softmax to produce attentions PV, PQ, PA. The attended modality vectors are then combined via compact bilinear (MCB) and trilinear (MCT) pooling for answer prediction. The method is evaluated primarily on VQA multiple-choice, with qualitative attention visualizations and limited quantitative results.

Relation to prior work: The work connects to co-attention for VQA (e.g., Lu et al. 2016; Fukui et al. 2016), bilinear pooling (MLB/MCB/MUTAN), structured attention networks, and dual attention models. Its stated novelty is explicit high-order potentials across any number of modalities and a “probabilistic” interpretation.

Strengths
- Ambition to model higher-order interactions among modalities, beyond pairwise co-attention.
- Practical use of MCT/MCB to mitigate exponential growth in fusion dimensionality.
- Some qualitative visualizations show question-conditioned shifts in image attention.
- Includes a 3-modality variant (using answer choices as input), which is relevant for the multiple-choice setting.

Weaknesses
Methodological clarity and correctness:
- “Mean-field inference” claim is unsubstantiated. Equations (1) amount to learned linear combinations of feature-derived scores followed by a softmax. There is no definition of a probabilistic graphical model (energy, factorization, variational objective) from which these updates follow, nor any derivation of the update equations. As written, this is a heuristic attention scoring mechanism, not mean-field inference.
- Ternary potential C3 complexity is not addressed. With nv≈196, nq fixed (unspecified), na=3000, explicitly forming C3 is computationally and memory prohibitive. The paper does not explain how C3 is computed/stored or approximated. Unlike the decision stage, no sketching/approximation is described for attention potentials.
- Ambiguous/incorrect notation. Q ∈ Rnq×d and V ∈ Rnv×d, yet (QWq):,i is used as “i-th word” (column) while “words” are along the first axis. Indexing is inconsistent. Similarly θV,Q.A vs θV,Q,A; aA is mistakenly written as aV in the weighted sum; typos in θ terms (e.g., θV,Q.A in Eq. (1)) and reuse of symbol d for both embedding size and MCB output size.
- The “marginalization” by 1×1 convs over C2/C3 is not mathematically defined; summation indices and learned weights (wiv, wiq, wiv,ia) are introduced without clear shapes or parameterization.

Experimental evaluation:
- Quantitative results are insufficient. The paper claims SOTA on VQA but provides no complete table in the text, no breakdown by answer types (Yes/No, Number, Other), and only one small ablation (64.57 vs 63.82) on val with VGG. Table 1 is referenced but not present.
- Only multiple-choice VQA appears to be evaluated; no open-ended results are reported, limiting comparability with standard VQA literature.
- Lack of ablations on (i) unary vs pairwise vs ternary contributions, (ii) learned weights α,β,γ (are higher-order potentials actually used/important?), (iii) cost/benefit of C3, (iv) alternative fusion backends (MLB/Tucker).
- Fairness: the 3-modality model uses answer choices as input; comparisons to open-ended systems are not appropriate, and direct comparisons to multiple-choice baselines that also use answers as input should be clearly separated.

Reproducibility and details:
- Missing key hyperparameters: max question length nq, tokenization, LSTM details, learning rate schedule/epochs, early stopping, gradient clipping, weight decay, initialization.
- Inconsistent training splits: sometimes “train+val,” sometimes “train” for ablations; not clearly specified per experiment.
- Parameter count claims (40M vs 70M) are not justified by a breakdown.
- Code link is malformed (“Torch framework1” and a truncated GitHub URL).

Related work and citations:
- Several misattributions and incomplete references. For example, RNNSearch is attributed to Hermann et al., but is Bahdanau et al. (2014). Many entries lack years/venues; some are missing authors or have duplicated commas. Not citing important bilinear/trilinear VQA methods beyond MCB (e.g., MUTAN at the time; MLB is listed but formatting is incomplete). Claims of easy extension beyond two modalities overlap with prior structured attention work; distinctions are not crisply articulated.

Writing/formatting issues:
- Numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., “and and”, “st Conference…”, inconsistent section references).
- Figure artifacts and captions (“Ternary Potentialconv conv Corr3…”) appear to be pasted incorrectly into the text.
- Symbol collisions and inconsistent notation as noted above.
- The paper frequently uses informal statements without quantitative backing (e.g., “state-of-the-art behavior”, “we found to improve performance significantly”).

Quality
Currently below the bar due to unclear/misleading probabilistic framing, missing complexity analysis for C3, insufficient and incomplete experimental evidence, and numerous notational/formatting issues.

Clarity
Significant issues in notation, derivation rigor, and presentation, which hinder understanding and reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. Modeling higher-order attention is interesting, but the implementation largely combines known components (co-attention-like scoring + MCB/MCT) without a rigorous new inference framework. The novelty over existing multi-modal co-/structured attention is not convincingly established.

Significance
Unclear due to the evaluation gaps. If ternary attention truly helps and can be made efficient, it could be impactful, but the paper does not provide sufficient evidence.

Questions for authors
- Please provide a precise probabilistic model (energy, factors) and a derivation of the mean-field updates. If the method is heuristic, remove the mean-field claim.
- How is C3 computed/marginalized efficiently? Provide time/memory complexity, implementation details, and ablations showing its impact.
- Report full VQA numbers (test-dev/std, per-type), compare fairly to multiple-choice baselines using answer inputs, and add open-ended results.
- Clarify dimensions, fix notation, and provide shapes for all tensors and parameters in Eqs. (1)–(3).
- Provide ablations isolating unary/pairwise/ternary contributions and learned α,β,γ weights.
- Fix references and misattributions; add missing related work.

Minor issues (non-exhaustive)
- aA was mistakenly written as aV in the attention-weighted sum for answers.
- θV,Q.A vs θV,Q,A inconsistent; several punctuation/typo errors; “and and”; “st Conference…”.
- Reuse of symbol d for both embedding size and MCB feature size (8192).
- Footnote and GitHub link formatting broken.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising, but the current submission has substantial methodological ambiguities, missing derivations, scalability concerns for ternary attention, and incomplete empirical validation, along with extensive clarity/formatting issues. A thorough revision addressing the above could make this a stronger contribution. Confidence: high.
