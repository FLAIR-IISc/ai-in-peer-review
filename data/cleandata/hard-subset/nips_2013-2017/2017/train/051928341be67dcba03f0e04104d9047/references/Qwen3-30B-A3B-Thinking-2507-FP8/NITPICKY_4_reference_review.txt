

# Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism that models correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., image, question, answer) for Visual Question Answering. The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms either fail to explicitly model multiple modalities or are limited to pairwise interactions, and they propose a probabilistic model based on high-order potentials with mean-field inference. The approach is evaluated on VQA, where it achieves state-of-the-art results.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel perspective on attention mechanisms by explicitly modeling high-order correlations between multiple modalities. The probabilistic formulation with mean-field inference provides a theoretically grounded approach to attention that can be extended to any number of modalities. The experimental results on VQA are promising, demonstrating improvements over baseline methods. The qualitative analysis in Figure 5-7 provides useful insights into how the attention mechanism operates.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. In Equation (1), the ternary potential is written as "θV,Q.A(ia)" instead of "θV,Q,A(ia)", which is a critical typo that affects the mathematical clarity. Similarly, in Section 3.2.3, the definition of C3 has inconsistent indexing: "θV,Q,A(iq)=tanh(∑_{iv=1}^{nv} ∑_{ia=1}^{na} w_{iv,ia}(C3)_{iq,iv,ia})" should be "(C3)_{iq,iv,ia}" but the paper writes it as "(C3)_{iq,iv,ia}" which is confusing.

2. **Mathematical Errors**: The definition of "corr3(q, v, a) = ∑_{l=1}^d qlvlal" is incorrect. This represents a scalar value, not a tensor as implied by the subsequent notation. The correlation between three vectors should be defined as a tensor product, not a summation. This fundamental error weakens the theoretical foundation of the proposed method.

3. **Methodological Ambiguities**: The paper lacks crucial details for reproducibility. The value of nq (question length) is never specified. The weight vectors w_{iv} in Equation (1) and w_{iv,ia} in Section 3.2.3 are not defined, making it impossible to understand how these are computed. The paper claims "we observe that question attention relies more on the unary question potential" but provides no quantitative evidence for this observation.

4. **Experimental Flaws**: The paper claims "state-of-the-art behavior" but only compares to older methods (Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7]). It fails to compare to more recent VQA SOTA methods from the VQA Challenge 2017/2018. The claim that "our 3-modality model" outperforms "2-modality model" is unsubstantiated, as the paper provides no results for the 2-modality model. The qualitative evaluation (Fig. 5) refers to a "two modality architecture" while the paper claims to be using a "3-modality model," creating significant confusion.

5. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. In Section 3.2.2, it writes "C2 = QWq(VWv)>" but the dimensions don't match (QWq is nq×d, VWv is nv×d, so (VWv)> is d×nv, making QWq(VWv)> = nq×nv, which is correct, but the notation should be QWq(VWv)^T). In Section 4.3, the paper writes "smax" for softmax instead of the standard "softmax" notation.

6. **Missing Critical Details**: The paper claims "the MCT setup yields 63.82% while 2-layer MCB yields 64.57%," but fails to specify the exact configuration of these models. The statement "we tested a different ordering of the input to the 2-modality MCB" is vague and lacks details necessary for reproducibility.

7. **Formatting Issues**: The title "High-Order Attention Models" uses inconsistent hyphenation ("High-Order" vs. "high-order" in the text). The reference list has a typo: "Learning to compose neural networks for question answering" lists "arXiv preprint arXiv:1601.01705,," with an extra comma. The paper also references "Torch framework1 [5]" without proper citation formatting.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents an interesting perspective on attention mechanisms and achieves competitive results on VQA, the numerous technical inconsistencies, mathematical errors, and lack of critical details for reproducibility are substantial flaws. The paper fails to properly define its core components, contains fundamental mathematical errors, and does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of state-of-the-art performance. The methodological ambiguities and inconsistent notation significantly undermine the paper's credibility.

Given the stringent quality standards of NIPS, these technical flaws are too significant to overlook, even though the core idea is potentially interesting. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues before being considered for publication.

**Recommendation: Reject**
