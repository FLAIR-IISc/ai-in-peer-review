This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to model interactions between multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer). The core idea is to learn potentials representing unary, pairwise, and ternary correlations between these modalities and use mean-field inference to compute attention weights. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, attributing the improvement to the ability to capture higher-order relationships.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. The problem of capturing complex interactions between modalities in VQA is well-defined, and the proposed approach addresses it in a principled manner. The paper builds upon existing attention mechanisms ([15, 25]) and extends them to handle higher-order correlations, which is a novel contribution. The related work section is comprehensive, covering various attention mechanisms for both image and textual data, and correctly positions the proposed method within the existing literature. The mathematical formulation of the attention mechanism, utilizing potentials and mean-field inference, is reasonably clear and well-explained. The ablation studies, comparing different decision-making units (MCT vs. 2-layer MCB), are also valuable.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The implementation details, while present, could be more thorough. The authors mention using pre-trained CNNs and LSTMs but provide limited information about the specific architectures and hyperparameters used. The justification for specific design choices, like the choice of tanh activation and 1x1 convolutions, is lacking.  The experimental setup lacks details regarding regularization and the specific training procedure beyond the optimizer and learning rate.  

The improvement over existing state-of-the-art results is marginal, and the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that the gains are solely attributable to the high-order attention mechanism. The experiments could be strengthened by comparing against stronger baselines or by conducting more detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each order of attention (unary, pairwise, ternary).  The failure case analysis (Fig. 8) highlights a limitation of the model – it can attend to irrelevant image regions even with different questions. A discussion about potential reasons for this failure and possible solutions would be beneficial. 

The notation is sometimes inconsistent; for example, the usage of 'iv', 'iq', and 'ia' to denote indices and the meaning of α, β and γ coefficients is not always intuitive. The reliance on tensor sketching for decision-making, while addressing computational concerns, adds complexity and may obscure the impact of the attention mechanism itself. A simpler decision-making process might have provided a clearer understanding of the core contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to modeling multi-modal interactions using high-order attention.
*   State-of-the-art results on VQA, although the improvement is modest.
*   Clear presentation and comprehensive related work.
*   Well-defined mathematical formulation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Marginal improvement over existing state-of-the-art.
*   Lack of detailed implementation and experimental details.
*   Limited ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each attention order.
*   Failure case analysis highlights limitations and lacks discussion of potential solutions.
*   Notation inconsistencies and added complexity from tensor sketching.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel and potentially valuable contribution to the field of attention mechanisms for multimodal reasoning. However, the limited experimental validation, lack of detailed implementation details, and marginal improvement over existing methods raise concerns about the overall impact and robustness of the proposed approach.  A major revision addressing these weaknesses, particularly a more rigorous experimental evaluation and clearer justification of design choices, is required for acceptance. Currently, I would rate this as a **weak reject**.
