Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a general “high-order attention” framework for multimodal reasoning, instantiated for visual question answering (VQA). The key idea is to model attention as a probabilistic module with unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, and candidate answers A). Unary potentials are computed via 1×1 convolutions on each modality’s embedding; pairwise potentials use a learned similarity matrix (inner products after linear projections), and ternary potentials use a trilinear correlation tensor. The final attentions PV, PQ, PA are obtained via a learned linear combination of these potentials followed by a softmax (described as a single mean-field iteration). The attended features are then fused using Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) or a Multimodal Compact Trilinear (MCT) pooling (via tensor sketching), followed by a classifier. Experiments on VQA multiple-choice (MC) are reported; qualitative visualizations are provided; limited ablations compare MCT vs 2-layer MCB and 2-modality vs 3-modality.

The work builds on: (i) co-/joint attention for VQA (e.g., Lu et al., Xu et al., Yang et al.), (ii) bilinear pooling for multimodal fusion (e.g., Fukui et al.’s MCB; related MLB/MFB), (iii) structured/graphical attention ideas (e.g., Kim et al., “Structured Attention Networks”). The novelty lies in (a) explicitly introducing higher-order potentials at the attention stage (including ternary V-Q-A), and (b) unifying them under a simple, learnable combination that can in principle scale to more modalities. Using candidate answers as an attention modality aligns with Jabri et al.’s discussion of answer-conditioning but here it is incorporated into a joint attention mechanism.

Strengths
- Conceptual generality: a clean design that composes unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials and could extend beyond VQA.
- Practicality: uses simple building blocks (1×1 convs, inner products) and efficient tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) for decision layers.
- Empirical indications: 3-modality attention improves over 2-modality; 2-layer MCB outperforms MCT in their setting.
- Qualitative analyses: visualizations help interpret how pairwise/ternary terms shift attention.
- Relevance to NeurIPS: attention and multimodal reasoning are core topics.

Weaknesses (by review criteria)
Quality
- “Probabilistic/mean-field” framing is not rigorously developed. The model is a learned linear combination of potentials with softmax; no explicit energy function, factor graph, or derivation of the mean-field updates is provided. The term “one mean-field iteration” is therefore misleading without formal backing.
- Computational concerns are not addressed. Constructing and marginalizing the ternary correlation tensor C3 scales as O(nv×nq×na×d), which could be prohibitive. The paper does not report memory/runtime, nor explain how this is tractable beyond MC where na≈18.
- Evaluation is limited to multiple-choice VQA. There are no open-ended VQA results, where the A-modality cannot be pre-specified as a small set of candidates. This limits conclusions about generality/impact.
- Baselines are narrow. Comparisons are primarily to Lu et al. (co-attention) and MCB; important contemporaries (e.g., MLB/Mutan/MFB, dual attention) are not compared, and backbone parity (VGG vs ResNet) and tuning fairness are not fully controlled.
- Ablations are incomplete. The paper does not isolate contributions of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials (e.g., turning off θV,Q,A), nor the learned mixing coefficients α,β,γ, nor the benefit of attention on the answer modality itself.

Clarity
- Some notation is hard to follow (e.g., the “convolution along the visual dimension” with scalar weights wiv, wiq; dimensions are unclear).
- The role of na is ambiguous: for MC they appear to attend over the 18 options; elsewhere they train a 3000-class classifier. It is unclear how the ternary attention interacts with these two regimes.
- Several references are misattributed or incomplete (e.g., RNNSearch attributed to Hermann et al.; many entries lack venues/years). Tables/figures referenced (e.g., Tab. 1) are not present in the excerpt; without numbers it’s hard to verify SOTA claims.

Originality
- The idea of high-order attention potentials (especially ternary V-Q-A at the attention stage) is a useful extension. MCT is a natural generalization of MCB. However, bilinear/trilinear fusion and co-attention are well-studied; the main novelty is in the explicit higher-order attention potentials and their combination. Overall, moderate originality.

Significance
- If gains over strong baselines held broadly (including open-ended VQA), the approach could be impactful. As presented, the significance is limited by the evaluation scope (MC only), lack of comparisons to stronger contemporaries, and unclear computational footprint for higher-order terms.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact nv, nq, na used for computing C3 at train/test time? For open-ended vs MC? What are memory and runtime per sample?
- Can you provide ablations removing θV,Q,A and/or answer attention to quantify each component’s contribution?
- How sensitive is performance to α, β, γ (learned mixing) and to d? Do you share coefficients across modalities?
- How do results compare to MLB/MFB/Mutan and dual-attention models under identical backbones and preprocessing?
- Can the method handle open-ended VQA without relying on the candidate answer set?

Suggestions for improvement
- Either formalize the probabilistic model (define energies, factorization, and derive mean-field updates) or present the method as a learned mixture of potentials without the mean-field claim.
- Add open-ended VQA results and evaluate on additional benchmarks (e.g., Visual7W) to demonstrate generality beyond MC.
- Provide comprehensive ablations: remove ternary terms; remove answer attention; vary α/β/γ; vary d; report compute/memory trade-offs.
- Broaden and strengthen baselines (MLB/MFB/Mutan, DAN, etc.) with the same visual backbone and consistent training.
- Clarify implementation details (exact shapes, how “convolutions along dimensions” are implemented) and fix referencing/attribution issues.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually appealing, general framework for higher-order multimodal attention.
- Empirical indications that ternary attention with answer modality can help.
- Clear qualitative visualizations and practical fusion via tensor sketching.

Arguments against acceptance
- Probabilistic claims underdeveloped; unclear computational feasibility of high-order potentials.
- Limited evaluation (MC only), modest and incomplete baselines, missing ablations.
- Clarity and citation issues reduce confidence and reproducibility.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The core idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the current submission lacks rigorous justification of the probabilistic framing, sufficient empirical breadth (especially open-ended VQA), careful comparisons, and clarity about computational cost. Addressing these points could make this a strong contribution.
