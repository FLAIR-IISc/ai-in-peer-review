Summary
The paper proposes a “high-order” attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, instantiated on VQA. The model defines unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over image regions, question words, and candidate answers, and then linearly combines these potentials followed by a softmax to obtain attention distributions per modality. For decision making, it uses Tensor Sketch–based pooling (MCB for pairwise, MCT for ternary) to approximate outer products and reduce parameter count. The authors report state-of-the-art performance on VQA multiple-choice and provide qualitative visualizations of attention. They claim the formulation naturally extends to more than two modalities.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to explicitly model higher-order interactions among modalities (image, question, answers) within the attention stage.
- Unifies unary, pairwise, and ternary correlations in a single framework; the linear combination with learnable weights is simple and potentially robust.
- Sensible integration with compact bilinear/trilinear pooling for decision layers; comparisons between MCB and MCT are relevant.
- Qualitative examples illustrate the effect of pairwise vs combined attentions and include at least one failure case.
- Implementation details for embeddings and optimization are given at a reasonable level, and code availability is mentioned.

Weaknesses
- The probabilistic/mean-field framing is not substantiated. Equations effectively implement a linear combination of learned scores with softmax; there is no explicit energy, factor graph, variational objective, or iterative inference. Calling this “mean-field inference” is misleading.
- Computational and memory complexity of ternary potentials is unclear. Constructing C3 over nv×nq×na (with na up to 3000) is seemingly intractable. The paper does not explain how C3 is computed/stored or whether any low-rank/approximate schemes are used. Training time claims lack sufficient detail to resolve this discrepancy.
- Empirical support for “state-of-the-art” is incomplete. The main tables are not present in the submission text, and it is unclear whether comparisons control for visual backbone, parameter count, and tuning. The authors themselves note confounds (lower embedding dimension in the 2-modality baseline), undermining the claim that improvements stem from higher-order attention rather than capacity choices.
- Baselines are not comprehensive or matched. If multiple-choice answers are used as inputs (attention over answers), the most relevant comparison is to methods that also condition on answer candidates (e.g., Jabri et al.). It is not clear that such baselines are reproduced with comparable settings.
- Limited analysis and ablations. There is no systematic ablation of unary vs pairwise vs ternary terms (e.g., zeroing α2–α4, β2–β4, etc.), no study of the learned combination weights, and no evaluation of effects of the “mean-field” step count (it appears to be one).
- Generality claim (“trivial extension to any number of modalities”) conflicts with the combinatorial growth of higher-order tensors in the attention stage. The paper does not reconcile this with practical computation beyond decision-layer sketching.
- Clarity issues: typos and inconsistencies (e.g., repeated aV in equations; ambiguities in the definition of 1×1 “convolution along a dimension” as a weighted sum), missing training objective specification for multiple-choice (loss and negative sampling), and incomplete reporting (exact numbers, per-answer-type breakdowns).

Quality
- Technical soundness is weakened by the lack of a rigorous probabilistic derivation and by the absence of a credible account of ternary potential computation at realistic scales. Claims of SOTA are not convincingly supported without controlled baselines and full numbers. The idea may be effective, but the evidence is not yet sufficient for a strong NeurIPS claim.

Clarity
- The paper is readable but contains several notational errors and imprecise descriptions (e.g., “mean-field,” “convolution along the visual dimension”). Experimental reporting should include full tables, controlled settings, and standard breakdowns (Yes/No, Number, Other).

Originality
- Modeling interactions via bilinear/trilinear forms and using tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) is known. The novelty lies in applying higher-order correlations within the attention computation and mixing them via learned weights. This is incremental relative to co-attention and bilinear pooling literature; nevertheless, the attempt to unify unary/pairwise/ternary attentions is a reasonable contribution if validated rigorously.

Significance
- VQA is widely studied. If convincingly demonstrated, a robust high-order attention module could be impactful. Given current ambiguities and limited ablation/controls, the significance is uncertain.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide a formal probabilistic model: energy function, factorization, and derivation showing how Eq. (1) arises from mean-field. Are there multiple iterations? Does it improve performance?
2. How is C3 computed and stored for na=3000, nv≈196, nq≈20? If approximations are used, specify them and their complexity.
3. Report full quantitative results (test-dev and test-std) with per-type breakdowns, and compare against baselines that also use answer candidates as input, matched for backbone, parameter count, and embedding dimension.
4. Ablations: remove pairwise/ternary terms; vary α,β,γ; compare MCT vs two-layer MCB under equal capacity; evaluate without answer input (open-ended VQA).
5. Evaluate on VQA v2.0 to mitigate language priors and show robustness.
6. Discuss runtime/memory and provide wall-clock and GPU memory usage per batch.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace the “mean-field” presentation with a precise and accurate description, or provide the full derivation and iterative inference.
- Include comprehensive ablations and fair, controlled baselines.
- Clarify and, if necessary, redesign the ternary attention computation to be tractable (e.g., low-rank factorization).
- Improve clarity and fix notational issues; provide full results in the main text.

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting idea to integrate higher-order correlations directly into attention.
- Simple, modular formulation; uses established components (MCB/MCT).
- Promising qualitative behavior and potential for broader multimodal use.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficiently rigorous methodology and misleading probabilistic framing.
- Questionable computational feasibility for ternary potentials with no clear resolution.
- Incomplete and potentially confounded empirical validation; lack of fair, matched baselines.
- Limited ablations; uncertain generality and robustness.

Overall recommendation: Reject (borderline). The paper presents a potentially useful idea but requires stronger theoretical grounding, clearer and tractable implementation of high-order attention, and more rigorous, controlled experimentation before it meets the bar for NeurIPS.
