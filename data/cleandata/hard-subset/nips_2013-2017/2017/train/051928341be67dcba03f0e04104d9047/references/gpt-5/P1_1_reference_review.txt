Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a general high-order attention framework for multimodal learning, instantiated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea is to model attention as a probabilistic-looking mechanism with learnable unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, and, for multiple-choice, answer options A). Unary potentials score each modality individually; pairwise potentials compute similarity matrices between projected features (e.g., Q–V); ternary potentials compute trilinear correlations over Q–V–A. The final attention distributions over each modality are obtained by a softmax over a learned linear combination of the respective unary, pairwise, and ternary terms. Attended vectors are then fused using Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) or its trilinear extension (MCT), following prior work, for answer prediction. The approach aims to be extensible to any number of modalities and to provide interpretability via attention maps. Experiments on VQA (multiple-choice) claim state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters, along with qualitative visualizations and limited ablations (MCT vs a two-layer MCB stack).

Relative to prior work, the paper is conceptually closest to co-attention (Lu et al., 2016), bilinear pooling models (Fukui et al., 2016; Kim et al., 2016), and structured attention (Kim et al., 2017). The novelty lies in explicitly defining and combining k-th order correlations as “potentials,” learning their relative importance, and extending co-attention to include answer choices as an input modality. The decision module leverages known sketching-based pooling (MCB/MCT).

Strengths
- Clear, modular recipe for constructing unary, pairwise, and ternary attention terms with simple projected inner products and marginalizations; likely easy to implement and extend.
- Unifying view over multiple modalities; explicitly allows k-way interactions beyond pairwise.
- Effective in practice: claims state-of-the-art on VQA multiple-choice with fewer parameters; qualitative attention maps are sensible.
- Sensible engineering: tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to avoid exponential cost; dropout, training details, and a (purported) code release.
- The learned weighting of different order potentials is a practical way to let the model adaptively trust signals across modalities.

Weaknesses
- Probabilistic justification is loose: the use of “potentials” and “mean-field” is not formally derived from a well-defined energy; the linear combination followed by softmax corresponds to one ad hoc update rather than a principled MF inference step.
- Computational complexity of ternary terms is not analyzed. Building C3 scales as O(nq·nv·na·d). The approach is only feasible if na is small (e.g., 18 MC options); it is unclear how this scales to open-ended VQA or more modalities.
- Experimental scope is narrow: results are reported only for multiple-choice VQA; no open-ended accuracy or per-type breakdown (Yes/No, Number, Other). Comparisons to strong bilinear baselines (MLB, MFB/MFH) are missing.
- Limited ablations: no study of the contribution of unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials; no analysis of the learned α/β/γ weights; no test of multiple MF iterations; no evaluation of removing answer-as-input.
- Some clarity gaps and typos (e.g., aA mis-typed as aV; shapes of “convolutions” over similarity tensors; learned marginalization weights wiv, wiq not fully specified).
- Using answer choices as an input modality changes the problem compared to open-ended VQA; fairness of comparison should be addressed carefully.

Quality
Technically, the modules are standard neural components (linear projections, inner products, tanh, softmax) arranged in a clear pipeline. The “mean-field” claim is more of a metaphor than a derivation; the paper would be stronger with a formal energy and MF update showing how the proposed marginalized terms arise. The experiments indicate improvements, but quantitative evidence is incomplete in the provided text: full tables, per-category metrics, confidence intervals, and strong baselines are missing. The limited ablations make it hard to attribute gains to higher-order attentions vs other choices (e.g., feature dimension, MCB design).

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well organized, with helpful figures. However:
- Precisely define shapes and parameters for marginalizations (e.g., wiv, wiq, wiq,ia: are these shared scalars, learned vectors, or 1×1 conv filters?).
- Provide the exact energy (if any) and a derivation of the MF update leading to Eq. (1), or tone down the probabilistic claims.
- Fix typos and notational inconsistencies (duplicate aV definition; missing subscripts; “convolution along dimension” needs a precise definition).
- Report complete evaluation details: dataset version, metrics (All/YesNo/Num/Other), exact SOTA numbers and baselines, variance over runs.

Originality
Moderate. Pairwise co-attention and bilinear pooling are established; the main novelty is the explicit construction and combination of high-order (including ternary) attention terms and the view of attention as a sum of potentials with learnable weights. Extending to include the answer modality for MC VQA is not entirely new but is cleanly integrated here.

Significance
Potentially useful and reusable design for multimodal attention. If the reported gains over strong baselines hold broadly (including open-ended VQA), the approach could influence subsequent VQA and multimodal models. As written, significance is tempered by limited experimental breadth and missing ablations.

Questions and suggestions
- Please provide a formal energy function and derive the MF updates that yield Eq. (1). If not feasible, reframe the method without probabilistic claims.
- Complexity analysis for C3: what are typical nq, nv, na at train/test? How does runtime/memory scale? Any approximations for larger na or more modalities?
- Full ablations: remove unary/pairwise/ternary; vary α/β/γ; multiple MF iterations; compare MCT vs stacked MCB under equal capacity; effect of using A as input vs not.
- Compare to MLB/MFB/MFH and more recent co-attention variants; report open-ended VQA and per-type scores.
- Clarify whether you use VGG or ResNet in SOTA results; ensure apples-to-apples comparisons (same features, same answer spaces).
- Release code and pretrained models; include inference speed and memory.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, general, and interpretable high-order attention construction.
- Competitive-to-SOTA results on VQA MC with fewer parameters.
- Clear motivation and modular design; promising for extension to more modalities.
- Qualitative visualizations and some implementation details.

Cons:
- Weak theoretical grounding of the “mean-field” interpretation.
- Narrow evaluation (MC only), missing strong baselines and ablations.
- Unclear computational scalability of ternary potentials.
- Some clarity/notation issues and minor errors.

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying the probabilistic framing. The method is practical and potentially impactful, but the paper would benefit from more rigorous analysis and broader experimentation.

Reviewer confidence: medium.
