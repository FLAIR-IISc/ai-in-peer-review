Summary
This paper proposes a general multimodal attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary correlations across modalities (image V, question Q, answers A) and aggregates them via a “mean-field”-style linear combination followed by softmax to produce attention distributions over each modality. Specifically, unary potentials are computed via 1×1 convs (i.e., linear projections with tanh) on each modality; pairwise potentials are obtained via similarity matrices C2=QWq(VWv)⊤ and reduced to per-modality scores by learned 1D kernels; ternary potentials are defined via a tri-linear correlation tensor C3 over all three modalities and similarly marginalized. The attended vectors aV,aQ,aA are then fused using compact bilinear or trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) via the tensor-sketch technique, followed by a classifier. The method is evaluated on VQA (multiple-choice), reports state-of-the-art accuracy with fewer parameters than some prior work, and includes qualitative attention visualizations. Compared to prior co-attention approaches (e.g., Lu et al. 2016), bilinear pooling (e.g., Fukui et al. 2016; Kim et al. 2017), dual/structured attention (Nam et al. 2016; Kim et al. 2017), the contribution is an explicit high-order attention formulation that is claimed to scale to more than two modalities.

Strengths
- Conceptual generality: a unified formulation for unary, pairwise, and ternary attention potentials that, in principle, extends to more modalities.
- Practicality: uses simple building blocks (1×1 convs, inner products, tanh) and standard feature extractors; decision fusion via established compact bilinear/trilinear pooling avoids tensor blow-up.
- Empirical evidence: improvements of 3-modality over 2-modality; comparison of MCT vs stacked MCB; qualitative attention that correlates with question words and candidate answers.
- Reproducibility: optimizer settings, dimensions, dropout, and a code link are provided.

Weaknesses
- Probabilistic formulation is underspecified: the “mean-field” claim is not derived from an explicit energy or variational objective; the inference update is a learned linear combination plus softmax rather than the outcome of a principled MF update. This weakens the theoretical grounding.
- Missing or incomplete quantitative evidence: references are made to state-of-the-art results and Table 1, but the actual numbers and comparisons are not included here. Only a small ablation (MCT vs 2-layer MCB) is numerically detailed.
- Scope of evaluation: only multiple-choice VQA is reported; open-ended accuracy, which is standard in prior VQA work, is not shown. Fairness of comparisons is complicated by different feature dimensions/backbones (512 vs 2048).
- Clarity and notation issues: several typos/inconsistencies (e.g., duplicate aV in equations, θV,Q.A typo, dimension/order ambiguities for C2/C3 and w weights). The definition and parameterization of the learned marginalization weights (wiv, wiq, wiq,ia) need clearer specification (are they shared kernels, position-specific parameters, or conv filters?).
- Computational considerations: building C3 per question/answer set could be expensive; while MC has only ~18 choices, the paper does not report memory/time complexity or runtime, nor discuss scaling to more modalities or higher answer cardinalities.
- Limited ablations: no analysis of the marginal contribution of unary/pairwise/ternary terms or of the learned coefficients (α,β,γ); no examination of robustness to answer biases when using answer-conditioned attention.

Quality
- Technical correctness appears reasonable for the implemented architecture; however, the probabilistic interpretation is not rigorously supported. The attention potentials are heuristic, and the “mean-field” language is not justified by a derivation. Experimental methodology is partially described, but key quantitative results are missing here. The ablations are limited and do not isolate the contribution of high-order terms versus stronger fusion alone.

Clarity
- The high-level idea is clear, and the modular breakdown (unary/pairwise/ternary) is helpful. Nonetheless, notational glitches and missing details impede reproducibility: precise tensor shapes, the definition and learning of marginalization kernels/weights, and the complete evaluation tables should be included. A brief complexity analysis would also improve clarity.

Originality
- Extending attention to explicitly include ternary potentials across image, question, and answers in a unified scheme is a meaningful step beyond common two-modality co-attention and bilinear pooling. However, much of the machinery (pairwise similarity matrices, MCB/MCT) is borrowed from prior work, and the “mean-field” angle is not developed beyond terminology. Overall, the combination is novel but incremental.

Significance
- If the claimed state-of-the-art with fewer parameters is borne out on standard VQA splits (including open-ended), the approach is of practical interest. The framework’s potential to handle additional modalities is attractive. However, without complete quantitative evidence and stronger ablations, the advance over strong two-modality baselines is difficult to assess definitively.

Specific suggestions
- Provide the full quantitative results (test-dev/test-std, per-category, with confidence intervals if possible) for both MC and open-ended settings, and include comparisons using identical visual backbones and embedding dimensions.
- Clarify the probabilistic model: specify an energy function over attention variables, derive the mean-field updates, or rephrase to avoid probabilistic claims if not intended.
- Precisely define the marginalization operations and parameters (wiv, wiq, wiq,ia): are they convolutional filters shared across positions, and what are their shapes?
- Add ablations: remove ternary potentials; vary α/β/γ; report gains from unary, pairwise, and ternary terms; analyze the effect of using answer-conditioned attention.
- Report computational cost and memory of C2 and C3, and end-to-end runtimes.
- Improve presentation: fix typos and indexing mistakes; ensure consistent dimensions; include the missing figures/tables; reference recent related bilinear/tri-linear attention works clearly.

Arguments for acceptance
- General and simple high-order attention framework with promising qualitative behavior.
- Potentially strong empirical performance with fewer parameters.
- Public implementation and clear module decomposition that could be reused.

Arguments against acceptance
- Probabilistic/mean-field framing is not substantiated.
- Incomplete quantitative evidence and limited ablations make it hard to attribute gains to high-order attention.
- Evaluation limited to multiple-choice; clarity and notation issues.

Overall, this is a potentially useful architectural contribution for multimodal attention, but it would benefit from a more rigorous formulation and a more comprehensive and controlled empirical evaluation.
