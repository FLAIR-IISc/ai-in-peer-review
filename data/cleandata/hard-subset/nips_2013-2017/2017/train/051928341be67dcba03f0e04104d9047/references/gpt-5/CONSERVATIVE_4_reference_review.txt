Summary
The paper proposes a “high-order attention” mechanism for multimodal tasks, instantiated on VQA. The model defines unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over image regions, question tokens, and (multiple-choice) answers. These are linearly combined and normalized with a softmax to produce attention distributions over each modality. Pairwise correlations are computed via similarity matrices between projected modalities; ternary correlations extend this to a 3-way tensor. The attended features are then fused via MCB or a trilinear extension (MCT), leveraging tensor sketching to avoid explicit outer-product blow-up. The authors claim state-of-the-art performance on VQA multiple-choice, provide qualitative attention visualizations, and argue the approach generalizes to any number of modalities.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on co-attention and multimodal bilinear pooling (e.g., Lu et al. 2016; Fukui et al. 2016; Kim et al. 2017 MLB; Kazemi et al. 2017 baseline), and cites structured attention (Kim et al. 2017). The proposed contribution is to explicitly combine unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions as “potentials” and to attend in each modality using these combined signals, while using MCB/MCT for decision making.

Strengths
- Clear motivation to capture multimodal interactions beyond simple concatenation or pairwise co-attention. The explicit decomposition into unary/pairwise/ternary components is conceptually appealing and aligns with intuitions about multimodal grounding.
- Implementation-wise, the design is relatively simple (1×1 “convs”/linear maps) and compatible with standard VQA pipelines. The use of compact bilinear/trilinear pooling is appropriate for taming the combinatorial growth in decision layers.
- Qualitative visualizations suggest that pairwise and ternary signals steer attention toward relevant regions/words.
- Code availability is a plus for reproducibility.

Weaknesses and concerns

Quality
- The probabilistic framing and “mean-field inference” claims are not substantiated. No joint energy or likelihood is defined, and the inference reduces to a single linear combination of potentials followed by softmax. This is neither an explicit CRF nor mean-field in the usual sense. If the model is intended as a heuristic aggregation of scores, it should be described as such.
- Experimental evidence is insufficient to isolate what drives gains. Key ablations are missing:
  - 3-modality vs 2-modality with matched capacity and features (e.g., 2048-dim image features, same classifier). The 2-modality baseline is weakened (512-d vs 2048-d used in strong baselines).
  - Ablate unary/pairwise/ternary terms individually and in combination; report the learned α/β/γ weights and whether multiple mean-field iterations help.
  - Critically, for the 3-modality setting, using answer options as inputs can dramatically change the task. A fair comparison requires baselines that also consume candidate answers (e.g., Jabri et al. 2016 binary classification per candidate), and a 3-modality model without ternary potentials but with answer input.
- Claims of state-of-the-art are not verifiable from the text provided: the key table is missing here, and the only numbers shown (val accuracy ~64% with VGG) are not SOTA for VQA at the time. It is unclear whether results are on Open-Ended or strictly Multiple-Choice; most prior work reports both.
- Fairness of comparisons is unclear: image backbone (VGG vs ResNet), embedding sizes, use of ensembles, number of glimpses, and data splits all materially affect VQA results. The paper acknowledges dimensionality differences but still draws strong conclusions.
- Computational aspects of ternary potentials are not discussed. For multiple choice, na is presumably 18 per example, but the text also mentions a 3000-answer vocabulary for outputs. It is unclear what na is during training/evaluation for ternary attention, and what the runtime/memory overhead is.

Clarity
- Several notational errors and typos: duplication in definitions (aA mislabeled as aV), ambiguous weights wiv,ia in marginalizations, and inconsistent indices make the method harder to follow. Figures seem to have labeling issues.
- The probabilistic interpretation is vague; if an energy-based model is intended, specify E and derive mean-field fixed points, or otherwise avoid that terminology.
- The paper claims trivial extension to any number of modalities, but neither the computational recipe nor experiments beyond three modalities are shown.

Originality
- Modeling pairwise correlations and using compact bilinear pooling are well-established in VQA. The ternary attention over answers is not entirely new in spirit: prior work has considered using candidate answers as inputs (Jabri et al.), and trilinear forms are a natural extension of bilinear pooling. The main novelty is the explicit linear combination of unary/pairwise/ternary “potentials” to produce attentions for each modality. This is incremental and would benefit from stronger theoretical or empirical justification.

Significance
- If validated, the approach could be a practical addition to VQA systems and potentially transfer to other multimodal tasks. However, the current empirical support is not strong enough to conclude that high-order attention, per se, is the cause of improvements rather than confounding factors (inputting answers, feature dimensionality, classifier choice). The generality claim (k-modalities) remains speculative.

Questions and requests for clarification
- Please define the full probabilistic model (energy or log-potentials), and the mean-field objective/iterations. Did you try more than one iteration? Do the learned α/β/γ converge to meaningful patterns?
- For ternary attention, what is na during training and test? Is it 18 candidate answers per question, or the top-3000 vocabulary? How is the 3000-answer softmax reconciled with attention over answers?
- Provide ablations: remove ternary potential but keep answer inputs; pairwise-only vs unary-only; report both MC and OE settings; control for image features and embedding size.
- Compare directly to Jabri et al. when using answers as inputs, and to MLB/MLP (Kim et al.) and strong co-attention baselines with matched features.
- Report runtime/memory of pairwise/ternary modules and the MCT vs 2-layer MCB decision units.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, modular idea; simple to implement and potentially useful in practice.
- Attempt to handle higher-order multimodal interactions beyond pairwise.
- Code and qualitative analyses.

Cons for acceptance
- Probabilistic claims are not justified; “mean-field” usage appears misleading.
- Insufficient and potentially unfair empirical validation; missing critical ablations and strong baselines for the answer-as-input setting.
- Clarity and notation issues hinder reproducibility.
- SOTA claim not convincingly supported from presented numbers.

Recommendation
Given the current state of the paper, I do not recommend acceptance. Strengthening the theoretical framing (or reframing without probabilistic claims), adding rigorous ablations and fair comparisons (especially for the 3-modality setting), reporting both MC and OE results with matched backbones/dimensions, and clarifying computational details would substantially improve the work.
