- Summary: Proposes an attention mechanism modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer with a mean-field-like update; ternary interactions via a 3-way tensor and fusion via MCT or 2-layer MCB; evaluated on multiple-choice VQA with reported SOTA and fewer parameters.

- Relation to prior work: Positioned against co-attention, stacked attention, dynamic memory, and MCB; claims novelty in explicit high-order (ternary) potentials and a probabilistic/mean-field interpretation, which is less explored relative to bilinear methods.

- Strength: Clear motivation and concrete architecture for higher-order cross-modal interactions.

- Strength: Simple, modular implementation using only 1x1 convolutions and inner products, easy to implement.

- Strength: Including answer choices as a modality is intuitive for multiple-choice VQA and yields strong empirical results.

- Strength: Uses compact sketching (MCB/MCT) to avoid parameter explosion in higher-order fusion.

- Strength: Provides ablations, qualitative analyses, and open-source code.

- Weakness: Probabilistic framing is underspecified—no defined joint distribution or energy, so calling it “mean-field” is potentially misleading.

- Weakness: Computational/memory complexity of ternary tensor C3 (nq×nv×na) is not analyzed and may be prohibitive; unclear if C3 is formed explicitly or implicitly.

- Weakness: Evaluation limited to multiple-choice VQA; using candidate answers as input may exploit dataset biases and is not commensurate with open-ended methods.

- Weakness: Empirical gains are modest and SOTA claims are hard to verify without strict apples-to-apples comparisons (backbones, dims, splits).

- Weakness: Missing thorough ablation isolating each potential’s contribution (unary vs +pairwise vs +ternary) and quantitative evidence of learned weight effects.

- Weakness: Some clarity issues and typos (duplicate variables, inconsistent notation, vague descriptions).

- Quality: Technically sound neural architecture using standard components, but probabilistic justification and computational feasibility of ternary attention are not rigorously supported; experiments are reasonable but narrow.

- Clarity: Well organized with clear figures, but notational slips and missing complexity/training/memory details hinder full reproducibility.

- Originality: Moderate — ternary attention across >2 modalities is a useful extension, but building blocks and fusion methods are largely known.

- Significance: Potentially impactful for tasks with multiple modalities available at inference, but current empirical scope limits immediate influence.

- Questions for authors: How is C3 computed (explicit/implicit) and what are time/memory profiles? Provide systematic ablations (unary; +pairwise; +ternary). Do gains hold without candidate answers (open-ended) or on Visual7W? Clarify the mean-field derivation with an energy/factor graph mapping to Eq.(1). How sensitive are results to embedding dim d and MCB/MCT size? Are marginalization weights w per-position or shared—formalize shapes and learning?

- Minor comments: Fix typos and inconsistent notation; report exact test-dev/test-std numbers with backbones and parameter counts for compared methods; consider reporting attention entropy or calibration metrics.

- Recommendation: Borderline accept — interesting and practically effective for multiple-choice VQA, but needs stronger probabilistic justification, computational clarity, and broader evaluation; confidence: medium.
