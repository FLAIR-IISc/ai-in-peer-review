Summary: The paper proposes a unified, high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning, instantiated on VQA. Attention is modeled probabilistically with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image regions, question tokens, and (for multiple-choice) answer candidates, and marginal attention distributions are obtained via a single mean-field-like softmax combination of potentials. To mitigate the explosion in parameters for decision making over correlated attended features, the method uses compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) via tensor sketching. On VQA multiple-choice, the approach claims state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters than competing co-attention architectures such as Lu et al. (NIPS’16) and MCB (Fukui et al.), and provides qualitative visualizations showing modality-aligned attentions.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled formulation of attention as high-order potentials with a simple, learnable combination, connecting to structured/mean-field ideas (cf. Kim et al., structured attention).
- Conceptually scalable to more than two modalities; cleanly generalizes co-attention to ternary interactions without bespoke engineering.
- Practicality: uses count-sketch–based pooling to control complexity and provides many implementation details (optimizer, dimensions, dropout, training time); public code is promised.
- Solid qualitative analyses; ablations between MCT and two-layer MCB are reported.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical support for “state-of-the-art” is under-specified in the text provided (Table 1 not shown). Statistical significance, per-type breakdowns (Yes/No, Number, Other), and comparisons to strong recent baselines are limited.
- Completeness/clarity: the role of na differs between classification (top-3k answers) and multiple-choice (18 options); the paper should more clearly separate the two regimes and justify when ternary attention is used.
- Computational analysis of the ternary potential is thin; complexity and memory for C3 and its marginalization (especially beyond multiple-choice) are not quantified.
- Limited ablation of the learned weights (α, β, γ) and of removing unary/pairwise/ternary terms to validate the contribution of each potential.

Quality: Method is technically sound and well-motivated; implementation choices (1×1 convs, tensor sketching) are appropriate. More thorough ablations and runtime/memory profiling would strengthen claims.

Clarity: Generally well-organized, with helpful figures; however, clearer delineation of datasets/settings (test-dev vs test-std; MC vs open-ended) and reporting of exact numbers are needed.

Originality: Extends co-attention to higher-order interactions in a principled way; distinguishes itself from prior bilinear/alternating attention work [7, 13, 15, 25].

Significance: Likely useful for VQA and other multimodal tasks; the ternary formulation over answers is particularly interesting for multiple-choice.

Constructive suggestions:
- Report full quantitative results with confidence intervals, per-category breakdowns, and training/inference cost.
- Add ablations on potentials and learned combination weights; analyze cases where high-order terms help/hurt.
- Evaluate on additional benchmarks (e.g., Visual7W, VQA v2.0) and with stronger visual features (e.g., object proposals).
- Discuss extending ternary attention beyond multiple-choice and strategies to prune/sparsify C3.
