This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA). The core idea is to model not just pairwise interactions between modalities (image and question) but also higher-order correlations to better direct attention to relevant elements. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a limitation of existing attention mechanisms that often treat modalities in an ad-hoc manner. The approach of explicitly modeling high-order correlations is a sensible direction, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed technique. The related work section is comprehensive, covering a broad range of attention mechanisms for both image and text data, and accurately positions the proposed method in the context of existing research. The experimental results are strong, showing improvement over previous state-of-the-art methods. The inclusion of qualitative examples (Figs. 5, 6, 7, and 8) helps to understand how the attention mechanism is working.

However, there are several weaknesses. While the core idea is strong, the presentation of the mathematical formulation (Eq. 1 and surrounding text) is dense and could be simplified. The notation is somewhat heavy and makes it difficult to quickly grasp the key concepts. The explanation of the mean-field inference step feels a bit hand-wavy and lacks sufficient detail for someone to fully understand how it operates. The explanation of the MCB and MCT units is also brief; while referencing the original papers is helpful, a more concise explanation of their role within *this* specific architecture would be beneficial.

The ablation study comparing MCT and the 2-layer MCB is minimal; reporting only a single result on the validation set. More extensive evaluation of these components, and their sensitivity to hyperparameters, would strengthen the paper. The failure case in Fig. 8, while useful, is not thoroughly discussed. A deeper analysis of why the attention mechanism fails in this scenario would be valuable. Furthermore, the computational complexity of the high-order attention mechanism is not discussed. Modeling higher-order interactions could potentially lead to significant computational overhead, and understanding the trade-offs between performance and computational cost is important.

The comparison to existing methods sometimes feels superficial. The authors state they use a lower embedding dimension compared to some baselines, acknowledging this impacts performance. However, a more detailed analysis of how the proposed method performs across different embedding dimensions would be more convincing. Additionally, the choice of RMSProp and its hyperparameters is not justified beyond being standard practice.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   State-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark (VQA).
*   Novel and well-motivated approach to attention modeling.
*   Comprehensive related work section.
*   Clear explanation of the core idea.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense and potentially confusing mathematical formulation.
*   Limited ablation study and hyperparameter analysis.
*   Lack of discussion regarding computational complexity.
*   Superficial comparison to baselines.
*   Insufficient analysis of failure cases.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of VQA. The proposed high-order attention mechanism is a novel and effective approach to modeling interactions between modalities. However, the paper would benefit from further clarification of the mathematical formulation, a more thorough ablation study, and a more in-depth discussion of the computational implications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these concerns. Specifically, I suggest the authors simplify the presentation of the mathematical details, expand the ablation study, analyze the computational complexity, and provide a more detailed discussion of the failure cases.
