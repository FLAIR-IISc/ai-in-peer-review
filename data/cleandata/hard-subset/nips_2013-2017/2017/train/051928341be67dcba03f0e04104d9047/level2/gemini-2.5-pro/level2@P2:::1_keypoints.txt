- Proposes a novel probabilistic high-order attention mechanism using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials for multi-modal fusion.  
- Applies a single mean-field-inspired update (linear combination + softmax) to produce attention distributions and uses attended features for prediction.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results, achieving state-of-the-art on the VQA benchmark.  
- Contribution is well-motivated and technically solid overall, with convincing experimental evidence.  
- Claiming full "mean-field inference" is overstated since only a single non-iterative feed-forward step is used; should be framed as mean-field-inspired.  
- Computation of the ternary potential tensor C3 is potentially very expensive (e.g., ~26×196×3000) and implementation/marginalization details are vague.  
- Unclear definition of weights (w_iv, w_ia) and unclear marginalization procedure hinder reproducibility and complexity analysis.  
- 3-modality model depends on a fixed answer set, limiting applicability to open-ended VQA or tasks with large/continuous answer spaces.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-structured, with helpful figures (Figure 3 aids understanding).  
- Descriptions of marginalization for pairwise/ternary potentials are too brief and need formal definitions.  
- The 2-layer MCB decision module description contains a likely typo and is confusing; a clearer formulation or diagram is needed.  
- Originality is strong: first principled incorporation of third-order (image-question-answer) interactions into attention and a clear potentials-based formulation.  
- Use of bilinear pooling (MCB) is not novel but is appropriately integrated into the high-order framework.  
- Significance is high: the approach advances VQA state-of-the-art and is generalizable to tasks with three or more modalities.  
- Pros: principled high-order attention, theoretical generality to more modalities, SOTA VQA performance, and generally good writing/visuals.  
- Cons: overstated mean-field claim, potential scalability issues with C3, dependence on fixed answer sets, unclear implementation details, and insufficiently controlled 2-modality comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with addressable clarity and limitation issues; recommend acceptance.
