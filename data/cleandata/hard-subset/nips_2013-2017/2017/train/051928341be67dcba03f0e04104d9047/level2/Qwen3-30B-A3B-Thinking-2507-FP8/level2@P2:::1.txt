

This paper proposes a "high-order attention mechanism" for visual question answering (VQA) that models correlations between multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The authors claim this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset by learning higher-order correlations between modalities, which they argue better directs attention to relevant elements compared to existing methods.

The paper is well-organized with clear figures (Figs. 3-8) that effectively illustrate the attention mechanisms. The mathematical formulation of the attention model (Eq. 1) and the description of the unary, pairwise, and ternary potential modules are technically sound. The qualitative results (Figs. 5-7) provide some evidence that the attention mechanism focuses on relevant regions for different questions, which is a valuable contribution to interpretability in VQA.

However, several critical issues undermine the paper's claims and contributions. First, the novelty claim is unsubstantiated. The paper states "our model develops a probabilistic model, based on high order potentials and performs mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities. This permits trivial extension of the model to any number of modalities," but this is not clearly distinct from existing work. The pairwise attention mechanism in Lu et al. (NIPS 2016) and Fukui et al. (ICLR 2016) already considers interactions between two modalities, and the paper fails to adequately contrast its approach with these established methods. The ternary potential (Section 3.2.3) appears to be a direct extension of pairwise attention, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how this specific extension leads to meaningful improvements over existing pairwise approaches.

Second, the claimed "state-of-the-art" performance lacks sufficient detail. The paper mentions "Tab. 1" but doesn't provide the actual results. Without seeing the comparative numbers against current SOTA methods (e.g., the 2017 VQA Challenge results), it's impossible to verify this claim. The paper reports a 64.57% accuracy with their 2-layer MCB approach, but doesn't state how this compares to the top-performing methods from the 2017 VQA Challenge (e.g., the 64.6% reported by the winning team in the VQA Challenge).

Third, the paper lacks critical ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of the high-order components. They compare a 2-modality vs. 3-modality model, but don't show how much of the performance gain comes from the ternary potential specifically versus other components. For instance, the improvement from the 2-modality model (which they claim achieves inferior performance) could simply be due to using a lower embedding dimension (512 vs. 2048), not the high-order attention. The paper doesn't isolate the effect of the high-order components.

The computational complexity of the ternary potential calculation (O(nq*nv*na)) is also concerning but not addressed. For VQA, with nq≈20 words, nv≈196 image patches, and na≈3000 possible answers, this would require approximately 1.18 billion operations per attention calculation, which seems prohibitively expensive compared to pairwise approaches.

While the paper's qualitative results are promising, the significance is limited by the lack of substantial improvements over existing methods. The VQA task is important, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how the high-order attention mechanism would generalize to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA, despite claiming it's "generally applicable."

The paper also misses opportunities to properly contextualize its contribution. For instance, it doesn't compare with the recently published "Multimodal Compact Trilinear Pooling" (MCT) by the same authors (Fukui et al., ICLR 2016), which already extends MCB to three modalities. This oversight weakens the novelty claim.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting attention mechanism with some promising qualitative results, it fails to substantiate its key claims of novelty and state-of-the-art performance. The method appears to be a straightforward extension of existing pairwise attention models without sufficient justification for why this extension provides meaningful improvements. The lack of detailed quantitative comparisons to current SOTA methods, the absence of ablation studies isolating the high-order components' contribution, and the unaddressed computational complexity are significant weaknesses.

Strengths: Clear organization, good figures, promising qualitative results, technically sound formulation of the attention model.

Weaknesses: Unsubstantiated novelty claim, insufficient quantitative comparison to SOTA, missing ablation studies, unaddressed computational complexity.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence supporting the main claims. The paper would benefit from: 1) detailed quantitative comparison to current SOTA methods, 2) ablation studies isolating the contribution of high-order components, 3) clearer distinction from existing work (particularly Fukui et al. 2016), and 4) discussion of computational complexity. Without these, the paper's contribution remains unclear and overstated.
