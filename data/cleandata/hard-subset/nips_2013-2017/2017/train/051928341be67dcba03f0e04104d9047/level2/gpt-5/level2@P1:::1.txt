Summary
This paper proposes a general multimodal attention mechanism that models unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions across modalities (image V, question Q, and answers A) via learnable “potentials,” and computes modality-specific attentions by a single mean-field-style update (linear combination of marginalized potentials followed by softmax). Pairwise interactions use bilinear correlations between embedded features; ternary interactions use a trilinear correlation tensor that can be marginalized to yield per-modality signals. Attended features are fused for decision making using compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) based on the tensor sketch algorithm. The method is applied to VQA (multiple-choice), where the authors report state-of-the-art performance on VQA test-dev/test-std with fewer parameters than prior work (e.g., MCB), along with qualitative attention visualizations. The approach relates to co-attention mechanisms (Lu et al. NIPS’16), bilinear pooling for multimodal fusion (Fukui et al. 2016; Kim et al. ICLR’17), and structured attention (Kim et al. 2017), but differs by formulating attention as high-order potentials with straightforward extension beyond two modalities and explicit marginalization.

Quality
- Soundness: The construction of unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and the use of bilinear/trilinear correlations are reasonable and align with prior multimodal attention literature. The linear combination of potentials with learned weights provides flexibility. The decision module leverages established MCB/MCT machinery.
- Probabilistic claim: The “mean-field” interpretation is suggestive rather than fully formal. An explicit energy/graphical model, factorization assumptions, and a derivation of the updates would strengthen the claim. As written, the update is a learnable weighted sum of potentials followed by softmax (arguably equivalent to one MF step in a specific CRF), but the underlying distribution and approximations are not precisely specified.
- Efficiency: The pairwise correlation matrix is standard. The ternary tensor could be expensive in principle; feasibility hinges on using only the 18 candidate answers per example (as in VQA MC), which should be clarified more explicitly (vs. the 3000-class answer vocabulary used for training the classifier). A brief complexity/memory analysis would be helpful.
- Empirics: The paper reports strong MC accuracy and shows MCT vs. stacked MCB analysis. However, ablation on the attention design itself is limited: it would be important to quantify the contributions of unary vs. pairwise vs. ternary potentials, the learned weights α,β,γ, and the effect of including answers as an input modality. Evaluation appears restricted to VQA v1 multiple-choice; open-ended results (and/or VQA v2) are not reported.
- Reproducibility: Implementation details, hyperparameters, and a code link are provided; this is appreciated.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-structured, with clear diagrams. A few notational issues/typos (e.g., “and aV = ∑ PA(ia) aia” should be aA) and occasional ambiguity around whether A denotes the global answer vocabulary vs. the per-instance MC options. The mean-field framing would benefit from a more explicit formulation. Otherwise, the technical exposition is understandable.

Originality
Moderate. Bilinear attention and high-order feature fusion are known; the main novelty is treating attention as a collection of high-order potentials across modalities with per-modality marginalization, and demonstrating that adding the answer modality can be exploited in MC VQA through ternary attention. The framework is clean and extensible beyond two modalities.

Significance
Empirically, attaining SOTA on VQA MC with fewer parameters is meaningful. The approach provides interpretable, multimodal attentions and a principled way to scale to higher-order interactions. The reliance on MC (and use of answer candidates as inputs) limits generality to open-ended VQA and to settings without candidate lists. Broader evaluation (e.g., VQA v2, Visual7W) and stronger ablations would make the impact clearer.

Pros
- Simple, modular formulation of multimodal attention via unary/pairwise/ternary potentials with learned reliability weights.
- Clean use of compact bilinear/trilinear pooling for decision making; parameter-efficient SOTA on VQA MC.
- Clear qualitative visualizations; code availability.
- Conceptually extensible to more than two modalities.

Cons
- Mean-field/probabilistic grounding is not fully formalized; the method operates more like a learned weighted potential combiner.
- Limited ablations on the attention components and the role of ternary interactions; unclear how much each part contributes.
- Evaluation scope limited to VQA v1 multiple-choice; no open-ended or VQA v2 results.
- Computational considerations of ternary potentials not analyzed; ambiguity about handling of answer modality (global vocab vs. per-example candidates).
- Minor clarity/notation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide an explicit probabilistic model (energy, factor graph) and derive the mean-field updates, clarifying assumptions and the role of the learned mixing weights.
- Add ablations:
  - Remove ternary potentials; remove pairwise potentials; unary-only baselines.
  - Vary α,β,γ or learn per-modality gates; report learned weights to interpret which potentials dominate.
  - Compare 3-modality vs. 2-modality without answer inputs on MC, and report open-ended results (without A).
- Clarify the computational complexity and memory of C3. State explicitly that na=18 per example (MC) during attention, and discuss feasibility for open-ended settings.
- Broaden evaluation: VQA v2, Visual7W, and type-wise breakdowns (Yes/No, Number, Other), as well as comparisons to strong bilinear baselines (e.g., MLB/MFB if available).
- Fix typos/notation and unify the description of answer embeddings across training and inference.

Overall recommendation
A solid and timely contribution with a clean, extensible multimodal attention framework and strong MC VQA results. While the probabilistic justification and ablations could be strengthened and the evaluation broadened, the paper’s ideas and empirical performance merit interest. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the modeling and expanding the experimental analysis.
