- Proposes a high-order attention mechanism for VQA modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer.
- Claims state-of-the-art performance by learning higher-order correlations to better direct attention.
- Paper is well-organized with clear figures illustrating the attention mechanisms (Figs. 3–8).
- Mathematical formulation and description of unary, pairwise, and ternary modules are technically sound (Eq. 1).
- Qualitative results show attention focusing on relevant image regions, aiding interpretability (Figs. 5–7).
- Novelty claim is unsubstantiated and not clearly distinct from existing work on pairwise attention.
- Fails to adequately contrast with prior pairwise interaction methods (Lu et al. 2016, Fukui et al. 2016).
- Ternary potential appears to be a direct extension of pairwise attention without demonstrated unique benefit.
- "State-of-the-art" claim lacks supporting numerical details; referenced Tab. 1 and comparisons are missing.
- Reported 64.57% accuracy for 2-layer MCB is not compared against known VQA Challenge top results, so SOTA is unverifiable.
- Missing critical ablation studies to isolate the contribution of ternary/high-order components versus other factors (e.g., embedding dimensionality).
- Computational complexity of ternary potential (O(nq * nv * na)) is prohibitive and unaddressed for realistic VQA sizes.
- Limited evidence that qualitative improvements translate into substantial quantitative gains or generalize beyond VQA.
- Overlooks comparison to Multimodal Compact Trilinear Pooling (MCT) and prior three-modality extensions, weakening novelty.
- Strengths: clear organization, good figures, promising qualitative results, and solid technical formulation.
- Weaknesses: unsubstantiated novelty, insufficient SOTA comparisons, missing ablations, and unaddressed computational cost.
- Recommendation: reject due to insufficient evidence; paper should add detailed SOTA comparisons, ablations, clearer differentiation from prior work, and complexity analysis.
