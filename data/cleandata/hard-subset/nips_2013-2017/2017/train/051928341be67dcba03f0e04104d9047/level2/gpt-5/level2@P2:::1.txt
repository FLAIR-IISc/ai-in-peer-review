Summary
This paper proposes a general attention mechanism that models unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” across multiple modalities (image, question, and answer) and combines them via a single mean-field-like update to produce marginal attentions over each modality. Pairwise interactions are encoded via a similarity matrix between modalities; ternary interactions are encoded via a 3-way correlation tensor. The attended vectors are then fused with either multimodal compact trilinear pooling (MCT) or a two-layer MCB stack for answer prediction. The method is evaluated on VQA (multiple choice), reporting state-of-the-art performance with fewer parameters than some prior systems, and includes qualitative visualizations of attention. The authors argue the framework extends naturally to k modalities.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against co-attention (Lu et al., NIPS’16), stacked attention, dynamic memory networks, and MCB pooling (Fukui et al., 2016). The key differences claimed are (i) learning high-order correlations explicitly via potentials over multiple modalities and (ii) a probabilistic interpretation with mean-field inference. The paper also mentions related structured/dual/bilinear attention (Kim et al. 2017; Nam et al. 2016), and bilinear/trilinear fusion via sketching (Pham & Pagh; Fukui et al.). While bilinear fusion and co-attention are well established, the explicit construction and use of ternary potentials for attention (and the discussion of extending to higher order) appears less explored at the time.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for modeling higher-order cross-modal interactions and a concrete architecture to compute them.
- Simple, modular construction of unary, pairwise, and ternary terms; uses only 1x1 convs and inner products, making it easy to implement.
- The combination of attention over three modalities in VQA (including answer choices) is intuitive for multiple-choice VQA and yields strong results.
- Uses compact sketching (MCB/MCT) to avoid the parameter explosion in higher-order fusion.
- Includes ablations (2-modality vs 3-modality; MCT vs 2-layer MCB) and qualitative analyses; open-source code is provided.

Weaknesses
- Probabilistic framing is underspecified. The paper invokes mean-field inference but does not define a joint distribution or energy function over the attention variables. The “potentials” are learned feature transforms combined linearly before a softmax; without a clear probabilistic model, calling this “mean-field” is potentially misleading.
- Computational/memory complexity of ternary potentials is not analyzed. Explicit construction of C3 over nq×nv×na can be prohibitively large (na≈3000). The paper appears to compute and marginalize via learned 1x1 convs, but it is unclear whether C3 is formed explicitly or computed implicitly; the practical complexity and memory footprint should be clarified.
- Evaluation is limited to multiple-choice VQA. Using answer choices as an input modality changes the task setting and can benefit from dataset biases in answer space. Comparisons to methods that do not use candidate answers at test time (open-ended setting) are not fully commensurate. Results on open-ended VQA (and/or Visual7W) would strengthen claims of generality.
- Empirical gains appear modest in the provided numbers (e.g., 64.57% vs 63.82% in the ablation), and the state-of-the-art claim is difficult to verify without complete tables and strict apples-to-apples settings (same visual backbone, feature dimensionality, train/val splits).
- Lack of thorough ablation on the contribution of each potential (unary vs +pairwise vs +ternary) to accuracy. The text mentions learned weights suggest different reliabilities, but quantitative evidence is missing.
- Some clarity issues and typos (e.g., duplicated aV definition for aA; inconsistent notation for Corr2/3 pipelines; description of “convolution along the visual dimension” could be formalized).

Quality
Technically sound in the sense of a well-defined neural architecture built from standard components (1x1 convs, inner products, sketching). However, the probabilistic interpretation is not rigorously supported, and the computational feasibility of ternary attention over large answer sets is not addressed. Experimental methodology is reasonable but narrow (multiple-choice only). Claims of state-of-the-art would benefit from more rigorous, matched comparisons.

Clarity
Well organized, with clear architectural figures and intuitive descriptions of unary, pairwise, and ternary modules. Some notational slips and missing details (complexity; training time/memory for ternary; whether C3 is explicit or implicit) hinder full understanding and reproducibility of the high-order attention computations.

Originality
Moderate. The idea of higher-order attention across more than two modalities is a useful extension, and the specific ternary attention design is novel relative to prior bilinear co-attention. However, the building blocks (inner products, 1x1 convs, sketching) are known, and the decision-layer trilinear fusion is a straightforward extension of MCB.

Significance
Potentially useful for tasks where multiple modalities (including candidate outputs) are available at inference time. If the approach scales and yields consistent gains, it could influence subsequent multimodal attention designs. The current empirical scope limits the immediate impact.

Questions for authors
1) How is C3 computed in practice? Is the nq×nv×na tensor explicitly instantiated? If not, describe the implicit computation and its time/memory complexity. Provide profiling numbers.
2) Provide a systematic ablation adding potentials: unary only; +pairwise; +ternary. How do accuracy and attention quality change?
3) Do the gains persist without using candidate answers as input (open-ended VQA)? If not feasible, evaluate on Visual7W multiple-choice to test generality.
4) Clarify the “mean-field” claim by specifying an energy or factor graph and showing how Eq. (1) corresponds to a valid mean-field update.
5) How sensitive are results to the embedding dimension d and the MCB/MCT output size?
6) Are the marginalization weights w learned per position or shared? Please formalize their shapes and learning.

Minor comments
- Fix typos (e.g., repeated aV; “and and”; inconsistent notation in Sec. 3.2.2/3.2.3).
- Report exact test-dev/test-std numbers with backbones and parameter counts for compared methods, ensuring same setting.
- Consider reporting attention entropy or calibration metrics to quantify attention quality.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The proposed higher-order attention idea is interesting and practically effective for multiple-choice VQA, with solid engineering and available code. Strengthening the probabilistic justification, clarifying computational details, and broadening the evaluation would significantly improve the paper. Confidence: medium.
