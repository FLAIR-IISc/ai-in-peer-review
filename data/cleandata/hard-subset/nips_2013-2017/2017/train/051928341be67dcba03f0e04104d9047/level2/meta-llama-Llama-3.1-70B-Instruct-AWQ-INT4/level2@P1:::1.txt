This paper proposes a novel and generally applicable form of attention mechanism that learns high-order correlations between various data modalities. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their high-order attention mechanism on the task of visual question answering (VQA), achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset. 

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of their approach. The introduction effectively sets the stage for the rest of the paper, highlighting the importance of attention mechanisms in cognitive-like tasks and the limitations of current approaches. The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of existing attention mechanisms for image and textual data.

The proposed high-order attention model is well-motivated and clearly explained. The authors provide a detailed description of the data embedding, attention, and decision-making components of their model. The use of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture correlations between different modalities is a key contribution of the paper. The authors also provide a clear explanation of the mean-field inference process used to obtain marginal probabilities from the potentials.

The experimental evaluation of the proposed approach is thorough and well-executed. The authors compare their approach to several baselines, including the approaches of Lu et al. and Fukui et al., and demonstrate the effectiveness of their high-order attention mechanism on the VQA real-image test-dev and test-std datasets. The qualitative evaluation of the approach is also well-done, with the authors providing several examples of the attention weights learned by their model.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their approach. While they mention that their model has 40 million parameters, they do not provide a detailed breakdown of the computational cost of each component of their model. This would be useful for readers who are interested in implementing the approach in practice.

Another potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a detailed comparison of their approach to other recent work on attention mechanisms for VQA. For example, they do not compare their approach to the approach of Nam et al., which also uses a dual attention mechanism. A more detailed comparison to other recent work would be useful for readers who are interested in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.

Overall, the paper is well-written and provides a clear and thorough explanation of the proposed approach. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed, and the authors provide a good qualitative evaluation of their approach. The paper is a strong contribution to the field of visual question answering and attention mechanisms.
