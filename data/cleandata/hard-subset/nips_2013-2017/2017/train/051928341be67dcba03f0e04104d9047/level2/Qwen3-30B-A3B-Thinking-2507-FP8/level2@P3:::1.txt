

This paper introduces a novel high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that models correlations across multiple data modalities (visual, textual, and answer representations) through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The approach extends beyond existing attention mechanisms that primarily focus on pairwise interactions between modalities, proposing a probabilistic framework grounded in mean-field inference that can trivially generalize to any number of modalities. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the standard VQA dataset, with both quantitative results and qualitative visualizations of attention maps.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of multimodal learning by formalizing attention as high-order correlations rather than single-modality or pairwise interactions. The work builds upon and clearly differentiates from recent attention mechanisms in VQA, particularly addressing limitations in approaches like Lu et al. [15] and Xu et al. [25] that cannot easily extend beyond two modalities. The theoretical foundation is sound, with well-defined mathematical formulations for unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, and the mean-field inference approach provides a principled way to compute marginal attention probabilities.

The quality of the work is strong. The experimental setup is thorough, with comprehensive comparisons to multiple baselines including state-of-the-art methods like those by Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7]. The paper provides both quantitative metrics on test-dev and test-std datasets, showing that the high-order attention model achieves 64.57% accuracy with a relatively simple architecture (40 million parameters compared to 70+ million in prior work). The qualitative analysis in Figures 5-7 effectively demonstrates how the attention mechanism correlates different modalities to answer questions correctly, with clear visual evidence of the model's focus on relevant regions. The ablation studies comparing 2-modality and 3-modality models convincingly show the value of high-order attention.

The clarity of the paper is good, with well-organized sections and helpful visualizations (Figures 3-4 particularly clarify the attention mechanisms). The mathematical notation is consistent, and the description of data embeddings (Section 4.1) and decision making (Section 4.2) is sufficiently detailed for replication. Some minor improvements could be made to the exposition of the tensor sketch algorithm and its implementation details for better reproducibility.

The originality of the work is clear. While attention mechanisms have been widely studied for VQA, the high-order correlation approach is novel in its formalization and generalization to any number of modalities. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, acknowledging relevant prior work while clearly articulating how this approach differs from and improves upon existing methods. The extension beyond pairwise attention represents a meaningful conceptual advancement.

The significance of this work is substantial. Achieving state-of-the-art results on the VQA benchmark with a relatively simple architecture demonstrates the practical value of high-order attention modeling. The framework's ability to generalize to any number of modalities suggests broader applicability beyond VQA to other multimodal tasks. The paper provides both theoretical insights and practical performance gains, making it valuable to both researchers and practitioners.

One minor weakness is that the paper does not thoroughly address how the high-order attention mechanism might perform on tasks with more than three modalities, though this is not a critical limitation for the VQA application. The authors could strengthen their analysis by including a brief discussion of potential scaling challenges for higher-order correlations.

The paper's approach is also notable for its simplicity compared to more complex attention architectures. The authors achieve strong results without extensive model engineering, which suggests the high-order attention mechanism could be readily adopted by other researchers working on multimodal tasks.

The review of related work is comprehensive and well-structured, with appropriate citations to key papers in VQA and attention mechanisms. The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of prior work and position their contribution within the broader context of multimodal learning.

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of multimodal learning by formalizing high-order attention mechanisms. The technical approach is sound, the results are compelling, and the work has clear potential for broader application. The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and makes a strong case for the value of modeling high-order correlations in multimodal attention. The work advances the state of the art in VQA while providing a general framework that could inspire future research in attention mechanisms across various multimodal tasks.
