## Review of "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering"

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism leveraging high-order correlations between data modalities, demonstrated on the Visual Question Answering (VQA) task. The core idea is to move beyond pairwise interactions to model relationships between multiple modalities (image, question, and answer) using potentials and mean-field inference. The authors claim to achieve state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing limitations of existing attention mechanisms that often treat modalities in an ad-hoc manner. The proposed approach of explicitly modeling high-order correlations is a logical extension of existing work and offers potential benefits in capturing complex relationships. The mathematical formulation, while dense, appears sound and the use of tensor sketching to manage computational complexity is a practical consideration. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating SOTA performance on the VQA dataset, and the ablation studies comparing different decision-making units (MCT vs. MCB) are valuable. The qualitative examples, showcasing attention maps, provide some intuition into the model’s behavior.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The writing, while generally clear, is occasionally verbose and repetitive, particularly in the related work section. The related work discussion, while extensive, could be more critical; it often describes existing methods without fully articulating their limitations *relative* to the proposed approach beyond the initial motivation. The ablation studies are limited – exploring different orders of correlation (beyond pairwise and ternary) or varying the dimensionality of the embedding space would strengthen the analysis.  The failure case analysis, while acknowledged, is brief and doesn’t offer much insight into the model’s shortcomings.  Furthermore, the improvement over existing methods, while statistically significant, appears incremental, raising the question of whether the added complexity of high-order correlations is fully justified.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to attention modeling with potential for broader applicability.
*   State-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark (VQA).
*   Clear mathematical formulation and practical consideration of computational complexity.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental improvement over existing methods.
*   Limited ablation studies.
*   Verbose writing and somewhat superficial related work discussion.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution with a promising idea. While the improvements aren't dramatic, the novelty and potential of the approach warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically focusing on streamlining the writing, expanding the ablation studies, and providing a more in-depth analysis of failure cases. The authors should also more clearly articulate the advantages of their approach over existing methods beyond the initial motivation.
