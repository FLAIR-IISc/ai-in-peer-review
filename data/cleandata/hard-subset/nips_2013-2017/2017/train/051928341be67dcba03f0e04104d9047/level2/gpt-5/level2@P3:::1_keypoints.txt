- Proposes a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image (V), question (Q), and candidate answers (A).  
- Computes pairwise correlations via similarity matrices and ternary correlations via a third-order tensor C3 capturing triple interactions.  
- Obtains marginal attention distributions by a single learned mean-field-like linear combination of potentials followed by softmax.  
- Fuses attended modality vectors using compact bilinear (MCB) or trilinear (MCT) pooling via tensor sketching for classification.  
- Demonstrates improved performance and answer-conditioned attention visualizations on VQA multiple-choice relative to several prior attention and pooling methods.  
- Distinguishes itself from prior co-attention and structured attention by explicitly modeling up to ternary correlations inside the attention module rather than only at fusion.  
- Leverages compact tensor sketching to keep high-order fusion computationally tractable and provides code and training details.  
- Conceptual novelty: clear general formulation of attention with explicit high-order potentials and simple mean-field combination.  
- Practical relevance: shows measurable gains from answer-conditioned attention in multiple-choice VQA with reasonable qualitative attention maps.  
- Sound engineering: uses established sketching techniques and reports state-of-the-art behavior with fewer parameters than some baselines.  
- Main limitation: scope limited to multiple-choice VQA—using candidate answers as an attention modality doesn’t apply to open-ended settings.  
- Attribution unclear: improvements may come from answer-conditioning and/or high-order attention; ablations isolating unary/pairwise/ternary effects are missing.  
- Probabilistic framing is informal: the mean-field interpretation is a learned linear mixture with no clear energy/factorization or convergence analysis.  
- Computational concerns: complexity and memory footprint of C3 and its marginalization are not explicitly analyzed, especially as na varies (e.g., 18 vs 3000).  
- Experimental breadth lacking: additional datasets (VQA v2.0, Visual7W) and per-question-type breakdowns are not provided to demonstrate generality.  
- Methodologically sound but reporting incomplete: full test-dev/test-std tables, per-category numbers, and stronger ablations are needed for fair assessment.  
- Clarity mostly good with intuitive illustrations, but notation issues, typos, and unclear tensor/convolution shapes require fixes.  
- Originality: novel explicit ternary attention with mean-field combination and emphasis on extensibility to more than two modalities.  
- Significance: likely to be adopted for multiple-choice VQA, but broader impact depends on demonstrating benefits without answer-conditioning.  
- Essential improvements requested: provide complete quantitative results and targeted ablations (unary; unary+pairwise; +ternary; without answer-modality).  
- Essential: clarify training/evaluation of the answer modality (na=18 vs 3000), embedding/selection of candidate answers, and training protocol.  
- Essential: add a complexity/memory analysis and practical runtime/peak-memory numbers for pairwise/ternary computations.  
- Recommended: evaluate on open-ended VQA and additional datasets to show generality and reduce language priors.  
- Recommended: experiment with more than one mean-field iteration and report effects, and ensure encoder/backbone parity for fair baseline comparisons.  
- Recommended: fix notation/typos and detail tensor shapes and learned 1×1 convolution roles used for marginalization.  
- Pros for acceptance: clear, general, and practically effective high-order attention; strong qualitative results; code availability.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited to multiple-choice, incomplete ablations/reporting, informal probabilistic justification, and light computational analysis.
