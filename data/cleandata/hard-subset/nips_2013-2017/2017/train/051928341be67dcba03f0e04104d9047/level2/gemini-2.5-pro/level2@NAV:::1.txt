This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically Visual Question Answering (VQA), that models high-order correlations between data modalities. The central claim is that extending beyond standard unary attention (on a single modality) or pairwise co-attention (between two modalities, e.g., image and question) to model third-order correlations (among image, question, and answer) leads to improved performance. The authors formalize this as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using a single step of mean-field inference to derive the final attention distributions. This work builds upon previous co-attention models for VQA, such as Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016), by providing a principled framework to incorporate an additional modality (answers) into the attention process.

The main strengths of the paper are its originality and significance. The formulation of high-order attention via potentials is an elegant and extensible idea that moves beyond the ad-hoc architectures common in this domain. The claim that this framework can be trivially extended to any number of modalities is a significant conceptual contribution. The paper’s quality is high, with the claims being strongly supported by experimental results. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the competitive VQA dataset demonstrates the practical value of the proposed model. The inclusion of qualitative visualizations, including a failure case, adds to the paper's clarity and provides valuable insight into the model's behavior.

However, the paper could be strengthened in a few areas. While the overall clarity is good, the connection to mean-field inference is stated but not deeply explored; a brief justification for why a single iteration is sufficient would be beneficial. The experimental analysis would be more compelling with a detailed ablation study that isolates the contribution of each potential (unary, pairwise, ternary). For instance, showing results for a model with only unary and pairwise potentials versus the full model would more directly validate the importance of the third-order term. Finally, the finding that a 2-layer MCB unit outperforms the conceptually cleaner MCT unit warrants a more thorough analysis beyond a brief mention of "greater expressiveness."

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated contribution to attention mechanisms. It addresses a relevant problem and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The proposed high-order attention framework is likely to inspire further research in multimodal learning.
