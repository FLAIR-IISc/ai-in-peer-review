- Summary: Proposes a unified high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) with mean-field–like marginalization and compact pooling (MCB/MCT) applied to VQA.

- Strength: Clear, principled formulation connecting attention to structured/mean-field ideas with a learnable combination of potentials.

- Strength: Naturally generalizes co-attention to more than two modalities, enabling ternary interactions without bespoke engineering.

- Strength: Practical implementation using count-sketch pooling and detailed training/implementation choices; public code promised.

- Strength: Solid qualitative analyses and reported ablations comparing MCT and two-layer MCB.

- Weakness: Claim of state-of-the-art is under-specified; missing statistical significance, per-type breakdowns, and comparisons to strong recent baselines.

- Weakness: Ambiguity about role of na across classification (top-3k answers) vs multiple-choice (18 options); paper should separate regimes and justify ternary use.

- Weakness: Computational/memory analysis of ternary potential C3 and its marginalization is thin and not quantified, especially for non–multiple-choice settings.

- Weakness: Limited ablation of learned combination weights (α, β, γ) and of removing individual potentials to validate each term’s contribution.

- Quality: Method is technically sound and well-motivated; implementation choices like 1×1 convs and tensor sketching are appropriate.

- Clarity: Generally well-organized with helpful figures, but needs clearer dataset/setting delineation (test-dev vs test-std; MC vs open-ended) and exact numeric reporting.

- Originality: Extends co-attention to principled higher-order interactions and distinguishes itself from prior bilinear/alternating attention work.

- Significance: Likely useful for VQA and multimodal tasks; ternary attention over answers is particularly interesting for multiple-choice.

- Suggestion: Report full quantitative results with confidence intervals, per-category breakdowns, and training/inference costs.

- Suggestion: Add ablations on potentials and learned weights; analyze cases where high-order terms help or hurt.

- Suggestion: Evaluate on additional benchmarks (e.g., Visual7W, VQA v2.0) and with stronger visual features like object proposals.

- Suggestion: Discuss extending ternary attention beyond multiple-choice and strategies for pruning or sparsifying C3 to control complexity.
