- Introduces a novel probabilistic attention mechanism for multi-modal tasks (demonstrated on VQA) that models and combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials.  
- Decomposes attention into learnable unary (single-modality), pairwise (two-modality), and ternary (image, question, answer) potentials combined via a single-step, mean-field-inspired fusion to produce attention distributions.  
- Attended features are fused using Multimodal Compact Pooling for final classification.  
- Novelty: principled incorporation of a third modality (answers) through ternary potentials, extending prior co-attention work.  
- Empirical quality: achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA and ablations show clear benefit of the ternary (3-modality) model over 2-modality variants.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized; model decomposition and Figure 3 aid understanding.  
- Weakness: the link to mean-field inference is under-justified and should be clarified or framed as heuristic inspiration.  
- Weakness: computation and implementation details of marginalized potentials (pairwise and ternary weight sharing/conv vs. independent parameters) are unclear and hurt reproducibility.  
- Weakness: evaluation is limited to VQA; additional experiments on other multi-modal tasks would better support claims of generality.  
- Weakness: qualitative analysis of the ternary model’s attention (Figure 6) is brief and needs deeper explanation of how the answer modality affects attention.  
- Pros summary: novel, principled high-order attention framework; strong empirical results; clear presentation; potentially extensible to other tasks.  
- Cons summary: theoretical connection to mean-field needs strengthening, implementation details require clarification, and generality claim needs broader evaluation.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with significant contribution; recommend acceptance pending clarifications.
