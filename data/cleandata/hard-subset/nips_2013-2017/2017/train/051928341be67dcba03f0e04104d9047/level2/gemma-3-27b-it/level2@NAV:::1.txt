## Review of High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering

This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism leveraging high-order correlations between data modalities, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core idea – moving beyond pairwise interactions to capture more complex relationships – is interesting and potentially impactful, as many cognitive tasks inherently involve integrating information from multiple sources. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, lending initial support to their approach. 

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear motivation and introduction of the problem. The related work section is comprehensive, covering existing attention mechanisms for both image and text, and effectively positions the proposed method within the current landscape.  The technical details of the high-order attention model are presented with reasonable clarity, outlining the use of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials and the mean-field inference process. The authors also address implementation details and experimental setup, which are crucial for reproducibility. A comparative analysis with existing methods like Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7] is performed, showcasing the benefits of their approach.

A key strength lies in the theoretical grounding of the method, framing attention as a probabilistic model with high-order potentials. The use of tensor sketching to address the computational complexity of high-order correlations is a clever solution. Qualitative results, visualizing the attention maps, further illustrate the model's behavior and confirm that it focuses on relevant image regions correlated with the question.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The gains over existing SOTA methods seem marginal, and the impact of the ternary potentials isn’t thoroughly investigated or convincingly demonstrated. The ablation studies could be more extensive, for example, testing the performance with only pairwise attention to better isolate the contribution of the higher-order correlations. While the authors acknowledge limitations, a discussion of potential biases in the VQA dataset, and how these might affect the results, would be valuable. Further, the figures are a bit difficult to interpret and could be improved for clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to attention mechanisms capturing higher-order correlations.
*   State-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and a well-structured presentation.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in multimodal learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Marginal performance gains compared to existing methods.
*   Limited ablation studies to isolate the impact of ternary potentials.
*   Lack of discussion regarding potential dataset biases.



**Overall:** This paper presents a promising direction for attention mechanism research. While the gains are not substantial, the theoretical novelty and strong experimental results make it a valuable contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses highlighted above. The authors should be encouraged to further explore the benefits of higher-order attention and conduct more thorough ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component.
