

This paper proposes a novel high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that models correlations across multiple data modalities (image, question, answer) rather than just pairwise interactions. The authors formalize attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to obtain marginal attention probabilities. They demonstrate that this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the standard VQA dataset.

The paper's main strengths include a clear technical contribution that extends existing attention mechanisms to higher-order correlations, a well-structured presentation of the mathematical formulation, and strong empirical results. The authors effectively show that their high-order attention mechanism outperforms previous approaches (including Lu et al. [15] and Fukui et al. [7]) on VQA benchmarks while using fewer parameters. The qualitative results in Figures 5-7 provide valuable insights into how the model attends to different modalities, which is particularly useful for understanding the model's behavior.

Regarding quality, the paper presents a technically sound approach with appropriate theoretical grounding in probabilistic modeling. The experimental setup is thorough and well-documented, with careful comparison to relevant baselines. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA with 40 million parameters, compared to 70+ million used in some previous approaches. The results are well-supported by quantitative metrics on both test-dev and test-std datasets.

The paper is generally clear in its presentation, with well-organized sections and helpful figures (Fig. 3 particularly clarifies the attention mechanisms). The mathematical formulations are precise and well-explained, though the description of the ternary potential calculation (Section 3.2.3) could be slightly more intuitive for readers unfamiliar with higher-order tensor operations. The writing is professional and accessible to machine learning researchers.

The originality of the work is well-justified. While some prior work (e.g., [15, 25]) discusses attention over two modalities, this paper is the first to systematically model and evaluate higher-order correlations (up to ternary) for multimodal tasks. The authors provide a clear comparison to related work, though they could better emphasize how their probabilistic approach differs from the "structured attention" of Kim et al. [14] and the "dual attention" of Nam et al. [19].

The significance of the contribution is strong. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA, a challenging multimodal task, and demonstrates that modeling high-order correlations is beneficial. The authors' insight that higher-order attention mechanisms can effectively guide attention to relevant elements across modalities is valuable for the field. The model's simplicity and effectiveness suggest potential applications to other multimodal tasks beyond VQA.

I have a few specific suggestions for improvement. First, the paper would benefit from a more detailed ablation study showing the contribution of each component (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) to the overall performance. The current results show that the 3-modality model outperforms the 2-modality model, but it's not clear how much each potential contributes. Second, while the paper states that higher-order correlations improve performance, it doesn't thoroughly explain *why* these correlations are necessary for VQA - a deeper analysis of when high-order interactions matter most would strengthen the contribution. Third, the comparison to related work could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding the "bilinear models" of Kim et al. [13] and the "tensor sketch" approach of Pham and Pagh [21].

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS, with a strong technical contribution that advances the state of the art in multimodal learning. The authors have made a significant contribution by formalizing high-order attention mechanisms in a way that's both theoretically grounded and practically effective. The work is clearly written, technically sound, and represents a meaningful advancement over prior attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks.

Overall, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with a novel approach that achieves state-of-the-art results while offering new insights into multimodal attention mechanisms. The work has clear significance for both researchers and practitioners working on multimodal learning tasks. The minor weaknesses identified above are easily addressable and don't detract from the paper's overall contribution.
