- Proposes a novel probabilistic attention mechanism that models unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture high-order inter-modal correlations.  
- Combines learned linear weights of these potentials and applies softmax (interpreted as a single mean-field inference step) to produce modality-specific attention distributions.  
- Applies the method to VQA, uses attended image/question/answer vectors fused with multi-layer MCB, and reports state-of-the-art results on VQA test-dev and test-std.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and components are described in sufficient detail with rigorous quantitative and qualitative evaluations.  
- Includes helpful visualizations of attention maps, including failure cases, giving a balanced assessment of strengths and limitations.  
- Clarity is high: well-written organization, clear explanations of unary/pairwise/ternary potentials, and useful figures (e.g., Fig. 3).  
- Reproducibility could improve by clarifying how the answer-modality A (top-3000 answers) is constructed and used during training/inference and its computational implications.  
- The connection to mean-field inference should be discussed further, specifically the rationale for using a single iteration and trade-offs with additional iterations.  
- Original contribution: a principled, extensible multi-order potential framework that departs from alternating co-attention and simple fusion approaches.  
- Significance: demonstrates practical value via SOTA VQA performance and offers a general framework applicable to other multi-modal tasks (e.g., video-dialogue, text-to-image).  
- Strengths summarized: novelty/generalizability, strong empirical performance, clear presentation, and potential high impact on multi-modal reasoning research.  
- Minor weaknesses: 2-modality baseline comparison may be unfair due to smaller embedding dimensionality; authors should acknowledge or reframe this limitation.
