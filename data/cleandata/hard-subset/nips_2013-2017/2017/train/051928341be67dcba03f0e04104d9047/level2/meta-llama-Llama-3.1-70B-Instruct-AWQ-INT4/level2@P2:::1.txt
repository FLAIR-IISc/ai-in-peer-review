This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism that learns high-order correlations between various data modalities, which is demonstrated to be effective for visual question answering (VQA). The authors provide a thorough review of related work on attention mechanisms for both image and textual data, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches. The proposed high-order attention model is well-motivated and clearly explained, with a focus on its ability to capture dependencies between multiple modalities.

The technical quality of the paper is high, with a clear and well-organized presentation of the proposed method. The authors provide a detailed description of the attention mechanism, including the use of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, as well as the mean-field inference approach. The experimental evaluation is also thorough, with a comparison to state-of-the-art methods on the VQA dataset.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide insights into the inner workings of the attention mechanism. The authors provide visualizations of the attention weights, which help to illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The qualitative evaluation also highlights the ability of the model to attend to relevant regions of the image and question.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear and concise presentation of the proposed method and experimental results. The authors have also made an effort to provide a thorough review of related work, which helps to situate the proposed approach within the broader context of attention mechanisms.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of attention mechanisms, particularly in the context of VQA. The proposed high-order attention model is a novel and effective approach to capturing dependencies between multiple modalities. The paper also provides a new perspective on the use of attention mechanisms in VQA, highlighting the importance of considering multiple modalities.

The significance of the paper is also high, as it has the potential to impact a wide range of applications that involve multimodal data, such as image captioning, visual question generation, and machine comprehension. The proposed approach is also relatively simple and easy to implement, which makes it accessible to a wide range of researchers and practitioners.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper for publication. The technical quality is high, the presentation is clear and well-organized, and the proposed approach makes a significant contribution to the field of attention mechanisms.
