Summary
The paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning, instantiated on Visual Question Answering (VQA). Attention is formulated as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over modalities (image V, question Q, and candidate answers A). Pairwise correlations are computed via similarity matrices (e.g., QWq(VWv)T), and ternary correlations via a third-order tensor C3 capturing triple interactions. Marginal attention distributions over each modality are obtained by a single mean-field-like step: a learned linear combination of unary, marginalized pairwise, and marginalized ternary potentials followed by softmax. The attended modality vectors are then fused through compact bilinear (MCB) or trilinear (MCT) pooling (via tensor sketching) for classification. Experiments on VQA multiple-choice demonstrate improvements over prior attention models such as hierarchical co-attention [Lu et al., NIPS’16] and MCB-based methods [Fukui et al., 2016], with qualitative visualizations indicating question- and answer-conditioned attention.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to co-attention [Lu et al., 2016], stacked attention [Yang et al., 2016], and structured attention [Kim et al., 2017], but differs by explicitly modeling and learning high-order (up to ternary) correlations across modalities within the attention module itself, rather than only at fusion. It also builds on compact bilinear/trilinear pooling [Fukui et al., 2016; Pham & Pagh, 2013] for decision-making. Dual attention [Nam et al., 2016] and bilinear pooling variants (e.g., low-rank bilinear pooling [Kim et al., ICLR’17]) are discussed; the presented approach aims to be easily extensible beyond two modalities, which is difficult for some prior co-attention designs.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clear, general formulation of attention with explicit unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and a simple mean-field combination, naturally accommodating more than two modalities.
- Practical relevance: demonstrates that including answer-conditioned attention (for the multiple-choice setting) yields measurable gains; qualitative examples show reasonable, question- and answer-conditioned focus.
- Sound engineering: leverages tensor sketching to keep high-order fusion tractable; provides training details and a code repository.
- Comparisons: evaluates MCT vs a two-layer MCB setup; reports state-of-the-art behavior on VQA multiple-choice with fewer parameters than some baselines.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Scope limited to the multiple-choice setting; using candidate answers as an attention modality is not applicable to open-ended VQA. Generality to open-ended (classification over a fixed vocabulary without per-question candidate inputs) is not demonstrated.
- Attribution of gains: improvements may stem from answer-conditioning and/or high-order attention; ablations isolating unary vs pairwise vs ternary potentials are not fully reported.
- Probabilistic framing is informal: the “mean-field” interpretation amounts to a learned linear mixture of potentials followed by softmax; no clear energy, factorization, or convergence analysis is provided. The benefits of additional mean-field iterations are not explored.
- Computational considerations: while ternary attention is plausible for multiple choice (18 candidates), the complexity and memory footprint of C3 and its marginalization should be analyzed explicitly; the paper would benefit from clarifying how na is handled during training vs evaluation (top-3000 vs 18 choices).
- Experimental breadth: results on additional datasets (e.g., Visual7W, VQA v2.0) and cross-question-type breakdowns would strengthen claims of generality and robustness.

Quality
- Methodologically sound and well-motivated; the attention computations reduce to linear algebra and pointwise nonlinearities. The decision module uses established sketching techniques.
- Empirical support shows advantages over strong baselines, but the reporting would benefit from complete tables (test-dev and test-std numbers by category), stronger ablations (removing ternary and/or answer-modality), and fairness controls (same CNN/LSTM backbones across baselines). Without full numbers in the text, it is difficult to assess margin sizes.

Clarity
- Generally well written with intuitive illustrations. A few notation issues and typos exist (e.g., repeated aV where aA is intended; shapes of convolution operations and the definition/learning of convolution weights w· need clarification).
- The probabilistic interpretation could be tightened: specify the underlying factor graph/energy, how potentials are normalized, and why a single iteration is sufficient.
- Computational complexity and memory costs for C2 and C3 (as functions of nv, nq, na, d) should be reported.

Originality
- The explicit high-order attention with ternary potentials and its mean-field combination is novel relative to widely used pairwise co-attention and fusion-only high-order pooling. Positioning against structured attention networks is appropriate; the proposed design emphasizes extensibility to arbitrarily many modalities.

Significance
- For multiple-choice VQA, the presented approach is likely to be adopted, especially the answer-conditioned attention and high-order potential design. Broader impact depends on demonstrating benefits without answer-conditioning (open-ended setting) and on more datasets.

Suggestions for improvement (prioritized)
Essential:
- Provide complete quantitative results (test-dev/test-std; per-category breakdowns) and ablations: (i) unary only; (ii) unary+pairwise; (iii) full with ternary; (iv) without answer-modality. Report gains attributable to each potential type.
- Clarify training/evaluation for the answer modality: when is na=18 vs 3000 used; how are candidate answers embedded and selected during training.
- Add a brief complexity/memory analysis for pairwise/ternary computations, with practical timings and peak memory.

Recommended:
- Evaluate on open-ended VQA and/or VQA v2.0 and Visual7W to demonstrate generality beyond multiple-choice and reduced language priors.
- Explore more than one mean-field iteration and report whether additional iterations help or saturate.
- Ensure fair comparisons by matching image/question encoders to baselines (e.g., ResNet-152, same LSTM settings).
- Improve clarity/notation and fix minor typos; detail the shapes of tensors and learned 1×1 convolutions used for marginalization.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, general, and practically effective high-order attention formulation.
- Demonstrated performance gains and compelling qualitative visualizations.
- Extensible framework beyond two modalities; code available.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited to multiple-choice; unclear benefits in open-ended VQA.
- Incomplete ablations and reporting hinder attribution and reproducibility of the claimed SOTA.
- Probabilistic justification remains informal; computational analysis is light.

Overall, the work presents a meaningful and timely contribution to multimodal attention with promising empirical results; strengthening the evaluation and clarifying the methodology would make the case more compelling.
