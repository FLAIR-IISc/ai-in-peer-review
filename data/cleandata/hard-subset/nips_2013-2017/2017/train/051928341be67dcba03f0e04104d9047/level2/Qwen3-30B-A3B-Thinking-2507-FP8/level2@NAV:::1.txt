

This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism that models correlations between multiple data modalities (e.g., visual, textual, and answer representations) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors argue that existing attention mechanisms typically focus on pairwise interactions between two modalities (e.g., image and question), while their approach generalizes to k-th order correlations across any number of modalities. The key claim is that this high-order attention mechanism achieves state-of-the-art performance on standard VQA benchmarks.

The paper demonstrates strong experimental support for their claims, with thorough comparisons against relevant baselines (Lu et al., Fukui et al.) on both VQA test-dev and test-std datasets. The authors provide ablation studies showing that their 3-modality model significantly outperforms 2-modality variants, and they carefully analyze the attention visualizations to demonstrate how their mechanism captures meaningful correlations between image regions, question words, and answer candidates. The technical details of their unary, pairwise, and ternary potential modules are well-explained, and the implementation using tensor sketch algorithms (MCT/MCB) is sound.

The paper's main strengths include its clear theoretical formulation of high-order attention as a probabilistic model with mean-field inference, the comprehensive experimental evaluation, and the insightful visualizations of attention patterns. The approach is well-motivated by the limitations of current attention mechanisms that treat multiple modalities as a single combined input rather than explicitly modeling their interactions.

However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability to even higher-order correlations. While the authors note the model's parameter efficiency compared to some baselines, they don't explicitly quantify the computational cost of the high-order attention modules. Additionally, the paper could strengthen its novelty claim by more directly contrasting with recent approaches like structured attention networks (Kim et al., 2017) that also model higher-order interactions.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks. The high-order attention framework is generalizable beyond VQA, and the state-of-the-art results on VQA demonstrate its practical value. The paper's clarity and completeness make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
