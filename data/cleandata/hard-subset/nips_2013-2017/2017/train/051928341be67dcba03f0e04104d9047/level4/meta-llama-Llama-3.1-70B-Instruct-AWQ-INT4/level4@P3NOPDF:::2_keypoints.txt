- Proposed method: generic multimodal attention combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image, question, and answer; outperforms prior work when ternary potentials are used.

- Strength: generic attention formulation potentially generalizable to other multimodal tasks.

- Strength: ablation study clarifies the contribution of ternary potentials.

- Strength: paper is clearly written.

- Weakness: Unclear what "attention over answers" means since answers are often single words; authors should clarify and show visualizations for answer attention.

- Weakness: Lacks visualizations of attention maps to support claims.

- Weakness: Table 1 suggests main gains come from ternary potentials; without them the model does not beat existing two-modality models (except HieCoAtt); needs more insight.

- Weakness: Should compare to existing models that use answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., ECCV16) given ternary potential importance.

- Weakness: No analysis of failure cases or model failure modes.

- Weakness: Several errors/typos and reporting inconsistencies (e.g., "mechanism" vs "mechanisms"; claimed validation evaluation vs Table 1 showing test-dev/test-std).

- Post-rebuttal: Authors' response on 2-modality performance lacks quantitative evidence; reviewer still recommends acceptance based on the generic framework and 3-modality SOTA gains.

- Post-rebuttal: Attention maps show higher correlation with human maps per rebuttal, likely better than HieCoAtt though unclear vs MCB.

- Question: Confusion over reported MCB vs MCT numbers—suspects the 68.4% and 69.4% figures are swapped and inconsistent with Table 1 (MCB = 68.6%).
