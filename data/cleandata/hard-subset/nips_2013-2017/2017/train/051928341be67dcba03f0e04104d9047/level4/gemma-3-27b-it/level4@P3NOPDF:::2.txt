## Review of NIPS 2017 Paper: [Paper Title]

This paper introduces a novel and generic attention mechanism for multimodal problems, specifically applied to the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core contribution lies in formulating attention using a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities: image, question, and answer. The authors demonstrate improved performance on the VQA dataset, particularly when leveraging these ternary potentials.

**Strengths:**

The proposed attention framework is a valuable contribution due to its generality. A modality-agnostic approach has the potential to generalize beyond VQA to other multimodal tasks, and this is a significant advantage. The ablation study, clearly delineating the impact of the ternary potentials, is particularly helpful in understanding the contribution of each component of the proposed model. The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

The inclusion of the answer modality within the attention mechanism warrants further clarification. Given that answers frequently consist of single words, or are treated as such despite being multi-word phrases, the utility of attending *to* the answer is unclear. The paper lacks any visualizations demonstrating attention weights assigned to the answer modality, hindering a complete understanding of its role. 

The results presented in Table 1 suggest that the performance improvement stems primarily from the introduction of ternary potentials. Without these potentials, the proposed model does not consistently outperform existing two-modality models (with the exception of HieCoAtt). It would be beneficial for the authors to provide a deeper analysis of this observation, explaining why the pairwise and unary potentials alone are insufficient to achieve state-of-the-art performance.

Given the importance of the ternary potential, a comparison against existing models that explicitly incorporate answer information—such as the baseline revisited in “Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines” (Jabri et al., ECCV16)—is necessary. This comparison would strengthen the claim of novelty and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in relation to directly comparable methods.

The paper currently lacks a discussion of failure cases. Analyzing the types of questions or images where the model performs poorly would provide valuable insights into its limitations and guide future research directions. Identifying these failure modes will contribute to a more complete understanding of the model's capabilities.

Finally, there are a few minor errors to address: 
* Line 38: "mechanism" should be "mechanisms."
* Line 237 states evaluation was performed on the validation set, however Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets. This discrepancy should be clarified.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**

While the authors’ response to concerns regarding the performance of the two-modality setup is not entirely convincing due to a lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance of this paper. The strength of the work lies in its generic attention framework for multiple modalities and the quantitative results demonstrating that three-modality attention surpasses the state-of-the-art. The rebuttal’s inclusion of quantitative evaluation comparing the proposed model’s attention maps to human attention maps is encouraging, indicating a stronger correlation than that observed with existing models like HieCoAtt. Although a comparison to models like MCB would be valuable, the observed improvement over HieCoAtt is significant.

I have a remaining clarification regarding the authors’ rebuttal response:

> Authors’ response: “MCB vs. MCT: MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.”

The reported performance figures for 3-MCT and 2-layer MCB appear to be swapped; 69.4% should correspond to the 3-MCT setup. Furthermore, the MCB performance reported in Table 1 is 68.6%, creating further confusion regarding the numbers cited in the rebuttal.



**Overall Score:** 7/10 (Accept with Minor Revisions) - The core idea is strong and well-executed, but addressing the points regarding answer modality attention, comparative analysis, and failure case discussion would significantly strengthen the paper.
