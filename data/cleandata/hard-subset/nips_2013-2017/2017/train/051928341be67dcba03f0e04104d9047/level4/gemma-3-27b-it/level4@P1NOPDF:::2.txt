The paper presents a novel method for modeling attention over images and questions in the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The key innovation is a generic formulation of attention for multiple modalities – specifically, a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials applied to the three modalities involved in VQA (image, question, and answer). The proposed approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and demonstrates superior performance to existing models when utilizing ternary potentials.

**Strengths:**

1. A generic formulation of attention applicable to multimodal problems, rather than being specific to a single task, is a valuable contribution, particularly if its generalizability can be demonstrated across multiple tasks.
2. The ablation study, which details performance without the ternary potential, is helpful in understanding the contribution of this component.
3. The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The approach incorporates attention over three modalities – image, question, and answer. However, the purpose of attention *over* answers is unclear, as most answers consist of single words, and even multi-word answers are treated as single tokens. The paper lacks visualizations demonstrating attention focused on the answer, and I request that the authors clarify this aspect.
2. The results in Table 1 suggest that the primary performance improvement stems from the ternary potential. Without it, the proposed model does not outperform existing models in the two-modality setup (with the exception of HieCoAtt). I would appreciate further discussion from the authors on this point.
3. Given that the ternary potential appears to be the main driver of performance improvement, I recommend comparing the proposed model with existing models that also utilize answers as inputs, such as “Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines” (Jabri et al., ECCV16).
4. The paper lacks a discussion of the proposed model’s failure cases. Analyzing the types of errors it makes would provide valuable insights for guiding future research.
5. Minor errors/typos:
    a. L38: “mechanism” should be “mechanisms”
    b. L237 states that the evaluation is performed on the validation set, however Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**

While the authors’ response to my concern regarding the model not outperforming existing models in the two-modality setup is not entirely convincing due to a lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance. This recommendation is based on the paper’s introduction of a generic attention framework for multiple modalities and the quantitative results demonstrating that the three-modality attention approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The quantitative evaluation of the proposed model’s attention maps against human attention maps (provided in the rebuttal) also appears promising, suggesting that the generated attention maps are more strongly correlated with human attention than those produced by existing models. Although the correlation value for state-of-the-art models such as MCB is unknown, the results appear significantly better than those for HieCoAtt. 

I have a question regarding one of the authors’ responses:

> Authors’ response: “MCB vs. MCT”: MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA, the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.

Are the numbers reversed here? Should 69.4% be the performance of 3-MCT? Also, the overall MCB performance reported in Table 1 is 68.6%. It’s unclear which number the authors are referencing when they report 68.4%.
