Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning, instantiated on visual question answering (VQA). The model defines attention as a probabilistic combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary “potentials” over image regions, question words, and candidate answers. Unary terms score each modality independently; pairwise terms model cross-modal correlations (e.g., image–question); and a ternary term captures joint dependencies among all three modalities (image–question–answer). The attentions are obtained via a learned linear combination of marginalized potentials followed by a softmax, which the authors interpret as a single mean-field iteration. The attended modality vectors are then combined for decision making using tensor-sketch–based pooling: either Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) pooling for pairwise interactions or its trilinear extension (MCT) for three modalities, with a reported preference for a two-layer MCB cascade in practice. On VQA, the method achieves state-of-the-art multiple-choice performance with fewer parameters than some prior systems.

The work builds directly on attention-based VQA models such as hierarchical co-attention [15], compact bilinear pooling [7], and related bilinear/low-rank techniques, while extending the attention mechanism to higher-order correlations across modalities. Compared with [15], which alternates or parallels attention across two modalities, this paper explicitly introduces k-th order potentials and a mean-field view. Compared with [7], which uses bilinear pooling for late fusion, this paper attempts to learn higher-order attentional structure prior to decision fusion.

Quality:
The technical components are mostly sound: the construction of unary/pairwise/ternary potentials via learned embeddings and correlation operators is clear; the use of tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) is appropriate to mitigate combinatorial blow-up; and the empirical evaluation is solid, with both quantitative results and attention visualizations. However, the probabilistic framing feels more motivational than formal: the derivation of Eq. (1) as mean-field is not fully specified (e.g., underlying energy, independence assumptions, or convergence), and the learned mixing coefficients for combining potentials are not reported or analyzed. Some ablations are discussed (e.g., MCT vs stacked MCB), but key comparisons are underdeveloped, such as why the 2-modality Unary+Pairwise variant underperforms [7] despite architectural similarity. Reporting these analyses would strengthen the paper’s claims.

Clarity:
While the paper is generally readable, the presentation is uneven. Several sections are dense, and some figures do not optimally support the narrative. Figure 2 sits at an awkward level of abstraction—neither detailed enough to convey the exact data flow nor abstract enough to highlight the core idea (unary/pairwise/ternary potential construction and combination). Figure 3 is introduced but apparently not referenced later, and ternary attention visualizations are scarce. Important implementation details (e.g., learned coefficients in Eq. (1), rationale for choosing sequential MCB over MCT) are mentioned but not substantiated with results or discussion.

Originality:
The main novelty lies in framing attention as a set of high-order potentials over multiple modalities and combining them via a learned mean-field step. Conceptually, this extends prior co-attention/bilinear approaches to higher order. That said, much of the mechanism (correlation-based pairwise/ternary terms, tensor sketch pooling) builds on known ideas, and the contribution is primarily a careful integration and extension rather than a fundamentally new attention principle. Still, the explicit high-order generalization and the potential to handle more than two modalities are useful.

Significance:
The model achieves competitive, in some cases state-of-the-art, performance on VQA with fewer parameters than some baselines. The qualitative visualizations suggest the higher-order attentions are meaningful. Given the prevalence of multimodal tasks, the approach could be adopted beyond VQA. The empirical gains appear modest but consistent, and the design is practical.

Strengths:
- Clear and general formulation of attention as high-order potentials across modalities.
- Strong empirical results on VQA with thorough qualitative visualizations.
- Sensible use of tensor sketching (MCB/MCT) to enable high-order interactions without prohibitive cost.
- Ablations on decision fusion (MCT vs stacked MCB) and comparisons to prior work.

Weaknesses:
- The probabilistic interpretation (mean-field inference) is not formalized; the connection reads as motivation rather than a principled derivation.
- Limited novelty relative to [7] and [15]; the method largely scales known ideas to higher order.
- Missing analyses: learned combination coefficients in Eq. (1), and a deeper discussion of why the 2-modality variant underperforms MCB [7].
- Presentation issues: figure clarity (Fig. 2), lack of explicit references to Fig. 3, and few ternary attention maps.

Questions for the authors:
- What are the learned values (and variances across runs) of the coefficients in Eq. (1)? Do different modalities emphasize different orders (e.g., image relying more on pairwise with question, as suggested)?
- Why does the 2-modality Unary+Pairwise model underperform MCB [7]? Is this due to the lower embedding dimension (512 vs 2048), differences in training, or the choice of decision module?
- You note that stacked MCB outperforms MCT. Please include numerical comparisons and discuss when and why stacked MCB is preferable.
- Can you show ternary attention maps (marginals) to illustrate how answer candidates influence image/question attention?
- Please clarify the probabilistic model (energy, factorization assumptions) if you intend the mean-field interpretation to be more than heuristic.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Tighten the exposition, especially around Sec. 3, and simplify Fig. 2 to foreground the unary/pairwise/ternary potential pipeline and the learned combination in Eq. (1).
- Reference and use Fig. 3 more explicitly; add ternary attention visualizations.
- Report the learned combination weights and provide ablations isolating the contribution of unary, pairwise, and ternary terms.
- Expand the discussion comparing MCT vs stacked MCB with concrete results and insights.

Overall assessment:
A solid, well-executed extension of co-attention to higher-order multimodal interactions with strong results on VQA. The paper’s impact would increase with a clearer, more rigorous probabilistic treatment and fuller analyses of the design choices. I lean toward acceptance contingent on improved clarity and additional analysis as outlined above.
