This paper presents a novel method for Visual Question Answering (VQA) that models attention across the image and question modalities. The primary contribution is a generic formulation for multimodal attention, structured as a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. For the VQA task, this framework is applied to three modalities: the image, the question, and the answer. When evaluated on the VQA dataset, the proposed approach, particularly with the inclusion of ternary potentials, is shown to outperform existing models.

### Strengths

1.  The proposal of a generic, task-agnostic framework for multimodal attention is a significant strength. Such a formulation has the potential for broad applicability if it can be generalized to other tasks beyond VQA.
2.  The paper includes a valuable ablation study that effectively isolates the performance contribution of the ternary potential, providing clear insight into its importance for the model's success.
3.  The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the methodology and contributions easy to understand.

### Weaknesses

1.  The concept of "attention over answers" is not sufficiently explained. Since most answers in the VQA dataset are single words (or are treated as such), the mechanism and utility of applying attention over them are unclear. The paper lacks visualizations for answer attention, which would be helpful for clarification.
2.  The empirical results in Table 1 indicate that the model's performance improvement is primarily driven by the ternary potential. In the 2-modality configuration (without the answer modality), the model does not outperform most contemporary methods, with the exception of HieCoAtt. This raises questions about the competitiveness of the unary and pairwise components of the model relative to prior work.
3.  Given the demonstrated importance of the ternary potential, the experimental comparison feels incomplete. The paper would be strengthened by a comparison to other models that also use answers as an input during training, such as the work of Jabri et al. (ECCV 2016, "Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines").
4.  The paper lacks a qualitative analysis of the model's failure cases. An investigation into the types of questions or images where the model fails would provide valuable insights for the community and guide future research.
5.  **Minor Issues:**
    *   L38: "mechanism" should be plural ("mechanisms").
    *   L237: The text states that evaluation is on the validation set, but Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets. This should be clarified.

### Post-Rebuttal Assessment and Recommendation

I have read the authors' rebuttal. While I appreciate the new results provided, the response to my concern about the model's performance in the 2-modality setting was not fully convincing, as it lacked quantitative evidence to substantiate the claims.

Despite this remaining concern, I am recommending acceptance. The primary reasons for this recommendation are the novelty of the generic attention framework for multiple modalities and the strong quantitative results of the 3-modality attention model, which outperforms the state-of-the-art.

Furthermore, the new results provided in the rebuttal, showing a quantitative evaluation of the model's attention maps against human attention maps, are a positive addition. The reported correlation suggests the model's attention is more aligned with human gaze than at least one prior method (HieCoAtt). While a comparison to other SOTA models like MCB would be more informative, the result is still a valuable indicator of the model's interpretability.

I have one final question for the authors for clarification. In your response regarding the "MCB vs. MCT" comparison, you stated: *"MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%."* Could you please confirm if these numbers are swapped? It seems the higher score (69.4%) should correspond to the proposed 3-MCT model. Additionally, the reported MCB performance of 68.4% does not appear to match the overall MCB performance of 68.6% listed in Table 1.
