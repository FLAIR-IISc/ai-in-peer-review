The paper proposes a novel method for modeling attention on images and questions for the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The main contribution lies in a generic formulation of attention for multiple modalities, combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials for three modalities (image, question, and answer) in VQA. The proposed approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and outperforms existing models when ternary potentials are utilized.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the generic formulation of attention for multimodal problems is useful, especially if it can be shown to generalize to more than one task. Secondly, the ablation study demonstrating the performance without ternary potentials is helpful in understanding the contribution of ternary potentials. Lastly, the paper is clearly written.

However, there are some weaknesses that need to be addressed. Firstly, the approach mentions attention over three modalities – image, question, and answer. However, it is unclear what attention over answers means, as most answers are single words, and even if they are multi-word, they are treated as single words. The paper does not provide any visualizations for attention over answers, and the authors should clarify this. Additionally, the authors should provide visualizations to support their claims.

Secondly, the results in Table 1 suggest that the main improvement in the proposed model comes from the ternary potential. Without the ternary potential, the proposed model does not outperform existing models for the two-modalities setup (except HieCoAtt). The authors should provide more insights into this.

Thirdly, since ternary potential seems to be the main factor in the performance improvement of the proposed model, the authors should compare the proposed model with existing models where answers are also used as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16).

Fourthly, the paper lacks any discussion on failure cases of the proposed model. It would be insightful to look into the failure modes so that future research can be guided accordingly.

Lastly, there are a few errors and typos that need to be corrected. For instance, "mechanism" should be replaced with "mechanisms" on line 38. Additionally, the paper mentions that the evaluation is on the validation set, but Table 1 reports numbers on the test-dev and test-std sets.

In the post-rebuttal comments, the authors' response to the concern of "Proposed model not outperforming existing models for 2 modalities" is not satisfactory due to the lack of quantitative evidence. However, I recommend acceptance because of the generic attention framework for multiple modalities proposed in the paper and the quantitative results of 3-modality attention outperforming the state-of-the-art (SOTA). The quantitative evaluation of the proposed model's attention maps against human attention maps (reported in the rebuttal) also looks good and suggests that the attention maps are more correlated with human maps than existing models. Although we do not know what this correlation value is for SOTA models such as MCB, I think it is still significantly better than that for HieCoAtt.

I have a question about one of the responses from the authors. In the response to "MCB vs. MCT," the authors mention that MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically, for VQA, the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev, where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. However, I believe the numbers are swapped, and 69.4% should be for 3-MCT. Additionally, the MCB overall performance in Table 1 is 68.6%, so I am unsure which number the authors are referring to when they report 68.4%.
