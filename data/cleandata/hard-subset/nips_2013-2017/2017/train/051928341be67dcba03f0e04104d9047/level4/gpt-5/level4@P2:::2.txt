Paper summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a generic, multimodal attention framework that models unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions across modalities. In the VQA setting, the modalities are image (V), question (Q), and answer candidates (A). The authors construct attention potentials at three orders—unary terms for each modality, pairwise terms between modality pairs (e.g., V–Q), and a third-order term coupling all three. They treat attention as a probabilistic model and use a single mean-field step to derive marginal attentions over each modality. For decision making, they fuse attended features using Multimodal Compact Bilinear (MCB) or Multimodal Compact Trilinear (MCT) pooling to approximate outer-product interactions efficiently. On the VQA dataset, the 3-modality (ternary) model achieves state-of-the-art results. The paper situates itself relative to co-attention (e.g., Lu et al.’s HieCoAtt), stacked/dynamic attention methods (e.g., Yang et al., Xiong et al.), and bilinear pooling approaches (Fukui et al.’s MCB), highlighting that prior work typically models at most two modalities or hand-crafts attention, whereas this work provides a general high-order formulation extensible to more modalities.

Strengths:
- A principled, general attention formulation for multimodal data (unary/pairwise/ternary) that is not tied to a specific task and is conceptually extensible to k modalities.
- Clear exposition of the probabilistic view (potentials and mean-field marginals) and the integration with compact bilinear/trilinear pooling for efficient high-order fusion.
- Solid empirical gains on VQA when ternary potentials are used; the ablation without ternary terms is informative.
- Qualitative attention visualizations help interpret the model’s behavior, and the framework provides a plausible path to improved interpretability.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Semantics of answer attention: It is unclear what “attention over answers” signifies when most answers are single words (and multi-word answers are treated as a single token). The paper does not provide sufficient visualization or discussion of A-attention. Please clarify how attention over answers is computed and used, and include visualizations illustrating its effect.
- Contribution of ternary potential: From Table 1, most gains appear to come from the ternary term; without it, the 2-modality variant does not exceed several strong baselines (except HieCoAtt). Please quantify where the ternary term helps (question types, answer types), and explain why the 2-modality setup trails SOTA methods despite similar capacity.
- Comparison to models that also use answers as inputs: Since the main lift stems from modeling A, please compare against methods that incorporate answer candidates (e.g., Jabri et al., “Revisiting VQA Baselines”) under consistent settings, or justify differences.
- Failure analysis: The paper lacks a substantive discussion of failure modes. An analysis stratified by question types and qualitative examples of typical errors would be valuable for future work.
- Minor issues:
  a) L38: “mechanism” → “mechanisms.”
  b) L237 references validation, but Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std. Please reconcile the splits used for all reported numbers.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically sound, with a clear probabilistic formulation and well-defined inference and fusion steps. Training details are provided. However, the case for the 2-modality setting is weaker; additional quantitative evidence would strengthen the claims.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Some ambiguity remains around answer attention and dataset splits.
- Originality: The high-order attention via potentials and mean-field, combined with compact multilinear pooling, is a novel and useful synthesis that extends beyond typical pairwise co-attention models.
- Significance: The ternary model achieves SOTA results on VQA and offers a general template for multimodal attention. If shown to transfer to other tasks/modalities, the impact could be substantial.

Pros for acceptance:
- General, extensible attention framework with a principled formulation.
- Strong empirical performance for 3-modality attention.
- Interpretable attention visualizations and a clear narrative linking high-order correlations to gains.
- Potentially broad applicability beyond VQA.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited evidence that the framework improves over strong baselines in the 2-modality regime.
- Insufficient clarity and visualization for answer attention.
- No detailed failure analysis.
- Incomplete comparisons to methods that also condition on answers.

Post-rebuttal comments:
I still find the justification for the underperformance of the 2-modality variant unconvincing due to limited quantitative support, but I lean toward acceptance given the generality of the proposed high-order attention framework and the strong 3-modality results. The reported correlation with human attention maps is encouraging; although comparison to SOTA models like MCB is missing, improvements over HieCoAtt suggest the approach produces more human-aligned attentions.

Clarification needed on author rebuttal (“MCB vs. MCT”):
- The rebuttal states: “For VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%.” Are these numbers swapped? One would expect the 3-MCT to be at least competitive. Additionally, Table 1 lists MCB overall performance as 68.6%. Please specify which configuration each number corresponds to (modalities used, pooling type, backbone features, and evaluation split) to resolve the discrepancies.
