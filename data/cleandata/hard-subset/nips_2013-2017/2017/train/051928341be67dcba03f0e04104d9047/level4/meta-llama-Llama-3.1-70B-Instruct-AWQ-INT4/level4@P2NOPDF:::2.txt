The paper proposes a novel method for modeling attention on images and questions for Visual Question Answering (VQA) tasks. The primary contribution lies in introducing a generic formulation of attention for multiple modalities, combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials for three modalities (image, question, and answer) in VQA. This approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and achieves superior performance to existing models when ternary potentials are employed.

The paper's strengths include:

1. The introduction of a generic attention formulation for multimodal problems, which is particularly valuable if it can be demonstrated to generalize across multiple tasks.
2. The ablation study, which provides insight into the performance improvement offered by the ternary potential.
3. The clear and well-structured writing style.

However, there are several weaknesses and areas for improvement:

1. The concept of attention over answers is not clearly explained, particularly given that most answers are single words or treated as single words even when they are multi-word. The authors should provide clarification and visualizations to support this aspect of their approach.
2. The results in Table 1 suggest that the primary performance improvement in the proposed model stems from the ternary potential. Without this potential, the model does not outperform existing models in the two-modalities setup (except for HieCoAtt). The authors should provide further insight into this observation.
3. Given the significance of the ternary potential in the proposed model's performance, it would be beneficial to compare this model with existing approaches that also utilize answers as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16).
4. The paper lacks a discussion on failure cases of the proposed model. Analyzing failure modes would provide valuable insights for future research.
5. Minor errors and typos were identified, including "mechanism" instead of "mechanisms" at line 38, and the discrepancy between the evaluation set mentioned in line 237 (validation set) and the reported results in Table 1 (test-dev and test-std sets).

Post-rebuttal comments:

Although the authors' response to the concern regarding the proposed model's performance in the two-modalities setup is not entirely satisfactory due to the lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance based on the paper's contributions, including the generic attention framework for multiple modalities and the quantitative results demonstrating the superiority of the three-modality attention approach. The evaluation of the proposed model's attention maps against human attention maps, as reported in the rebuttal, also shows promise, indicating a higher correlation with human maps compared to existing models. However, it is essential to note that the correlation value for state-of-the-art (SOTA) models like MCB is unknown.

Regarding one of the authors' responses, there appears to be a discrepancy in the reported numbers. The authors state that the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev, while the 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. However, this seems to be a mistake, as the numbers should be swapped. Furthermore, the overall performance of MCB in Table 1 is 68.6%, which does not match the number mentioned in the authors' response. Clarification on this point would be appreciated.
