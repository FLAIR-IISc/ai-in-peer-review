- Introduces a high-order attention mechanism modeling k-th order correlations (unary, pairwise, ternary) across image, question, and answer via probabilistic potentials.  
- Generalizes beyond pairwise methods (e.g., MCB), trivially extending to k modalities rather than task-specific architectures.  
- Reports state-of-the-art VQA performance (69.4% on test-dev) using a 3-modality framework.  
- Ablation studies show ternary potentials are the primary driver of performance gain (69.4% vs. 68.6% without them).  
- Qualitative visualizations demonstrate attention alignment with human reasoning, especially on complex questions.  
- Paper is well-structured and clear; figures and Section 3 balance rigor and accessibility.  
- Ambiguity in the answer modality: answers are embedded as single vectors, making "answer attention" potentially redundant and unvisualized.  
- The 3-modality advantage depends heavily on ternary terms, and the 2-modality baseline underperforms prior work, calling the framework’s standalone benefit into question.  
- Authors should compare to related work that also uses answers as input (e.g., Jabri et al. ECCV 2016).  
- Missing failure analysis and discussion of why the model makes certain errors (e.g., ambiguous attention cases).  
- Inconsistent reporting between rebuttal and Table 1 regarding MCB/MCT numbers undermines result confidence and needs correction.  
- Overall recommendation: technically sound and significant; recommend acceptance contingent on clarifying answer-attention rationale and resolving numerical inconsistencies.  
- Pros: novel, principled multimodal attention; strong empirical results and clear exposition.  
- Cons: insufficient justification/visualization for answer attention, weaker 2-modality baseline, missing failure analysis, and numerical discrepancies.
