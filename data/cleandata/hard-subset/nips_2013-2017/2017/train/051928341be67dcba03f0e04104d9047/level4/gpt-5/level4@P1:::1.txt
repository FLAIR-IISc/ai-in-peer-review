Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, instantiated here for VQA. The model learns unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over the image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) modalities; combines these marginalized potentials linearly (Eq. 1) to produce attention distributions; and uses compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) for decision making. The authors argue that high-order correlations among modalities yield better attention and improved performance, and they report strong results on the standard VQA dataset. Relative to prior work, this approach extends co-attention and bilinear pooling methods [7, 15] by explicitly modeling attention at multiple orders (unary/pairwise/ternary) and framing attention as a (lightweight) probabilistic model.

General impression:
Overall, the method is interesting and the results are quite good. The attention visualizations help support the claim that gains stem from improved attention rather than just increased capacity. The writing, however, is sometimes confusing; with more time, I expect the presentation could be clarified.

Strengths:
- Interesting model with strong empirical results.
- Thorough qualitative and quantitative experiments.
- The shift from unary/pairwise attentions to the final attention is compelling. I would have liked to see marginalized ternary attention maps as well; if these were included and I missed them, please point me to the relevant section or supplement.

Weaknesses:
- The writing/presentation is jumbled at times, which reduces clarity. Figures could do more to organize the narrative (see below).
- Novelty feels limited. While the architecture is more complex than and outperforms the MCB VQA model [7], much of the added complexity echoes the intuition of [7] at lower (unary) and higher (ternary) orders. This is not necessarily a flaw, but the contributions would benefit from clearer articulation and differentiation from [7, 15].
- The probabilistic framing is not made formally enough to be more than motivational. That is acceptable, but then the language should reflect this; alternatively, tighten the formal connection (e.g., derivation details for the mean-field step, assumptions, and how the linear combination relates to inference).
- Figure 2 sits at an awkward level of abstraction—neither detailed enough to understand the computation nor abstract enough to highlight the core idea. I suggest simplifying it to foreground the unary/pairwise/ternary potential generation and how these feed into the attention and decision modules.
- Figure 3 appears never to be referenced (unless I missed it).

Quality:
- The method seems technically sound, and the empirical evaluation is careful. The attention visualizations are informative.
- Some design choices (e.g., the linear combination coefficients in Eq. 1, the decision to use sequential MCB vs. single MCT) would benefit from additional analysis or ablations.

Clarity:
- The manuscript would be improved by tightening the exposition around the probabilistic interpretation, streamlining notation around potentials, and revising figures to better align with the text.
- A brief summary table/diagram of modules (unary/pairwise/ternary) and their inputs/outputs could help.

Originality:
- The core idea—explicit high-order attention across modalities—is a natural extension of co-attention/bilinear pooling. The contribution lies more in the systematic organization and empirical validation than in fundamentally new primitives.

Significance:
- The reported results are strong. The approach is likely to be useful to practitioners and extendable to other multimodal tasks.

Questions and requests for clarification:
- What values were learned for the linear coefficients that combine the marginalized potentials in Eq. (1)? It would be insightful to know whether different modalities relied more heavily on different potential orders.
- The 2-modality Unary+Pairwise model underperforms MCB [7] despite a seemingly similar architecture. There is little discussion of this. Do you have intuition for this result? Is it related to the switch to MCB/MCT in the decision head, embedding dimensionality, or other training choices?
- The discussion comparing sequential MCB versus a single MCT layer in the decision head is interesting, but no results are shown. Could you share the observed performance differences and any qualitative differences in the resulting attentions?
- If available, please include marginalized ternary attention maps. These would help illustrate what the third-order potentials contribute beyond pairwise interactions.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong empirical results with informative visualizations.
- Clear practical relevance for VQA and likely broader applicability to multimodal tasks.
- Systematic exploration of attention at multiple orders.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited conceptual novelty relative to existing co-attention and bilinear pooling work.
- Clarity issues in exposition and figures.
- Probabilistic framing is underdeveloped; either formalize it or present it explicitly as motivation.
