1. Paper proposes a generic multimodal attention framework integrating unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer, with ternary potentials significantly improving VQA performance.  
2. Strength: Framework is task-agnostic and potentially generalizes beyond VQA for multimodal reasoning.  
3. Strength: Thorough ablation study isolates the impact of ternary potentials.  
4. Strength: Paper is well-structured and clearly written.  
5. Weakness: "Attention over answers" is ambiguous—operational meaning and lack of answer-attention visualizations make the mechanism unclear.  
6. Weakness: Model gains rely heavily on ternary potentials; two-modality variant performs worse than most baselines and needs explanation.  
7. Weakness: Missing comparisons to relevant baselines that incorporate answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al.), limiting evaluation completeness.  
8. Weakness: No failure analysis or discussion of common error patterns is provided.  
9. Weakness: Minor errors—typo ("mechanism"→"mechanisms") and discrepancy between claimed validation evaluation and Table 1 reporting test-dev/test-std.  
10. Post-rebuttal: Authors’ explanation for two-modality gap lacks quantitative support (no metrics for two-modality variant).  
11. Post-rebuttal: Reported strong correlation with human attention is promising but lacks comparison values for SOTA models like MCB.  
12. Post-rebuttal: Critical inconsistency in reported MCB vs. MCT accuracies (68.4% vs. 68.6% swapped); authors must clarify which value corresponds to which model.  
13. Recommendation: Accept, as the ternary multimodal attention is a meaningful advance, provided the authors fix discrepancies and clarify answer-attention, two-modality behavior, baseline comparisons, and failure modes.
