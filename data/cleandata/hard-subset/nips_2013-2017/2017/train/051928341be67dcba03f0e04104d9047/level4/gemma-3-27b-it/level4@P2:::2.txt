The paper presents a novel method to model attention for Visual Question Answering (VQA), proposing a generic formulation of attention for multiple modalities using a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer representations. The approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset when leveraging ternary potentials.

**Strengths:**

1.  The proposed generic formulation of attention for multimodal problems is a valuable contribution, offering potential for broader applicability beyond VQA. Its ability to handle an arbitrary number of modalities is a significant advantage.
2.  The ablation study demonstrating the impact of the ternary potentials provides insight into the contribution of each component of the proposed model.
3.  The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  The inclusion of “attention over answers” is unclear. Given that answers often consist of single words or short phrases, the contribution of attending to the answer itself needs further justification. The lack of visualization for attention weights over the answer modality weakens this aspect of the method.
2.  The performance improvement appears heavily reliant on the ternary potentials. Without them, the model does not consistently outperform existing two-modality approaches (with the exception of HieCoAtt). A more in-depth analysis of why the ternary potentials are so crucial is needed.
3.  The comparison to existing work could be strengthened. Specifically, it would be beneficial to compare against models like Jabri et al. (ECCV 2016), which explicitly incorporate answers as inputs, to better understand the advantage of the proposed ternary attention mechanism.
4.  The paper lacks a discussion of failure cases. Analyzing instances where the model performs poorly would provide valuable insights and guide future research directions. This would demonstrate a more thorough understanding of the method’s limitations.
5.  There are minor errors and inconsistencies:
    a. Line 38: "mechanism" should be "mechanisms".
    b. Line 237 states evaluation on the validation set, while Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets. This discrepancy needs clarification.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper proposes a novel and potentially impactful approach to multimodal attention. The concept of high-order potentials is well-motivated and the results demonstrate the benefit of considering ternary interactions in VQA. While some aspects require further clarification and analysis, the potential for generalization and the state-of-the-art performance on VQA make this a strong submission.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The proposed attention mechanism is a novel and generic framework for handling multiple modalities.
*   The inclusion of ternary potentials leads to state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset.
*   The paper is well-written and the core ideas are clearly explained.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution of “attention over answers” is not well-justified or demonstrated.
*   Performance relies heavily on the ternary potentials, and the reasons for this are not fully explored.
*   A more comprehensive comparison to related work, particularly models utilizing answer information, is needed.
*   The lack of failure case analysis limits the understanding of the model’s limitations.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance, leaning towards strong acceptance. The generic attention framework and the strong performance with the ternary potentials outweigh the identified weaknesses. Addressing the clarification regarding the numbers in the rebuttal would further strengthen the recommendation. The proposed method has the potential to significantly influence research in multimodal learning.
