This paper introduces a new approach to modeling attention over image and text for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The key contribution is a general attention formulation across multiple modalities using a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over the three VQA modalities (image, question, answer). The method is evaluated on the VQA dataset and, when incorporating ternary potentials, achieves better performance than prior models.

Strengths:
1. A task-agnostic, multimodal attention framework is valuable, particularly if it can be demonstrated to extend beyond a single application.
2. The ablation comparing performance with and without the ternary potential helps quantify its impact.
3. The manuscript is clearly written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses:
1. The notion of “attention over answers” is underexplained. Most answers are single tokens, and multi-word answers are treated as a single unit; the paper also does not visualize answer attention. Please clarify what answer attention captures in this setting.
2. Table 1 suggests the primary gains come from the ternary potential. Without it, the proposed two-modality setup does not surpass several existing two-modality baselines (except HieCoAtt). Please elaborate on this behavior.
3. Since the ternary potential appears central to the improvement, please compare against models that also incorporate answers as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV’16).
4. The paper would benefit from a discussion of failure cases to illuminate limitations and guide future work.
5. Other issues/typos:
   a. L38: mechanism -> mechanisms
   b. L237 states the evaluation is on the validation set, but Table 1 reports results on test-dev and test-std.

Post-rebuttal comments:
While the rebuttal regarding “no improvement over existing two-modality models” lacks compelling quantitative support, I recommend acceptance based on the general multimodal attention framework and the strong three-modality results. The reported correlation between the model’s attention maps and human attention (from the rebuttal) is also encouraging and appears higher than HieCoAtt’s, though it remains unclear how it compares to state-of-the-art models like MCB.

A clarifying question about the authors’ response on “MCB vs. MCT”:
> Authors’ response — “MCB vs. MCT”: MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.
Are these numbers reversed? Should 69.4% correspond to 3-MCT? Also, Table 1 lists MCB overall at 68.6%, so it is unclear which result the reported 68.4% refers to.
