This paper introduces a new approach for modeling attention across images and questions within the Visual Question Answering (VQA) framework. The primary contribution is a generalizable attention formulation designed for multiple modalities, which combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials for the image, question, and answer modalities in VQA. The method is validated on the VQA dataset, where it demonstrates superior performance over existing models, particularly when employing the ternary potentials.

**Strengths:**
1. The proposal of a generic, task-agnostic attention formulation for multimodal problems is a significant contribution, especially if its applicability can be demonstrated on tasks beyond VQA.
2. The inclusion of an ablation study that evaluates performance without the ternary potential is valuable, as it clearly isolates and quantifies the impact of this component on the model's success.
3. The manuscript is well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper describes attention over three modalities: image, question, and answer. However, the concept of "attention over answers" is not well-motivated. Most answers in the dataset are single words, and even multi-word answers are typically treated as a single unit. The authors do not provide any visualizations for answer attention, making its role and function unclear. Clarification on this point is needed.
2. The results presented in Table 1 indicate that the performance improvement of the proposed model is primarily driven by the ternary potential. In the two-modality configuration (without the ternary potential), the model does not consistently outperform existing baselines (with the exception of HieCoAtt). The authors should provide more insight into this observation.
3. Given that the ternary potential appears to be the main source of the model's performance gains, the experimental comparison should be expanded. Specifically, the proposed model should be benchmarked against existing methods that also utilize answer information as an input, such as the work by Jabri et al. (ECCV 2016).
4. The paper lacks a discussion of the model's failure cases. An analysis of the scenarios where the model fails would provide valuable insights and help guide future research in this area.
5. Minor Issues/Typos:
    a. L38: "mechanism" should be plural ("mechanisms").
    b. There is a discrepancy between the text and the tables. L237 states that the evaluation was performed on the validation set, whereas Table 1 reports results for the test-dev and test-std splits.

**Post-rebuttal comments:**
While the authors' response regarding the two-modality model's performance is not fully convincing due to a lack of quantitative evidence, I am recommending acceptance. This recommendation is based on the novelty of the generic multi-modal attention framework and the strong empirical results showing that the three-modality attention model outperforms the state-of-the-art.

The new quantitative analysis comparing the model's attention maps to human attention maps, provided in the rebuttal, strengthens the paper. These results suggest a higher correlation with human gaze patterns than existing models like HieCoAtt. While it remains unknown how this correlation compares to top-performing models such as MCB, the improvement over HieCoAtt is still significant.

I have a follow-up question regarding one of the authors' responses:
> Authors' response -- "MCB vs. MCT": MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.

Could the authors please clarify the performance numbers cited here? It seems possible that the values for 3-MCT (68.4%) and MCB (69.4%) have been swapped, as one would expect the proposed model to have the higher score. Additionally, the reported MCB performance of 68.4% in the rebuttal differs from the 68.6% value presented in Table 1 of the main paper.
