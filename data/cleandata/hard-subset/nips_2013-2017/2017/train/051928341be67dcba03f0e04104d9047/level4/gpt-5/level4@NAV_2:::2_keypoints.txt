- Introduces a general multimodal attention formulation (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) over image, question, and answer; ternary potentials improve VQA performance.
- Strength: Task-agnostic multimodal attention framework that could extend beyond VQA.
- Strength: Ablation study clearly quantifies the impact of the ternary potential.
- Strength: Manuscript is clearly written and easy to follow.
- Weakness: “Attention over answers” is underexplained—most answers are single tokens, multi-word answers treated as one unit, and no answer-attention visualizations; unclear what it captures.
- Weakness: Without ternary potential, the two-modality setup does not surpass several existing two-modality baselines (except HieCoAtt); needs explanation.
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to models that incorporate answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., ECCV’16 Revisiting VQA Baselines).
- Weakness: No discussion of failure cases to illuminate limitations and guide future work.
- Weakness: Minor issues/typos—L38 “mechanism”→“mechanisms”; L237 states evaluation on validation set but Table 1 reports test-dev and test-std.
- Post-rebuttal: Reviewer leans to accept based on the general multimodal framework and strong three-modality results despite weak rebuttal on two-modality comparisons.
- Post-rebuttal: Reported higher correlation with human attention than HieCoAtt is encouraging but unclear how it compares to SOTA like MCB.
- Clarifying question: The reported MCB vs MCT numbers are inconsistent (3-MCT 68.4% vs 2-layer MCB 69.4%, while Table 1 lists MCB 68.6%)—which numbers correspond to which model and split?
