

The paper introduces a novel method for modeling attention across image and question modalities in Visual Question Answering (VQA), proposing a generic framework that unifies unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities (image, question, answer). Evaluated on VQA, the approach achieves state-of-the-art results when ternary potentials are incorporated.

**Strengths:**  
1.  The generic multimodal attention formulation is valuable, particularly if it demonstrates broader applicability beyond VQA.  
2.  The ablation study effectively isolates the contribution of ternary potentials, clarifying their impact.  
3.  The paper is well-written and clearly structured.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1.  The concept of "attention over answers" lacks clarity. Since most answers are single words or short phrases treated as atomic units, it is unclear how attention operates over them. The paper provides no visualizations of answer attention, making this component difficult to interpret. The authors should clarify the mechanism and provide visual evidence.  
2.  Table 1 suggests ternary potentials drive the primary performance gain. Without them, the proposed model (in the two-modality setup) fails to outperform existing baselines (except HieCoAtt). The authors should explain why the two-modality variant underperforms and whether this indicates a fundamental limitation.  
3.  Given that ternary potentials are the key to the model’s success, the authors should compare against models explicitly incorporating answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., *Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines*, ECCV 2016), rather than solely against two-modality baselines.  
4.  The paper omits discussion of failure cases. Analyzing where the model struggles would provide valuable insights for future work.  
5.  Minor errors:  
    -   L38: "mechanism" → "mechanisms"  
    -   L237: Claims evaluation on validation set, but Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std results.  

**Post-rebuttal Comments:**  
While the authors’ response to Weakness 2 remains unsatisfying due to insufficient quantitative evidence, I maintain my recommendation for acceptance. The paper’s core contribution—a generic multimodal attention framework—holds significant value, and the 3-modality results demonstrably outperform SOTA. The rebuttal’s quantitative comparison of attention maps against human annotations is also compelling, showing stronger correlation than existing models (e.g., MCB vs. HieCoAtt).  

However, I have a question regarding the authors’ response to the MCB/MCT comparison:  
> *"MCB vs. MCT: MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. For VQA, 3-MCT yields 68.4% on test-dev, while 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. Other MCB extensions with >2 modalities performed worse."*  

This appears inverted: Table 1 reports 2-layer MCB at 68.6% (test-dev), not 69.4%. If 3-MCT achieved 68.4%, this would contradict Table 1’s 68.6% for MCB and the rebuttal’s claim that 3-MCT outperforms MCB. The authors should clarify the reported numbers and their relationship to Table 1.  

Overall, the paper’s framework is a meaningful contribution to multimodal attention modeling. The minor concerns about answer attention, the two-modality performance gap, and the MCB/MCT comparison do not undermine the core innovation, especially given the strong empirical results with ternary potentials. I recommend acceptance.
