

The paper introduces a novel high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA). The authors propose a framework that models attention through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture correlations across multiple data modalities (image, question, and answer). This approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the standard VQA dataset, with the ternary potential component proving particularly effective.

The paper makes a significant contribution by presenting a generalizable attention framework applicable to any number of data modalities, moving beyond task-specific attention mechanisms. This is particularly valuable for multimodal learning where multiple data sources interact in complex ways. The authors clearly demonstrate the value of high-order attention through careful ablation studies, showing that the ternary potential component provides the most substantial performance gains. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the technical contribution accessible to readers.

The main strengths of the paper include its general framework for multimodal attention, the thorough ablation study showing the incremental contribution of each attention component, and the clear presentation of results. The framework's ability to naturally extend to more than two modalities is a notable advantage over existing approaches that are limited to pairwise interactions.

However, several concerns remain. First, the paper mentions attention over answers as a data modality, but this is not clearly explained or visualized. Most answers are single words, and the paper doesn't provide visualizations of attention over answers, making it difficult to understand how this component functions. The authors should clarify what attention over answers means in practice and why it's beneficial.

Second, the results in Table 1 indicate that the main performance gain comes from the ternary potential. Without it, the model performs similarly to or worse than existing two-modality approaches (except HieCoAtt). The authors should provide more detailed analysis on why the ternary potential is so crucial and how it differs from existing approaches that incorporate multiple modalities.

Third, the paper should compare with approaches that also incorporate answers as inputs, such as Jabri et al.'s "Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines" (ECCV 2016). This would better position their work in the literature and address the concern about whether the attention over answers provides a meaningful contribution.

Fourth, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of failure cases, which would be valuable for understanding the limitations of the approach and guiding future research. A small analysis of cases where the model fails would provide important insights.

The paper contains minor issues: "mechanism" should be pluralized as "mechanisms" on line 38, and there's a discrepancy between the claim that evaluation was on the validation set (line 237) and the reported results on test-dev and test-std sets.

Post-rebuttal, while the authors' response to the 2-modality performance concern remains unsatisfactory without quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance due to the paper's significant contribution with its generalizable attention framework. The additional evidence showing improved correlation between the model's attention maps and human attention maps (compared to existing models) is compelling and suggests the attention mechanism captures meaningful relationships.

Regarding the MCB vs. MCT comparison, the authors' response states "MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%." This is confusing because Table 1 reports MCB as 68.6% on test-dev. If the numbers are correct, the authors should explain why the more expressive 2-layer MCB performs better than the theoretically more general 3-MCT approach, as this contradicts the paper's central claim about the importance of higher-order attention.

Despite these concerns, the paper's contribution to multimodal attention is substantial and well-supported by results. The framework is elegant, extends naturally to multiple modalities, and achieves state-of-the-art performance. The potential for this approach to inspire future work in multimodal learning makes it a valuable contribution to the field.
