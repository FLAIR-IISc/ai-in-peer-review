## Review for NIPS 2017 Submission

**Overall Impression:**

This paper presents an interesting and promising approach to visual question answering (VQA) that builds upon existing multi-hop attention mechanisms. The reported results are strong, and the attention visualizations provide compelling evidence that the performance gains stem from improved attention modeling, rather than simply from increased model capacity. While the core ideas are well-motivated, the presentation currently lacks clarity in several areas. With revisions addressing these points, I believe this work has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the VQA literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Performance:** The proposed model achieves competitive results on standard VQA benchmarks, demonstrating its effectiveness.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors provide thorough quantitative and qualitative evaluations, which strengthens the claims made in the paper. 
* **Novel Attention Mechanism:** The shift from unary and pairwise attentions to the final integrated attention is a conceptually appealing idea. The hierarchical refinement of attention offers a nuanced approach to modeling relationships between visual elements and the question. I would have appreciated seeing visualizations of the intermediate, marginalized trinary attention maps to better understand this refinement process – these were not readily apparent in the main text or supplement.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The probabilistic motivation, even if not fully formalized, provides a sensible framework for the proposed attention mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Presentation:** The writing and overall presentation feel somewhat disorganized at times, hindering a clear understanding of the model’s components and their interactions. Careful rewriting to streamline the explanation would be beneficial.
* **Incremental Novelty:** While the architecture demonstrably outperforms the MCB for VQA model [7], the core contribution feels somewhat incremental. Much of the added complexity appears to extend the intuition of [7] to higher (trinary) and lower (unary) orders. This is not necessarily a fatal flaw, but the authors should more explicitly articulate the novel contributions of their work, particularly highlighting any aspects beyond this generalization. 
* **Insufficiently Developed Probabilistic Connection:** The paper invokes a probabilistic connection to motivate the attention mechanism. However, this connection is not developed formally, leaving it largely as motivational justification. I recommend that the authors either strengthen this connection with a more rigorous derivation or adjust the language to clearly indicate its primarily motivational role.
* **Figure 2 – Level of Abstraction:** Figure 2 is problematic in its current form. It lacks sufficient detail to convey the inner workings of the network, yet it’s also too complex to provide a concise overview of the approach. Simplifying the figure to emphasize the generation of unary, pairwise, and trinary potentials would significantly improve its clarity.
* **Unreferenced Figure:** Figure 3 is included in the submission but is never referenced in the main text. Either integrate a discussion of this figure or remove it.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Marginalized Potential Coefficients:** What values were learned for the linear coefficients used to combine the marginalized potentials in equation (1)? Investigating whether different modalities benefit from different potential orders (e.g., higher-order potentials being more important for image features) could provide valuable insights.
* **Unary+Pairwise Underperformance:** The 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise model’s underperformance compared to MCB [7] is surprising given the architectural similarities. A more detailed discussion of this result would be helpful. Is this related to the transition to the MCB/MCT decision computation modules? Exploring this further could reveal critical design considerations.
* **Sequential MCB vs. Single MCT:** The discussion of using sequential MCB versus a single MCT layer for the decision head is intriguing. However, the paper does not present any results comparing these approaches. Could the authors share any observations from their experiments with these different decision heads?
* **Ablation Studies:** While the paper demonstrates strong overall performance, additional ablation studies could strengthen the analysis. For example, exploring the impact of removing the trinary attention component or varying the order of attention refinement could provide further insight into the model’s behavior.



I believe that addressing these concerns will significantly improve the clarity and impact of this paper.
