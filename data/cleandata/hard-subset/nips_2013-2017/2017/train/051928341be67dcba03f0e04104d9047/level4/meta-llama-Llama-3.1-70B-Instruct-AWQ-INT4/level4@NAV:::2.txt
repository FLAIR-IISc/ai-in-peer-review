The authors propose a novel attention modeling approach for Visual Question Answering (VQA) by introducing a generic formulation that incorporates unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities: image, question, and answer. The evaluation on the VQA dataset demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly when ternary potentials are employed, leading to improved performance over existing models.

Strengths:
1. The paper's main contribution lies in its generic attention formulation, which can be applied to various multimodal problems, showcasing its potential for generalizability across tasks.
2. The ablation study provides valuable insights into the impact of ternary potentials on the model's performance, helping to understand their significance.
3. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the content easily accessible.

Weaknesses:
1. The concept of attention over answers requires clarification, as most answers consist of single words, and the paper lacks visualizations to support this aspect. Further explanation from the authors would be beneficial.
2. The results in Table 1 suggest that the ternary potential is the primary driver of the proposed model's performance improvement. However, without ternary potential, the model does not outperform existing models in the 2-modalities setup (except for HieCoAtt). The authors should provide more insight into this observation.
3. Given the significance of ternary potential in the proposed model's performance, a comparison with existing models that utilize answers as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16), would be valuable.
4. The paper would benefit from a discussion on failure cases, as analyzing the model's limitations can inform future research directions.
5. Minor errors and typos were found:
a. L38: "mechanism" should be "mechanisms."
b. The evaluation set mentioned in L237 (validation set) does not match the reported results in Table 1 (test-dev and test-std sets).

Post-rebuttal comments:
Although the authors' response to the concern regarding the proposed model's performance in the 2-modalities setup is not entirely convincing due to the lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance based on the paper's contribution of a generic attention framework for multiple modalities and the quantitative results demonstrating the superiority of 3-modality attention. The authors' rebuttal also presents a promising evaluation of the proposed model's attention maps against human attention maps. However, a comparison with state-of-the-art models like MCB is necessary to fully appreciate the results.

Regarding the authors' response to the "MCB vs. MCT" comment, it appears that the numbers might be swapped. Specifically, the 3-MCT setup should yield 69.4% on test-dev, not 68.4%. Additionally, the reported MCB performance in Table 1 is 68.6%, which does not match the authors' mentioned value of 68.4%. Clarification on this point is needed.
