This paper introduces a novel approach to modeling attention in Visual Question Answering (VQA) by presenting a generalized attention formulation applicable to multiple modalities. The core innovation lies in the use of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across the image, question, and answer modalities. Evaluation on the VQA dataset demonstrates that incorporating ternary potentials leads to performance gains over existing VQA models.

The strengths of this work include the development of a generic multimodal attention framework, potentially useful beyond the specific VQA task, and a well-designed ablation study that clearly isolates the contribution of the ternary potential. The paper is also well-written and easy to follow.

However, several weaknesses should be addressed. The notion of attention *over* the answer modality is unclear, particularly given that answers are often single words or treated as such, and the paper lacks visualizations to support this claim. Further clarification and visual examples would be beneficial. While the results indicate that the ternary potential is key to the performance improvement, the model doesn’t consistently outperform existing methods in a two-modality setup (excluding HieCoAtt). A more in-depth discussion of this observation is needed. Given the importance of the ternary potential, a comparison against models that explicitly incorporate answer information, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16), would be valuable. Finally, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the model’s failure cases to guide future research. 

Minor errors/typos were noted: (a) L38: “mechanism” should be “mechanisms”; (b) L237 states evaluation is on the validation set, while Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**

Despite some lingering concerns regarding the authors’ explanation for the lack of improvement in the two-modality setting, I recommend acceptance. The proposed generic attention framework for multiple modalities is a significant contribution, and the quantitative results demonstrating superior performance with three-modality attention are compelling. The authors’ supplemental evaluation of attention map correlation with human attention maps is also promising, suggesting improved alignment compared to existing models like HieCoAtt, even without knowing the correlation values for other state-of-the-art methods like MCB. 

I have a question regarding the authors’ response concerning “MCB vs. MCT”: The reported performance figures appear reversed – 69.4% should correspond to 3-MCT, not 2-layer MCB. Furthermore, the MCB performance reported in Table 1 is 68.6%, creating a discrepancy with the 68.4% cited in the rebuttal.
