

Review:  
This paper introduces a novel multimodal attention framework for Visual Question Answering (VQA), formalizing attention through a unified combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer modalities. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA when leveraging ternary potentials, demonstrating the efficacy of this generic formulation.  

**Strengths:**  
1.  **Generalizable framework:** The proposed attention mechanism is task-agnostic, offering a promising foundation for multimodal problems beyond VQA. Its broader applicability is a significant contribution.  
2.  **Ablation clarity:** The ablation study effectively isolates the contribution of ternary potentials, providing clear evidence of their impact.  
3.  **Clarity of presentation:** The paper is well-structured and written, facilitating easy comprehension of the method.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1.  **Ambiguity in answer attention:** The paper proposes attention over the *answer* modality, yet answers are typically single words or short phrases. The interpretation of "attention over answers" is unclear, and the absence of visualizations for answer attention (e.g., highlighting answer tokens) undermines this aspect. Authors must clarify the mechanism and provide visual evidence.  
2.  **Dominance of ternary potentials:** Table 1 reveals that without ternary potentials, the model only marginally outperforms baselines (e.g., HieCoAtt) in the 2-modality setup. The primary performance gain stems from ternary potentials, yet the paper does not sufficiently explain *why* this combination is so impactful compared to existing 2-modality approaches. Quantitative justification for this shift is needed.  
3.  **Incomplete comparison baseline:** Given ternary potentials’ critical role, the comparison should include models that explicitly incorporate answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., ECCV 2016, "Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines"). This would better contextualize the novelty and contribution of the ternary formulation.  
4.  **Lack of failure analysis:** The paper omits discussion of model failures. Analyzing error cases would provide valuable insights for future work and demonstrate a deeper understanding of the model’s limitations.  
5.  **Minor errors:**  
    -   L38: "mechanism" → "mechanisms" (plural).  
    -   L237: Claims evaluation on validation set, but Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std results. Clarify the evaluation protocol.  

**Post-rebuttal comments:**  
While the authors’ response to the 2-modality performance concern lacks quantitative support (e.g., explicit comparison metrics), the paper’s core contribution—a generic multimodal attention framework—remains compelling. The quantitative validation of attention maps correlating with human attention (reported in rebuttal) is a strong positive, suggesting the model’s attention aligns better with human reasoning than prior work (e.g., HieCoAtt). Although the correlation values for SOTA baselines like MCB are not provided, the improvement over HieCoAtt is evident.  

**Critical query on authors’ response:**  
The authors state: *"2-layer MCB yields 69.4% [on test-dev], while 3-MCT yields 68.4%."* However, Table 1 reports MCB at 68.6% on test-dev. This suggests a possible misattribution: the 69.4% figure likely corresponds to 3-MCT (not MCB), as 3-MCT should outperform 2-modality MCB. Clarification is essential to resolve this discrepancy.  

**Recommendation:**  
I recommend acceptance. The generic multimodal attention framework is a meaningful advance, and the ternary potential’s performance gains are well-supported by results. The rebuttal’s attention map correlation with human behavior further strengthens the contribution. While the weaknesses require addressing in the final version (particularly answer attention clarity, 2-modality justification, and failure analysis), the core innovation and results justify acceptance. The minor MCB/MCT confusion in the rebuttal does not negate the paper’s overall merit.
