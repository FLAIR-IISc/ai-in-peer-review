The paper "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering" proposes a novel attention mechanism that learns high-order correlations between various data modalities. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on the task of visual question answering (VQA), achieving state-of-the-art performance on the standard VQA dataset.

The main idea of the paper is to model attention as a probability distribution over the input data, using a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The unary potentials capture the importance of individual features, while the pairwise and ternary potentials capture correlations between features. The authors use a mean-field inference approach to compute the attention probabilities, which are then used to weight the input features.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing attention mechanisms. The proposed approach is also well-evaluated, with both quantitative and qualitative experiments demonstrating its effectiveness.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. The writing is sometimes unclear, and the presentation of the model could be more concise. Additionally, the novelty of the approach feels a bit limited, as it builds on existing work on attention mechanisms.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its thorough evaluation. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the performance of their approach, including both quantitative and qualitative results. The qualitative results, in particular, are impressive, and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed attention mechanism.

Another strength of the paper is its clarity of presentation. While the writing is sometimes unclear, the authors provide a clear explanation of the model and its components. The figures are also well-designed, and help to illustrate the key concepts of the paper.

One area where the paper could be improved is in its discussion of related work. While the authors provide a good overview of existing attention mechanisms, they could provide more context on how their approach differs from existing work.

In terms of originality, the paper is a clear advance over existing work on attention mechanisms. The proposed approach is novel, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness on a challenging task.
