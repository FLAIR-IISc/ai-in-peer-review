The paper presents a novel method to model attention for multimodal input, specifically for the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The main novelty lies in proposing a generic formulation of attention for multiple modalities – a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer modalities in VQA. The proposed approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and achieves state-of-the-art performance when incorporating ternary potentials.

**Strengths:**

1.  The formulation of a generic attention mechanism applicable to multimodal problems is a valuable contribution. The framework’s potential for generalization beyond the specific task of VQA is promising.
2.  The ablation study demonstrating the performance gain from including ternary potentials provides insights into the contribution of each component of the proposed model.
3.  The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  The inclusion of attention over the answer modality is unclear. Given that answers often consist of single words or short phrases treated as single units, the purpose and impact of attending to the answer is not well-motivated. The lack of visualization for attention over answers further diminishes clarity.
2.  The significant performance improvement appears largely driven by the addition of ternary potentials. Without them, the proposed model does not consistently outperform existing methods in a two-modality setup (except for HieCoAtt). A more detailed analysis of this dependency would be beneficial.
3.  Given the performance gains with ternary potentials, a comparison against existing models that explicitly incorporate answers as inputs – such as the work by Jabri et al. (ECCV 2016) – would strengthen the evaluation and better position the contribution of this work.
4.  The paper lacks a discussion of failure cases. Analyzing instances where the model performs poorly would provide valuable insights into its limitations and guide future research directions.
5.  Minor errors/typos exist:
    a.  L38: “mechanism” should be “mechanisms.”
    b.  L237 states evaluation is on the validation set, yet Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper proposes a novel and potentially impactful attention mechanism applicable to a broad range of multimodal tasks.
*   The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on VQA when utilizing ternary potentials.
*   The ablation study helps to understand the contribution of the different components.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The role of attention over the answer modality is not well-justified or demonstrated.
*   The substantial performance gain appears heavily reliant on the ternary potential, and the model’s performance without it is not particularly competitive.
*   The lack of failure case analysis limits the understanding of the model’s limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of multimodal attention mechanisms. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty and promising results justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised regarding the answer modality attention and the clarification of performance without ternary potentials. A discussion of failure cases would also strengthen the paper.
