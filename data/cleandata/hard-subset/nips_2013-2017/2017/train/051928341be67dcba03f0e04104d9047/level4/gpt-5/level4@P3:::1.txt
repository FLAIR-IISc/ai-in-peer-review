Summary
This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning, instantiated on visual question answering (VQA). The method models attention as a probabilistic system with unary (per-modality), pairwise (cross-modality), and ternary (three-way) potentials over image regions, question words, and candidate answers. A single mean-field iteration combines these potentials—via learnable weights followed by softmax—to produce attention distributions over each modality. The attended representations are then fused using sketching-based polynomial pooling (MCB for pairwise, MCT for ternary), avoiding the parameter blow-up of explicit outer products. On VQA, the approach achieves state-of-the-art results with extensive quantitative and qualitative analyses, including visualizations of the attention maps.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on a large body of attention literature in vision and NLP. Relative to co-attention and hierarchical attention approaches (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Yang et al. [27]), bilinear and low-rank pooling methods (e.g., Fukui et al. [7], Kim et al. [13]), and structured/alternating attention (e.g., Kim et al. [14], Xu et al. [25]), the contribution is to (i) explicitly decompose attention into multi-order potentials across modalities, (ii) combine them via a mean-field-inspired weighting, and (iii) use compact polynomial sketching (MCB/MCT) for decision fusion. Compared to [15, 25], the framework is conceptually more extensible to more than two modalities. Compared to [7], which uses bilinear pooling for fusion and single-network attention, this work emphasizes high-order correlations within the attention mechanism itself.

Strengths
- Clear and generally useful idea: modeling multi-order correlations in attention and combining them with learnable weights.
- Strong results on VQA with careful engineering to control model size (40M parameters vs. 70M in some baselines).
- Thorough experiments: comparisons to relevant baselines; qualitative attention visualizations that are informative and appear question-dependent.
- Practical fusion design (MCB/MCT) that scales with modality order and aligns with prior best practices.

Weaknesses
- The probabilistic interpretation (potentials + mean-field) is mostly motivational. The inference is effectively a single learned linear combination followed by softmax; this should be either formalized (derivation/energy function, assumptions) or reframed as a principled weighted mixture.
- Novelty is incremental: the main advance is lifting known pairwise mechanisms to a unified multi-order formulation and adding ternary attention. This is valuable but should be positioned more clearly with respect to [7, 15, 13, 14].
- Some presentation issues impede clarity (figure references, notation, and narrative flow).
- Limited ablations disentangling the contributions of unary vs. pairwise vs. ternary potentials and the learned combination weights.

Quality
- The method is technically sound overall, and implementation details are mostly sufficient for reproduction (optimizer, dimensions, dropout, answer set, training time).
- However, the “probabilistic model” requires a more rigorous treatment if claims are to be taken beyond intuition. For example, specify the energy, clarify the role of learned α/β/γ as variational parameters, and justify a single mean-field iteration empirically (does more help?).
- Important ablations are missing or only briefly mentioned: (i) effect of removing each potential order, (ii) sensitivity to attention fusion weights, (iii) controlled comparison to [7] with matched embedding dimension, same image backbone, and same decision head.

Clarity
- The writing is at times confusing, with notation overload and occasional typos (e.g., “and and,” “aV” repeated for answers, “θV,Q.A”). Please proofread and harmonize notation.
- Figure 2 sits at an awkward granularity. Consider simplifying to emphasize the flow: embeddings → unary/pairwise/ternary potentials → weighted combination (Eq. 1) → attended vectors → MCB/MCT fusion → classifier.
- Figure 3 is not consistently referenced in the main text; ensure each subfigure is discussed where the corresponding module is introduced.
- It would be helpful to show marginalized ternary attention maps (per modality) alongside unary and pairwise maps to illustrate the effect of the three-way term.

Originality
- The approach is a thoughtful synthesis: multi-order attention potentials with learned weighting and compact polynomial fusion. While each ingredient has precedent, the unified treatment and extension to ternary attention for VQA are useful. The novelty is moderate.

Significance
- Achieving SOTA on VQA with a relatively compact model is meaningful, and the approach is likely to be adopted or extended. The framework’s extensibility to additional modalities is appealing, though only two/three are evaluated here.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Learned weights: Please report the learned α/β/γ in Eq. (1) (averages across runs or per modality). Do modalities rely on different orders? This would substantiate the design.
- Two-modality underperformance vs. MCB [7]: Provide a controlled ablation with matched embedding size (e.g., 2048), same image features, same decision head. If the gap persists, analyze whether it stems from the attention mechanism or from the fusion layer choice.
- MCB vs. MCT: You note that two sequential MCB layers outperformed a single MCT layer; please include quantitative results on val/test, and discuss why (e.g., self-interaction of image features, better inductive bias).
- Complexity: For the ternary tensor C3, clarify memory/computation. Are correlations computed explicitly or factorized/approximated? Provide the effective cost and any sampling/stride strategies.
- Multi-hop/iterations: Did you try multiple mean-field iterations or stacked attention steps? Even reporting negative results would be informative.
- Qualitative: Include ternary attention visualizations and additional examples where ternary terms visibly change the attended regions or answers.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong empirical performance with informative analyses.
- General and extensible design that unifies multi-order attentional interactions.
- Practical fusion with compact sketching; competitive parameter count.

Cons for acceptance
- Probabilistic framing is underdeveloped; formal clarity is lacking.
- Incremental novelty relative to prior co-/bilinear attention work.
- Missing ablations and some clarity issues (figures, notation).

Overall assessment
A solid and practically useful contribution to multimodal attention with convincing results on VQA. With improved clarity, stronger ablations, and a crisper treatment of the probabilistic framing, this paper would be notably stronger. My recommendation is weak accept.
