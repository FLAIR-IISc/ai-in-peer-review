The paper introduces a novel attention mechanism for Visual Question Answering (VQA), designed to model interactions between images, questions, and answers. The core contribution is a general formulation of attention based on unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across these three modalities. Evaluation on the VQA dataset demonstrates that incorporating ternary potentials leads to performance improvements over existing approaches.

Strengths:
1. The proposed generic attention formulation, applicable to multimodal problems beyond a specific task, is valuable, particularly if demonstrated to generalize effectively.
2. The ablation study, isolating the impact of ternary potentials, provides useful insight into their contribution to performance.
3. The paper is clearly and concisely written.

Weaknesses:
1. The attention mechanism is applied to three modalities—image, question, and answer. However, the meaning of “attention over answers” is unclear, as answers frequently consist of single words or are treated as such even when multi-word. The paper lacks visualizations of this attention over answers, and clarification in this regard would be beneficial.
2. The results in Table 1 suggest that the performance gains are primarily attributable to the ternary potentials. Without them, the proposed model does not consistently outperform existing two-modality models (with the exception of HieCoAtt). Further discussion of this observation is requested.
3. Given the importance of ternary potentials to the performance improvement, a comparison with existing models that explicitly incorporate answers as inputs—such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16)—would be informative.
4. The paper lacks a discussion of failure cases. Analyzing the model’s shortcomings would provide valuable direction for future research.
5. Minor errors/typos:
a. L38: “mechanism” should be “mechanisms”
b. L237 states evaluation was performed on the validation set, while Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets.

Post-rebuttal comments:
While the authors’ response to the concern regarding performance relative to existing two-modality models is not fully convincing due to a lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance. This recommendation is based on the novelty of the generic attention framework for multiple modalities, and the quantitative results demonstrating that three-modality attention outperforms the state-of-the-art. The authors’ rebuttal also presents compelling evidence, through quantitative evaluation of attention maps against human attention maps, that the proposed attention maps exhibit stronger correlation with human attention than those of existing models. Although the correlation value for state-of-the-art models like MCB is unknown, the presented results suggest a significant improvement over HieCoAtt.
