This paper introduces a novel high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to model correlations between different data modalities (image, question, and answer). The core idea is to learn these correlations – unary, pairwise, and ternary – to direct attention effectively to relevant elements within each modality. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, and provide visualizations suggesting improved attention focusing.

The work builds upon the growing body of research employing attention mechanisms in cognitive tasks like VQA [6], image captioning [26], and machine comprehension [8]. Existing attention mechanisms [15, 26] are often limited by ad-hoc data representations and task-specific designs. This paper addresses these limitations by proposing a more general and flexible attention mechanism capable of learning interactions across multiple modalities. The approach is related to probabilistic modeling of attention [14] and bilinear models [13], but extends them to handle an arbitrary number of modalities, a key distinction from prior work. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Performance:** The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness.
*   **Novelty in High-Order Correlations:** The exploration of high-order correlations (beyond pairwise) between modalities is a valuable contribution. While building upon existing attention mechanisms, it extends them in a principled way to capture more complex relationships.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The paper presents both quantitative and qualitative results, including visualizations of attention maps that help understand how the model focuses on relevant image regions and question words. The ablation study comparing MCT and MCB decision modules is also a strong point.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing attention mechanisms and motivates the need for a more general approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Writing:** The writing can be dense and occasionally confusing, hindering a full understanding of the technical details. More concise and focused explanations would improve readability.
*   **Limited Formalization of Probabilistic Connection:** The paper positions the approach as a probabilistic model but lacks a rigorous formalization of this connection. The probabilistic intuition is presented more as motivation than a core mathematical foundation.
*   **Figure Presentation:** Figure 2 is at an unsuitable level of abstraction. It lacks sufficient detail to understand the network's internal workings, yet isn’t high-level enough to easily convey the core idea. Revising this figure to emphasize the unary/pairwise/trinary potential generation would be beneficial. Figure 3 is defined but not referenced, making its purpose unclear.
*   **Underperformance of 2-Modality Model:** The observation that the 2-modality model with unary and pairwise attention underperforms the MCB baseline [7] is puzzling and deserves more thorough investigation and discussion. The potential role of the decision computation modules (MCB/MCT) should be explored further.

**Specific Questions & Suggestions:**

*   **Learned Coefficients:** What values were learned for the linear coefficients (α, β, γ) in Equation (1)? Analyzing these coefficients could reveal whether different modalities rely more on different orders of potentials.
*   **Marginalized Trinary Attention:** While the paper describes ternary potentials, visualizations of the resulting marginalized attention maps are missing. Including these would provide further insight into the model's behavior.
*   **Ablation Study Results:** Elaborate on the observations from the experiment comparing the sequential MCB versus the single MCT layer for the decision head. Providing the results would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Clarify Novelty:** Further clarify the key contributions of the work, particularly in relation to existing methods like [7]. Is the primary novelty in extending the approach to higher-order correlations, or in the specific implementation details of the attention mechanism?



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to attention modeling for VQA.
*   It achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark dataset.
*   The qualitative results suggest that the learned attention mechanisms are meaningful and contribute to the performance gains.
*   The thorough experimental evaluation provides strong support for the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The writing clarity needs improvement to facilitate a better understanding of the technical details.
*   The probabilistic connection is not fully formalized, weakening the theoretical foundation of the approach.
*   The underperformance of the 2-modality model relative to the MCB baseline requires further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of visual question answering. While the writing could be improved and the probabilistic connection more rigorously defined, the novelty of the approach, the strong experimental results, and the insightful qualitative analysis make it a worthy candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity and elaboration of the probabilistic formulation, as well as further discussion regarding the 2-modality model’s performance.
