- Proposal: novel multimodal attention for VQA modeling image, question, and answer jointly with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials.  
- Empirical result: improvements on VQA when ternary potentials (three-modality attention) are included.  
- Strength: introduces a generic, task-agnostic multimodal attention formulation that could generalize beyond VQA.  
- Strength: includes an ablation isolating ternary potentials to quantify their contribution.  
- Strength: paper is clearly written.  
- Weakness: “attention over answers” is unclear—answers are often single tokens and the paper lacks visualizations or qualitative examples of answer-side attention.  
- Weakness: Table 1 implies most gains come from ternary potentials; without them the model generally does not outperform two-modality baselines—need explanation why the two-modality instantiation underperforms.  
- Weakness: should compare to models that also use answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., “Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines”).  
- Weakness: lacks analysis of failure cases to show where and why the model fails.  
- Weakness: minor errors/typos—L38 pluralization and L237 claims validation set but Table 1 shows test-dev/test-std results (reconcile).  
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer leans to accept but finds the rebuttal on two-modality performance not fully convincing due to missing quantitative evidence.  
- Post-rebuttal: three-modality attention shown to outperform state of the art; reported quantitative correlation with human attention maps is encouraging but lacks comparison to strong SOTA baselines like MCB.  
- Question for authors: reported MCB vs. MCT numbers appear inconsistent—are 68.4% and 69.4% swapped, and which exact MCB configuration corresponds to the cited 68.4%/68.6% figures?
