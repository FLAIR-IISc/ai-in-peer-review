- Proposes high-order attention for VQA that models unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) and combines marginalized potentials linearly (Eq.1).  
- Uses compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) in the decision head and reports strong results on standard VQA.  
- Extends co-attention and bilinear pooling prior work by explicitly modeling multi-order (unary/pairwise/ternary) attention and framing attention probabilistically.  
- Overall method is interesting with convincing empirical gains and supportive attention visualizations.  
- Writing and presentation are sometimes confusing and could be clarified with better figures and exposition.  
- Strength: interesting model design with strong quantitative and qualitative evaluation.  
- Strength: shift from unary/pairwise attentions to final attention is compelling; would like marginalized ternary maps.  
- Weakness: limited conceptual novelty—largely an extension/organization of ideas from [7,15] rather than fundamentally new primitives.  
- Weakness: probabilistic framing is motivational but not formalized; needs derivation/assumptions or toned-down language.  
- Weakness: Figure 2 is at an awkward abstraction level; suggest simplifying to show potential generation and flow to attention/decision.  
- Weakness: Figure 3 appears unreferenced.  
- Quality: technically sound method and careful empirical evaluation; attention visualizations are informative.  
- Analysis gap: need ablations on design choices (linear combination coefficients in Eq.1, sequential MCB vs single MCT, embedding dims).  
- Clarity: tighten exposition/notation around potentials and provide a summary table/diagram of modules and I/O.  
- Originality: contribution is systematic organization and empirical validation of high-order attention rather than novel primitives.  
- Significance: strong results and likely practical utility for VQA and other multimodal tasks.  
- Questions: what values were learned for linear coefficients in Eq.1 and do modalities rely on different potential orders?  
- Questions: why does the Unary+Pairwise 2-modality model underperform MCB [7]—is it due to decision head (MCB/MCT), embedding dims, or training choices?  
- Questions: performance and qualitative differences between sequential MCB and single MCT in the decision head—please provide results.  
- Request: include marginalized ternary attention maps to illustrate third-order contributions.  
- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical results, useful visualizations, and systematic multi-order attention exploration.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited novelty, exposition/figure clarity issues, and underdeveloped probabilistic framing.
