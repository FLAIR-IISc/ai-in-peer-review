### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel and generic framework for modeling multi-modal attention, applied to the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core contribution is a principled, energy-based formulation that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities: the image, the question, and the answer. The proposed model is evaluated on the VQA dataset, where it achieves state-of-the-art results, with experiments demonstrating that the inclusion of ternary potentials is the primary driver of this performance gain.

**Strengths**

1.  **Generic Attention Framework:** The primary strength of this work is its proposal of a generic and principled framework for multi-modal attention. By formulating the problem in terms of potentials, the method is not tied to a specific task or number of modalities. This has the potential for broad applicability to other multi-modal problems.
2.  **Effective Ablation Study:** The paper includes a valuable ablation study that effectively isolates and demonstrates the significant contribution of the ternary potentials to the model's overall performance. This analysis clearly validates the central hypothesis of the paper.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Unclear Role of "Attention over Answers":** The concept of applying attention to the answer modality is not sufficiently motivated or clarified. In VQA, most answers are single words (or treated as such), making it difficult to understand what "attention over answers" intuits. The paper provides no visualizations for this third modality, unlike the image and question attentions. This conceptual ambiguity should be addressed.
2.  **Performance Dependency on Ternary Potential:** The results in Table 1 suggest that the model's performance gains are almost exclusively derived from the inclusion of the ternary potential. When configured with only two modalities (image and question), the proposed model does not outperform existing methods (with the exception of HieCoAtt). This raises questions about the novelty or effectiveness of the unary and pairwise components of the framework when compared to other state-of-the-art 2-modality attention mechanisms.
3.  **Missing Baselines:** Given the critical importance of the answer modality (via the ternary potential) to the model's success, a comparison with other models that also incorporate answer information as input is essential. For instance, comparing against the work of Jabri et al. ("Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines", ECCV 2016), which treats VQA as a classification problem over answers, would provide a more complete picture of the proposed model's standing.
4.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a qualitative analysis of its failure cases. Understanding the types of questions or images where the model struggles would provide valuable insights into its limitations and guide future research.

**Minor Issues**
*   L38: "mechanism" should be pluralized to "mechanisms".
*   L237 states that evaluation is performed on the validation set, but Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std splits. This discrepancy should be clarified.

### Post-Rebuttal Assessment

I have read the authors' rebuttal and appreciate their detailed responses. My overall assessment is positive, and I am recommending acceptance.

The new quantitative results presented in the rebuttal, which compare the model's attention maps with human attention maps, are a strong addition to the paper. The reported improvement in correlation over the HieCoAtt baseline is encouraging and provides evidence that the learned attentions are meaningful. While a comparison against stronger baselines like MCB for this metric would have been more conclusive, the provided results still strengthen the paper's claims.

The authors' explanation for why the 2-modality model does not outperform all existing baselines remains somewhat unsatisfactory without more detailed quantitative evidence. However, this concern is mitigated by the fact that the paper's primary contribution is the n-modal framework itself, and its effectiveness is convincingly demonstrated in the 3-modality setting.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**

Despite the remaining concern about the 2-modality performance, I recommend acceptance. The paper's core contribution—a novel, generic, and principled framework for n-modal attention—is significant and well-executed. The strong empirical results of the 3-modality model demonstrate its effectiveness on a challenging task. This work has the potential to influence future research in multi-modal learning.
