

The paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically targeting Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core innovation lies in modeling *k*-th order correlations (unary, pairwise, ternary) across modalities (image, question, answer) via probabilistic potentials, rather than relying on task-specific architectures. This generalizes beyond prior work like Lu et al. (NIPS 2016) and Fukui et al. (ICLR 2016), which focused on pairwise interactions (e.g., MCB pooling). The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art VQA results (69.4% on test-dev) using a 3-modality framework, with ablation studies confirming the critical role of ternary potentials.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel Framework for Multimodal Attention**: The high-order potential formulation provides a principled, task-agnostic approach to modeling cross-modal dependencies. Unlike existing methods (e.g., [15, 7]), it trivially extends to *k* modalities, addressing a key limitation in current literature.  
2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The ablation study (Table 1) convincingly shows that ternary potentials drive the performance gain (69.4% vs. 68.6% without them), while the 2-modality baseline underperforms prior work (e.g., 68.6% MCB vs. 69.4% 3-modality). The qualitative analysis (Fig. 5–7) effectively demonstrates attention alignment with human reasoning, particularly for complex questions.  
3. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-structured. Figures 3–4 clearly illustrate the attention mechanics, and the technical exposition (Section 3) balances mathematical rigor with accessibility. The ablation design directly supports the main claims.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Ambiguity in Answer Attention**: The paper treats answers as a first-class modality (e.g., θ<sub>A</sub>, PA) but uses single-word embeddings (Section 4.1) for all answers. This renders "answer attention" largely redundant (e.g., a 3-word answer like "a red car" is embedded as a single vector), yet the method treats it as a modality. *No visualizations of answer attention are provided* (Fig. 5–7 show only image/question attention), making it unclear how this component contributes. The authors should clarify whether answer attention is meaningful or redundant, and justify the embedding strategy.  
2. **Overreliance on Ternary Potentials**: The 3-modality model’s superiority (69.4% vs. 68.6% for 2-modality) hinges almost entirely on ternary potentials. However, the 2-modality baseline (without ternary terms) underperforms existing models (e.g., MCB at 68.6% vs. Lu et al.’s 68.8% [15]), suggesting the *framework* itself—not just ternary terms—needs stronger justification. The authors should compare against Jabri et al. (ECCV 2016), which also uses answers as input but avoids high-order attention.  
3. **Lack of Failure Analysis**: The paper omits discussion of failure cases (e.g., Fig. 8’s ambiguous umbrella attention), which is critical for assessing robustness. A brief analysis of *why* the model fails (e.g., over-reliance on color vs. object semantics) would strengthen the evaluation.  
4. **Rebuttal Confusion on MCB/MCT**: The authors’ rebuttal claims "3-MCT yields 68.4% and 2-layer MCB 69.4%," but Table 1 reports MCB at 68.6% (test-dev). This inconsistency undermines confidence. The authors must clarify these numbers (e.g., "3-MCT: 69.4%, 2-layer MCB: 68.4%") or correct the table.  

**Recommendation**  
The paper presents a technically sound, well-motivated framework for high-order multimodal attention, with compelling empirical results on VQA. Its significance lies in offering a *generalizable* architecture beyond VQA-specific designs, addressing a gap noted in recent NIPS work [15, 7]. However, the ambiguity around answer attention and the reliance on ternary potentials without deeper analysis of the framework’s broader applicability require resolution.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro*:  
- Novel, principled approach to multimodal attention (not task-specific).  
- Strong VQA results (SOTA on test-dev) with clear ablation.  
- High clarity and technical rigor.  

*Con*:  
- Answer attention component lacks justification and visualization.  
- 2-modality baseline underperforms prior work, weakening the framework’s broader claim.  
- Failure cases and MCB/MCT numerical discrepancy unaddressed in the paper.  

**Final Assessment**  
This work makes a meaningful contribution to attention mechanisms for multimodal learning, with strong VQA results. While the answer attention ambiguity and numerical confusion are concerning, the core idea is significant and well-executed. I recommend **acceptance** with the expectation that the authors clarify the answer attention rationale and resolve the numerical discrepancy in the camera-ready version. The framework’s generality and SOTA performance outweigh the current weaknesses.
