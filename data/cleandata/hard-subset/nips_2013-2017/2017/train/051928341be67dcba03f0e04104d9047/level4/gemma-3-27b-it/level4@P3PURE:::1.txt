General Impression:
Overall, the proposed method is interesting and yields strong results. The attention maps presented appear to demonstrate that these gains stem from improved attention mechanisms rather than simply increased model capacity. While the writing could be strengthened for greater clarity, I believe the authors could address these points with further refinement.

Strengths:
- The model is interesting and achieves good performance.
- The experiments are thorough, both qualitatively and quantitatively.
- I was particularly impressed by the progression from unary/pairwise attentions to the final attention mechanism. However, I would have appreciated seeing visualizations of marginalized trinary attention maps as well; I may have missed these in the text or supplementary materials. 

Weaknesses:
- The presentation was occasionally jumbled and difficult to follow.
- The novelty of the approach feels somewhat limited. While the architecture demonstrably outperforms the MCB for VQA model [7] and is more complex, much of this added complexity appears to extend the intuition of [7] to higher (trinary) and lower (unary) orders. This is not necessarily a significant flaw, but I recommend the authors more clearly articulate the specific contributions of their work (and any that I may have overlooked).
- The probabilistic connection is not clearly established. It currently appears more motivational than formally justified; this is acceptable, but I suggest either formally solidifying this connection or adjusting the language to reflect its current status.
- Figure 2 is at an inappropriate level of abstraction. It lacks the detail needed to understand the network's functionality, yet it isn’t abstract enough to provide a clear overview of the approach. I suggest simplifying the figure to emphasize the generation of unary/pairwise/trinary potentials.
- Figure 3 is not referenced anywhere in the text, as far as I could tell.

Some things I'm curious about:
- What values were learned for the linear coefficients used to combine the marginalized potentials in equation (1)? It would be interesting to know if different modalities benefit from different potential orders.
- I found it surprising that the 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise model underperforms MCB [7], given their architectural similarities. I was disappointed that this result was not discussed in more detail. Do the authors have any intuition as to why this occurs? Could it be related to the switch to MCB/MCT decision computation modules?
- The discussion of using sequential MCB versus a single MCT layer for the decision head was intriguing, but no corresponding results were presented. Could the authors elaborate on their observations regarding these two approaches?
