This paper introduces a novel method for Visual Question Answering (VQA) that models attention over both the image and the question. The primary contribution is a generalized attention framework for multiple modalities, formulated as a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials for the image, question, and answer modalities. The proposed approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset, where the version including ternary potentials outperforms existing models.

**Strengths:**
1.  The proposal of a generalized, task-agnostic attention framework for multimodal problems is a significant contribution, particularly if its applicability can be demonstrated beyond VQA.
2.  The ablation study effectively demonstrates the performance contribution of the ternary potential, which is crucial for understanding the model's behavior.
3.  The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The concept of applying "attention over answers" is unclear. Given that most answers in VQA are single words (and treated as such in this work), the mechanism and utility of an attention distribution over them require further explanation. The absence of visualizations for answer attention makes this point more obscure.
2.  The results in Table 1 suggest that the performance gains are almost entirely attributable to the ternary potential. Without it, the proposed 2-modality model does not surpass existing methods (with the exception of HieCoAtt). The authors should provide more insight into why their 2-modality formulation does not offer a competitive advantage on its own.
3.  Given the demonstrated importance of the ternary potential, a comparison with other models that also leverage answer information as input, such as Jabri et al., ECCV 2016 ("Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines"), is warranted.
4.  The paper lacks a discussion of the model's failure cases. An analysis of its error modes would provide valuable insights and guide future research.
5.  Other errors/typos:
    a.  Line 38: "mechanism" should be "mechanisms".
    b.  Line 237 states that the evaluation is on the validation set, but Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std splits. Please clarify this discrepancy.

**Post-rebuttal comments:**
Although the authors' response regarding the underperformance of the 2-modality model was not entirely convincing due to a lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance. This recommendation is based on the novelty of the proposed generic attention framework for multiple modalities and the strong quantitative results showing that the 3-modality attention model outperforms the state-of-the-art.

Furthermore, the quantitative evaluation of the model's attention maps against human attention maps, provided in the rebuttal, is a valuable addition. The results suggest a stronger correlation with human attention than that of existing models like HieCoAtt. While a direct comparison to SOTA models such as MCB is missing, the reported improvement is still a significant and positive signal.
