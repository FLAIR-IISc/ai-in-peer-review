This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, demonstrated on the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The central idea is to model attention as a probabilistic inference problem over multiple modalities. The framework defines unary potentials for individual modalities (image, question, answer), pairwise potentials for correlations between two modalities (e.g., image-question), and, most notably, ternary potentials to capture high-order correlations among all three. The final attention distributions for each modality are derived by linearly combining these potentials and performing a single mean-field inference step. This approach is contrasted with prior work like co-attention models (Lu et al., 2015) and bilinear pooling methods (Fukui et al., 2016), with the key distinction being the proposed model's principled and generalizable formulation for handling more than two modalities through high-order potentials. The method is evaluated on the VQA dataset, where it achieves state-of-the-art results.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work is the proposal of a generic and principled probabilistic framework for high-order attention. Formulating attention in terms of potentials and mean-field inference is a novel approach in this context. This provides a clear and extensible method for incorporating an arbitrary number of modalities, which is a significant contribution beyond task-specific, ad-hoc architectures. The potential for this framework to be applied to other multimodal problems is high.

2.  **Quality of Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the standard VQA benchmark. The thorough ablation study effectively isolates and demonstrates the significant contribution of the ternary (image-question-answer) potentials, which validates the central hypothesis of the paper. This provides strong empirical evidence for the importance of modeling high-order correlations in VQA.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the proposed methodology is presented in a clear and organized manner. The decomposition of the model into data embedding, attention, and decision-making modules is logical and easy to follow. The illustrations, particularly Figure 3, are helpful in understanding the architecture of the potential functions.

### **Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection / Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Clarity on Answer Attention:** The concept of "attention over answers" requires further clarification. In the VQA dataset, most answers are single words, and the paper states that multi-word answers are embedded as single n-grams. It is therefore unclear what it means to compute an attention distribution over components of a single-token answer. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of this mechanism and, if applicable, visualizations of the answer attention distributions similar to those provided for image and question modalities.

2.  **Performance of the 2-Modality Model:** Based on Table 1, the performance of the proposed model without the ternary potential (the 2-modality version) appears to be below that of other contemporary 2-modality models like the MCB baseline. This raises the question of whether the performance gains are solely due to the inclusion of answer information via the third modality, or if the underlying framework for pairwise attention is competitive on its own. A stronger 2-modality baseline would make the claims about the benefits of the ternary potential even more compelling.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** The paper could be strengthened by a more direct comparison with other models that also leverage answer information as an input. For instance, "Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines" (Jabri et al., ECCV 2016) frames the problem as a multi-class classification where answer embeddings are used directly. A discussion comparing the proposed high-order attention approach to these alternative ways of incorporating answer information would help to better contextualize the contribution.

4.  **Lack of Failure Case Analysis:** The qualitative analysis focuses exclusively on successful examples. An inclusion and discussion of failure cases would provide valuable insights into the model's limitations. Understanding when and why the high-order attention fails (e.g., focusing on a distracting but salient object, as hinted in Figure 8) would guide future research and provide a more balanced evaluation of the method's capabilities.

### **Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This paper presents a novel and well-motivated framework for multimodal attention. The generalization to high-order correlations is a significant conceptual contribution, and the strong empirical results on the VQA task demonstrate its practical value. While there are weaknesses related to the clarity of the answer attention mechanism and the competitiveness of the 2-modality baseline, these do not fatally undermine the paper's core contribution. The proposed high-order attention model is an important step towards building more sophisticated and principled multimodal systems. Therefore, I recommend acceptance. The authors are encouraged to address the points raised above to further strengthen the manuscript.
