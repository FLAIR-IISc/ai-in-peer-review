- Paper proposes a generic multimodal attention framework for VQA that jointly models attention over image, question, and answer using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials.
- Method evaluated on VQA and achieves state-of-the-art performance when ternary potentials are included.

Strengths
- The multimodal attention formulation is task-agnostic and potentially applicable beyond VQA, increasing impact.
- Ablation study isolating ternary potentials clarifies where performance gains arise.
- The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses / Questions
- Answer-side attention is underspecified; need explanation and visualizations for what attention over answers represents given atomic/multiword answers.
- Most improvement appears to come from ternary potentials; two-modality version does not clearly outperform strong baselines—need analysis and quantitative evidence for why it lags.
- Must compare directly to prior work that includes answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., ECCV 2016) for proper context.
- Paper lacks analysis of failure cases; discussion of typical errors would clarify limitations and guide future work.
- Minor issues: (a) typo: “mechanism” → “mechanisms”; (b) inconsistency between text claiming validation-set evaluation and Table 1 reporting test-dev/test-std results.

Post-rebuttal
- Rebuttal did not fully resolve the two-modality concern, but reviewer leans toward acceptance because the framework is principled and the ternary model yields strong results.
- Additional attention-to-human map evaluation is encouraging, showing higher correlations than prior work (relative to HieCoAtt), though comparisons to leading baselines like MCB are missing.

Clarification requested on rebuttal claims
- Confusion over reported MCT vs. MCB numbers (3-MCT 68.4% vs. 2-layer MCB 69.4%): are these reversed, and which MCB configuration corresponds to the 69.4% / 68.6% figures reported in the paper/table?
