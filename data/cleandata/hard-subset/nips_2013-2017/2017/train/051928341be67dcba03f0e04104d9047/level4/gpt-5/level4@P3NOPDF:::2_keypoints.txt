- Paper proposes a generic multimodal attention framework combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials applied to VQA, with strongest gains from ternary interactions.
- Strength: modality-agnostic, general formulation potentially transferable beyond VQA.
- Strength: informative ablation isolating ternary potentials to quantify three-way contributions.
- Strength: paper is well written and easy to follow.
- Major concern: “answer attention” is insufficiently motivated and described given most VQA answers are single tokens or collapsed labels.
- Clarify granularity of answer attention (tokens, subwords, candidate answers, or answer embeddings).
- Clarify how answer attention is computed and used during training/inference (candidate set vs. embedding sequence).
- Provide qualitative visualizations or quantitative diagnostics (e.g., sparsity, entropy) for answer attention.
- Concern: pairwise (two-modality) variants do not consistently beat strong baselines, raising doubts about pairwise modeling strength vs. specialized methods.
- Provide quantitative evidence/ablations showing two-modality setup is competitive (training protocol, capacity control, feature backbones).
- Compare against methods that explicitly use answers at inference (e.g., Jabri et al. ECCV’16) and other candidate-answer approaches.
- Request analysis of failure cases: when ternary attention hurts, per-question-type effectiveness, and typical attention error patterns.
- Clarify evaluation splits: reconcile statement about validation evaluation with Table 1 reporting test-dev/test-std and specify splits used for model selection to avoid leakage.
- Minor issues: fix L38 “mechanism”→“mechanisms” and ensure consistent reporting of dataset splits.
- Post-rebuttal: rebuttal partially addressed two-modality concerns but lacked sufficient quantitative evidence; reviewer still recommends acceptance.
- Reasons for acceptance: novel unified attention framework, strong empirical gains from three-modality setup, and promising alignment with human attention.
- Request clarification on rebuttal numbers: apparent inconsistency between reported MCB and 3-MCT scores (69.4% vs. 68.4%) and which variants/configurations correspond to those numbers.
- Ask whether reported MCB/3-MCT results are directly comparable to Table 1 with same splits, preprocessing, and training schedule.
