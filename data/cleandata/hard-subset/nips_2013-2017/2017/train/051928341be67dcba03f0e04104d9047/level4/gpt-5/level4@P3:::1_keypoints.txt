- Introduces high-order attention for multimodal learning (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) combined via a single mean-field-inspired weighted softmax for VQA.  
- Fuses attended representations using sketching-based polynomial pooling (MCB for pairwise, MCT for ternary) to avoid explicit outer products.  
- Reports state-of-the-art VQA results with quantitative and qualitative analyses, including attention visualizations, using a compact ~40M-parameter model.  

- Builds on co-attention, bilinear pooling, and structured attention; contribution is explicit multi-order potential decomposition, mean-field-style combination, and compact fusion.  
- Framework is conceptually more extensible to >2 modalities than many prior two-way methods.  

- Strength: clear and useful idea of modeling multi-order attention with learnable weighting.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance with controlled model size.  
- Strength: thorough experiments and informative, question-dependent attention visualizations.  
- Strength: practical, scalable fusion design using MCB/MCT aligned with best practices.  

- Weakness: probabilistic framing is largely motivational—actual inference is effectively a single learned linear combination + softmax.  
- Weakness: incremental novelty—main advance is lifting pairwise mechanisms to a unified multi-order/ternary formulation.  
- Weakness: presentation issues (figure references, overloaded notation, typos) hinder clarity.  
- Weakness: limited ablations separating contributions of unary, pairwise, ternary potentials and learned combination weights.  

- Quality: technically sound and mostly reproducible, but requires a more rigorous probabilistic treatment (explicit energy, role of α/β/γ, justification for one mean-field iteration).  
- Missing crucial ablations: removing each potential order, sensitivity to fusion weights, and controlled comparison to MCB with matched settings.  

- Clarity: writing and notation need proofreading and harmonization; figures should be simplified and consistently referenced.  
- Suggestion: visualize marginalized ternary attention per modality alongside unary/pairwise maps to illustrate ternary effects.  

- Originality: thoughtful synthesis of existing components with moderate novelty in unifying and extending to ternary attention.  
- Significance: meaningful improvement on VQA with compact model and an extensible framework, though evaluation is limited to two/three modalities.  

- Requested analyses: report learned α/β/γ weights; perform controlled ablation vs. MCB with matched embeddings/backbone/decision head.  
- Requested analyses: provide quantitative MCB vs MCT comparisons and explain why sequential MCB might outperform single MCT.  
- Requested clarifications: detail memory/computation for ternary tensor C3 and any factorization/approximation strategies.  
- Requested experiments: try multiple mean-field iterations or stacked attention and report results (including negative).  
- Requested qualitative: include ternary attention visualizations and examples where ternary terms change attended regions/answers.  

- Pros for acceptance: strong empirical results, extensible multi-order attention design, compact fusion approach, competitive parameter count.  
- Cons for acceptance: underdeveloped probabilistic framing, incremental novelty, missing ablations and clarity issues.  

- Overall assessment: solid, practically useful contribution to multimodal attention with weak accept recommendation contingent on improved clarity, stronger ablations, and a crisper probabilistic treatment.
