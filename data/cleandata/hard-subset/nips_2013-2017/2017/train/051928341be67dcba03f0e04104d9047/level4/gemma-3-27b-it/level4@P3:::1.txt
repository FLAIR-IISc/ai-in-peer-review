This paper proposes a novel high-order attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA), aiming to capture correlations between different data modalities (visual, textual, and answer). The core idea is to model interactions between modalities using increasingly higher-order potentials, from unary to ternary, and to use mean-field inference to determine attention weights. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset, suggesting the effectiveness of their approach.

This work builds on the growing body of research utilizing attention mechanisms in multimodal tasks like VQA [15, 25, 1, 24, 23, 11, 14, 19, 13], image captioning [26], and machine comprehension [8]. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing attention mechanisms, specifically their ad-hoc data representation and task-specificity. This paper addresses these issues by proposing a generalizable attention mechanism applicable to any number of modalities. The approach shares similarities with probabilistic modeling of attention [14] and bilinear models [13], but distinguishes itself with its explicit modeling of high-order correlations and ease of extension to multiple modalities.  Related work in joint attention mechanisms, such as those presented in [15, 25], are also appropriately discussed.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Performance:** The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness.
*   **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly modeling high-order correlations between modalities is a valuable contribution, extending existing attention mechanisms. The ability to generalize to an arbitrary number of modalities is a significant advantage.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper includes both quantitative (performance on VQA datasets) and qualitative (attention map visualization) evaluations, providing insights into the model’s behavior. The comparison to existing methods like [7, 15] is valuable.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing attention mechanisms and provides a compelling motivation for the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Writing:** While the core idea is interesting, the writing can be dense and occasionally confusing. Improved clarity and a more streamlined presentation would enhance the paper's accessibility. Specifically, the jump between the probabilistic intuition and the equations feels abrupt.
*   **Limited Formalization of Probabilistic Connection:** The connection to a probabilistic model is presented more as a motivation than a formal derivation. Strengthening this aspect, or moderating the claims around it, would be beneficial.
*   **Figure 2’s Abstraction Level:** The system architecture diagram (Figure 2) is at an unhelpful level of abstraction. It lacks the detail needed to understand the network’s operation but is also too complex to easily grasp the overall approach. A simplified, more focused diagram emphasizing the unary/pairwise/ternary potential generation would improve understanding.
*   **Unreferenced Figure 3:** Figure 3 is introduced but never explicitly referenced in the text, diminishing its impact.
*   **Underperformance of 2-Modality Model:** The underperformance of the 2-modality model compared to MCB [7] is not adequately explained. A deeper discussion of this result and potential reasons for it would strengthen the analysis.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to attention modeling in multimodal tasks.
*   The method achieves state-of-the-art performance on a standard benchmark, demonstrating its practical value.
*   The thorough experimental evaluation, including both quantitative and qualitative results, provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   The potential for generalization to other multimodal tasks is significant.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The writing quality could be improved for better clarity and accessibility.
*   The connection to a formal probabilistic model could be strengthened.
*   The reasons for the 2-modality model’s underperformance require further investigation and discussion.

**Specific Questions/Suggestions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide more details on the learned values of the linear coefficients in Equation (1)? Does the weighting vary across modalities and potential orders?
*   Could you elaborate on the observed differences between the sequential MCB and MCT decision-making modules (beyond the brief mention in Section 4.3)? Were any results obtained that could be shared?
*   Please clarify the relationship between the proposed approach and existing bilinear models [13].
*   Consider revising Figure 2 to focus on the unary/pairwise/ternary potential generation process.
*   Please explicitly reference Figure 3 in the text.
*   Provide a more detailed explanation for why the 2-modality model underperforms MCB [7].



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed approach is novel, the results are strong, and the experimental evaluation is thorough. However, the weaknesses in writing clarity and the lack of detailed explanation for certain results prevent me from recommending unconditional acceptance at this time. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon addressing the concerns raised above, particularly improving the clarity of the presentation and providing a more in-depth analysis of the 2-modality model’s performance.
