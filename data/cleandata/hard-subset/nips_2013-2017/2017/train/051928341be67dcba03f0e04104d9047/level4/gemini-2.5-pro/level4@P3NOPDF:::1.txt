This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, framed as a combination of unary, pairwise, and trinary interactions between different data modalities. The model factorizes the final attention into these components, which are then combined to produce a final result. The approach is evaluated on the VQA task, where it achieves strong results. The qualitative analysis, particularly the visualization of attention maps, suggests that these performance gains are attributable to the proposed mechanism rather than simply an increase in model capacity.

Overall, the proposed method is interesting and the empirical results are compelling. However, the paper's presentation could be significantly improved for clarity, and the novelty and contributions could be more sharply defined in relation to prior work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Effective Model and Strong Results:** The proposed model is conceptually interesting and achieves strong empirical results on a challenging benchmark. The factorization of attention into different orders of interaction is an intuitive and powerful idea.
*   **Thorough Experimentation:** The paper provides a comprehensive set of experiments, including both quantitative performance benchmarks and insightful qualitative analyses. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component of the model.
*   **Insightful Qualitative Analysis:** The qualitative visualizations of the attention maps are particularly effective in demonstrating how the model operates. The comparison between the initial unary/pairwise attentions and the final combined attention provides compelling evidence that the performance gains stem from a more refined attention mechanism.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The primary weakness of the submission lies in its presentation. The writing is occasionally unclear and jumbled, which makes it difficult to fully grasp the technical details of the proposed architecture. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to improve its narrative flow and clarity.
*   **Positioning and Novelty:** While the proposed architecture outperforms prior work like MCB [7], its novelty could be better articulated. The core idea appears to be a logical extension of the multimodal factorization concept from MCB to include lower-order (unary) and higher-order (trinary) interactions. While this is a valid and effective contribution, the authors should more clearly delineate how their approach moves beyond this extension and what new principles it introduces.
*   **Weak Probabilistic Connection:** The connection to a probabilistic framework, mentioned as motivation, is not developed formally. As it stands, it serves more as an analogy than a rigorous foundation. The authors should either formalize this connection (e.g., by deriving the model from a specific probabilistic graphical model) or reframe this section to clarify that it is purely for intuition.
*   **Clarity of Figures:** The figures could be improved for clarity.
    *   Figure 2 is presented at a challenging level of abstraction; it is neither a high-level overview nor a detailed schematic, making it difficult to follow the data flow and component interactions. A simplified diagram emphasizing the generation and combination of unary, pairwise, and trinary potentials would be more effective.
    *   Figure 3 appears to be unreferenced in the main text, making its purpose unclear.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  Regarding Equation (1), what were the learned values for the linear coefficients that combine the marginalized potentials? It would be interesting to see if different modalities or task types rely more heavily on certain orders of potentials.
2.  The paper reports that the "2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise" model underperforms the original MCB model [7], which is surprising given their architectural similarities. The text lacks a discussion of this result. Could the authors provide some intuition? For instance, is this performance drop related to the switch from MCB to MCT for the decision module, or are other factors at play?
3.  The authors mention exploring the use of sequential MCB layers versus a single MCT layer for the decision head. This is an interesting architectural choice, but no corresponding results are presented. Could the authors elaborate on their observations from these experiments?
4.  The qualitative results for unary and pairwise attention are insightful. Were marginalized trinary attention maps also generated? If so, including them in the main text or supplement could further strengthen the qualitative analysis and the justification for the trinary component.
