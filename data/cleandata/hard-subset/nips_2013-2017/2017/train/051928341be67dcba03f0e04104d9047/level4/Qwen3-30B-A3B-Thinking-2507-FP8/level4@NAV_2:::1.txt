

The paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism that models k-way correlations across multiple data modalities (e.g., visual, textual, and answer representations) for visual question answering (VQA). It extends existing attention frameworks by formalizing unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to derive attention distributions, and applying multimodal compact trilinear pooling (MCT) for decision making. The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on VQA, with qualitative analysis demonstrating how attention mechanisms align with question semantics.  

**Strengths:**  
- The high-order attention framework provides a principled probabilistic extension beyond prior work (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]), with clear theoretical grounding in modeling multi-modal dependencies.  
- Comprehensive experiments validate the model’s effectiveness: the 3-modality architecture outperforms 2-modality baselines and achieves SOTA results (64.57% on VQA test-std) with fewer parameters (40M vs. >70M in [7]).  
- Qualitative analysis effectively illustrates how unary/pairwise attention integrates with question context (Fig. 5), and the attention maps align with human interpretation (Fig. 7).  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Clarity:** The exposition is occasionally disjointed (e.g., Section 3.2’s equation-heavy presentation obscures the probabilistic intuition). Figure 2 lacks sufficient architectural detail to trace potential generation pathways, and Figure 3 is unexplained in the text.  
- **Originality:** The contribution appears incremental—extending MCB [7] to higher-order correlations (ternary) and lower-order (unary) potentials largely replicates existing intuition without deeper theoretical novelty. The authors should explicitly clarify how this framework fundamentally differs from prior multi-modal attention (e.g., Kim et al. [14] or Nam et al. [19]).  
- **Probabilistic Formalism:** The connection between the attention mechanism and probabilistic modeling is motivational rather than formal (e.g., "smax" in Eq. 1 lacks justification as a mean-field approximation). The authors should either formalize the inference or temper the probabilistic claims.  
- **Missing Analysis:** The underperformance of the 2-modality unary+pairwise model versus MCB [7] (Table 1) warrants deeper discussion—was this due to architectural differences (e.g., MCB vs. MCT) or embedding dimensionality? Additionally, the qualitative analysis omits ternary attention maps (Fig. 6 only shows 2-modality), despite their relevance to the core contribution.  

**Specific Suggestions:**  
1. Revise Figure 2 to explicitly highlight unary/pairwise/ternary potential generation (e.g., by annotating the correlation units in Fig. 4).  
2. Clarify the relationship between this work and [7] in the introduction (e.g., "While MCB [7] pools pairwise features, we model k-way interactions via high-order potentials").  
3. Report learned coefficients (α₁–α₅, β₁–β₅, γ₁–γ₅) in Eq. 1 to demonstrate modality-specific reliance on potential types (e.g., "Question attention heavily weights θ_Q and θ_Q,A").  
4. Include ternary attention visualizations (as in Fig. 5) to validate the 3-modality model’s interpretability.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper presents a technically sound, empirically strong approach that advances VQA performance through a well-structured attention framework. While the novelty is modest, the results are compelling, and the method is generally applicable beyond VQA. With revisions addressing clarity, formalization, and comparative analysis, this work would make a strong contribution. The significance is high due to its potential to generalize to other multi-modal tasks.
