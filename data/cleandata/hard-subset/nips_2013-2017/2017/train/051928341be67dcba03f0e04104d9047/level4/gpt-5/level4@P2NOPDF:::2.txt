This paper introduces a generic attention framework for Visual Question Answering (VQA) that jointly models attention over image, question, and answer. The core contribution is a unified formulation using unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities. The method is evaluated on the VQA dataset and achieves state-of-the-art results when ternary potentials are included.

Strengths:
1. The proposed multimodal attention formulation is task-agnostic and potentially applicable beyond VQA, which increases its value and impact.
2. The ablation study quantifying the effect of removing ternary potentials is helpful for understanding where the gains come from.
3. The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses and questions:
1. Attention over answers is underspecified. Most answers in VQA are single tokens, and even multiword answers are typically treated as atomic units. What does answer-side attention represent in this setting? The paper would benefit from explicit discussion and visualizations illustrating answer attention.
2. Table 1 suggests that most of the improvement stems from the ternary potential. Without it, the model does not clearly outperform strong two-modality baselines (with the exception of HieCoAtt). Please analyze why the two-modality version lags behind existing methods and provide quantitative evidence supporting the claimed advantages.
3. Given that ternary interactions drive the gains, a direct comparison to models that also incorporate answers as inputs (e.g., “Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines,” Jabri et al., ECCV 2016) is important for context.
4. The paper lacks an analysis of failure cases. Discussing typical errors or failure modes would provide useful guidance for future work and help clarify the limits of the proposed formulation.
5. Minor issues:
   a. Line 38: “mechanism” should be “mechanisms.”
   b. Line 237 states that evaluation is on the validation set, but Table 1 reports results on test-dev and test-std. Please resolve this inconsistency.

Post-rebuttal comments:
While the rebuttal did not fully address the concern about the two-modality setup (the response lacks compelling quantitative support), I am inclined to recommend acceptance. The proposed framework is a principled and general approach to multimodal attention, and the ternary (three-modality) model delivers strong, state-of-the-art results. The additional evaluation comparing the model’s attention maps to human attention maps is also encouraging; the reported correlations exceed those of prior work (at least relative to HieCoAtt). Even though correlations for leading baselines like MCB were not provided, the evidence suggests a meaningful improvement.

Clarification request on authors’ rebuttal (“MCB vs. MCT”):
The rebuttal states: “MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.”
- Are these numbers reversed? Intuitively, 3-MCT should not underperform a 2-layer MCB if the main paper claims gains from ternary potentials.
- Additionally, Table 1 reports MCB at 68.6% overall. Please clarify which MCB configuration corresponds to 69.4% and reconcile this with the table and the 68.4% figure for 3-MCT.
