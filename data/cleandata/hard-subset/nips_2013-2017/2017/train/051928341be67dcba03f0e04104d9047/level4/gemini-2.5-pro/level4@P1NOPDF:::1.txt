**General Impression**

Overall, this paper proposes an interesting method that achieves strong results. The provided attention maps suggest these performance gains may stem from the improved attention mechanisms rather than simply from increased model capacity. However, I found the writing to be occasionally unclear, which sometimes made the core contributions difficult to parse. With revisions to improve clarity, I believe this could be a stronger submission.

**Strengths**

*   The paper presents a compelling model that achieves impressive results on the given tasks.
*   The authors conduct a thorough set of qualitative and quantitative experiments to validate their approach.
*   The model's ability to shift between different orders of attention (unary, pairwise, and trinary) is a key strength and appears to be effective.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The presentation is sometimes convoluted, which hinders a clear understanding of the paper's contributions. In particular, Figure 2 strikes a difficult balance: it is not detailed enough to fully explain the network's functionality, yet it is not abstract enough to provide a clear, high-level overview of the approach. A simplified figure that more clearly emphasizes the generation of unary, pairwise, and trinary potentials would be more effective. Additionally, I could not find any reference to Figure 3 in the main text.

*   **Novelty:** The novelty of the work appears somewhat limited. While the proposed architecture is more complex and outperforms the MCB model from [7], much of this additional complexity seems to stem from extending the core intuition of [7] to higher (trinary) and lower (unary) orders. This is not a major flaw, but the authors should more clearly delineate their specific contributions relative to prior work.

*   **Probabilistic Connection:** The connection to probabilistic models is not well-established. The link is presented more as a motivation than a formal framework. The authors should either formalize this connection or adjust the language to clarify its primarily motivational role.

**Questions for the Authors**

*   It would be beneficial to visualize the marginalized trinary attention maps. Were these included in the text or supplementary material and I missed them?
*   What were the learned values for the linear coefficients used to combine the marginalized potentials in Equation (1)? It would be interesting to see if different modalities rely more heavily on different orders of potentials.
*   It is surprising that the 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise model underperforms the MCB model [7], despite having a similar architecture. This result was not discussed in the text, and I would appreciate the authors' intuition on this. Could this performance drop be related to the switch from MCB to MCT in the decision computation modules?
*   The discussion on using sequential MCB layers versus a single MCT layer for the decision head was interesting, but no empirical results were provided. Could the authors elaborate on what was observed in this comparison?
