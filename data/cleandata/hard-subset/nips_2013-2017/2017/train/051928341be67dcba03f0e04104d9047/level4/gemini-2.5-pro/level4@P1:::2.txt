This paper introduces a novel, generalized framework for modeling attention over multiple data modalities. The core idea is to represent attention as a combination of unary, pairwise, and higher-order potentials. The authors apply this framework to the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA) by modeling interactions between the image, question, and answer modalities using up to third-order (ternary) potentials. When evaluated on the standard VQA dataset, the proposed model, particularly the version incorporating ternary potentials, achieves state-of-the-art performance.

**Strengths:**

1.  The proposed framework provides a generic and extensible formulation for multimodal attention. This is a valuable contribution, as it is not tailored to a specific task and could potentially be applied to other problems involving more than two modalities.
2.  The ablation study comparing the performance of the 2-modality (pairwise) model against the 3-modality (ternary) model is effective. It clearly demonstrates the significant contribution of the proposed ternary potentials to the model's overall performance.
3.  The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  The concept of "attention over answers" is not well-justified. Since most answers in the VQA dataset are single words (and multi-word answers are embedded as single n-grams), it is unclear what benefit an attention mechanism provides in this modality. The paper would be strengthened by a clearer explanation of this component and by providing visualizations of the answer attention maps, similar to those provided for the image and question.
2.  The results in Table 1 suggest that the performance gain comes almost entirely from the inclusion of the ternary potential. The 2-modality version of the model does not appear to outperform most prior state-of-the-art methods that also use two modalities (with the exception of HieCoAtt). This raises questions about the novelty and effectiveness of the unary and pairwise components of the framework in isolation. The authors should elaborate on why their 2-modality baseline is not more competitive.
3.  Given that the ternary potential involving the answer modality is crucial to the model's success, a comparison with other methods that also use answers as an input seems necessary. For instance, the baseline model in "Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines" (Jabri et al., ECCV 2016) should be included as a point of comparison.
4.  While Figure 8 provides one example of a failure case, a broader analysis of common failure modes would be insightful for guiding future research.
5.  Minor errors/typos:
    a. In the introduction (second paragraph), the phrasing "present day attention mechanisms... are however often lacking" should be corrected for grammar, perhaps to "However, present-day attention mechanisms are often lacking...". Please check for similar minor grammatical issues.
    b. There is a point of confusion regarding the evaluation datasets. The quantitative evaluation section mentions training on the 'train+val' set for the main results in Table 1 (reported on test-dev/std), but also mentions evaluating on the 'val' set when training on 'train' for an ablation study (MCT vs. 2-layer MCB). Please clarify which data splits are used for each reported result, including the qualitative examples, to avoid ambiguity.
