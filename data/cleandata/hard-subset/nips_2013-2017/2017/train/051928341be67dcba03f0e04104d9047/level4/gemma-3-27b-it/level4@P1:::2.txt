The paper presents a novel method to model attention for visual question answering (VQA), proposing a generic formulation of attention for multiple modalities—a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer. The approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and achieves state-of-the-art performance when utilizing ternary potentials.

**Strengths:**

1.  The generic formulation of attention for multimodal problems is a valuable contribution, particularly its potential for generalization beyond specific tasks.
2.  The ablation study demonstrating the impact of ternary potentials provides insight into the effectiveness of the proposed components.
3.  The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  The inclusion of attention over answers is unclear. Given that answers often consist of single words, or are treated as such, the benefit of attending *to* the answer representation itself isn’t fully explained. The paper lacks visualizations illustrating attention focused on answer elements. Clarification and visualization would be beneficial.
2.  The results in Table 1 suggest that the primary performance gain stems from the ternary potentials. Without them, the proposed model doesn’t consistently outperform existing two-modality models (except HieCoAtt). Further discussion regarding this observation is warranted.
3.  Since the ternary potential appears critical for performance improvement, a comparison with existing models that also incorporate answer information—such as Jabri et al. (ECCV16)—would strengthen the evaluation.
4.  The paper lacks a discussion of failure cases. Analyzing these cases would provide valuable insights for future research directions.
5.  Minor errors/typos:
    a. L38: “mechanism” should be “mechanisms.”
    b. L237 states the evaluation is on the validation set, while Table 1 presents results for the test-dev and test-std sets.

**Post-rebuttal comments:**

While the authors’ response to the concern regarding the two-modality performance doesn’t fully satisfy due to a lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a valuable generic attention framework for multiple modalities and demonstrates state-of-the-art results with three-modality attention. The quantitative evaluation of the proposed model’s attention maps against human attention maps (reported in the rebuttal) is encouraging and suggests a stronger correlation than existing models. Although the correlation value for state-of-the-art models like MCB isn’t provided for comparison, the observed improvement over HieCoAtt is significant. 

I have a question regarding the authors’ response:

> Authors’ response – “MCB vs. MCT”: MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.

Are the numbers swapped here? 69.4% should be for 3-MCT, correct? Additionally, the MCB performance in Table 1 is 68.6%; clarification on the 68.4% figure would be appreciated.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and generic approach to multimodal attention.
*   State-of-the-art performance on VQA with ternary potentials.
*   Promising alignment with human attention patterns (as shown in the rebuttal).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited improvement over existing methods without ternary potentials.
*   Lack of comparison to related work that incorporates answer information.
*   Missing analysis of failure cases.
