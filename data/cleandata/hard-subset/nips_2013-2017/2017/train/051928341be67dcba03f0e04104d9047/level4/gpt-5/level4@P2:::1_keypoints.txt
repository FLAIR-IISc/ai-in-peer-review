- Proposes a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning (unary, pairwise, ternary potentials) applied to VQA.  
- Defines attention via learned linear combinations of marginalized potentials followed by softmax, interpreted as a single mean-field iteration.  
- Combines attended modality vectors using tensor-sketch pooling: MCB for pairwise and MCT (or cascaded MCB) for ternary fusion.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art multiple-choice VQA performance with fewer parameters than some baselines.  
- Builds on prior co-attention and bilinear pooling work ([15], [7]) and extends attention to k-th order cross-modal interactions.  
- Technical implementation is mostly sound: learned embeddings, correlation operators, and tensor sketching are appropriate.  
- Probabilistic/mean-field framing is motivational but not formally derived (missing energy, independence assumptions, convergence analysis).  
- Learned mixing coefficients for combining potentials (Eq. 1) are not reported or analyzed.  
- Some ablations are present (MCT vs stacked MCB), but key comparisons are underdeveloped (e.g., why 2-modality Unary+Pairwise underperforms MCB [7]).  
- Presentation is uneven: dense sections, suboptimal figures (Fig. 2 too abstract/ambiguous, Fig. 3 introduced but not referenced).  
- Ternary attention visualizations are scarce; more marginal maps are needed to illustrate answer influence.  
- Main novelty is explicit high-order potential framing and mean-field interpretation; much of the mechanism reuses known components.  
- Empirical gains are modest but consistent; approach is practical and potentially generalizable beyond VQA.  
- Strength: clear, general formulation of attention as high-order potentials across modalities.  
- Strength: strong empirical results with qualitative visualizations supporting higher-order attention meaningfulness.  
- Strength: sensible use of tensor sketching to enable tractable high-order interactions.  
- Strength: includes ablations on decision fusion and comparisons to prior methods.  
- Weakness: probabilistic interpretation is not formalized and reads as heuristic.  
- Weakness: limited novelty relative to existing bilinear/co-attention methods—mainly an extension to higher order.  
- Weakness: missing analyses (combination weights, reasons for 2-modality underperformance).  
- Weakness: presentation issues—figure clarity, missing references, and few ternary maps.  
- Questions: report learned Eq. (1) coefficients and their variability across runs.  
- Questions: why does Unary+Pairwise underperform MCB—embedding dims, training differences, or decision module?  
- Questions: provide numerical comparisons and discussion on when stacked MCB outperforms MCT.  
- Questions: show ternary attention marginals to illustrate answer influence on image/question attention.  
- Suggestions: tighten exposition (Sec. 3), simplify Fig. 2 to highlight potential pipeline and Eq. (1) combination.  
- Suggestions: explicitly reference/use Fig. 3 and add ternary attention visualizations.  
- Suggestions: report combination weights and run ablations isolating unary/pairwise/ternary contributions.  
- Overall: solid, well-executed extension of co-attention to higher-order interactions with recommendation to accept pending clearer probabilistic treatment and fuller analyses.
