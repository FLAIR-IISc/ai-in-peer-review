General Impressions:

Overall, I think the proposed method is interesting. The results are quite good, and the attention maps suggest that these benefits are not only due to increased model capacity but also due to improved attention mechanisms. I found the writing to be a bit weak and sometimes a bit confusing, although I think the author could reasonably improve the presentation with more time.

Strengths:

- Interesting model with good results.
- Comprehensive qualitative and quantitative experiments.
- I am quite impressed with the switch between one-dimensional/ pairwise attention and final attention. Although I would have liked to see the marginalized triplet attention maps somewhere. Did I remember these in the text or supplement?

Weaknesses:

- As I said above, I found the writing/presentation to be a bit disorganized at times.
- Novelty seems a bit limited here. Undoubtedly, the architecture is more complex than the MCB for the VQA model [7] and performs better, but most of this additional complexity is repeating the intuition of the higher (triplet) and lower (one-dimensional) orders [7]. I don't think this is a major problem, but I would suggest that the author clarify these contributions (and any contributions I missed).
- I don't think the probabilistic relationship is drawn very well. It seems not to be formally structured enough to be considered anything more than a motivator, which is fine, but I would suggest that the author either strengthen this relationship more formally or adjust the language to be clearer.
- Figure 2 is at a strange level of abstraction where it is not detailed enough to understand the functionality of the network, but also not abstract enough to easily grasp the outline of the approach. I would suggest simplifying this figure to more clearly emphasize the one-dimensional/pairwise/triplet probabilistic generation.
- Figure 3 is never mentioned unless I remembered it.

Some things I am curious about:

- What values were learned for the linear coefficients to combine the marginalized probabilities in equations (1)? It would be interesting if different modalities leveraged different probabilistic orders.
- I find it interesting that the 2-way one-dimensional+pairwise model performs worse than the MCB [7], despite having similar architecture. I was disappointed that there wasn't much discussion about this in the text. Is there any intuition in this result? Does it relate to the swap in the MCB/MCT decision computing module?
- The discussion on using sequential MCB versus single MCT layers for the decision head was quite interesting, but no results were shown. Could the author explain a bit about what was observed?
