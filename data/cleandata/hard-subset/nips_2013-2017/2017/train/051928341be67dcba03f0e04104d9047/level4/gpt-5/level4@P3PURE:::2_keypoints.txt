- Proposes a generic, task-agnostic multimodal attention formulation combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer modalities.
- Evaluated on VQA and achieves state-of-the-art performance when ternary potentials are included.
- Strength: generic multimodal attention could generalize beyond VQA.
- Strength: ablation study removing the ternary potential quantifies its contribution.
- Strength: paper is clearly written.
- Weakness: unclear what “attention over answers” means in practice; lacks definition, learning details, and visualizations/examples for answer attention.
- Weakness: most gains appear driven by the ternary potential; without it the model largely does not outperform two-modality baselines—needs explanation and quantitative evidence isolating architectural/training differences.
- Weakness: should compare against prior models that take answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., ECCV’16).
- Weakness: no analysis of failure cases or typical error modes.
- Weakness: typos and inconsistencies (e.g., “mechanism”→“mechanisms”; discrepancy between line 237 stating validation evaluation and Table 1 reporting test-dev/test-std).
- Post-rebuttal: reviewer tentatively recommends acceptance despite limited quantitative rebuttal, citing the compelling framework and strong three-modality results.
- Post-rebuttal: reported quantitative alignment of model attention with human attention is encouraging, though comparisons to stronger SOTA like MCB are missing.
- Post-rebuttal: requests clarification and consistency for reported MCB vs. MCT numbers (68.4% vs. 69.4% vs. Table 1’s 68.6%).
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on clarifying the answer-attention mechanism, reconciling reported numbers (MCB vs MCT/Table 1), and adding failure-case discussion.
