- Proposes a unified probabilistic multimodal attention framework modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials with a mean-field marginalization step.  
- Applies the framework to VQA with modalities V (image), Q (question), and A (answer candidates), and uses MCB/MCT for fused decision making.  
- Novelty: explicitly models higher-order (ternary) attention dependencies in a probabilistic, extensible way compared to prior co-/co-attention and bilinear fusion methods.  
- Strength: general, modular attention formulation that conceptually scales to arbitrary numbers of modalities and is not tied to VQA.  
- Strength: clear description of unary/pairwise/ternary potentials and learnable reliability weights for combining them.  
- Strength: competitive state-of-the-art VQA results with ablations showing benefit from ternary potentials.  
- Strength: empirical comparison of MCT vs multi-stage MCB for fusion and useful qualitative attention visualizations; code released.  
- Weakness: unclear meaning and utility of “answer attention” (PA); needs clarification, visualizations, and discussion of whether A just reweights candidates.  
- Weakness: 2-modality variant underperforms strong baselines (e.g., MCB); limited controlled evidence that differences aren’t due to embedding/backbone/regularization.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison to “answers-as-input” baselines (e.g., Jabri et al.) to isolate the effect of conditioning on A vs true high-order attention.  
- Weakness: inconsistency in metric reporting (validation vs test-dev/test-std) and unclear whether results are single-model or ensembles.  
- Weakness: potential computational and memory cost for computing/marginalizing the ternary tensor C3; runtime/memory overhead not reported.  
- Weakness: limited failure analysis and examples showing when ternary attention helps or hurts and whether it biases toward frequent/unusual answers.  
- Quality: sound technical ideas with coherent probabilistic interpretation and single-step mean-field update; experiments thorough but empirical isolation of contributions is weak.  
- Clarity: overall clear and reproducible, but needs clarification on PA, dataset split reporting, and minor typos/equation issues.  
- Originality: modeling attention via explicit higher-order potentials and mean-field marginalization is novel and useful relative to existing methods.  
- Significance: advances VQA in the three-modality setting and could transfer to other multimodal tasks; broader validation on additional datasets would strengthen claims.  
- Suggestions: add comparisons to answer-conditioned baselines, matched 2-modality baselines with controlled capacity/backbone, report compute/memory costs, and include more failure analyses.  
- Post-rebuttal: request reporting of human-attention correlation for strong baselines (e.g., MCB) and clarification/reconciliation of conflicting MCB vs MCT accuracy numbers.  
- Pros (acceptance): conceptually clean, extensible framework; strong ternary-interaction gains; clear exposition, visualizations, and released code.  
- Cons (against acceptance): weak 2-modality competitiveness, missing direct answer-conditioned comparisons, reporting inconsistencies, and evaluation limited to one benchmark.  
- Recommendation: Accept, citing principled high-order attention framework and demonstrated benefits despite some empirical and reporting gaps.
