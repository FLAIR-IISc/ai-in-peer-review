This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism for Visual Question Answering (VQA), formulated as a generic approach applicable to multimodal problems. The core contribution lies in representing attention using a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across the image, question, and answer modalities. Evaluated on the VQA dataset, the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance when leveraging these ternary potentials.

**Strengths:**

1. The proposed attention formulation’s generality is a significant strength. A task-agnostic approach to multimodal attention has the potential for broader applicability beyond VQA.
2. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the importance of the ternary potentials, clearly illustrating their contribution to the model’s performance.
3. The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The inclusion of the answer as a modality for attention is unclear. Given that answers frequently consist of single words (or are treated as such even when multi-word), the practical effect of attending *to* the answer is not well-justified. The paper lacks visualizations of this attention mechanism, hindering understanding. Clarification and visual examples are needed.
2. The results in Table 1 suggest that the primary performance gain stems from the ternary potentials. Without them, the proposed model does not consistently outperform existing two-modality approaches (with the exception of HieCoAtt). The authors should provide further insight into this observation.
3. Considering the importance of ternary potentials, a comparison to models that explicitly incorporate the answer as input – such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16) – would be valuable.
4. The paper lacks a discussion of failure cases. Analyzing scenarios where the model performs poorly would provide valuable insights and guide future research.
5. Minor errors/typos were noted:
    a. Line 38: “mechanism” should be “mechanisms”.
    b. Line 237 states evaluation was performed on the validation set, but Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets. This discrepancy should be addressed.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**

Despite a somewhat unsatisfying response to the concern regarding performance without ternary potentials – lacking quantitative supporting evidence – I recommend acceptance. The paper’s strength lies in its presentation of a generic attention framework for multiple modalities and the demonstrated state-of-the-art performance achieved with the three-modality attention mechanism. The reported quantitative evaluation of attention maps against human attention maps in the rebuttal is encouraging, suggesting a stronger correlation than existing models like HieCoAtt. While the correlation value for state-of-the-art models such as MCB remains unknown, the improvement over HieCoAtt appears significant.

**Question for Authors:**

Regarding your rebuttal response concerning “MCB vs. MCT”: You state “Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%.” This seems reversed; 69.4% should correspond to the 3-MCT setup. Furthermore, the overall MCB performance reported in Table 1 is 68.6%. Please clarify which MCB performance number (68.4% or 68.6%) you are referencing.
