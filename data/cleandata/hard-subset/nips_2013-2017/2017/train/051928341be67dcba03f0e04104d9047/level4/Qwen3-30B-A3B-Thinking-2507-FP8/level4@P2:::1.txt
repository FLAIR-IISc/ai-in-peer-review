

This paper proposes a high-order attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically applied to visual question answering (VQA). The core idea extends standard attention models by explicitly modeling k-th order correlations between data modalities (e.g., image, question, answer), learning unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to guide attention. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art VQA performance using this approach, with qualitative analysis showing attention maps align with task-relevant regions. This work builds on prior attention mechanisms in VQA (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) but generalizes their framework to higher-order interactions, contrasting with existing methods that typically handle only pairwise or single-modality attention.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The experimental evaluation is thorough, with strong quantitative results on standard VQA benchmarks (test-dev/test-std) outperforming prior work (e.g., [7]) despite a smaller model (40M parameters vs. 70M+). The qualitative analysis (Figs. 5–7) effectively illustrates how unary/pairwise potentials contribute to attention, and the ablation study (3-modality vs. 2-modality) validates the necessity of high-order modeling. The tensor sketch-based decision module (MCT/MCB) is well-motivated and implemented consistently.  
*Originality:* The extension to k-th order attention (beyond pairwise) is a meaningful conceptual contribution, distinct from prior work that treats attention as a single network ([15, 25, 7]). The probabilistic formulation using mean-field inference for marginalizing potentials provides a clear, generalizable framework applicable to any number of modalities.  
*Significance:* Achieving SOTA performance with a simpler model (vs. complex baselines) demonstrates practical value for multimodal reasoning. The approach opens avenues for modeling richer cross-modal interactions in other tasks (e.g., captioning, visual grounding).

**Weaknesses:**  
*Clarity:* The writing occasionally obscures the core contribution. The methodology section (Sec. 3) is dense and lacks clear progression—e.g., Eq. (1) combines potentials without explaining *why* linear coefficients (αᵢ, βᵢ, γᵢ) are needed or how they differ across modalities. Figure 2’s abstraction level is confusing: it’s too detailed to grasp the high-level idea but too vague to clarify the architecture. Figure 3 is introduced but never referenced in the text, weakening the narrative.  
*Originality & Novelty:* The novelty is overstated relative to [7] (which uses MCB for pairwise interactions). The paper essentially generalizes [7]’s intuition to higher orders (ternary) and lower orders (unary), with limited discussion of *why* this extension is non-trivial beyond empirical gains. The probabilistic connection (Sec. 3.2) is underdeveloped—presented as motivation but not formalized as a key theoretical contribution.  
*Technical Depth:* The analysis of why the 2-modality unary+pairwise model underperforms MCB ([7]) is missing despite its importance. The authors note a 0.75% gap but offer no intuition (e.g., is it due to the decision module shift from MCB to MCT?). Similarly, the 2-layer MCB vs. MCT comparison (Sec. 4.2) lacks results, making the claim about "greater expressiveness" unsubstantiated. The absence of trinary attention maps (e.g., for answers) in qualitative results limits validation of the ternary potential design.

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept** (with minor revisions). The paper makes a solid contribution to attention modeling for multimodal tasks, with strong empirical results and a clear generalization of prior work. The weaknesses are addressable through minor clarifications:  
1. *Revise Figures 2–3* to simplify abstraction (e.g., focus on potential generation flow) and explicitly reference Fig. 3.  
2. *Clarify the probabilistic framework* in Sec. 3.2 (e.g., state whether mean-field inference is optimal or approximate).  
3. *Add analysis* for the 2-modality performance gap and trinary attention maps (if feasible in revision).  
4. *Refine novelty discussion* to explicitly contrast with [7]’s MCB and clarify the theoretical value of k-th order potentials beyond empirical gains.  

The work is technically sound, significant for VQA, and aligns well with NIPS’ focus on principled advances in machine learning. The minor clarity issues do not undermine the core contribution.
