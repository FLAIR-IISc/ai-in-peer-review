

The paper introduces a novel attention mechanism for Visual Question Answering (VQA) by formulating a generic multimodal attention framework incorporating unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities (image, question, answer). The approach demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset when ternary potentials are employed.  

**Strengths:**  
1. The generic multimodal attention formulation is broadly applicable beyond VQA, offering significant potential if validated across multiple tasks.  
2. The ablation study isolating the contribution of ternary potentials provides clear insight into their critical role in performance gains.  
3. The paper is well-structured and clearly written.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. The paper proposes attention over the answer modality, but it remains unclear what "attention over answers" entails. Most answers are single words (or treated as such), making visual interpretation of attention maps impractical. The absence of answer attention visualizations warrants clarification.  
2. Table 1 indicates that the proposed model without ternary potentials fails to outperform existing methods (except HieCoAtt) in the two-modality setup. The authors should elaborate on why ternary potentials are indispensable for competitive performance.  
3. Given that ternary potentials drive the primary performance gain, the paper should compare against models explicitly leveraging answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., *Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines*, ECCV 2016).  
4. The paper lacks discussion of failure cases, which would be valuable for identifying limitations and guiding future work.  
5. Typos/errors:  
   - L38: "mechanism" → "mechanisms"  
   - L237: Claims evaluation on validation set, but Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std results.  

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**  
While the authors’ response to the two-modality performance concern lacks quantitative support, I recommend acceptance due to the paper’s novel generic multimodal attention framework and the strong empirical results for the 3-modality setup. The rebuttal’s additional analysis—showing higher correlation between the model’s attention maps and human attention than existing methods (e.g., MCB vs. HieCoAtt)—is compelling. However, a critical inconsistency remains: the authors state "3-MCT yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%," but Table 1 reports MCB at 68.6% and MCT at 68.4%. This discrepancy requires clarification (e.g., whether the numbers refer to different model variants or datasets). The correlation value for SOTA models like MCB is not provided, but the rebuttal’s evidence still suggests MCT’s attention maps align better with human attention than HieCoAtt.  

**Final Recommendation:** Accept.
