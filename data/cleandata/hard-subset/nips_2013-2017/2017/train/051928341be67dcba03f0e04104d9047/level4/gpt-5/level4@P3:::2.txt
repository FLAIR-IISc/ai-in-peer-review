Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a unified, probabilistic framework for multimodal attention that models unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across modalities. In the context of VQA, the modalities are image (V), question (Q), and answer candidates (A). The model interprets attention as a set of learnable potentials: (i) unary (per-modality importance), (ii) pairwise (cross-modality correlations via similarity matrices), and (iii) ternary (three-way correlations via a correlation tensor). Marginals over each modality are obtained via a single mean-field step, with learnable weights that combine the potential types before a softmax. The attended vectors are then fused for decision making using compact tensor sketching: MCB for pairwise and MCT for trilinear pooling.

Compared to prior VQA attention work, such as HieCoAtt (Lu et al., NIPS’16), stacked/co-attention networks (Yang et al.; Xu & Saenko), dual attention (Nam et al.), and bilinear pooling (Kim et al.), the main contribution is to explicitly model higher-order attention dependencies in a probabilistically motivated, extensible way. Relative to MCB (Fukui et al.), which focuses on efficient multimodal fusion but treats attention largely as a single network, the proposed framework treats attention itself as a structured model over modalities. The paper also connects to structured attention networks (Kim et al.) and similarity-matrix-based co-attention (Xu & Saenko), but extends them to higher orders and multiple modalities with a straightforward mean-field formulation.

Strengths:
- General, modular formulation of attention that scales conceptually to an arbitrary number of modalities; not tied to VQA.
- Clear description of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, with an intuitive mean-field combination and learnable reliability weights.
- Competitive to state-of-the-art results on VQA with three-modality attention; ablations indicate the benefit of ternary potentials.
- Empirical comparison of MCT vs multi-stage MCB for decision fusion, with analysis of which performs best in practice.
- Useful qualitative visualizations of attention over image and question; code availability improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Meaning of “answer attention”: While the model defines attention over answer candidates, most answers are single tokens (or n-grams collapsed to single vectors). What does an “attention map” over answers convey beyond reweighting the candidate list? Please clarify the utility of PA, include visualizations, and discuss whether modeling A primarily helps via the ternary potential rather than a meaningful “spatial” or “sequential” attention.
- Two-modality competitiveness: The 2-modality version appears weaker than strong baselines (e.g., MCB), except against HieCoAtt. The rebuttal cites embedding size and tuning, but quantitative evidence is limited. Please add a controlled comparison matching backbone (ResNet vs VGG), embedding dimensionality, parameter count, and regularization to isolate the modeling contribution.
- Comparison to “answers-as-input” baselines: Since the gains are largely from the ternary potential, a comparison to models that also condition on answer candidates (e.g., Jabri et al., ECCV’16) is important to disentangle the effect of high-order attention from simply conditioning on A.
- Metric reporting inconsistency: The paper states validation evaluation in the text but reports test-dev/test-std in Table 1. Please reconcile datasets used for model selection vs final reporting. Also clarify whether numbers are from single-model or ensembles.
- Complexity and scalability: Computing and marginalizing C3 may be costly. Please report memory/runtime overhead of ternary potentials and any approximations (e.g., low-rank/projection) beyond tensor sketching in the classifier.
- Failure analysis: The paper includes limited discussion of failure modes. More examples would be helpful to diagnose when the ternary attention helps vs hurts, and whether answer attention over-emphasizes frequent or “unusual” answers.

Quality:
The technical ideas are sound and well-motivated. The probabilistic interpretation, single-step mean-field update, and learnable mixing of potentials are coherent. Experiments are thorough on VQA, with ablations over potential order and decision fusion. However, the 2-modality baseline underperformance and limited comparison to “answers-as-input” methods weaken the empirical isolation of the core contribution.

Clarity:
Overall clear and well-organized. The attention pipeline and decision fusion are explained with sufficient detail to reproduce. A few issues:
- Clarify PA’s interpretation and provide corresponding visualizations.
- Resolve dataset split inconsistencies (validation vs test-dev/test-std).
- Minor typos: “mechanism” → “mechanisms”; duplicated “and” in related work; in Eq. (attended modalities) the last term appears to redefine aV for answers—should be aA.

Originality:
Modeling attention via explicit higher-order potentials and mean-field marginalization is a novel and useful perspective relative to existing co-/bi-linear attention and fusion schemes. The approach integrates naturally with compact bilinear/trilinear pooling for decision making.

Significance:
The paper advances the state of the art on VQA in the three-modality setting and offers a broadly applicable formulation that could transfer to other multimodal tasks (e.g., captioning, VQG, text+audio). Demonstrating on an additional task or dataset (e.g., VQA v2, Visual7W) would further support generality.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide quantitative comparisons to Jabri et al. and other answer-conditioned baselines, controlling for architecture and parameters.
- Add matched 2-modality baselines (same embedding size/backbone) to assess the benefit of structured attention vs capacity.
- Report compute/memory cost for ternary potentials and offer approximations if needed.
- Include more failure cases and an analysis of common error types.
- Report correlations to human attention alongside results from strong baselines (e.g., MCB) for context.

Post-rebuttal comments and clarification:
The additional analysis correlating model attention with human attention is encouraging; please report the numbers for strong SOTA baselines (e.g., MCB) for proper context. Regarding the “MCB vs. MCT” remark: the rebuttal states 3-MCT yields 68.4% on test-dev and 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%, while Table 1 shows MCB at 68.6%. Are these numbers swapped, or are they from different training regimes/splits? Please correct and consolidate.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Conceptually clean, general, and extensible attention framework.
- Strong empirical gains when modeling ternary interactions.
- Clear exposition, code availability, and meaningful visualizations.
- Potential impact beyond VQA.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- Limited competitiveness of the 2-modality variant against strong baselines.
- Missing direct comparisons to answer-conditioned baselines.
- Some reporting inconsistencies and minor clarity issues.
- Evaluation confined to a single benchmark/task.

Recommendation:
Accept. Despite questions about the 2-modality baseline and reporting clarity, the paper introduces a principled, high-order attention framework with demonstrated benefits in a challenging multimodal setting. The contribution is likely to influence subsequent work on structured attention across modalities.
