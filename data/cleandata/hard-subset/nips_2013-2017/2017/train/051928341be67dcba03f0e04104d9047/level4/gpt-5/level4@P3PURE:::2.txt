This paper proposes a novel method for modeling attention over both images and questions for Visual Question Answering (VQA). Its main contribution is a generic attention formulation for multiple modalities that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities (image, question, and answer). The approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and outperforms existing models when ternary potentials are used.

Strengths:
- A generic, task-agnostic attention formulation for multimodal problems is valuable, especially if it can be shown to generalize beyond a single task.
- The ablation study that removes the ternary potential is helpful for quantifying its contribution.
- The paper is clearly written.

Weaknesses and questions:
- The paper claims to model attention over three modalities—image, question, and answer—but it is not clear what “attention over answers” means in practice. Most answers are single words, and even multiword answers are typically treated as a single token. The paper also lacks visualizations or qualitative analyses for answer attention. Please clarify how answer attention is defined, learned, and used, and provide illustrative examples or visualizations.
- From Table 1, most of the improvement appears to come from the ternary potential. Without the ternary term, the proposed model does not outperform existing two-modality baselines (except HieCoAtt). Please explain this gap and provide quantitative evidence to support your explanation (e.g., analyses isolating architectural or training differences, or capacity-controlled comparisons).
- Because the ternary potential seems to drive the gains, please compare against models that also take answers as inputs, such as “Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines” (Jabri et al., ECCV’16).
- The paper does not discuss failure cases. An analysis of typical failure modes would be valuable for guiding future work.
- Other errors/typos and inconsistencies:
  a. Line 38: “mechanism” → “mechanisms.”
  b. Line 237 states that evaluation is on the validation set, but Table 1 reports results on test-dev and test-std. Please reconcile this discrepancy.

Post-rebuttal comments:
- Although the response regarding the lack of improvement in the two-modality setting was not fully convincing due to limited quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance. The proposed generic attention framework for multiple modalities is compelling, and the three-modality results outperform the state of the art. The quantitative evaluation of the model’s attention maps against human attention maps (as reported in the rebuttal) is also encouraging and suggests higher correlation with human attention than prior models. While the correlation for stronger SOTA baselines such as MCB was not provided, the reported improvement over HieCoAtt appears substantial.
- Clarification request on the authors’ “MCB vs. MCT” response: You state that 3-MCT yields 68.4% on test-dev, while 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%, and that other multi-modality MCB variants underperform. Are these numbers swapped? Intuitively, 69.4% seems like it might correspond to 3-MCT. Also, Table 1 reports 68.6% for MCB overall, so it is unclear how the 68.4% figure aligns with the table. Please clarify which configurations these numbers correspond to and ensure consistency with Table 1.

Recommendation: Accept, contingent on clarifying the answer-attention mechanism, reconciling reported numbers (especially MCB vs. MCT), and adding discussion of failure cases.
