

This paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism designed to model complex interactions across multiple data modalities, with a focus on visual question answering (VQA). The authors propose a probabilistic framework that incorporates unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to capture correlations between image, question, and answer modalities. Unlike previous attention mechanisms that typically operate on two modalities (e.g., image and question), their approach explicitly models high-order interactions, which they demonstrate leads to state-of-the-art performance on the VQA benchmark.

**Quality** - The paper presents a technically sound method with thorough experimental validation. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the contribution of ternary potentials to overall performance, showing significant improvements over two-modality approaches. However, the explanation of "attention over answers" is insufficiently developed, as most answers are single words or simple phrases, making the interpretation of attention weights challenging. The paper also lacks a detailed analysis of failure cases, which would strengthen the evaluation. The experimental setup is well-documented, though there's a discrepancy in the reported validation set usage versus the test-dev/test-std results in Table 1.

**Clarity** - The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations of the proposed framework. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the visualizations (Fig. 5-7) effectively illustrate the attention mechanisms. The authors provide a helpful schematic of the high-order attention modules (Fig. 3), making the technical content accessible. The writing is organized logically, progressing from the problem statement to the proposed solution and experimental validation.

**Originality** - The core contribution of modeling high-order attention across multiple modalities is novel and generalizable beyond VQA. While previous work (e.g., Lu et al. [15], Fukui et al. [7]) has explored two-modality attention, the authors' probabilistic framework for k-order attention represents a significant conceptual advance. The approach differs from recent bilinear models (e.g., Kim et al. [13]) by providing a principled way to extend to any number of modalities. However, the paper could better articulate how this differs from the "dual attention" framework (Nam et al. [19]) and whether the high-order attention framework has been tested on tasks beyond VQA.

**Significance** - The demonstrated performance improvements on VQA (69.4% on test-dev with 3-modality attention) represent a meaningful advance over previous state-of-the-art (68.6% with MCB). The framework's potential applicability to other multimodal tasks (e.g., image captioning, visual reasoning) suggests broader significance. However, the paper would benefit from more discussion on how this approach could be applied to other domains beyond VQA, and whether the high-order attention framework can be integrated with recent advances in multimodal representation learning.

**Strengths:**
1. The high-order attention framework provides a principled, generalizable approach to multimodal attention beyond two-modal interactions.
2. The ablation study clearly demonstrates the contribution of ternary potentials to overall performance.
3. The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear explanations and helpful visualizations.
4. The results on VQA represent a meaningful improvement over previous state-of-the-art.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The concept of "attention over answers" is not adequately explained or visualized, particularly given that answers are typically single words or simple phrases.
2. The performance gain appears heavily dependent on the ternary potential, with the two-modality version not significantly outperforming existing models (except HieCoAtt).
3. The paper lacks comparison with approaches that incorporate answer information as input (e.g., Jabri et al. [9]), which would better contextualize the contribution.
4. The absence of failure case analysis limits the insights into the model's limitations.
5. There's a discrepancy between the reported validation set usage (L237) and the test-dev/test-std results in Table 1.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a technically sound, novel framework for high-order attention that demonstrates significant improvements on VQA. While the explanation of attention over answers needs clarification and additional comparisons would strengthen the contribution, the core framework represents a meaningful advance in multimodal attention modeling. The authors' rebuttal addressing the ternary potential's role (showing 69.4% with 3-modality MCB vs. 68.4% with MCT) is convincing, and the attention map correlation with human attention (0.68 vs. 0.55 for HieCoAtt) further validates the approach. The minor issues noted can be addressed through clarification in the final version.

The paper's contribution to multimodal attention modeling is significant, and its generalizable framework could inspire future work across various multimodal tasks. With the requested clarifications regarding answer attention and additional comparisons to answer-inclusive models, this work would make a strong contribution to the NIPS community.
