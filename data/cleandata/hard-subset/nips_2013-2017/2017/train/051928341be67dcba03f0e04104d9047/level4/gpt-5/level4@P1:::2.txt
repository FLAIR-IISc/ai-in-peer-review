The paper proposes a generic high-order attention mechanism for multimodal learning, instantiated for Visual Question Answering (VQA). The model defines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials over the image (V), question (Q), and answer (A) modalities, and performs a mean-field step to produce attention distributions over each. Attended features are then fused via compact bilinear/trilinear pooling (MCB/MCT) for answer prediction. Evaluated on the VQA dataset, the three-modality variant achieves state-of-the-art performance; ablations indicate that the ternary potential is the main driver of the gains. Qualitative examples show question-conditioned image attention; however, attention over answers is not visualized.

Strengths:
1. A generic, task-agnostic formulation of attention over multiple modalities (unary, pairwise, ternary) is a valuable contribution, especially if it can generalize beyond VQA.
2. The ablation removing ternary potentials helps quantify their contribution.
3. The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses:
1. Attention over answers: The model attends to image, question, and answers, but it is unclear what “attention over answers” means in practice, since most answers are single words and multi-word answers are embedded as a single vector. The paper does not visualize answer attention. Please clarify how answer attention is computed and interpreted, and include qualitative examples.
2. Two-modality performance: From Table 1, the proposed model without ternary potentials does not outperform existing two-modality baselines (except HieCoAtt). This suggests that the main improvement comes from the ternary potential. Please discuss why the two-modality instantiation lags behind comparable models and provide quantitative evidence (e.g., sensitivity to embedding dimension, architectural choices, or training hyperparameters) supporting your explanation.
3. Comparison to models that use answers as inputs: Since ternary potentials appear to be the key factor, please compare against prior work that also uses answers as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV 2016).
4. Failure analysis: The paper lacks discussion of failure cases. Qualitative analysis of typical errors and failure modes would be helpful for understanding limitations and guiding future work.
5. Typos/inconsistencies:
   - L38: “mechanism” → “mechanisms.”
   - L237 mentions evaluation on the validation set, while Table 1 reports test-dev and test-std numbers. Please reconcile.

Post-rebuttal comments:
I appreciate the additional results. Although the response regarding two-modality performance was not fully convincing (lacking supporting quantitative evidence), I recommend acceptance based on the generic multimodal attention framework and the strong three-modality results. The reported correlation of the model’s attention with human attention maps is encouraging and suggests improved interpretability. While the comparison to HieCoAtt is favorable, it would be helpful to also report this metric for strong SOTA baselines such as MCB.

A clarification question on the rebuttal’s numbers:
- Authors’ response (“MCB vs. MCT”): “MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.”
Are these numbers swapped? Intuitively, 3-MCT should be 69.4% if it outperforms 68.4%. Also, the overall MCB performance in Table 1 is 68.6%, which doesn’t match 68.4%. Please clarify which configuration corresponds to each number and ensure consistency with Table 1.

Overall assessment with respect to the review criteria:
- Quality: The method is technically sound, with a clear probabilistic formulation (unary/pairwise/ternary potentials, mean-field inference) and solid empirical results. However, the two-modality baseline underperforms relative to comparable work, and failure analysis is limited.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. Please clarify answer attention, dataset splits vs. reported metrics, and reconcile performance numbers in the rebuttal.
- Originality: The high-order attention formulation and its generality across modalities are novel and differentiable from prior co-attention and bilinear pooling approaches.
- Significance: The demonstrated gains from modeling ternary interactions are noteworthy for VQA and likely of interest for other multimodal tasks. The framework’s generality increases its potential impact.

Recommendation: Accept, contingent on clarifying answer attention, reconciling reported numbers (MCB vs. MCT and validation vs. test), and adding a brief failure analysis.
