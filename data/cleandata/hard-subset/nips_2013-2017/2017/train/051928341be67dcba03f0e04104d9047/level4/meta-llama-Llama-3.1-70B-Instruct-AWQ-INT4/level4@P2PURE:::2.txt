The paper proposes a novel method for modeling attention in Visual Question Answering (VQA) tasks, leveraging a generic formulation of attention that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities: image, question, and answer. The approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset, demonstrating improved performance over existing models when incorporating ternary potentials.

Strengths:

1. The paper presents a versatile attention formulation that can be applied to multimodal problems, offering potential generalizability across tasks.
2. The ablation study provides valuable insights into the contribution of ternary potentials to the model's performance.
3. The paper is well-written and clear in its presentation.

Weaknesses:

1. The concept of attention over answers is unclear, particularly given that most answers are single words or treated as single entities. The authors should clarify this aspect and provide visualizations to illustrate attention over answers.
2. The results in Table 1 suggest that the primary improvement in the proposed model stems from the ternary potential. Without this component, the model does not outperform existing models in the two-modalities setup (except for HieCoAtt). The authors should provide further explanation for this observation.
3. Given the significance of ternary potential in the proposed model's performance, a comparison with existing models that incorporate answers as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16), would be beneficial.
4. The paper lacks a discussion on failure cases, which would provide valuable insights for future research.
5. Minor errors and typos:
   a. Line 38: "mechanism" should be pluralized to "mechanisms."
   b. Line 237 mentions evaluation on the validation set, whereas Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets.

Post-rebuttal comments:

Although the authors' response to the concern about the proposed model not outperforming existing models in the two-modalities setup is not entirely satisfactory due to the lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance based on the paper's contribution of a generic attention framework for multiple modalities and the quantitative results demonstrating the superiority of three-modality attention over the state-of-the-art. The evaluation of the proposed model's attention maps against human attention maps, as reported in the rebuttal, appears promising, suggesting a stronger correlation with human maps than existing models. However, the correlation value for state-of-the-art models like MCB is unknown, and further comparison would be beneficial.

Regarding the authors' response to "MCB vs. MCT," it seems that the numbers might be swapped. Specifically, the 3-MCT setup should yield 69.4%, not 68.4%. Additionally, the reported MCB performance in Table 1 is 68.6%, which does not match the 68.4% mentioned in the response. Clarification on this point would be appreciated.
