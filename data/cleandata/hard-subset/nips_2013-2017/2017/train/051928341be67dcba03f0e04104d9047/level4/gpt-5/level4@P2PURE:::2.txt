The paper proposes a novel method for modeling attention over both the image and the question in Visual Question Answering (VQA). Its primary contribution is a generic multimodal attention formulation that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities (image, question, and answer). The approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and outperforms existing models when ternary potentials are included.

Strengths:
1. A task-agnostic, generic formulation of attention for multimodal problems is valuable, especially if it can be shown to generalize beyond a single task.
2. The ablation study that removes the ternary potential is helpful for quantifying its contribution.
3. The paper is clearly written.

Weaknesses:
1. The method claims to attend over three modalities—image, question, and answer—but it is unclear what “attention over answers” entails, given that most answers are single words and multiword answers are treated as single tokens. The paper also lacks visualizations of answer attention. Please clarify.
2. Based on Table 1, the main performance gain appears to come from the ternary potential. Without it, the proposed model does not outperform existing two-modality baselines (except HieCoAtt). Please elaborate on this.
3. Since the ternary potential seems to be the key driver of the performance improvement, the paper should compare against models that also use answers as inputs, such as “Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines” (Jabri et al., ECCV 2016).
4. The paper does not discuss failure cases. An analysis of failure modes would be informative and could guide future work.
5. Other errors/typos:
   a. L38: mechanism -> mechanisms
   b. L237 states that evaluation is on the validation set, but Table 1 reports test-dev and test-std results.

Post-rebuttal comments:
Although the authors’ response to the concern that the proposed model does not outperform existing models in the two-modality setting was unconvincing due to a lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance. The generic multimodal attention framework is a meaningful contribution, and the three-modality results outperform the state of the art. The quantitative evaluation of the model’s attention maps against human attention maps (reported in the rebuttal) is also encouraging and suggests higher correlation with human maps than existing models. While we do not know the corresponding correlation for stronger SOTA models such as MCB, it still appears significantly better than HieCoAtt.

I have a question regarding one of the authors’ responses:
> Authors’ response — “MCB vs. MCT”: MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.

Are these numbers swapped? Should 69.4% be for 3-MCT? Also, Table 1 reports an overall MCB performance of 68.6%, so it is unclear which number the authors are referencing when they report 68.4%.
