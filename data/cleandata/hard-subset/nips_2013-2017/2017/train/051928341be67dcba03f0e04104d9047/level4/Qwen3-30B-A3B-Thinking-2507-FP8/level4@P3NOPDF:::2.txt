

The paper introduces a novel multimodal attention framework for Visual Question Answering (VQA), proposing a generic formulation that integrates unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer modalities. The key contribution lies in extending attention mechanisms to explicitly model interactions among all three modalities, with empirical results demonstrating that the ternary potential component significantly boosts performance on the VQA benchmark, outperforming prior methods.  

**Strengths:**  
1. **Generality of the framework:** The proposed attention formulation is task-agnostic and could potentially generalize beyond VQA, offering a valuable foundation for multimodal reasoning.  
2. **Ablation study:** The thorough ablation (particularly the comparison without ternary potentials) effectively isolates the contribution of the core innovation.  
3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, facilitating understanding of the technical contributions.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Ambiguity in "attention over answers":** The paper treats answers as single tokens (or short sequences) but lacks clarity on what "attention over answers" entails operationally. Since answers are typically short and discrete, the interpretation of attention weights applied to them is unclear. Crucially, no visualizations of answer-focused attention maps are provided, making it difficult to assess the mechanism’s validity. The authors should clarify both the operational meaning and provide visual evidence.  
2. **Overreliance on ternary potentials:** As Table 1 shows, the proposed model’s gains are almost entirely attributable to the ternary potential. Without it, the model performs worse than most baselines (except HieCoAtt) in the two-modality setup. The authors must explain why the ternary component is so critical and whether the two-modality variant is inherently suboptimal.  
3. **Incomplete comparison with relevant baselines:** The ternary potential’s success warrants comparison with methods explicitly incorporating answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al.’s *Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines*). Current results lack such benchmarks, limiting the evaluation’s completeness.  
4. **Absence of failure analysis:** The paper omits discussion of model failures, which would provide valuable insights for future work. A brief analysis of common error patterns (e.g., ambiguous questions, visual distractors) would strengthen the contribution.  
5. **Minor errors:**  
   - Line 38: "mechanism" → "mechanisms"  
   - Line 237: Claims evaluation on validation set, but Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std results. This discrepancy requires correction.  

**Post-rebuttal Considerations:**  
While the authors’ rebuttal addresses the "two-modality performance" concern by attributing the gap to the absence of ternary potentials, the explanation lacks quantitative support (e.g., performance metrics for the two-modality variant). The quantitative correlation between the model’s attention maps and human attention (reported in rebuttal) is a strong positive, suggesting superior alignment with human reasoning compared to baselines like HieCoAtt. However, the absence of correlation values for SOTA models (e.g., MCB) leaves some ambiguity.  

A critical unresolved issue pertains to the authors’ claim regarding MCB vs. MCT:  
> *"Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations... and found them to yield inferior results."*  
Table 1 reports MCB at 68.6% (test-dev), not 69.4%. Given that 3-MCT’s reported accuracy (68.4%) is lower than MCB’s (68.6%), it appears the percentages may be swapped. The authors must clarify whether 68.4% corresponds to MCB or MCT, and reconcile this with Table 1.  

**Recommendation:**  
Despite the unresolved technical queries, I recommend **acceptance**. The core contribution—a generic, multimodal attention framework with ternary interactions—represents a meaningful advance in VQA methodology. The quantitative improvements over SOTA (especially with ternary potentials) and the compelling human-attention alignment in the rebuttal justify the contribution. The weaknesses, while significant, do not undermine the paper’s primary novelty. Addressing the clarifications above in the camera-ready version would further strengthen the work. The authors should resolve the MCB/MCT percentage discrepancy and provide the requested clarifications on answer attention and failure modes.
