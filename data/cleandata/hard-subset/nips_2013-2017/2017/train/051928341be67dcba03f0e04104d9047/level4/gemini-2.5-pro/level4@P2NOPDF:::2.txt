This paper presents a novel method for Visual Question Answering (VQA) that models joint attention across the image and the question. The primary contribution is a generic attention formulation for multiple modalities, defined by a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. In the context of VQA, this framework is applied to three modalities: the image, the question, and the answer. The proposed approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset, where the model incorporating ternary potentials outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths:**
1.  The proposal of a generic, task-agnostic attention formulation for multimodal problems is a significant strength. Such a framework has the potential for broad applicability if its generalizability to other tasks can be demonstrated.
2.  The ablation study, which isolates the impact of the ternary potential, is valuable. It clearly demonstrates the significant contribution of this component to the model's overall performance.
3.  The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The concept of applying attention over the answers is not clearly justified. Given that most answers in the VQA dataset are single words (or are treated as such in the experimental setup), the utility of an attention mechanism in this context is unclear. The authors should clarify the motivation and implementation of "answer attention." Providing visualizations for answer attention, similar to those for the image and question, would be beneficial.
2.  The results in Table 1 suggest that the performance gain is almost entirely attributable to the ternary potential. Without it, the proposed model (in its 2-modality configuration) does not consistently outperform existing baselines, with the exception of HieCoAtt. The authors should provide further analysis as to why the 2-modality version of their model is not more competitive on its own.
3.  Given the crucial role of the ternary potential, which incorporates the answer modality, a comparison with other methods that also leverage answers as inputs is warranted. Specifically, the authors should compare their model against relevant baselines such as Jabri et al. (ECCV 2016, "Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines").
4.  The paper lacks a discussion of the model's failure cases. An analysis of incorrect predictions would provide valuable insights into the model's limitations and could help guide future research.
5.  Minor errors and points for clarification:
    a.  L38: "mechanism" should be "mechanisms".
    b.  L237 states that the evaluation is conducted on the validation set. However, Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets. Please clarify this discrepancy.

**Post-rebuttal comments:**
After reviewing the authors' rebuttal, I am recommending acceptance. This recommendation is based on the novelty of the generic multi-modal attention framework and the strong quantitative results achieved by the 3-modality model, which surpasses the state-of-the-art. While the authors' response regarding the model's performance in the 2-modality setting was not entirely convincing due to a lack of supporting quantitative evidence, other aspects of the rebuttal were strong.

The new evaluation comparing the model's attention maps with human attention maps is a valuable addition. The reported correlation suggests a significant improvement over prior work (HieCoAtt), lending credibility to the model's attention mechanism. While a direct comparison to the SOTA model (MCB) on this metric is missing, the provided result is encouraging.

I have one remaining question regarding a statement in the rebuttal:

> Authors' response -- "MCB vs. MCT": MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.

Is there a typo in these numbers? It seems the performance figures for MCT and MCB might have been swapped, as one would expect the 3-MCT model to achieve the higher score (69.4%). Furthermore, clarification is needed for the 68.4% figure, as the reported overall MCB performance in Table 1 is 68.6%.
