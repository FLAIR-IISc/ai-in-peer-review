

The paper introduces a high-order attention mechanism designed to model correlations across multiple data modalities (e.g., image, question, answer) for visual question answering (VQA). The authors frame attention as a probabilistic model with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials, using mean-field inference to derive attention weights. They demonstrate state-of-the-art VQA performance on standard benchmarks, claiming that higher-order correlations improve attention alignment compared to prior work like [7, 15].

**Quality**  
The technical approach is sound, with thorough quantitative evaluation on VQA test sets and insightful qualitative analysis of attention maps. The ablation studies (e.g., 2-modality vs. 3-modality models) support the claim that high-order attention is beneficial. However, the probabilistic interpretation remains largely motivational rather than formally grounded—equation (1) combines potentials linearly without rigorous justification for the chosen structure or learned coefficients (αᵢ, βᵢ, γᵢ). The authors omit analysis of these coefficients, which could clarify why certain modalities rely more on unary vs. higher-order potentials. Additionally, the 2-modality model’s inferior performance compared to [7] (despite architectural similarities) lacks explanation, weakening the case for the proposed framework’s necessity. The MCT vs. 2-layer MCB comparison in Section 4.2 is intriguing but lacks results, leaving a key claim unverified.

**Clarity**  
The writing is occasionally jumbled, particularly in the description of the attention mechanism. For example, Section 3.2’s presentation of equations (1) and the role of marginalized potentials is confusing without clearer links to the figures. Figure 2 (system overview) is overly abstract—too detailed to grasp the high-level approach yet too vague to explain the mechanism. Figure 3 (illustrating unary/pairwise/ternary potentials) is referenced in the text but never explicitly discussed, creating a disconnect. The authors should simplify Figure 2 to emphasize the potential-generation pipeline and clarify how Figure 3 supports the model’s design. The term "trinary" should be corrected to "ternary" consistently.

**Originality**  
The core idea—extending attention to higher-order modalities—is incremental. While the probabilistic framing is novel, the approach largely reinterprets [7]’s MCB framework at unary and ternary orders. The authors acknowledge this but do not sufficiently distinguish their contribution: their model adds complexity (e.g., ternary potentials) without clearly demonstrating *why* higher-order correlations are uniquely beneficial for VQA beyond what [7] achieves with pairwise interactions. The claim of "general applicability" is underexplored, as the paper focuses exclusively on VQA. Related work (Section 2) properly contextualizes [7, 15] but understates how closely the proposed framework parallels their methods.

**Significance**  
The state-of-the-art results on VQA are significant, and the attention maps convincingly show improved alignment with question semantics (e.g., Fig. 7). However, the marginal gains over [7] (e.g., 64.57% vs. 63.82% on val) are modest, and the paper does not convincingly argue that high-order attention is *necessary* for these gains. The authors’ claim that "the model is simple" is misleading—their 3-modality architecture requires additional tensor operations and inference steps compared to [7]. Without evidence that high-order attention generalizes beyond VQA (e.g., to image captioning or visual reasoning), the broader impact remains uncertain.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**  
*Pro:* Strong empirical results, clear qualitative analysis of attention, and a well-structured evaluation protocol.  
*Con:* Incremental novelty, underdeveloped probabilistic formalism, unclear justification for model complexity, and unresolved performance discrepancies (e.g., 2-modality model vs. [7]).

**Recommendation**  
The paper makes a modest contribution to attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks. While the results are compelling, the incremental nature of the novelty and unresolved technical gaps (probabilistic grounding, model complexity justification) prevent it from being a strong candidate for NIPS. The authors should either: (1) rigorously formalize the probabilistic model and demonstrate *why* high-order correlations are essential, or (2) provide compelling evidence that their framework generalizes beyond VQA to other tasks. As-is, the work is better suited for a venue with a narrower scope (e.g., CVPR). I recommend rejection.
