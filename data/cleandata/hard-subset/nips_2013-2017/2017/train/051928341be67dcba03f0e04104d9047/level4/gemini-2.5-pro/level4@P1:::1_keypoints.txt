- Introduces an attention mechanism combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials to model high-order cross-modal correlations for VQA, achieving state-of-the-art results.  
- Unary potentials capture intra-modality feature importance; pairwise and ternary capture 2- and 3-way modality correlations, linearly combined into final attention.  

Strengths:
- Proposes an intuitive model that achieves strong, SOTA performance on a challenging benchmark.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough with extensive quantitative comparisons.  
- Qualitative visualizations (especially Figure 5) effectively illustrate how unary and pairwise potentials contribute to final attention, supporting that gains are from the mechanism, not just capacity.  

Weaknesses:
- Writing and presentation are sometimes unclear, making parts of the model hard to follow.  
- Novelty feels incremental; the paper should better articulate concrete advances over prior work like MCB.  
- The claimed connection to probabilistic models and mean-field inference is more motivational than formal and needs either formalization or reframing as intuition.  
- Figure 2 is at an awkward abstraction level—neither implementation-detail nor clear high-level overview; it should be revised to better show how potentials are generated and combined.  
- Figure 3 is not referenced in the text, making it hard to connect that diagram to Section 3.2.  

Questions for authors:
- Please report the learned linear coefficients (αi, βi, γi) to show how different modalities weight unary/pairwise/ternary potentials.  
- Why does the 2-modality (Unary+Pairwise) model underperform MCB despite architectural similarity—due to lower embedding dimensionality or other factors?  
- Include the MCT vs. 2-layer MCB decision-head comparison in a table to clarify val/test-dev/test-std impacts.  
- Were marginalized ternary attention maps visualized (e.g., in the supplement)? If not, please provide them to complete the qualitative analysis.
