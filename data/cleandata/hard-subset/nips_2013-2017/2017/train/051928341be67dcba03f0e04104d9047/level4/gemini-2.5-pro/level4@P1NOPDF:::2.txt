This paper introduces a novel attention method for the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core contribution is a generic, multi-modal attention framework that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. In the context of VQA, this is applied to the image, question, and answer modalities. The model is evaluated on the VQA dataset, where it demonstrates state-of-the-art performance, particularly when leveraging the proposed ternary potentials.

### Strengths:
1.  The proposed generic formulation for multi-modal attention is a valuable contribution, as it is not task-specific and holds the potential for broader applicability to other multi-modal problems.
2.  The ablation study effectively isolates the contribution of the ternary potentials, clearly demonstrating their importance to the model's overall performance.
3.  The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

### Weaknesses:
1.  The concept of applying "attention over answers" is not well-justified. Given that most answers in the VQA dataset are single words (and multi-word answers are often treated as a single token), it is unclear what "attending" over the answer space entails. The lack of visualizations for answer attention further obscures this aspect of the model. The authors should provide a clearer explanation of this mechanism.
2.  Analysis of Table 1 suggests that the performance gains are almost entirely driven by the ternary potential. In the two-modality setting (without the ternary component), the proposed model does not outperform most existing methods (with the exception of HieCoAtt). It would be beneficial for the authors to elaborate on why the two-modality version of their model is not more competitive.
3.  Given the critical role of the ternary potential, which incorporates the answer modality during training, a comparison with other methods that also leverage answer information as input is warranted. A relevant baseline would be "Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines" (Jabri et al., ECCV 2016).
4.  The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the model's failure cases. An analysis of its limitations and common error types would provide valuable insights for the community and guide future research.
5.  Minor errors/typos:
    a.  L38: "mechanism" should be "mechanisms".
    b.  L237 states that the evaluation is performed on the validation set, but Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets. This should be clarified.

### Post-rebuttal comments:
While I am not fully convinced by the authors' response regarding the model's performance in the two-modality setting (which lacked quantitative support), I am now recommending acceptance.

My recommendation is based on the strength of the paper's primary contributions: the novel, generic framework for multi-modal attention and the state-of-the-art results achieved by the three-modality version of the model. Furthermore, the additional quantitative evaluation provided in the rebuttal, comparing the model's attention maps with human attention, is compelling. The reported correlation is a significant improvement over HieCoAtt and suggests that the model is learning meaningful attention. While a comparison to other SOTA models like MCB is missing, this result nonetheless strengthens the paper.

I have one remaining question for the authors regarding their response:

> Authors' response: "MCB vs. MCT": MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.

Are the numbers in this statement swapped? It seems the higher performance (69.4%) should correspond to the proposed 3-MCT model, not the 2-layer MCB. Additionally, Table 1 reports the overall performance of MCB as 68.6%, so I am unsure what the 68.4% figure refers to.
