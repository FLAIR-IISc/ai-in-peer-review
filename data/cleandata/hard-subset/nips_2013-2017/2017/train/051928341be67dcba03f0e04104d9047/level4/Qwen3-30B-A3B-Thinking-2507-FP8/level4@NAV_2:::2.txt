

The paper introduces a novel attention framework for multimodal tasks, specifically applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA), by modeling high-order correlations through unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer modalities. The approach demonstrates state-of-the-art results on VQA benchmarks, with the ternary potential component proving particularly effective. This work builds on recent NIPS contributions like Lu et al. (2016) and Fukui et al. (2016), extending attention mechanisms beyond pairwise interactions to generalize across arbitrary modalities while maintaining computational efficiency via tensor sketching.

Strengths:
1. The proposed high-order attention framework is genuinely task-agnostic, offering a principled extension beyond existing pairwise attention models (e.g., Lu et al., 2016; Fukui et al., 2016) that are constrained to two modalities. This generalizability is a significant contribution to multimodal learning.
2. The ablation study effectively isolates the impact of ternary potentials, demonstrating their critical role in achieving SOTA performance and providing clear evidence of their necessity.
3. The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly written, with detailed technical explanations of the attention mechanisms and their implementation.

Weaknesses:
1. The interpretation of "attention over answers" is unclear, as answers are typically single words or short phrases treated as atomic units. The paper lacks visualizations of answer attention maps, making it difficult to assess how the model interprets this modality. Clarification is required.
2. Table 1 reveals that the proposed model without ternary potentials (2-modality setup) does not outperform existing 2-modality baselines (e.g., Lu et al., 2016; Fukui et al., 2016), except for HieCoAtt. The authors should explicitly address why the ternary component is necessary for performance gains rather than merely being an additive improvement.
3. The paper should compare against models that incorporate answers as input (e.g., Jabri et al., 2016), which explicitly question the need for attention mechanisms. This comparison is critical to contextualize the novelty of the proposed approach.
4. No failure case analysis is provided, despite the qualitative results showing a clear failure mode (Fig. 8). Discussing limitations and error patterns would strengthen the paper’s contribution.
5. Typos/clarifications: Line 38 should correct "mechanism" to "mechanisms"; Line 237 references validation set evaluation, but Table 1 reports test-dev/std results—please clarify.

Post-rebuttal comments:  
While the authors’ response to the 2-modality performance concern lacks quantitative evidence, the generic multimodal attention framework and its SOTA 3-modality results justify acceptance. The rebuttal’s correlation analysis with human attention maps (showing better alignment than HieCoAtt) is compelling. However, the authors’ numerical claim regarding MCT vs. MCB (reported 68.4% for MCT vs. 69.4% for 2-layer MCB) appears inconsistent with Table 1 (MCB: 68.6%) and the test-dev results. Please confirm whether the numbers are swapped or refer to different configurations. Given the framework’s novelty and strong empirical results, I recommend acceptance.
