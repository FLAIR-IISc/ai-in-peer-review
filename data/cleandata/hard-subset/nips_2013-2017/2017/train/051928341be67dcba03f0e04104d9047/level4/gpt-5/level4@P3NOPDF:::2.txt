Summary and contributions:
This paper proposes a generic attention framework for multimodal reasoning that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across image, question, and answer modalities. Applied to Visual Question Answering (VQA), the model explicitly incorporates attention over all three modalities and reports improvements on the VQA benchmark, with the strongest gains arising from the ternary (three-way) potentials.

Strengths:
- The formulation is general and, in principle, applicable beyond VQA. A modality-agnostic design is valuable if it can be shown to transfer to other tasks.
- The ablation study isolating the effect of ternary potentials is informative and helps quantify the contribution of three-way interactions.
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Attention over answers is insufficiently motivated and described. In VQA, most answers are single tokens and are often treated as atomic classes. It is unclear what “answer attention” means when answers are single words or short phrases collapsed to a single label. Please clarify:
  - What is the granularity of answer attention (tokens, subwords, candidate answers, or answer embeddings)?
  - How is attention over answers computed and used during training/inference (e.g., over a candidate set vs. over an embedding sequence)?
  - Can you provide qualitative visualizations or at least quantitative diagnostics (e.g., sparsity, entropy) for answer attention?
- From Table 1, the largest gains appear to come from the ternary potential. Without ternary interactions, the two-modality variants do not consistently outperform strong baselines (except HieCoAtt). This raises two concerns:
  - Is the modeling of pairwise interactions weaker than in specialized two-modality attention methods? If so, why, and can you strengthen this component?
  - Please provide quantitative evidence or additional ablations supporting your claim that the two-modality setup is competitive (e.g., improved training protocols, capacity control, or feature backbones).
- Since the answer modality is central to your improvements, a comparison with methods that explicitly incorporate the answer as input is needed. In particular, please compare against Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV’16) and any other contemporaneous approaches that use candidate answers or answer embeddings at inference time.
- The paper would benefit from an analysis of failure cases. For example:
  - When does the ternary attention hurt or fail to help?
  - Are there specific question types (e.g., counting, spatial relations, commonsense) where the three-way interaction is most/least effective?
  - What typical error patterns emerge in the attention maps (e.g., diffuse vs. mislocalized image attention, question tokens over/under-attended)?
- Clarification on evaluation splits: Line 237 states evaluation on the validation set, while Table 1 reports test-dev and test-std numbers. Please reconcile these statements and specify which splits were used for model selection and hyperparameter tuning to avoid leakage.

Minor issues:
- L38: “mechanism” → “mechanisms.”
- Please ensure consistent reporting of dataset splits (val vs. test-dev/test-std) throughout.

Post-rebuttal comments and recommendation:
The rebuttal partially addresses the concern about two-modality performance but lacks sufficient quantitative evidence to demonstrate competitiveness with strong two-modality baselines. Nonetheless, I recommend acceptance based on:
- The novelty and potential impact of a unified attention framework that handles unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions across modalities.
- Strong empirical gains from the three-modality configuration, which outperforms prior art on VQA.
- The promising quantitative alignment between the model’s attention maps and human attention maps reported in the rebuttal. While a direct comparison to top-performing models like MCB on this metric is missing, the improvement over HieCoAtt suggests that the learned attention is qualitatively better.

Request for clarification on rebuttal numbers:
The rebuttal states: “MCB vs. MCT: MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.”
- Are these numbers swapped? Intuitively, 3-MCT should meet or exceed 2-layer MCB if it is the stronger configuration in your main results. Also, Table 1 lists MCB overall at 68.6%. Please clarify:
  - Which MCB variant corresponds to 69.4% (and under what settings)?
  - Which exact configuration of 3-MCT yields 68.4% (backbone, features, number of layers)?
  - Are these results directly comparable to Table 1 (same data splits, preprocessing, and training schedule)?

Overall assessment:
This paper contributes a flexible and principled attention framework for multimodal reasoning and demonstrates compelling gains when modeling three-way interactions among image, question, and answer. The work would be strengthened by (1) a clearer explanation and visualization of answer attention, (2) a more competitive two-modality baseline or stronger evidence supporting the current two-modality performance, (3) comparisons to methods that condition on answers, and (4) analysis of failure modes. With these clarifications and additions, the paper would make a solid and broadly useful contribution.
