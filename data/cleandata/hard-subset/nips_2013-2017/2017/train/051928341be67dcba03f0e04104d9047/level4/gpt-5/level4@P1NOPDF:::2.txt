This paper proposes a novel attention mechanism for Visual Question Answering (VQA) that jointly models attention over multiple modalities—image, question, and answer—using a generic formulation with unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The method is evaluated on the VQA dataset and shows improvements over existing models when ternary potentials are included.

Strengths:
1. The paper introduces a generic, task-agnostic formulation of multimodal attention. This is valuable, especially if it can be shown to generalize beyond a single task.
2. The ablation study isolating the effect of ternary potentials is helpful for quantifying their contribution.
3. The paper is clearly written.

Weaknesses:
1. The notion of “attention over answers” is unclear. Most answers are single words; even multiword answers are treated as a single token. What does attention over answers mean in this context? The paper does not include any visualizations or qualitative analyses of answer-side attention. Please clarify and, if possible, provide examples.
2. Table 1 suggests that most of the performance gain comes from the ternary potential. Without it, the proposed model does not outperform existing two-modality baselines (except HieCoAtt). Please shed light on this: why doesn’t the two-modality instantiation of your framework match or exceed other two-modality methods?
3. Because ternary potentials (i.e., incorporating answers as inputs) appear to drive the gains, the paper should compare against models that also use answers as inputs, such as “Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines” (Jabri et al., ECCV 2016).
4. The paper lacks a discussion of failure cases. Analyzing where and why the model fails would be insightful and could help guide future research.
5. Other errors/typos:
   a. L38: “mechanism” → “mechanisms.”
   b. L237 states that evaluation is on the validation set, but Table 1 reports test-dev and test-std results. Please reconcile this discrepancy.

Post-rebuttal comments:
Although the response regarding “the proposed model not outperforming existing models for two modalities” is not entirely convincing due to the lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance. The paper proposes a generic multimodal attention framework and demonstrates that three-modality attention outperforms the state of the art. The quantitative evaluation of the model’s attention maps against human attention maps (reported in the rebuttal) is also encouraging and suggests that your maps are more correlated with human behavior than those of existing models. While we do not have the correlation value for strong SOTA baselines like MCB, the improvement over HieCoAtt appears substantial.
