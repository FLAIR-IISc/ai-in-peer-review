This paper introduces a novel method for modeling attention on images and questions for the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The primary contribution lies in proposing a generic formulation of attention for multiple modalities, combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials for three modalities (image, question, and answer) in VQA. The proposed approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and outperforms existing models when ternary potentials are employed.

Strengths:

1. The generic formulation of attention for multimodal problems is valuable, particularly if it can be shown to generalize to more than one task.
2. The ablation study demonstrating performance without ternary potentials is useful for understanding the contribution of ternary potentials.
3. The paper is clearly written.

Weaknesses:

1. Although the approach mentions attention over three modalities – image, question, and answer – it is unclear what attention over answers entails, as most answers are single words, and even multi-word answers are treated as single words. The paper lacks visualizations for attention over answers, and clarification from the authors would be appreciated.
2. The results in Table 1 suggest that the primary improvement in the proposed model stems from the ternary potential. Without the ternary potential, the proposed model does not outperform existing models in the two-modalities setup (except HieCoAtt). The authors should provide further insight into this observation.
3. Given that the ternary potential appears to be the main factor in the performance improvement of the proposed model, a comparison with existing models that also utilize answers as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16), would be beneficial.
4. The paper lacks a discussion on failure cases of the proposed model. Analyzing failure modes would provide valuable insights for future research.
5. Other errors/typos:
    a. Line 38: "mechanism" should be replaced with "mechanisms."
    b. Line 237 mentions that the evaluation is on the validation set, whereas Table 1 reports numbers on the test-dev and test-std sets.

Post-rebuttal comments:
Although the authors' response to the concern about the proposed model not outperforming existing models for two modalities is not entirely satisfactory due to the lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance based on the generic attention framework for multiple modalities proposed in the paper and the quantitative results of three-modality attention outperforming the state-of-the-art. The quantitative evaluation of the proposed model's attention maps against human attention maps (reported in the rebuttal) appears promising, indicating that the attention maps are more correlated with human maps than existing models. However, it is unclear what this correlation value is for state-of-the-art models like MCB. Nevertheless, it seems significantly better than that for HieCoAtt.

Regarding one of the authors' responses:
> Authors' response – "MCB vs. MCT": MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically, for VQA, the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev, whereas the 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. The authors tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.

It appears that the numbers might be swapped, as 69.4% should correspond to 3-MCT. Additionally, the overall performance of MCB in Table 1 is 68.6%. Therefore, it is unclear which number the authors are referring to when reporting 68.4%.
