

The paper introduces a novel method for modeling attention between image and question modalities in Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core innovation is a generic attention formulation for multiple modalities, combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities (image, question, and answer). The approach achieves state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset when ternary potentials are incorporated.  

Strengths:  
1. The generic attention framework for multimodal problems, applicable beyond a single task, is valuable if demonstrated to generalize across multiple tasks.  
2. The ablation study isolating the contribution of ternary potentials provides clear insight into its impact.  
3. The paper is well-written and clear.  

Weaknesses:  
1. The paper proposes attention over three modalities (image, question, answer), but the meaning of "attention over answers" is unclear. Most answers are single words (or treated as such), and no visualizations of answer attention are provided. Clarification is required.  
2. Table 1 indicates that performance gains primarily stem from ternary potentials. Without them, the model does not outperform existing two-modality approaches (except HieCoAtt). The authors should explain this dependency.  
3. Given ternary potentials drive the improvement, the authors should compare against models using answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., ECCV 2016).  
4. The paper lacks analysis of failure cases, which would guide future research.  
5. Typos/errors:  
   a. L38: "mechanism" → "mechanisms"  
   b. L237 states evaluation on validation set, but Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std results.  

Post-rebuttal comments:  
Despite the authors’ unsatisfactory response to the two-modality performance concern (lacking quantitative evidence), I recommend acceptance due to the generic multimodal attention framework and the strong 3-modality results. The rebuttal’s reported correlation between proposed attention maps and human attention (superior to HieCoAtt) is promising. However, the correlation value for SOTA models (e.g., MCB) remains unreported.  

Clarification needed on authors’ response:  
> "MCB vs. MCT": 3-MCT achieves 68.4% on test-dev, while 2-layer MCB achieves 69.4%. Other multi-modality MCB variants yield inferior results.  
Are these percentages swapped? Table 1 reports MCB at 68.6%, so the reference to "68.4%" is unclear.
