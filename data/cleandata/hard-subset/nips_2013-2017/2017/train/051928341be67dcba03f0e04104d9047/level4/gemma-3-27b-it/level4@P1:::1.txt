**Strengths:**

*   Interesting model with strong results.
*   Thorough qualitative and quantitative experiments.
*   I am impressed by the shift between unary and pairwise attentions, and the resulting final attention. I would have liked to see visualizations of the marginalized ternary attention as well. Did I miss these in the text or supplementary materials?

**Weaknesses:**

*   The writing and presentation are sometimes jumbled.
*   The novelty feels somewhat limited. While the architecture is more complex than and outperforms the MCB model for VQA [7], much of this added complexity simply extends the intuition of [7] to higher (ternary) and lower (unary) orders. This isn't necessarily a major issue, but I suggest the authors clarify these contributions (and any I may have missed).
*   The probabilistic connection isn't clearly established. It doesn't seem formalized enough to be more than motivational, which is acceptable, but the authors should either solidify this connection with a more formal treatment or adjust the language for clarity.
*   Figure 2 is at an unhelpful level of abstraction. It lacks sufficient detail to understand the network's functionality, yet isn't abstract enough to easily capture the overall approach. I suggest simplifying this figure to more clearly emphasize the unary, pairwise, and ternary potential generation.
*   Figure 3 is not referenced in the main text.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What values were learned for the linear coefficients used to combine the marginalized potentials in equations (1)? It would be interesting to know if different modalities benefitted from different potential orders.
*   I found it surprising that the 2-Modality Unary+Pairwise model underperforms MCB [7] despite a similar architecture. I was disappointed that this wasn’t discussed in more detail. Do you have any intuition for this result? Is it related to the switch to MCB/MCT decision computation modules?
*   The discussion of using sequential MCB versus a single MCT layer for the decision head was interesting, but no results were presented. Could the authors elaborate on their observations?

The authors propose a novel attention mechanism for visual question answering (VQA) that learns high-order correlations between data modalities. They demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset. The core idea is to model interactions between modalities – an image and a question, for example – using not only pairwise correlations but also higher-order correlations. This allows the model to more effectively direct attention to relevant elements within each modality.

The related work section provides a comprehensive overview of existing attention mechanisms for both image and text data, highlighting limitations in capturing multi-modal interactions and task-specific design. The authors position their work as addressing these limitations by offering a generally applicable attention mechanism that learns these correlations.

The proposed higher-order attention models are formally defined, outlining the unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials. The model employs mean-field inference to obtain marginal probabilities, allowing for a straightforward extension to any number of modalities. The decision-making component uses tensor sketch algorithms to efficiently handle high-dimensional correlations.

The experimental setup and results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset. Qualitative results, including attention visualizations, show the model’s ability to focus on relevant regions and question words. The authors compare their model to existing methods, highlighting the benefits of incorporating higher-order correlations.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in multi-modal learning (VQA) with a novel approach.
*   The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on a standard benchmark.
*   The attention visualizations provide insight into the model's behavior and support the claim that higher-order correlations are beneficial.
*   The paper is well-structured and provides a detailed description of the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The writing could be clearer and more concise.
*   The novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental, building upon existing work in attention mechanisms and bilinear pooling.
*   The probabilistic connection isn't fully developed.
