This paper introduces a novel approach to attention modeling for Visual Question Answering (VQA), presenting a generalized attention formulation applicable to multimodal problems. The core innovation lies in representing attention through a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across the image, question, and answer modalities. Evaluation on the VQA dataset demonstrates that incorporating ternary potentials leads to performance gains over existing state-of-the-art models.

The work’s strengths include the potential broad applicability of its generic attention formulation beyond the specific VQA task, and the insightful ablation study that effectively isolates the contribution of the ternary potentials. Furthermore, the paper is well-written and clearly presented.

However, several areas require attention. First, the notion of attention *over* the answer modality is unclear, given that answers are typically single words or treated as such, even when multi-word. The paper lacks visualizations illustrating this attention, and clarification is needed. Second, the results suggest that the primary performance improvement stems from the ternary potential; the model doesn't consistently outperform existing methods in a two-modality setting (excluding HieCoAtt). Further discussion on this point would be valuable. Third, given the importance of the ternary potential, a comparison to models that explicitly utilize the answer as input – such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16) – is warranted. Fourth, the paper would benefit from a discussion of the model’s failure cases to guide future research. Finally, there are minor errors: in line 38, “mechanism” should be “mechanisms”, and line 237 states evaluation on the validation set, while Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets.

**Post-rebuttal comments:** While the authors’ response regarding the performance in the two-modality setup is not fully convincing due to the absence of quantitative support, I recommend acceptance. The proposed generic attention framework for multiple modalities, coupled with the demonstrated performance gains achieved through the use of three-modality attention, represents a significant contribution. The additional quantitative evaluation of attention maps against human attention maps, as presented in the rebuttal, is promising, suggesting a higher correlation than existing models (although a comparison to MCB is missing). 

I also have a question regarding the authors’ response concerning “MCB vs. MCT”: The reported numbers appear reversed – 69.4% should correspond to 3-MCT, and the stated MCB performance (68.6% in Table 1) differs from the reported 68.4%.
