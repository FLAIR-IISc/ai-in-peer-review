Review- The paper introduces a new approach to model attention over the image and the question for Visual Question Answering (VQA). Its primary contribution is a generic, multimodal attention formulation that combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities (image, question, answer) in VQA. Evaluated on the VQA dataset, the method surpasses prior models when ternary potentials are included.

Strengths:
1. A task-agnostic, general attention formulation for multimodal problems is valuable, especially if it can be demonstrated to generalize beyond a single task.
2. The ablation removing ternary potentials is helpful for quantifying the contribution of the ternary term.
3. The paper is clearly written.

Weaknesses:
1. The method claims attention over three modalities—image, question, and answer—but it is unclear what “attention over answers” entails, since most answers are single tokens and even multiword answers are treated as a single unit. No visualizations of answer attention are shown; please clarify.
2. Table 1 suggests that the principal gains are due to the ternary potential. Without the ternary term, the two-modality setup does not outperform existing models (except HieCoAtt). Please elaborate on this.
3. Given that ternary potentials appear to drive the improvements, please compare against methods that also use answers as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16).
4. The paper does not discuss failure cases. Analyzing failure modes would be insightful and guide future work.
5. Other errors/typos:
a. L38: mechanism -> mechanisms
b. L237 states that evaluation is on the validation set, but Table 1 reports results on test-dev and test-std. Please reconcile.

Post-rebuttal comments:
Although the response to the concern about “the proposed model not outperforming existing models for two modalities” is not fully convincing due to a lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance based on the proposed generic multimodal attention framework and the strong three-modality results that outperform SOTA. The quantitative evaluation of the model’s attention maps against human attention maps (reported in the rebuttal) is also encouraging and suggests higher correlation with human maps than existing models. While we do not know the correlation value for top-performing models such as MCB, it still appears notably better than HieCoAtt.
