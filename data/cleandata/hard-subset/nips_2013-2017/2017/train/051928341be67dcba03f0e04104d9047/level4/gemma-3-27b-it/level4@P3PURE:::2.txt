The paper presents a novel method to model attention over both the image and question for the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The main novelty lies in proposing a generic formulation of attention for multiple modalities – a combination of unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities (image, question, and answer) within the VQA context. The proposed approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and outperforms existing models when utilizing ternary potentials.

**Strengths:**

1. A generic formulation of attention for multimodal problems, independent of a specific task, is a valuable contribution, particularly if its generalizability to multiple tasks can be demonstrated.
2. The ablation study clearly demonstrates the impact of the ternary potentials, providing insight into their contribution to performance.
3. The paper is clearly and concisely written.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The approach incorporates attention over three modalities – image, question, and answer. However, the purpose of attention over answers is unclear, as most answers consist of single words, and even multi-word answers are treated as single tokens. The paper lacks visualizations illustrating attention over answers, and I request the authors clarify this aspect of their methodology.
2. Table 1 suggests that the primary performance improvement stems from the ternary potential. Without it, the proposed model does not outperform existing models in the two-modality setup (with the exception of HieCoAtt). I would appreciate further discussion of this point from the authors.
3. Given the apparent importance of the ternary potential to performance gains, I suggest the authors compare their model with existing approaches that explicitly incorporate answers as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16).
4. The paper lacks a discussion of failure cases. Examining the model’s limitations would be insightful and could guide future research directions.
5. Minor errors/typos:
    a. L38: “mechanism” should be “mechanisms”.
    b. L237 states that the evaluation is performed on the validation set; however, Table 1 reports results on the test-dev and test-std sets.

**Post-rebuttal comments:**

Although the authors’ response to the concern regarding the model’s performance compared to existing methods in the two-modality setting is not entirely convincing due to a lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance. This recommendation is based on the paper's proposal of a generic attention framework for multiple modalities and the quantitative results demonstrating that the three-modality attention outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The quantitative evaluation of attention maps against human attention maps (presented in the rebuttal) is encouraging and suggests a stronger correlation with human attention than existing models. While the correlation values for state-of-the-art models like MCB are unknown, the reported results appear significantly better than those for HieCoAtt.

I have a question regarding one of the authors’ responses:

> Authors’ response: “MCB vs. MCT”: MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically for VQA the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev where 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. We tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.

Are the numbers swapped here? It seems 69.4% should correspond to 3-MCT. Additionally, the MCB performance reported in Table 1 is 68.6%, so clarification is needed regarding which value the authors are referencing when stating 68.4%.
