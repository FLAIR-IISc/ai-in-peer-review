- Introduces a probabilistic high-order attention framework (unary, pairwise, ternary) to model complex interactions across image, question, and answer modalities for VQA.  
- Explicit modeling of ternary (3-modality) interactions yields state-of-the-art VQA performance (69.4% on test-dev).  
- Method is technically sound with thorough experiments and a convincing ablation showing ternary potentials drive the performance gains.  
- Explanation and interpretation of "attention over answers" is insufficient given answers are often single words or short phrases.  
- Paper lacks a detailed analysis of failure cases, limiting insight into model weaknesses.  
- Experimental setup is well-documented but contains a discrepancy between reported validation set usage and results in Table 1.  
- Writing, mathematical formulation, and visualizations are clear and make the technical content accessible.  
- The high-order attention idea is novel and generalizable beyond VQA, extending prior two-modality/bilinear approaches.  
- The paper should better differentiate itself from "dual attention" work and test applicability beyond VQA.  
- Results show a meaningful improvement over prior state-of-the-art (MCB 68.6% → 69.4%), indicating real significance.  
- Broader applicability to other multimodal tasks is plausible but not demonstrated or discussed in depth.  
- Strengths: principled, generalizable framework; clear ablation for ternary potentials; excellent writing and visualizations; strong VQA results.  
- Weaknesses: unclear answer-attention interpretation; gains rely heavily on ternary potential; missing comparisons to answer-inclusive models; no failure-case analysis; validation/test reporting discrepancy.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify answer attention, add comparisons, and resolve reporting inconsistencies.  
- Rebuttal evidence cited: 3-modality MCB achieves 69.4% vs 68.4% with MCT, and attention-map correlation with human attention is 0.68 vs 0.55 for HieCoAtt.
