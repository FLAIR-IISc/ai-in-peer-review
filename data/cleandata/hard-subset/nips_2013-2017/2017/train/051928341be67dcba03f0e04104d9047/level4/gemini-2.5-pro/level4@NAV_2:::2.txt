This paper introduces a novel attention mechanism for multimodal tasks, specifically demonstrated on the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The central contribution is a general framework for high-order attention that models correlations across different data modalities. For VQA, the authors formulate attention as a probabilistic model combining unary potentials (within a single modality like the image), pairwise potentials (between two modalities, e.g., image-question), and ternary potentials (across all three modalities: image, question, and answer). This approach, based on mean-field inference over potentials, contrasts with prior work that often uses hierarchical co-attention or feature fusion before attention. The authors demonstrate that their full three-modality model, which leverages these high-order correlations, achieves state-of-the-art performance on the standard VQA dataset.

**Strengths:**
1.  The proposed attention framework is formulated in a general and principled manner. The idea of modeling multimodal interactions as a combination of k-order potentials is elegant and potentially applicable to a wide range of tasks beyond VQA, which is a significant strength.
2.  The paper includes a valuable ablation study comparing the performance of the two-modality model (unary and pairwise potentials) with the full three-modality model (including ternary potentials). This analysis clearly isolates and quantifies the performance gains attributable to the novel high-order component of the model.
3.  The paper is well-written and organized. The authors clearly explain their probabilistic formulation, from the individual potentials to the mean-field inference step, making the methodology easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The concept of "attention over answers" requires further clarification and justification. Given that most answers in the VQA dataset are single words or are treated as single n-grams from a vocabulary of 3000 candidates, it is not intuitive what an attention distribution over this list of possible answers represents. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation and qualitative analysis of this answer attention mechanism.
2.  The experimental results in Table 1 suggest that the performance improvement stems almost entirely from the inclusion of the third-order (ternary) potential. The two-modality version of the proposed model is not competitive with several existing state-of-the-art two-modality methods. This raises questions about the novelty and effectiveness of the proposed unary and pairwise potential formulation when compared to prior art.
3.  Given that the answer modality is critical to the model's success, the comparison to related work is incomplete. The authors should benchmark their approach against other models that also incorporate answer information as input, such as Jabri et al., "Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines" (ECCV 2016), to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
4.  The paper would be strengthened by including a qualitative analysis of its failure cases. Understanding the types of questions or images where the model fails would provide valuable insights for future research and a more balanced view of the method's capabilities.
5.  Minor points:
    *   In the Introduction: "present day attention mechanism are however often lacking" should be "mechanisms".
    *   The text describes evaluating different decision units on the 'val' dataset, but Table 1 reports final results on 'test-dev' and 'test-std'. Clarifying the dataset used for each reported number would improve reproducibility.

**Summary and Recommendation:**
This paper presents a compelling and novel framework for high-order attention that achieves excellent results on VQA. The main argument for acceptance is the general, probabilistically-grounded formulation that could inspire new research directions in multimodal learning. The state-of-the-art performance empirically validates the idea that modeling high-order correlations involving all relevant modalities is beneficial. However, the contribution's impact is slightly diminished by the fact that the underlying two-modality model is not competitive on its own, and the paper lacks a clear justification for the "answer attention" component and comparisons to other methods that use answer information. Overall, the strengths in novelty and empirical performance outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is a solid contribution, and I would recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the points of clarification raised in this review.
