The paper presents a novel method to model attention for multimodal input, specifically for the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The core contribution lies in a generic formulation of attention that utilizes high-order potentials – unary, pairwise, and ternary – to model correlations between modalities (image, question, and answer). The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on the VQA dataset when incorporating ternary potentials.

This work builds upon the growing interest in attention mechanisms for multimodal tasks, following lines of research seen in Lu et al. [15], Xu et al. [25], and Fukui et al. [7]. These previous works have primarily focused on pairwise interactions or compact bilinear pooling. The authors address limitations of existing attention mechanisms by proposing a framework capable of capturing higher-order correlations and extending to any number of modalities, a feature not explicitly addressed in prior work like Kim et al. [13] or Nam et al. [19]. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Generality:** The proposed attention framework is a significant strength. Its ability to handle multiple modalities with a consistent formulation offers broader applicability beyond VQA, potentially impacting areas like image captioning [26] and machine comprehension [8].
2. **High-Order Correlations:** The incorporation of ternary potentials demonstrably improves performance, suggesting that modeling interactions between all three modalities (image, question, and answer) is crucial for VQA.
3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The mathematical formulation of the attention mechanism is relatively accessible, and the system architecture is clearly illustrated.
4. **Ablation Study:** The inclusion of an ablation study, demonstrating the contribution of the ternary potentials, strengthens the analysis and provides insights into the model’s behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Attention over Answers:** The concept of “attention” over the answer choices requires further clarification. Given that answers are often single words or short phrases, it is unclear how meaningful this attention mechanism is. The lack of visualization for answer attention further exacerbates this concern.
2. **Dependence on Ternary Potentials:** While the ternary potentials are effective, the model's performance without them is not competitive with existing two-modality approaches (except HieCoAtt). This raises the question of whether the framework's core benefit is genuinely the high-order interactions or simply the effective modeling of ternary relationships. More detailed analysis would be helpful.
3. **Comparison with Answer-as-Input Methods:** A direct comparison with methods that explicitly incorporate the answer as input, such as Jabri et al. [9], is missing. This comparison would help contextualize the contribution of learning attention over the answers versus directly using them as features.
4. **Failure Case Analysis:** The paper lacks a discussion of failure cases. Identifying scenarios where the model struggles would offer valuable insights for future research and highlight potential limitations of the approach.
5. **Minor Errors:** Several minor errors exist: L38: "mechanism" should be "mechanisms"; L237 claims evaluation on the validation set while Table 1 reports results on test-dev and test-std.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and potentially impactful attention framework with broader applicability beyond VQA.
* The incorporation of ternary potentials leads to state-of-the-art results on the VQA dataset.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the technical details of the approach.
* The ablation study provides useful insights into the model's components.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on ternary potentials for significant performance gains raises questions about the core benefits of the overall framework.
* The unclear explanation and lack of visualization for attention over answers weaken the contribution.
* The absence of comparison with alternative approaches that directly leverage answer information limits the contextualization of the results.
* The lack of failure case analysis hinders a comprehensive understanding of the model’s limitations.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, the proposed work presents a valuable contribution to the field of multimodal attention. The generic formulation and the demonstrated improvement with ternary potentials are promising. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Clarify the meaning and utility of attention over answers, potentially with visualizations.
2.  Provide a more thorough analysis of the performance without ternary potentials, potentially including a more detailed comparison to existing two-modality methods.
3.  Include a comparison to methods like Jabri et al. [9] that explicitly use the answer as input.
4.  Discuss failure cases and potential areas for future research.
5.  Correct the minor errors in the text.
