The paper presents a novel method for modeling attention on images and questions for the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The main contribution lies in proposing a generic formulation of attention for multiple modalities, combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials for three modalities (image, question, and answer) in VQA. The proposed approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and outperforms existing models when ternary potentials are used.

Strengths:

1. The generic formulation of attention for multimodal problems is useful, especially if it can be shown to generalize to more than one task.
2. The ablation study demonstrating the performance without ternary potentials is useful for understanding the contribution of ternary potentials.
3. The paper is clearly written.

Weaknesses:

1. The approach mentions attention over three modalities – image, question, and answer. However, it is unclear what attention over answers means, as most answers are single words, and even multi-word answers are treated as single words. The paper does not provide visualizations for attention over answers, and clarification from the authors would be helpful.
2. The results in Table 1 suggest that the main improvement in the proposed model comes from the ternary potential. Without the ternary potential, the proposed model does not outperform existing models for the two-modalities setup (except HieCoAtt). Further insight from the authors on this point would be appreciated.
3. Given that ternary potentials seem to be the primary factor in the performance improvement of the proposed model, a comparison with existing models that also use answers as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16), would be valuable.
4. The paper lacks a discussion on failure cases of the proposed model. Analyzing the failure modes would provide insight for future research.
5. Other errors/typos:
a. L38: "mechanism" should be "mechanisms".
b. L237 mentions that the evaluation is on the validation set, but Table 1 reports numbers on the test-dev and test-std sets.

Post-rebuttal comments:

Although the authors' response to the concern about the proposed model not outperforming existing models for two modalities is not entirely satisfactory due to a lack of quantitative evidence, I recommend acceptance based on the generic attention framework for multiple modalities proposed in the paper and the quantitative results of three-modality attention outperforming the state-of-the-art. The quantitative evaluation of the proposed model's attention maps against human attention maps (reported in the rebuttal) also appears promising, suggesting that the attention maps are more correlated with human maps than existing models. Although the correlation value for state-of-the-art models like MCB is unknown, the proposed model's performance is still significantly better than that of HieCoAtt.

I have a question regarding one of the authors' responses:
> Authors' response – "MCB vs. MCT": MCT is a generic extension of MCB for n-modalities. Specifically, for VQA, the 3-MCT setup yields 68.4% on test-dev, whereas the 2-layer MCB yields 69.4%. The authors tested other combinations of more than 2-modalities MCB and found them to yield inferior results.

Are the numbers swapped here? Shouldn't 69.4% be the result for 3-MCT? Additionally, the overall performance of MCB in Table 1 is 68.6%, so it is unclear which number the authors are referring to when reporting 68.4%.
