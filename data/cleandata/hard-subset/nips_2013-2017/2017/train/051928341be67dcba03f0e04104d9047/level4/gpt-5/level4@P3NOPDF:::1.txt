Summary:
The paper proposes a higher-order attention model that integrates unary, pairwise, and trinary potentials for multimodal reasoning, with a focus on VQA. The approach is well-motivated, the empirical results are strong, and the attention visualizations suggest the gains come from improved reasoning rather than simply increased capacity. That said, the exposition is at times unclear, the novelty over prior work (e.g., MCB [7]) feels incremental, and the probabilistic framing is more motivational than formal. With clearer writing, tighter figures, and a more explicit articulation of contributions and probabilistic connections, the paper would be significantly strengthened.

Strengths:
- Interesting and well-engineered model that achieves competitive or superior results.
- Thorough qualitative and quantitative experiments; attention maps are informative and align with the claimed improvements in reasoning.
- The transition from unary/pairwise attentions to a final, consolidated attention is compelling and appears to drive performance gains.

Weaknesses:
- Clarity and organization issues hinder comprehension. Some sections are difficult to follow, and terminology is not always introduced consistently.
- The novelty appears limited relative to MCB [7]. Much of the complexity builds on the same intuition by extending to trinary interactions and adding unary components. This may be reasonable, but the contributions need to be delineated more crisply.
- The probabilistic interpretation is not formalized enough to be taken as more than intuition. As presented, it functions as high-level motivation rather than a rigorous foundation.
- Figure 2 operates at an awkward level of detail: it neither enables a full understanding of the network nor abstracts the approach cleanly. The main idea of generating and marginalizing unary/pairwise/trinary potentials could be highlighted more directly.
- Figure 3 is never referenced in the main text (unless I missed it), which undermines its utility.

Questions and requests for clarification:
- Are marginalized trinary attention maps included anywhere (main text or supplement)? Seeing these would help validate that higher-order interactions are contributing as claimed.
- What values were learned for the linear coefficients that combine the marginalized potentials in Eq. (1)? Do different modalities favor different orders (e.g., stronger weight on pairwise vs. trinary terms)? Reporting these would give insight into the model’s behavior.
- The 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise variant underperforms MCB [7], despite architectural similarity. Can the authors provide intuition or analysis? Is this due to changes in the decision head (MCB vs. MCT), optimization dynamics, or differences in feature preprocessing?
- The discussion comparing sequential MCB layers to a single MCT layer in the decision module was intriguing, but no empirical results were provided. Could the authors share observations or a brief ablation comparing these choices?
- How robust are the attention mechanisms across different question types (e.g., counting vs. attribute queries)? Any failure-case analysis would be helpful to understand limitations.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the core contributions relative to MCB [7] and related baselines. Explicitly list what is new (e.g., trinary potential construction, unified marginalization and combination, specific architectural or training advances) and how each contributes empirically via ablations.
- Strengthen the probabilistic connection. Either provide a more formal derivation (e.g., clearly defining the potentials, their factorization, and assumptions under which the combination approximates inference) or reframe the language to emphasize that the interpretation is heuristic/motivational rather than a strict probabilistic model.
- Revise Figure 2 to better highlight the pipeline: (1) compute unary/pairwise/trinary potentials, (2) marginalize as needed, (3) combine via learned coefficients, and (4) feed into the decision module. A modular diagram with consistent notation keyed to the equations would improve readability.
- Ensure Figure 3 is referenced in the main text where relevant, and that the caption explains exactly what is being visualized and how it supports the claims.
- Include marginalized trinary attention visualizations and, if possible, an ablation that removes trinary terms to show their incremental value over unary+pairwise.
- Report the learned combination coefficients from Eq. (1), possibly stratified by modality or question type, to provide insight into which orders the model relies on.
- Expand the discussion on why 2-Modalities Unary+Pairwise underperforms MCB [7]. If the cause is suspected to be the decision head, include a controlled comparison where only the decision module is varied.
- Provide a small ablation comparing sequential MCB layers versus a single MCT layer in the decision head, even if results are preliminary.

Overall assessment:
This is a solid and promising piece of work with strong empirical results and compelling qualitative evidence. The main limitations are in exposition, the somewhat incremental nature of the novelty relative to MCB [7], and the underdeveloped probabilistic framing. Addressing the clarity issues, tightening the figures, explicitly enumerating contributions, and providing the requested ablations/visualizations would make the paper significantly stronger and easier to evaluate.
