- Paper proposes a generic multimodal attention framework modeling unary, pairwise, and ternary interactions, using a single mean-field step and MCB/MCT pooling; achieves SOTA on VQA with ternary terms.  
- Strength: principled, general attention formulation extensible to k modalities.  
- Strength: clear probabilistic view (potentials + mean-field) and integration with compact bilinear/trilinear fusion.  
- Strength: solid empirical gains when ternary potentials are used; ablation without ternary is informative.  
- Strength: qualitative attention visualizations aid interpretability.  
- Weakness: unclear semantics and visualization of attention over answers (A-attention); needs explanation of computation and examples.  
- Weakness: most gains appear to come from the ternary term; authors should quantify where ternary helps (question/answer types) and explain why 2-modality lags SOTA.  
- Weakness: missing comparisons to methods that also use answer candidates (e.g., Jabri et al.) under consistent settings.  
- Weakness: no substantive failure analysis or error stratification by question type; qualitative error examples are lacking.  
- Minor: typo L38 “mechanism” → “mechanisms.”  
- Minor: dataset-split inconsistency—L237 references validation but Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std; reconcile reported splits.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound formulation and training details, but weaker case/evidence for 2-modality setting.  
- Clarity assessment: generally well written but ambiguity remains around answer attention and dataset splits.  
- Originality assessment: novel synthesis of high-order potentials + mean-field + compact multilinear pooling beyond pairwise co-attention.  
- Significance assessment: ternary model’s SOTA on VQA is impactful and could generalize to other multimodal tasks if shown transferable.  
- Pros for acceptance: general/extensible framework, strong 3-modality results, interpretable visualizations, potential broad applicability.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited evidence of 2-modality gains over strong baselines, insufficient A-attention clarity, no failure analysis, incomplete comparisons to answer-conditioned methods.  
- Post-rebuttal stance: reviewer still unconvinced about 2-modality underperformance but leans to accept given generality and strong 3-modality results; notes encouraging human-attention correlation but missing direct MCB comparisons.  
- Clarification requested on rebuttal claim “MCB vs. MCT”: reported numbers seem inconsistent—ask authors to specify which score corresponds to which configuration (modalities used, pooling type, backbone features, and evaluation split).
