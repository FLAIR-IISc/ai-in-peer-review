

The paper introduces a novel method for modeling attention between image and question modalities in Visual Question Answering (VQA). The primary novelty lies in proposing a generic attention formulation for multiple modalities—combining unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials across three modalities (image, question, and answer). Evaluated on the VQA dataset, the approach achieves state-of-the-art results when ternary potentials are employed.  

Strengths:  
1. The generic attention formulation for multimodal problems, which is not task-specific, is valuable if it generalizes beyond a single task.  
2. The ablation study demonstrating performance without ternary potentials provides useful insight into their contribution.  
3. The paper is clearly written.  

Weaknesses:  
1. The approach describes attention over three modalities (image, question, answer), but it is unclear what "attention over answers" entails. Since answers are typically single words (or treated as such even when multiword), the paper lacks visualizations of attention over answers. Clarification is required.  
2. Table 1 suggests the primary performance gain stems from ternary potentials. Without them, the proposed model does not outperform existing two-modality models (except HieCoAtt). The authors should elaborate on this observation.  
3. Given that ternary potentials drive the main improvement, the paper should compare against models that similarly incorporate answers as inputs (e.g., *Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines* by Jabri et al., ECCV 2016).  
4. The paper omits discussion of failure cases. Analyzing these would guide future research and is valuable.  
5. Other errors/typos:  
   a. Line 38: "mechanism" → "mechanisms"  
   b. Line 237 states evaluation on the validation set, but Table 1 reports results on test-dev and test-std sets.  

Post-rebuttal comments:  
Although the authors' response to the concern about the proposed model not outperforming existing models in the two-modality setup lacks quantitative evidence and remains unsatisfactory, I recommend acceptance due to the generic multimodal attention framework and the strong quantitative results of the 3-modality approach. The rebuttal's quantitative comparison of the proposed model's attention maps with human attention (showing higher correlation than existing models) is promising. While the correlation value for SOTA models like MCB is not provided, it appears significantly better than for HieCoAtt.  

I have a question regarding the authors' response:  
> Authors' response — "MCB vs. MCT": MCT is a generic extension of MCB for *n*-modalities. For VQA, 3-MCT achieves 68.4% on test-dev, while 2-layer MCB achieves 69.4%. Other multi-modality MCB combinations yielded inferior results.  
Are these numbers swapped? Should 69.4% correspond to 3-MCT? Additionally, Table 1 reports MCB at 68.6%, so it is unclear which model the authors reference when stating 68.4%.
