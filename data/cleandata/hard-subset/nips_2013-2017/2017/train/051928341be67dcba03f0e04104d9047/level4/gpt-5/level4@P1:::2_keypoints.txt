- Paper proposes a generic high-order attention mechanism (unary/pairwise/ternary) for multimodal learning, applied to VQA with mean-field inference and MCB/MCT fusion.  
- Three-modality (V,Q,A) model achieves state-of-the-art on VQA; ablations show ternary potential drives most gains.  
- Strength: Task-agnostic, generalizable formulation of multimodal attention (unary/pairwise/ternary).  
- Strength: Ablation removing ternary potentials quantifies their contribution.  
- Strength: Paper is clearly written and easy to follow.  
- Weakness: “Attention over answers” is unclear (answers often single-vector); no visualizations or explanation of how answer attention is computed or interpreted.  
- Weakness: Two-modality instantiation (without ternary) does not outperform comparable baselines, suggesting gains mainly come from ternary term; authors should explain why and provide quantitative sensitivity analyses.  
- Weakness: Need explicit comparison to prior work that uses answers as inputs (e.g., Jabri et al., ECCV 2016).  
- Weakness: Paper lacks failure analysis and qualitative examination of typical error modes.  
- Weakness: Typos/inconsistencies—L38 pluralization; L237 mentions validation while Table 1 reports test-dev/test-std—please reconcile.  
- Post-rebuttal: Reviewer appreciates added results and recommends acceptance, but finds the two-modality response still not fully convincing without quantitative support.  
- Post-rebuttal: Reported correlation with human attention maps is encouraging; suggest reporting that metric for strong SOTA baselines like MCB for comparison.  
- Clarification requested: Inconsistency in authors’ rebuttal numbers for MCB vs MCT (68.4% vs 69.4%) and mismatch with Table 1’s 68.6%—please clarify which configuration corresponds to each number.  
- Overall quality: Technically sound probabilistic formulation with solid empirical results, but two-modality underperformance and limited failure analysis reduce confidence.  
- Overall clarity: Generally clear but needs clarification on answer attention, dataset split vs reported metrics, and rebuttal performance numbers.  
- Originality: High-order attention formulation is novel and distinguishes itself from co-attention and bilinear pooling approaches.  
- Significance: Ternary interaction gains are noteworthy for VQA and may generalize to other multimodal tasks.  
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on clarifying answer attention, reconciling reported numbers (MCB vs MCT and validation vs test), and adding a brief failure analysis.
