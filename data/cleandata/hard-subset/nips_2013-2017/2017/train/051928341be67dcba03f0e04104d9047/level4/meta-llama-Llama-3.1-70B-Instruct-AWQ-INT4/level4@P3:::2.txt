The paper "High-Order Attention Models for Visual Question Answering" proposes a novel attention mechanism for multimodal input data, specifically designed for the task of Visual Question Answering (VQA). The main contribution of the paper is a generic formulation of attention for multiple modalities, which combines unary, pairwise, and ternary potentials for 3 modalities (image, question, and answer) in VQA.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, including the formulation of the attention mechanism and the experimental setup. The proposed approach is evaluated on the VQA dataset and achieves state-of-the-art performance when ternary potentials are used.

Strengths:

1. The generic formulation of attention for multimodal problems is a significant contribution, as it can be applied to various tasks beyond VQA.
2. The ablation study showing the performance without ternary potential is helpful in understanding the impact of ternary potential on the overall performance.
3. The paper is clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand their approach.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper lacks clarity on the attention mechanism over answers, as most answers are single words, and even multi-word answers are treated as single words. The authors should provide more insight into this aspect and consider visualizing attention over answers.
2. The results in Table 1 suggest that the main improvement in the proposed model comes from the ternary potential. Without the ternary potential, the proposed model does not outperform existing models for 2 modalities setup (except HieCoAtt). The authors should provide more analysis on this aspect and discuss the limitations of their approach.
3. The paper would benefit from a comparison with existing models that use answers as inputs, such as Revisiting Visual Question Answering Baselines (Jabri et al., ECCV16).
4. The paper lacks discussion on failure cases of the proposed model, which would provide valuable insights for future research.
5. Minor errors and typos are present throughout the paper, such as "mechanism" instead of "mechanisms" on line 38.

Post-rebuttal comments:

Although the authors' response to some of the concerns is not entirely satisfactory, the paper's contributions, including the generic attention framework for multiple modalities and the quantitative results of 3-modality attention outperforming SOTA, are significant. The quantitative evaluation of the proposed model's attention maps against human attention maps, as reported in the rebuttal, also suggests that the attention maps are more correlated with human maps than existing models.

However, there are still some concerns that need to be addressed, such as the clarification on the attention mechanism over answers and the comparison with existing models that use answers as inputs. Additionally, the authors should provide more analysis on the limitations of their approach and discuss the failure cases of the proposed model.
