Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count data that combines Poisson factorization with a deep, recurrent prior over time. The observed counts xvt are modeled via Poisson rates that factorize into component loadings φvk, time-varying scores θtk, component weights λk, and feature sparsity terms ψv. To capture temporal dependencies, the prior over θt is parameterized by a hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t, with RNNs producing the (Gamma) prior parameters from a local temporal window. Inference is done in two stages: (i) mean-field variational inference with latent count augmentation (to keep Gamma–Poisson conjugacy for φ, ψ, λ, θ given auxiliary counts xvtk), and (ii) learning neural network parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that matches expectations between a forward (generative) and backward (recognition) RNN and includes a reconstruction loss. Experiments on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) compare against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM, reporting MSE and one-step-ahead PMSE. The paper claims improved performance on most datasets and discusses ladder-style architectures and “pruning” behavior.

Quality
- Technical soundness: There are several serious issues.
  - The generative model and inference are not clearly or rigorously specified. Equations (3)–(4), (9)–(10) define “implicit” Gamma conditionals whose shape parameters are outputs of RNNs, and then form posteriors by summing prior and “posterior-network” shapes (e.g., α + γ in Eq. 10). This does not derive from a coherent probabilistic model and departs from standard ELBO-based amortized VI. It is unclear whether the stated updates correspond to a valid posterior under any joint distribution.
  - The “Probability AutoEncoder” objective (Eqs. 15–18) maximizes an integral of p(Θ|H(0))p(H(0)|Θ) and then replaces it with squared-error matching of expectations and a reconstruction term. This is ad hoc and not a principled variational bound; there is no guarantee it optimizes marginal likelihood or a lower bound.
  - Claims of conditional conjugacy are correct for φ, ψ, λ, θ under the Poisson–Gamma augmentation, but only if the priors are fixed. Introducing network-produced prior parameters for θ (and h) is fine in principle (amortized VI), but the derivations provided (e.g., Eq. 8–14) contain notational inconsistencies and unclear conditioning (e.g., λk posterior depends on βθ + θ·k Σv ψv φvk, which is unusual).
  - Metrics focus on MSE/PMSE rather than predictive log-likelihood or held-out Poisson deviance, which are more appropriate for count models.
- Experimental support:
  - Key tables and figures are described but not shown here; no confidence intervals or statistical tests; limited details on data splits and prediction protocols.
  - Baselines are not clearly or fairly tuned. For example, LSTM settings are brief and may be poorly matched to high-dimensional counts; PGDS hyperparameters are fixed from prior work without tuning; no comparison to other relevant dynamic count models (e.g., Dynamic Poisson Factorization, dynamic gamma process models).
  - Reproducibility is weak: no code, incomplete training details (optimizer, learning rates, exact architectures), and ambiguous modeling choices.

Clarity
- The manuscript is difficult to follow, with many grammatical errors, ambiguous notation, and typos (e.g., repeated φ in Eq. 7 instead of ψ, inconsistent use of βθ, unclear indices, undefined constants). The description of the deep temporal hierarchy and the roles of ffeed/fback networks is not precise enough to reproduce the method. The reference list is incomplete and inconsistently formatted; some cited items appear missing.
- The model’s conceptual contribution (what is generative vs. what is inference) needs to be made explicit, with a coherent ELBO or a clear justification for deviating from it.

Originality
- The problem of dynamic Poisson factorization with long-range dependencies is important and well-studied. Using RNNs to parameterize Gamma priors over factor scores is a natural extension seen in related lines of work (deep/exponential families, deep gamma belief networks, recurrent Poisson factorization, deep state-space models, Ladder VAE). The combination proposed here feels incremental. The Probability AutoEncoder objective is nonstandard but, as presented, lacks theoretical justification and resembles consistency/expectation-matching heuristics rather than a novel, well-founded method.

Significance
- If rigorously formulated, a deep/recurrent prior for dynamic PFA could be useful. However, given the current issues in modeling and evaluation, it is hard to assess impact. The empirical claims are not sufficiently substantiated, and the method’s objective lacks clear guarantees. As is, the contribution is unlikely to be adopted or built upon.

Pros
- Tackles an important setting: high-dimensional, sparse, dynamic count data.
- Attempts to integrate interpretable Poisson factorization with deep recurrent structure for long-term dependencies.
- Broad experimental scope across synthetic and multiple real datasets; qualitative discussion of factor interpretability.

Cons
- Ambiguous and partially incorrect derivations; unclear whether a coherent probabilistic model/ELBO underlies the method.
- Ad hoc training objective with no theoretical guarantees.
- Clarity and notation issues; typos and missing details impede reproducibility.
- Evaluation metrics and baselines are suboptimal for count modeling; no held-out likelihood.
- Related work coverage is incomplete; novelty over prior deep/recurrent count models is limited.

Questions for authors
- What is the exact generative model? Please write the full joint distribution p(X, Θ, Φ, Λ, Ψ, H) and specify which networks are generative vs. inference.
- Can you provide an ELBO that your algorithm optimizes, or a principled justification for the squared-error objective? Why not use standard amortized VI with reparameterization or augmentation tricks suitable for Gamma variables?
- How are positivity constraints enforced for network outputs (e.g., softplus for shapes/rates)? How are rates vs. shapes parameterized?
- What is the prediction protocol (one-step-ahead forecasting of counts or factors)? Please report predictive log-likelihood and compare to tuned baselines.
- Can you include ablations (no λ/ψ, shallow vs. deep, with/without ladder), more modern baselines (deep state-space models for counts), and sensitivity analyses?

Typos/clarifications
- Eq. 7 likely has a typo: λ φv should be λ ψv.
- Several posterior expressions (Eq. 8–12) mix indices and constants; please check and correct.
- References are incomplete; several citations in text do not match the bibliography.

Overall recommendation: Reject
Rationale: Despite addressing a relevant problem, the paper lacks a rigorous and clear probabilistic formulation, employs an ad hoc training objective, and does not provide sufficiently convincing or reproducible empirical evidence. Substantial revision is needed to clarify the model, ground the learning method in a principled objective, and strengthen the evaluation.

Confidence: High
