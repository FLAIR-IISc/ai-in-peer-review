- Summary: Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) adding feature- and component-wise positive scales and a deep RNN-driven hierarchical prior over Poisson factorization for sequential count data, with two-stage variational + squared-error “probability autoencoder” training.

- Novelty: Stacking a windowed, ladder-like RNN prior over PF factors and the bespoke autoencoder loss are the paper’s distinguishing elements, though similar deep recurrent latent-variable models exist.

- Strength: Targets high-dimensional, sparse sequential count data and aims to capture richer temporal dependencies than first-order gamma chains.

- Strength: Conceptually interesting fusion of Poisson factorization with deep temporal priors.

- Strength: Empirical results claim improved MSE/PMSE on synthetic and several real datasets versus PGDS, PFA, and LSTM in many cases.

- Strength: Authors demonstrate awareness of hierarchical latent-variable pitfalls (pruning, limited depth utility) and attempt mitigation via ladder-like architecture.

- Major weakness (objective): The “probability autoencoder” squared-error losses are heuristic, not derived as an ELBO or marginal-likelihood bound, breaking probabilistic guarantees and coordinate-ascent monotonicity.

- Major weakness (terminology): Calling the RNN-shaped Gamma conditionals “implicit distributions” is misleading; they are explicit parametric Gammas.

- Major weakness (conjugacy/updates): Paper omits a full ELBO, gradients, and convergence analysis; update equations contain notational errors and unclear terms.

- Major weakness (likelihood mismatch): Squared-error on rates is a poor surrogate for Poisson log-likelihood and may bias learning and probabilistic coherence.

- Major weakness (temporal modeling): Temporal context limited to fixed window c per layer; unclear capacity for true long-range dependencies beyond c×depth and no ablations on c or depth.

- Major weakness (identifiability): Additional λ_k and ψ_v scales risk identifiability issues; priors are weak with no shrinkage or sparsity-inducing discussion.

- Experimental weakness (metrics): Uses nonstandard MSE/PMSE for counts; lacks predictive log-likelihood/perplexity which are more appropriate for PF.

- Experimental weakness (baselines): Missing comparisons to strong deep latent time-series and deep PF baselines (e.g., DMM/VRNN, DEF, gamma belief networks with recurrence).

- Experimental weakness (fairness/details): LSTM baseline applied without proper likelihood/link, insufficient RNN architecture and training details, no runtime/budget comparisons, and unclear K selection strategy.

- Reproducibility: No ELBO, no released code, limited hyperparameter tuning strategy, and unclear statistical significance of reported improvements.

- Clarity: Manuscript contains grammatical errors, ambiguous notation, typos, under-specified network/training details, and incomplete/misaligned references; some formulaic mistakes (e.g., Bernoulli–Poisson indicator).

- Originality assessment: Idea is reasonable and timely but not entirely novel given related deep/recurrent latent-variable literature; main contributions lack theoretical justification.

- Significance: Potentially useful if retrained with principled probabilistic objectives and fair baselines, but current methodological and evaluation shortcomings limit impact.

- Key questions for authors: Can you derive and optimize a principled ELBO including network parameters; how does performance change using Poisson log-likelihood; clarify temporal receptive field; provide corrected updates and full ELBO; add stronger baselines, log-likelihood metrics, ablations, runtime, and code.

- Suggestions: Replace heuristic loss with principled variational objective (with amortized inference if desired), clean up notation/equations/references, use appropriate count-model metrics and baselines, address identifiability/shrinkage, and provide thorough ablations and sensitivity analyses.

- Pros vs. cons: Pros — important problem, interesting model idea, promising signals; Cons — heuristic/unjustified training objective, clarity and notation issues, incomplete related work, nonstandard evaluation, missing strong baselines, reproducibility concerns.

- Overall recommendation: Borderline reject (score 4) with moderate reviewer confidence (3/5).
