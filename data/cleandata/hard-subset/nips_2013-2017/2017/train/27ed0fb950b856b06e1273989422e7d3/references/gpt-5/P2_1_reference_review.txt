Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for sequential count matrices. The observation model is Poisson with rate decomposed into factors φ, time-varying scores θ, and two additional Gamma-scaled parameters λ (component importance) and ψ (feature sparsity). Temporal dependence is modeled via a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t, with windowed recurrent neural networks parameterizing implicit conditional Gamma distributions “forward” (prior) and “backward” (auxiliary posterior). Inference proceeds in two stages: (i) mean-field coordinate-ascent updates for Gamma-distributed variables leveraging Poisson–Gamma conjugacy after introducing latent allocation counts; (ii) learning RNN parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” loss that matches expectations of prior and posterior nets and reconstructs data via squared error. Experiments on synthetic and five real datasets compare to PGDS, PFA, and LSTM.

This paper sits among dynamic Poisson factorization (e.g., PGDS), recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation, Gamma belief networks for counts, deep Poisson factor analysis, and variational RNN/VRNN/Deep Kalman models with Poisson likelihoods. The stated novelty is a deep, windowed recurrent prior/posterior for θ via Gamma parameter nets and an ad-hoc training objective.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The coordinate updates for φ, θ, λ, ψ via Poisson–Gamma conjugacy are standard once the neural net parameters are fixed. However, the “Probability AutoEncoder” objective (Eq. 16–18) is not a derivation of an ELBO or any proven bound. It replaces a principled variational objective with L2 matching of expectations and a reconstruction loss. No guarantees are provided that optimizing this objective approximates the true posterior or maximizes marginal likelihood. This weakens the probabilistic coherence of the method.
- Derivations and notation contain errors/ambiguities. For example, complete conditionals (Eq. 8) seem to mislabel rate hyperparameters (βθ used for λk), and several definitions confuse shapes/rates versus expectations. The specification of p(h(n)t|·) and p(θt|·) as Gamma with RNN-determined “shape” requires strict positivity constraints and careful parameterization, which are not described. The use of both λk and ψv multiplies the usual non-identifiability in factor models; no constraints or regularization beyond vague Gamma priors are discussed.
- The deep temporal structure is insufficiently specified: what RNN variants are used (LSTM/GRU/vanilla), activations ensuring positivity, window size rationale, how α and γ are combined (Eq. 14), and how the top layer prior is defined. There is no complexity/runtime analysis.
- Experimental methodology: Metrics (MSE/PMSE) for high-dimensional sparse counts can be dominated by zeros; predictive log-likelihood/perplexity would be more appropriate. Key details (train/validation splits, early stopping, hyperparameter selection, uncertainty metrics) are missing. Results are summarized qualitatively without tables/figures here; even in the text DDPFA underperforms on ICEWS prediction. No statistical significance or variance is reported.
- Comparisons omit several strong and closely related baselines (e.g., recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation; deep gamma belief networks with temporal links; VRNN/DKS with Poisson likelihoods; dynamic topic models). Without these, it is hard to assess true gains from the proposed deep recurrent prior.

Clarity
The paper is difficult to follow due to numerous grammatical issues, inconsistent notation, missing definitions (e.g., Gamma parameterization), and incomplete references (many entries lack years/venues). The generative model and inference steps should be formalized more cleanly, with pseudocode and clear parameterization choices. Figures/tables referenced are not provided here; in the full paper, they should be legible and reproducible. As written, an expert reader could not reproduce the method.

Originality
The idea of placing deep/recurrent structure on Poisson factor scores is timely, but similar ideas exist: PGDS addresses dynamics; recurrent PF for recommendation models long-term effects; deep PF and gamma belief networks capture hierarchical count structure; VRNN-style models use RNN-parameterized distributions. The specific “Probability AutoEncoder” loss appears novel but is ad-hoc and weaker than existing amortized variational methods. The added λ and ψ factors are not new and may exacerbate identifiability.

Significance
If robustly validated, a deep dynamic PF could be useful for sequential count modeling. However, given (i) the lack of a principled inference objective or theory, (ii) unclear/possibly flawed derivations, and (iii) limited empirical validation and comparisons, the current contribution is not yet convincing enough to advance the state of the art for NeurIPS.

Strengths
- Addresses an important problem: long-range temporal dependencies in sparse count matrices.
- Leverages conjugacy for efficient coordinate updates.
- Interpretable factor structure, with some qualitative insights on ICEWS-like data.
- Acknowledges practical issues (“pruning”) and attempts a ladder-style variant.

Weaknesses
- Inference objective is not an ELBO or bound; training is ad-hoc.
- Derivational/notation errors and missing details undermine confidence in correctness.
- Model identifiability and positivity constraints not addressed.
- Omitted strong baselines; evaluation metrics suboptimal; lack of uncertainty/log-likelihood reporting.
- Writing quality and references not at NeurIPS standard; reproducibility is low.

Questions for authors
- Can you derive a principled ELBO (e.g., via reparameterization or augmentation) and train the nets by maximizing it? If not, what guarantees does your loss provide?
- How do you ensure positivity of Gamma parameters output by RNNs?
- Why are both λ and ψ needed given scale non-identifiability? Have you tried removing one?
- Please report predictive log-likelihood and include stronger baselines (recurrent PF, deep gamma belief networks, VRNN with Poisson).
- Provide ablations on depth, window size, and the ladder vs non-ladder variant, with runtime and sensitivity analyses.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Relevant problem and intuitive high-level idea (deep/recurrent priors for Poisson factors).
- Some empirical improvements claimed across diverse datasets.

Cons:
- Weak theoretical grounding and potential technical errors.
- Insufficient clarity and reproducibility.
- Missing key baselines and stronger metrics; mixed empirical story.

Recommendation
Reject (leaning to weak reject). The paper tackles an important topic but falls short on quality and clarity, and the novelty is not convincingly validated. With a principled variational objective, cleaner derivations, stronger comparisons, and improved presentation, a future version could be competitive.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
