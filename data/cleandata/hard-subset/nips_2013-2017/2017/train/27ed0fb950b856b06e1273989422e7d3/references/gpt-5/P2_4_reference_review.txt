Summary:
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA), a sequential count modeling framework that combines Poisson factorization with a deep hierarchy of latent variables and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) that parameterize implicit conditional distributions over time. Each count xvt is decomposed into latent counts xvtk with Poisson rates θtk φvk λk ψv. Temporal dependence is modeled via a multi-layer structure h(n)t, where RNNs produce Gamma prior/posterior parameters across a local temporal window. Inference alternates mean-field variational updates for Gamma-conjugate blocks (Θ, Φ, Λ, Ψ, H) with a “Probability AutoEncoder” training stage that fits RNN parameters using L2 losses between expectations under variational posteriors and network outputs. Experiments on four synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE and next-step PMSE, reporting improvements in most cases and qualitative factor trajectories.

Relation to prior work:
The model builds on Poisson factorization for count data (e.g., beta/gamma-negative binomial processes; Zhou et al.), dynamic PF with Gamma transitions (PGDS), deep PF variants (deep exponential families; deep Poisson factor models), and RNN-based modeling of temporal structure. The idea of using neural networks to parameterize conditional distributions is related to amortized inference and neural topic models with temporal dynamics. The “Probability AutoEncoder” echoes VAE-style objectives and ladder architectures. However, connections to closely related temporal topic models (e.g., dynamic topic models, recurrent neural topic models), recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendations, and deep PF with explicit temporal priors are not comprehensively discussed, and several citations are incomplete.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important, practical setting: high-dimensional, sparse sequential count data with long-range dependencies.
- Conceptually appealing integration of Poisson factorization with deep temporal structure via RNN-parameterized Gamma priors.
- Uses conjugate structure to retain efficient coordinate-ascent VI for core latent variables.
- Includes both synthetic and multiple real datasets, with qualitative analyses of factor activations over time.
- Identifies practical challenges (“pruning,” inefficacy of deeper layers) and discusses potential remedies (ladder architecture).

Weaknesses:
- Inference objective for the neural components is ad hoc. The “Probability AutoEncoder” L2 losses (Eqs. 16–18) are not derived from a principled ELBO or likelihood; there is no guarantee of consistent or convergent joint inference. This undermines theoretical soundness.
- The paper alternates mean-field VI with neural network training, but does not present a unified objective, gradients, or convergence properties. No discussion of bias introduced by decoupled training.
- Evaluation metrics (MSE/PMSE) are mismatched to count models; predictive log-likelihood, Poisson deviance, or held-out perplexity would be more appropriate. Uncertainty calibration is not assessed.
- Baselines are limited. Missing comparisons to dynamic topic models, neural dynamic topic models, deep PF with temporal dependencies, and recurrent Poisson factorization baselines that are closer in spirit than a plain LSTM.
- Clarity and presentation need significant improvement: notation is inconsistent; several equations have typos; the role of λk and ψv versus simple scaling ambiguities is not discussed; the deep hierarchy and boundary conditions are hard to follow; references are incomplete.
- Reproducibility is weak: key architectural choices (RNN type, hidden sizes per layer, c window effects), training details, hyperparameter tuning, and computational cost are not provided; no code.
- Empirical section lacks statistical rigor: no standard errors, significance tests, or ablations beyond a brief table; only last-step forecasting is evaluated on very short sequences (e.g., T=17 for NIPS).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: Fair to weak. The conjugate parts are standard and correct, but the neural training objective is not grounded in a proper variational bound. Important algorithmic details are missing.
- Empirical support: Mixed. Some improvements are reported, but metrics, baselines, and reporting are insufficient to support strong claims.

Clarity:
- Below average. Notation is often ambiguous; grammar/typos hinder comprehension; several key design choices are under-specified. The relationship between prior and posterior RNNs and their parameter outputs should be made much clearer. Figures/tables are referenced but not described in enough detail to reproduce results.

Originality:
- Moderate. The combination of PF with RNN-parameterized Gamma priors and a ladder-like deep hierarchy is interesting, but related ideas exist (deep PF, neural topic models with temporal dynamics, recurrent PF). The “Probability AutoEncoder” objective appears novel but lacks theoretical justification.

Significance:
- Currently limited by the weak theoretical grounding and empirical evaluation. With a principled ELBO/unified training and stronger experiments, the approach could be impactful for temporal count modeling.

Pros for acceptance:
- Tackles a relevant problem with a creative, modular architecture.
- Retains conjugacy for efficient updates while attempting to capture long-range temporal dependencies.
- Qualitative factor analyses suggest potentially improved interpretability.

Cons for acceptance:
- Lack of a principled training objective for the neural components undermines correctness.
- Insufficient baselines and inappropriate metrics weaken empirical claims.
- Clarity and completeness issues impede reproducibility and review.
- Redundant scaling variables (Φ, Λ, Ψ, Θ) raise identifiability concerns not addressed.

Questions for authors:
- Can you derive a single ELBO for the full model that includes the RNN-parameterized priors/posteriors and optimize it end-to-end? Why use L2 losses rather than maximizing a bound on predictive likelihood?
- How are α and β parameters constrained to ensure valid Gamma distributions and stable training? Do you use softplus or other positivity transforms?
- Please report predictive log-likelihood/Poisson deviance and compare to dynamic topic models and recurrent PF baselines. How sensitive are results to c, K, depth N?
- How do you address identifiability/scaling degeneracy among Φ, Λ, Ψ, and Θ?
- Provide computational complexity, wall-clock times, and code.

Overall recommendation: Reject in current form. The paper targets an important problem with a promising idea, but lacks a principled inference objective, strong baselines/metrics, and clear exposition. With a unified ELBO-based training, clearer model specification, stronger experimental protocol, and improved writing, this work could become much more compelling.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
