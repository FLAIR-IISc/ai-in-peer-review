Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA), a model for sequential count data X ∈ RV×T+. As in Poisson Factorization (PF/PFA), xvt is modeled via a sum over K latent factors with nonnegative loadings φvk and time-varying factor strengths θtk; additional Gamma-distributed feature- and component-wise scalars ψv and λk are introduced to encourage sparsity and smoothness. The paper’s main contribution is a deep temporal prior on Θ: a hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t (n = 0..N) coupled across time within a sliding window via neural networks. Specifically, Γ distributions parameterized by RNNs (feed-forward in time windows) define “implicit” conditional priors and auxiliary “posterior” mappings (recognition networks). Inference alternates: (i) mean-field VI with Gamma factors for Θ,Φ,Ψ,Λ,H using standard Poisson–Gamma conjugacy with multinomial augmentation; (ii) learning the neural network parameters with a custom “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that matches moments between recognition/prior networks and adds a reconstruction term on X via expected Poisson rate.

The work relates to dynamic PF and count models (PGDS; Gamma–NB processes), deep latent variable models (deep exponential families, Gamma belief networks, deep Poisson factorization via RBM/SBN), and neural sequence models (LSTM). It also connects to amortized VI and VAE-style recognition networks, ladder architectures, and deep Markov models/VRNNs in spirit, though the loss deviates from a principled ELBO.

Strengths
- Addresses an important setting: high-dimensional sparse sequential count data common in text, events, and recommender systems.
- Combines PF with a deep temporal prior intended to capture both short-range (windowed) and long-range dependencies, going beyond first-order Markov Gamma dynamics.
- Inference for the PF core is standard, efficient, and scalable (multinomial augmentation; Gamma updates).
- Empirical results on synthetic and multiple real datasets are promising by the reported metrics (MSE/PMSE), and the qualitative analysis claims more persistent factor activations than PGDS.
- The authors are candid about observed issues (pruning; diminishing returns from depth) and point to relevant literature (auxiliary variables; ladder architectures).

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation

Quality (technical soundness and validation)
- Objective/learning: The central weakness is the training objective for the neural temporal prior. The paper does not derive a single coherent ELBO that includes the RNN-parameterized conditionals. Instead, it proposes a heuristic “Probability AutoEncoder” loss that matches expectations with L2 distances and adds a reconstruction term on X via expected rates. This lacks a probabilistic justification, bias is unclear, and no guarantees (or even a bound) are provided. By contrast, amortized VI with an ELBO (reparameterization, score function, or implicit reparameterization for Gamma) is standard and practical. The stated reason that “few samples are drawn… so differentiable variational Bayes is not suitable” is not convincing.
- Inference consistency: Mean-field updates use α parameters produced by neural nets from expectations of other latent variables (ffeed(⟨h⟩), fback(⟨h⟩)), but the resulting procedure mixes coordinate updates with heuristic amortization. It is unclear whether this converges or what it optimizes.
- Likelihood and metrics: Evaluation uses MSE and PMSE on counts. For count models, held-out predictive log likelihood, Poisson deviance, or perplexity (for text) are more appropriate. Without likelihood-based evaluation, it’s hard to assess whether the model improves probabilistic fit.
- Baselines: Comparisons omit strong modern baselines that also model temporal dependencies with neural amortization and Poisson/NB outputs (e.g., dynamic topic models; deep Markov models/VRNN with Poisson emissions; recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation). The LSTM baseline is not tailored to counts (loss function not specified; Poisson/NB likelihood vs MSE).
- Ablation/complexity: No ablation isolating the contribution of the deep hierarchy vs. a single RNN prior on Θ, or of ψ and λ. No runtime or memory analysis vs PGDS/PFA. Sensitivity to K, window size c, and depth is only partially explored (Table 3 mentions diminishing returns but details are thin).

Clarity
- The paper is difficult to follow in places. Equations mix notation (e.g., Λ and Ψ sometimes “absorbed”, sometimes not). The role of λk and ψv beyond shrinkage is not empirically analyzed. Equations (11)–(14) omit some indices and rely on digamma-based expectations but do not fully spell out the coordinate updates.
- The “implicit distribution” terminology is confusing; the conditionals are explicit Gamma distributions parameterized by neural nets.
- The Probability AutoEncoder section (Eqs. 15–18) is informal and uses expressions like maximizing ∫ p(Θ|H(0)) p(H(0)|Θ) dH(0) with no derivation; the transition to squared-error terms between expectations is ad hoc.
- References are often incomplete and several key related works are missing or under-cited (e.g., Blei & Lafferty’s Dynamic Topic Models; Deep Kalman Filters; VRNN/Deep Markov Models; neural variational topic models with temporal dynamics).

Originality
- The idea of placing a neural temporal prior on PF is reasonable, but similar combinations exist: recurrent Poisson factorization (Hosseini et al.), dynamic topic models, neural/deep Markov models with Poisson/NB likelihoods, deep exponential families with temporal couplings. The novelty here is the specific deep hierarchical windowed prior plus the two-network (prior/recognizer) Gamma parameterization. However, without a principled training objective, the contribution is more an engineering variant than a clear theoretical advance.

Significance
- If the method were put on a principled variational footing and compared with appropriate baselines via likelihood-based metrics, it could be impactful for temporal count modeling. As is, the significance is limited by methodological uncertainty and evaluation choices. The qualitative insights (e.g., more persistent factor activations) are interesting but insufficient alone.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Can you derive a single ELBO that includes both the PF likelihood and the deep temporal prior, with amortized inference networks? Consider implicit reparameterization gradients for Gamma or augmentations (e.g., Kumaraswamy/Weibull approximations, shape augmentation) to enable end-to-end training.
- Evaluate with held-out predictive log likelihood/Poisson deviance/perplexity. Report standard errors over multiple splits.
- Add baselines: dynamic topic models; VRNN/Deep Markov Model with Poisson/NB emissions; recurrent Poisson factorization; deep PF variants (e.g., Gamma belief networks with temporal edges).
- Provide ablations: remove ψ/λ; shallow vs deep; only Θ-level RNN prior; window size; with/without ladder connections. Analyze runtime scalability.
- Clarify and correct the “Probability AutoEncoder” section. If you keep the heuristic loss, justify it theoretically (e.g., as minimizing moment-matching divergences) and discuss trade-offs.
- Clarify boundary conditions for the temporal windows, parameterizations ensuring positivity (e.g., softplus), and the exact architectures and training details (hidden sizes per layer, activation functions, optimizers, learning rates).
- Use likelihood-based losses for LSTM baselines (Poisson/NB) to ensure fairness.

Minor issues
- Many typos/grammar; incomplete references (missing venues/years/authors in several entries).
- Equation (11) notation is hard to parse; ensure consistent use of indices and φ, ψ symbols.
- Figures and tables referenced but not sufficiently described in text (we cannot assess statistical significance).

Pros
- Targets an important problem (temporal counts).
- Sensible combination of PF with neural temporal structure.
- Scalable PF core; promising qualitative behavior.

Cons
- Lack of a principled variational objective; heuristic training.
- Incomplete and sometimes unclear exposition.
- Evaluation metrics and baselines not fully appropriate; limited ablations.
- References incomplete and missing key related work.

Recommendation
Score: 4 (reject). The idea is promising, but the current paper lacks a sound training objective, thorough and appropriate evaluation, and clarity. With a principled ELBO-based approach, stronger baselines/metrics, and clearer exposition, this work could be significantly strengthened.

Confidence: medium.
