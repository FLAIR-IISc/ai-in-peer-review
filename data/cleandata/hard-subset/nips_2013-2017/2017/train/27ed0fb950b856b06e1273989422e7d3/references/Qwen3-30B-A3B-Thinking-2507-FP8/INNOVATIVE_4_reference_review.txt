

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a novel approach to modeling sequential count data by incorporating deep recurrent neural networks into Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) to capture long-term temporal dependencies. The authors build on previous work like PGDS (Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System) and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis, but introduce a hierarchical deep structure where latent temporal factors are modeled through multiple layers of recurrent networks. The key innovation is using this deep architecture to model complex time dependencies that shallow models cannot capture, with variational inference for parameter estimation.

The paper makes a compelling case for the necessity of deep structure in temporal count data analysis. Previous methods like PGDS use simple Gamma transitions that assume weak time dependence (θ_t dependent only on θ_{t-1}), while LSTM struggles with high-dimensional or long-sequence data. The DDPFA model's deep structure with multiple layers of recurrent networks allows it to capture more complex temporal patterns. The authors demonstrate this on synthetic datasets with various time patterns (SDS1-4) and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, Ebola, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population), showing competitive or superior performance compared to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA baselines.

**Strengths:**
1. **High Novelty**: The core contribution—using deep recurrent networks to model temporal dependencies in Poisson factorization—is genuinely novel. While there's work on deep Bayesian models (e.g., Deep Exponential Families), applying this to sequential count data with a hierarchical temporal structure represents a significant conceptual leap that addresses a clear gap in the literature.

2. **Strong Motivation**: The paper effectively identifies the limitations of existing methods (shallow temporal dependencies in PGDS, high-dimensional challenges for LSTM) and positions DDPFA as a solution that captures both short-term and long-term patterns.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experiments cover diverse synthetic and real-world datasets, showing the model's versatility across different applications (recommendation systems, text analysis, epidemiology, etc.).

4. **Clear Theoretical Foundation**: The mathematical formulation builds logically on established Poisson-Gamma conjugacy, with a well-structured variational inference framework.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Novelty in Inference**: While the model structure is novel, the inference method (variational inference with RNNs) is largely standard. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its inference approach from recent work on deep variational models.

2. **"Pruning" Problem**: The paper acknowledges a significant issue where many latent connections become inactive ("pruning"), undermining the claimed benefits of the deep structure. This problem isn't adequately addressed or mitigated.

3. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims DDPFA "captures long-time dependence" but doesn't provide a clear theoretical justification for why their specific deep structure is superior to simpler alternatives.

4. **Lack of Ablation Study**: The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate how different components of the model contribute to performance, making it hard to assess the true value of the deep structure versus other elements.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. While the paper has methodological limitations, its core conceptual contribution is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant publication at NIPS. The authors have identified a meaningful gap in the literature and proposed a bold new direction for modeling sequential count data. The minor flaws in inference implementation and the "pruning" issue don't diminish the overall significance of the new approach.

The paper aligns perfectly with NIPS's emphasis on novel, impactful ideas. The authors' bold approach to incorporating deep learning into Poisson factorization for temporal data analysis represents a genuine new direction that could inspire future work in Bayesian time series modeling. While the technical execution has room for improvement, the conceptual contribution is strong enough to overcome these limitations.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Novel conceptual framework for capturing temporal dependencies in count data
- Clear identification of limitations in existing methods
- Demonstrated utility across diverse real-world applications
- Well-structured mathematical formulation

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Inference method not sufficiently novel
- "Pruning" problem undermines claimed benefits
- Some overstated claims about model capabilities
- Limited ablation study to validate deep structure's contribution

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by introducing a new direction for analyzing sequential count data, and I believe it would be a meaningful addition to the NIPS proceedings.
