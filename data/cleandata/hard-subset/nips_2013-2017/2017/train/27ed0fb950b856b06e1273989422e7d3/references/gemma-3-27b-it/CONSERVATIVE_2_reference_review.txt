## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) leveraging recurrent neural networks within a Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) framework to model sequentially observed count data. The motivation, to capture complex temporal dependencies beyond those addressed by existing dynamic factor models like PGDS and temporal Dirichlet processes, is reasonable. The authors aim to improve upon existing methods by incorporating a deep structure to model both short- and long-term dependencies.

While the idea of integrating deep learning with established Bayesian factor models is not entirely novel (as highlighted by the authors themselves with references to Deep Exponential Families and similar approaches), the specific combination with PFA and recurrent networks, and the variational inference approach, present a potential contribution. However, the presentation and empirical validation fall short of convincing me of the significance of this contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of modeling sequential count data and identifies limitations in existing approaches.
*   **Reasonable Model Formulation:** The combination of PFA with RNNs to capture temporal dynamics is a conceptually sound approach. The attempt to address sparsity through the inclusion of Λ and Ψ is also positive.
*   **Variational Inference:** Utilizing variational inference is a practical approach given the complexity of the proposed model.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** The experimental evaluation, while including several datasets, lacks depth. The comparison to baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) is superficial. The parameter settings for baselines are simply stated as being "set in [2]" or are otherwise not fully detailed, hindering reproducibility and fair comparison. Crucially, there’s no ablation study demonstrating the contribution of the deep structure *relative* to a simpler PFA with a standard Gamma transition, or a shallow RNN layered on top of PFA.
*   **Insufficient Justification of Architectural Choices:** The choice of RNN architecture and hyperparameters (window size, layer sizes) feels arbitrary. The paper doesn't provide a rationale for these choices, nor does it explore sensitivity to these parameters. Why RNNs instead of, for example, Transformers which are demonstrably better at capturing long-range dependencies?
*   **Overstated Claims:** The authors claim “excellent results” based on the presented tables, but the improvements over baselines are often marginal, particularly on the real-world datasets. The results in Table 2 are often not statistically significant or demonstrate consistent improvement.
*   **Vague Discussion of Limitations:** While acknowledging the “pruning” problem and the limitations of mean-field variational inference, the authors don't adequately discuss the potential impact of these issues on the results or propose concrete future directions for mitigation.
*   **Presentation Issues:** The notation is sometimes unclear and the writing could be more concise. The discussion of the variational inference process feels unnecessarily complex and difficult to follow.
*   **Redundancy:** The lengthy list of references to related work, while demonstrating awareness of the field, feels excessive and doesn't meaningfully contribute to the paper's argument.

**Specific Concerns:**

*   The performance of DDPFA in the ICEWS prediction task is *worse* than PGDS, which the authors downplay. This raises serious concerns about the model's ability to generalize.
*   The results in Table 3, showing that adding layers doesn’t consistently improve performance, are acknowledged but not adequately addressed. This suggests a fundamental issue with the model’s architecture or the inference procedure.
*   The use of MSE as the sole evaluation metric is limiting. Considering metrics relevant to count data (e.g., Root Mean Squared Poisson Deviation) might provide a more nuanced evaluation.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

*   **Against:** The paper presents an incremental contribution with limited empirical validation. The lack of a rigorous ablation study and the marginal improvements over baselines do not justify acceptance to NIPS. The architectural choices are not well justified and the limitations are not adequately addressed. The presentation could be significantly improved.
*   **For:** The idea of combining deep learning with PFA is interesting and the variational inference approach is well-motivated. The paper demonstrates a reasonable understanding of the existing literature.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the idea holds some promise, the paper lacks the technical depth, rigorous evaluation, and clarity required for publication at NIPS. The authors need to significantly strengthen the experimental validation, provide a more thorough analysis of the model's limitations, and improve the presentation before this work could be considered for publication. A major revision with a substantially more compelling empirical analysis would be necessary.
