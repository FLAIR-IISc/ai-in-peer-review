

# Review: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a novel approach for analyzing sequentially observed count data. Building upon Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS), the authors introduce a deep hierarchical structure with multiple layers of latent variables to capture complex long-term temporal dependencies. The model incorporates recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions and uses variational inference for parameter estimation. The authors evaluate DDPFA on both synthetic datasets and five real-world applications (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population), comparing against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA baselines.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-motivated mathematical foundation. The model extends previous work by introducing a deep structure to capture long-term dependencies, which is a valid and meaningful extension of Bayesian factor analysis. The variational inference framework is properly formulated, and the mean-field approximation is correctly applied. The experimental design is comprehensive, with both synthetic and real-world datasets, and multiple evaluation metrics (MSE and PMSE).

However, the paper has some quality concerns. The theoretical justification for why a deep structure is necessary rather than a simpler recurrent structure (like LSTM) is weak. The experimental results show that DDPFA performs poorly on the ICEWS prediction task compared to PGDS, but the paper fails to adequately explain this limitation. The authors acknowledge the "pruning" issue in Section 5, where some latent dimensions only contain noise, but don't provide a thorough analysis of this limitation or how it might be mitigated.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from introduction to experiments. The mathematical formulation is mostly clear, though some concepts (like the deep structure of latent variables) could benefit from more intuitive explanations for readers unfamiliar with Bayesian factorization. The experimental setup is sufficiently detailed to allow reproduction, though some hyperparameters (like the specific neural network architectures beyond layer size) could be more explicitly stated.

The paper's clarity is somewhat hindered by its dense mathematical notation. While the technical depth is appropriate for NIPS, some concepts could be better illustrated with diagrams or more concrete examples. The figures (2 and 3) help visualize the factor activation patterns but could be more informative.

## Originality

The core idea of combining deep learning with Poisson factorization for temporal count data is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from previous work, particularly noting the limitations of shallow models like PGDS in capturing long-term dependencies. The deep structure with multiple layers of latent variables is a distinctive feature.

However, the paper's novelty is somewhat limited by the fact that deep extensions to factor analysis have been explored before (e.g., Deep Exponential Families, Deep Poisson Factor Analysis). The authors do cite these works, but could better articulate how DDPFA differs from these prior deep approaches specifically in the context of temporal count data. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent deep learning approaches to time series analysis.

## Significance

The problem of analyzing sequential count data is significant, with applications in recommendation systems, text analysis, and network analysis. The paper demonstrates that DDPFA can outperform existing methods in many cases, particularly for capturing complex temporal patterns. The results on the EBOLA and ASP datasets are particularly compelling, showing where PGDS and LSTM fail to capture the necessary dependencies.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the fact that the performance gains over baselines are not consistently large across all datasets. The ICEWS prediction task shows DDPFA underperforming PGDS, which suggests the model may not be universally superior. The paper also doesn't provide strong evidence that the deep structure is necessary for the observed improvements, as the authors acknowledge that adding more layers doesn't consistently improve performance.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in sequential count data analysis with clear real-world applications.
2. The deep structure with multiple layers of latent variables is a novel approach to capturing long-term dependencies.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive across diverse datasets.
4. The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach (e.g., "pruning" issue, limited performance on ICEWS).

**Weaknesses:**
1. The theoretical justification for the deep structure is weak compared to simpler recurrent approaches.
2. The paper doesn't adequately explain why DDPFA underperforms on the ICEWS prediction task.
3. The significance of the deep structure is not sufficiently demonstrated, as adding more layers doesn't consistently improve performance.
4. The comparison with more recent deep learning approaches to time series analysis is limited.

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The work represents a technically sound extension to the field of dynamic count data analysis with clear real-world applications. The model is novel in its specific combination of deep learning with Poisson factorization for temporal dependencies, and the experimental results are generally supportive of the approach.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses in its theoretical justification and explanation of limitations. The performance gains over baselines are not consistently large, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why a deep structure is necessary rather than simpler recurrent approaches. The "pruning" issue and poor performance on ICEWS need more thorough discussion.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis of why a deep structure is beneficial for this problem
2. Better explain the ICEWS performance discrepancy
3. Compare against more recent deep learning approaches to time series analysis
4. Provide a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential solutions to the "pruning" issue

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field but doesn't significantly advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. It's a solid piece of work that would be appropriate for a conference like NIPS, but with the suggested improvements, it could be stronger.
