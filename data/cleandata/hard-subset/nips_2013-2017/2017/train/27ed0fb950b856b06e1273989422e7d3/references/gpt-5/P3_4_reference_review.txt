Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA) for sequential count data. Observations x_vt are modeled as Poisson with rate ∑_k θ_tk φ_vk λ_k ψ_v, where φ, θ, λ, ψ are positive latent factors. To capture temporal dependencies in θ, the authors introduce a hierarchical temporal latent structure h^(n)_t (n=0..N) parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs): a “prior” network feeds α-parameters (Gamma shapes) from higher layers and past windows, and an auxiliary “posterior” network feeds γ-parameters from lower layers and future windows. Inference alternates: (i) mean-field coordinate ascent updates for conjugate Gamma-Poisson parts using latent count augmentation; (ii) training the RNNs via a custom “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that matches expectations of conditional distributions and reconstruction terms via L2 losses, akin to a ladder-style VAE but without an explicit ELBO. Experiments on synthetic sequences and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, Int’l Disasters, Sheep) compare DDPFA against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE and next-step PMSE; DDPFA is claimed to perform best on most datasets.

Relation to prior work
The model builds on Poisson Factorization (PF) and dynamic PF with Gamma transitions (e.g., Zhou et al.; PGDS), and connects to deep latent models (DEFs, gamma belief networks) and amortized inference paradigms (VAE, ladder VAE). It also overlaps with recurrent Poisson models (e.g., Recurrent Poisson Factorization for recommendation) and dynamic topic models. The idea of combining PF with RNN-based priors to capture complex temporal dependencies is natural and timely. However, the paper does not position itself clearly against closely related deep temporal PF variants, and the training objective differs substantially from standard ELBO-based amortized VI.

Strengths
- Addresses an important problem: modeling long-range dependencies in high-dimensional sparse count sequences.
- Combines conjugate PF structure with amortized neural components for temporal dynamics; uses count augmentation to retain tractable mean-field updates for φ, ψ, λ, θ.
- Attempts to incorporate multi-scale temporal structure via depth (windows per layer; deeper layers intended to capture longer horizons).
- Empirical evaluation covers several datasets where PF-based models are commonly used, and offers some interpretability anecdotes (ICEWS factors).

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical soundness
- The “Probability AutoEncoder” objective (Eqs. 15–18) is ad hoc. The paper abandons an ELBO in favor of L2 losses between expectations of conditional distributions and a reconstruction term on X. There is no theoretical justification that optimizing this objective corresponds to maximizing a lower bound on the marginal likelihood or yields consistent estimators. Well-established alternatives exist (reparameterized gamma variational families; black-box VI; SG-MCMC).
- The hierarchical Gamma updates for h^(n) (Eq. 10, 14) sum shape parameters α and γ from separate RNNs. This is not a standard conjugate update and needs derivation. As written, p(h^(n)_t|·) is “implicit” yet treated as Gamma; the probabilistic semantics are unclear.
- Temporal posterior networks (fback) use future windows (t..t+c). This constitutes smoothing, which can be appropriate for training. However, the paper does not detail how prediction is performed without leakage. If θ_t or h^(n)_t at train time incorporate future observations via γ or via the posterior network, one-step-ahead evaluation must ensure only past is used to infer states and produce predictions. The procedure is not specified and risks data leakage.
- Notation and some conditionals are inconsistent/incorrect (e.g., Eq. 8 typo “Φ” twice; Eq. 9 index ambiguity x_{v·k} vs x_{·tk}; Bernoulli–Poisson link uses indicator 1(x>1) rather than 1(x≥1)). Several equations appear dimensionally ambiguous.

Clarity and completeness
- Writing needs significant polishing; many grammatical issues, undefined notation, and incomplete references (missing years/venues). The model description mixes “implicit distributions,” deterministic neural outputs, and Gamma priors/posteriors without a precise generative story and inference factorization.
- The training algorithm is not described with sufficient detail for reproduction (e.g., exact prediction pipeline, handling of missing steps, batching, initialization, optimizer details, runtime/complexity).

Originality
- The architectural idea—PF with deep temporal RNN priors and ladder-style auxiliary networks—is a reasonable extension of DEFs/GBNs/PGDS. However, similar combinations exist (e.g., recurrent PF for temporal recommendation, deep Poisson models with RNNs). The main originality is the specific two-network (prior/posterior) Gamma-shape parameterization and the custom objective, but the latter is not theoretically grounded.

Empirical evaluation and significance
- Metrics (MSE/PMSE) are not standard for count models; predictive log-likelihood or Poisson deviance are more appropriate. Using only the last time step for prediction is weak; multiple rolling splits and confidence intervals are needed to assess robustness.
- Baselines: LSTM details are unclear (Poisson loss vs. MSE? scaling?), hidden size likely underpowered for high-dimensional ICEWS; important baselines are missing (dynamic topic models, recurrent PF variants, Hawkes/Poisson processes with history, Deep Poisson RNN models cited as [12, 23]). Hyperparameter tuning fairness is not addressed.
- Claims about depth and long-range dependence are made, but ablations are limited and inconclusive; the paper itself acknowledges “pruning” and minimal gains from added layers.

Specific questions and suggestions
1) Provide a principled ELBO and derive amortized VI with reparameterized Gamma (e.g., implicit reparam gradients for Gamma, or augmentation-based reparam) or BBVI with control variates. Justify the current objective theoretically or replace it.
2) Clarify the full generative model, including exact conditional distributions for h^(n), parametrization (shape/rate), and how the networks define these conditionals.
3) Describe precisely the prediction protocol to avoid future leakage. Ideally, compare filtering vs. smoothing for training and strictly use past-only information for one-step-ahead forecasts.
4) Report predictive log-likelihood (or Poisson deviance) and perform rolling forecasts over multiple steps. Include uncertainty estimates.
5) Strengthen baselines: recurrent Poisson factorization, deep Poisson models with RNNs, dynamic topic models. Ensure comparable model capacity and fair tuning.
6) Improve clarity: fix notation, equation indices, and references; provide algorithm pseudocode and complexity analysis.
7) Add ablations: window size c; depth N; with/without λ and ψ; with/without ladder/posterior networks.
8) Consider scalable sparse updates leveraging nnz(X) and minibatch SVI; report training time.

Pros
- Timely problem; idea of combining PF with deep temporal priors is compelling.
- Uses conjugacy where possible; attempts to address known issues in hierarchical VAEs (ladder architecture).
- Some qualitative interpretability analyses.

Cons
- Inference objective is not principled; potential data leakage; unclear probabilistic semantics.
- Clarity and rigor issues in model and derivations; incomplete references.
- Evaluation metrics/protocols and baselines are insufficient to support strong claims.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper tackles an important problem with a potentially interesting hybrid approach, but the current formulation lacks theoretical grounding for its training objective, has clarity/notation issues, and does not provide sufficiently rigorous and fair empirical validation. With a principled ELBO-based inference, clearer generative/inference descriptions, and stronger experiments, this line could be impactful. Confidence: medium.
