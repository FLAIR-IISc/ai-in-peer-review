Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count data. Observations xvt are modeled by a Poisson factorization with factors φvk, time-varying scores θtk, and feature- and factor-wise scalings ψv and λk. Temporal dependence is modeled via a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t with a recurrent structure: p(θt | h(0)t−c:t) and p(h(n)t | h(n+1)t−c:t) are “implicit” (neural) distributions. Inference is via a two-stage variational scheme: (i) mean-field updates for Gamma-Poisson parts using auxiliary multinomial allocations xvtk; and (ii) training the RNN parameters using a “Probability AutoEncoder” loss that matches expectations of prior/posterior networks with L2 losses. Experiments on synthetic and five real datasets compare DDPFA against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA, reporting MSE/PMSE improvements on most tasks and qualitative factor visualizations.

Relation to prior work
This work aims to combine Poisson factorization (e.g., PFA, PGDS, negative-binomial/gamma processes) with deep/recurrent latent structures (DEFs, deep PF with RBM/SBN, Gamma Belief Networks, recurrent PF, Ladder VAE). The idea of driving Poisson factors with temporal RNN-style dynamics is natural and has appeared in related forms (e.g., recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendations, deep exponential families). The proposed combination—RNNs that output Gamma parameters for hierarchical temporal latents—could be novel, but the relationship to these lines of work is not clearly delineated, and several references are incomplete.

Strengths
- Targets an important problem: modeling long-range, nonlinear temporal dependence in high-dimensional sparse count sequences.
- Uses a modular construction that preserves some conjugacy (Poisson–Gamma) for efficient coordinate updates over φ, λ, ψ, θ.
- Attempts to address known issues in hierarchical VAEs (e.g., pruning) and explores “ladder-like” connections.
- Empirical results suggest gains over PFA, PGDS, and LSTM on several datasets; qualitative analyses (factors over time) are potentially insightful.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Inference objective: The “Probability AutoEncoder” loss is not a principled ELBO or a known variational bound. The derivation moves from an intractable objective to minimizing L2 distances between expectations of distributions and reconstruction errors. There is no guarantee this optimizes a lower bound on the marginal likelihood or is consistent. This weakens claims of probabilistic learning and makes comparisons to likelihood-based baselines problematic.
- Conjugacy claims vs. implicit priors: The paper asserts conditional conjugacy for θ,h when the priors are “implicit distributions modeled as RNNs.” In practice, the RNN outputs Gamma shape parameters, and the posterior is stated as Gamma with shape equal to a sum of “prior” and “posterior” pseudo-counts (Eq. 10). This is ad hoc and not derived from a coherent generative model; it’s unclear what joint density is being optimized.
- Clarity/notation: Several definitions are ambiguous. The roles of αh(n) and γh(n) (prior vs. posterior networks) and why their shapes add are not justified. The networks (RNN vs. LSTM/GRU), activations, and parameterizations (ensuring positivity) are not specified. References are incomplete. Some equations contain typos (e.g., λ/φ subscripts, summation indices). Reproducibility is poor: no code, no full hyperparameter details, no training schedules beyond brief mentions.
- Evaluation: Metrics are MSE/PMSE on raw counts; no predictive log-likelihood, perplexity, or held-out likelihood typically used for PF/topic models. Baselines are limited: no deep PF baselines (DEF, Deep PF RBM/SBN), no sequential VAEs for counts, no NB process dynamical models beyond PGDS, no DTM-style baselines for NIPS. Dataset/task alignment is uneven (e.g., LSTM on high-dimensional bags-of-counts without showcasing appropriate architectures).
- Ablations: The contributions of Λ and Ψ, depth, window size c, and the ladder connections are not thoroughly ablated. The paper acknowledges issues like pruning and limited gains from depth but does not diagnose them rigorously.
- Scalability/complexity: No analysis of computational complexity, memory scaling with V,T,K,N,c, or runtime comparisons to PGDS/PFA/LSTM.

Assessment by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The model design is interesting, but the inference objective is not theoretically grounded. Several updates (especially for h) appear heuristic. Experimental evidence is mixed and uses limited metrics and baselines. Overall technical soundness is questionable without a principled objective.
- Clarity: The manuscript is hard to follow due to notation inconsistencies, missing specification details, and incomplete references. Key modeling choices are not justified.
- Originality: Combining PF with deep recurrent Gamma priors is plausible and may be new in this exact form, but the overlap with existing deep PF and recurrent PF work is not clearly differentiated.
- Significance: Potentially useful direction, but given the concerns above, the impact is limited. The lack of a principled training objective and stronger evaluations undermines claims.

Questions for authors
- What exact joint model and variational family are being optimized? Can you provide an ELBO and show how the RNN parameters are trained to maximize it (e.g., via amortized variational inference or SGVB with reparameterizations/augmentations for Gamma)?
- How is positivity enforced for shape/rate outputs, and how do you ensure valid Gamma parameters during training?
- What is the computational complexity per iteration? How does runtime compare to PGDS/PFA/LSTM?
- Can you report predictive log-likelihoods and add baselines like DEF, deep PF with SBN/RBM, sequential VAEs for counts, and NB-process dynamical models?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem (temporal count modeling).
- Promising empirical improvements on several datasets.
- Attempts to combine deep recurrent structure with PF in a way that could be impactful.

Cons for acceptance
- Inference objective lacks theoretical grounding; unclear what is actually optimized.
- Ambiguous model specification and ad hoc posterior forms.
- Limited evaluation metrics and baselines; reproducibility concerns.
- Writing/notation issues impede understanding; references incomplete.

Recommendation
I do not recommend acceptance in its current form. The paper needs: (i) a principled ELBO (or other justified objective) with clear derivation; (ii) precise model/inference specification; (iii) stronger, likelihood-based evaluations and broader baselines; and (iv) improved clarity and reproducibility. If these are addressed, the direction could be promising.
