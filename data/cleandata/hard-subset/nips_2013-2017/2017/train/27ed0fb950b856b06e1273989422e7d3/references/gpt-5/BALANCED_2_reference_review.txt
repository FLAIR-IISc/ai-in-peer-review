Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for multivariate count time series. Like Poisson Factorization (PF), the model assumes xvt ~ Poisson(sum_k θtk φvk λk ψv) with Gamma priors for φ, λ, ψ, and θ. The novelty is a deep temporal hierarchy over θ: a stack of latent variables h(n)t where each layer’s conditional (both “prior” and “auxiliary posterior”) is parameterized by recurrent neural networks over a local time window of size c. The top layer is also Gamma-distributed. Inference is a two-stage scheme: (i) mean-field variational updates for conjugate variables (using standard Poisson-Gamma conjugacy with latent count allocations), where the shape parameters for θ and h are provided by neural networks; and (ii) learning the RNN parameters via a “probability autoencoder” objective that uses squared-error losses between expectations of the distributions rather than a standard ELBO. Experiments on four synthetic datasets and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA to PGDS, PFA, and LSTM, reporting MSE and one-step PMSE.

This work relates to PF-based models for dynamic counts (e.g., Gamma-Poisson state-space models and PGDS), deep latent variable models for counts (deep PF, Gamma belief networks), and sequence models for counts (e.g., recurrent PF). It also sits near variational autoencoders and ladder architectures for hierarchical latent variables.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to combine PF with deep recurrent parameterizations, aiming to capture both short- and long-range temporal dependencies.
- Uses conjugacy to retain closed-form coordinate updates for many variables; computationally attractive if implemented sparsely.
- Empirical results show improved MSE/PMSE over baselines on several datasets; discussion of failure cases (ICEWS prediction) is honest.
- The qualitative analysis hints at more persistent factor activations compared to PGDS, which can be valuable in practice.

Weaknesses
Quality (technical soundness)
- The core learning objective for the neural networks (Eq. 15–18) is not a variational bound or a likelihood; it uses ad hoc squared-error losses between expectations of distributions. There is no guarantee it optimizes ELBO or predictive likelihood, nor any convergence analysis.
- The generative model is only partially specified. The text alternates between calling p(h(n)t | ·) an “implicit distribution” and then assuming Gamma conditionals; the dependence across layers is not fully formalized (e.g., how the random h(n+1) are integrated when deriving posteriors for h(n)).
- The mean-field updates for θ and h treat network-generated shapes as fixed, effectively plugging in expectations from other layers. This double approximation (MF + deterministic plug-in) is not justified by a bound.
- The multinomial allocation over xvtk for each nonzero count may be expensive; there is no complexity/scalability analysis or sparse implementation details.
- Evaluation uses MSE/PMSE rather than predictive log-likelihood/perplexity for counts; no uncertainty-aware metrics or calibration; no ablations on c, choice of RNN, or effect of Λ/Ψ.
- Baselines are limited. For text/time counts, missing comparisons to Dynamic Topic Models, deep PF variants trained with stochastic VI, recurrent PF for recommendation, or Gaussian process–based intensity models.

Clarity
- Numerous typos and notation inconsistencies (e.g., mixing βθ into λ updates, undefined indices, missing figure details). The “probability autoencoder” is not clearly defined; the relationship to ELBO is unclear.
- The architecture and RNNs are underspecified (RNN type, number of units, activations, parameterization of Gamma shapes/rates to ensure positivity and stability).
- References are incomplete and sometimes inaccurate; some claims (e.g., long-time dependence via depth) are not clearly tied to the model mathematically.

Originality
- Combining a PF with deep recurrent parameterizations is incremental relative to PGDS, deep PF (including Gamma belief networks), and recurrent PF. The main novelty is the particular bi-directional RNN “prior/posterior” pairing with a squared-error training objective, but this is not theoretically grounded.

Significance
- The idea of richer temporal parameterization for PF is important, but the current method lacks a sound training objective and rigorous evaluation. Without predictive log-likelihood and stronger baselines, it is hard to assess state-of-the-art impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical approach to enrich PF temporal dynamics with RNNs.
- Closed-form MF updates for conjugate blocks; potentially scalable with sparse implementation.
- Empirical gains on several datasets and a thoughtful qualitative analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- Learning objective is not a valid variational bound or likelihood; lacks theoretical justification.
- Clarity issues and incomplete model specification hinder reproducibility.
- Limited baselines and metrics; no predictive likelihood.
- Claims about long-range dependence vs. windowed RNNs and depth are not substantiated.

Detailed suggestions
- Replace the squared-error “probability autoencoder” objective with a principled ELBO that includes the RNN-parameterized Gamma conditionals. Use amortized variational inference with reparameterization/augmentation for Gamma (e.g., shape-rate reparameterizations or implicit reparameterization gradients), or adopt auxiliary-variable bounds that preserve conjugacy.
- Provide a clear, complete generative specification: exact forms of p(h(n)t | h(n+1)), p(θt | h(0)), parameterizations, and treatment of randomness across layers in the ELBO.
- Evaluate with held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity on counts; for recommendation-like data use ranking metrics; for text include topic coherence.
- Strengthen baselines: dynamic topic models, deep PF with temporal priors, recurrent PF, Neural Poisson/Gaussian process intensity models. Ensure fair tuning and report training time.
- Include ablations: depth N, window c, with/without Λ and Ψ, effect of ladder connections, and network capacity. Report calibration and uncertainty.
- Discuss computational complexity and memory for large V, T, K; describe sparse data structures and minibatch SVI for scalability.
- Improve clarity: fix typos, unify notation, provide complete references, and describe architectures and optimization hyperparameters to enable reproduction.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper tackles an important problem and shows promising empirical signals, but the learning objective is not theoretically sound, the model specification and inference are unclear, and the evaluation is not rigorous enough for NeurIPS standards. With a principled ELBO, stronger baselines/metrics, and improved clarity and reproducibility, the work could become significantly stronger.
