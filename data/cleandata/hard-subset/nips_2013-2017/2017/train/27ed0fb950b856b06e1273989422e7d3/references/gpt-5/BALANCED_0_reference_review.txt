Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count matrices that extends Poisson factorization by introducing a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t and a recurrent (windowed) dependence across time. The observed counts xvt are modeled via Poisson rates that factor across features and components, with additional global component- and feature-level scales (λk, ψv). Temporal dependence over factor scores θt is modeled through Gamma priors whose shape parameters are outputs of RNNs taking as input recent latent states in the layer above; deeper layers similarly use RNNs to capture longer-range dependence. Inference is two-stage: (i) coordinate-ascent mean-field variational updates for conjugate parts (Φ, Λ, Ψ, Θ, H) assuming Gamma forms, and (ii) learning the RNN parameters using a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that matches expectations between top-down and bottom-up conditionals and reconstructs X through the decoder.

The problem—capturing long- and short-term dependence in dynamic count data—has been studied in PGDS (Poisson-Gamma dynamical systems), dynamic Poisson factorization for recommendation, and recurrent Poisson factorization (e.g., Gopalan & Blei 2015; Hosseini et al.), as well as deep latent variable models such as Deep Exponential Families (Ranganath et al.) and Gamma Belief Networks (Zhou et al.). The novelty claimed is combining PF with a deep, RNN-parameterized Gamma hierarchy that purportedly preserves conjugacy for efficient updates, with an auxiliary autoencoder-style training objective for the neural parameters.

Strengths
- Addresses an important setting: high-dimensional, sparse sequential count data in text, events, and epidemiology.
- Ambitious attempt to combine dynamic PF with deep temporal structure to capture both short- and long-range dependencies.
- Uses Gamma parameterizations to retain simple coordinate updates for many variables, which is computationally attractive.
- Broad empirical scope across synthetic and several real datasets; some qualitative analysis of learned factors.

Weaknesses

Quality (technical soundness)
- The “Probability AutoEncoder” objective (Eqs. 15–18) is ad hoc and not derived from a valid ELBO or likelihood-consistent objective. Multiplying/combining conditionals p(Θ|H(0)) and p(H(0)|Θ), and optimizing L2 distances between expectations, lacks probabilistic justification. This undermines claims of variational inference and the validity of learned RNN parameters.
- Mean-field factorization across time appears to ignore the temporal dependence the model posits (the q factorization in Eq. 5 does not model sequential coupling). This mismatch can severely limit capturing dynamics.
- Several equations and modeling choices are inconsistent or likely incorrect:
  - Eq. (1)–(2) and Eq. (7) show typos (duplicated φ, missing ψ, etc.). Please check the normalized multinomial allocation and rate definitions.
  - The Bernoulli–Poisson link defines b = 1(x > 1); standard is b = 1(x > 0). The conditional forms that follow are then incorrect as written.
  - Introducing both Λ and Ψ in addition to Φ and Θ induces severe non-identifiability (scaling invariances) without constraints or normalization.
  - The posterior for h(n)tk uses a Gamma with shape αh(n)tk + γh(n)tk, mixing “prior” and “posterior” signals in the same parameter without a principled likelihood derivation.
- No discussion of computational complexity, memory, or convergence properties. The two-stage alternating procedure may be unstable; no diagnostics are reported.
- Evaluation uses MSE/PMSE on counts rather than predictive log-likelihood/perplexity or held-out likelihood typical for count models; this weakens claims of probabilistic improvements.

Clarity
- The paper is hard to follow: numerous typos and grammatical errors, inconsistent notation, undefined symbols, and incomplete references (many lack years/venues). Several figures/tables are referenced but not shown.
- The ladder architecture and windowing details (boundary handling, c) are not precisely specified. It’s unclear how the RNNs are structured at each layer and how information flows during training and inference.

Originality
- Neural parameterization of PF dynamics has precedents (dynamic PF, recurrent PF, amortized inference for count models, deep PF via GBN/DEFs). The main novelty appears to be the specific combination of an RNN-parameterized Gamma hierarchy with a two-stage “probability autoencoder” training, which is insufficiently justified and overlaps conceptually with variational RNN/VRNN/DEF approaches.

Significance
- Without a principled objective and stronger empirical evidence (likelihood-based metrics, fair/tuned baselines, ablations), it is hard to conclude that the method advances the state of the art. The empirical section lacks statistical rigor, uncertainty estimates, and reproducibility details (code, seeds, hyperparameter tuning protocols).

Arguments for acceptance
- Timely problem with potentially useful idea of neural parameterization while preserving some conjugacy.
- Broad set of datasets and some qualitative insights.

Arguments against acceptance
- Core training objective is not theoretically grounded; inference is not a coherent VI scheme.
- Equation errors, unclear notation, and incomplete references hinder evaluation and reproduction.
- Limited and potentially unfair evaluation (metrics, baselines, tuning), and missing key comparisons (e.g., DPF/GBN variants, VRNN-like models with Poisson likelihood).
- Non-identifiability and modeling issues are unaddressed.

Constructive suggestions
- Re-derive the learning as a proper ELBO with amortized inference networks q(h(n)t | x1:T) (or structured amortization), and optimize with reparameterization or implicit reparameterization gradients for Gamma/Poisson (e.g., Figurnov et al., 2018), or use Rao–Blackwellized SGVB for count models.
- If preserving conjugacy is a goal, restrict neural parameterization to produce valid prior hyperparameters and maintain a consistent mean-field family that respects temporal couplings (e.g., structured VI with Markov dependence).
- Fix the Bernoulli–Poisson link, rate definitions, and normalization in the latent-count allocations. Enforce identifiability via normalization (e.g., sum-to-one over φ columns) or hierarchical shrinkage priors.
- Evaluate with predictive log-likelihood/perplexity on held-out time steps and entries; report means and variances across multiple runs and seeds. Include calibration metrics.
- Strengthen baselines and comparisons: PGDS, recurrent PF, GBN/DEF-based DPFA, VRNN/STORN with Poisson likelihoods, and dynamic topic models; conduct hyperparameter tuning for all methods.
- Add ablations on c (window size), depth N, K, and the ladder vs non-ladder variants; report runtime and scalability.
- Clean up exposition: consistent notation, complete references (authors, venue, year), include all figures/tables, and provide code for reproducibility.

Recommendation: Reject
While the problem is important and the high-level idea is interesting, the current submission lacks a principled learning objective, contains technical and presentation errors, and does not provide sufficiently rigorous empirical validation. I encourage the authors to address the above issues and resubmit a revised, theoretically grounded and carefully evaluated version.
