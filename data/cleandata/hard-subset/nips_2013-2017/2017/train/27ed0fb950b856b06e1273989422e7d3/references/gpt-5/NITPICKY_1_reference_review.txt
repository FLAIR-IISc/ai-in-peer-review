Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA) for sequential count data. Observations xvt are modeled Poisson with rate ∑k θtk φvk λk ψv. Temporal dependence is introduced via a deep hierarchy of latent K-dimensional variables h(n)t, where p(θt | h(0)t−c:t) and p(h(n)t | h(n+1)t−c:t) are parameterized by recurrent neural networks. The priors are chosen as Gamma so that, purportedly, conditional posteriors remain Gamma. Inference alternates: (i) mean-field coordinate updates for Gamma factors and latent variables with auxiliary count allocations xvtk; (ii) training “probability autoencoder” networks via squared-error losses between expectations of distributions output by the networks, plus a reconstruction loss that uses X ≈ ⟨Ψ⟩⟨Λ⟩⟨Φ⟩⟨p(Θ|H(0))⟩. Experiments compare with PGDS, PFA, and LSTM on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, ID, ASP) using MSE/PMSE metrics.

This builds on Poisson factorization and Gamma-Poisson conjugacy (e.g., Zhou et al.; Gopalan et al.; PGDS), deep latent models (DEFs, DPFA, Ladder VAE), and RNNs for temporal structure.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to blend Poisson factorization with deep RNN-based latent dynamics to capture longer-range dependencies.
- Uses count-appropriate likelihood instead of Gaussian assumptions common in RNN baselines.
- Conceptually interesting: using networks to output prior parameters of conjugate Gamma distributions.
- Includes experiments on multiple real datasets; claims of improved fitting/prediction on several.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Quality/technical soundness
- Objective and learning: The “probability autoencoder” losses (Eqs. 15–18) are ad hoc and not derived from an ELBO or a well-posed probabilistic divergence. Replacing intractable integrals with squared error of expectations (Eq. 16) lacks justification and can be badly biased for skewed Gamma variables. The collapsed reconstruction loss uses an L2 loss on counts; a Poisson (or NB) log-likelihood or deviance would be more principled.
- Causality/leakage: The backward network fback uses future inputs (t+1:t+c) to infer current h(n)t (Eq. 14). If these “posterior” networks are used in training for a forecasting model, this risks leaking future information. The paper does not clearly separate training-time smoothing from test-time filtering, nor explain how one-step-ahead prediction avoids using future targets.
- Identifiability/scaling: The multiplicative factors θ, φ, λ, ψ are non-identifiable without constraints (e.g., column/row-normalization or anchored priors). The paper acknowledges Λ and Ψ “can be absorbed into Θ” but keeps them “to extract sparsity,” yet does not address identifiability or implement constraints. This undermines interpretability and stability of VI.
- Conjugacy with neural priors: The claim that complete conditionals remain Gamma is delicate. It holds only if the prior over θtk is Gamma with fixed rate and shape αθtk>0. However, αθtk is output by an RNN; how positivity and numerical stability are enforced is unspecified (softplus? clamping?), and the dependence on future states (via fback) complicates conditional independence assumptions.
- Mean-field factorization (Eq. 5) is incorrect/inconsistent: ψ is feature-wise (ψv) but indexed by k; H is indexed as h(n)tk yet earlier defined as K-dimensional vector per t; product indices are muddled. This affects the validity of the VI derivations.
- Update equations contain multiple errors:
  - Eq. 7 has θtφvλφv in the multinomial parameter; ψv is missing and φv is duplicated.
  - Eq. 8 has λk|Θ,Φ,Φ, ... with Φ repeated and uses βθ (rate for θ) in λk’s posterior rate; likely a typo but undermines reproducibility.
  - Eq. 11 uses Ψ(·) to denote the digamma function, but Ψ elsewhere denotes the feature-wise parameters; the notational collision is confusing.
  - Handling of xvt=0 is glossed: Eq. 11 only updates for xvt>0; the effect on other updates is not discussed.
- The claim that “differentiable variational Bayes is not suitable” because sampling is “high-cost and useless” is not convincing; reparameterizations and low-variance gradient estimators for Gamma exist, and amortized VI with ELBO optimization is standard. The proposed alternative lacks guarantees.
- No complexity analysis, computational cost, or convergence behavior. Alternating between MF-VI and non-ELBO network training may not improve a coherent objective.

Clarity
- Writing has many grammatical errors and inconsistent notation; core objects (RNN type, architecture, activations, positivity transforms, window c, number of layers, K per dataset) are underspecified or scattered.
- Figures and Tables are referenced but not provided; critical to assess empirical claims.
- Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues/authors) and some important related work is not properly positioned.
- The term “implicit distribution” is misused: the model specifies explicit Gamma conditionals; “implicit” usually refers to non-tractable densities.

Originality
- Combining PF with RNN-parameterized priors is a natural extension of PGDS/DEF/DPFA ideas; novelty is incremental. The proposed “probability autoencoder” training is nonstandard but also insufficiently justified.

Significance
- Potentially useful if demonstrated convincingly, but current experiments are not compelling:
  - No held-out log-likelihood/perplexity or predictive log-likelihood; MSE/PMSE on counts is not ideal for Poisson models.
  - No error bars/variance across runs; no hyperparameter tuning fairness; LSTM baseline appears under-tuned.
  - No ablations isolating depth vs. window size vs. Λ/Ψ.
  - The reported negative result on ICEWS is not analyzed rigorously.

Reproducibility
- Insufficient details to reproduce: data preprocessing, splits, initialization, optimizer/lr, architecture, constraints, stopping, code availability, random seeds.

Specific errors/formatting/notation issues (non-exhaustive)
- Eq. 1–2: clarify whether ψv, λk are scalars; ensure consistent capitalization; avoid overloading Ψ as both parameter matrix and digamma function.
- Eq. 5: q(ψk|ψ∗k) should be q(ψv|ψ∗v).
- Eq. 7: missing ψv and duplicated φv.
- Eq. 8, 12: repeated Φ; wrong rate symbols (βθ vs βλ); dimensions of βh vs βθ unclear.
- Typos: “nest hierarchical Dirichlet process,” “fanatical data,” “strcuture,” “Natrually,” “thi figure,” “distrbution,” etc.
- Many citations incomplete; ensure correct and complete bibliographic entries.

Pros
- Interesting direction: neural parameterization of Gamma-Poisson dynamics.
- Attempts to capture longer-range temporal structure beyond first-order PGDS.
- Applied to multiple real datasets.

Cons
- Objective and training are not principled; ad hoc losses replace ELBO without justification.
- Numerous mathematical/notation errors and ambiguities; conjugacy/use of future information unclear.
- Evaluation metrics, baselines, and reporting are inadequate; no uncertainty or statistical significance.
- Identifiability and scaling issues unaddressed; architecture details missing; reproducibility low.
- Writing quality and references below NeurIPS standards.

Questions for authors
- Provide a coherent ELBO and gradients for network parameters, or justify the proposed loss theoretically.
- How do you prevent future information leakage during training and testing for forecasting?
- How do you enforce positivity of network outputs and ensure numerical stability?
- How are Λ and Ψ identifiable; do you normalize φ or θ?
- Report predictive log-likelihood on held-out counts with error bars; include more competitive dynamic PF baselines and tuned LSTM/GRU.

Recommendation: Reject

While the idea is promising, the paper in its current form has too many technical, methodological, and presentation issues to meet NeurIPS standards. A revised submission should fix the derivations/notation, adopt a principled training objective, improve evaluation, and significantly enhance clarity and reproducibility. Confidence: high.
