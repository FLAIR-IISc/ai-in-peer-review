Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA) for sequential count data. Observations xvt are modeled as Poisson with rate ∑k θtk φvk λk ψv, where θtk are time-varying factor scores, φvk are factor loadings, and ψv, λk are feature- and component-wise scales. To capture temporal dependencies beyond first-order Markov assumptions used in prior Poisson–Gamma dynamic models, the authors introduce a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t with a recurrent structure over a window of past time steps. The conditional distributions p(θt | h(0)t−c:t) and p(h(n)t | h(n+1)t−c:t) are parameterized via RNNs that output parameters (e.g., Gamma shapes). Inference combines mean-field variational updates for conjugate parts of the model with a separate training stage for the RNN parameters using a “probability autoencoder” loss that matches moments between encoder and decoder and encourages reconstruction.

The work builds on Poisson factorization (PF) and dynamic PF (e.g., Poisson–Gamma models and PGDS), as well as deep latent variable models (Deep Exponential Families, Gamma Belief Networks, Deep Poisson factor models) and neural sequential latent variable models (VRNNs, Deep Kalman Filters). Prior PF with dynamics typically imposes shallow Gamma–Poisson Markovian structure or transition matrices; here the aim is to obtain deeper, potentially long-range dependencies via RNN-parameterized priors. Related PF for recommendations and text (e.g., Gopalan et al. 2015; Hosseini et al. 2018) and deep hierarchical count models (Gan et al. 2015; Zhou et al. 2016) are relevant comparisons. For sequential latent-variable modeling with amortized inference, VRNNs (Chung et al., NIPS 2015) and DKF (Krishnan et al., NIPS 2015) are closely related.

Strengths
- Targeted to an important setting: sparse, high-dimensional sequential count data across multiple domains (text, events, epidemiology).
- Attempts to combine PF’s interpretability with deep recurrent priors to capture long-range dependencies.
- Uses auxiliary latent counts to retain conjugacy for Φ, λ, ψ, θ given xvtk, which is standard and efficient in PF.
- Includes experiments on multiple synthetic and real-world datasets and ablations (depth, ladder connections), and compares against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM.

Weaknesses (technical soundness and clarity)
- Generative model is not fully specified. Equation (4) refers to “implicit” distributions parameterized by RNNs, but later updates require explicit Gamma forms. The paper states h(N)t is Gamma-distributed, but for n < N the distributional form is unclear. It is also unclear whether the generative prior is strictly causal; some parts suggest using future information in the “posterior network,” but elsewhere dependencies include current/past values in a way that blurs prior vs. inference roles.
- The mean-field VI objective is misstated as minimizing KL(p||q) (Eq. 6), which is not tractable; standard VI minimizes KL(q||p). Subsequent derivations rely on conjugate updates but then break from ELBO-based training for the neural parameters.
- The “Probability AutoEncoder” objective (Eqs. 15–18) is nonstandard and insufficiently justified. The loss replaces the ELBO with squared-error penalties between expectations of distributions and reconstructions. No derivation connects this to a bound on marginal likelihood or predictive performance. It resembles an autoencoder rather than variational inference, undermining probabilistic consistency.
- The update for xvtk (Eq. 11) is heuristically presented; a proper derivation should show the multinomial responsibilities from the conditional Poisson decomposition using expected log-rates. Indices and notation are inconsistent (e.g., ψv vs. Ψv, φvk terms).
- Scale identifiability is not addressed. With λk and ψv in addition to φ and θ, the model has multiple scale degrees of freedom; without normalization constraints (e.g., column-normalized Φ or Dirichlet priors), posterior multimodality can hinder learning and interpretability.
- The deep structure and ladder architecture are described post hoc; the exact parameterization of ffeed and fback, activation functions ensuring positivity of Gamma parameters, and boundary conditions for windows are not provided. No pseudocode or complexity analysis is included.
- Theoretical properties (e.g., stability, convergence of the two-stage procedure) are not analyzed. The use of expectations in place of sampling in the neural loss can bias training and ignores uncertainty.
- The paper contains numerous grammatical errors and inconsistent notation, and refers to figures and tables not provided, which hinders verification.

Originality
- Combining PF with RNN-parameterized priors to model long-range dependencies is a reasonable idea, but similar goals have been pursued: recurrent PF, neural autoregressive topic models, VRNN/DKF-style temporal latent variable models, and deep PF variants. The specific “probability autoencoder” training objective appears novel but lacks theoretical grounding. The ladder variant is known (Ladder VAEs) and its use here is incremental.

Significance
- Potentially useful if a principled, scalable inference scheme were provided; however, as presented, the non-ELBO training and unclear generative/inference split limit reproducibility and confidence in results. Empirical significance is difficult to assess due to missing tables/figures and insufficient reporting (no likelihood-based metrics, confidence intervals, or ablations on window size c).

Empirical evaluation
- Datasets are relevant, but results are described without numbers (Tables 1–3, Figures 1–3 not included). Evaluation uses MSE/PMSE; for count models, predictive log-likelihood or Poisson/NB deviance is more appropriate. Details on train/validation/test splits, hyperparameter tuning, and computational costs are missing. Comparisons omit closely related deep baselines (DEFs/DPFA, VRNN/DKF on counts). The Bernoulli–Poisson link is mentioned but not evaluated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a complete, consistent generative model. Specify explicit distributions for all latent layers or clearly separate priors from inference networks.
- Replace the “probability autoencoder” with a proper ELBO. Use amortized variational inference with reparameterization or implicit-reparameterization gradients for Gamma (e.g., Figurnov et al., NeurIPS 2018) or use auxiliary variable augmentations for Gamma/NB (as in GBNs).
- Address identifiability by normalizing Φ columns or fixing scale of one factor per component; discuss priors accordingly.
- Clarify and standardize notation, include algorithmic details and complexity, and ensure all figures/tables are present.
- Evaluate with predictive log-likelihood/perplexity and calibration; include comparisons to deep PF (Gan et al. 2015), DEFs (Ranganath et al. 2015), VRNN/DKF, and recurrent PF; report uncertainty and multiple random seeds.
- Analyze the effect of window size c and depth on performance; justify ladder connections within an ELBO.
- Consider non-causal vs. causal settings: for forecasting, the generative prior should be causal.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem: long-range dynamics in sequential count data.
- Attempts to leverage deep/recurrent structure while retaining PF’s conjugacy for some variables.
- Broad empirical scope across several domains (if results are substantiated).

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete and inconsistent specification of the generative model; unclear use of “implicit” distributions.
- Nonstandard and unjustified training objective for neural parameters; not ELBO-based.
- Significant clarity issues (notation, grammar) and missing experimental details/tables.
- Lack of comparisons to the most relevant deep baselines and use of suboptimal evaluation metrics.
- Identifiability and scaling issues not addressed.

Recommendation
- Not ready for publication at NeurIPS in its current form due to concerns about technical soundness, clarity, and evaluation. A revised version with a principled variational objective, complete model specification, and thorough empirical validation could be impactful.
