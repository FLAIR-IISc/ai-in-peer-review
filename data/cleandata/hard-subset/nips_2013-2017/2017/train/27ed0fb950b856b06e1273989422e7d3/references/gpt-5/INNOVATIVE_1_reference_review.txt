Summary
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA), a generative model for high-dimensional sequential count data. Observations are modeled with a Poisson factorization X ≈ ΦΘ^T with additional feature- and component-wise scales Ψ and Λ. The novelty lies in how temporal dependence is modeled: Θ is driven by a hierarchy of latent variables h(n)_t whose conditional distributions are “implicit” and parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs). Conjugate Gamma priors are retained on Φ, Λ, Ψ (and on θ_t given RNN-produced shape/rate), enabling mean-field coordinate updates for the conjugate blocks. Neural parameters are learned in a second stage via a “Probability AutoEncoder” loss that aligns forward and backward conditionals across the hierarchy and includes a reconstruction term. The model targets long-range temporal dependencies via depth and a temporal window c, and is evaluated on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disaster, Sheep), comparing against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE/PMSE. DDPFA generally outperforms baselines, with some exceptions, and the paper discusses “pruning” and ladder-style fixes.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Poisson factorization and dynamic extensions (PGDS; temporal gamma chains), deep latent variable models for counts (deep Poisson factor analysis, gamma belief networks), and neural autoregressive/recurrent models. Closest conceptually are: (i) PGDS and related gamma-Poisson state space models, (ii) deep Poisson/gamma belief networks that introduce hierarchical gamma structures for counts, and (iii) recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation. The contribution here is to replace linear Markovian dynamics with RNN-parameterized Gamma conditionals for θ_t (and deeper h(n)_t), providing flexible non-linear, long-range temporal dependencies while retaining conjugate inference for the count-generating blocks. The “Probability AutoEncoder” training objective is a non-standard alternative to ELBO maximization.

Strengths
- Bold, interesting direction: combining Poisson factorization with deep, RNN-driven hierarchical gamma dynamics is a compelling way to capture long-term, non-linear temporal structure in sparse count sequences.
- Hybrid inference strategy: keeps conjugacy where possible and learns neural dynamics separately; potentially attractive for scalability and stability compared to full black-box VI.
- Empirical promise: on several datasets, DDPFA improves MSE/PMSE over PGDS, PFA, and (often) LSTM, and produces interpretable factors with sustained activation consistent with real-world phenomena.
- The discussion of failure modes (“pruning”) and ladder-style mitigation shows thoughtful reflection and a path forward.

Weaknesses
- Objective and theory: the “Probability AutoEncoder” loss (L2 between expectations of conditional distributions plus reconstruction) is ad hoc and not clearly connected to an ELBO or a principled divergence. Claims that “differentiable VB is not suitable” for Gamma or that sampling is “useless” are inaccurate; reparameterization/implicit reparameterization for Gamma and variance-reduced stochastic VI exist. Convergence properties and bias induced by the two-stage training are unclear.
- Generative story clarity: the “implicit” distributions p(h(n)_t|·) are defined via RNN outputs (e.g., Gamma shapes), but the precise parameterization (shape/rate), sharing across time/layers, and whether networks are uni-/bi-directional is not clearly specified. The relationship between ffeed/fback and the auxiliary posterior is confusing.
- Evaluation and baselines: Using MSE/PMSE on counts is not standard for probabilistic models; predictive log-likelihood/perplexity would be more appropriate. Baselines omit stronger relatives (e.g., recurrent Poisson factorization in recommendations; deep gamma belief networks with temporal structure; dynamic topic models with richer state transitions; Hawkes or NHP for events). LSTM likelihood mismatch is not addressed.
- Reproducibility and details: Many implementation details are missing (network architectures, parameterizations, initialization, complexity per iteration, wall-clock, sensitivity to K, c, priors). References are incomplete, and some statements/citations appear mismatched.
- Notation/typos: Several inconsistencies (e.g., subscripts/superscripts, missing sums, unclear shapes), making the paper hard to follow in places.

Quality
Technically ambitious but the training objective lacks a solid probabilistic foundation, and the inference story mixes conjugate VI with an unprincipled loss. Empirical results are encouraging but evaluated with limited metrics and baselines.

Clarity
The high-level idea is understandable, but the paper needs substantial tightening in notation, precise specification of the implicit distributions, network designs, and the learning algorithm. Figures/tables referenced are not provided here; ensure they are legible and informative.

Originality
Moderately high. While deep PFAs and recurrent PF approaches exist, the specific combination of hierarchical RNN-parameterized gamma dynamics with conjugate Poisson factorization and the proposed two-stage training is a distinct angle. The “Probability AutoEncoder” idea is novel but under-justified.

Significance
If stabilized and tied to a principled objective (or empirically demonstrated to be robust), the approach could influence modeling of sequential count data across topics, recommendation, and event streams. Current evaluation suggests promise but falls short of a clear state-of-the-art advance.

Questions for authors
- Precisely define p(h(n)_t|·) and p(θ_t|·): which parameters are output by the RNNs, and how are positivity/shape constraints enforced?
- Are ffeed and fback bidirectional RNNs? Do they share weights across time/layers?
- Can you connect Eq. 16–18 to a lower bound or known divergence (e.g., ELBO with auxiliary variables, wake-sleep, amortized MLE)? Why not optimize a tractable ELBO with reparameterization/implicit reparameterization?
- Report predictive log-likelihood and compare to recurrent Poisson factorization, deep gamma belief networks with temporal links, dynamic topic models with neural transitions.
- Provide ablations for Λ and Ψ (do they help beyond absorbing into Θ?), window size c, and depth N.
- Give complexity, runtime, and memory vs PGDS/LSTM on ICEWS/NIPS.
- Release code or pseudocode sufficient for reproduction.

Pros
- Novel, bold attempt to fuse PF with deep non-linear temporal dynamics.
- Retains interpretability and some conjugacy for scalability.
- Promising empirical gains and insightful qualitative analysis.

Cons
- Training objective lacks theoretical grounding; unclear convergence.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; weak evaluation metrics and baselines.
- Several technical inaccuracies/claims about VI.

Recommendation
Weak accept (6). The paper pushes a fresh and potentially impactful direction for dynamic count modeling by marrying PF with deep hierarchical RNN dynamics. Despite theoretical rough edges and clarity issues, the idea is exciting enough that, with revisions to clarify the model and strengthen the evaluation, it could stimulate follow-up work. I encourage the authors to better ground the learning objective, expand baselines/metrics, and improve exposition.

Confidence: 0.6
