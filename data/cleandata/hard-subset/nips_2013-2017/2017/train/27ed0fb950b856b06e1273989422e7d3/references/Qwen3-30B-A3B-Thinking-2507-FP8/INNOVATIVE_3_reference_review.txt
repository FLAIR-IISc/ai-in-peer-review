

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, which extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating deep recurrent neural networks to capture complex temporal dependencies in count data. The authors argue that existing methods like PGDS [2] and standard PFA fail to adequately model long-time dependencies due to their shallow structure, and they claim that their deep hierarchical approach can better capture these complex temporal patterns.

The paper's main technical contribution is a model that uses a deep hierarchical structure with recurrent neural networks to represent the implicit distributions of latent variables across time steps. The authors introduce a novel inference approach using variational inference combined with probability autoencoders, where the parameters of the neural networks are learned via a loss function that balances reconstruction error and data fitting.

The paper evaluates DDPFA on four synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population), comparing against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA baselines. The results show that DDPFA performs well on most datasets, particularly in capturing complex temporal patterns that simpler models miss.

**Strengths:**
- The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods in modeling long-time dependencies in sequential count data.
- The model design is well-motivated by the need to capture more complex temporal patterns than shallow models can handle.
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic and diverse real-world datasets.
- The paper provides a detailed inference algorithm with clear mathematical formulations.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why a deep structure is necessary for capturing temporal dependencies in count data. The empirical results show only marginal improvements over simpler baselines like PFA, and the paper acknowledges that adding more layers doesn't significantly improve performance (Table 3).
- The theoretical justification for the deep structure is weak. The authors claim it captures "long-time dependence," but don't provide a clear explanation of how the deep structure specifically addresses this limitation better than simpler alternatives like LSTM or other RNN-based approaches.
- The paper doesn't adequately differentiate its approach from existing deep Poisson factorization methods [9,10]. The novelty appears to be incremental rather than fundamental.
- The paper acknowledges significant issues with their approach (Table 3), including "pruning" (where many latent dimensions contain only noise) and the "uselessness of adding network layers," which undermines their core claim about the value of the deep structure.
- The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the deep structure is necessary for the observed improvements, making it difficult to assess whether the complexity is justified.

**Significance and Novelty Assessment:**
While the paper introduces a novel model architecture combining PFA with deep learning, the contribution appears to be incremental rather than groundbreaking. The paper doesn't present a fundamentally new approach to modeling sequential count data but rather applies deep learning to an existing framework. The empirical gains over simpler baselines are modest, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the deep structure is essential for these improvements.

The paper's claim that "deep structure is included in order to capture the long-time dependence" is not sufficiently supported. The authors acknowledge that the deep structure doesn't consistently outperform simpler models (e.g., DDPFA performs worse than PGDS on ICEWS prediction), and the paper doesn't provide a clear theoretical argument for why deep structure should be beneficial for this specific problem.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it represents an incremental improvement rather than a significant new direction in the field. The novelty is limited to applying deep learning to an existing framework without convincing evidence that the deep structure is necessary or substantially beneficial. The paper acknowledges its own limitations (Table 3) and doesn't provide strong enough justification to overcome these concerns.

The paper would benefit from either:
1) A stronger theoretical justification for why deep structure is needed for temporal count data, or
2) More compelling empirical evidence showing clear and substantial advantages over simpler alternatives.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over methodological refinement, I find this paper's contribution too incremental to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The field would benefit more from a paper that fundamentally rethinks how we model temporal dependencies in count data, rather than a modest extension of existing approaches.
