Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA) for sequential count data. Building on Poisson factorization, it introduces: (i) additional component- and feature-specific scale variables (λk, ψv) to encourage sparsity/smoothness; (ii) a deep hierarchy of latent Gamma-distributed variables h(n)t with short-window temporal dependencies; and (iii) “implicit” conditional distributions whose parameters (e.g., Gamma shapes α) are produced by recurrent neural networks. Inference alternates between mean-field VI updates (treating neural network outputs as fixed) and training the networks via a “Probability AutoEncoder” loss that matches expectations across encoder/decoder Gamma distributions and reconstructs X via expected rates. Experiments on four simple synthetic datasets and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, international disasters, sheep population) compare to PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE/PMSE.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of Poisson factorization (e.g., HPF; Zhou et al.’s Gamma–NB process and PGDS) and deep/temporal latent-variable models (Deep Exponential Families; VAE; Ladder VAE; deep Poisson topic models; recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation). Using RNNs to parameterize temporal transitions in Poisson factor models is close in spirit to recurrent Poisson factorization and neural topic models; stacking Gamma layers resembles Gamma belief networks. The paper does not position itself clearly against these: what is fundamentally new vs. HPF/PGDS with an amortized/RNN prior, or vs. temporal DEFs? Additionally, the learning objective departs from standard ELBO-based training popularized in VAE/DEF work, but its validity is not established.

Strengths
- Addresses an important problem: dynamic, sparse count modeling with potentially long-range dependencies.
- Attempts to combine interpretability of Poisson factorization with flexible temporal dynamics via RNNs.
- Includes both synthetic and real datasets; compares to PGDS and PFA; some qualitative discussion of factor activation.

Weaknesses
Technical correctness/rigor:
- The probabilistic semantics and variational objective are unclear. Once the Gamma shape/rate parameters are functions of neural networks, the claimed conjugacy no longer strictly holds; yet the paper proceeds with conjugate mean-field updates by treating network outputs as fixed. This hybrid procedure is not justified by a valid ELBO.
- The “Probability AutoEncoder” loss (Eqs. 15–18) is not derived as a lower bound or unbiased objective. Matching expectations with squared error and reconstructing X via expected rates lacks theoretical grounding. Consequently, it is unclear what is being optimized and whether the combined procedure converges to a meaningful solution.
- Identifiability/scale ambiguity: adding λk and ψv on top of φvk and θtk introduces redundant scaling. Without constraints or normalization, the model is ill-posed; the paper does not address this.
Modeling/design:
- The claimed “long-time dependence” is at odds with the finite window c used throughout and with the authors’ own observation that deeper layers did not help (“pruning” and “uselessness of adding layers”).
- The top-down/bottom-up ladder description is vague; conditional forms for p(h(n)t|·) are “implicit” and not specified beyond being RNN outputs for Gamma parameters.
Empirical evaluation:
- Metrics (MSE/PMSE) are not standard for count models; predictive log likelihood/perplexity is the norm. No calibration analysis.
- Baselines are weak/inadequately tuned. LSTM setup is unclear for high-dimensional sparse counts; no strong contemporary baselines (e.g., HPF variants, recurrent Poisson factorization, temporal DEFs) are included.
- No ablations to isolate contributions (RNN prior vs. depth vs. λ/ψ). Limited analysis of window size c. No runtime/scalability results.
- No statistical significance or multiple runs; hyperparameter fairness across methods is unclear.
Clarity/reproducibility:
- Many typographical errors and incomplete references; figures/tables are referenced but not visible here, and key architectural/training details (network sizes per layer, optimizer specifics, initialization, data splits) are insufficient for reproduction.

Quality
Given the lack of a well-defined objective and theoretical justification for the combined VI/autoencoder training, the work is not technically solid. Claims about capturing long-range dependencies are not convincingly supported, and empirical validation uses nonstandard metrics with limited baselines.

Clarity
The exposition is difficult to follow, with ambiguous notation, missing derivations, and many typos. The role of implicit distributions and the exact training loop are not clearly specified.

Originality
The high-level idea—augmenting Poisson factorization with RNN-parameterized temporal dynamics and depth—has precedents. The proposed “Probability AutoEncoder” objective appears novel but is insufficiently grounded.

Significance
As presented, the contribution is limited. Without a proper ELBO or established alternative and stronger empirical evidence against state-of-the-art baselines, the impact is doubtful.

Pros
- Tackles a relevant problem (dynamic count modeling).
- Attempts to combine deep temporal priors with interpretable Poisson factors.
- Some qualitative insights about factor persistence.

Cons
- Lacks a valid probabilistic objective; conjugacy claims are inconsistent with RNN-parameterized priors.
- Nonstandard, unjustified training loss; no guarantees.
- Weak baselines/metrics; limited, inconclusive experiments.
- Identifiability concerns; insufficient ablations and scalability analysis.
- Clarity and references need substantial improvement.

Questions for authors
1) Can you provide a rigorous ELBO that includes network parameters and show how your updates optimize it? If not, what is the theoretical justification for Eqs. 15–18?
2) How do you resolve scale identifiability among φ, θ, λ, ψ? Are there normalization constraints?
3) Why not use reparameterization/implicit reparameterization for Gamma or SG-MCMC to train a proper VAE/DEF objective?
4) How does performance change with predictive log likelihood on held-out time steps? Please include stronger baselines.
5) What is the computational complexity and wall-clock runtime relative to PGDS/HPF? How does it scale with V, T, K?

Suggestions
- Replace the ad hoc loss with a principled ELBO (or IWAE-style bound) and use appropriate reparameterizations or auxiliary-variable methods.
- Provide ablations (no depth, no λ/ψ, varying c), report predictive log likelihood/perplexity, multiple seeds, and stronger baselines.
- Address identifiability via normalization or constraints.
- Improve clarity: precise conditional definitions, derivations, and complete references.

Recommendation: Reject
