

# Peer Review: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

## Summary and Context
This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), an extension of Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) that incorporates deep neural networks to model complex temporal dependencies in sequential count data. The authors build on prior work in dynamic Poisson models (PGDS) and deep Poisson factorization (e.g., Deep Poisson Factor Analysis based on RBM/SBN), introducing a multi-layer latent structure with recurrent neural networks to capture long-term temporal patterns. The model uses variational inference with a probability autoencoder for parameter learning and is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets.

## Strengths
1. **Problem relevance**: The paper addresses an important problem in analyzing sequential count data, with clear applications in recommendation systems, text analysis, and time series analysis.

2. **Model structure**: The deep structure with multiple layers of latent variables provides a reasonable approach to modeling complex temporal dependencies beyond simple Markov assumptions.

3. **Experimental evaluation**: The paper conducts comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including challenging ones like ICEWS and EBOLA. The comparison with relevant baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) is appropriate.

4. **Technical detail**: The inference algorithm is described with sufficient mathematical detail, including the mean-field variational approach and the probability autoencoder component.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical justification**: The paper lacks a compelling theoretical argument for why a deep structure is necessary. The authors claim "complex time dependence" requires deep modeling, but fail to demonstrate that simpler models (like PGDS with longer time lags) cannot capture the same patterns. The claim that "the independence assumption is made on θt−1 and θt+1 if θt is given" in previous work is inaccurate—PGDS explicitly models temporal dependencies through the Gamma transition.

2. **Comparative analysis**: The paper does not sufficiently compare against the most relevant prior work—specifically, the Deep Poisson Factor Analysis models based on RBM/SBN cited in the introduction. The performance gains over these models are not demonstrated, making it unclear what new value the proposed architecture provides.

3. **"Deep" aspect is superficial**: The experimental results in Tables 1-3 show only marginal improvements over baselines, with the "deep" structure providing minimal gains (Table 3 shows diminishing returns with additional layers). The paper acknowledges "pruning" issues where many latent dimensions contain only noise, which undermines the value of the deep structure.

4. **Methodological concerns**: The inference approach combines variational inference with neural networks in a way that lacks theoretical grounding. The loss function (Equation 18) appears to be a heuristic adaptation of VAE loss, but the paper fails to justify why this specific formulation is appropriate for Poisson count data.

5. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims "excellent results" and "better effectiveness and interpretability" without providing sufficient evidence. For example, the ICEWS prediction task shows DDPFA performing worse than PGDS, contradicting the paper's claims.

6. **Reproducibility concerns**: The paper lacks sufficient detail about neural network architectures (e.g., exact number of hidden units in RNNs), training procedures, and hyperparameters. The statement "the size of the window is 4" without justification is insufficient for replication.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field of dynamic Poisson factorization by adding a deep structure. However, the core innovation is not sufficiently justified, and the empirical results do not demonstrate that the added complexity is warranted. The "deep" aspect appears to be more of a structural modification than a meaningful improvement, with the paper itself acknowledging diminishing returns from additional layers.

The most significant issue is the lack of clear distinction from prior deep Poisson factorization work. The paper cites "Deep Poisson Factor Analysis based on RBM or SBN" as prior work but fails to demonstrate how DDPFA differs meaningfully from these approaches. Without this, it's difficult to assess the novelty.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a relevant problem and provides a technically detailed model, the contribution is not sufficiently novel or impactful. The claimed advantages over simpler models are not convincingly demonstrated, and the paper fails to establish a clear theoretical or empirical justification for the deep structure. The marginal performance gains in most experiments do not justify the increased model complexity.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more thorough comparison with existing deep Poisson factorization approaches
2. A clear theoretical justification for why the deep structure is necessary
3. Better justification for the architectural choices (e.g., number of layers, window size)
4. More rigorous statistical evaluation of the performance gains

As it stands, the paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way, which is a critical requirement for NIPS. The results are interesting but not sufficiently novel or compelling to warrant publication.
