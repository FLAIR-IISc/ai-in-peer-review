Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count data that extends Poisson factorization (PF) with (i) dynamic latent factors θt with deep, windowed temporal dependencies parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs), (ii) a multi-layer hierarchy h(n)t to capture longer-range dependencies, and (iii) feature- and component-specific gamma scales ψv and λk to encourage sparsity and adaptivity. The generative likelihood is Poisson with rate ∑k θtk φvk λk ψv. The temporal prior for θt is “implicit,” with Gamma parameters output by an RNN taking as input a window of lower-layer latents; the higher layers h(n)t are also modeled by RNN-conditioned Gamma distributions. Inference alternates between (a) mean-field VI with conjugate Gamma-Poisson updates for Φ, Λ, Ψ, Θ (using auxiliary latent counts xvtk), and (b) updating the RNN parameters by minimizing a reconstruction-style “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that matches expectations of the (implicit) prior and posterior and a squared-error reconstruction term. Experiments on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Populations) compare against PGDS, LSTM, and static PFA, reporting MSE/PMSE.

Relation to prior work
The model builds on PF and dynamic PF variants, notably PGDS and related Gamma/NB-process models, Gamma Belief Networks, and deep PF (e.g., Deep Exponential Families; deep PF with SBN/RBM). It also relates to deep sequential latent variable models (VRNNs, Deep Markov Models) with Poisson emissions, and to recurrent PF for temporal recommendation. The idea of using amortized networks to parameterize latent variable distributions is standard; the novelty here is combining PF’s conjugacy for some variables with RNN-parameterized Gamma priors for dynamic factors, plus a ladder-like structure.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to combine the modeling strengths of PF (factor interpretability, conjugacy) with flexible deep temporal structure via RNNs.
- The hierarchical latent design aims to capture longer-term dependencies beyond first-order Markov; windowing plus depth could, in principle, model complex temporal patterns.
- Alternating between closed-form mean-field updates for conjugate blocks and learned implicit priors is computationally appealing.
- Experiments span several datasets and include both fitting and one-step prediction; some qualitative interpretability is discussed (e.g., ICEWS factors).

Major weaknesses and technical concerns
- Probabilistic model specification is unclear/inconsistent. The generative process for h(n) is top-down (Eq. 4), but the posterior updates (Eq. 10/14) add two “shape” terms α and γ, where γ is computed by a “posterior” network conditioned on the lower layer. This mixes prior and posterior in the same Gamma parameter and does not correspond to a coherent generative model. No derivation shows that the complete conditionals for h(n) remain Gamma under this construction.
- Objective mismatch and lack of theory. The paper introduces an ELBO (Eq. 6) but trains the RNN parameters with squared-error reconstruction losses (Eqs. 16–18), not by maximizing a valid ELBO (no bound, no guarantee of convergence, potential double-counting). There is no proof that the two-stage procedure optimizes a single objective or increases any bound. This undermines claims of principled Bayesian inference.
- Related work and baselines are incomplete. Modern deep sequential latent-variable baselines (VRNN, Deep Markov Models with Poisson emissions) and recurrent PF variants are not compared; several citations are incomplete/mislabeled. Without these, it is hard to assess novelty and performance.
- Evaluation metrics are weak. MSE/PMSE are not ideal for counts; predictive log-likelihood (or held-out perplexity) is standard. No uncertainty-aware evaluation is provided. Hyperparameter selection, validation splits, and fairness of baseline tuning are under-specified.
- Identifiability and scaling issues. Introducing Λ and Ψ on top of Φ and Θ exacerbates non-identifiability; no constraints (e.g., column normalization of Φ) or post-scaling are described. Claims about “temporal smoothness constraints” from Λ/Ψ are not supported (they are static).
- Notational and correctness issues. Eq. 7 appears to include φ twice; several equations have typos, and the notation (e.g., θshp, θrte) is inconsistently defined. The Bernoulli-Poisson link is discussed but not used in experiments. References are incomplete.
- Practical details missing. Network architectures, RNN types, window size sensitivity, K selection, depth ablations, computational complexity, and runtime are not sufficiently reported. The paper notes “pruning” and diminishing returns from depth but does not provide systematic ablations.

Quality
- Technical soundness is questionable due to the unclear generative story for h(n) and the non-ELBO training objective. While the conjugate blocks are standard, the overall algorithm lacks a principled convergence guarantee or a unifying probabilistic objective.

Clarity
- The manuscript is difficult to follow: important definitions are ambiguous; figures/tables referenced are not included; several equations contain errors; many grammatical issues. Reproducibility is low given missing implementation and tuning details.

Originality
- The idea of RNN-parameterized Gamma priors for PF dynamics is interesting, but similar ideas exist in deep sequential latent models and in deep PF. The proposed “Probability AutoEncoder” training is non-standard but insufficiently justified.

Significance
- Potentially useful if a principled ELBO-based training is provided and strong baselines are matched. As presented, the impact is limited by methodological and empirical weaknesses.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you specify a coherent generative model for all layers (top-down only) and an explicit amortized variational family q with RNN encoders, then optimize a single ELBO? How do Eqs. 10/14 arise from the model?
2. Why not use predictive log-likelihood for evaluation? How were hyperparameters chosen for all methods?
3. What is the exact RNN architecture, parameter counts, and complexity per iteration? How do results vary with window size c, K, depth N?
4. How do you address non-identifiability among Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ? Do you normalize Φ columns or fix expected λk or ψv?
5. Can you compare against VRNN/DMM with Poisson emissions and recurrent PF baselines?

Suggestions
- Recast DDPFA as a coherent generative model with top-down priors and amortized approximate posteriors, and train with a valid ELBO (black-box VI; implicit reparameterization for Gamma; or augmentation-based methods).
- Replace MSE/PMSE with held-out log-likelihood, and add ablations (no Λ/Ψ; no depth; no ladder; varying c).
- Tighten notation, fix equations, and complete references. Provide code and detailed training protocols.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem (dynamic count data) with a potentially powerful combination of PF and deep temporal modeling.
- Uses conjugacy where available; attempts to improve interpretability and long-range dependence.
- Broad experimental scope.

Cons
- Incoherent probabilistic specification for deep latents; non-principled training objective.
- Missing strong baselines and appropriate metrics; limited empirical support.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; notation errors.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. The paper tackles a relevant problem with an interesting direction, but requires a principled objective, clearer modeling/inference, stronger baselines, and more rigorous evaluation.
