

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper introduces Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA), a novel approach to modeling sequentially observed count data by incorporating deep structures to capture long-time dependence. The authors build upon previous work on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS), arguing that these shallow models fail to capture complex temporal dependencies. Their key innovation is using a deep, recurrent structure to model the temporal evolution of latent factors, with variational inference for training.

The paper makes a compelling case for the need to model long-time dependence in count data, which is common in applications like recommendation systems, text analysis, and event tracking. While previous work (e.g., PGDS) used shallow temporal structures with Gamma transitions, DDPFA employs a deep architecture with multiple layers of latent variables connected through recurrent neural networks. The authors present a "ladder-like architecture" to address limitations of hierarchical latent variable models, and they demonstrate their approach on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel Conceptual Approach:** The paper presents a genuinely novel application of deep structures to Bayesian Poisson factorization for capturing long-time dependencies. This is a bold step beyond standard dynamic models that typically use shallow temporal structures.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments on four synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population) provide strong empirical evidence for the approach's effectiveness.
3. **Clear Differentiation:** The paper effectively demonstrates how DDPFA outperforms PGDS, PFA, and LSTM in capturing complex temporal patterns, particularly in datasets with long-term dependencies (e.g., ICEWS, EBOLA).
4. **Honest Limitations:** The authors acknowledge limitations like the "pruning" problem and the diminishing returns of adding more layers, which demonstrates scientific integrity.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of Clear Differentiation from Standard Deep Learning:** While the paper claims novelty, it doesn't sufficiently explain how their deep structure fundamentally differs from standard LSTM or RNN approaches that have been successfully applied to sequential count data. The distinction between their "deep structure" and standard deep learning methods for sequential data is blurred.
2. **Incremental Nature of the Contribution:** The core idea—using deep networks to model temporal dependencies in count data—is not entirely novel. Deep Poisson Factor Analysis and similar approaches have been explored before (cited in the paper). The paper's contribution appears to be an incremental extension rather than a paradigm shift.
3. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical argument for why a deep structure is necessary for capturing long-time dependence, rather than simply using a longer window with a shallow model or standard deep learning.
4. **"Ladder Architecture" Not Fully Justified:** The ladder architecture is mentioned as a solution to a problem with hierarchical latent variable models, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this specific architecture is necessary or superior to standard deep learning approaches.

**Significance Assessment:**
The paper addresses an important problem in sequential count data analysis with broad applicability. The results show that DDPFA can capture complex temporal patterns better than existing methods, particularly in cases where long-term dependencies are crucial. However, the significance of the contribution is somewhat diminished by the incremental nature of the approach and the lack of clear differentiation from standard deep learning methods.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a novel and bold approach to modeling sequential count data with deep structures, which aligns with NIPS's preference for innovative ideas. While the methodology is not entirely novel, the specific application to Poisson factorization with a deep temporal structure represents a meaningful extension that could inspire new research directions. The empirical results are strong, and the paper's honesty about limitations is commendable.

The main drawback is that the novelty is somewhat incremental rather than transformative. However, given the reviewer's emphasis on novelty over methodological perfection, and the paper's clear demonstration of a new approach to a challenging problem, I recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Presents a novel application of deep learning to capture long-time dependence in Poisson factorization
- Demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods on multiple datasets
- Introduces a ladder-like architecture to address limitations of hierarchical models
- Provides comprehensive experimental validation with both synthetic and real-world data

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- The core idea is somewhat incremental, building on existing deep learning approaches
- Lacks clear differentiation from standard deep learning methods for sequential data
- The "deep" structure isn't convincingly shown to be necessary rather than just another deep learning approach

Overall, while the paper doesn't represent a revolutionary breakthrough, it offers a meaningful and well-executed extension to the field that aligns with NIPS's interest in novel approaches to challenging problems. The novelty of the specific application outweighs the incremental nature of the technical contribution, making this a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
