

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) that extends traditional Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating deep neural networks to model complex temporal dependencies in count data. Building on previous work like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis, the authors introduce a deep structure with multiple layers of latent variables and recurrent neural networks to capture both short-term and long-term temporal patterns in time series count data.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound model that builds on established work in Bayesian factor analysis and deep learning. The variational inference algorithm is well-motivated and properly derived, with appropriate mathematical notation. The model extends existing approaches by adding a deep structure to capture long-term dependencies, which is a reasonable direction for improvement.

However, the paper lacks strong theoretical justification for why a deep structure is necessary. The claim that previous shallow models "may still have some shortcomings in exploring long-time dependence" is not sufficiently supported with theoretical analysis or empirical evidence showing where shallow models fail. The comparison with PGDS is somewhat superficial, and the paper doesn't provide a clear analysis of when the deep structure provides benefits over simpler alternatives.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, with results on four synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population). However, the results lack statistical significance testing, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements over baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) are meaningful. The paper acknowledges limitations like the "pruning" problem and diminishing returns of adding layers, which is commendable.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the generative model is well-defined. The connection between the deep structure and temporal dependencies could be clarified with more detailed examples or visualizations. The paper references Figures 1-3, which would likely enhance clarity, but these are not included in the provided text.

The explanation of the neural network architecture and training procedure is somewhat superficial. The "ladder-like architecture" mentioned as a solution to the pruning problem isn't well-explained in the main text, and the transition between the mean-field variational inference and the probability autoencoder section could be smoother.

## Originality

The combination of Poisson factor analysis with deep recurrent structures is somewhat novel, but the paper doesn't clearly differentiate its contribution from existing deep Poisson factorization approaches. While the authors cite Deep Poisson Factor Analysis based on RBM/SBN [9,10], they don't provide a compelling argument for why their deep structure is fundamentally different or better. The paper mentions that previous deep models like Deep Exponential Families [8] or Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9,10] have limitations, but doesn't establish a clear gap that DDPFA fills.

The ladder-like architecture is an interesting modification, but its novelty isn't sufficiently established. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with related deep learning approaches for time series count data that have been presented at NIPS or other top conferences.

## Significance

The problem of modeling temporal dependencies in count data is important for applications in recommendation systems, text analysis, and network analysis. The results on synthetic data are promising, with DDPFA outperforming baselines in most cases. However, the real-world results are mixed - DDPFA performs well on some datasets (EBOLA, ASP) but not on others (ICEWS), where PGDS performs better. The paper claims "better effectivity and interpretability" but doesn't provide strong evidence for these claims.

The significance is limited by the fact that the improvements aren't dramatic across all datasets, and the paper doesn't clearly establish how much better the method is than existing approaches. The ICEWS prediction results being worse than PGDS is particularly problematic, as this is a key real-world dataset.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses an important problem in time series analysis, but the contribution is not sufficiently differentiated from existing work. The deep structure is an interesting idea, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this approach is necessary or how it significantly improves upon existing methods. The experimental validation is promising but lacks statistical significance testing, and the paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach.

**Strengths:**
- Clear problem statement and relevant application areas
- Good experimental evaluation on multiple datasets
- Well-structured mathematical formulation
- Acknowledgment of limitations

**Weaknesses:**
- Weak theoretical justification for the deep structure
- Lack of statistical significance testing in experiments
- Insufficient comparison with related deep learning approaches
- Mixed results on real-world datasets
- Superficial analysis of when the deep structure provides benefits

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection with the possibility of resubmission after addressing the weaknesses. The paper has potential but needs stronger theoretical justification, more thorough comparison with related work, and better experimental analysis with statistical significance testing. The authors should also clarify the conditions under which their deep structure provides benefits over simpler alternatives.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of why a deep structure is necessary for capturing temporal dependencies
2. Conduct statistical significance tests on the experimental results
3. Include a more thorough comparison with related deep learning approaches for time series count data
4. Clarify the "ladder-like architecture" and its benefits
5. Provide deeper analysis of why the model performs poorly on ICEWS but well on other datasets
6. Add a section on scalability analysis to address how the model handles larger datasets
