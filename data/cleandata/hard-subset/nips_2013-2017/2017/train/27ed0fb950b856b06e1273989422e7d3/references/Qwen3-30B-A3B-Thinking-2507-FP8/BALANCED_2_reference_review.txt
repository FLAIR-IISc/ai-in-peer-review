

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

## Summary
This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) that extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating deep learning to capture complex temporal dependencies in sequential count data. The authors build upon previous work like PGDS [2] and deep Poisson factorization methods [9, 10, 12], introducing a deep structure with multiple layers of latent variables connected via recurrent neural networks to model long-term temporal patterns. The model uses variational inference for parameter estimation and is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population).

## Strengths
1. **Problem Relevance**: The paper addresses an important problem in sequential count data analysis with applications in recommendation systems, text analysis, and network analysis.
2. **Technical Approach**: The integration of deep learning with Poisson factorization is a reasonable extension of existing work, and the variational inference framework is well-motivated for scalability.
3. **Experimental Evaluation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets, with appropriate baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) and evaluation metrics (MSE, PMSE).
4. **Interpretability**: The case study on ICEWS demonstrates the model's ability to learn meaningful temporal patterns (e.g., Israel-Palestinian conflict, six-party talks), providing valuable interpretability.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Novelty**: The approach combines existing techniques (PFA, deep learning, variational inference) without sufficiently distinguishing from recent deep Poisson factorization work [9, 10, 12]. The depth of the model is very shallow (only 2 layers in experiments), and Table 3 shows diminishing returns from adding more layers, questioning the value of the "deep" aspect.
2. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison**: The paper fails to compare with more recent deep models specifically designed for count data, such as Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9, 10] and Deep Exponential Families [12], which directly address the same problem.
3. **Inference Limitations**: The use of mean-field variational inference is a significant simplifying assumption that may not capture complex posterior dependencies, and the paper doesn't adequately address this limitation or explore more sophisticated inference methods.
4. **Inconsistent Performance**: The model shows poor performance on the ICEWS prediction task (Table 2), which isn't adequately explained or contextualized within the paper.
5. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper lacks a clear theoretical argument for why the deep structure improves performance beyond empirical results, and the connection to established deep learning theory is weak.

## Specific Recommendations
1. **Clarify Novelty**: Conduct a more thorough comparison with recent deep Poisson factorization methods [9, 10, 12] to better establish how DDPFA differs and improves upon these approaches.
2. **Expand Baseline Comparison**: Include comparisons with more recent deep learning models specifically designed for count data, such as Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9, 10] and Deep Exponential Families [12].
3. **Address Inference Limitations**: Discuss the limitations of mean-field variational inference for this problem and consider experiments with more sophisticated inference methods (e.g., structured variational inference, MCMC).
4. **Analyze ICEWS Failure**: Provide a detailed analysis of why DDPFA performs poorly on ICEWS compared to PGDS, which would help establish the model's strengths and limitations.
5. **Justify Depth**: Conduct experiments to determine the optimal depth for the model and provide theoretical justification for the chosen architecture rather than relying on empirical results from limited experiments.

## Overall Assessment
The paper presents a technically sound approach to modeling sequential count data with reasonable empirical results. However, the novelty is somewhat incremental, and the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that the deep structure provides meaningful advantages over simpler approaches. The limited depth of the model (2 layers) and the lack of comparison with more recent deep Poisson factorization methods weaken the contribution. While the experimental results are promising on some datasets, the inconsistent performance (particularly on ICEWS) and incomplete baseline comparison prevent the paper from making a strong contribution to the field.

The paper would benefit significantly from a more rigorous comparison with recent deep learning approaches for count data, a deeper analysis of the model's limitations, and a clearer justification for the "deep" aspect of the model. Without these improvements, the contribution does not substantially advance the state of the art in this domain.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper presents a reasonable extension to existing work but fails to sufficiently distinguish itself from recent deep Poisson factorization approaches and doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the deep structure provides meaningful advantages. With significant revisions to address the novelty, baseline comparison, and theoretical justification, this could be a strong contribution.
