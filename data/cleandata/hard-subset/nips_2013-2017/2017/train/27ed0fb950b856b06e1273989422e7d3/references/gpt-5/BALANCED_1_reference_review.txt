Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA) for sequential count data. Observations xvt are modeled via a Poisson factorization with feature- and component-wise multiplicative weights: xvtk ~ Poisson(θtk φvk λk ψv), with θt capturing time-varying factor strengths, φ the loadings, and λ, ψ providing component- and feature-level scaling and sparsity. The core novelty is a deep, recurrent prior placed on θt (and auxiliary latent layers h(n)t), where the conditional distributions p(θt | h(0) t−c:t) and p(h(n)t | h(n+1) t−c:t) are “implicit” and parameterized by RNNs; the top latent layer uses Gamma transitions with a temporal window. Inference alternates between mean-field VI (leveraging Gamma–Poisson conjugacy with auxiliary counts) and training the RNNs via a “probability autoencoder” objective that aligns encoder/decoder expectations with L2 losses, followed by fine-tuning.

The work builds on Poisson factorization and dynamic count models (e.g., PGDS/Poisson–Gamma dynamical systems; negative-binomial processes; recurrent Poisson factorization), and on deep latent variable models such as Deep Exponential Families, Gamma Belief Networks, Ladder VAE, and VRNN-style amortized inference. Related NeurIPS/AISTATS work includes Ranganath et al. (DEFs), Zhou et al. (Gamma/Poisson processes, GBN), Schein et al. (PGDS), and deep topic models/DPF variants. The paper also mentions Bernoulli–Poisson links for binary data.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to marry Poisson factorization for counts with deep/recurrent structure to capture both short- and long-range temporal dependencies.
- Uses auxiliary multiplicative factors (λ, ψ) to encourage component/global sparsity and interpretability.
- Provides a pragmatic alternating optimization scheme that can leverage conjugate updates for many variables and gradient-based training for the RNN components.
- Includes experiments on several synthetic and real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, disasters, sheep population) and compares to PFA, PGDS, and LSTM.
- The discussion candidly acknowledges issues (e.g., pruning, limited benefits from depth) and explores a ladder-style architecture as a remedy.

Weaknesses
Quality (soundness and support)
- The probabilistic model and variational objective are not rigorously derived. The “implicit” RNN priors combined with conjugate updates assume Gamma forms (Eqs. 9–14), but it is unclear under what conditions these remain valid when prior shapes are outputs of a neural network that also depends on other latent variables in the hierarchy. The paper does not present a coherent ELBO that the full training procedure monotonically optimizes.
- The “probability autoencoder” objective (Eqs. 15–18) replaces intractable terms with L2 losses on expectations; there is no theoretical justification or bound linking this to the marginal likelihood. As such, the two-stage algorithm may not be optimizing a single well-defined objective.
- Important modeling choices are underspecified: exact parameterization and constraints of RNN outputs to ensure valid Gamma parameters; the top-layer temporal prior; whether priors are stationary; and how the auxiliary posterior networks avoid circularity/double counting.
- Evaluation uses MSE/PMSE rather than predictive log-likelihood/perplexity more standard for count models; the split protocol (e.g., rolling forecasting vs last-step prediction) is minimally described. No uncertainty estimates or calibration are reported.
- Runtime, convergence diagnostics, and sensitivity to K, depth, window size, and hyperparameters are largely missing. Figures/tables referenced are not included for verification.

Clarity
- The paper is difficult to follow: notation is often inconsistent, terms like “implicit distribution” are used informally, and some equations contain typos (e.g., subscript/superscript confusion). Many references are incomplete.
- The generative process (especially for the deep hierarchy) should be stated cleanly before inference, with precise definitions of all conditional distributions.

Originality
- Placing a deep/recurrent prior over Poisson factor scores is a reasonable idea; however, conceptually similar approaches exist: VRNN/Deep Markov Models for sequences, DEF/GBN for count hierarchies, and recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation. The paper’s key novelty appears to be the specific combination with Gamma-Poisson conjugacy and the ad-hoc probability autoencoder training, which is not fully justified.

Significance
- The empirical improvements over PGDS/PFA/LSTM (as presented) are promising on some datasets, but baselines are limited given the literature (e.g., recurrent Poisson factorization, DEF/GBN variants, dynamic topic models). Without stronger baselines, standard predictive likelihood metrics, and ablations with clear gains from depth and recurrence, it is hard to assess impact.
- The lack of a principled learning objective diminishes the likelihood that others will build on this method.

Questions for the authors
1) What is the exact ELBO being optimized jointly over (Θ, Φ, Λ, Ψ, H, network params)? Can you derive it and show how your updates correspond to coordinate ascent/gradient steps on that ELBO?
2) How are the RNN outputs constrained to valid Gamma parameters (positivity, stability)? Are these priors stationary/shared across time?
3) How do you prevent information leakage between encoder/decoder networks that share latent variables within the same minibatch/time window?
4) Can you report predictive Poisson log-likelihood (or held-out perplexity) with rolling forecasts, and include stronger baselines (e.g., recurrent Poisson factorization, Deep Markov Models adapted to counts, GBN/DEF variants)?
5) Please provide code, data splits, and runtime comparisons.

Pros for acceptance
- Tackles an important problem: modeling long-range dependencies in sequential count data.
- Interesting hybrid of conjugate Poisson factorization and deep recurrent priors; potentially useful if formalized.
- Empirical results suggest benefits over PGDS/PFA on several tasks; interpretability (factors over time) is appealing.

Cons for acceptance
- Method lacks a rigorous, unified probabilistic/variational formulation; training objective is ad-hoc and not tied to a bound.
- Clarity and completeness issues (equations, missing figures/tables, references).
- Limited and non-standard evaluation; missing key baselines and predictive likelihood metrics.
- Reproducibility concerns (no code, insufficient details).
- Modest novelty relative to existing deep sequential latent variable models for counts.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper addresses a relevant problem and contains promising ideas, but the current form falls short on theoretical soundness, clarity, and evaluation. Strengthening any two of these might make it competitive.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a clear generative model and a single ELBO; use amortized inference for all latent variables with reparameterizable Gamma surrogates (e.g., Weibull/Lognormal or augmentation) or implicit VI with learned ratios; or adopt a Deep Markov Model framework with count likelihoods.
- Replace the L2 “probability autoencoder” with a principled variational objective (e.g., ladder VAE/VRNN ELBO with KL terms across layers/time). Prove convergence/monotonicity or provide empirical diagnostics.
- Use standard predictive metrics (held-out log-likelihood, perplexity) with rolling forecasts and multiple horizons; include stronger baselines and ablations isolating the effect of depth, window size, λ/ψ, and ladder connections.
- Improve exposition, fix notation/typos, complete references, and release code.
