

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes a "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model" (DDPFA) that extends Poisson Factor Analysis with deep structures and recurrent neural networks to capture long-time temporal dependencies in sequential count data. The authors claim their model outperforms existing methods (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) on synthetic and real-world datasets, particularly in capturing complex temporal patterns.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a comprehensive experimental evaluation across four synthetic and five real-world datasets. The problem of analyzing sequential count data is relevant to NIPS, and the authors make a reasonable effort to compare against several baselines. The implementation details and experimental setup are sufficiently described.

**Weaknesses:** The paper suffers from significant methodological and evidentiary shortcomings that undermine its main claims.

First, the core contribution of "deep structure to capture long-time dependence" is poorly justified and contradicted by the authors' own experimental results. Table 3 explicitly shows that adding more layers or expanding dimensions in upper layers does not significantly improve performance ("performance cannot be improved distinctly by adding more layers"). The authors themselves note that "the uselessness of adding network layers" is a problem, which directly contradicts the paper's central premise. If deeper layers provide no benefit, the "deep" aspect of the model is unnecessary and appears to be added without proper justification.

Second, the paper lacks rigorous validation of its claims. The authors claim DDPFA "has the best performance in fitting and prediction task of all the datasets" but provide no statistical significance testing to support this assertion. The comparison with baselines is superficial, limited to only three methods (PGDS, LSTM, PFA), and fails to explain why traditional methods like PGDS fail on certain datasets when DDPFA succeeds. The paper's claim that PGDS "may still have some shortcomings in exploring the long-time dependence" is not substantiated with evidence beyond the limited results presented.

Third, the paper fails to provide theoretical justification for why deep structures are necessary for capturing temporal dependencies in count data. The authors cite "deep models" as being widely used in Bayesian models but do not demonstrate that deep structures are theoretically or empirically necessary for this specific problem. The paper's own experiments (Table 3) show that the deep structure may even be detrimental to performance, which should have prompted a fundamental reconsideration of the approach rather than its inclusion as a main contribution.

Fourth, the paper notes "pruning" as a problem (where "some dimensions in the latent layers only contain data noise") but doesn't address how this issue is specifically solved by their proposed method. This is a known problem with variational inference that the authors should have acknowledged and addressed, rather than presenting it as a minor observation.

Finally, the paper's experimental design lacks depth. The window size (4) is chosen without justification, and the number of layers (2) is fixed without exploring the impact of different depths. The paper claims "the long-time activation of factors is shown in this figure" (Figure 2), but the figure provides no statistical evidence to support this claim.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses a relevant problem and provides a reasonable experimental evaluation, it fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its main claim that a deep structure is necessary and beneficial for capturing long-time dependencies in sequential count data. The authors' own results undermine the core contribution of the paper, showing that deeper layers provide no significant benefit. The paper reads more like a "deep learning for the sake of deep learning" approach rather than a well-justified methodological advancement. The authors would need to substantially revise their work to provide rigorous theoretical justification, statistical validation of their claims, and evidence that the deep structure is genuinely necessary and beneficial for the problem at hand. Without these, the paper does not meet the quality and significance standards expected at NIPS.
