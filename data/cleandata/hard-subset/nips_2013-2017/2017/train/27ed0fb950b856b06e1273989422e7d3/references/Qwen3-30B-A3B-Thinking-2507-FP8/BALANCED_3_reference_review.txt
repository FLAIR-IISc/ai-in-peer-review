

**Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"**

**Summary**  
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA), a model for sequential count data that extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) by incorporating deep recurrent structures to capture complex temporal dependencies. Building on prior work (e.g., [2, 12]), DDPFA uses variational inference with RNNs to model latent factor transitions across layers, aiming to overcome PGDS’s shallow temporal dependencies and PFA’s lack of long-range modeling. The authors evaluate DDPFA on synthetic datasets and five real-world applications (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, etc.), comparing against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

**Strengths**  
1. **Problem Relevance**: The paper addresses a meaningful gap in modeling dynamic count data (e.g., text, event sequences), where long-term dependencies are critical but underexplored in Bayesian factorization.  
2. **Technical Execution**: The variational inference framework is well-structured, with clear derivations for mean-field updates and a novel "ladder" architecture to mitigate layer redundancy (Table 3).  
3. **Empirical Rigor**: Experiments cover diverse datasets (synthetic and real-world), and the ablation study (Table 3) effectively identifies limitations of depth/width in the deep structure.  
4. **Clarity**: The model description is logically organized, and the connection between Bayesian factorization and deep learning is well-motivated.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Significant Experimental Shortcomings**:  
   - DDPFA fails on ICEWS prediction (Table 2), while PGDS (which uses a transition matrix) performs better. The paper does not analyze *why* this occurs, undermining claims of superiority.  
   - The "pruning" issue (Table 3) and diminishing returns from deeper layers suggest the model may not fully leverage its depth. The authors acknowledge this but offer no concrete solutions beyond "try other inference methods."  
2. **Originality Concerns**:  
   - The deep structure resembles prior work (e.g., [12] on recurrent Deep Poisson Factor Modeling), yet the paper does not sufficiently differentiate DDPFA’s innovations. The "ladder" architecture is mentioned as a fix for redundancy, but its novelty over existing hierarchical VAEs (e.g., [24]) is unclear.  
3. **Technical Gaps**:  
   - The mean-field VI approximation may limit expressiveness (noted in the paper but unaddressed). The "pruning" phenomenon (where latent dimensions capture noise) is linked to MF-variational inference, yet no ablation with alternative inference (e.g., MCMC) is provided.  
   - Figure 1 (illustrating the deep structure) is referenced but missing, reducing clarity.  
4. **Overstated Significance**:  
   - While DDPFA outperforms baselines on most datasets, the ICEWS failure and layer redundancy issues temper claims of "better performance" and "interpretability." The paper overemphasizes "long-time dependence" without quantifying *how much* deeper layers contribute (e.g., via layer-wise error decomposition).

**Recommendation**  
**Reject**. While the work is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem, the experimental weaknesses (ICEWS failure, unexplained pruning) and insufficient differentiation from prior deep Poisson models prevent it from meeting NIPS’s standards for significance and novelty. The paper would benefit from:  
1. **Deeper analysis of ICEWS failure** (e.g., comparing transition dynamics with PGDS).  
2. **Clarifying novelty** against [12] and hierarchical VAEs (e.g., [24]), specifically how the ladder architecture solves "pruning" beyond standard techniques.  
3. **Including Figure 1** and adding ablation studies with alternative inference (e.g., SVI vs. MF-variational).  
4. **Tempering claims** of "superior" performance to align with results (e.g., "DDPFA shows promise on most datasets but requires further refinement for robustness").  

**Constructive Suggestions**  
- **Address ICEWS**: Investigate if PGDS’s transition matrix (which smooths θₜ) inherently handles ICEWS’s noise better than DDPFA’s RNN-based approach.  
- **Expand on "pruning"**: Propose a mechanism to identify and prune redundant latent dimensions (e.g., via KL divergence thresholds), rather than attributing it to inference method alone.  
- **Compare to recent deep Poisson work**: Explicitly contrast with [12] and [9] to clarify how DDPFA’s deep structure differs (e.g., depth vs. recurrence).  
- **Quantify depth benefits**: Show error reduction per added layer (e.g., layer 1 vs. layer 2 in Table 3) to justify architectural choices.  

**Conclusion**  
The paper makes a reasonable contribution to dynamic count modeling but falls short due to unresolved experimental weaknesses and insufficient novelty. With targeted revisions addressing the ICEWS failure, layer redundancy, and clearer differentiation from prior work, it could be a strong candidate for future publication. As submitted, it does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS.
