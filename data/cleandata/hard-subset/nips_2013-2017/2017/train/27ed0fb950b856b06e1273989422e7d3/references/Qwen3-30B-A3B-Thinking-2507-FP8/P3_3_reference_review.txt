

# Peer Review: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

## Summary
This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) that extends existing Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) by incorporating recurrent neural networks to capture long-term temporal dependencies in sequential count data. The authors claim their "deep" architecture better models complex temporal patterns compared to previous methods. The model is evaluated on synthetic datasets and five real-world applications, showing improvements over PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA baselines.

## Strengths
1. **Problem Relevance**: The paper addresses an important problem in sequential count data analysis with applications across recommendation systems, text analysis, and network analysis.
2. **Experimental Results**: The model consistently outperforms baselines on most datasets (particularly in capturing long-term temporal patterns), as demonstrated in Tables 1-2 and Figures 2-3. The interpretability of factors (e.g., Figure 3 showing Israel-Palestinian conflict patterns) is a notable strength.
3. **Acknowledgement of Limitations**: The authors properly identify the "pruning" issue (where some latent dimensions contain only noise) and the limited benefits of adding more layers, showing appropriate scientific honesty.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Misleading "Deep" Architecture
The paper's main claim of "deep" structure is fundamentally misleading. The model uses a single recurrent layer for temporal dependencies (Equations 3-4), not a true deep architecture with multiple hidden layers. The "depth" appears to be in the hierarchical variational structure rather than in the temporal modeling. This mischaracterization is problematic because:
- It fails to distinguish from standard RNN approaches for temporal dependencies
- It doesn't leverage the true benefits of deep learning architectures
- It conflates "hierarchical" with "deep," which are distinct concepts in machine learning

The authors cite Deep Poisson Factor Analysis based on RBMs/SBNs [9, 10] but don't adequately differentiate their approach from these prior works. The paper's title and claims overstate the novelty.

### 2. Insufficient Theoretical Justification
The paper lacks strong theoretical justification for why their approach should outperform simpler alternatives. The variational inference procedure relies on mean-field approximations that may not be well-suited for capturing complex dependencies. The authors don't explain why their specific architecture is better than a standard RNN for the temporal dependencies, which would be a more straightforward approach.

### 3. Questionable Model Formulation
The model formulation (Equation 1) introduces redundant parameters (λk, ψv) that aren't properly justified. The claim that these "extract sparsity" is vague, as the standard PFA model already handles sparsity through the Gamma prior. The authors should clarify why these additional parameters are necessary and how they improve upon standard Poisson factorization.

### 4. Modest Performance Gains
The improvements over baselines are modest in most cases. On the ICEWS dataset (a major real-world application), DDPFA performs worse than PGDS in prediction tasks. The paper doesn't adequately explain this failure, which undermines the claimed advantage of the model. The "long-time dependence" claim is weakened when the model fails to capture patterns in a significant real-world dataset.

### 5. Inadequate Handling of "Pruning" Issue
The authors identify the "pruning" problem (where some latent dimensions contain only noise) but don't offer a novel solution. This is a well-known issue in variational inference [24], and the paper merely acknowledges it without proposing a meaningful fix. The suggestion to use auxiliary MCMC is mentioned but not implemented or evaluated, making the claim of addressing this problem superficial.

## Technical Concerns
- The inference procedure (Equations 11-14) lacks sufficient derivation detail, making it difficult to verify the correctness of the variational updates.
- The "ladder architecture" mentioned to address pruning (Table 3) isn't clearly explained or justified in the text.
- The model's window size (c=4) and layer count (N=2) are arbitrary choices with no thorough ablation study to justify them.

## Recommendation
I recommend **weak rejection** for this paper. The problem addressed is important, and the paper demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines on most datasets. However, the contribution doesn't provide a compelling enough advancement to warrant publication at NIPS for the following reasons:

1. The "deep" aspect of the model is misleading and doesn't represent a meaningful technical contribution.
2. The paper fails to adequately differentiate from prior deep Poisson factorization work [9, 10].
3. The theoretical justification is weak and the model formulation is questionable.
4. The performance gains over baselines are modest, particularly on the ICEWS dataset where the model fails.

The authors should substantially revise the paper to:
- Clarify the true nature of the "depth" in their model
- Provide a more thorough comparison with existing deep Poisson factorization methods
- Strengthen the theoretical justification for their approach
- Address the "pruning" issue with a novel solution rather than just acknowledging it

Without these revisions, the paper doesn't demonstrate a sufficiently novel contribution to advance the state of the art in sequential count data modeling.

## Summary of Arguments

*Pro Acceptance:*
- Addresses a meaningful problem in sequential count data analysis
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines on most datasets
- Provides interpretable factors for domain-specific insights

*Con Rejection:*
- The "deep" aspect is misleading and not truly deep
- Insufficient differentiation from prior work on deep Poisson factorization
- Limited theoretical justification for the approach
- Modest performance gains over baselines
- "Pruning" issue is a known problem not addressed with novel solutions

This paper makes a modest contribution to the field but doesn't represent a significant enough advancement to warrant publication at NIPS. The authors should substantially revise the paper to clarify the novelty and strengthen the theoretical and experimental foundations.
