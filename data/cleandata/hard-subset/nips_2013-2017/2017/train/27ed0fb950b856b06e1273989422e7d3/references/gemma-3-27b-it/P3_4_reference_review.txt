## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel deep learning approach to dynamic Poisson factor analysis (DDPFA), aiming to capture complex temporal dependencies in sequential count data. While the idea is interesting and builds upon established methods like PFA and PGDS, the paper suffers from a lack of rigorous experimental validation and clarity in presentation. The results, while promising, are not compelling enough to warrant strong acceptance given the limitations discussed below. I lean towards a **weak reject** with significant revisions needed.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of modeling sequentially observed count vectors, a common scenario in various fields like recommendation systems and text analysis. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing Bayesian factor analysis models (PFA, PGDS) and temporal Dirichlet processes in capturing long-range dependencies and complex relationships between factors.  The authors propose DDPFA, which integrates recurrent neural networks (RNNs) into the PFA framework to model the temporal evolution of latent factors, and stacks multiple layers to capture long-term dependencies. This aligns with the broader trend of leveraging deep learning for Bayesian modeling, referencing relevant work like Deep Exponential Families, Deep Poisson Factor Analysis, and Neural Autoregressive Density Estimation. The paper correctly positions itself within the evolving landscape of deep probabilistic modeling.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of PFA with deep recurrent networks for modeling dynamic count data is a potentially valuable contribution. The idea of using RNNs to capture complex temporal dependencies in the factor loading and scores is interesting and goes beyond simple Gamma-based transitions.
* **Model Flexibility:**  The deep architecture offers the potential to capture hierarchical dependencies and non-linear relationships within the data, a clear advantage over shallower models.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear derivation of the model, outlining the probabilistic assumptions and the factorization approach. The variational inference approach is a reasonable choice for tackling the complexity of the model.
* **Exploration of Datasets:** The authors evaluate their model on a diverse set of datasets, including both synthetic and real-world scenarios.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Rigorous Comparison & Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares DDPFA with PGDS, LSTM, and PFA, the comparison is not comprehensive.  The hyperparameters for LSTM are simply *stated* to be chosen, but no justification or sensitivity analysis is provided. Critically, there's a lack of *ablation studies*. How much of the performance gain comes from the deep structure *vs.* simply using an RNN at the first layer? Without these studies, it's difficult to ascertain the true benefit of the full DDPFA model.
* **Clarity and Presentation:**  The paper suffers from several clarity issues. Equation (11) is poorly explained, and its connection to the overall inference process isn’t immediately apparent. The description of the loss function (Eq. 18) and the ladder-like architecture is also convoluted and requires careful reading. Figures 1 and 2, while helpful, are not fully explained in the caption and require further elaboration in the text.
* **Experimental Setup & Hyperparameter Tuning:** The hyperparameters for DDPFA seem rather arbitrarily chosen. A more systematic hyperparameter search would be crucial to demonstrate the robustness of the model. The choice of K (number of factors) is also not justified clearly for each dataset.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the DDPFA model, particularly the scalability to high-dimensional data and long time series.  Variational inference can be expensive, and the addition of RNNs exacerbates this issue.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim of "excellent results" is not fully supported by the experimental evidence.  While DDPFA performs well on some datasets, it doesn't consistently outperform all baselines, and the improvements are often marginal. The discussion of the ICEWS dataset highlights a weakness of the model, yet this is glossed over.
* **Discussion of "Pruning" and "Uselessness of Layers":** While identifying these issues is good, the paper doesn't offer any solutions or directions for future research to address them. Simply stating the problems without proposing mitigations weakens the contribution.

**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The core idea of integrating deep learning with PFA for dynamic count modeling is novel.
* The model is theoretically well-defined, with a clear probabilistic formulation.
* The empirical results show some promising performance gains on certain datasets.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of rigorous experimental validation and ablation studies.
* Clarity issues in the presentation and description of the model.
* Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
* Overstated claims and insufficient justification for hyperparameter choices.
* The identified problems of "pruning" and "uselessness of layers" are not addressed.




**5. Recommendations:**

The authors should focus on the following revisions:

* **Conduct thorough ablation studies:** Evaluate the contributions of the deep structure and the RNNs independently.
* **Perform a systematic hyperparameter search:**  Justify the choices of hyperparameters and demonstrate the robustness of the model.
* **Improve clarity and organization:** Rewrite sections with unclear explanations, provide more detailed captions for figures, and streamline the presentation of equations.
* **Address the scalability concerns:** Discuss the computational complexity of the model and potential strategies for improving scalability.
* **Provide a more balanced discussion of the results:** Acknowledge the limitations of the model and provide a more nuanced interpretation of the experimental findings.
* **Explore solutions to the "pruning" and "uselessness of layers" problem:** Discuss potential strategies to mitigate these issues, such as regularization techniques or architectural modifications.




In conclusion, while DDPFA presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach to modeling dynamic count data, the current manuscript requires substantial revisions to address the identified weaknesses and demonstrate its true scientific contribution.  I recommend **weak rejection** at this stage, pending significant revisions.
