

# Peer Review: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

## Summary
This paper introduces Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA), a novel approach for modeling sequentially observed count data. Building upon Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS), the authors extend these models by incorporating deep neural networks to capture complex long-term temporal dependencies. The key innovation is a hierarchical structure where each layer of latent variables is connected through recurrent neural networks, allowing the model to learn intricate relationships across time steps. The authors propose a variational inference approach with a probability autoencoder for parameter estimation. The model is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population), showing improvements over baselines including PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

The paper situates itself well within the literature, referencing recent advances in Bayesian factor analysis (e.g., PGDS [2]) and deep probabilistic models (e.g., Deep Exponential Families [8]). It addresses a clear gap in the literature: previous models struggle with capturing long-time dependence due to their shallow temporal structure, while standard deep learning approaches like LSTM are not specifically designed for count data.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The integration of deep learning with Poisson factorization for sequential count data represents a meaningful extension of previous work. The hierarchical structure with recurrent connections to model temporal dependencies is well-motivated and novel.

2. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation across diverse datasets, including synthetic data with varying complexity and five real-world applications. The comparison with multiple baselines (PGDS, LSTM, and PFA) is appropriate and demonstrates the model's advantages.

3. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written with clear mathematical formulations. The structure flows logically from problem statement to model development, inference, and experimental results.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The application to real-world datasets like ICEWS (international relations events) and EBOLA (outbreak data) demonstrates practical value for important domains where count data analysis is critical.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Analysis**: The paper lacks theoretical analysis of the model's properties. There are no convergence guarantees, bounds on approximation error, or analysis of the model's expressive capacity. This is a significant gap for a NIPS submission.

2. **Comparison with Deep Count Models**: While the paper cites Deep Poisson Factor Analysis based on RBMs/SBNs [9,10], it doesn't provide a detailed comparison with these approaches. The paper claims DDPFA outperforms shallow models, but the comparison with other deep count models is insufficient.

3. **Implementation Details**: The paper lacks specific details about neural network architectures used for the probability autoencoder (e.g., number of hidden units, activation functions). These details would be crucial for reproducibility.

4. **Limitation Analysis**: The paper acknowledges "pruning" and "uselessness of adding network layers" as issues but doesn't thoroughly investigate why these occur or how they might be addressed. The reference to [25] about hierarchical latent variable models is underutilized.

5. **LSTM Baseline Appropriateness**: Using LSTM as a baseline for count data is questionable, as LSTM is designed for continuous-valued sequences. The paper doesn't explain how the authors adapted LSTM for count data, which weakens the comparison.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound extension of Poisson factor analysis with promising empirical results. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and insufficient comparison with relevant deep count models are significant weaknesses for a NIPS submission. The model's novelty is somewhat incremental, building on existing deep probabilistic models rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Well-motivated problem addressing a clear gap in temporal count data analysis
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on diverse datasets
- Clear writing and organization
- Demonstrated practical utility on real-world problems

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Lack of theoretical foundation for the proposed method
- Inadequate comparison with relevant deep probabilistic models for count data
- Superficial treatment of LSTM as a baseline
- Insufficient discussion of limitations and their causes

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** with major revisions. The paper has significant potential but requires substantial improvements to meet NIPS standards. The authors should:

1. Add theoretical analysis of the model's properties
2. Provide a more thorough comparison with existing deep count models
3. Include detailed implementation specifications for reproducibility
4. Expand the discussion of limitations and their causes
5. Justify the LSTM baseline choice or replace it with a more appropriate deep count model baseline

The paper would benefit from a more critical evaluation of its own limitations and a stronger connection to the theoretical foundations of deep probabilistic models. With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field of temporal count data analysis.

## Final Note
The paper addresses an important problem with a well-constructed model that shows empirical promise. However, as a NIPS submission, it needs to demonstrate not only empirical success but also a solid theoretical foundation and clear distinction from existing deep probabilistic models. The current version falls short in these areas, but with the suggested revisions, it could become a strong contribution to the field.
