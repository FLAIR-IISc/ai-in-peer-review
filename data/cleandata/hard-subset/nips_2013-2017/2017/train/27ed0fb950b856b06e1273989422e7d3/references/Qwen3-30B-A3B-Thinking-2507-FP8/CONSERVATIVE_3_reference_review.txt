

# Review: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), which extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with deep recurrent neural networks to model temporal dependencies in count data. The authors claim their model better captures long-time dependence compared to existing methods like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS), LSTM, and standard PFA. The model introduces a deep hierarchical structure where each layer's latent variables depend on the previous layer, with recurrent neural networks parameterizing the transition distributions.

## Strengths

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough description of the proposed model and inference procedure. The experimental section demonstrates the model's application on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including ICEWS, NIPS corpus, Ebola outbreak data, International Disaster data, and Annual Sheep Population data. The authors provide sufficient details for replication, including hyperparameter settings and experimental protocols.

## Weaknesses

**1. Insufficient Justification for Deep Structure:** The paper claims that the "deep" architecture is necessary to "capture the long-time dependence" but fails to provide adequate theoretical or empirical justification for why depth is required. The model's hierarchical structure (Equations 3-4) appears to be a straightforward extension of existing hierarchical models without demonstrating that the depth actually contributes to improved performance. The authors acknowledge "pruning" issues where some latent dimensions contain only noise, which undermines their claim that the deep structure is useful. The paper cites [25] which argues that "only using the bottom latent layer of hierarchical variational autoencoders should be enough," yet the paper's own results in Table 3 show only marginal improvements with additional layers.

**2. Inadequate Experimental Validation:** The paper compares against three baselines (PGDS, LSTM, and PFA), but the improvements claimed are marginal across most datasets (Table 2). For instance, in the ICEWS dataset, DDPFA's prediction performance is actually worse than PGDS. The paper attributes this to "smoothed data obtained from the transition matrix in PGDS," but doesn't provide evidence that PGDS is actually capturing the relevant temporal patterns better than DDPFA. The synthetic datasets (SDS1-4) show only modest improvements that don't convincingly demonstrate the necessity of the deep structure.

**3. Lack of Theoretical Foundation:** The paper makes strong claims about capturing "complicated temporal dependence" but provides no theoretical analysis to support this claim. The justification for using the deep structure is purely empirical, without demonstrating why the specific architecture proposed is better than simpler alternatives. The paper doesn't address whether the same performance gains could be achieved with a shallower model with more features or better hyperparameter tuning.

**4. Insufficient Comparison to Relevant Work:** The paper cites Deep Poisson Factor Analysis based on RBMs or SBN [9,10] but doesn't directly compare against these or other recent deep models for count data. The paper also doesn't address how its approach differs from Deep Exponential Families [8] or other deep latent variable models for time series.

**5. Unsubstantiated Claims about "Long-Time Dependence":** The paper claims the model captures "long-time dependence" that PGDS cannot, but the experimental results don't provide clear evidence of this. The paper's Figure 2 and 3 show that DDPFA captures more factors at each time step, but this doesn't necessarily demonstrate superior long-term pattern learning. The authors acknowledge that LSTM performs poorly on SDS4 due to "noise in the synthetic data and long time steps," yet they don't explain why their deep model handles this better.

## Critical Assessment

The paper represents a reasonable extension of existing Poisson factorization models with deep learning, but the contribution is incremental rather than transformative. The authors have taken a standard approach (combining PFA with deep learning) and added a deep hierarchical structure without sufficient justification. The marginal performance improvements on the benchmark datasets do not justify the increased complexity of the model.

The paper's main weakness is its failure to demonstrate that the deep structure is actually necessary for improved performance. The authors acknowledge that the deep structure may be "useless" (Table 3) and that their model suffers from "pruning" issues. This undermines the core motivation for the deep architecture. The paper's claims about capturing "long-time dependence" are not well-supported by the evidence presented.

The paper also fails to address a critical issue in deep probabilistic modeling: the potential for overparameterization without clear benefits. The model introduces significant additional complexity (with neural networks parameterizing the transition distributions) without demonstrating that this complexity is justified by improved performance or interpretability.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound in its implementation and provides a well-structured experimental evaluation, the contribution does not meet the bar for NIPS. The novelty is limited, the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims of superiority, and the paper fails to provide adequate justification for the deep structure. The marginal performance improvements over baselines do not constitute a significant advancement in the field of temporal count data modeling. The paper would be more appropriate for a conference with lower standards for novelty, but it does not meet the high bar for NIPS, where the focus is on substantial theoretical or empirical advances.

The authors would need to substantially strengthen their paper by: (1) providing clear evidence that the deep structure is necessary (not just beneficial) for capturing long-term dependencies; (2) comparing against more relevant baselines; (3) addressing the "pruning" issue and demonstrating how the deep structure actually overcomes it; and (4) providing theoretical justification for the architecture choice. Without these improvements, the paper does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS.
