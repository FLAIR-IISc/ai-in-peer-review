Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a count time-series model that factorizes X into Φ, Θ with additional feature and component scales Ψ, Λ, and models temporal dependence via a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n) with windowed recurrent neural networks. Conditionals are specified as Gamma distributions whose shape parameters are outputs of RNNs (forward “prior” and backward “posterior” networks). Inference alternates between (i) mean-field VI updates for Gamma–Poisson components with auxiliary allocations xvtk and (ii) training the RNN parameters using a “Probability AutoEncoder” loss based on squared deviations between expectations of forward/backward conditionals and a reconstruction term to X. Experiments on synthetic data and five real datasets compare DDPFA against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM.

Relation to prior work
The paper aims to extend Poisson factorization with temporal structure beyond Gamma-Markov models (e.g., PGDS) by using deep, windowed RNNs; it is related to Deep Exponential Families, recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation, and ladder/auxiliary inference networks (e.g., Ladder-VAE). The hybrid VI/autoencoder training is reminiscent of wake-sleep or amortized VI, but the loss used departs from standard ELBO-based training.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to combine Poisson factorization with deep recurrent hierarchies to capture long-range dependencies.
- Inclusion of feature-wise and component-wise scales (Ψ, Λ) for sparsity/importance modeling.
- Recognizes limitations of shallow temporal models and discusses learned factor interpretability qualitatively.
- Broad evaluation across multiple datasets and baselines (if adequately presented).

Major weaknesses (technical correctness and clarity)
- Objective inconsistency: The “Probability AutoEncoder” loss (Eqs. 15–18) is not a valid ELBO or tight bound on marginal likelihood; training alternates between MFVI and an ad hoc squared-error objective. There is no guarantee the two-stage procedure optimizes a coherent objective or converges.
- Conjugacy claims are unclear: While Gamma–Poisson can be conjugate if prior parameters are deterministic functions of other latent variables, the paper mixes “prior” and “auxiliary posterior” neural networks and then adds shape parameters (e.g., Eq. 10, 14) without derivation. Many “complete conditionals” conflate prior/posterior terms by simple addition.
- Derivations contain errors/inconsistencies:
  - Eq. 5: q(ψk|ψ∗k) uses index k, but ψ is defined per feature v elsewhere; inconsistent indexing throughout (ψv vs ψk).
  - Eq. 7: multinomial parameter uses θtφvλφv (duplicated φv) and omits ψv in numerator but includes ψv in denominator; dimensions and normalization are inconsistent.
  - Eq. 8: “λk|Θ,Φ,Φ” repeats Φ; βθ appears without prior definition; several rate parameters (βθ, βh) are used interchangeably/inconsistently.
  - Eq. 9: shape uses xv·k with no t index (should depend on t); rate uses βθ though θ prior rate earlier denoted βh; symbols are overloaded.
  - Eq. 10 and Eq. 14: h(n)tk ~ Gamma(αh(n)tk + γh(n)tk, βh) adds an auxiliary “posterior” term to the shape with no probabilistic justification.
  - Eq. 11: update given only for xvt > 0; handling of zeros is unclear despite Poisson support.
- Generative model ambiguity: The top layer h(N) depends only on past; lower layers use forward/backward windows, but the precise conditional distributions (parameterizations and positivity constraints) are not specified. It is unclear how α’s are kept positive (softplus?).
- Identifiability: With Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ all positive scales, the model has severe scaling indeterminacy; no normalization constraints or post-hoc rescaling are described.
- Evaluation issues: Key tables/figures are not included in the submission; metrics are MSE/PMSE rather than held-out predictive log likelihood/perplexity, which are standard for count models; no confidence intervals, multiple seeds, or hyperparameter tuning protocol; fairness vs LSTM unclear; no ablations for Ψ/Λ; no runtime/scalability analysis.
- Theoretical justification is lacking: no derivation of the proposed loss from a bound; no discussion of biased gradients or double-counting information in forward/backward networks; no convergence guarantees.
- Related work and references: Important references are incomplete or misnumbered; e.g., PGDS (Schein et al., 2016), Ladder-VAE (Sønderby et al., 2016) are not consistently cited; many entries in the bibliography are missing venues/years/pages.
- Presentation/formatting: Numerous typos, grammar issues, and formatting glitches (e.g., “st Conference…”, footnotes inline, broken equations, inconsistent notation and symbols). Figures/Tables are referenced but absent.

Minor issues and nits
- Eq. 1 claims Λ and Ψ can be absorbed into Θ, yet they are retained; discuss why and how you address identifiability.
- The Bernoulli–Poisson link is described but not used in experiments; clarify scope.
- Hyperparameters are fixed ad hoc (α=1, β=1/2) without sensitivity analysis.
- Window size c=4 is arbitrary; no analysis of long-term dependence beyond small windows.
- The claim that “differentiable variational Bayes is not suitable” because “few samples are drawn” is unconvincing; reparameterization (e.g., implicit/shape-rate tricks for Gamma) or score-function estimators with control variates are standard.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a precise ELBO and show that your algorithm monotonically improves it? If not, what objective is actually optimized?
2. Please correct the conditional distributions (Eqs. 7–14), define all symbols, and provide a derivation for adding α and γ in the Gamma shapes for h(n).
3. How do you ensure positivity and stability of neural outputs for Gamma parameters? What parameterization is used?
4. How do you address identifiability among Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ?
5. Can you report held-out predictive log likelihood with standard splits, multiple runs, and include the missing tables/figures?

Reproducibility
Insufficient. No code or pseudocode; key implementation details missing; results tables/figures not present; references incomplete.

Overall assessment
While the goal—deep temporal modeling of count matrices—is important and in scope for NeurIPS, the paper in its current form suffers from substantial technical, methodological, and presentation issues. The proposed training objective lacks a principled variational foundation; the derivations contain inconsistencies; experiments are insufficiently documented; and the manuscript has numerous notation and formatting errors. I encourage the authors to (i) formalize the generative model and ELBO, (ii) adopt a coherent amortized VI framework, (iii) fix identifiability and notation, and (iv) present rigorous, reproducible experiments.
