Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA), a model for sequential count data. Like Poisson factorization (PFA), it models xvt ~ Poisson(sumk θtk φvk λk ψv), decomposing counts into K components and introducing Gamma priors for φ, λ, ψ. The innovation is a deep, recurrent hierarchy over time for θ and auxiliary latent variables h(n) (n=0..N), where the conditional distributions p(θt|h(0) t−c:t) and p(h(n) t|h(n+1) t−c:t) are “implicit” Gamma distributions whose parameters are outputs of recurrent neural networks (RNNs). Training alternates between mean-field variational updates for conjugate parts (using latent count allocations xvtk) and a separate “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that fits the RNN parameters by minimizing squared error between expectations of certain conditional distributions (Eqs. 16–18), combined with layer-wise training and fine-tuning.

This sits at the intersection of Poisson factorization for counts (e.g., Beta-NB/PFA, NB processes), dynamic count models (PGDS), deep latent variable models for counts (Deep Exponential Families, Gamma Belief Networks), and RNN-based temporal models (e.g., Recurrent Poisson Factorization). The authors cite several of these but do not clearly position the proposed method w.r.t. dynamic topic models (DTM), neural autoregressive topic models, or recent amortized variational approaches for Gamma/Poisson models.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to capture both short- and long-range temporal dependencies in count data via a deep hierarchical recurrent structure.
- Retains conjugacy where possible, enabling relatively simple mean-field updates for φ, λ, ψ, and θ given the neural parameters.
- Claims of improved MSE/PMSE on a mix of synthetic and real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, disasters, sheep), with interpretability examples (e.g., ICEWS factors).

Weaknesses and detailed assessment

Quality
- The probabilistic training objective is not well founded. The model uses mean-field VI for the conjugate part but learns RNN parameters via a bespoke “Probability AutoEncoder” loss (Eqs. 15–18) that replaces an intractable expectation with squared-error terms between expectations of conditionals. This is not a valid ELBO or likelihood surrogate, and no guarantee is provided that optimizing this loss improves the marginal likelihood or a lower bound. The argument that reparameterized gradient VI is “not suitable” because few samples are drawn is not convincing; standard VAEs use single-sample gradients successfully, and Gamma reparameterization (or implicit reparameterization) is available.
- The overall inference is thus a two-stage, partially decoupled procedure without a coherent global objective. As a result, it is unclear what posterior is being approximated or whether the learned networks are consistent with the generative model.
- Many derivations and notations have errors or ambiguities: Eq. 7’s multinomial parameters, typos in conditionals in Eq. 8, inconsistent use of log and digamma in Eq. 11, and missing details on how RNN outputs are constrained to be positive (e.g., softplus) for Gamma shape/rate parameters. The role of Λ and Ψ is under-justified and introduces scale non-identifiability with Φ and Θ.
- Computational complexity is not analyzed. The latent count allocation xvtk scales as O(VTK) and can be expensive for large counts; there is no discussion of sparse or augmentation tricks that avoid explicit allocations (common in NB/PFA literature).
- Evaluation uses MSE/PMSE, not held-out log-likelihood or perplexity customary for count models and dynamic topics, and lacks uncertainty-aware metrics. Hyperparameter tuning and fairness across baselines (e.g., PGDS, LSTM) are not clearly controlled; no runtime comparisons are provided.

Clarity
- The paper is difficult to follow: numerous typos, missing details, and inconsistent notation (e.g., b = 1(x > 1) for Bernoulli-Poisson link; typically x > 0). The definition of the deep hierarchy and the bidirectional conditionals (using t−c..t and t..t+c) is not crisply specified, and figures referred to are not included here. The “Probability AutoEncoder” is insufficiently formalized.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues) and some key related work is not discussed or distinguished (e.g., Dynamic Topic Models; amortized VI for deep count models; Recurrent Poisson Factorization in recommendation; Poisson RNNs).

Originality
- Using RNNs to parameterize conditionals in a deep Gamma-Poisson hierarchy is interesting, but similar ideas exist: Deep Exponential Families with temporal structure, Gamma Belief Networks, Deep Poisson Factor Models with recurrent components, and Recurrent Poisson Factorization. The main purported novelty is the training scheme, which is unfortunately ad hoc and not theoretically grounded.

Significance
- The problem is important, and improved temporal modeling of counts is valuable. However, given the unclear objective, limited evaluation metrics, and lack of strong baselines beyond PGDS/LSTM/PFA, it is hard to conclude that this advances the state of the art. The interpretability claims are suggestive but anecdotal.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a principled ELBO that includes the neural network parameters via amortized variational inference. Consider q(h,θ|x) parameterized by recognition networks, and optimize the ELBO with reparameterized gradients (Gamma/Lognormal reparameterizations, implicit reparameterization, or augmentation to maintain conjugacy).
- Justify Λ and Ψ mathematically and address identifiability; consider hierarchical sparsity priors or normalization constraints.
- Replace MSE/PMSE with held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity and compare against stronger baselines (DTM; neural autoregressive topic models; deep PF with recurrence). Include ablations: effect of depth N, window c, ladder vs non-ladder, and removal of Λ/Ψ.
- Clarify how positivity of Gamma parameters is ensured; specify RNN architectures, activation functions, optimizer settings, and computational cost.
- Discuss how the bidirectional conditioning (using future in the auxiliary posterior) impacts training and prediction; ensure that prediction at t+1 uses only past information.
- Clean up notation, fix derivational errors, and provide code for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem (temporal counts).
- Interesting attempt to blend deep hierarchical gamma-Poisson structure with RNNs.
- Some empirical gains reported and qualitative factor analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- Training objective for the neural components is ad hoc and not a valid variational bound.
- Derivations/notation errors and lack of theoretical guarantees.
- Limited evaluation metrics and baselines; no likelihood-based assessments or runtime analysis.
- Clarity and referencing issues.

Overall recommendation
I lean toward rejection in the current form due to the lack of a coherent probabilistic training objective and insufficiently rigorous evaluation, despite the interesting modeling direction. With a principled ELBO, stronger baselines, and clearer exposition, this work could become a solid contribution.

Confidence: High.
