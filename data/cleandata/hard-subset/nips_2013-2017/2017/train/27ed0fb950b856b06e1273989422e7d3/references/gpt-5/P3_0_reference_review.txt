Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count data. Observations xvt are modeled via a Poisson likelihood with factor loadings Φ, time-varying factor scores Θ, and global scalars Ψ (feature-wise) and Λ (component-wise). Temporal dynamics are introduced through a multi-layer hierarchy H(n) where θt and h(n)t depend on a window of past latent states via “implicit distributions” parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs). Inference proceeds in two stages: (i) mean-field coordinate-ascent VI for the conjugate Gamma-Poisson parts using auxiliary latent counts; (ii) a “Probability AutoEncoder” (PAE) training of the RNN parameters via a squared-error loss between expectations of conditional distributions, plus a data reconstruction term. Experiments on synthetic and five real datasets compare DDPFA to PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE/PMSE.

Relation to prior work
The model builds on Poisson factorization for counts (e.g., Beta-NB, HPF) and dynamic extensions (PGDS). The deep temporal component is related to Deep Exponential Families, Gamma Belief Networks (GBN), and more broadly to Deep Markov Models/VRNNs with Poisson outputs. The paper also mentions a recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation. The use of auxiliary counts for Poisson-Gamma conjugacy is standard. The novelty lies in replacing parametric Markovian dynamics with RNN-parameterized Gamma “priors” over Θ and H, with an ad hoc training objective.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant problem: modeling long-term temporal dependencies in high-dimensional, sparse count time series.
- Combines interpretable Poisson factorization with flexible neural dynamics.
- Conjugate updates for Φ, Λ, Ψ, and Θ (conditional on “prior” shapes) can be efficient for large matrices.
- Empirical results (as reported) suggest improvements on several datasets, and the qualitative factor trajectories are potentially useful.

Major weaknesses
1) Generative model and inference are not rigorously specified.
- The “implicit” distributions p(θt|h(0)t−c:t) and p(h(n)t|h(n+1)t−c:t) are said to be modeled by RNNs, but then instantiated as Gamma distributions with shape parameters output by RNNs. It is unclear what rates are used, whether they are fixed or learned, and how positivity is enforced.
- Eq. (10) and (14) propose posterior distributions h(n)tk ~ Gamma(αh(n)tk + γh(n)tk, βh), where γ is an “auxiliary posterior signal” from a backward network. Adding prior and posterior “shape” parameters is not a valid Bayesian update. There is no derivation showing how these distributions arise from an ELBO.
- Several indexing issues suggest incorrect conditionals (e.g., in Eq. (8–9) xv·k appears to sum over all times rather than v at time t; β symbols are inconsistently used). These issues cast doubt on the correctness of the coordinate updates.

2) The “Probability AutoEncoder” objective is ad hoc and lacks a theoretical guarantee.
- The objective starts from ∫ p(Θ|H(0))p(H(0)|Θ)dH(0), then moves to minimizing squared distances between expectations of conditional distributions (Eqs. 16–18). This is not an ELBO, nor a known lower bound on the marginal likelihood; no connection to variational EM is provided.
- The stated rationale that “differentiable variational Bayes is not suitable” because sampling is costly is unconvincing; for Gamma variables, reparameterization or BBVI with control variates exists, and amortized variational inference with stochastic gradients is standard.

3) Ambiguity about dynamics and “deep” structure.
- The temporal dependency appears limited to a fixed window c as RNN inputs; it is unclear if true recurrence across the entire sequence (BPTT) is used, or just a feed-forward network over a window.
- The top layer h(N) is said to depend only on past states (no current H(N)t), but the exact transition distribution is not specified.
- The paper itself reports that adding more layers did not improve performance, and that “pruning” occurs. This undermines the central claim that depth is necessary for long-term dependencies.

4) Experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the claims.
- Metrics: using MSE/PMSE for count data is suboptimal. Predictive log-likelihood/Poisson deviance or perplexity (for text) are more appropriate and allow fair comparisons to PGDS/HPF/DTM/GBN/VRNN.
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to deep sequential latent-variable models with Poisson outputs (e.g., Deep Markov Models/VRNN), dynamic topic models, gamma belief networks with temporal structure, and recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation.
- Details: critical hyperparameters, RNN architectures, training stability, runtime, and sensitivity to K and c are not systematically reported. No uncertainty quantification or calibration. Some datasets (e.g., ICEWS) have strong nonstationarities; evaluation should include rolling multi-step forecasts and robust baselines.

5) Claims about Ψ and Λ imposing smoothness are not substantiated; as global scalars, they do not encode temporal smoothing.

Clarity and reproducibility
- The paper is difficult to follow, with many notation inconsistencies, grammatical errors, and missing definitions. A thorough rewrite is needed: define all conditionals precisely, fix indices and rates, specify architectures and optimization details, and provide pseudocode.
- No code or reproducibility plan is provided.

Originality and significance
- The idea of marrying Poisson factorization with neural temporal priors is interesting and could be impactful if made principled and empirically validated. However, similar ideas have been explored (deep exponential families with sequential structure, RPF, DMM/VRNN with Poisson outputs). The current paper does not clearly differentiate itself methodologically, nor does it provide a sound training objective or strong empirical evidence.

Suggestions for improvement
- Derive a principled ELBO for the full model and optimize it with amortized variational inference: q(θt,h(n)t | x1:T) parameterized by inference networks. Use reparameterization for Gamma (or implicit reparameterization/BBVI with control variates) and KL terms that avoid pruning (free bits/KL annealing).
- Clarify the temporal model: either true recurrent transitions (z_t ~ p(·|z_{t−1}) with RNN) or explicit windowed dependencies, and justify the choice. Consider DMM/VRNN formulations as baselines/alternatives.
- Use appropriate evaluation metrics (held-out predictive log-likelihood, Poisson deviance, perplexity) and stronger baselines (DTM, HPF/GBN, RPF, DMM/VRNN). Include multi-step forecasts and ablations over K, c, depth, and the ladder component. Report training time and memory.
- Provide interpretability analyses (top words per factor over time for NIPS), and uncertainty quantification.
- Fix notation, provide complete conditionals with correct indices, and ensure the conjugate updates are correct.

Pros
- Tackles an important, practical problem (dynamic count modeling).
- Conceptually appealing combination of PF and neural dynamics.
- Some promising qualitative and quantitative results.

Cons
- Inference objective is not theoretically justified; updates appear ad hoc.
- Multiple technical and notation errors in the model and updates.
- Experimental evaluation and baselines are insufficient; metrics are suboptimal.
- Depth does not demonstrably help; pruning indicates inference issues.
- Clarity and reproducibility are poor.

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: High
