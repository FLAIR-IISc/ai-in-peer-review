This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA) model, aiming to capture both short- and long-term dependencies in sequentially observed count data. The model extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to model the temporal evolution of latent factors. Variational inference is employed for estimation, with a focus on learning the parameters within the neural networks. The authors evaluate DDPFA on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing its performance against PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a relevant problem in sequential count data analysis. The idea of combining PFA with deep learning to capture complex temporal dependencies is natural and potentially powerful. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing approaches (e.g., the independence assumption in PGDS and the difficulty of LSTMs with long sequences and high dimensionality) and position DDPFA as a solution. The presentation of the model is generally clear, with a reasonable mathematical formulation. The experimental setup is thorough, including both synthetic and real-world data, and comparisons to several baselines. The inclusion of visualizations (Figures 2 & 3) is helpful for understanding the learned factor representations.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. Firstly, while the core idea is promising, the experimental results are not consistently convincing. While DDPFA often outperforms the baselines, there are instances (e.g., ICEWS prediction) where it underperforms. The authors acknowledge this but don’t delve deeply into the reasons why, or attempt to diagnose the failure modes. The relatively small performance gains in several experiments question the practical significance of the proposed approach.

Secondly, the inference procedure, while described, is quite complex and relies heavily on mean-field variational inference.  The authors acknowledge the potential issues with MF-VI (“pruning” and inability to leverage the hierarchical structure) and reference relevant work ([24, 25]), but these are presented as future work rather than adequately addressed within the current paper. The loss function used for training the neural networks (Eq. 18) is complex and may be difficult to optimize effectively. A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter choices would be valuable.

Thirdly, the clarity suffers from some inconsistencies and redundancies. The writing style is sometimes verbose and repetitive. For instance, the same points are reiterated multiple times in the introduction.  There are also instances of unclear phrasing and notation. Equation (11) refers to Ψ and a 'shp' and 'rte' superscript, which is only explained later. The transition between the mathematical derivations and the explanation of the neural network architecture could be smoother.

Fourthly, the related work section, while extensive, feels somewhat disjointed. The connections between the different referenced models and the proposed DDPFA aren't always clearly articulated. For example, the discussion of Deep Exponential Families [8] feels tangential.  More targeted references to recent advances in variational autoencoders and their application to time series data would be beneficial.

Finally, the paper lacks a discussion of computational complexity and scalability. Training deep neural networks with variational inference can be computationally expensive. The authors should provide some insights into the computational resources required for their experiments and discuss the limitations of the model in terms of handling large-scale datasets.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of PFA and deep learning for sequential count data.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation on diverse datasets.
*   Clear mathematical formulation of the model.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Inconsistent experimental results and modest performance gains.
*   Reliance on potentially problematic mean-field variational inference.
*   Lack of discussion regarding computational complexity and scalability.
*   Some clarity issues in the writing and mathematical presentation.
*   The identified problems of "pruning" and the hierarchical structure are acknowledged but not addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **reject**. While the idea is interesting and the presentation is generally clear, the inconsistent experimental results, the reliance on potentially problematic inference methods, and the lack of discussion regarding computational concerns prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The paper shows promise, but requires significant revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should investigate the reasons for the inconsistent performance, explore alternative inference methods, provide a more detailed analysis of computational complexity, and improve the clarity of the presentation. Addressing the "pruning" problem and better leveraging the hierarchical structure of the model would also significantly strengthen the paper.
