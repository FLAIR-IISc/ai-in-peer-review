Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for sequential count vectors. Building on Poisson factorization xvt ~ Poisson(sum_k θtk φvk λk ψv), the authors introduce a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t that aims to capture short- and long-range temporal dependencies. The conditional distributions p(θt | h(0)t−c:t) and p(h(n)t | h(n+1)t−c:t) are modeled as recurrent neural networks (RNNs), with Gamma distributions whose shape parameters are outputs of the networks. Inference is split into two stages: (i) mean-field variational inference (MFVI) with auxiliary latent counts to exploit Gamma–Poisson conjugacy for Φ, Λ, Ψ, Θ (treating the network outputs as fixed priors), and (ii) learning the network parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that uses squared-error losses between expectations from encoder/decoder networks rather than a standard ELBO. Experiments on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population) report better MSE/PMSE than PFA, LSTM, and PGDS in most cases; some qualitative analyses of temporal factors are provided. The authors also discuss practical issues like “pruning” of latent units and the limited benefits of increased depth, and mention a ladder-like architecture variant.

Relation to prior work
DDPFA is positioned relative to dynamic PFA models with Gamma chain priors (e.g., PGDS), deep latent variable models (DEFs, deep PFA, gamma belief networks), and RNN-based models (NADE, LSTM). There is relevant closely related work on recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation, deep Poisson factor modeling with recurrent structures, hierarchical Poisson/gamma factorization (HPF/GBN), and neural point-process models for counts/intensities. The paper cites several but the bibliography is incomplete and some key technical distinctions are not clearly articulated.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to combine Poisson factorization with deep/recurrent priors to capture long-range temporal dependence in count data.
- Practical MFVI updates leveraging conjugacy for Φ, Λ, Ψ, Θ are standard and useful; the multinomial allocation for xvtk is appropriate.
- Empirical results suggest improvements over PFA/PGDS/LSTM on multiple datasets, with qualitative analyses of learned temporal factors.
- The paper is honest about limitations (e.g., pruning, depth not always helping) and attempts architectural remedies (ladder variant).

Weaknesses
- Objective/inference: The proposed “Probability AutoEncoder” uses ad hoc squared-error losses between expectations and does not optimize a principled lower bound on the marginal log-likelihood. The two-stage procedure (MFVI with fixed networks, then network training with an auxiliary loss) is not an EM or VI M-step and lacks guarantees (no monotonic ELBO improvement). This undermines the theoretical soundness of the approach.
- Derivational gaps: Updates for h(n)tk as Gamma(αh(n)tk + γh(n)tk, βh) seem heuristic; α and γ are outputs of forward/backward networks, but no derivation shows that this is a valid posterior under a coherent generative model. Similarly, using network outputs as “prior shapes” makes the model look conditionally conjugate, but the forward/backward coupling is not derived from Bayes rule.
- Clarity and rigor: Many notational inconsistencies and typos; several equations are ambiguous (e.g., Eq. 7 has φv twice; Eq. 8 repeats Φ in conditioning; indices/summations unclear). The full joint model and ELBO are never written explicitly. Details of the RNNs (architecture, activation ensuring positive shape/rate, hidden sizes, regularization) are missing. The “ladder-like” variant is mentioned but not precisely specified.
- Evaluation methodology: Using MSE/PMSE on count data is nonstandard. Predictive log-likelihood or Poisson deviance would be more appropriate, as would held-out predictive likelihood and calibration. Statistical significance of improvements is not reported. Baselines are limited; missing comparisons include hierarchical Poisson factorization (HPF), deep gamma belief networks for sequences, Poisson autoregressive (INGARCH) baselines, and neural count models with Poisson/NB outputs.
- Identifiability and scaling: Introducing both λk and ψv exacerbates scale indeterminacy between Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ. Without normalization (e.g., Dirichlet over columns of Φ or constraints on Λ/Ψ), interpretability and stability may suffer.
- References: Many entries are incomplete (missing venues/years), and some relevant recent work on reparameterized Gamma/NB variational families and amortized VI for count models is not cited.

Quality
The empirical results are promising but the learning objective is not principled and the inference derivations for the deep hierarchy are incomplete. Lack of a coherent ELBO (or other justified objective) and missing theoretical guarantees are major concerns.

Clarity
The paper is hard to follow due to notation/typos and missing definitions. Key algorithmic details (architectures, hyperparameters beyond a few scalar priors, optimization schedules, runtime/memory) are absent. Pseudocode and a precise statement of the joint model and the optimized objective would help.

Originality
The combination of PFA with deep recurrent priors is a reasonable direction, but related ideas exist (deep PFA, recurrent PF for recommendations, gamma belief networks). The main novelty—the Probability AutoEncoder objective—appears to be an ad hoc training heuristic rather than a new, principled method.

Significance
Potentially useful if made rigorous, because modeling long-range temporal dependencies in high-dimensional count sequences is important. As it stands, the impact is limited by the lack of a principled inference objective and limited evaluation.

Questions and requests for clarification
1) Please write the full joint distribution and the ELBO that you intend to maximize. If not optimizing an ELBO, justify the training objective and discuss convergence properties.
2) Derive the posterior forms for h(n)tk, or clearly state the modeling assumption under which h(n)tk ~ Gamma(α+γ, βh) is valid.
3) Specify RNN architectures (GRU/LSTM/vanilla), hidden sizes, activations, and how you enforce positivity of Gamma parameters. How are α, β constrained?
4) How do you prevent scale indeterminacy among Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ? Do you normalize columns of Φ or rescale Θ periodically?
5) Provide predictive log-likelihood results (or Poisson deviance) and calibration of predictive intervals. Include additional baselines (HPF/GBN, INGARCH, neural count models).
6) Describe sensitivity to c (window size), K, depth, and provide runtime comparisons to PGDS and LSTM. Report standard deviations over multiple runs.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem (dynamic high-dimensional counts).
- Empirically promising on several datasets.
- Attempts to integrate deep/recurrent structure with PF and discusses practical pitfalls.

Cons for acceptance
- Lacks a principled, well-defined training objective and theoretical justification.
- Derivations for deep latent updates are heuristic.
- Clarity issues and incomplete experimental evaluation/baselines.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper needs a principled objective (ELBO or equivalent), clearer derivations, stronger baselines, and improved clarity. I encourage the authors to (i) formulate and optimize a proper ELBO with amortized inference (reparameterized Gamma/NB or implicit VI with control variates), (ii) normalize to address identifiability, (iii) evaluate with log-likelihood metrics and broader baselines, and (iv) substantially improve exposition and references.
