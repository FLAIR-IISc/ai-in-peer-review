- Introduces Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), combining Poisson factorization with RNNs to model temporal count data.  
- Hierarchical latent structure uses RNNs across layers to capture short- and long-term temporal patterns.  
- Uses variational inference to learn parameters and reports improved performance over PGDS, LSTM, and PFA on synthetic and real datasets.  
- “Deep” aspect is limited (only two layers), questioning the strength of the deep claim.  
- Technical formulations and variational inference are sound and clearly presented.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive across multiple synthetic and real-world datasets.  
- Lacks theoretical analysis justifying why the deep structure improves long-term dependency modeling.  
- Mean-field variational approximation may be too strong for this complex model and could limit inference quality.  
- Paper acknowledges pruning and inference limitations but does not deeply analyze their impact.  
- Writing and organization are clear, but implementation details (RNN architecture, hyperparameter tuning) are insufficient for reproducibility.  
- Does not sufficiently explain mechanistically how the deep architecture enhances long-term dependency capture versus simpler models.  
- Novel combination of deep RNNs with Poisson factorization, but novelty is limited by shallow depth and unclear distinction from related deep Poisson models.  
- Fails to compare against some recent deep probabilistic models (e.g., Deep Markov Models, Deep Factor Analysis).  
- Addresses an important problem area (sequential count data) with practical applications in recommendations, text, and epidemiology.  
- Performance gains are dataset-dependent and not uniformly large; DDPFA underperforms PGDS on ICEWS in one task.  
- Strengths: comprehensive experiments, clear motivation, relevant baselines, acknowledgment of limitations, and interpretability demonstration.  
- Weaknesses: limited theoretical justification, shallow “deep” model, insufficient comparisons to newer models, strong mean-field assumption, and limited discussion of failure cases.  
- Recommendation: weak accept — solid contribution but needs stronger theory, broader comparisons, and more implementation detail.  
- Suggested improvements: provide theoretical analysis of depth benefits, compare with recent deep probabilistic models, detail RNN/training choices, and study depth vs. performance.
