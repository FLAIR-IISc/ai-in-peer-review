## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, a novel approach to modeling sequentially observed count data. Building upon Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA), the authors integrate recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to capture complex temporal dependencies between factors, and a deep structure to model long-term relationships. The model is trained using variational inference. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of DDPFA on both synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, Ebola, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population), comparing its performance against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on dynamic count matrix factorization. It acknowledges the success of PFA and its extensions like PGDS, highlighting their limitations in capturing long-range temporal dependencies. The authors also place their work within the broader context of deep Bayesian models, referencing relevant works like Deep Gaussian Processes, Deep Exponential Families, and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis.  The connection to temporal Dirichlet processes is also appropriately noted as a related alternative approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of PFA with deep RNNs to model both short and long-term temporal dependencies is a genuinely novel contribution. This addresses a clear limitation of previous dynamic factor models.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The model is built upon a solid probabilistic foundation with clear derivations of the variational inference procedure.  The inclusion of feature-wise and temporal smoothness constraints via the Ψ and Λ parameters is a sensible addition.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a comprehensive experimental evaluation using both synthetic and real-world datasets. Comparing against relevant baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) strengthens the findings.
* **Interpretability Insights:** The visualizations (Figure 2 & 3) offer some initial insights into the learned factors and their temporal dynamics, adding to the interpretability of the model.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, clearly explaining the model and its inference procedure.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Variational Inference Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the potential limitations of mean-field variational inference (MF-VI), particularly the "pruning" problem where some latent variables become inactive. They correctly point to the potential benefits of alternative inference methods like MCMC.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis. While parameters are stated, the justification for these choices isn't thoroughly addressed.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While comparisons with baselines are provided, a more detailed ablation study exploring the individual contributions of the RNN depth and the inclusion of Ψ and Λ would be beneficial.  For example, what is the performance gain from adding the second layer of the RNN?
* **ICEWS Prediction Performance:** The authors themselves note the suboptimal performance of DDPFA in the ICEWS prediction task. A deeper investigation into why PGDS outperforms DDPFA in this specific scenario would be valuable.
* **Scalability concerns:** While the paper shows results on several datasets, the computational cost of training deep models with variational inference could be a concern for very large-scale datasets. This is not discussed.
* **Lack of Standardized Metrics:** The use of both MSE and PMSE is confusing. It’s unclear why both are reported and which is more important. Standardizing on a single metric would improve clarity.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound model for dynamic count matrix factorization.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of DDPFA on a variety of datasets.
* The work addresses an important problem in sequential data analysis and has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art.
* The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and suggest potential future directions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on MF-VI and its known limitations raises concerns about the accuracy of the inference procedure.
* The lack of detailed hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies weakens the empirical validation.
* The ICEWS prediction results suggest that the model may not generalize well to all types of sequential data.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of dynamic count matrix factorization. While the limitations of the variational inference procedure and the lack of a more thorough ablation study are concerning, the novelty and empirical performance of DDPFA are compelling.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions.  The authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion of hyperparameter tuning and provide justification for the chosen values.
2.  Conduct a more detailed ablation study to assess the individual contributions of the model components (RNN depth, Ψ, Λ).
3.  Investigate the reasons for the suboptimal performance on the ICEWS prediction task.
4. Briefly address scalability considerations.
5. Standardize on a single performance metric.
