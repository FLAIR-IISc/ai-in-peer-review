Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA) for sequential count data. It augments Poisson factorization with two main ingredients: (i) a deep hierarchy of Gamma-distributed latent states h(n)t that feed into time-varying factor scores θt, and (ii) recurrent neural networks that parameterize implicit conditional distributions p(h(n)t | past/future context) and p(θt | past context). Training alternates between mean-field variational updates for conjugate parts (using auxiliary latent counts to preserve Gamma–Poisson conjugacy) and a “probability autoencoder” stage that trains the RNNs via a reconstruction-style loss operating on distributional expectations rather than a standard ELBO. The model also introduces feature-wise (ψv) and component-wise (λk) Gamma variables to control sparsity and scaling.

This sits at the intersection of Poisson factorization for counts (e.g., beta/negative-binomial process PF, hierarchical PF, PGDS), deep latent variable models (deep exponential families, gamma belief networks), and recurrent/deep structured inference (e.g., deep Kalman filters, VRNN). Closest prior art includes PGDS (explicit linear transitions over factors), recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation, deep PF variants (RBM/SBN-based), and dynamic topic models. The novel aspects here are (a) a multilayer ladder-like temporal hierarchy on top of PF; (b) bidirectional RNNs to specify shape parameters of Gamma conditionals across time and depth; and (c) the nonstandard “probability autoencoder” training that couples an RNN prior and an auxiliary RNN “posterior.”

Strengths
- Novel direction: Integrates conjugate Gamma–Poisson machinery with deep/RNN parameterizations in a hierarchical time model. This is a bold and interesting architectural idea that could open new lines of work on deep dynamic count modeling.
- Interpretable latent structure: Maintains PF interpretability (Φ, Θ) while augmenting with ψ and λ for sparsity/importance, potentially useful in text, events, and recommendations.
- Practical inference scaffold: Uses auxiliary Poisson counts to keep conditional conjugacy, enabling simple mean-field updates for many variables despite nonconjugate RNN priors.
- Empirical promise: On several synthetic and real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disasters, Sheep), DDPFA often improves MSE/PMSE over PFA/LSTM/PGDS; qualitative factor analyses are sensible. The discussion of “pruning” and depth utility is candid and informative, and the ladder fix is interesting.

Weaknesses
- Objective not well-grounded: The “probability autoencoder” loss (Eqs. 15–18) is ad-hoc and not a provable ELBO or likelihood surrogate. It operates on expectations and L2 distances between expected sufficient statistics, without guarantees of bounding the log evidence or convergence properties. This is the most serious concern about soundness.
- Clarity and notation: Several equations contain typos/inconsistencies (e.g., duplication of φv in Eq. 7, missing definitions, inconsistent shp/rte parameters, ambiguous dependence in Eqs. 3–4, 9–14). The description of h(N) dynamics (uses only past) versus lower layers (use past and future) is unclear. It is difficult to reproduce as written.
- Baselines and positioning: Missing comparisons to important related models, e.g., dynamic topic models (Blei & Lafferty, 2006), hierarchical Poisson factorization (HPF) as a strong static baseline, recurrent Poisson factorization variants beyond PGDS, and sequence VAEs with Poisson/negative-binomial likelihoods (VRNN, SRNN, DKF-like). It is hard to assess progress vs state of the art with only PGDS/LSTM/PFA.
- Theoretical and practical details: No analysis of the effective temporal receptive field (window c vs depth N). Lack of guarantees/constraints for positive Gamma shapes from RNNs (e.g., softplus). Training schedule and complexity not fully specified (e.g., wall-clock, memory, GPU). The alternation between coordinate ascent and RNN training is plausible but lacks justification regarding monotonic improvement of a common objective.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameter selection and preprocessing choices are under-specified; code is not mentioned. Some datasets (ICEWS) are challenging; reporting more implementation details would help.

Quality
Moderate. The core modeling idea is compelling, but the inference objective is not rigorously derived, and clarity issues hinder verification. Experiments are encouraging but not exhaustive.

Clarity
Below average. The manuscript needs careful editing for notation, missing definitions, and figure/table references. A pseudocode algorithm and an explicit statement of the full generative model and training objective would help significantly.

Originality
Good. The hierarchical RNN-parameterized Gamma priors coupled with PF and the probability autoencoder training constitute a novel combination and direction beyond PGDS and standard deep PF.

Significance
Potentially meaningful. If stabilized and better grounded, the approach could influence dynamic count modeling across text/events/recs. Current evaluation suggests promise but falls short of conclusive state-of-the-art claims.

Questions for the authors
- Can you formalize the objective optimized by the alternation (mean-field VI + probability autoencoder)? Is there an ELBO (even a surrogate) that is provably increased?
- How are positivity constraints enforced on Gamma shape parameters output by RNNs?
- Please provide architectural details (hidden sizes, activations), optimization settings, and wall-clock metrics.
- How sensitive are results to K, c, N? Can you quantify the effective temporal receptive field?
- Can you add baselines: DTM, HPF, VRNN/DKF-like with Poisson/NB emission, and recent recurrent PF variants?
- Please release code or include sufficient details for reproduction.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold and original integration of PF, deep hierarchy, and RNNs for temporal dependence.
- Empirical gains on several datasets; interpretable factors.
- Addresses an important, widely applicable problem (sequential counts).

Cons for acceptance
- Objective/inference lacks rigorous grounding; ad-hoc loss functions.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; several notational errors.
- Limited baseline coverage; mixed performance on ICEWS prediction.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite methodological imperfections and clarity issues, the paper proposes a fresh and potentially influential direction for dynamic count modeling that aligns with NeurIPS’s interest in bold, novel ideas. Strengthening the objective, tightening the write-up, and expanding baselines would considerably improve the work.

Confidence
Medium.
