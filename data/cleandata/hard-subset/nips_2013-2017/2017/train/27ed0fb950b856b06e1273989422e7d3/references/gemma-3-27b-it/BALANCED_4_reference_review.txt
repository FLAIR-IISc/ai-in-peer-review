## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper presents a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) designed to capture both short- and long-term dependencies in sequentially observed count data. The core idea is to integrate recurrent neural networks (RNNs) into the standard Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) framework to model complex temporal relationships between latent factors, and to stack these RNNs to capture long-range dependencies. The authors employ variational inference for model training and demonstrate the effectiveness of DDPFA on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing it to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a line of work exploring Bayesian factor analysis for sequential count data, specifically PFA and its dynamic extensions like PGDS. The motivation for this work is clear: existing dynamic models often struggle to capture complex, long-term dependencies. The authors correctly identify the limitations of Gamma-based transition models (e.g., [1, 2]) and temporal Dirichlet processes [3] in representing intricate temporal patterns.  The incorporation of deep learning techniques, specifically RNNs, represents a natural progression, aligning with the broader trend of leveraging deep hierarchical models in Bayesian learning, as evidenced by references to nested Chinese Restaurant Processes [4], deep Gaussian processes [6, 7], and deep Poisson factor models [9, 10]. The comparison to Neural Autoregressive Density Estimators [11] and Deep Poisson Factor Modeling with RNNs [12] contextualizes the contribution effectively, highlighting how DDPFA distinguishes itself.  The paper also references recent work combining variational inference and deep learning [23], which is important given the chosen inference method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of PFA with deep RNNs to capture both short- and long-term dependencies in count data is a conceptually novel approach. The stacking of RNN layers and the ladder architecture (discussed toward the end of the paper) address potential shortcomings of single-layer deep models, showing awareness of recent developments.
* **Technical Soundness:** The model formulation appears mathematically sound and well-motivated. The derivation of the variational inference procedure, while detailed, seems logically consistent with standard VI techniques. The use of Gamma distributions as priors is justified.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The paper presents a relatively extensive experimental evaluation, including both synthetic datasets designed to test specific properties of the model and real-world datasets from diverse domains (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, etc.). Comparing against several baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) strengthens the empirical analysis.
* **Interpretability Discussion:** The discussion of factor interpretation, with reference to Figures 2 and 3, adds value by attempting to connect the latent factors to meaningful phenomena in the ICEWS dataset.  The observation about non-localized factor activation is insightful.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge and discuss potential issues like the "pruning" problem and the limitations of MF-VI, referencing related work [24, 25] which demonstrates a good understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Notation:** The notation is sometimes dense and could benefit from more careful explanation. For example, the subscripts for different variables (e.g., φvk, θtk) could be more consistently applied throughout the paper.
* **Experimental Detail:** While the experiments are comprehensive, the description of hyperparameter tuning and model selection is relatively brief. It's not entirely clear how the hyperparameters were chosen, and a more detailed ablation study would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Limited Justification for Architectural Choices:** The choice of RNN architecture (e.g., the specific type of RNN unit) and the number of layers isn’t thoroughly justified. Why were RNNs chosen over other recurrent architectures like LSTMs directly within the factor model, instead of only as a baseline?
* **Performance on ICEWS:** The reported performance on the ICEWS dataset is somewhat underwhelming, with PGDS actually performing better in the prediction task. The authors acknowledge this but don’t offer a deep explanation or explore potential reasons for the discrepancy.
* **Inference Scalability:** The paper doesn't address the computational cost of the proposed inference procedure, particularly for high-dimensional datasets. Variational inference can still be expensive, and the added complexity of the deep structure might exacerbate this issue. Scalability considerations are crucial for practical applications.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The model presents a novel and technically sound approach to modeling sequential count data with complex temporal dependencies.
* The experimental results are promising, demonstrating the effectiveness of DDPFA on a variety of datasets.
* The authors demonstrate awareness of related work and address potential limitations of the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The clarity of the presentation could be improved through more careful notation and a more detailed discussion of experimental setup.
* The performance on the ICEWS dataset is a concern, and requires further investigation.
* The computational scalability of the proposed inference procedure is not addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of dynamic count data modeling. The proposed DDPFA model is innovative and well-motivated, and the experimental results are encouraging.  However, the paper would benefit from improvements in clarity, a more thorough exploration of experimental details and limitations, and a discussion of computational scalability.  

**Recommendation:**

**Reject with Strong Encouragement to Resubmit.** I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form, but with a strong encouragement to resubmit after addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The core ideas are valuable, and with some revisions, this paper could be a significant contribution to NIPS.  Specifically, the authors should focus on clarifying the presentation, expanding the experimental analysis (including hyperparameter sensitivity), and addressing the computational scalability concerns. Addressing the ICEWS performance would also be beneficial.
