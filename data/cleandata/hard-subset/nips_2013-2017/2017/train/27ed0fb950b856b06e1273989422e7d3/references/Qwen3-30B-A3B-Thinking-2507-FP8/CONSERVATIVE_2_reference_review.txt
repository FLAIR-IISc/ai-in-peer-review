

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequentially observed count data, extending existing Poisson factorization approaches with a deep recurrent neural network structure. The authors claim their model better captures long-time dependence compared to previous methods like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS), PFA, and LSTM.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon established Poisson factorization models (PFA) and recent deep learning approaches for time series analysis. It introduces a hierarchical structure with multiple layers of latent variables, where each layer is modeled using recurrent neural networks to capture temporal dependencies. The authors claim their model outperforms existing methods on synthetic and real-world datasets, particularly in capturing long-term dependencies. The work relates to prior NIPS contributions like "Deep Exponential Families" (Ranganath et al., 2015) and "Scalable Deep Poisson Factor Analysis" (Gan et al., 2015), though it doesn't clearly differentiate its contribution from these prior works.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a coherent mathematical formulation of the proposed model. The experimental design is reasonable, with both synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, etc.) used to evaluate performance. The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach, such as the "pruning" problem and the "uselessness of adding network layers," which demonstrates some self-critique.

**Weaknesses**

Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from significant methodological and experimental shortcomings:

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The authors claim their deep structure "captures long-time dependence" but provide no theoretical evidence to support this. The paper states previous models "may still have shortcomings in exploring the long-time dependence," yet fails to demonstrate that this is a genuine limitation of existing methods or that their approach fundamentally solves it. The paper's own results (Table 3) show that adding more layers doesn't improve performance, undermining the core claim.

2. **Insufficient Experimental Validation**: The paper compares with only three baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) on a limited set of datasets (4 synthetic, 5 real-world). Crucially, the paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for performance improvements, making it impossible to assess whether the observed gains are meaningful. The real-world datasets are small in number and size, particularly with the ICEWS dataset where DDPFA's performance is "not good enough" compared to PGDS.

3. **Questionable Novelty**: The paper's contribution appears to be a straightforward combination of existing techniques rather than a significant methodological advance. The authors cite prior work on "Deep Poisson Factor Modeling with a recurrent structure" (Ref 12) but don't clearly distinguish their approach from this. The paper's claim that the deep structure is necessary to capture "long-time dependence" is contradicted by their own results showing that adding more layers doesn't improve performance.

4. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The comparison with LSTM is particularly weak. The paper states LSTM "performs worse in SDS4 because the noise in the synthetic data and the long time steps make the neural network difficult to memorize enough information," but doesn't explain why their deep structure would be better suited for this task. The authors don't compare with more recent time series deep learning methods that might be better suited for this problem.

5. **Misleading Claims**: The paper claims DDPFA has "better effectivity and interpretability" but provides no evidence for this beyond marginal performance improvements on some datasets. The paper's own Figure 2 shows that their model's factors are "not activated locally compared to PGDS," which contradicts the claim of improved interpretability.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents a marginal extension of existing methods without sufficient evidence of its necessity or superiority. The claimed benefits of the deep structure are not supported by the experimental results, which actually demonstrate that adding layers doesn't improve performance. The paper's theoretical justification is weak, and the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claimed advances.

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's claims unsubstantiated. The authors haven't demonstrated that their deep structure is genuinely needed to capture long-term dependencies in count data—this is particularly concerning given their own results showing that adding layers doesn't improve performance. The paper lacks the rigorous validation required for NIPS, especially given the claim of capturing "long-time dependence" as a key innovation.

I recommend rejection on the grounds of insufficient evidence for the claimed novelty and improvements, and failure to demonstrate that the deep structure is necessary for the problem at hand. The paper would benefit from significantly stronger theoretical justification, more comprehensive experimental validation with statistical significance tests, and a clearer differentiation from prior work on deep Poisson factorization.
