### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, a novel approach for modeling high-dimensional, sequential count data. The core idea is to extend the classical Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) framework by incorporating a deep, hierarchical structure to capture complex temporal dependencies. Unlike previous dynamic PFA models, such as the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), which assume simple Markovian transitions between time steps, DDPFA uses a hierarchy of latent variables where the temporal priors are parameterized by Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). This allows the model to learn more flexible and long-range patterns in the evolution of the latent factors. Inference is performed using a two-stage hybrid approach: mean-field variational inference is used to update the latent variables of the PFA model, while the parameters of the RNNs are trained separately using a loss function inspired by variational autoencoders. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on several synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improvements in fitting and prediction tasks over baselines like PFA, PGDS, and LSTM.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution of the paper—integrating a deep, RNN-based temporal prior into a Poisson factorization model—is novel and significant. It addresses a key limitation of existing dynamic count models that rely on simple, conjugate Markovian priors (e.g., Gamma-Gamma transitions in PGDS), which may be too restrictive to capture the complex dynamics present in real-world data. This combination of a well-understood probabilistic model (PFA) with the expressive power of deep learning is a promising research direction.

2.  **Model Expressiveness:** By using RNNs to define implicit distributions for the temporal evolution of factors, the model has the potential to capture a much richer class of temporal dependencies than its predecessors. This is a clear conceptual advantage over models that are limited to first-order Markov assumptions.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation on a good variety of datasets, including four synthetic and five real-world corpora. The comparison against relevant baselines (a static model PFA, a dynamic Bayesian model PGDS, and a standard deep learning model LSTM) is appropriate and helps to position the proposed model's contributions. The results generally demonstrate the superior performance of DDPFA, particularly in fitting the data.

4.  **Honest Discussion of Limitations:** The authors are transparent about some of the model's limitations, such as the diminishing returns of adding more layers and the "pruning" phenomenon where latent variables become inactive (Section 4.2). This self-critical analysis is commendable and strengthens the paper.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Technical Soundness of the Inference Procedure:** The inference section is the most significant weakness of the paper. The proposed two-stage procedure lacks clarity and rigorous justification.
    *   **Incoherent Objective:** The paper seems to lack a single, coherent objective function that the entire learning procedure optimizes. Mean-field VI is derived from minimizing a KL divergence (or maximizing the ELBO), but the "Probability AutoEncoder" stage optimizes a heuristic L2 loss on the expectations of latent variables (Eq. 16, 17, 18). It is not shown how this L2 loss relates to the ELBO of the overall generative model. This makes the inference scheme appear ad-hoc rather than principled.
    *   **Vague Justification:** The connection to Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) is mentioned, but the proposed loss function is not the standard VAE objective (ELBO), which consists of a reconstruction term and a KL regularization term. The motivation for using an L2 loss on expectations instead of a more standard objective is not explained. The statement that "differentiable variational Bayes is not suitable" is a strong claim that requires much more substantiation.
    *   **Lack of Detail:** The description of the inference algorithm is insufficient for reproducibility. It is unclear how the two stages (mean-field updates and neural network training) are interleaved. For example, how many coordinate-ascent iterations are performed for every SGD step on the network parameters? The architecture of the RNNs (`ffeed`, `fback`) is also not specified.

2.  **Writing and Presentation:** The paper would benefit from significant copy-editing for clarity and grammatical correctness. Several sections, particularly Section 3 on Inference, are difficult to follow due to imprecise language and notation. For instance, the role of `H(0)'` in Eq. 16 is not clearly defined, and the relationship between the "prior" network `ffeed` and the "posterior" network `fback` is confusingly described.

3.  **Experimental Baselines:** The details of the LSTM baseline are sparse. To ensure a fair comparison, it is crucial to know the exact input/output structure and the loss function used for the LSTM (e.g., was it trained to predict the count vectors directly using a Poisson negative log-likelihood loss or a mean squared error loss?). The claim that LSTM struggles with long time steps in SDS4 is counter-intuitive, as this is precisely what LSTMs are designed for, suggesting the baseline might not have been optimally configured.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Formalize the Inference:** The authors should ground their inference procedure in a more principled framework. The most natural way to do this would be to formulate a single objective function—the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) for the entire DDPFA model. The inference networks (`ffeed` and `fback`) can be framed as components of an amortized variational inference scheme. The `fback` network would define the recognition model (variational posterior) `q(h|...)`, and the `ffeed` network would define the generative model's prior `p(h|...)`. The training objective would then become the standard ELBO, which can be optimized jointly with respect to both the variational parameters and the neural network weights, likely using reparameterization gradients. This would unify the two stages of inference and place the work on a much stronger theoretical footing.

2.  **Improve Clarity and Notation:** A thorough revision of the paper is needed to improve clarity.
    *   In Section 3, explicitly define the full joint distribution `p(X, Z)` and the variational distribution `q(Z)`, where `Z` includes all latent variables (Θ, Φ, etc., and H).
    *   Clearly define the architectures of the RNNs used.
    *   Provide a concrete algorithm block that details the step-by-step training procedure, showing how the updates for the different parameters are coordinated.

3.  **Strengthen the Experiments:**
    *   Provide full details for the LSTM baseline.
    *   Given the authors' finding that deeper layers provide little benefit, it would be interesting to analyze this further. Is this an optimization issue? Could alternative architectures like residual connections between layers help? Exploring this would add significant value to the paper.
    *   Consider adding a more recent and powerful baseline for dynamic topic modeling or sequential count data to further demonstrate the significance of the results.

### **Decision**

This paper presents an interesting and promising idea for modeling dynamic count data. The approach of using RNNs to create expressive temporal priors for Poisson factorization is novel and well-motivated. However, the paper is significantly undermined by a poorly explained and seemingly unprincipled inference algorithm. The lack of a clear, unified objective function makes it difficult to assess the technical soundness of the work. While the empirical results are encouraging, they cannot fully compensate for these fundamental methodological issues.

Therefore, I recommend **rejection** for the paper in its current state. However, I believe the core idea has strong potential. If the authors can reformulate their inference procedure within a principled framework like amortized variational inference (optimizing a single ELBO) and substantially revise the paper for clarity, it could become a strong contribution to the field.
