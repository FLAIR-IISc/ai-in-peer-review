Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count data x1:T ∈ RV×T that extends Poisson Factorization (PF/PFA) with a deep hierarchical set of time-evolving latent variables. Observations are modeled as xvt ~ Poisson(∑k θtk φvk λk ψv), where φ (feature loadings), θ (time-varying factors), λ (component importance), and ψ (feature sparsity) are positive. Temporal dependence is modeled via multiple latent layers h(n)t; the conditional distributions p(θt | h(0) t−c:t) and p(h(n) t | h(n+1) t−c:t) are “implicit” and parameterized by RNNs that output Gamma parameters. Inference alternates: (i) mean-field VI for latent variables using conjugacy with auxiliary latent counts, and (ii) training of the RNN parameters by a “probability autoencoder” objective that resembles, but does not equal, a VAE ELBO (squared-error losses between expectations of prior/posterior conditionals).

Empirical evaluation compares DDPFA to PGDS (Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems), PFA, and LSTM on synthetic sequences and five real datasets (ICEWS events, NIPS word counts, Ebola cases, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population), reporting MSE (fit) and one-step PMSE (prediction). DDPFA often outperforms baselines, with some exceptions (ICEWS prediction where PGDS does well). The paper argues that DDPFA captures long-range and multi-factor activation more faithfully than PGDS and LSTM.

This topic is well within NeurIPS scope and connects to PF/PFA, Gamma/Negative-Binomial process models, dynamic topic models, deep exponential families, hierarchical VAEs, and recurrent PF for recommendation.

Strengths
- Ambitious goal: combining PF with deep, RNN-parameterized temporal structure to capture longer-range dependencies in count sequences.
- Uses conjugacy where possible (Gamma–Poisson) for efficient coordinate updates of many latent variables.
- Empirical coverage across synthetic and diverse real datasets; includes interpretable examples (e.g., ICEWS factors).
- Attempts to address known issues with deep latent variable models (e.g., ladder-like architecture to mitigate “inactive units”/pruning).

Weaknesses
- Technical soundness and clarity: the model and inference are specified with many inconsistencies and errors (see below), and the training objective for the neural components lacks a principled probabilistic derivation. It is not a proven ELBO nor a bound on marginal likelihood.
- Notational mistakes and derivation gaps: several equations have indexing/typo errors (e.g., Eq. 7 missing ψv and duplicating φv; q(ψk) vs ψv; repeated Φ in conditionals; inconsistent use of βθ vs βλ), undermining reproducibility.
- The “probability autoencoder” loss (Eqs. 15–18) is ad-hoc: uses L2 distances between expectations of conditional distributions and reconstructs X by plugging in expectations. No guarantee it increases ELBO or yields consistent estimators; no comparison to standard amortized VI (reparameterization, implicit gradients).
- Relation to prior work is incomplete. Dynamic topic models (Blei & Lafferty, 2006), recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation, and other deep dynamic count models are not adequately contrasted. The novelty relative to deep exponential families with temporal structure and prior recurrent PF is not sharply delineated.
- Experimental methodology: baselines appear under-tuned (e.g., fixed iteration counts, single LSTM configuration); metrics (MSE/PMSE) are limited for count/text (no held-out log-likelihood/perplexity, overdispersion assessment, or calibration). No ablation for λ/ψ, window size c, depth, or the RNN components vs shallow PF. No runtime/scalability analysis.
- Writing and organization issues: grammar errors, incomplete references (missing venues/years), and missing/unclear figures/tables impede understanding.

Quality
- The mean-field updates for PF components are standard, but the deep temporal part is not rigorously tied into a single variational objective. The alternation between MFVI and heuristic squared-error training may work empirically but lacks guarantees.
- Important derivations are either missing or incorrect due to notation issues. Without a precise generative story and a coherent ELBO, the approach is hard to trust or reproduce.

Clarity
- The paper is hard to follow. Equations contain errors; definitions of α and γ as “prior/posterior information” from adjacent layers via RNNs are vague; the indexing of windows is inconsistent; the role of Λ and Ψ (kept vs absorbed) is confusing. References are incomplete.

Originality
- The high-level idea—deep hierarchical time dependence for PF—is interesting, but closely related to prior deep latent variable models (deep exponential families) and recurrent PF. The main claimed novelty is the use of an RNN-parameterized Gamma family across layers, but this is conceptually incremental. The proposed “probability autoencoder” objective is unusual, but not theoretically grounded.

Significance
- If made rigorous and clearly presented, a deep dynamic PF that reliably improves long-horizon count prediction could be impactful. As written, due to the methodological and presentation issues, the contribution is limited.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem (long-range dynamics in sparse counts).
- Promising empirical results across several datasets.
- Attempts to mitigate known deep latent model issues (ladder structure).

Arguments against acceptance
- Lacks a principled variational objective; neural training is heuristic and not connected to a bound.
- Multiple technical and notational errors; unclear generative/inference procedures.
- Incomplete comparison to strong baselines and limited evaluation metrics; unclear hyperparameter tuning fairness.
- Poor clarity and incomplete references.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a precise generative model and a single coherent ELBO that includes both PF variables and the RNN-parameterized conditionals; or explicitly adopt amortized VI with reparameterization for Gamma (e.g., implicit reparameterization or augmentation) or score-function estimators with control variates.
- Fix notation and derivations; ensure all conditionals and updates are correct and reproducible; provide a supplemental with full updates and algorithm pseudocode.
- Justify the ladder architecture and show ablations: with/without deep layers, varying window c, role of Λ/Ψ, and different priors. Include comparisons to DTM, recurrent PF for recommendation, deep exponential families with temporal links, and deep Poisson models with Bernoulli-Poisson links.
- Use appropriate metrics: held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity for text, count calibration, and for recommendation tasks, ranking metrics (MAP/NDCG/AUC). Report uncertainty and significance.
- Fair baseline tuning (grid search), and report training time and scalability.
- Improve writing, organization, and references (complete citations with venues/years).

Recommendation: Reject (current form)
While the problem is important and results are promising, the paper lacks a rigorous and clearly presented inference framework and contains numerous technical and clarity issues. With a principled objective, corrected derivations, stronger baselines, and clearer exposition, the work could be much stronger in a future submission.

Reviewer confidence: medium.
