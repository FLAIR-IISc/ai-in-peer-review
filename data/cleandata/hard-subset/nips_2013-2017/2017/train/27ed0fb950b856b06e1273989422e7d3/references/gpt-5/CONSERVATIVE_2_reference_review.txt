Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA) for sequential count data. Observations xvt are modeled via a Poisson factorization with factors Φ, time-varying scores Θ, and additional positive scalars Λ and Ψ for component- and feature-wise scaling. Temporal dependence is modeled by a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t with recurrent connections over a fixed window c. The conditional distributions p(θt|h(0)t−c:t) and p(h(n)t|h(n+1)t−c:t) are “implicit” Gamma distributions whose shape parameters are produced by RNNs. Inference uses mean-field variational updates for conjugate parts (via Poisson-Gamma augmentation and xvtk latent counts) and a two-stage “Probability AutoEncoder” procedure to train the RNNs via squared-error objectives between expectations. Experiments on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, Sheep population) compare against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA under MSE/one-step PMSE; DDPFA is reported to win on most.

Relation to prior work
The model builds on Poisson factorization with Gamma priors and time dynamics (e.g., PGDS; negative binomial processes; hierarchical Gamma belief networks), and on deep latent variable models (DEFs, deep Poisson factorization, RBM/SBN-based topic models) and RNN-based temporal models. The work also touches variational autoencoders but deviates from standard ELBO training by proposing a bespoke squared-error objective. Several relevant sequential latent variable models (e.g., VRNN/Deep Markov Models; dynamic gamma/Poisson belief networks; neural Hawkes/Poisson RNNs; recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation) should be discussed and compared more thoroughly.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant problem: modeling temporal dependence in high-dimensional sparse counts.
- Attempts to combine conjugate Poisson factorization with recurrent deep structure for longer-range temporal dependencies.
- Uses standard Poisson-Gamma augmentation to enable closed-form mean-field updates for Φ, Λ, Ψ, Θ given auxiliary counts.
- Broad empirical sweep across several datasets.

Weaknesses and concerns (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance)
- Methodological soundness: The “Probability AutoEncoder” objective (Eqs. 15–18) replaces a principled ELBO with ad hoc L2 losses on expectations 〈p(·)〉. There is no derivation showing this optimizes any likelihood bound; convergence and bias are unclear. The statement that differentiable variational Bayes is “not suitable” because sampling is “high-cost and useless” is unconvincing given amortized/stochastic VI is standard for such models.
- Inference consistency: The model relies on conjugacy to update Θ, yet the prior shape αθtk is produced by an RNN that depends on latent variables. Under mean-field, these become moving targets; the resulting updates are not guaranteed to correspond to optimizing a single coherent ELBO.
- Identifiability and redundancy: Introducing Λ and Ψ multiplicatively with Φ and Θ exacerbates scale nonidentifiability without constraints (e.g., column normalization of Φ, priors tying scales). The paper does not discuss how this is handled in inference or evaluation.
- Temporal modeling: Despite the claim of long-term dependence, the RNNs appear to condition on fixed windows of size c (set to 4 in experiments). How this produces long-range dependencies beyond stacked depth is not substantiated.
- Evaluation metrics: MSE/PMSE are not standard for count models. Predictive log-likelihood, Poisson deviance, held-out perplexity (for text), and calibration would be more appropriate. For binary/compound links, AUC/NLL should be used when relevant. No uncertainty evaluation is provided.
- Baselines and ablations: Comparisons omit several strong and directly relevant baselines (deep Poisson factorization, deep exponential families, recurrent Poisson factorization, hierarchical Poisson factorization with temporal dynamics, deep Markov models/VRNNs). LSTM is not a competitive count model unless paired with proper link/likelihood. Hyperparameter tuning parity is unclear. Only last-step prediction is tested.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Notation is inconsistent; several equations contain typos or unclear indices; probabilistic semantics of “implicit Gamma distributions” from RNNs are vague. The architecture of RNNs (hidden sizes, activations), optimization details, initialization, and runtime are under-specified. Many references are incomplete (missing years/venues). No code or reproducibility checklist.
- Empirical claims: Reported gains are modest and sometimes not present (e.g., ICEWS prediction). The paper itself reports that adding layers gives little benefit and highlights “pruning,” which suggests the deep structure may not be contributing as intended under the chosen inference.
- Novelty: Combining Poisson factorization with RNN-based dynamics and multiple latent layers is incremental relative to prior deep/exponential family and dynamic gamma/Poisson models; the distinctive part (the Probability AutoEncoder training) lacks theoretical support.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a principled ELBO for the full model, with amortized inference networks for the temporal priors, and optimize it with reparameterization or conjugate-computation VI? If not, what objective is the current training optimizing?
- How sensitive are results to c, K, depth, and to the presence/absence of Λ and Ψ? Can you add ablations and identifiability constraints?
- Please report predictive log-likelihood/Poisson deviance and held-out perplexity (for NIPS), with multiple random splits and hyperparameter tuning parity across methods.
- What are the exact RNN architectures, parameter counts, and training schedules? What is training time and memory footprint per dataset?
- Can you compare to DEF/DPF (e.g., Gan et al. 2015), VRNN/DMM, recurrent Poisson factorization, and dynamic gamma belief networks?

Pros
- Addresses an important problem: temporal modeling of sparse counts.
- Leverages conjugate structure for some efficient updates.
- Empirical coverage across several domains.

Cons
- Objective and inference are not theoretically grounded; ad hoc losses.
- Insufficient baselines and inappropriate metrics for counts.
- Limited clarity; incomplete references; weak reproducibility.
- Long-range dependence claim not convincingly supported.
- Gains over established methods are not robustly demonstrated.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper tackles a worthwhile problem but falls short on methodological rigor, clarity, and empirical validation against established baselines. I encourage the authors to (i) formulate and optimize a principled ELBO with amortized inference, (ii) adopt appropriate likelihood-based metrics, (iii) strengthen baselines and ablations, and (iv) improve clarity, references, and reproducibility (including code).

Confidence
High.
