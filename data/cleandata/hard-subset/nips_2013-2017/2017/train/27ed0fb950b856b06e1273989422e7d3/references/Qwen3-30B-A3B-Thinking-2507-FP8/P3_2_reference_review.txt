

# Peer Review: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

## Summary and Context

This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA), an extension of Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) designed for sequential count data. The authors aim to capture long-term temporal dependencies by introducing a deep hierarchical structure with recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to model the transition between time steps. The model builds upon previous work like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis, but claims to better capture complex time dependencies through its deep structure.

The paper makes a reasonable attempt to position itself within the NIPS literature, referencing recent work on hierarchical models (e.g., Deep Exponential Families [8]) and temporal Poisson models [2, 3]. However, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related approaches, particularly the recent "Deep Poisson Factor Modeling with a recurrent structure" [12], which appears to have similar goals.

## Strengths

1. **Problem Relevance**: The paper addresses an important problem—modeling sequential count data with complex temporal dependencies—which is relevant to applications in recommendation systems, text analysis, and event prediction.

2. **Experimental Validation**: The paper evaluates the model on both synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, etc.), demonstrating competitive performance compared to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA baselines.

3. **Methodological Integration**: The integration of deep learning with Bayesian factor analysis represents a reasonable attempt to combine the strengths of both approaches—deep models for capturing complex relationships and Bayesian methods for interpretability.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper makes a strong claim about capturing "long-time dependence" but provides no theoretical analysis of why the deep structure would be necessary. The experimental results (Table 3) actually show diminishing returns with additional layers, undermining the core motivation of the work.

2. **Superficial Comparison to Related Work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from existing deep Poisson models. For example, [12] also proposes a recurrent structure for Poisson factorization, yet the paper fails to clarify how DDPFA differs in a meaningful way. The connection to "Deep Poisson Factor Analysis based on RBM or SBN" [9, 10] is also not well-explained.

3. **Inadequate Demonstration of "Deep" Advantage**: The paper claims that the deep structure captures long-term dependencies, but the experiments don't convincingly show that the deep approach outperforms simpler temporal models. In fact, Table 3 shows minimal improvement with additional layers, suggesting the deep structure may be unnecessary.

4. **Methodological Ambiguity**: The paper's description of the inference algorithm is confusing. The relationship between the "ladder-like architecture" mentioned in the discussion and the actual model structure isn't clear. The reference to [25] for "pruning" issues is problematic since [25] isn't cited in the reference list.

5. **Lack of Interpretability Evidence**: The paper claims "better interpretability" but provides no concrete evidence. Figure 2 shows factors for ICEWS, but doesn't explain how these factors are more interpretable than those from simpler models like PGDS.

6. **Overstated Significance**: The paper claims the model "outperforms shallow models," but the performance gains are marginal (e.g., 2-5% improvement in MSE across most datasets), and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why these small gains are significant in practical applications.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a technically sound attempt to extend Poisson factorization to handle temporal dependencies, but the core contribution is not sufficiently novel or justified. The deep structure appears to add complexity without clear benefits, as evidenced by the diminishing returns with additional layers (Table 3). This is a significant concern, as the paper's main claim hinges on the superiority of the deep approach.

The paper also suffers from a common pitfall in deep learning for probabilistic modeling: it adds layers without sufficient justification for why they're necessary. As noted in [25], hierarchical latent variable models often don't benefit from deeper structures, and the paper's own results confirm this. The "ladder-like architecture" solution to the "pruning" problem is mentioned but not thoroughly justified or evaluated.

The evaluation is somewhat limited. For instance, the comparison with LSTM is problematic because LSTM is a general sequence model that isn't specifically designed for count data, making the comparison unfair. Additionally, the paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the model (e.g., the deep structure vs. the RNN-based transition).

## Recommendation

**Weak Reject**

The paper addresses a relevant problem but fails to convincingly demonstrate the novelty and value of its deep approach. The experimental results don't support the main claim that the deep structure is necessary to capture long-term dependencies. The methodological contributions are incremental at best, and the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from existing work in the field.

The paper could be strengthened by:
1. Providing a clearer theoretical justification for the deep structure
2. Conducting more thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the deep layers
3. Better differentiating from related work, particularly [12]
4. Providing stronger evidence for the claimed interpretability benefits

Without these improvements, the paper doesn't advance the state of the art significantly in a way that would justify acceptance at NIPS. The marginal performance gains with the deep structure don't sufficiently outweigh the added complexity, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why the deep approach is needed for the problem at hand.

The paper would be better suited for a more applied venue where the focus is on specific applications rather than methodological novelty. For NIPS, the contribution is not sufficiently novel or well-justified to warrant acceptance.
