

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes a "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model" (DDPFA) that extends Poisson Factor Analysis with deep neural network structures to capture complex temporal dependencies in count data. The authors claim their model can better handle long-term dependencies than existing methods like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) and standard PFA, using recurrent neural networks to model the temporal dynamics of latent factors. The paper presents experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets showing improved performance in prediction and fitting tasks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough description of the model and inference algorithm.
- The experimental section provides reasonable comparisons against relevant baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
- The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach, including the "pruning" problem and poor performance on the ICEWS prediction task.
- The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the existing literature in dynamic Poisson factorization and deep Bayesian models.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's primary weakness is its lack of rigorous theoretical justification for the proposed deep structure. While the authors claim their model captures "long-time dependence" better than existing methods, they provide no theoretical analysis demonstrating why the deep neural network structure should outperform simpler transition models. The Gamma-Poisson transition structure used in PGDS has a solid theoretical foundation in Bayesian time series modeling, while the DDPFA's neural network approach appears more ad hoc.

The experimental validation is insufficiently thorough. The authors compare against only three baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA), but fail to include other relevant approaches such as the dynamic topic models mentioned in the introduction (e.g., temporal Dirichlet process). More critically, the paper claims "excellent results" but the performance improvements over baselines are modest across most datasets (typically 1-5% improvement in MSE/PMSE), with DDPFA actually performing worse than PGDS on the ICEWS prediction task. This undermines the paper's central claim of superior performance.

The paper also fails to adequately address the "pruning" problem they acknowledge in Section 5. The observation that "some dimensions in the latent layers only contain data noise" is a significant limitation that the authors don't properly resolve. They merely note this problem exists and suggest it might be solved with "other inference methods," without providing evidence that their approach is fundamentally sound.

The authors claim their model has "better interpretability" than existing methods, but provide no concrete evidence or examples to support this assertion. The paper's Figure 2 shows factors activated at different time points, but this is not particularly novel compared to what could be achieved with simpler dynamic factor models.

The most concerning aspect is the paper's lack of methodological rigor. The authors use a mean-field variational inference approach with a "probability autoencoder" for training, but provide no theoretical justification for why this specific variational approach should work better than standard variational inference for this problem. The paper cites the Auto-Encoding Variational Bayes paper (Kingma & Welling, 2013) but fails to address the well-known limitations of mean-field variational inference for capturing complex dependencies, which is exactly what their model is attempting to do.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents an incremental extension of existing Poisson factorization methods with a deep neural network structure. While the authors have done a reasonable job of implementing their model and showing modest performance improvements on some datasets, the paper lacks the theoretical rigor, thorough experimental validation, and clear demonstration of significant advantages over established methods that would justify publication at NIPS.

The paper's claims about "capturing complex temporal dependence" and "better interpretability" are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The experimental results show only marginal improvements over baselines, and the model fails on a significant real-world dataset (ICEWS), which undermines the authors' main claims. The methodological approach is also questionable, as the authors use mean-field variational inference on a model that is specifically designed to capture complex dependencies, which is known to be problematic.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper represents an incremental extension of existing methods without sufficient theoretical justification or compelling empirical evidence of significant advantages over established baselines. The authors have not provided adequate validation that their proposed deep structure actually captures complex temporal dependencies better than simpler, well-established methods. The paper would benefit from substantially more thorough experimental validation, including comparisons with more relevant baselines, analysis of failure cases, and theoretical justification for the proposed architecture. Without these improvements, the paper does not meet NIPS' standards for quality, significance, or originality.
