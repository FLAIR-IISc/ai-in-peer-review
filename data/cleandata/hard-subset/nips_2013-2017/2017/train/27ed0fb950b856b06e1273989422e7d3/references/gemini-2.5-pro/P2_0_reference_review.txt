### **Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count data. The goal is to capture complex, long-range temporal dependencies that are missed by traditional models like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS), which often rely on a first-order Markov assumption. The core idea is to extend Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by introducing a deep, hierarchical structure of latent variables over time. The temporal evolution of these latent variables is not governed by a simple conjugate prior, but by a more flexible implicit distribution modeled by a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN). The inference procedure is a two-stage hybrid approach: latent variables are updated using mean-field variational inference, while the parameters of the RNNs are trained separately using a reconstruction-based loss function, termed a "Probability AutoEncoder." The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on several synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improvements over PFA, PGDS, and LSTM baselines in both fitting and prediction tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known limitation of existing dynamic topic models and count matrix factorization methods—the difficulty of capturing non-Markovian, long-range temporal dependencies. The proposed solution of using a deep hierarchy of latent variables with dynamics parameterized by RNNs is a novel and powerful combination of probabilistic modeling and deep learning techniques. It is a logical and significant step beyond prior work like PGDS [2], providing a much more expressive framework for modeling temporal dynamics.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering a diverse set of four synthetic and five real-world datasets with varying characteristics (dimensionality, time-series length). The proposed DDPFA model consistently outperforms relevant and strong baselines, including a standard dynamic model (PGDS), a pure deep learning model (LSTM), and a static baseline (PFA). This provides compelling evidence for the model's effectiveness.

3.  **Honest and Insightful Analysis of Limitations:** A major strength of this work is the authors' candid discussion of the model's limitations in Section 4. They investigate the effect of model depth and discover that simply adding more layers does not always improve performance. Crucially, they correctly identify this as being related to the "pruning" or "posterior collapse" problem observed in other hierarchical variational models [24, 25], where upper latent layers are ignored by the optimizer. This self-awareness and connection to broader challenges in the field significantly strengthen the paper and demonstrate a deep understanding of the problem space.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Justification for the Inference Procedure:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the description and justification of the "Probability AutoEncoder" used for training the neural network parameters.
    *   The connection between the main variational objective (minimizing KL divergence, Eq. 6) and the loss function for the neural networks (Eq. 16-18) is not made clear. The VI updates for the latent variables and the network parameter updates seem to be optimizing two different, disconnected objectives. A principled approach would jointly optimize a single evidence lower bound (ELBO), where the RNNs parameterize parts of the variational distribution or the prior.
    *   The jump from the intractable integral in Eq. 15 to the specific L2 loss in Eq. 16 is not derived or justified. It resembles a standard autoencoder loss, but its theoretical grounding within this probabilistic model is missing. Why L2 loss on the expectation of the posteriors? How does this approximate the original objective?
    *   The authors' claim that "differentiable variational Bayes is not suitable" because sampling is "high-cost and useless" is confusing. Reparameterization-based methods (as in VAEs [21]) are the standard for exactly this type of model and would allow for end-to-end optimization of a single ELBO. The current two-stage procedure feels ad-hoc in comparison.

2.  **Insufficient Model and Implementation Details for Reproducibility:** The paper lacks some key details needed to fully understand and reproduce the work.
    *   The exact architecture of the RNNs is not specified (e.g., vanilla RNN, GRU, LSTM?). The mechanism for how the window of inputs (`h(n+1)t−c, ..., h(n+1)t`) is processed to produce the parameters for `h(n)t` is also unclear.
    *   In the discussion of limitations, the authors mention that using a "ladder-like architecture" leads to much better results (Table 3). However, this architecture is never described in the model section, making it impossible to evaluate this important claim or understand the best-performing version of their model. This is a critical omission.

3.  **Minor Clarity Issues:** The writing is generally clear, but some parts could be improved. For instance, the distinction between the prior network `ffeed` and the posterior network `fback` and how they form an "autoencoder" structure could be explained more intuitively, perhaps with a more detailed diagram. The role of `c` (the window size) and its interaction with the deep structure could also be elaborated upon.

**Overall Evaluation and Recommendation**

This paper introduces a novel and promising model for a significant problem in time-series analysis. The core idea is elegant, and the empirical results are strong and well-supported. The honest self-evaluation of the model's architectural limitations is also a major plus.

However, the work is significantly hampered by a lack of clarity and theoretical justification for its inference procedure. The ad-hoc nature of the "Probability AutoEncoder" loss function and its disconnection from the main variational objective raises questions about the technical soundness of the learning algorithm. Furthermore, the omission of key architectural details (like the "ladder-like architecture") hurts the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

Despite these weaknesses, the novelty of the modeling approach and the strength of the empirical results are compelling. The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating the potential of combining deep hierarchical structures and RNNs for dynamic count data. With significant revisions to clarify the inference algorithm and provide missing model details, this could be a strong paper.

**Decision:** Weak Accept.

The paper is recommended for acceptance on the condition that the authors address the major clarity issues regarding the inference procedure and model architecture in the final version. The positive aspects—originality, significance, and strong empirical results—outweigh the weaknesses, but the issues with technical clarity are substantial and must be fixed.
