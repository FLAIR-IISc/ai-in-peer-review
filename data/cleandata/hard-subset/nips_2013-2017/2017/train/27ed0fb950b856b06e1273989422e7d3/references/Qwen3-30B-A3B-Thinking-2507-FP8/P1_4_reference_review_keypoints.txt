- Proposes DDPFA: a deep hierarchical Poisson factorization model using recurrent connections to capture long-term temporal dependencies in sequential count data.
- Uses variational inference with a probability autoencoder to estimate model parameters.
- Evaluated on synthetic and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) and compared to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.
- Empirical results show improvements over baselines, demonstrating practical utility for real-world count data tasks.
- Technically novel integration of deep learning with Poisson factorization and hierarchical recurrent latent structure.
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse datasets and appropriate baseline comparisons.
- Well-written and clearly organized with clear mathematical formulations.
- Demonstrates relevance to important application domains (e.g., international events, disease outbreaks).
- Lacks theoretical analysis (no convergence guarantees, error bounds, or expressive-capacity analysis).
- Insufficient comparison with other deep count models (e.g., deep Poisson models based on RBMs/SBNs).
- Missing implementation details for the probability autoencoder (architecture, hidden units, activations), hindering reproducibility.
- Limited analysis of observed issues like pruning and the ineffectiveness of added layers; related literature underutilized.
- Questionable use of LSTM as a baseline for count data without explaining adaptations for discrete counts.
- Overall assessment: promising and empirically strong but lacking theoretical grounding and deeper comparisons; novelty is incremental.
- Recommendation: weak acceptance with major revisions to add theory, better baselines, implementation details, and deeper limitation analysis.
- Required revisions suggested: add theoretical analysis, compare with deep count models, provide implementation specifics, investigate limitations, and justify or replace the LSTM baseline.
