## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) aiming to capture both short-term and long-term dependencies in sequentially observed count data. The authors build upon existing Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) models by incorporating recurrent neural networks (RNNs) into the temporal dynamics of the latent factors and adding a deep structure to model long-range dependencies. Variational inference is employed for parameter estimation. The model is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating competitive performance compared to several baselines.

While the idea of combining deep learning with Bayesian factor models for sequential count data is promising and addresses a relevant research direction, the paper suffers from a number of weaknesses, ranging from methodological concerns to significant presentation issues. These issues, despite the potential novelty, lead me to recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Modeling dynamic count data is a crucial task with applications in numerous domains.
*   **Novelty (Potential):** The integration of RNNs and deep structures within a PFA framework *could* represent a novel approach, though the degree of novelty is diminished by the existence of related work (discussed below).
*   **Empirical Evaluation:**  The inclusion of both synthetic and real-world datasets is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** The experimental section lacks a truly thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods. While LSTM and PGDS are included, the parameter tuning for these baselines isn’t sufficiently justified. Crucially, comparisons to more recent deep generative models specifically designed for sequential data (e.g., various GRU/LSTM-based variational autoencoders, temporal point process models with neural networks) are missing. The claim of "excellent results" feels overstated without this context.
*   **Methodological Concerns – Variational Inference:** The variational inference implementation is described at a high level, lacking crucial details. Specifically, the coordinate ascent method within the variational framework is not adequately explained. The choice of mean-field approximation is standard, but the potential for posterior collapse is not addressed. The connection between the neural network parameters and the Gamma distributions used for variational approximation isn't entirely clear. Why is the neural network's output directly used as parameters for Gamma distributions? Justification beyond "it works" is needed.
*   **Methodological Concerns – Neural Network Architecture & Training:** The specific architecture of the RNNs is not detailed, aside from stating they are "recurrent neural networks." What type of RNN is used (e.g., vanilla RNN, LSTM, GRU)? The justification for the window size 'c' is missing.  The loss function (Equation 18) is a somewhat strange combination of reconstruction loss and a regularization term that seemingly pushes latent representations toward their prior. The motivation for this particular form is unclear.  The statement about "pruning" in section 4, while acknowledged, isn't explored deeply enough – it raises significant concerns about the model’s representational capacity and the validity of the learned latent variables.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The notation is inconsistent, making it difficult to follow the mathematical derivations. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased. The connection between equations and the accompanying text is often unclear. For example, Equation (11) is introduced without sufficient context. Figure 1 is referenced but not provided in the submitted material, making it impossible to assess the visualization of the proposed model structure.
*   **Related Work:** The related work section, while mentioning relevant papers, doesn’t fully position the contribution of DDPFA within the existing literature. The authors mention Deep Poisson Factor Analysis based on RBM/SBN [9, 10], but do not discuss the advantages/disadvantages of using RNNs over these earlier models.  The discussion of differentiable variational Bayes in relation to the pruning problem is brief and lacks depth. The connection to related deep generative time series models is almost completely absent.
*   **Minor Issues:** There are several minor grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper. For example, "st Conference" in the title is incorrect.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The model proposes a conceptually interesting integration of deep learning and Bayesian factor analysis.
*   The empirical results, while not overwhelmingly conclusive, show promising performance on some datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The methodological details are insufficiently detailed and lack justification.
*   The experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to support the claims of superiority.
*   The clarity and presentation of the paper are significantly lacking.
*   The related work section is incomplete and fails to adequately position the contribution within the broader literature.
*   The potential for posterior collapse within the variational inference framework isn’t addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea behind DDPFA has merit, the paper is ultimately hampered by a lack of rigor, clarity, and a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The numerous methodological concerns and presentation issues outweigh the potential novelty. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, provide more detailed methodological explanations, conduct a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and improve the clarity and presentation of the paper before resubmitting.
