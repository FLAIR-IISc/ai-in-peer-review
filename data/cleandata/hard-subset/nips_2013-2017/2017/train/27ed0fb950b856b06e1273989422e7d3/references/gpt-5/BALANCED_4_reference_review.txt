Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for sequential count data. Observations xvt are modeled as Poisson with rate sum over factors θtk φvk λk ψv. Temporal dependence is introduced via a deep hierarchy of latent states h(n)t, where RNNs parameterize “implicit” conditional distributions that produce Gamma parameters for θt and for the h(n)t across layers and time windows. Inference alternates mean-field updates for conjugate variables (with auxiliary latent counts xvtk) and learning the RNN parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that encourages consistency between forward (prior) and backward (posterior) networks and reconstructs either Θ or X. Experiments on four synthetic and five real datasets compare against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE/PMSE, reporting gains in most settings.

This paper builds on: Poisson factorization for counts [Zhou et al.; Gopalan et al.], dynamic count models incl. PGDS and non-homogeneous Poisson processes, deep latent variable models for counts and topics (Deep Exponential Families; deep Poisson FA), and recurrent latent-variable models (VRNN/Deep Markov Models). The novelty claimed is a deep temporal hierarchy with forward/backward RNNs specifying Gamma priors/posteriors for count factors, intending to capture long-range dependencies.

Strengths:
- Ambitious attempt to combine Poisson factorization with deep temporal structure to capture long- and short-term dynamics in count data.
- Leverages Gamma–Poisson conjugacy with auxiliary latent counts to retain closed-form mean-field updates for many variables.
- Empirical results show improvements over PFA/PGDS/LSTM on several datasets, and the discussion acknowledges known issues such as pruning in hierarchical VAEs and proposes a ladder-style architecture.
- The model is motivated by practical applications (ICEWS, NIPS, disease, disasters) where temporal dependencies and sparsity matter.

Weaknesses:
- The probabilistic model and variational objective are insufficiently specified and sometimes inconsistent. Core equations (e.g., Eqs. 3–4, 9–18) blur the line between priors, posteriors, and amortized approximations. The “Probability AutoEncoder” objective is not a valid ELBO; it uses L2 distances between expectations rather than maximizing a well-defined lower bound. No guarantee it optimizes a coherent likelihood.
- Identifiability: Introducing Φ, Θ, Λ, and Ψ simultaneously without normalization/constraints creates severe scale indeterminacy across component, time, and feature factors. This can destabilize learning and confound interpretations attributed to λk and ψv.
- Temporal modeling/forecasting is unclear. The posterior “backward” RNN uses future inputs (θt+1:t+c), which is appropriate for smoothing but risks leakage if used during training for forecasting tasks. The paper does not separate training objectives for filtering vs smoothing nor describe how forecasting at t+1 is performed without future data.
- Evaluation methodology is limited: MSE/PMSE on counts is not standard for count models; predictive log-likelihood/perplexity and calibrated uncertainty would be more informative. Baselines (PFA, PGDS, LSTM) are not thoroughly tuned or matched; no comparisons to strong modern baselines (DEF, Deep Markov Models/VRNN, DTM/HDMM variants, RPF for recommendations). No ablation on window size c, number of layers, or whether backward networks are needed.
- Clarity/notation: Several equations contain typos or ambiguous terms (e.g., Eq. 7 double φv; Eqs. 9–14 mix α and γ as both prior/posterior parameters without precise definitions; “implicit distributions” not formally defined). The generative process is not fully specified (e.g., parameterization of Gamma: shape/scale vs rate, constraints enforced by RNN outputs).
- Reproducibility: Architecture details, optimization hyperparameters, and preprocessing for datasets are incomplete. References are often missing venues/years; some key related works are not cited or discussed.

Quality:
As written, the technical contribution is not convincingly sound. The variational procedure departs from a standard ELBO and substitutes a heuristic loss, but without justification or analysis. The interplay of conjugate updates and neural-network-driven Gamma parameters needs a clear derivation and convergence discussion. Forecasting methodology is underspecified.

Clarity:
The paper is difficult to follow. The generative model, inference, and training objectives should be presented with precise notation. Many typos and missing details hinder understanding. Figures/tables are referenced to support claims, but quantitative detail is sparse.

Originality:
The idea of combining Poisson factorization with amortized deep temporal inference is interesting, but close in spirit to DEFs, deep Poisson FA, RPF, and deep state-space models (VRNN/DMM) that parameterize conditionals with RNNs. The proposed two-network (prior/posterior) construction is common; the ladder observation is not new. The specific “Probability AutoEncoder” objective appears novel but is ad hoc.

Significance:
Potentially useful if made principled and empirically solid, given the importance of sequential count modeling. However, the current form limits impact due to methodological ambiguity and evaluation gaps.

Questions for authors:
- Please provide the exact ELBO you optimize and how the neural network parameters enter. If not optimizing an ELBO, what is the probabilistic justification of Eqs. 15–18?
- How do you avoid train–test leakage from the backward RNN when evaluating one-step-ahead predictions?
- How are λk and ψv identifiable alongside Φ and Θ? Do you impose normalization (e.g., column-normalized Φ, sum-to-one constraints) or regularization?
- What RNN architectures, nonlinearities, and constraints ensure positivity of Gamma parameters?
- Can you report predictive log-likelihood and perplexity, and include stronger baselines (DEF, DMM/VRNN, DTM), with hyperparameter tuning?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Specify the full generative model and derive a valid ELBO with amortized inference networks for p(θt|h) and q(h|·). Consider reparameterization/auxiliary-variable gradients for Gamma (or use NB augmentation) to enable end-to-end ELBO optimization.
- Separate smoothing and filtering: train a forward model for forecasting without future inputs, and optionally a smoother for analysis.
- Address identifiability by absorbing λ, ψ or imposing normalization/priors that break scale invariances; otherwise interpretability claims are weak.
- Use standard evaluation metrics (predictive log-likelihood), calibration (PP-checks), and comprehensive ablations (c, depth N, with/without backward net).
- Compare to stronger baselines and report runtime/complexity. Release code and detailed settings.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem (dynamic count modeling) with a potentially powerful deep temporal approach.
- Empirical results suggest benefits over shallow models on some datasets.

Cons for acceptance:
- Methodological soundness of the training objective is unclear; not a principled variational inference procedure.
- Insufficient clarity, incomplete references, and limited/restricted evaluation with weak baselines.
- Identifiability and leakage concerns undermine claims of interpretability and forecasting.

Recommendation: Reject. The direction is promising, but the paper requires a clearer, principled inference framework, stronger and properly tuned baselines with standard metrics, and a more rigorous presentation to meet NeurIPS standards.
