

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

The paper proposes a "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model" (DDPFA) to model sequentially observed count data. It builds on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS), introducing a deep hierarchical structure with recurrent neural networks to capture long-term temporal dependencies. The authors claim their model outperforms PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:** The paper is well-structured, with clear mathematical formulations. The experimental design covers both synthetic and real-world datasets, and the authors appropriately compare against relevant baselines. The model's interpretation of latent factors (e.g., in Figure 2) provides some interesting insights into temporal patterns.

**Weaknesses:** The paper suffers from several critical issues that undermine its scientific contribution.

First, the paper lacks rigorous justification for the deep structure. The authors claim their model "captures long-time dependence" but fail to demonstrate why a deep architecture is necessary when simpler models (like PGDS) with appropriate temporal smoothing might suffice. The experimental results in Table 3 reveal that "pruning" and "uselessness of adding network layers" are significant problems, contradicting the core premise of the deep structure. The authors acknowledge that "expanding the dimension in bottom layer is more useful than in upper layers," which suggests the deep architecture may be largely redundant.

Second, the experimental validation is insufficient. The paper compares against only three baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) on a limited set of datasets (4 synthetic, 5 real-world). The improvements over baselines are modest (Table 2), and no statistical significance tests are provided. The authors acknowledge that "the DDPFA's result in ICEWS prediction task is not good enough," yet they don't investigate why the model fails in this case or how to address it.

Third, the inference procedure is questionable. The authors use mean-field variational inference, which is known to underestimate uncertainty and fail to capture complex posterior dependencies. They acknowledge this issue when noting "this problem is caused by MF-variational inference used in our model," yet they offer no alternative approach or justification for using this method. The paper also fails to provide sufficient details about the neural network architectures or training procedures to enable reproducibility.

Fourth, the novelty claim is weak. The paper builds on existing work (references [8-12] show prior work on deep Poisson factorization) but doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from or improves upon these methods. The "deep" aspect appears to be a straightforward application of neural networks to an existing framework rather than a novel architectural innovation.

**Critical Assessment:** The paper presents an incremental extension of existing Poisson factorization models with a deep structure. The claimed improvements over baselines are marginal and not statistically validated. The authors acknowledge significant limitations with their approach (pruning, uselessness of adding layers), yet fail to provide a compelling justification for why these issues shouldn't undermine their core contribution. The paper lacks the methodological rigor expected at NIPS, particularly in validating the necessity of the deep structure and thoroughly evaluating the model's limitations.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound in its execution, it fails to meet the bar for novelty and significance at NIPS. The contribution is incremental rather than transformative, the validation is insufficient, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why the deep structure is necessary or beneficial. The authors would need to provide more rigorous evidence of the deep structure's necessity, conduct more thorough experiments with statistical significance testing, and better justify the novelty of their approach to warrant acceptance. The paper currently reads more like a methodological extension than a significant scientific contribution.
