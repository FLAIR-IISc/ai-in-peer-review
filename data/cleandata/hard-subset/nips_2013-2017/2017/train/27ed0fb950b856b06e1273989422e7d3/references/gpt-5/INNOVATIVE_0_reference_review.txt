Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count data that combines Poisson factorization with a deep, temporally structured hierarchy of latent variables. Counts xvt are modeled as Poisson with rates that factorize into feature- and component-specific nonnegative terms (φ, ψ, θ, λ). Temporal dependencies are introduced via multiple latent layers h(n)t, where Gamma distributed variables have parameters produced by recurrent neural networks over a sliding window of past/future latent states. Inference is done in two stages: (i) coordinate-ascent mean-field variational updates exploiting Gamma–Poisson conjugacy (with the RNNs treated as fixed), and (ii) learning the RNN parameters using a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that couples a forward prior p(Θ|H(0)) and a backward “posterior” p(H(0)|Θ) (and analogously between higher layers), approximated via L2 losses on expectations rather than a standard ELBO. Experiments on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population) show improved MSE/one-step PMSE over PFA, PGDS, and LSTM in most cases, with some exceptions (e.g., ICEWS prediction where PGDS does better).

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Poisson factorization and dynamic Gamma–Poisson models (e.g., PGDS), deep latent variable models (Deep Exponential Families, Gamma Belief Networks), and recurrent models for sequences (e.g., LSTM). It is closest in spirit to prior attempts at combining count factorization with temporal/recurrent structure (e.g., recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation; “Deep Poisson factor modeling with a recurrent structure” cited as [12]) and to amortized variational inference for sequential latent variables (e.g., VRNN/Deep Kalman Filter families), but extends with a multi-layer, bidirectional ladder-like structure where Gamma parameters are outputs of RNNs. The use of separate feature- and component-wise scalings ψ and λ to encourage sparsity/smoothness echoes earlier PF variants.

Strengths
- Ambitious and novel direction: marrying conjugate Poisson factorization with deep temporal structure parameterized by RNNs. The idea of using RNNs to output Gamma shape parameters across layers is interesting and potentially impactful for long-range dependencies in sparse counts.
- Empirical scope: multiple synthetic and diverse real datasets; competitive to strong baselines in most settings.
- Interpretability claims (e.g., long-lived factors for ICEWS) are appealing; the model could provide richer temporal factors than PGDS.
- The paper explicitly discusses practical issues (“pruning” and the effectiveness of ladder architectures), which is useful.

Weaknesses
- Objective/inference lacks principled grounding. The “Probability AutoEncoder” replaces an ELBO with L2 losses between expectations of distributions (Eqs. 16–18). It is unclear what is being optimized, whether it is a lower bound, and whether gradients correspond to any coherent probabilistic objective. This goes beyond a “minor flaw”.
- Clarity and rigor: The presentation is difficult to follow, with numerous typos, notation inconsistencies (e.g., indices t vs tk, bold vs scalar), and undefined variables. Figures/tables are referenced but not shown; key architectural choices (RNN type, parameterization of α/β, exact inputs/outputs, window c) and training schedules need clearer specification. The mean-field family (Eq. 5) and independence assumptions are not fully justified given the RNN couplings.
- Identifiability/scale: introducing φ, θ, λ, ψ without normalization invites scale indeterminacies. The paper does not discuss constraints or regularization to avoid degenerate scaling (beyond Gamma priors), nor how this affects interpretability.
- Temporal modeling: despite claims of long-range dependence, the model relies on a fixed window c and deeper layers; there is no analysis showing effective memory length or why deeper layers guarantee long-term dependencies.
- Empirical evaluation: one-step prediction only, unclear train/validation/test splits, no held-out likelihood or perplexity for text, no uncertainty metrics or statistical significance, limited ablations (beyond a small table) and missing comparisons to close relatives (e.g., recurrent PF for recsys, VRNN/DKF-like models adapted to counts).
- References are incomplete and in places inaccurate; several relevant works on sequential variational models and count RNNs are missing.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide a principled ELBO (or IWAE-style bound) for your model, and train the RNN parameters by maximizing it? If not, what guarantees does the proposed L2-based objective provide?
- How exactly are the Gamma parameters parameterized by the RNNs (shape, rate, or both)? Are there constraints to ensure positivity and stability?
- How is identifiability handled when φ, θ, λ, ψ are all free? Any normalization (e.g., column-normalized φ) or priors to prevent scale blow-ups?
- What is c, the window size, in each experiment? Any sensitivity analysis, and how does the deeper hierarchy extend effective memory?
- What are the runtime/memory costs relative to PGDS/LSTM? Any convergence diagnostics?
- Could you report held-out likelihood for text (NIPS) and multi-step forecasting?

Pros for acceptance
- Bold attempt to inject deep temporal structure into PF with a novel RNN-parameterized hierarchical Gamma construction.
- Promising empirical results on several datasets, with potential impact for sequential count modeling across domains.
- The ladder-like bidirectional architecture is interesting and could stimulate follow-up research.

Cons for acceptance
- The training objective is ad hoc and not tied to a coherent probabilistic bound; this undermines claims of Bayesian inference.
- Significant clarity issues and missing details impede reproducibility.
- Limited/partial evaluation and missing baselines weaken the evidence of superiority.
- Overlap with prior work combining PF and recurrent/deep structures is not fully disentangled.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The high-level idea is novel and appealing, and I would be enthusiastic with a clearer, principled variational objective and a more rigorous/complete evaluation. As written, the inference procedure and presentation fall short of NeurIPS standards. I encourage the authors to (i) reformulate training under a proper ELBO with amortized inference (e.g., reparameterized Gamma or augmentation), (ii) address identifiability, (iii) expand comparisons and metrics, and (iv) substantially revise the exposition.
