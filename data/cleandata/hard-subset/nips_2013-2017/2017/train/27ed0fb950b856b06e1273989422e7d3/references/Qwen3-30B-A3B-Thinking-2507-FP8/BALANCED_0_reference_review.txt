

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

**Summary**: This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count data. The authors extend traditional Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating a deep hierarchical structure with recurrent neural networks to capture complex temporal dependencies. The model uses variational inference for training and is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets, with comparisons to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA. The work builds on prior NIPS contributions such as Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems [2] and Deep Exponential Families [8], positioning itself as addressing the limitation of shallow temporal dependencies in previous models.

**Strengths**:
1. **Practical Relevance**: The model addresses a meaningful problem in analyzing sequential count data across multiple domains (recommendation systems, text analysis, network analysis). The evaluation on diverse real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, etc.) demonstrates practical applicability.

2. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The authors conduct thorough experiments on both synthetic and real-world data, comparing against relevant baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA). The results show that DDPFA outperforms baselines on most datasets, particularly in capturing long-term dependencies.

3. **Clear Model Formulation**: The mathematical formulation of the model is well-structured, with clear definitions of the generative process and inference algorithm. The introduction of the "ladder-like architecture" to address hierarchical latent variable issues is a thoughtful contribution.

4. **Interpretability**: The paper provides insightful visualizations (Figures 2-3) that demonstrate how DDPFA captures multiple active factors over time, which is an important advantage over PGDS that only captures single-factor activation.

**Weaknesses**:
1. **Theoretical Weaknesses**: The paper lacks sufficient theoretical grounding. While the authors claim their deep structure captures long-term dependencies, they provide no theoretical justification for why a shallow model isn't sufficient. The variational inference approach is presented without convergence guarantees or error analysis.

2. **Reproducibility Concerns**: Critical details for reproducing the results are missing, including specific neural network architectures (number of units, activation functions), exact hyperparameter selection procedures, and the rationale for the window size (c=4). The paper mentions "size of the window is 4" but doesn't explain how this was chosen.

3. **Incomplete Comparison**: The comparison is limited to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA. The authors should compare with more recent deep Poisson models like Scalable Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9,10] and Deep Poisson Factor Modeling [12], which would better contextualize their contribution.

4. **Unaddressed Limitations**: The paper acknowledges the "pruning" problem (where many latent connections are severed) but doesn't adequately analyze its impact on performance or provide solutions. The authors mention it's caused by mean-field variational inference but don't explore alternative inference methods that might mitigate this issue.

5. **Incremental Contribution**: While the deep structure is a reasonable extension, the novelty is somewhat incremental given prior work on deep Poisson models. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of how DDPFA differs from and improves upon existing deep Poisson approaches.

**Quality**: The technical content is sound, with a well-defined model and reasonable experimental design. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and incomplete discussion of limitations reduce the overall quality of the contribution.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, but some key implementation details are missing, which would hinder reproducibility.

**Originality**: The combination of deep learning with Poisson factorization is a reasonable extension, but the novelty is somewhat incremental compared to recent work. The specific "ladder-like architecture" is a novel contribution but lacks sufficient justification.

**Significance**: The model addresses a meaningful problem with practical applications, and the results suggest it could be useful in real-world settings. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the incremental nature of the contribution and lack of strong theoretical justification.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments**:

*Pro*: The model addresses a meaningful problem with comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets; the deep structure with ladder-like architecture is a thoughtful approach to capturing long-term dependencies; the results demonstrate clear advantages over baselines in most experiments.

*Con*: The contribution is incremental compared to recent deep Poisson models; the theoretical justification is weak; critical implementation details are missing; the "pruning" problem is acknowledged but not properly addressed.

**Recommendation**: Weak reject. The paper presents a reasonable extension to existing work on Poisson factor analysis for sequential data, but the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and theoretical grounding to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. The authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by:

1. Providing a more thorough theoretical analysis of the deep structure's necessity and the variational inference method
2. Addressing the "pruning" problem with either a solution or more detailed analysis
3. Adding a more comprehensive comparison with recent deep Poisson models
4. Including sufficient implementation details for reproducibility
5. Providing clearer justification for the model's novelty relative to existing work

With these improvements, the paper could make a stronger contribution to the field.
