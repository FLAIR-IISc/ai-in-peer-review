Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count matrices X ∈ RV×T+. As in Poisson Factorization (PF), it models xvt ~ Poisson(∑k θtk φvk λk ψv), where θtk are time-varying factor scores, φvk loadings, and λk, ψv are component- and feature-wise scales. The novelty is a deep temporal hierarchy over latent variables: θt depends on a window of lower-layer variables h(0)t−c:t, and h(n)t depends on a window of the layer above h(n+1)t−c:t via “implicit” conditional distributions parameterized by RNNs. Inference alternates: (i) mean-field VI with Poisson-multinomial augmentation yields conjugate Gamma updates for φ, ψ, λ, θ when the RNN outputs (e.g., shape parameters) are treated as fixed; (ii) training the RNNs via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that matches expected encoder/decoder outputs and reconstructions via squared error. Experiments on four synthetic and five real datasets compare DDPFA to PFA, PGDS, and an LSTM in terms of MSE and one-step-ahead PMSE.

This work relates to: Poisson/Gamma-based dynamic PF (e.g., PGDS), recurrent Poisson factorization (Hosseini et al., 2017), deep Poisson factor analysis and gamma belief networks (Gan et al., 2015; Zhou et al., 2016), dynamic topic models (Blei & Lafferty, 2006), neural autoregressive topic models (DocNADE), and more recent deep sequential latent-variable models with count likelihoods. The paper positions itself as combining PF with a deep, RNN-based temporal dependency structure.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important and challenging setting: long-range temporal dependence in high-dimensional sparse count time series.
- Attempts to leverage both conjugacy in PF and flexible temporal models (RNNs) for improved predictive performance.
- Empirical results span diverse datasets (international events, text corpus by year, epidemic counts, disasters, sheep population), and include both fitting and one-step prediction.
- The authors openly discuss observed issues (e.g., “pruning” and limited utility of deeper layers), which is constructive.

Weaknesses:
- Unclear and incomplete probabilistic specification. The “implicit distributions” p(θt | h(0)t−c:t) and p(h(n)t | h(n+1)t−c:t) are described as RNNs that output shape parameters for Gamma distributions, but the full generative model is not rigorously defined. It is not clear which parameters are fixed vs learned, how positivity and stability are enforced, and how the “posterior” Gamma for h(n) (with shape equal to sum of two neural nets) arises from any likelihood. The special-casing of the top layer is also unclear/inconsistent (Eq. 4).
- Objective mismatch and lack of theoretical grounding. The paper claims VI but does not present or optimize a valid ELBO. The “Probability AutoEncoder” trains encoder/decoder RNNs by minimizing squared errors between expectations of distributions (Eqs. 16–18), which is not a bound on marginal likelihood or a principled divergence. There is no guarantee that this objective is consistent with VI, and no analysis of bias.
- Derivations and notation issues. Several conditionals in Eq. 8/12 contain typos or ambiguous terms (e.g., repeated Φ, mismatch of indices, missing sums). The allocation update (Eq. 11) uses digamma expectations as standard, but the route from Eq. 3–4 to the Gamma conditionals for θ and h is not derived—αθtk is simply “calculated through a RNN.” Without an explicit ELBO and gradients, the proposed coordinate updates plus NN training are hard to justify.
- Evaluation metrics and baselines. Using MSE/PMSE on raw counts is a weak fit metric for probabilistic models; held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity is standard in PF/topic modeling. The LSTM baseline is not adapted to counts (no Poisson/negative binomial output/link), disadvantaging it. Comparisons omit recent strong baselines (e.g., Recurrent PF, Deep PF/GBN for sequences, dynamic Poisson/Gamma state space models with long memory). Hyperparameters (K, window c) are not cross-validated or justified; fairness to PGDS and LSTM is unclear.
- Clarity and presentation. Numerous grammar/typos, incomplete references (authors/venues/dates missing), and missing details (architecture, parameterization to enforce positivity, optimization settings, convergence criteria). Important figures/tables are referenced but not explained in detail; no uncertainty or statistical significance reported.
- The authors themselves report that deeper layers do not help much and that “pruning” occurs, which undermines the main motivation (deep structure for long-range dependencies). The proposed “ladder-like” fix is mentioned but not elaborated or evaluated rigorously.

Quality:
Given the lack of a clear generative model and a principled objective, the technical soundness is questionable. Claims of variational inference are not supported by an ELBO; the autoencoder-style losses are ad hoc. Experimental evidence is mixed and uses suboptimal metrics; important baselines are missing.

Clarity:
The paper is hard to follow. Precise definitions, consistent notation, and a formal objective are needed. Many equations and references need correction. Please provide a full ELBO, its terms, and gradients; or clearly state you use a different objective and justify it.

Originality:
Combining PF with deep temporal dependencies via RNNs is a sensible direction and has partial precedents. The specific idea of using RNNs to parameterize Gamma conditionals over time windows may be novel, but the absence of a principled training objective makes it hard to assess the contribution.

Significance:
Potentially valuable if formalized properly, but in its current form the impact is limited. The empirical gains, measured via MSE, are not sufficient to establish state-of-the-art performance.

Questions for the authors:
- Please provide the exact generative model: full factorization, parameterizations of all conditional distributions (including positivity constraints), and the role of λ, ψ versus absorbing them into Θ.
- What is the precise training objective? If it is VI, please write the ELBO and show how each update optimizes it. If it is the squared-error autoencoder, explain why this is appropriate and how it relates to likelihood.
- How are α and β for the Gamma distributions ensured positive? What RNN architectures and nonlinearities are used?
- Why does h(n) have a “posterior” Gamma with shape equal to sum of two neural networks (α + γ)? From which likelihood does γ arise?
- Report held-out predictive log-likelihoods and compare to recurrent PF, PGDS variants, DEEP PF/GBN for sequences. Include ablations on window size c, depth, and K with cross-validation.
- Provide code and sufficient details to reproduce results.

Pros:
- Tackles an important problem (dynamic count modeling with long-range dependencies).
- Interesting idea to enrich PF with deep temporal latent variables driven by RNNs.
- Broad set of datasets; some reported improvements.

Cons:
- Unclear and likely incorrect inference objective; no ELBO or likelihood-based training.
- Incomplete/ambiguous model specification and derivations.
- Weak evaluation metrics and baselines; unclear experimental fairness.
- Writing and references need substantial revision.
- Main claim (benefit of depth) not convincingly supported.

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: Medium-high.
