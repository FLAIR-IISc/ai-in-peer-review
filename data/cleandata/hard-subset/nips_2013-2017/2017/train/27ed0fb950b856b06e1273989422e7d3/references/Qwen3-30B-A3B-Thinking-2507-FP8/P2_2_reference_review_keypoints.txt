- Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA) with hierarchical RNN-modeled latent layers for sequential count data.  
- Uses variational inference and is evaluated on synthetic data plus five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population).  
- Technically sound with clear mathematical formulation and a well-structured inference algorithm.  
- Comprehensive experimental comparisons against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.  
- Shows strong performance on most datasets and captures complex temporal patterns better than PGDS and LSTM in many cases.  
- Theoretical justification for the deep architecture is weak and not convincingly demonstrated.  
- The loss function (Eq. 18) appears ad-hoc and lacks sufficient theoretical grounding.  
- Acknowledges the “pruning” problem (many latent dimensions are noisy) but does not adequately analyze or address its effects.  
- Uses mean-field variational inference, which may be inappropriate for capturing complex latent dependencies; alternatives are not explored.  
- Paper is generally clear, but does not clearly differentiate the proposed “deep” approach from prior deep Poisson factorization methods (e.g., RBM/SBN).  
- Explanation of the hierarchical structure (Eqs. 3–4) could benefit from more intuitive examples.  
- Contribution is incremental rather than novel; insufficient distinction from prior deep Poisson factorization work.  
- Significance is limited: mixed dataset performance (e.g., underperforms on ICEWS) and pruning issues reduce broad applicability.  
- Strengths: well-structured model, thorough experiments, good baseline comparisons, and honest acknowledgment of limitations.  
- Weaknesses: incremental novelty, weak theoretical motivation for depth, ad-hoc loss, limited empirical advantage over state-of-the-art, and insufficient differentiation from shallow/deep prior approaches.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak reject — technically competent but lacks the novelty and theoretical justification for NIPS acceptance.
