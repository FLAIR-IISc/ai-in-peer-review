## Review: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, aiming to capture complex temporal dependencies in sequentially observed count data by combining Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with recurrent neural networks. The core idea – leveraging deep learning to model the temporal evolution of latent factors in a count data setting – is compelling and addresses a known limitation of existing dynamic factor models like PGDS, which often rely on simplifying assumptions about temporal relationships. The approach builds upon a strong foundation of Bayesian factor analysis and integrates recent advancements in deep learning for probabilistic modeling.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of recurrent neural networks with PFA for modeling dynamic count data is a genuinely novel approach. While deep learning has been applied to similar problems (e.g., [8, 9, 10, 12] cited in the paper), the specific formulation of DDPFA, particularly the stacking of RNN layers to capture different temporal scales, feels fresh and promising.  This is a genuinely interesting direction.
* **Potential Significance:**  The promise of capturing more nuanced temporal dependencies could lead to improvements in a wide range of applications including recommendation systems, time series analysis, and text analysis (as highlighted in the introduction). The motivation for capturing long-time dependencies beyond simple Gamma-Gamma transitions (as noted with a critique of PGDS) is well articulated.
* **Comprehensive Model:** The paper demonstrates a good understanding of existing literature and positions DDPFA within that context.  The inclusion of feature-wise and component-wise sparsity parameters (Ψ and Λ) appears to be a thoughtful extension of standard PFA.
* **Experimental Validation:** The paper presents results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing DDPFA against several baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA). While not overwhelmingly superior, the results generally show competitive or improved performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Inference Complexity & Scalability:** The variational inference procedure, while standard, appears complex and potentially computationally expensive. The authors don’t offer much discussion on the practical scalability of this approach to very large datasets.  The lack of concrete computational cost analysis is a concern.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The model likely has a large number of hyperparameters (neural network architecture, learning rate, variational parameters, Gamma priors), and the sensitivity of performance to these choices is not thoroughly investigated.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While comparisons to baselines are provided, a more targeted ablation study would be valuable. For instance, how much performance gain is attributable to the deep structure *versus* simply using a single RNN layer?
* **Figure 2 and 3 Interpretation:** The visual comparison in Figure 2 is somewhat hand-wavy. It *claims* DDPFA learns less localized factors, but a more quantitative assessment of factor activation patterns would be stronger.
* **"Pruning" and Ladder Architecture Discussion:** The discussion of "pruning" and the mention of a ladder-like architecture towards the end feels somewhat tacked on. It would be stronger to integrate this discussion earlier and potentially explore a ladder architecture directly within the paper, given its potential to address the identified issue.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The core idea is genuinely novel and could open up new avenues for research in dynamic count data modeling.
* The paper demonstrates a solid understanding of relevant literature and provides a technically sound implementation.
* The experimental results, while not definitive, provide evidence that DDPFA can perform competitively with existing methods.
* The identified limitations are acknowledged, indicating author awareness.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the inference procedure and potential scalability issues are significant concerns.
* The lack of a detailed hyperparameter analysis and a comprehensive ablation study weakens the empirical validation.
* The discussion of potential improvements (ladder architecture, addressing pruning) feels like an admission of limitations rather than a fully explored solution.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I lean towards **acceptance** with minor revisions. The novelty and potential significance of the DDPFA model are compelling. While the paper isn't perfect, it represents a valuable contribution to the field. The authors should be encouraged to address the scalability concerns and conduct a more thorough ablation study in future work. I suggest the following revisions:

1. **Scalability Discussion:** Add a section discussing the computational cost of the inference procedure and potential strategies for scaling to larger datasets (e.g., stochastic variational inference, distributed computing).
2. **Ablation Study:** Include a more focused ablation study to assess the contribution of each component of the model (e.g., RNN depth, sparsity parameters).
3. **Factor Activation Quantification:** Provide a more quantitative analysis of factor activation patterns, rather than relying solely on visual inspection in Figure 2.
4. **Clarify Inference:**  Provide a clearer explanation of the coordinate ascent method and the rationale for the chosen mean-field approximation.

I believe that addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the NIPS community.  This is a bold approach and I think the program would benefit from seeing it presented.
