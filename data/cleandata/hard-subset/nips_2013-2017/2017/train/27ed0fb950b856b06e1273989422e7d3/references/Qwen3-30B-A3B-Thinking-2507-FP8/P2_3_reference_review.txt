

# Review: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

## Summary
This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, extending Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) for sequential count data by incorporating deep recurrent structures to capture both short-term and long-term temporal dependencies. The authors build upon existing dynamic Poisson models (PGDS, PFA) and deep learning approaches (Deep Exponential Families, Deep Poisson Factor Analysis) to introduce a hierarchical structure where latent variables are modeled through recurrent neural networks. The inference uses variational methods with a novel probability autoencoder component to train the neural networks. The model is evaluated on synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, ASP).

## Strengths

**Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a coherent attempt to combine deep learning with Bayesian Poisson factorization for sequential count data. The deep structure with multiple layers of latent variables (Equations 3-4) is a logical extension of previous work on dynamic Poisson models. The authors clearly identify the limitation of prior work in capturing long-term dependencies (e.g., PGDS assumes independence between θt-1 and θt+1 given θt), and their deep architecture aims to address this gap.

**Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive experimental comparison with three baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The results demonstrate consistent improvements over PFA and PGDS across most datasets, with particularly strong performance on the EBOLA and ASP datasets where long-term dependencies are critical. The ablation study (Table 3) on model depth and dimensionality provides valuable insights into the model's behavior.

**Clarity of Core Idea:** The main contribution—using deep recurrent structures to capture long-term temporal dependencies in count data—is clearly articulated. The explanation of how the deep structure differs from previous shallow approaches is well-motivated, and the figures (Figures 2-3) effectively illustrate the interpretability advantages of their model.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Novelty:** While the combination of deep learning with Poisson factorization is not entirely new (cited works [9, 10, 12, 23] already explore similar ideas), the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from these prior works. The "deep" aspect is primarily implemented through a single recurrent layer with minimal architectural innovation, which appears similar to existing deep Poisson factorization models. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of how their specific architecture differs from prior deep Poisson factorization approaches.

**Inadequate Baseline Comparison:** The comparison with LSTM is problematic as LSTM is a general sequence model not specifically designed for count data. The authors do not compare with other specialized sequence models for count data (e.g., Poisson Recurrent Neural Networks). Additionally, the LSTM implementation details are insufficiently described (e.g., number of layers, activation functions), making it difficult to assess whether the comparison is fair.

**Insufficient Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks theoretical analysis of why their deep architecture would better capture long-term dependencies than simpler approaches. The empirical results show improvements, but there's no analysis of how the depth of the network relates to the complexity of the temporal dependencies in the data. The ablation study (Table 3) shows diminishing returns with additional layers, suggesting the deep structure may not be necessary.

**Implementation Details:** Critical implementation details are missing, such as the specific neural network architectures (number of layers, units per layer, activation functions), optimization details (learning rates, batch sizes), and hyperparameter tuning procedures. The paper claims to use "stochastic gradient descent for the neural networks" for 10 epochs in each iteration, but this is insufficient for a robust comparison.

**Evaluation Metrics:** The paper uses MSE and PMSE as evaluation metrics, which are standard for regression tasks but not ideal for count data where overdispersion and sparsity are common challenges. The authors could have included metrics more relevant to count data analysis (e.g., negative log-likelihood, predictive log-probability).

## Overall Assessment

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear model and reasonable experimental evaluation, but the results are not sufficiently compelling to demonstrate a significant advancement. The experimental design lacks sufficient rigor in comparing with relevant baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and generally clear, but some technical details (particularly about the neural network architecture) are missing, making it difficult to fully assess the method.

**Originality:** The paper's main contribution is incremental. While combining deep learning with Poisson factorization is not novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how their specific architecture differs from or improves upon existing deep Poisson factorization models.

**Significance:** The problem addressed (modeling sequential count data) is important, but the improvements over existing methods are modest and not convincingly demonstrated. The paper does not present compelling evidence that their approach will significantly impact the field.

## Recommendation

I recommend *weak acceptance* for this paper. The work is technically sound and addresses a meaningful problem, but the novelty is limited and the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient depth. The paper would benefit from:
1. A more thorough comparison with prior deep Poisson factorization models
2. More detailed implementation and hyperparameter specifications
3. A stronger theoretical justification for the deep structure
4. More appropriate evaluation metrics for count data

While the results are promising, the paper doesn't demonstrate a clear, significant advancement over existing approaches. With the suggested improvements, this could become a stronger contribution to the field.

*Note: The paper's scope aligns with NIPS' focus on machine learning and statistics, so it is within the conference's scope.*
