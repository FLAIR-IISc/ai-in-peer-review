## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel extension to Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) incorporating recurrent neural networks to model temporal dependencies in count data. While the idea is conceptually sound and builds upon a solid foundation of related work, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in several areas, limited novelty beyond the combination of existing techniques, and insufficient experimental validation to demonstrate significant advantages over simpler baselines, particularly given the model complexity. I lean towards a **weak reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and clarify.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The authors address the problem of analyzing sequentially observed count vectors, a common scenario in various domains. They rightly identify the limitations of existing dynamic factor models like PGDS, which struggle to capture long-range dependencies due to simplifying assumptions.  The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), which utilizes recurrent neural networks within a PFA framework to learn complex temporal relationships. 

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, covering Bayesian factor analysis, dynamic Poisson models, and deep Bayesian models.  It correctly positions DDPFA as a contribution within the lineage of these works. However, the discussion of related work could benefit from a more nuanced exploration of existing recurrent neural network approaches specifically applied to count data (beyond those briefly mentioned), particularly within the context of time series analysis. 

**2. Strengths:**

* **Conceptually Sound:** The idea of leveraging RNNs to model the temporal dynamics within a PFA framework is a natural and potentially powerful extension.  The motivation for capturing complex temporal dependencies is well-articulated.
* **Solid Foundation:** The paper builds on well-established models like PFA and incorporates existing techniques like variational inference, providing a strong foundation.
* **Exploration of Different Datasets:**  The authors evaluate their model on a diverse set of datasets, including both synthetic and real-world data, which is commendable.
* **Addressing limitations of prior work:** The paper correctly identifies the shortcomings of existing approaches like PGDS and attempts to address them with the proposed deep structure.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Clarity:** The presentation suffers from a lack of clarity in several areas.  The notation is dense, and the connections between equations are not always immediately apparent.  The description of the variational inference procedure, particularly the connection between the different layers and the loss function (Eqs. 15-18), is difficult to follow. The explanation of the "ladder-like architecture" is also brief and lacks sufficient detail.
* **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of RNNs and PFA is not trivial, the core contribution feels incremental.  Applying established deep learning techniques (RNNs) to an existing Bayesian model does not necessarily constitute a breakthrough.  The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight what *specifically* about the DDPFA architecture is uniquely beneficial beyond simply adding depth.
* **Experimental Evaluation:**  The experimental results are mixed. While DDPFA performs well on some datasets, the improvements over baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) are not consistently substantial. Furthermore, the claim of "excellent results" seems overstated given the performance on the ICEWS dataset and the limited ablation studies.  The analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity is missing. The discussion of why LSTM struggles is superficial and doesn't explore potential mitigation strategies.
* **Computational Cost:** Deep models inherently have increased computational cost. The paper doesn’t discuss the computational demands of DDPFA compared to simpler baselines. This is important for practical applicability.
* **"Pruning" and Auxiliary MCMC:** The acknowledgment of "pruning" and the suggestion of using auxiliary MCMC is concerning.  It suggests the authors are aware of fundamental issues with the current variational inference approach and that the reported results may be affected.
* **Overly complex inference:** The inference procedure appears exceedingly complex. The benefit of this complexity isn’t convincingly demonstrated, and the potential for instability or convergence issues is not adequately addressed.



**4. Significance & Originality:**

The significance of the work is moderate. The proposed model has the potential to improve the analysis of dynamic count data, but the current results do not demonstrate a substantial leap forward.  The originality is limited; it’s a clever combination of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach.  The paper would benefit from a more critical self-assessment of its contribution to the field.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The idea of combining RNNs and PFA is reasonable and potentially useful.
* The paper explores a diverse set of datasets.
* The authors identify limitations of existing models and propose a solution.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of clarity and detailed explanations.
* Limited novelty beyond a combination of existing techniques.
* Inconsistent and sometimes modest experimental improvements.
* Complex inference procedure with potential instability (indicated by the "pruning" issue).
* Insufficient discussion of computational cost.



**6. Recommendations for Revision:**

* **Clarity:**  Rewrite key sections with a focus on clarity and conciseness. Provide more detailed explanations of the variational inference procedure and the loss function.
* **Novelty:**  More clearly articulate the unique benefits of the DDPFA architecture.  What specific aspect of the deep structure is crucial for performance?
* **Experiments:**  Conduct a more thorough experimental evaluation, including:
    * Ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (RNN layers, depth).
    * Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
    * Comparison to more recent and relevant RNN-based time series models.
    * A more rigorous analysis of the performance on the ICEWS dataset.
* **Computational Cost:** Include a discussion of the computational cost of DDPFA compared to simpler baselines.
* **Address "Pruning":**  Discuss the implications of the "pruning" issue and propose potential solutions. Explore alternative inference methods (e.g., auxiliary MCMC).
* **Simplify Inference:** If possible, simplify the inference procedure or provide a stronger justification for its complexity.




In conclusion, while the paper presents a conceptually interesting idea, it falls short of the quality expected for NIPS publication in its current form.  Significant revisions are needed to address the clarity, novelty, and experimental validation issues.  I recommend a weak reject and encourage the authors to resubmit a revised version after addressing these concerns.
