- Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), combining Poisson factor analysis with deep recurrent structures to model short- and long-term dependencies in sequential count data.

- Technical contribution: introduces a multi-layer latent deep recurrent architecture as an extension of dynamic Poisson models to capture longer temporal dependencies.

- Inference method: uses variational methods with a probability autoencoder component to train the neural networks.

- Experimental evaluation: compares DDPFA to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA on synthetic and five real-world datasets, showing consistent improvements over PFA and PGDS, especially on datasets with long-term dependencies (EBOLA, ASP).

- Ablation study: provides analysis of model depth and dimensionality, showing insights but diminishing returns with additional layers.

- Clarity: core idea and differences from shallow approaches are clearly presented, with helpful figures illustrating interpretability.

- Limited novelty: the approach is incremental and similar to existing deep Poisson factorization works; "deep" implementation is mainly a single recurrent layer without strong architectural innovation.

- Inadequate baseline comparison: LSTM comparison is problematic and lacks details; fails to compare to specialized sequence models for count data (e.g., Poisson RNNs).

- Insufficient theoretical justification: no formal analysis explaining why the deep architecture better captures long-term dependencies or how depth relates to temporal complexity.

- Missing implementation details: key NN architecture and optimization specifics (layers, units, activations, learning rates, batch sizes, tuning) are not provided.

- Evaluation metrics: uses MSE and PMSE which are suboptimal for count data; should include metrics like negative log-likelihood or predictive log-probability to address overdispersion and sparsity.

- Overall assessment: technically sound and within scope but incremental, with modest and not fully convincing improvements due to experimental and methodological gaps.

- Recommendation: weak accept, contingent on more thorough baseline comparisons, detailed implementation/hyperparameter reporting, stronger theoretical justification, and more appropriate count-data metrics.
