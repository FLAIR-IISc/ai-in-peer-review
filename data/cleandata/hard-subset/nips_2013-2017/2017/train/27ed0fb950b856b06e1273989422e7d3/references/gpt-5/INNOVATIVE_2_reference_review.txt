Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA), a hybrid model for sequential count matrices that blends Poisson factorization with a deep, recurrent, hierarchical latent structure. Observations are modeled as Poisson with rates factored into feature loadings Φ, time-varying factor scores Θ, and additional feature- and component-wise scaling variables Ψ and Λ to encourage sparsity and smoothness. Temporal dependence is modeled via a multi-layer hierarchy h(n)t, where Gamma distributions parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs) provide “implicit” priors/posteriors over the Gamma shape parameters governing Θ and h(n). Inference alternates between (i) mean-field coordinate ascent updates leveraging Gamma–Poisson conjugacy (with α, γ parameters coming from RNNs) and (ii) training the RNNs using a “Probability AutoEncoder” loss that encourages consistency between a forward prior and a backward “posterior” network via squared-error losses between distributional expectations. Experiments on four synthetic and five real datasets compare DDPFA with PGDS, PFA, and LSTM. The authors report improved MSE/PMSE on most tasks and show qualitative factors in ICEWS.

Strengths
- Ambitious direction: coupling conjugate count modeling with deep, learned temporal structure via RNN-parameterized Gamma priors/posteriors is an appealing idea for capturing long-range, nonlinear dependencies in sparse sequential counts.
- Retains conditional conjugacy for Poisson–Gamma blocks, enabling efficient mean-field updates despite the deep temporal architecture.
- The ladder-like bidirectional structure (prior from upper layer, “posterior” from lower layer) is a sensible architectural response to known weaknesses of hierarchical VAEs (e.g., pruning).
- Empirical results suggest gains over PGDS/PFA and often LSTM; qualitative ICEWS factors are interpretable and persistent over time, addressing a known limitation of some dynamic topic/factor models.

Weaknesses
- Objective is not well-founded. The “Probability AutoEncoder” training replaces a principled ELBO with an ad-hoc squared-error loss between expectations of two distributions, and a reconstruction loss for Θ (and later X). The expression ∫ p(Θ|H(0)) p(H(0)|Θ) dH(0) is not a standard evidence objective. Without a valid lower bound or likelihood-based objective, there are no guarantees that the learned RNNs correspond to posteriors/priors consistent with the generative model. The claim that “differentiable VB is not suitable” is unconvincing given existing reparameterizations or augmentation for Gamma/Poisson and widely used black-box VI.
- Clarity and rigor issues: there are numerous typos, missing/misnumbered references, and several equations mix indices or overload notation (e.g., Eq. 8-10). The MF updates for the deep variables rely on α, γ from RNNs but the derivations are only sketched. The probabilistic semantics of the “posterior network” are unclear (is it amortized inference or a learned regularizer?).
- Identifiability: introducing Ψ and Λ in addition to Φ and Θ creates scale non-identifiability (ΦΘTΛΨ vs. re-scaled decompositions). The paper does not discuss constraints or regularization to resolve this beyond weak Gamma priors.
- Temporal modeling: the window size c is small (c=4 in experiments), and long-range dependence is argued to emerge by stacking layers, but no ablation shows that increasing c (or depth) improves long-horizon prediction; indeed Table 3 suggests additional layers often do not help.
- Evaluation: relies on MSE/PMSE; for count models, held-out log-likelihood/perplexity is a standard metric. There is no calibration, uncertainty quantification, or ablations for c, K, Λ/Ψ. Important baselines are missing (e.g., Recurrent Poisson Factorization for recommendation; deep Poisson factor models with RNN/temporal structure; dynamic deep topic models). Runtime and scalability are not reported.
- Reproducibility: key details of the RNNs (architecture, activations, parameterization of Gamma shape/rate, optimization settings) and the full inference schedule are insufficient. Figures/tables are referenced but not fully included; references are incomplete.

Relation to prior work
The paper is positioned relative to PGDS, PFA, DEFs, GBNs, and some deep/recurrent models, but misses or does not compare against several closely related works on recurrent Poisson factorization and dynamic deep count models. The proposed idea of using neural networks to parameterize Gamma priors is related to amortized variational families and neural parameterizations in deep exponential families; the ladder idea is connected to Ladder VAE. The novelty here is the specific combination for dynamic count matrices with conjugate MF updates plus a bespoke autoencoder objective, but the theoretical footing is weaker than contemporary amortized VI approaches.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide a proper ELBO (or surrogate bound) that includes the neural parameterizations and is optimized end-to-end? Why not use reparameterized or augmented Gamma distributions (or implicit reparam) and amortized inference?
2. How do you address scale non-identifiability among Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ? Do you normalize factors or impose constraints?
3. Why is c fixed at 4? How does performance vary with c and depth? Can you show long-horizon forecasting beyond one step?
4. Please compare against recurrent Poisson factorization and other neural dynamic count models, and report held-out log-likelihood/perplexity.
5. Provide architectural details, training schedules, and runtime/memory comparisons.

Minor comments
- Numerous typos and grammatical issues; several references are incomplete or mismatched.
- Eq. 7: clarify notation for normalized rates and include expectations explicitly.
- Clarify the parameterization of Gamma (shape/rate vs. shape/scale) consistently throughout.

Verdict
While the high-level idea—deep, RNN-parameterized Gamma priors within Poisson factorization for long-range temporal structure—is interesting and potentially impactful, the current paper lacks a principled and clear inference objective, has clarity/reproducibility issues, and omits important baselines/metrics. Given these concerns, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.

Recommendation: Reject (novel direction but insufficiently rigorous and validated)
Confidence: Moderate
