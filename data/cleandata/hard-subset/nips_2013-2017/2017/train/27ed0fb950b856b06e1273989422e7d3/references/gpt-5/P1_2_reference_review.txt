Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for sequential count data. Starting from Poisson factorization X ~ Poisson(Φ Θ^T), the model introduces two additional positive scale variables: feature-wise ψ_v and component-wise λ_k, so that x_vt ~ Poisson(∑_k θ_tk φ_vk λ_k ψ_v). To capture temporal dependencies, the authors posit a deep hierarchy of latent K-dimensional variables h^(n)_t, with windowed temporal conditioning p(h^(n)_t | h^(n+1)_{t−c:t}) parameterized by RNNs; the bottom layer provides shape parameters α_θ_t for Gamma priors on θ_tk. Inference uses a two-stage approach: (i) mean-field variational updates exploiting Gamma–Poisson conjugacy for Φ, Λ, Ψ, Θ, and h, assuming the RNN outputs are fixed; (ii) a “probability autoencoder” training of the RNN parameters via squared-error losses between expectations of prior/posterior networks and between data and reconstruction.

The paper is related to: Poisson factorization and gamma/negative binomial processes (Zhou et al.), dynamic Poisson-Gamma models (PGDS), deep Poisson FA and deep exponential families (Gan et al., Ranganath et al.), gamma belief networks, and temporal models like recurrent PF for recommendation. The idea of using neural networks to parameterize conditional distributions of latent variables is similar in spirit to deep latent variable time-series models (e.g., VRNN, Deep Markov Models), though such works are not cited. The paper’s novelty lies in stacking a deep, windowed RNN-driven prior over PF factors and training with a bespoke autoencoder-like loss.

Strengths
- Tackles an important setting: high-dimensional, sparse, sequential count data with long-range dependencies.
- Conceptually interesting combination of PF with a deep temporal prior that could capture richer dependencies than first-order Markov gamma chains.
- Empirical results on synthetic and several real datasets suggest improved MSE/PMSE over PGDS, PFA, and LSTM in many cases.
- The discussion of hierarchical latent variable pitfalls (pruning, limited utility of deeper layers) and the attempt to mitigate with a ladder-like architecture shows awareness of practical issues.

Weaknesses and technical concerns (Quality)
- Objective derivation: The “probability autoencoder” losses (Eqs. 15–18) are heuristic squared-error objectives between expectations of distributions, not derived as an ELBO or any valid bound on the marginal likelihood. There is no guarantee of optimizing a probabilistically meaningful objective once network parameters are updated, and coordinate-ascent monotonicity is broken.
- Misuse of “implicit distribution”: The conditionals are explicit parametric Gammas whose shape parameters are outputs of RNNs. Calling them “implicit” is misleading.
- Conjugacy claims and updates: While conditional conjugacy holds if Gamma priors with RNN-shaped parameters are treated as fixed, the paper omits a complete ELBO, gradients, and convergence properties. Several update equations contain notational errors (e.g., Eq. 7 appears to double-count φ_v; Eq. 8 repeats Φ; dot-sum notations xv·k, x··k are undefined; Eq. 11 logs on rates are unusual).
- Likelihood mismatch in training: For count data with Poisson likelihood, using squared error on rates/reconstructions is a poor surrogate to the Poisson log-likelihood; this may bias learning and undermines probabilistic coherence.
- Temporal modeling: The temporal context is restricted to a fixed window c at each layer; it is unclear how the model captures true long-range dependencies beyond c (and c×depth), and no ablation on c or depth vs. sequence length is provided.
- Overparameterization/identifiability: Introducing both λ_k and ψ_v in addition to Φ and Θ risks identifiability issues. Priors (Gamma(1,2)) are weak and there is no shrinkage or sparsity-inducing prior discussion.
- Experimental evaluation:
  - Metrics: MSE/PMSE on counts are nonstandard for PF; predictive log-likelihood/perplexity or held-out likelihood is typical and more appropriate.
  - Baselines: Missing comparisons to strong deep latent time-series baselines with Poisson likelihood/outputs (e.g., Deep Markov Models, VRNN/STORN variants), and to deep PF models (DEF, gamma belief networks with recurrent components).
  - Fairness and details: LSTM applied to raw counts without a link/likelihood; model/architecture details (RNN type, units, activations) are insufficient; no runtime or computational budget comparisons; K selection not justified or cross-validated.
  - Reproducibility: No ELBO, no code, limited hyperparameter tuning strategy. Tables/figures referenced do not clarify whether improvements are statistically significant.

Clarity
- The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors, ambiguous notation, and typos that impede understanding (examples above). Important elements (network architectures, training schedules, precise loss weighting) are under-specified. References are incomplete/misaligned (missing years/venues; some key related work on deep latent time-series not cited). The Bernoulli–Poisson link definition has an off-by-one indicator (should be 1(x≥1), not 1(x>1)).

Originality
- Combining PF with a deep temporal prior learned by RNNs is a reasonable and timely idea but not entirely novel; related deep/recurrent latent variable models exist in the literature. The specific ladder-like, windowed design and the “probability autoencoder” loss are the distinctive elements, but the latter lacks theoretical justification.

Significance
- Potentially useful if shown to deliver consistent improvements under proper probabilistic training and fair baselines. As presented, due to methodological and evaluation shortcomings, it is hard to assess impact; the contribution does not yet clearly advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Questions for the authors
- Can you derive a principled ELBO for the full model, including network parameters, and optimize it (e.g., with reparameterization/implicit reparameterization for Gamma) instead of L2 losses?
- How does performance change when optimizing Poisson log-likelihood (or deviance) rather than squared error?
- Please clarify the exact temporal receptive field and how long-range dependencies beyond window size are captured.
- Provide complete and corrected update equations and the full ELBO expression.
- Include stronger baselines (DMM/VRNN-style with Poisson likelihood; deep PF variants), predictive log-likelihood metrics, ablations on c, depth, K, λ/ψ inclusion, and runtime.
- Specify architectural details and release code.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Replace the heuristic “probability autoencoder” with a principled variational objective; amortize inference if desired.
- Clean up notation, equations, and references; add missing related work (VRNN, DMM, LVAE).
- Use appropriate evaluation metrics for count models and broader baselines; ensure fair comparisons.
- Address identifiability/shrinkage (e.g., hierarchical priors; normalization constraints).
- Provide thorough ablations and sensitivity analyses; discuss uncertainty calibration.

Pros vs. cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important problem; interesting model idea; promising empirical signals.
Cons:
- Heuristic, ungrounded training objective; clarity/notation issues; incomplete related work; limited, nonstandard evaluation; missing strong baselines; reproducibility concerns.
