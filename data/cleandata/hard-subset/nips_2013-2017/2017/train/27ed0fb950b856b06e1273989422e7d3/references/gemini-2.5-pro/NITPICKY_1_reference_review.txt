**Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"**

**Summary**
This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a hierarchical Bayesian model designed to analyze sequential count data. The core idea is to extend the standard Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) framework by modeling the temporal evolution of the factor scores (Θ) with a deep, multi-layered structure. Each layer in this hierarchy utilizes a recurrent neural network (RNN) to parameterize the conditional distributions of its latent variables, conditioned on the layer above. This deep structure is intended to capture more complex and long-range temporal dependencies than existing models like the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), which typically rely on a first-order Markov assumption. Inference is handled with a two-stage procedure: a coordinate-ascent mean-field variational approach to update the latent variables, and a separate optimization step based on a "Probability AutoEncoder" to train the parameters of the RNNs. The model's efficacy is demonstrated on several synthetic and real-world datasets, where it generally outperforms baselines including PFA, PGDS, and LSTM.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novelty of the Core Idea:** The primary contribution—integrating a deep, RNN-based generative process for the temporal factor scores within a PFA model—is conceptually appealing. It provides a principled way to move beyond the limitations of simple Markovian dynamics and potentially capture richer temporal patterns.
2.  **Empirical Performance:** The model shows strong quantitative results across a diverse set of synthetic and real-world datasets, often achieving lower fitting and prediction errors than established baselines. This suggests that the proposed deep dynamic structure is effective in practice.
3.  **Honest Discussion of Limitations:** The authors commendably include a discussion of the model's shortcomings, such as the "pruning" phenomenon where latent dimensions are ignored by the optimizer, and the observation that simply adding more layers does not always improve performance. This self-awareness strengthens the paper.

**Weaknesses**
The paper, in its current state, suffers from a substantial number of flaws in clarity, technical soundness, and presentation, which collectively undermine its contributions.

1.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally difficult to read and understand.
    *   The model description in Section 2 is opaque. The precise mechanism by which the RNNs define the "implicit probability distribution" `p(h(n)t |·)` is not clarified until the inference section. Key details, such as the RNNs outputting the shape parameters of a Gamma distribution, should be part of the model definition.
    *   The notation is dense and at times inconsistent. For instance, the time windows used for the prior (`t-c:t`) and the auxiliary posterior (`t:t+c`) are confusing and not adequately justified.
    - The writing style is replete with grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., "fanatical data" presumably meaning financial data; "1000 times gibbs sampling").

2.  **Technical Soundness of Inference:** The inference section contains critical errors and poorly justified methodological choices.
    *   **Incorrect Objective Function:** Equation (6) incorrectly specifies the objective as minimizing KL(p||q), whereas variational inference minimizes KL(q||p). This is a fundamental error in the formulation.
    *   **Ad-Hoc Loss for Neural Networks:** The training procedure for the neural networks, termed "Probability AutoEncoder," is not derived from the variational objective (the ELBO). The loss function in Equation (16) is a heuristic combination of L2 norms on the expectations of latent variables. The justification "similar to variational AutoEncoder" is insufficient, as VAEs are derived from a principled lower bound on the log-likelihood. Equation (15), which supposedly motivates this, is mathematically nonsensical as written: `∫ p(Θ|H(0))p(H(0)|Θ)dH(0)`. This ad-hoc procedure makes it unclear what objective the overall algorithm is truly optimizing.
    *   The description of the inference steps is convoluted, with concepts like `h(-1)tk`, `ffeed`, and `fback` being introduced without sufficient preamble, making the algorithm nearly impossible to reproduce from the text alone.

3.  **Formatting and Professionalism:** The manuscript exhibits a lack of care that is inappropriate for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Incomplete References:** A significant number of the citations in the bibliography are incomplete, missing authors, venues, years, or page numbers (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 6, 8, 9, 11] and many others). This is a major oversight.
    *   **Submission Artifacts:** The paper includes a footer identifying it as a "NIPS 2017" paper, which is improper for a submission under review.
    *   **Missing Content:** The discussion relies heavily on Figures 2 and 3 and Table 3, none of which were included in the provided text. Their absence makes it impossible to verify the qualitative analysis and the ablation study results.

4.  **Experimental Rigor:** While the results are promising, the analysis is superficial. Claims about why baselines fail (e.g., "it is a tough challenge for LSTM network to memorize enough useful patterns while... the dimension of the data is particular high") are speculative and not substantiated with any analysis.

**Recommendation: Rejection**

While the paper presents a promising research direction, the execution is deeply flawed. The lack of clarity in the model and inference descriptions, coupled with fundamental technical errors in the variational formulation and an ad-hoc training objective, makes the work difficult to trust or build upon. Furthermore, the pervasive formatting issues, particularly the incomplete bibliography, suggest a rushed preparation process that falls well below the standard expected for NIPS.

For the paper to be reconsidered, it would require a complete and thorough revision. This would involve:
1.  A ground-up rewrite of Sections 2 and 3 to clearly and correctly define the model and the inference procedure. The training objective for the RNNs should be rigorously derived from a single, coherent probabilistic framework (e.g., the ELBO).
2.  A meticulous proofreading to correct all grammatical errors, typos, and formatting inconsistencies.
3.  A complete and correctly formatted bibliography.
4.  A more rigorous analysis of the experimental results, replacing speculative claims with evidence-based arguments.

The core idea is valuable, but the current manuscript is not ready for publication.
