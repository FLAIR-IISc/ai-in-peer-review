

# Peer Review: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

## Summary
This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequentially observed count data, such as time series of word counts, event frequencies, or recommendation logs. The authors extend traditional Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS) by incorporating deep recurrent structures to capture complex long-term temporal dependencies. The model uses a hierarchical latent variable structure where each layer's distribution is modeled via recurrent neural networks, and employs variational inference for parameter estimation. The approach builds upon prior work in deep probabilistic models (e.g., Deep Exponential Families) and Bayesian factor analysis for count data, aiming to overcome limitations in previous models that struggle with long-term dependencies.

## Strengths

1. **Problem Relevance**: The paper addresses an important problem in time series analysis of count data, which has applications in recommendation systems, text analysis, and event prediction. The motivation for capturing long-term dependencies is well-justified.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors evaluate their model on both synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population), comparing against relevant baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA). The results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines in most cases.

3. **Clear Model Description**: The model architecture and inference procedure are well-described with appropriate mathematical notation. The introduction of the "ladder-like architecture" to address hierarchical model limitations is a thoughtful contribution.

4. **Honest Limitations Discussion**: The authors acknowledge limitations of their approach, particularly the "pruning" issue where some latent dimensions contain only noise, and discuss potential solutions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Foundation**: While the model is technically sound, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis. There's no discussion of convergence properties, theoretical guarantees, or model capacity analysis. The paper would benefit from more rigorous theoretical grounding.

2. **Novelty Questionable**: The core idea of combining deep learning with Poisson factorization for temporal data is not sufficiently novel. The paper cites relevant prior work (e.g., Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9, 10], Deep Poisson Factor Modeling [12]), but doesn't clearly differentiate how DDPFA advances beyond these approaches. The "ladder-like architecture" is mentioned but not sufficiently contrasted with existing hierarchical approaches.

3. **Inadequate Experimental Comparison**: The comparison with baselines is limited. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the performance differences. The LSTM comparison is particularly weak, as the authors don't explain why LSTM (a standard time series model) performed poorly on the datasets, making it difficult to assess whether DDPFA's advantages are due to the model structure or specific implementation choices.

4. **Lack of Computational Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity or training time compared to baselines. For a model with deep neural networks, this is a significant omission for a NIPS submission.

5. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims "excellent results" in prediction and fitting tasks without providing sufficient context about how much improvement is achieved over baselines. The performance gains in some cases (e.g., ICEWS prediction) are modest.

## Assessment Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound in its model specification and implementation. The experimental results support the main claims, but the lack of theoretical analysis and limited statistical testing of results weakens the quality assessment. The authors are honest about limitations but don't provide sufficient evidence to support their claims.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation. However, some concepts (like the "ladder-like architecture" and how it differs from standard hierarchical models) could be clarified. The explanation of the neural network components in the inference procedure could be more detailed.

**Originality**: The combination of deep learning with Poisson factorization for temporal data is somewhat novel, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from recent work in this area (e.g., Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9, 10], Deep Poisson Factor Modeling [12]). The novelty appears incremental rather than transformative.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is important, and the model shows promise for applications in time series analysis of count data. However, the improvements over existing methods are not substantial enough to represent a major advance in the field. The paper doesn't provide compelling evidence that this model will be widely adopted or that it significantly advances the state of the art.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a reasonable extension of existing work on Poisson factorization for sequential data, with promising empirical results on several datasets. However, the novelty is limited, the theoretical foundation is weak, and the experimental evaluation doesn't fully justify the claims of significant improvements over baselines.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. More rigorous theoretical analysis
2. Clearer differentiation from recent deep Poisson factorization approaches
3. More comprehensive experimental analysis with statistical significance testing
4. Detailed computational complexity analysis
5. Better explanation of how the "ladder-like architecture" differs from existing hierarchical approaches

While the paper has merit, it doesn't represent a significant contribution to the field that would warrant acceptance at NIPS. The incremental nature of the contribution, combined with the limited theoretical foundation, suggests it would be better suited for a more application-focused venue.

**Pro**: Addressing a relevant problem with a well-structured model, comprehensive evaluation on multiple datasets, honest discussion of limitations.

**Con**: Limited novelty compared to recent work, weak theoretical foundation, inadequate experimental comparison, no computational analysis.

**Overall Recommendation**: Reject. While the paper is technically sound, the contribution does not represent a significant advance over existing work in the field, and the paper would benefit from substantial revisions to address the weaknesses identified above.
