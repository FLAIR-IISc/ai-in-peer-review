Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count matrices that builds on Poisson Factorization (PF). The observed counts xvt are modeled via a PF with feature- and component-specific Gamma priors (φ, ψ, λ) and time-varying factor scores θt. To capture temporal dependencies beyond first-order Markov assumptions in prior PF/PFA/PGDS models, the authors introduce a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t, with recurrent neural networks parameterizing “implicit” conditional Gamma distributions across a sliding time window of size c. Inference alternates between mean-field VI updates for conjugate Gamma-Poisson components (treating neural network outputs as fixed prior parameters) and learning the network parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that aligns prior and auxiliary posterior networks using L2 losses on expectations. Experiments on synthetic datasets and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disasters, Sheep) compare DDPFA against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE and one-step PMSE, with DDPFA often reported as best.

Relation to prior work
The model is related to Poisson factorization and Gamma-Negative Binomial process models (e.g., Zhou et al.), dynamic Poisson-gamma systems with transitions (PGDS), deep latent variable models (DEFs, deep PF), and RNN-based sequential latent models (VRNN, Deep Markov Models). The paper cites several of these lines, but comparisons are limited to PGDS, PFA, and a vanilla LSTM, and the proposed “Probability AutoEncoder” deviates from standard ELBO-based amortized VI.

Strengths
- Tackles an important and practical problem: modeling high-dimensional, sparse sequential counts with temporal dependence.
- The idea of using neural networks to parameterize time-varying Gamma priors for PF is natural and potentially powerful, while retaining some conjugacy for tractable updates.
- The model tries to combine deep hierarchical temporal structure with PF, which could, in principle, capture longer-term dependencies than shallow Markovian dynamics.
- Empirical section covers a range of datasets (including ICEWS and NIPS) and includes both fitting and prediction tasks.

Weaknesses
Methodological soundness:
- The inference scheme is not derived from a single, coherent objective. The mean-field coordinate updates are standard for conjugate Gamma-Poisson models, but the temporal priors are “implicit” functions of other latent variables via RNNs; the paper treats their outputs as fixed during coordinate updates and then learns networks with an ad-hoc “Probability AutoEncoder” objective (Eqs. 15–18). This is not an ELBO, has no guarantee of lower-bounding the log marginal likelihood, and lacks theoretical justification. It is unclear what quantity is optimized overall or whether the procedure converges to a well-defined optimum.
- The loss functions (L2 between expectations of Gamma-distributions) ignore distributional uncertainty and are not equivalent to minimizing a KL or maximizing a bound; this undermines the probabilistic semantics.
- The claim that “differentiable variational Bayes is not suitable” due to sampling cost is unconvincing. Reparameterized or implicit gradients for Gamma/Poisson (e.g., augmentation, implicit reparam) are standard; alternatives like black-box VI or SVI exist and should be considered if scalability is a concern.

Clarity and correctness:
- The notation and exposition are often confusing or inconsistent (e.g., Eq. 7 parameters; some λ/φ/ψ subscripts; repeated βθ/βh without clear definitions; mixing “implicit” priors with explicit Gamma conjugacy). Several equations appear to have typos or mismatched symbols, making it hard to verify correctness.
- The hierarchical temporal structure is insufficiently specified. The role of the window c, the exact architecture of RNNs per layer, the conditioning sets (some forward/backward networks), and how h(N) differs from other layers are not clearly and consistently defined.
- The proposed “ladder-like” modification is mentioned post hoc to address pruning, but is not described precisely enough to reproduce or to assess its contribution.

Empirical evaluation:
- Evaluation metrics (MSE and PMSE on counts) are not standard for count models; predictive log-likelihood or held-out perplexity for Poisson factorization is more appropriate. Without likelihood-based metrics and statistical tests, the claimed improvements are hard to assess.
- Baselines are insufficient and potentially unfair. Dynamic topic models, temporal Poisson factorization for recommendations, Hawkes/GPP intensity models, Deep Markov Models/VRNNs, DEF-based temporal topic models, and Recurrent Poisson Factorization should be included or discussed. Hyperparameter tuning seems asymmetric (e.g., fixed K=10 vs. unknown tuning for baselines), and runtime/complexity is not reported.
- Long-range modeling claims are not substantiated: the window c is small (4), and there is no ablation over c, number of layers, or explicit analysis of the receptive field. Reported “long-term” gains appear anecdotal.
- Key artifacts (figures and tables referenced) are missing in the text provided; even assuming they exist, the paper lacks variance across runs, significance, and ablations isolating the effect of the deep temporal prior vs. other components.

Reproducibility:
- Many implementation details are missing: network architectures, optimization hyperparameters per dataset, initialization, stopping criteria, and data preprocessing. No code is provided. The alternating training scheme requires careful scheduling that is not fully specified.

Originality and significance:
- Combining PF with NN-parameterized temporal priors is an incremental idea relative to prior PF/PGDS/DEF/VRNN/DMM lines. The introduced “Probability AutoEncoder” seems ad hoc and, without theoretical grounding or clear empirical advantage over standard amortized VI, does not convincingly advance the state of the art.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you derive a single ELBO for the full model (including networks) and optimize it via amortized VI? If not, please justify the current loss with theory (e.g., upper/lower bounds) and provide convergence diagnostics.
2. Why not use standard reparameterization or implicit reparameterization for Gamma to train end-to-end? What is the actual computational bottleneck?
3. Please report predictive Poisson log-likelihood/perplexity and include stronger baselines (DMM/VRNN, dynamic topic models, recurrent PF).
4. Provide ablations: effect of c, number of layers, removal of ψ/λ, removing deep hierarchy (just RNN on θ), and the ladder variant.
5. Clarify and correct equations (especially Eqs. 7–14) and precisely define all rates/shapes and conditioning sets.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant problem (dynamic count modeling).
- Sensible intuition: neural temporal priors for PF may capture richer dependencies.
- Some empirical indications of improved PMSE/MSE.

Cons
- Inference objective is ad hoc and not a principled bound; unclear convergence.
- Clarity issues and possible technical inaccuracies in derivations.
- Evaluation uses nonstandard metrics, limited baselines, and lacks ablations/statistics.
- Limited novelty relative to established sequential latent-variable models.

Recommendation
Reject. While the problem is important, the paper, in its current form, lacks methodological rigor (no coherent ELBO or principled training objective), clarity, and thorough empirical validation against established baselines with appropriate metrics. I encourage the authors to (i) reformulate training around a well-defined ELBO with amortized inference, (ii) provide clearer, correct derivations and implementation details, and (iii) substantially strengthen the empirical evaluation. Confidence: high.
