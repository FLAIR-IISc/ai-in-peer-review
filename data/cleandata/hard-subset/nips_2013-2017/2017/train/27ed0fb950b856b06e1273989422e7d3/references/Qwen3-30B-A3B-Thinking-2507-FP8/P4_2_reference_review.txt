

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), an extension of Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) designed for sequentially observed count vectors. Building on prior work in dynamic Poisson factorization [1, 2], the authors introduce a deep neural network structure to capture long-time dependence in time series count data. The model uses recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions and employs variational inference for parameter estimation. The authors evaluate their model on four synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population), comparing it with PGDS, LSTM, and PFA baselines.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound model with clear mathematical formulation. The inference algorithm combines mean-field variational inference with neural networks to approximate the posterior distribution. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic and real-world datasets with multiple baselines. The model demonstrates improved performance over baselines on most datasets, particularly in capturing long-time dependencies (e.g., Figure 3 shows better long-time activation of factors). However, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of convergence properties or statistical guarantees, and the comparison with LSTM is not entirely fair since LSTM is not designed specifically for count data. The paper identifies limitations like "pruning" and "uselessness of adding network layers" but doesn't provide theoretical explanations for these phenomena.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. However, some aspects could be clarified: the relationship between the deep structure and time dependence is not fully explained, and the motivation for using Gamma distributions (as opposed to Dirichlet, commonly used in similar models) could be strengthened. The paper would benefit from more intuitive explanations of complex equations.

**Originality:** The core idea of using deep neural networks to capture long-time dependence in Poisson factorization is novel. The authors properly cite related work, including Deep Exponential Families [8] and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9, 10], and position their work relative to these. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from prior deep Poisson factorization approaches. The comparison with existing deep Poisson factorization models is not detailed enough, making it difficult to assess the specific novelty of the proposed architecture.

**Significance:** The problem of analyzing sequentially observed count vectors is important in applications like recommendation systems, text analysis, and network analysis. The model's ability to capture long-time dependencies addresses a clear limitation of existing methods, as evidenced by improved performance on several datasets. The interpretability aspect (e.g., Figure 3 showing long-time activation of factors) adds practical value. However, the model doesn't consistently outperform baselines (e.g., on the ICEWS prediction task), and the significance would be strengthened by more convincing evidence of practical utility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The model addresses a genuine limitation in existing dynamic Poisson factorization models.
- The experimental results demonstrate improved performance on multiple datasets.
- The model provides better interpretability through long-time factor activation patterns.
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The theoretical foundation is underdeveloped without convergence or statistical guarantees.
- The comparison with LSTM is not entirely fair since LSTM is not designed for count data.
- The novelty relative to existing deep Poisson factorization models is not sufficiently articulated.
- The experimental evaluation lacks statistical significance testing.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of dynamic Poisson factorization by introducing a deep structure to capture long-time dependence. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened and the novelty relative to prior work more clearly articulated, the model demonstrates improved performance on most datasets compared to baselines. The authors provide thoughtful discussion of limitations, relating them to prior work on hierarchical latent variable models. The paper meets the technical and empirical standards expected at NIPS, though with room for improvement in theoretical justification and comparative analysis.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical foundation and clarify the novelty relative to existing deep Poisson factorization models.
