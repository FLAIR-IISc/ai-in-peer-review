

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes a "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model" (DDPFA) that integrates recurrent neural networks into Poisson factor analysis to model sequential count data. The authors aim to capture complex temporal dependencies through a deep structure, claiming this addresses limitations in previous shallow models like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) and standard Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA). The paper builds upon existing work on Poisson factorization, neural network-based extensions, and deep hierarchical models.

The main novelty claims center on using a deep structure with recurrent neural networks to model the transition between time steps, enabling the capture of "long-time dependence" that previous shallow models cannot. The paper presents a variational inference approach where neural networks represent implicit distributions for the latent variables, and introduces a "Probability AutoEncoder" for training the networks.

The authors validate their model on four synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population), comparing against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA baselines. They report improvements in both fitting and prediction tasks, particularly for datasets with complex temporal patterns. The paper acknowledges limitations, including "pruning" (where latent dimensions become noise) and the "uselessness of adding network layers," referencing related work that suggests deeper layers may not be necessary.

However, I have significant concerns about the novelty and significance of the contribution:

1. **Lack of clear theoretical novelty**: The paper doesn't convincingly distinguish its deep structure from existing deep Poisson factorization approaches (e.g., Deep Poisson Factor Analysis based on RBM/SBN [9,10] or Deep Poisson Factor Modeling with recurrent structure [12]). The proposed architecture appears to be a straightforward application of deep learning to an existing model rather than a fundamentally new approach to sequential count modeling.

2. **Incremental contribution**: The improvements shown in the experiments (Tables 1-2) are modest, and the paper fails to demonstrate that these improvements specifically arise from the "deep" aspect rather than simply adding more parameters. The authors acknowledge that "expanding the dimension in bottom layer is more useful than in upper layers" and that "adding more layers" doesn't significantly improve performance (Table 3), which undermines their main novelty claim.

3. **Insufficient justification for deep structure**: The paper doesn't provide a strong theoretical argument for why a deep structure is necessary for capturing long-time dependence in count data. Previous work (e.g., Deep Exponential Families [8]) has shown benefits of deep structures for certain data types, but the paper doesn't explain why this is particularly relevant for sequential count data.

4. **Undermined novelty by acknowledged limitations**: The paper's own findings about the limitations of the deep structure (Table 3) directly contradict their main novelty claim. If adding layers doesn't improve performance and the bottom layer alone is sufficient (as referenced in [25]), then the "deep" aspect isn't actually providing meaningful advantage.

The paper has several strengths: clear problem statement, good motivation for modeling sequential count data, well-organized structure, and thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets. The introduction of a "Probability AutoEncoder" for training the neural networks is a reasonable technical contribution.

However, as a reviewer prioritizing novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper's contribution to be primarily incremental rather than transformative. The integration of deep learning with Poisson factorization is interesting but not sufficiently novel compared to existing work. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that the deep structure provides a fundamental advantage over simpler approaches for the specific problem of sequential count data modeling.

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem, it doesn't offer a significant new direction or insight that would justify acceptance at NIPS. The modest performance improvements don't outweigh the lack of clear novelty, and the acknowledged limitations of the deep structure weaken the paper's central claim.

**Summary of key points:**

*Pro:*
- Clear problem statement and good motivation
- Well-organized and clearly written
- Comprehensive experimental validation across diverse datasets
- Acknowledgment of limitations (pruning, layer usefulness)

*Con:*
- Lack of clear theoretical novelty
- Incremental contribution rather than transformative
- Insufficient justification for deep structure
- Limitations directly contradict main novelty claim
- Improvements not convincingly linked to "deep" aspect

I recommend rejection as the contribution does not meet the bar for significant novelty expected at NIPS. The paper represents an incremental extension of existing work rather than a bold new direction. The authors would need to substantially revise their paper to demonstrate a truly novel approach or provide stronger evidence that the deep structure provides a fundamental advantage over simpler alternatives.
