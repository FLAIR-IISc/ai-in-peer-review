Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count matrices that builds on Poisson Factorization (PFA). The observed count xvt is modeled as Poisson with rate sum over factors θtk φvk λk ψv. To capture temporal dependence, the factor scores Θ are driven by a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t, where both “prior” and “posterior” conditionals are parameterized by recurrent neural networks over a fixed temporal window. The authors derive mean-field variational updates for conjugate parts (using auxiliary latent counts) and introduce a “Probability AutoEncoder” stage that trains the RNN parameters via squared-error losses between expectations of Gamma conditionals rather than a standard ELBO. Experiments on four synthetic datasets and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA, reporting improvements in MSE/PMSE on most tasks.

Relation to prior work
The model touches several existing lines: dynamic Poisson factorization with Gamma chains (e.g., PGDS), deep latent count models (Deep Exponential Families; augmentable Gamma Belief Networks), and temporal Poisson factorization with recurrent structure (e.g., Recurrent Poisson Factorization for recommendations). The idea of combining PFA with recurrent dynamics is not new, and deep hierarchies for count models have been studied. The contribution appears to be the specific combination with λ, ψ scalings and the proposed “Probability AutoEncoder” training objective.

Strengths
- Targets an important setting: sequential high-dimensional sparse count data.
- Uses conjugate augmentation to keep many updates closed form.
- Attempts to model longer-range dependence via depth and recurrent parameterization.
- The paper acknowledges known issues in hierarchical VAEs (e.g., pruning) and discusses architectural choices (ladder connections).

Weaknesses

Quality
- Inference objective: The “Probability AutoEncoder” deviates from standard VI. The RNN parameters are trained via ad hoc squared-error losses on expectations (Eqs. 16–18), not by maximizing an ELBO or predictive log-likelihood. There is no theoretical justification, consistency argument, or guarantee that this optimizes a well-defined bound on marginal likelihood.
- The claim that “differentiable variational Bayes is not suitable” is unconvincing; reparameterization/implicit gradients for Gamma or black-box VI are established, and SG-MCMC alternatives exist (e.g., Deep Exponential Families, Augmentable Gamma Belief Networks).
- Long-range dependence remains limited by a fixed window c at each layer; it is unclear how stacking layers offers effective long memory rather than compounding short windows. No analysis/ablation on c or depth vs. horizon captured.
- Evaluation metrics (MSE/PMSE) are not standard for count models; predictive log-likelihood/held-out perplexity are typical and more faithful to the Poisson likelihood.
- Baselines appear mismatched and under-specified: LSTM details for count modeling (link function, likelihood) are unclear; important baselines like dynamic topic models, recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendations, hierarchical Poisson factorization variants with temporal dynamics, and deep count models (DEF, GBN) are absent.
- No uncertainty quantification, confidence intervals, or statistical significance; no hyperparameter sensitivity; no ablations for λ and ψ.
- Synthetic datasets are simplistic; data generation process is unclear (deterministic functions?) and does not stress the model realistically.
- Reproducibility is weak: network architectures, activation functions, learning rates, and implementation details are missing; no code link.
- Computational complexity, memory costs, and runtime are not reported.

Clarity
- The paper contains frequent typos and notational inconsistencies. Several equations are hard to parse (e.g., Eq. 8–11 notation mixing and subscript conventions). Figures/Tables referenced (and claimed superior results) are not visible here; the text alone is insufficient for reproduction.
- The role of λ and ψ (vs. absorbing into Φ and Θ) is not convincingly motivated; no ablation.
- The description of combining “prior” and “posterior information” for h as Gamma shape additions (Eq. 10) is heuristic and not clearly derived.

Originality
- The combination of PFA with recurrent dynamics and deep latent hierarchies closely overlaps prior art (e.g., Recurrent Poisson Factorization; Deep Poisson factor models with recurrent structure; Gamma belief networks). The main new element is the non-ELBO training objective, which is not principled.
- Related work coverage is incomplete and some references are improperly formatted; several influential works on black-box VI for non-reparameterizable distributions and temporal count models are missing.

Significance
- Without rigorous and fair comparisons using standard metrics, it is difficult to conclude state-of-the-art performance. The current results, training budgets, and metrics do not convincingly demonstrate superiority over established baselines.
- The lack of a principled inference objective limits adoption and interpretability of gains.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant problem (sequential count modeling) with a model that is, on paper, flexible.
- Uses conjugate structure to keep some updates efficient.
- Empirical results, where shown, claim improvements on a diverse set of datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Inference/training objective is not principled; no ELBO or likelihood-based guarantee.
- Weak and non-standard evaluation; missing key baselines and metrics; no statistical rigor.
- Unclear novelty relative to existing deep/recurrent Poisson factor models.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; notation and reference problems.
- Limited analysis of long-range dependence, ablations, sensitivity, and computational costs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace the squared-error “Probability AutoEncoder” with a principled ELBO or predictive log-likelihood objective; consider implicit reparameterization or black-box VI for Gamma variables, or SG-MCMC.
- Evaluate with held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity, and include stronger baselines (e.g., Recurrent Poisson Factorization, DTM, DEF/GBN variants with temporal links).
- Provide ablations on c, depth N, λ/ψ, and window size; report runtimes and scalability.
- Strengthen synthetic experiments with realistic generative processes and larger horizons.
- Improve clarity, notation, and completeness of references; release code.

Recommendation
Given the methodological concerns about the training objective, the limited and non-standard evaluation, and unclear novelty over established dynamic/deep count models, I do not recommend acceptance at this time.
