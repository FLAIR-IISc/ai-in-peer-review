## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel deep learning approach (DDPFA) to dynamic factor analysis of count data, motivated by limitations of existing methods like PGDS and temporal Dirichlet processes. The core idea of leveraging RNNs to model temporal dependencies within a Poisson factorization framework is interesting and potentially valuable. However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling experimental results demonstrating a substantial advantage over established baselines, and the presentation lacks sufficient detail regarding implementation and hyperparameter tuning. While the concept is promising, the paper as it stands doesn't quite meet the bar for acceptance at NIPS.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the challenges of modeling sequentially observed count data, particularly its high dimensionality, sparsity, and temporal dependencies. It accurately summarizes existing approaches like PFA, PGDS, and temporal Dirichlet processes, pointing out their shortcomings (e.g., limited ability to capture long-range dependencies, inflexibility to different data patterns). The authors rightly position DDPFA as building upon these works by integrating deep learning (specifically RNNs) to capture complex temporal relationships. The connection to related work in deep Bayesian models (nested CRP, deep GPs, Deep Poisson Factor Analysis) is also well-established.  The motivation for using a Gamma distribution instead of Dirichlet for the factor loading prior, given the inclusion of the feature-wise parameter ψv, is reasonable.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of Poisson Factorization with deep RNNs for modeling temporal dependencies is a novel and potentially powerful approach. It addresses a clear limitation of existing methods by allowing for more flexible and expressive modeling of time dynamics.
* **Clear Model Formulation:** The mathematical formulation of DDPFA is generally clear and well-presented. The use of Poisson distributions and Gamma priors is standard in this domain.
* **Comprehensive Comparison:** The authors compare DDPFA against several relevant baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
* **Interesting Insights:** The visualization in Figure 2 and 3, showing factor activations, provides some qualitative insight into the model's behavior and suggests it may capture more nuanced dependencies compared to PGDS.
* **Consideration of Practical Issues:** The paper acknowledges and attempts to address practical challenges like "pruning" and the limitations of mean-field variational inference.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Advantage:**  The experimental results are not convincingly superior. While DDPFA performs well on some datasets, it doesn't consistently outperform the baselines, particularly PGDS.  The MSE/PMSE improvements are often marginal. The ICEWS prediction results are even worse than PGDS.
* **Lack of Implementation Details:** The paper lacks crucial implementation details.  The architecture of the RNNs (number of layers, hidden units, activation functions), the optimization algorithm used (e.g., Adam, SGD, learning rate), and the initialization strategy are not specified.  This makes it difficult to reproduce the results and assess the sensitivity of the model to these choices.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The justification for the chosen hyperparameters is weak. The authors state they used settings from previous work (PGDS) or arbitrary values (alpha/beta for Gamma priors). A more thorough hyperparameter search is needed to demonstrate the model's robustness and optimize its performance.
* **Variational Inference Concerns:** The paper acknowledges the potential limitations of MF-VI (pruning, local optima), but doesn’t offer a clear path forward beyond noting the need for alternative inference methods. 
* **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The computational complexity of DDPFA, especially with deep RNNs, is not discussed.  A comparison of training time and memory usage with the baselines would be valuable.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** While they mention issues with layer depth, a more systematic ablation study exploring the contribution of each component (deep structure, RNNs, feature-wise/temporal smoothness constraints) would strengthen the paper.



**4. Significance & Originality:**

The originality of the approach is moderate. Combining deep learning with PFA is a natural extension, and similar ideas have been explored in related areas (e.g., Deep Poisson Factor Analysis using RBMs). However, the specific integration of RNNs for temporal modeling within the PFA framework *is* novel. The significance of the work is currently limited by the lack of a substantial performance gain over existing methods.  If the model could demonstrably outperform state-of-the-art approaches on more challenging datasets, its impact would be much higher.

**5. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Arguments FOR acceptance:**

*  Novel combination of deep learning and Bayesian factor analysis.
*  Clear mathematical formulation.
*  Comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines.
*  Addresses a challenging problem in sequential count data analysis.

**Arguments AGAINST acceptance:**

*  Limited empirical advantage over baselines.
*  Lack of implementation details and hyperparameter tuning discussion.
*  Concerns about the limitations of mean-field variational inference.
*  Scalability and computational cost are not addressed.
*  Ablation studies are missing.

**6. Recommendation:**

**Reject.**

While the idea behind DDPFA is promising, the paper falls short of NIPS standards due to the lack of compelling experimental results, insufficient implementation details, and concerns regarding the robustness of the variational inference procedure.  I believe the authors would benefit from conducting a more thorough hyperparameter search, providing more detailed implementation information, and exploring alternative inference methods.  A more systematic evaluation with rigorous ablation studies would also significantly strengthen the paper.  I encourage the authors to address these points and resubmit in the future.
