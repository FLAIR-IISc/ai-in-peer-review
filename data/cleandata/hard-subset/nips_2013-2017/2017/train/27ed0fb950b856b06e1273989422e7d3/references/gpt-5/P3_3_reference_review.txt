Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a sequential extension of Poisson factorization for count data. The model decomposes a count tensor X into factors Φ, per-time scores Θ, and additional feature- and component-level scales Ψ and Λ. To capture temporal and longer-range dependencies beyond first-order Markovian gamma transitions (as used in PGDS), the authors introduce a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t with recurrent neural networks providing time-local “implicit” conditional distributions for gamma shape parameters at each layer. Inference alternates mean-field coordinate updates for conjugate variables (using Poisson–Gamma augmentation with latent counts xvtk) and training of the RNN parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that penalizes squared deviations between expectations of encoder/decoder distributions and reconstruction error of either Θ or X. Experiments on four synthetic datasets and five real datasets (ICEWS events, NIPS corpus, Ebola cases, International Disasters, Sheep population) compare DDPFA against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE and one-step PMSE, with qualitative factor visualizations.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Poisson factorization and gamma-negative binomial process models (Zhou et al.), dynamic PFAs such as PGDS, and deep latent variable models (deep exponential families, deep Poisson FA). Incorporating RNNs into PFAs is related to recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendations and neural autoregressive topic models. The “ladder” discussion connects to ladder VAEs. However, the paper departs from standard amortized variational inference by replacing an ELBO with a heuristic squared-loss objective over expectations.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: capturing long-range temporal dependence in count data while retaining factor interpretability.
- Uses the additive Poisson/gamma conjugacy to keep a tractable core, potentially enabling scalable updates for Φ, Ψ, Λ, and Θ.
- The hierarchical temporal structure is intuitively appealing and could model multi-scale dependencies.
- Broad experimental scope across synthetic and varied real datasets; some qualitative insights on learned factors are provided.

Weaknesses
- The probabilistic formulation and variational inference are insufficiently rigorous. The “Probability AutoEncoder” objective (Eqs. 15–18) is heuristic and not shown to be a valid bound (e.g., an ELBO) on the marginal likelihood. It mixes expectations under different networks with squared losses without establishing consistency or convergence guarantees.
- The posterior updates for h(n) (Eq. 14) effectively inject “prior” and an additional “posterior” term (γ) both produced by networks, leading to h(n) ~ Gamma(α+γ, β). This does not arise from a coherent generative model and risks double-counting signals. It is unclear what likelihood corresponds to γ.
- Claims about conjugacy are overstated. While Gamma–Poisson conjugacy holds when the prior is Gamma, the use of dual networks to set shape parameters and the ladder-style coupling break standard conjugacy; the resulting coordinate updates may not be true mean-field optima of any ELBO.
- The decision to avoid reparameterization because “only a few samples are drawn” is not convincing. Reparameterization (or implicit reparameterization) for Gamma/Poisson is standard and would allow principled amortized VI on an ELBO. The current objective lacks likelihood-based evaluation and could overfit or behave unpredictably.
- Evaluation metrics (MSE/PMSE) are misaligned with count models; predictive log-likelihood/perplexity would be more appropriate. No uncertainty estimates or statistical tests are provided.
- Comparisons are limited and uneven: LSTM is trained with square loss (not Poisson/NB likelihood), and important baselines (e.g., recurrent Poisson factorization, deep Poisson models with amortized VI, dynamic topic models) are missing. Hyperparameter tuning parity is unclear.
- Many presentation issues: notation errors (e.g., Eq. 7 duplicates φ; several typos), undefined choices (ensuring positivity of network outputs for Gamma shapes/rates), lack of architectural details, and incomplete citations.
- Scalability concerns: maintaining xvtk for all v,t,k can be prohibitive for large V,T,K; no subsampling/minibatch or sparse updates are described.

Quality
- Technical soundness is questionable due to the heuristic training objective and unclear posterior updates for deep layers. Claims about long-range dependence would be stronger with ablations and likelihood-based evaluation.
- Experiments suggest gains on some datasets, but the metrics and baselines limit the evidential weight.

Clarity
- The manuscript is difficult to follow in places, with inconsistent notation, typos, and missing details on networks (activation functions to enforce positivity, hidden sizes, optimizer, learning rates), priors, and preprocessing. Reorganizing the model and inference sections and providing a clear generative graph and ELBO would significantly improve clarity.

Originality
- The high-level idea—combine PFAs with deep recurrent structure—is interesting but related to existing lines (deep PFAs, recurrent PFs, DEFs). The proposed training objective is unusual but not justified theoretically.

Significance
- If made principled and scalable, the approach could impact sequential count modeling. As presented, the lack of a sound variational objective and limited evaluation reduce the paper’s significance.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Provide a precise generative model with all conditional distributions and parameterizations. Ensure network outputs meet domain constraints (e.g., softplus for Gamma shape/rate).
- Derive and optimize a valid ELBO with an amortized inference network q(h,θ|x) and a prior p(h,θ) parameterized by RNNs. Use reparameterization or implicit reparameterization for Gamma; alternatives include augmentation or reparameterized NB likelihoods.
- Remove or justify the ad-hoc γ terms; if a ladder architecture is desired, follow established ladder VAE derivations to keep a coherent variational family.
- Use predictive log-likelihood/perplexity and held-out likelihood for evaluation. Report uncertainty and perform hyperparameter tuning parity. Compare to stronger baselines (recurrent PF, deep PF with amortized VI, DTM).
- Address scalability: exploit sparsity in X, minibatch updates over (v,t), and collapsed or amortized handling of xvtk.
- Clarify the role of Λ and Ψ (sparsity vs smoothness), and provide ablations showing their effect.
- Clean up notation and citations; include architectural/training details and release code for reproducibility.

Pros
- Timely problem with practical relevance.
- Conceptually appealing combination of PF and deep recurrent structure.
- Empirical improvements reported on several datasets; qualitative factor analysis provided.

Cons
- Heuristic and unvalidated training objective; unclear probabilistic semantics.
- Incomplete and sometimes inappropriate evaluation.
- Presentation and reproducibility issues; missing strong baselines.
