Summary
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count matrices. Building on Poisson Factorization (PFA), the authors introduce (i) feature- and component-specific Gamma priors (ψv, λk) for sparsity/importance and (ii) a deep temporal hierarchy over time-varying factor scores θt mediated by latent variables h(n)t. The conditional distributions for θt and h(n)t are parameterized via recurrent neural networks (RNNs) that output Gamma shape parameters over a fixed temporal window c, stacking multiple layers to capture longer-range dependencies. Inference alternates between mean-field variational updates for the Gamma variables (leveraging standard Gamma–Poisson conjugacy but with network-provided shapes) and a “Probability AutoEncoder” stage that trains the RNN parameters with squared-error losses between expectations of distributions and reconstructions. Experiments on synthetic data and five real datasets compare against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA using MSE/PMSE.

Relation to prior work
The paper relates to: Poisson factorization and NB-process models for counts (e.g., HPF, PFA, PGDS); deep latent variable models (DEFs, GBNs); and RNN-based temporal models. Closer dynamic count models such as recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendations and dynamic Poisson factorization are relevant. The idea of making prior parameters functions of RNNs has been explored in deep temporal generative models, but not widely within PFA with conjugate updates. The novelty here is the specific combination of PFA with a stacked RNN-parameterized Gamma prior over θ and an autoencoder-like training criterion.

Strengths
- Targets an important problem: capturing long-range temporal dependencies in high-dimensional sparse count data.
- Attempts to combine the interpretability of PFAs with flexible temporal dynamics via RNNs.
- Includes sparsity/importance variables (ψ, λ) which may aid interpretability and regularization.
- Evaluates on multiple datasets spanning text, international events, epidemiology, and demographics.
- Acknowledges known issues in deep hierarchical VAEs (e.g., pruning) and attempts to address with ladder-like connections.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness
- Lack of a principled objective: The “Probability AutoEncoder” losses in Eqs. (15–18) are not derived as a valid ELBO or any bound on the marginal likelihood; they are squared-error heuristics over expectations. Consequently, there are no guarantees that the alternating procedure maximizes a coherent probabilistic objective or converges.
- Notational confusion between generative and inference models: The paper writes p(h(n)t|…) for both generative priors and what are essentially amortized posteriors; using p instead of q obscures the model’s probabilistic semantics.
- Implicit distributions: The model calls the RNN-parameterized conditionals “implicit,” but they are explicit Gammas with deterministic parameters from the RNNs. This could be fine, but the two-stage training that separately fits these networks to heuristic losses is not standard VI or MCMC and undermines probabilistic consistency.
- Conjugacy claims: The coordinate updates rely on Gamma–Poisson conjugacy with shape terms coming from RNNs. Once the RNN parameters are learned by a non-ELBO objective, it is unclear what posterior is being approximated.
- No predictive likelihoods: Evaluation uses MSE/PMSE on counts, which is misaligned with the Poisson likelihood. Predictive log-likelihood/perplexity is standard in PF literature and is crucial for fair comparison.

Experiments/validation
- Baselines appear underpowered or mismatched: LSTM is trained with MSE rather than a Poisson/NB likelihood; no hyperparameter search or regularization details; unclear if PGDS and PFA were tuned comparably.
- Limited comparisons: Missing comparisons to closely related deep/dynamic Poisson factorization or recurrent Poisson factorization methods beyond PGDS; no Hawkes-like baselines for long-range dependence.
- Missing quantitative evidence: The manuscript references Tables/Figures but they are not provided here. Without actual numbers/plots, it is impossible to verify claims of superiority or interpretability.
- No ablations: It is unclear what contributes most to gains (depth, RNN window c, ψ/λ, ladder connections). The paper itself notes minimal gains from depth and pruning issues.
- One-step prediction only; no multi-step evaluation; no uncertainty calibration; no runtime/complexity analysis; no code or reproducibility details.

Clarity
- The paper suffers from numerous grammatical errors, ambiguous notation, and inconsistent symbols (e.g., superscripts “shp/rte,” α/β definitions). Some equations contain typos. References are incomplete. Key design choices (architecture, optimizer, learning rates, initialization, early stopping) are missing.

Originality
- Combining PF with RNN-parameterized priors is plausible, but similar ideas exist in recurrent PF, deep PF/GBNs, and DEFs. The distinctive contribution seems to be the heuristic “Probability AutoEncoder” training, which is not convincingly justified.

Significance
- Given the lack of a sound training objective and the absence of compelling, well-controlled empirical evidence against strong baselines with appropriate likelihoods, the current version does not convincingly advance the state of the art.

Questions and requests for authors
- Provide a clear generative model and a valid ELBO (or IWVI) that includes the RNN parameters; derive gradients and train end-to-end. If heuristic losses are retained, justify them theoretically and empirically.
- Report predictive log-likelihood/perplexity on held-out data; include multi-step forecasts.
- Run fair baselines: PGDS and PFA with hyperparameter tuning; LSTM and temporal neural models with Poisson/NB likelihoods; include recurrent Poisson factorization baselines.
- Provide ablations for depth, ladder connections, window c, and ψ/λ. Analyze sensitivity and stability across random seeds.
- Include all figures/tables, data preprocessing details, and release code.

Minor comments
- Many typos (“fanatical data,” “nest hierarchical,” “Natrually,” etc.) and incomplete references; please proofread and complete citations.
- Clarify computational cost and scalability with V,T,K and number of layers.
- Clarify whether the Bernoulli–Poisson link or real-valued links were actually used in any experiments.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper tackles an important problem, but the proposed training is not grounded in a principled probabilistic objective, the exposition is unclear, and the experimental evaluation lacks the rigor needed to substantiate the claims. A future version with a proper ELBO-based training, stronger and fair comparisons, and clearer writing could be much more compelling.
