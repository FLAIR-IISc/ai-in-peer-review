The paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA) model to capture both short-term and long-term temporal dependencies in sequentially observed count data. It extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to model factor dynamics and leverages variational inference for parameter estimation. While the idea of combining deep learning with dynamic factor models is potentially valuable, the paper suffers from several shortcomings in technical detail, clarity, and experimental validation, ultimately leading to a recommendation for rejection.

The introduction lacks sufficient context and justification for the proposed approach. It broadly mentions challenges in analyzing sequential count data but fails to pinpoint the specific limitations of existing methods (PGDS, temporal Dirichlet processes) that DDPFA uniquely addresses. The claims about “weak points” in existing methods are vague and unsubstantiated. Moreover, the paper alludes to datasets with “fanatical data and disaster data” without defining what these entail or explaining why existing methods are unsuitable for them.

The model description in Section 2 is convoluted and contains inconsistencies. Equation (1) seems redundant given equation (2). The rationale behind absorbing Λ and Ψ into Θ in the "regular setting" is unclear. The notation is inconsistent; for instance, h(n)t is introduced but the meaning of 'n' isn't clearly explained initially. The explanation of the deep structure (equations 3 & 4) is abstract. How exactly does the RNN model p(h(n)t | …)? What is the architecture of the RNN? This is critical information and its omission severely hinders understanding. The choice of a Gamma distribution for φvk, λk, and ψv is mentioned but the motivation beyond merely citing previous work is lacking. The Bernoulli-Poisson link discussion feels tacked on and lacks depth – what specific scenarios benefit from this?

The inference section relies heavily on variational inference, which, as the authors later acknowledge in the discussion of results, can suffer from issues like “pruning” and is susceptible to becoming stuck in local optima. The derivation of the conditional distributions (equations 8, 9, 10) is stated without sufficient detail, making it difficult to verify its correctness. The description of the loss function and the training procedure for the neural networks is also superficial. The mention of "ladder-like architecture" is sudden and appears almost as an afterthought. The justification for using mean-field VI, despite its known limitations, is not persuasive.

The experimental section is problematic. The selection of synthetic datasets is arbitrary and lacks justification. The description of each dataset is insufficient; the reader needs to understand their key characteristics to evaluate the results. The table captions are missing. The comparison with PGDS, LSTM, and PFA is not thorough. The authors present MSE and PMSE but don’t discuss their practical significance. The claim that DDPFA "has the best performance" is not universally supported by the results – in the ICEWS dataset, PGDS outperforms DDPFA in prediction.  The analysis of Figure 2 is weak. Simply stating that “factors are not activated locally” isn't insightful without quantifying the degree of co-activation or comparing it to a baseline. Figure 3 offers limited insight. The discussion of the “pruning” problem and the ladder architecture is overly critical of their own work and feels somewhat disconnected from the main contribution. The discussion of limitations feels too brief given the acknowledged issues with MF-VI.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Lack of Clarity:** The paper is poorly written and difficult to follow due to convoluted explanations, inconsistent notation, and missing details.
*   **Technical Weaknesses:** The mathematical derivations are insufficiently detailed, and the justification for design choices is lacking.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experimental setup is weak, the results are not thoroughly analyzed, and the comparisons with existing methods are not convincing.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the idea of combining deep learning with dynamic factor models is interesting, the paper does not demonstrate a substantial advance over existing approaches.
*   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** Many claims are made without adequate supporting evidence.

**Arguments for Acceptance (weak):**

*   The idea of using RNNs to model temporal dependencies in dynamic factor analysis is potentially novel.
*   The paper explores a combination of techniques from different fields (Bayesian statistics, deep learning) which is sometimes valuable.



Overall, despite the potential of the idea, the numerous flaws in clarity, technical detail, and experimental validation outweigh the limited novelty of the approach. The paper requires substantial revision before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. I recommend rejection.
