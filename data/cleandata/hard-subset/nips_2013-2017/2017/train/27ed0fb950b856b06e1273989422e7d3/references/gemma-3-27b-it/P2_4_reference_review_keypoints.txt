- Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), combining Poisson factorization with RNNs and variational inference to model sequential count data.
- Strength: Addresses an important problem of modeling temporal dependencies in sequential count data for applications like recommendation and text.
- Strength: Novel combination of Poisson factorization and deep hierarchical RNNs to capture short- and long-term dependencies.
- Strength: Comprehensive experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola) with comparisons to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.
- Strength: Generally clear model description with explicit probabilistic equations.
- Weakness: Individual components (Poisson factorization, RNNs, variational inference) are standard; novelty mainly in their combination rather than fundamental new methods.
- Weakness: Relies on mean-field variational inference, which may prune factors and fail to capture complex latent correlations; alternative inference (e.g., MCMC) is not explored.
- Weakness: Lacks thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, leaving robustness of architecture and learning rates unclear.
- Weakness: Limited interpretability of learned factors; deeper analysis of factor meanings per dataset is missing.
- Weakness: Inconsistent performance (e.g., PGDS outperforms on ICEWS), suggesting the model is not universally superior.
- Weakness: Some sections are dense and could be clarified; connections between equations and practical implications are not always clear.
- Quality (NIPS): Technically sound but inference limitations and analysis gaps reduce confidence — 7/10.
- Clarity (NIPS): Mostly clear but needs clearer explanations of math and design choices — 7.5/10.
- Originality (NIPS): Combination is novel but components are not fundamentally new — 6.5/10.
- Significance (NIPS): Shows potential but limited improvements and acknowledged issues constrain impact — 6/10.
- Pro-accept: Novel approach to a relevant problem, competitive experimental results, and generally clear presentation.
- Con-accept: Mean-field inference limitations, limited component novelty, inconsistent empirical performance, and insufficient hyperparameter analysis.
- Overall recommendation: Reject — promising idea but inference issues, modest novelty, and inconsistent results need addressing before acceptance.
