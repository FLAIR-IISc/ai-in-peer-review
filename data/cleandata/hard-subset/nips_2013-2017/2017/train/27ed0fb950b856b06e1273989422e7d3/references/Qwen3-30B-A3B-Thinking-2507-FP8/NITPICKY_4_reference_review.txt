

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

## Summary
The paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) that extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with deep recurrent neural networks to capture complex temporal dependencies in sequential count data. The authors claim their model can better capture both short-term and long-term time dependencies compared to previous approaches like PGDS and shallow PFA models. The model uses variational inference with a neural network-based probability autoencoder for training.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a relevant problem in time series analysis of count data, which appears in applications like recommendation systems, text analysis, and network analysis. The authors make a reasonable attempt to extend previous work with a deep structure to capture long-term dependencies. The experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets (particularly on the EBOLA and ASP datasets) suggest that DDPFA outperforms baseline methods in some cases, indicating potential utility of the approach.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors and Inconsistencies
The paper contains numerous technical errors and inconsistent notation that undermine the credibility of the work. For example:
- Equation (1) has a formatting error: "∑K k=1" should be "∑_{k=1}^K"
- Inconsistent notation throughout (e.g., "h(n)t" vs "h^(n)_t")
- In equation (9), the posterior distribution is written as "θtk|Ψ,Λ,Φ,h(0), β,X ∼ Gamma(αθtk + xv·k, βθ + λk ∑ v ψvφvk)" without defining what βθ represents
- Equation (11) uses inconsistent notation with "shp" and "rte" that are never properly defined

### Methodological Issues
The paper lacks sufficient methodological detail to understand the model's actual innovation:
- The claim that "deep structure is included in order to capture long-time dependence" is not substantiated with clear explanation of how the architecture achieves this
- The experimental design lacks statistical significance testing to support the claim that DDPFA outperforms baselines
- Hyperparameter selection (e.g., K=3 for some datasets) is not justified or analyzed
- The paper mentions "pruning" as a problem but provides no evidence or analysis of this phenomenon beyond a brief mention

### Writing and Clarity Problems
The writing contains numerous grammatical errors and unclear explanations:
- "the amount of words used is large and many words appear only few times" (should be "only a few times")
- "Poisson Factorize Analysis (PFA)" should be "Poisson Factorization Analysis (PFA)"
- The paper claims "Poisson+(λ) is a truncated Poisson distribution" but never defines this distribution
- The model description in equations (3) and (4) is confusing and lacks clarity on the deep structure's temporal dependencies

### Experimental Evaluation
The experimental evaluation has significant limitations:
- The paper claims DDPFA "has the best performance" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests
- For count data, MSE and PMSE are not the most appropriate evaluation metrics; Poisson deviance would be more suitable
- The LSTM model is described as "a classical time sequence model" without sufficient architectural details for fair comparison
- The ICEWS dataset shows poor performance for DDPFA in prediction tasks, but the paper doesn't analyze why this occurs

### Formatting and Reference Issues
- References contain incomplete information (e.g., "A. Ayan, J. Ghosh, M. Zhou, , , ")
- The paper cites "NIPS 2017" in the title but the references include works from 2017-2018, suggesting a mismatch in conference timing
- The paper references "NIPS" inconsistently with "Neural Information Processing Systems"

## Critical Assessment
While the paper addresses a relevant problem, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and methodological gaps significantly undermine its contribution. The claims about the model's ability to capture "long-time dependence" are not substantiated with clear explanation or evidence. The experimental results are presented without sufficient statistical rigor to support the authors' conclusions.

The paper also fails to properly contextualize its contribution relative to existing work. For instance, it mentions "deep Gaussian process [6, 7]" and "Deep Exponential Families [8]" but doesn't clearly explain how their approach differs from these existing deep models. The reference to "ladder-like architecture" (citing [25]) is mentioned but not properly explained in relation to their model.

## Recommendation
This paper presents a potentially interesting idea but is undermined by multiple technical errors, inconsistent notation, and insufficient methodological detail. The numerous minor flaws, particularly the inconsistent notation and unclear model description, prevent me from being confident in the technical soundness of the proposed approach. The experimental evaluation lacks statistical rigor and appropriate metrics for count data.

While the problem addressed is relevant, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claimed advantages of the proposed model over existing approaches. The paper would need substantial revision to address the technical errors, clarify the model architecture, and provide more rigorous experimental evaluation before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.

**Recommendation: Reject**
