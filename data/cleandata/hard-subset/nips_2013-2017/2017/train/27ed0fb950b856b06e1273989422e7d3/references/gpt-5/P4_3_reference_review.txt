Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Analysis (DDPFA) for sequential count data. Observed counts xvt are modeled via Poisson factorization with K components: xvt ~ Poisson(∑k θtk φvk λk ψv), where θtk are time-varying factor scores, φvk are factor loadings, and λk, ψv are Gamma-distributed global component- and feature-wise scalars to modulate importance and sparsity. Temporal dependence is introduced via a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t. The prior p(θt | h(0) t−c:t) and inter-layer transitions p(h(n)t | h(n+1) t−c:t) are specified as implicit Gamma distributions whose shape parameters are outputs of recurrent neural networks (RNNs). Training alternates between (i) mean-field coordinate-ascent variational inference (MFVI) for conjugate Poisson–Gamma parts, treating the neural-network parameters as fixed, and (ii) a “Probability AutoEncoder” procedure that fits the neural networks via a reconstruction-style squared-error loss between distributional expectations, inspired by ladder/stacked autoencoders and VAEs.

Experiments compare DDPFA to PGDS (Poisson–Gamma dynamical system), PFA (static Poisson factorization), and LSTM on synthetic sequences and five real datasets (ICEWS daily events, NIPS yearly word counts, EBOLA daily cases/deaths, International Disasters yearly counts, Annual Sheep Population), reporting MSE for reconstruction and one-step-ahead prediction (PMSE). The paper claims consistent improvements for DDPFA on most tasks.

This work relates to: Poisson factorization and gamma/negative-binomial processes (Zhou et al.), dynamic count models such as PGDS, deep latent variable models for counts (Deep Exponential Families; deep Poisson factor models), recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation, and ladder/stacked autoencoders and VAEs for amortized inference. The main distinction is the use of a deep, RNN-parameterized Gamma prior over θt and a bidirectional ladder-like inference heuristic for temporal dependence.

Strengths
- Addresses an important setting: high-dimensional, sparse, temporally dependent count data in text, events, and epidemiology.
- Combines Poisson factorization’s interpretability with deep recurrent structure to capture both short- and long-range temporal dependencies.
- Exploits conjugacy to retain simple MFVI updates for φ, θ, λ, ψ given fixed neural parameters.
- Includes several real datasets with nontrivial scale (e.g., ICEWS) and shows improved reconstruction/prediction errors over static PFA and often over PGDS and LSTM.
- Discussion acknowledges practical issues seen in deep hierarchical variational models (e.g., “pruning”) and suggests ladder-style connections.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (soundness, support)
- Objective is not principled. The “Probability AutoEncoder” loss (Eqs. 15–18) is an ad-hoc squared-error criterion on distributional expectations rather than a rigorously derived ELBO or predictive log-likelihood. There is no guarantee it lower-bounds the marginal likelihood or leads to consistent estimation. The proposed alternation (MFVI with fixed network parameters, then MSE training of the networks) does not optimize a single coherent objective.
- The implicit prior/posterior parameterizations via RNNs are not accompanied by derivations showing that the overall procedure maximizes an ELBO with amortized inference. Established alternatives exist: reparameterization for Gamma (e.g., via implicit or generalized reparameterization), variance-reduced score estimators, or auxiliary-variable bounds for implicit priors.
- Some modeling details are questionable or under-specified:
  - The Bernoulli–Poisson link uses b=1(x>1); standard practice is b=1(x≥1).
  - Prior/variational forms for θtk and h(n)tk fix rate parameters while learning shapes via RNNs; rationale and stability of this choice are not analyzed.
  - The posterior networks use future time steps (e.g., p(h(0)t | θt, …, θt+c)), which is permissible for offline inference but requires care in prediction; the paper does not clarify how one-step-ahead forecasts avoid peeking into the future.
  - The MFVI factorization assumes independence across time in q, while temporal coupling is only via network-generated prior parameters. There is no analysis of how this affects posterior calibration.
- Evaluation uses MSE/PMSE. For count models, predictive log-likelihood, Poisson deviance, or held-out perplexity are standard and more aligned with the generative model. Without likelihood-based metrics, it is hard to assess whether improvements reflect better probabilistic modeling or just regression-style tuning.
- Baselines may not be fully competitive:
  - LSTM trained with MSE on raw counts is disadvantaged; count-aware loss (Poisson/NB) or appropriate transformations are common.
  - PGDS hyperparameters and iterations are fixed from prior work; sensitivity analysis or tuning parity would strengthen claims.
- References are incomplete (missing venues/years) and some equations have indexing errors (e.g., q(ψk) vs q(ψv)), typographical mistakes, and notational inconsistencies, which hinder reproducibility.
- No complexity/scalability analysis or runtime comparison is provided, despite neural components and multiple VI/training loops.

Clarity
- The exposition of the deep temporal structure (Eqs. 3–4) and the ladder “prior/posterior” networks would benefit from a precise generative process, graphical model, and a clear separation between model and variational families.
- The Probability AutoEncoder section should present a formal objective or justify the chosen surrogate with references and analysis.
- Several typos and ambiguous notations (e.g., ψk vs ψv, “Bernoulli-Poisson distribution”) reduce clarity.
- Missing tables/figures in the submission text make it difficult to verify claims.

Originality
- Using RNNs to parameterize priors over PF latent factors is a reasonable extension. However, related work has explored deep/recurrent structures for count factorization (e.g., Deep Exponential Families; deep Poisson factor models; recurrent Poisson factorization). The paper should more clearly articulate what is fundamentally new (e.g., specific ladder-style bidirectional networks over Gamma shapes; inclusion of λk and ψv with temporal deep priors) and differentiate from “Deep Poisson Factor Modeling with a recurrent structure” cited by the authors.
- The proposed training objective departs from standard ELBO-based methods but lacks theoretical grounding, limiting its contribution.

Significance
- If made principled and clarified, the idea of deep recurrent priors for Poisson factorization could be impactful for sequential count modeling. Current empirical gains are promising but not conclusive given the evaluation metrics, baseline choices, and the lack of likelihood-based assessment or ablations (e.g., impact of λ/ψ, depth, window size c) beyond brief comments.
- Practical adoption is limited by unclear training objective and incomplete reproducibility details.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem with a potentially useful combination of PF and deep recurrent structure.
- Demonstrates improved reconstruction and prediction on several datasets.
- Provides interpretability via factors and claims long-range activation patterns.

Arguments against acceptance
- Training objective is not a valid likelihood bound; overall algorithm lacks theoretical justification.
- Evaluation does not use standard probabilistic metrics; baselines may be under-tuned.
- Clarity and rigor issues (notation errors, incomplete references, missing details) impede reproducibility.
- Novelty over prior deep/recurrent PF models is not convincingly established.

Suggestions for improvement
- Derive and optimize a principled ELBO with amortized variational families for h and θ (e.g., Gamma reparameterization or generalized reparameterization; auxiliary-variable bounds for implicit priors), or provide theoretical justification for the proposed surrogate objective.
- Report predictive log-likelihood/Poisson deviance and include stronger, properly tuned baselines (e.g., LSTM with Poisson/NB loss, recurrent PF variants).
- Clarify how one-step-ahead prediction is performed without future information; provide ablations for c, depth, λ/ψ inclusion, and ladder connections.
- Fix notation/typos, complete citations, and provide algorithmic details (architectures, initialization, optimization settings, complexity).
- Compare explicitly to deep exponential families and deep/recurrent PF models, highlighting concrete differences.
