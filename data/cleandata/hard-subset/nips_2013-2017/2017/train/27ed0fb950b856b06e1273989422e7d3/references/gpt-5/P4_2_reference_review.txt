Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential, high-dimensional count data. As in Poisson Factorization (PF), observed counts xvt are modeled via a Poisson likelihood with K latent components: xvt ~ Poisson(sum_k θtk φvk λk ψv), where φvk are factor loadings, θtk are time-varying factor scores, λk are component-wise weights, and ψv are feature-wise shrinkage terms. To capture temporal dependence, the model introduces a hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t (n = 0,…,N) and parameterizes conditional Gamma priors for θt and h(n)t via recurrent neural networks (RNNs) over finite windows of past states, while using a separate RNN as an auxiliary posterior (a bidirectional/“ladder”-like structure). Inference is described as a two-stage procedure: (i) mean-field variational updates for local variables (auxiliary counts xvtk and Gamma variational factors for Θ, Φ, Λ, Ψ, H) exploiting Poisson–Gamma conjugacy when the RNN outputs are treated as fixed hyperparameters; and (ii) learning the RNN parameters with a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that matches expectations from the auxiliary posterior to the prior and includes a reconstruction term.

Empirically, DDPFA is compared against PGDS (a Poisson–Gamma dynamical system), LSTM, and a static PFA on synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS events, NIPS corpus, Ebola time series, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population). Metrics are MSE (fit) and PMSE (one-step-ahead prediction). The paper reports that DDPFA typically outperforms baselines, with exceptions (ICEWS prediction), and presents qualitative factor interpretations. An ablation suggests limited gains from adding more layers; a ladder-style variant helps mitigate “pruning” in hierarchical VAEs.

The work relates to: Poisson/gamma dynamic models and PGDS; recurrent Poisson factorization and temporal recommendation; deep PFAs and deep exponential families; VAE/ladder VAE; and auxiliary-variable approaches to variational inference.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and common setting: sequential sparse count data with potentially long-range temporal dependence.
- Combines PF’s conjugacy with RNNs to flexibly parameterize temporal priors, a natural and potentially powerful hybrid.
- Includes feature-wise (ψv) and component-wise (λk) shrinkage, which can aid identifiability/regularization.
- Empirical evaluation spans multiple domains; qualitative factor trajectories (ICEWS) indicate interpretability.
- The paper openly discusses observed failure modes (layer “pruning”) and explores a ladder-style architecture to alleviate them.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness
- The learning objective for the neural network parameters is not derived from a principled ELBO or predictive score. The proposed “Probability AutoEncoder” uses L2 losses between expectations from auxiliary posterior/prior and a reconstruction term. There is no proof it corresponds to maximizing a lower bound on the marginal likelihood, nor analysis of bias introduced by substituting expectations for samples. This undermines the claimed variational inference framework.
- The paper dismisses reparameterized gradients for Gamma/Poisson models as “unsuitable” without justification. Established methods (e.g., implicit reparameterization gradients, augmentation tricks) can train such models end-to-end under a coherent ELBO.
- Several conditionals/equations have inconsistencies or unclear notation (e.g., ψ indexed by v but variational factor written as q(ψk); β subscripts reused; the λk conditional in Eq. 8 uses βθ ambiguously; dimensions of h(n)tk and inputs to ffeed/fback are not always precise). This makes it difficult to verify derivations.
- The hierarchy’s generative structure is described as “implicit” but then instantiated as explicit Gamma conditionals with RNN-produced parameters. The role of future context is relegated to the auxiliary posterior, but the factorization and independence assumptions across layers/time are not rigorously stated.
- No complexity/runtime analysis or convergence guarantees for the alternating updates. The interaction between coordinate-ascent VI and SGD on the networks (outer-inner loops) is not formally justified.

Originality
- The combination of PF with RNN-parameterized temporal priors is incremental relative to prior work on recurrent Poisson factorization, PGDS with transition structure, deep PFAs/def, and neural autoregressive topic models. The main novelty is the particular ladder-style, multi-layer hierarchy and the bespoke training loss, but the latter is also the least well-justified.

Significance
- Claimed empirical gains are promising, but the evidence is limited: results rely on MSE/PMSE (which are coarse for counts), and important baselines are missing (e.g., dynamic topic models, deep Markov models for counts, state-space models with Poisson observations). The paper itself notes that extra layers did not consistently help, which weakens the case for the deep hierarchy.
- Without a principled training objective, it is unclear if other researchers can reliably build upon this method.

Clarity
- The manuscript has numerous typos/grammar issues, notational inconsistencies, and incomplete references. Key pieces are missing: a clear generative model specification with all conditional independencies, precise update equations with correct indices, and a concise algorithm box for the training loop. Some statements overreach (e.g., about prior methods’ inability to capture long-range dependence) without quantitative support.

Suggestions for improvement
- Derive and optimize a coherent ELBO that includes the RNN-parameterized Gamma priors. Use amortized variational inference and reparameterization/implicit gradients for Gamma (or appropriate augmentation), or provide a clear bound that your PA objective maximizes. Compare against this principled alternative.
- Clarify model specification and notation; include a complete graphical model and pseudocode for the alternating optimization; fix all indexing issues (ψv vs ψk, β subscripts).
- Expand experimental evaluation: use held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity for count data; include additional baselines (dynamic topic models, deep Markov models for counts, RPF); perform hyperparameter tuning for all methods; report runtime and sensitivity to window size c, depth, and K; provide uncertainty calibration.
- Improve and complete citations (with authors/venues) and situate contributions precisely versus prior art.

Pros for acceptance
- Tackles an important problem with a potentially useful hybrid of PF and deep recurrent priors.
- Demonstrates empirical improvements on several datasets and interpretable latent factors.
- Attempts to diagnose and mitigate known issues in hierarchical variational models.

Cons for acceptance
- Training objective for neural components is ad hoc and not tied to a valid variational bound; technical soundness is questionable.
- Notational/derivational clarity is insufficient to verify correctness or reproduce the method.
- Marginal novelty relative to existing recurrent/deep PF work; limited evidence that depth (the central claim) is beneficial.
- Experimental evaluation lacks stronger baselines, likelihood-based metrics, and ablations supporting long-range dependence claims.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. While the problem is important and the hybrid modeling direction is promising, the current submission lacks a principled and clearly justified inference objective, contains several technical/notation issues, and provides limited evidence for the advantages of the proposed deep hierarchy. With a coherent ELBO-based training, clearer presentation, and a stronger empirical study, this work could become a solid contribution.
