### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, a new method for modeling high-dimensional sequential count data. The work aims to capture complex, long-range temporal dependencies that may be missed by traditional dynamic factor models. The core idea is to extend the Poisson Factorization Analysis (PFA) framework by placing a deep, hierarchical prior on the time-varying factor scores (Θ). This deep structure consists of multiple layers of latent variables (h), where the temporal dynamics within and between layers are modeled by Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). These RNNs define implicit conditional probability distributions.

The authors propose a two-stage inference procedure. First, given fixed neural network parameters, they use mean-field variational inference (VI) with coordinate ascent to update the latent variables of the PFA model and the deep hierarchy. Second, they update the parameters of the RNNs using a loss function inspired by variational autoencoders, which minimizes the reconstruction error of the data and a regularization term on the latent representations. The model is evaluated on several synthetic and real-world datasets, where it is compared against PFA, the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), and an LSTM baseline. The results generally show that DDPFA achieves a better fit to the data (lower MSE) and can learn interpretable, non-local temporal factors.

This work relates to a line of research on dynamic topic models and Bayesian matrix factorization. It builds directly upon PFA and dynamic extensions like PGDS [Cemgil et al., 2008; Zhou et al., 2012], arguing that the Markovian assumptions in these models are too restrictive. It also draws inspiration from the broader field of deep generative models, such as Deep Exponential Families and Variational Autoencoders, by using neural networks to parameterize complex distributions over latent variables. The key contribution is the specific architecture combining PFA with stacked RNNs for temporal modeling and the associated hybrid inference scheme.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Model:** The paper addresses a significant limitation of existing dynamic count data models, namely their reliance on simple, often Markovian, transition dynamics. The proposed DDPFA model, which uses a deep recurrent structure to learn complex, long-range dependencies, is a novel and intuitive approach to this problem. The model is highly expressive and has the potential to better capture the rich dynamics present in real-world time series.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance on Fitting:** The experimental results, particularly the Mean Squared Error (MSE) on held-in data, are compelling. Across a variety of synthetic and real-world datasets, DDPFA consistently outperforms the baselines, including the strong PGDS model. This supports the central claim that the model's enhanced flexibility allows it to better explain the observed data.

3.  **Good Interpretability:** The qualitative analysis of the learned factors in Figure 2 and Figure 3 is a valuable strength. It demonstrates that DDPFA learns factors that are activated over longer time periods, in contrast to the more localized factors learned by PGDS. This provides evidence that the model is indeed capturing the intended long-range dependencies and can yield meaningful scientific insights.

4.  **Honest Evaluation of Limitations:** The authors are transparent about the model's weaknesses, such as the "pruning" phenomenon and the limited benefit of adding more layers (Table 3). This honest self-assessment is commendable and points toward important avenues for future research.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Rigor of the Inference Procedure:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the description and justification of the inference algorithm for the neural network parameters.
    *   The objective function in Eq. (15) and its subsequent simplification to the L2-loss in Eq. (16) and (18) are not well-justified from a probabilistic standpoint. The paper claims this is "similar to variational AutoEncoder," but it deviates significantly. A standard VAE minimizes the KL divergence between the approximate posterior and the prior, which is derived directly from the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO). Here, the use of L2 distance on the expectations of latent variables seems ad-hoc and lacks a clear connection to the Bayesian model it is supposed to be inferring.
    *   It is unclear why a more standard amortized inference approach (e.g., Stochastic Variational Inference with the full ELBO as the objective) was not used. The claim that "differentiable variational Bayes is not suitable" because only a few samples are drawn is not a sufficient explanation, as many SVI methods operate successfully in this regime.
    *   The two-stage alternating optimization procedure—updating VI parameters via coordinate ascent and then updating NN weights via SGD on a separate loss—is not guaranteed to optimize a single, coherent objective function. This makes the convergence properties of the algorithm unclear and questions the technical soundness of the inference.

2.  **Experimental Concerns:**
    *   The comparison to an LSTM is not fully convincing. LSTMs are typically used for discriminative prediction tasks, not as generative models of count matrices. The paper should provide more details on how the LSTM was adapted for this task (e.g., its output layer, loss function) to ensure a fair comparison.
    *   The model's poorer predictive performance (PMSE) compared to PGDS on the ICEWS dataset is a significant negative result that is somewhat understated. This suggests that the model's high flexibility might lead to overfitting, and that simpler models with stronger inductive biases (like the Markovian assumption in PGDS) can be superior for prediction. This trade-off deserves a more thorough discussion.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper could be significantly improved in terms of clarity. Section 3 is particularly difficult to follow. The notation is sometimes ambiguous (e.g., `p(H(0)|Θ)` is an "auxiliary posterior" but its role is not clearly defined). The references are incomplete and poorly formatted, with several missing publication venues and years, and apparent citation mismatches (e.g., citing [2] for PGDS).

### **Arguments for/against Acceptance**

*   **For:** The paper introduces a novel and powerful model for an important problem. The core idea is elegant, and the empirical results for data fitting are strong, demonstrating the potential of this modeling approach.
*   **Against:** The inference procedure lacks theoretical grounding and appears heuristic. This is a major technical flaw. The experimental results, while strong on fitting, show weakness in prediction and reveal internal model issues (pruning), which tempers the overall contribution. The lack of clarity in key sections makes the work difficult to understand and reproduce.

### **Conclusion and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the core modeling idea presented in this paper is promising, the significant concerns regarding the technical soundness and clarity of the inference procedure prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The current inference algorithm feels disconnected from the probabilistic model, making it difficult to assess what is truly being optimized.

My primary suggestion for the authors is to **reformulate the inference within a unified, principled framework**. I strongly recommend adopting a standard amortized VI approach where both the generative RNNs (p-model) and recognition RNNs (q-model) are trained jointly to maximize the ELBO.
1.  **Define a Single Objective:** Clearly write down the ELBO for the entire DDPFA model.
2.  **Amortize Inference:** Use the "backward" RNNs to parameterize the variational distributions `q(h^(n)|h^(n-1))` and `q(θ|h^(0))`.
3.  **Joint Optimization:** Use stochastic gradient descent with the reparameterization trick (or score function estimators for Gamma distributions) to jointly optimize the parameters of the variational distributions and the generative model. This would unify the two inference stages and place the method on solid theoretical ground. See Kingma & Welling (2014) and subsequent work on SVI for deep generative models.

Additional suggestions:
*   **Rewrite Section 3:** A rewrite focused on clarity, starting from the ELBO, would tremendously improve the paper.
*   **Strengthen Experimental Analysis:** Provide a deeper analysis of the ICEWS prediction result. Why does DDPFA fail here? Is it overfitting? Also, please clarify the LSTM baseline implementation details.
*   **Address Model Pruning:** While this may be future work, acknowledging recent techniques to mitigate this issue in VAEs (e.g., KL annealing, alternative priors) would strengthen the discussion.
*   **Proofread and Format:** A thorough proofread to improve language and fix the reference list is necessary.

This is a promising research direction, and with a more rigorous inference framework, this work could become a significant contribution to the field.

**Final Recommendation: Reject**
