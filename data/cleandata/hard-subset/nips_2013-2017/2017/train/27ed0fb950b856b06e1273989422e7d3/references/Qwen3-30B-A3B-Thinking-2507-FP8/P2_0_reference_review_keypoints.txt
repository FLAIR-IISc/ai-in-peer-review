- Model is technically sound and builds on Bayesian factor analysis and deep learning with a well-motivated variational inference algorithm.
- The paper introduces a deep recurrent structure to capture short- and long-term temporal patterns in count data.
- There is weak theoretical justification for why a deep structure is necessary; claims about shallow models' shortcomings are not well supported.
- Comparison with PGDS and other baselines is superficial and lacks clear analysis of when the deep model helps.
- Experimental evaluation uses multiple synthetic and real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, ASP) but omits statistical significance testing.
- Results are mixed: DDPFA outperforms baselines on some datasets (EBOLA, ASP) but underperforms on key real-world data like ICEWS.
- The paper acknowledges practical issues such as the "pruning" problem and diminishing returns from adding layers.
- Writing and mathematical formulation are generally clear, but explanations of network architecture and training are superficial.
- The "ladder-like" architecture is mentioned as a fix for pruning but is not well explained or justified in the main text.
- The method is somewhat novel but not clearly differentiated from existing deep Poisson factorization approaches.
- Claims of improved interpretability and effectiveness are not convincingly supported by evidence.
- Significance is limited because improvements are not consistent or dramatic across datasets.
- Recommendation is rejection with encouragement to resubmit after addressing theoretical, experimental, and comparative weaknesses.
- Suggested fixes: provide theoretical justification for depth, perform statistical significance tests, compare to more related deep learning baselines, clarify ladder architecture, analyze ICEWS failure, and add scalability analysis.
