This paper proposes a novel method for capturing long-range temporal dependencies within sequences of count data. The core idea is to use a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) to model the evolution of latent variables, which are assumed to follow a gamma distribution. The RNN learns to approximate the shape parameters of these gamma distributions at each time step, effectively capturing the temporal dynamics. This approximation integrates information from both a top-down prior (from higher layers in the hierarchy) and a bottom-up likelihood (from the observed data). For inference, the model parameters are updated based on the expectation of the latent variables, a technique that laudably reduces the variance associated with sampling-based methods and improves computational efficiency. Overall, the proposed model presents an elegant and effective approach to handling long-range dependencies in dynamic topic models.

While the core ideas are promising, the manuscript could be significantly strengthened by addressing several points related to clarity, experimental validation, and positioning within the existing literature. My detailed comments are below:

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript would benefit from a careful proofreading to correct several typos, for instance, in Equation (10). Furthermore, the model's architecture is complex, and the current description would be greatly enhanced by a detailed graphical illustration. A diagram depicting the generative process and the flow of information between variables over time would significantly improve reader comprehension. For an excellent example, the authors could refer to Figure 1 in Chung et al. (NIPS 2015) [1].

2.  **Model Justification:** A key modeling choice is to fix the scale parameter of the gamma distributions while only learning the shape parameter. The rationale for this design choice is not sufficiently discussed. Providing a more detailed analysis or justification for this simplification would strengthen the paper's technical contribution.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive. The authors demonstrate the model's performance with a two-layer architecture, but an ablation study is needed to understand the impact of model depth. Specifically, a comparison with single-layer and deeper versions of the model would provide valuable insights into the benefits of the hierarchical structure. Additionally, the sensitivity of the model to other key hyperparameters, such as the temporal window size and the number of latent factors, should be analyzed to provide a more complete picture of the model's behavior.

4.  **Related Work:** The related work section should be updated to include and discuss more recent advancements in deep Poisson factor analysis. The current manuscript overlooks several highly relevant papers. Notably, works such as the Augmentable Gamma Belief Network [2] and Deep Latent Dirichlet Allocation with topic-layer-adaptive SGR MCMC [3] should be discussed to properly contextualize the contributions of this paper.

In summary, this paper introduces a compelling and well-motivated model. Addressing the points above—particularly regarding the experimental analysis and comparison with recent literature—would substantially improve the quality and impact of the manuscript.
