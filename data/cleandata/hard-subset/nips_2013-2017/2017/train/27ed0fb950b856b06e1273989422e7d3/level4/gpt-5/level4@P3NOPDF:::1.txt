Summary
The paper proposes a deep dynamic Poisson factorization model that extends classical Poisson factorization to capture temporal dependencies. It replaces standard state-space dynamics with a simplified recurrent neural network aimed at modeling long-term dependencies. Inference is performed via variational inference, with an additional maximization step for the neural network parameters. Results are reported on five real-world datasets.

Strengths
- The problem is important: incorporating temporal structure into Poisson factorization is relevant for recommendation and count data modeling.
- The attempt to use a neural architecture to capture long-term dependencies is interesting.
- Variational inference enables scalability, and the empirical evaluation spans multiple datasets.

Major concerns
1) Model formulation and RNN claim (Eq. 4)
- The structure in Eq. 4 does not correspond to a standard RNN: h_t^(n) depends only on top-layer memory vectors and not on previous time steps of the same layer. Please justify this simplification and discuss its implications for modeling long-term dependencies. If this is intentional (e.g., a top-down temporal pathway), clarify the design choice and its advantages over conventional recurrent connections.

2) Incomplete generative specification (Section 2)
- Key pieces of the model are missing. It is unclear how the Gamma in Eq. 4 is parameterized, and the distribution generating θ_t in Eq. 3 is not specified. Please provide a complete generative process with explicit priors, parameterizations (shapes/rates for all Gammas), and clear notation.

3) “Implicit distributions” discussion (lines 110–118)
- Eqs. 9 and 10 define explicit Gamma distributions with known shapes and rates. Since p(θ_tk | h^(0)) is available in closed form, calling it “implicit” is misleading. Please revise this discussion to avoid confusion.

4) Unclear statement (lines 30–31)
- The sentence “This method may have weak points in analyzing other data with different pattern long-time dependence, such as fanatical data and disaster data” is unclear. Please clarify what data types you mean (perhaps “financial” and “disaster” data?) and what specific long-term dependency patterns challenge the method, with examples if possible.

5) Objective and optimization (lines 133–155; Eq. 15)
- The subsection is difficult to follow. What exactly is the “loss function” in Eq. 15? If this is the ELBO, please write it explicitly and state which terms are optimized w.r.t. variational parameters vs. neural network parameters. If it is a different objective (e.g., reconstruction with regularization), define and justify it. Also clarify the optimization schedule (e.g., alternating VI updates and gradient steps), and provide a concise algorithm description.

6) Evaluation metrics
- MSE/PMSE are not well aligned with Poisson factorization objectives. Please include predictive log-likelihood (held-out Poisson likelihood), perplexity, and/or ranking metrics when relevant. Report time-split evaluation and calibration if possible.

7) Model capacity and sensitivity
- The chosen K (line 207) seems small. Please include experiments with larger K (e.g., K = 100) and a sensitivity analysis over K and depth, along with runtime and convergence behavior.

8) Results presentation (lines 224–232; Fig. 3)
- The connection between the text and Fig. 3 is unclear. Specify what the figure shows, axes, baselines, and how it supports the claims. Make figures and captions self-contained.

9) Variational updates in the main text
- Eqs. 11–14 could be moved to the appendix. In the main paper, focus on intuition, computational complexity, and the overall algorithm.

10) Figure quality
- The caption and text in Fig. 4 are hard to read. Please increase font sizes and improve clarity.

Writing and presentation
- Reduce passive voice, especially in the abstract, and simplify sentences where possible.
- Fix typos and improve consistency. Examples:
  - Line 15: “high-dimensional”
  - Line 22: “factorize”
  - Line 24: “property of the”
  - Lines 29, 34: “Dirichlet”
  - Line 34: “nested”
  - Fig. 1: “visual representation”
  - Fig. 1: Consider replacing “transmission” with “transition” or a more precise term, depending on intent
  - Line 49: avoid incomplete sentences
  - Add missing articles throughout (e.g., “Although the Dirichlet distribution is often used as a prior distribution”)
- Proofread the paper carefully for grammar and style.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a complete, consistent generative model and inference description (including priors, parameterizations, and constraints).
- Clarify the architectural choice that departs from standard RNNs; if beneficial, compare against a true RNN baseline.
- Make the objective explicit (ELBO or otherwise) and detail the optimization scheme with convergence criteria.
- Expand the evaluation with more appropriate metrics, sensitivity analyses, and clearer figures and captions.
- Move algebraic update details to the supplement to free space for analysis and discussion.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important problem with a potentially interesting approach, but the current presentation leaves significant gaps in the model specification, justification of the architecture, clarity of the objective and optimization, and appropriateness of the evaluation. With substantial revisions along the lines above, the work could become much stronger. In its current form, I cannot recommend acceptance.
