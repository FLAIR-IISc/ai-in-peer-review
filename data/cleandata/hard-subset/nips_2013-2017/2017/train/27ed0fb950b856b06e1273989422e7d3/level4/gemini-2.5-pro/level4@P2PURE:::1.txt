This paper introduces the deep dynamic Poisson factorization model, which extends Poisson Factorization (PF) to incorporate temporal dependencies. In contrast to previous dynamic PF models, this work employs a simplified recurrent neural network structure to capture long-term dependencies. Inference is performed using variational inference, alternating with a maximization step for the neural network parameters. The model is evaluated on five real-world datasets.

While the core idea is potentially interesting, the paper requires significant improvements in its execution to meet the standards of a top-tier conference like NIPS. My main concerns are detailed below.

*   **Model Formulation:** The structure described in Eq. 4 deviates from a standard RNN architecture. Specifically, the hidden state at a given layer, $h_t^{(n)}$, is conditioned only on the layer above it ($h_t^{(n+1)}$), not on its own state at the previous timestep ($h_{t-1}^{(n)}$), as would be expected in a recurrent formulation. The authors should clarify the motivation for this simplified structure.

*   **Incomplete Model Specification:** The model specification in Section 2 appears to be incomplete. For instance, it is unclear how the Gamma distribution in Eq. 4 is parameterized, and the prior distribution for $\theta_t$ (from which the document-specific topic proportions in Eq. 3 are drawn) is not specified.

*   **"Implicit Distributions":** The characterization of the distributions in lines 110-118 as "implicit" is inaccurate. The distributions for $\theta_{tk}$ (Eq. 9) and $h$ (Eq. 10) are, in fact, explicit Gamma distributions with clearly defined shape and rate parameters. Given that these conditional distributions can be computed in closed form, they do not fit the definition of implicit distributions.

*   **Unclear Terminology:** The sentence in lines 30-31, "This method may have weak points in analyzing other data with different pattern long-time dependence, such as fanatical data and disaster data," is unclear. The authors should elaborate on what is meant by "fanatical data" and "disaster data" and clarify the specific weaknesses being discussed.

*   **Unclear Loss Function:** The description of the optimization procedure for the neural network parameters (lines 133-155) is difficult to follow. Could the authors please provide a more precise definition of the "loss function" in Eq. 15 and explain its role in the overall inference scheme?

*   **Inappropriate Evaluation Metric:** Mean Squared Error (MSE/PMSE) is not an appropriate evaluation metric for models based on the Poisson likelihood. The evaluation should be supplemented with, or replaced by, a more suitable metric like perplexity or predictive log-likelihood, which directly measures the model's fit to the count data.

*   **Experimental Setup:** The number of latent components, K, used in the experiments (line 207) appears to be quite small. The authors should consider experimenting with larger values (e.g., K=100) to better assess the model's capacity.

*   **Clarity of Figures and Discussion:** The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and the results presented in Figure 3 is not clear. The authors should revise the text to more explicitly link their narrative to the figure. Additionally, the caption for Figure 4 is illegible and should be reformatted.

*   **Presentation and Readability:**
    *   To improve the readability of the main text, the detailed variational update equations (Eqs. 11-14) could be moved to the supplementary material.
    *   The authors are encouraged to review the manuscript for overuse of the passive voice, particularly in the abstract, to improve clarity and impact.
    *   The manuscript suffers from poor writing quality and contains numerous grammatical errors and typos that hinder readability. A thorough proofreading is necessary. Examples include:
        *   Line 15: "high-dimensional"
        *   Line 22: "factorize"
        *   Line 24: "property of the"
        *   Lines 29, 34: "Dirichlet"
        *   Line 34: "nested"
        *   Fig. 1: "visual representation"
        *   Fig. 1: "transmission" (this may not be the most appropriate term)
        *   Incomplete sentences (e.g., line 49)
        *   Numerous missing articles throughout the text (e.g., "Although Dirichlet distribution is often used as prior distribution").
