Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for sequential count data. Building on Poisson factorization (PF), the model augments factor scores with a multi-layer temporal structure intended to capture both short- and long-range dependencies. The time dynamics are parameterized by neural networks: each layer’s latent variables are conditioned on a temporal window of variables from adjacent layers, and shape parameters of Gamma-distributed latents are produced by recurrent networks. Inference is performed via mean-field variational inference (VI) for the latent variables, combined with a separate neural-network training stage using an autoencoder-like objective. The model includes feature- and component-specific Gamma variables (ψ, λ) to encourage sparsity and smoothness. Experiments compare DDPFA to PGDS (Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System), LSTM, and a static PFA on synthetic data and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population).

The work sits at the intersection of dynamic PF (e.g., PGDS; recurrent PF in recommender systems) and deep latent factor models (deep exponential families, deep PF, gamma belief networks), as well as RNN-based sequence models (e.g., LSTMs, autoregressive topic models). The contribution aims to combine PF with a deep temporal parameterization of Gamma latent variables via neural networks.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to integrate deep temporal structure with Poisson factorization for sequential counts.
- Uses Gamma–Poisson conjugacy with auxiliary counts to derive tractable VI updates, which can scale.
- Incorporates feature- and component-level sparsity controls (ψ, λ), which can improve interpretability.
- Broad empirical scope across diverse real datasets; qualitative analysis claims persistent, interpretable factors.

Major concerns and questions
- Temporal architecture: Eq. 4 does not describe a standard RNN. h_t^(n) depends on a window of top-layer variables (h^(n+1)) rather than on h_{t-1}^{(n)} from the same layer. Please justify this design choice and discuss trade-offs versus conventional intra-layer recurrences. If intended as a ladder-style top-down temporal prior, make this explicit and provide the precise conditional graphs.
- Incomplete or ambiguous model specification: The distributions in Eqs. 3–4 are not fully parameterized (e.g., which Gamma shape/rate, how computed). Please provide explicit forms for p(θ_t | h^(0)_{t−c:t}) and p(h_t^(n) | h^{(n+1)}_{t−c:t}), including the exact neural parameterizations, the choice of activation to ensure positivity, and the windowing indices.
- “Implicit distributions”: The text describes Eqs. 9–10 as “implicit.” However, both are explicit Gamma posteriors with known shapes and rates (given the neural outputs). Please revise the terminology or clarify what aspect is implicit (e.g., the deterministic mapping from inputs to Gamma parameters via neural nets).
- Probability autoencoder objective: Eq. 15 and its approximations (Eqs. 16–18) are unclear. What exact likelihood/objective is optimized with respect to network weights? How is the integral in Eq. 15 derived, and how do you justify the substitutions leading to squared-error terms in Eqs. 16–18? Please connect this to standard variational autoencoder or amortized VI formulations (e.g., ELBO with recognition/decoder networks), and explain why reparameterization or score-function estimators are not used here. A derivation and discussion of bias introduced by replacing distributions with expectations would help.
- Evaluation metrics: MSE/PMSE alone are not well aligned with PF objectives. Please report predictive log-likelihood (held-out likelihood of counts), perplexity for text, or negative binomial/Poisson deviance. Also include uncertainty-sensitive metrics where appropriate.
- Model capacity: The chosen K (e.g., K=3 or K=10) appears too small for datasets like ICEWS or NIPS. Please include results for larger K (e.g., 50–200), with sensitivity analyses for K, depth N, and window size c.
- Empirical clarity: The narrative around Fig. 3 (lines 224–232) is hard to follow; please spell out what each factor represents, how factor trajectories were constructed, and how these relate to the events in ICEWS. Ensure captions are legible and self-contained.
- Derivation placement: The VI update equations (Eqs. 11–14) can be moved to the supplement; use main text space to clarify modeling and learning choices.
- Reproducibility: Provide training details (optimizers, learning rates, initialization, early stopping), compute budgets, and wall-clock times for DDPFA and baselines. Clarify the ladder variant used and ablation results (with/without ladder, with/without ψ/λ).

Quality
- The core idea is plausible, but the current presentation leaves key distributions and learning objectives insufficiently specified. Claims about long-range dependence would be stronger with predictive likelihood gains, larger K, and ablations. The autoencoder losses need a principled justification or an ELBO-based alternative.

Clarity
- The paper needs substantial editing for grammar and precision. Define all distributions with parameters, unify notation for shape/rate, and improve figures/captions. Avoid calling explicit Gamma conditionals “implicit.”

Originality
- Combining PF with a deep temporal parameterization is interesting but overlaps with recurrent PF and deep exponential families. The novelty lies in the ladder-like temporal conditioning and ψ/λ controls; emphasize how this differs from prior recurrent PF and deep PF work.

Significance
- Potentially useful for sequential count modeling if shown to outperform strong baselines on predictive likelihood and interpretability at realistic K. Current evidence is mixed (e.g., ICEWS) and underpowered by capacity choices and metrics.

Pros for acceptance
- Integrates deep temporal structure with PF while retaining conjugate updates.
- Empirical breadth and qualitative interpretability claims.
- Addresses known issues (e.g., pruning) and explores ladder architectures.

Cons against acceptance
- Model and objective insufficiently specified; misuse of “implicit.”
- Evaluation metrics and capacity choices limit conclusions.
- Figures and exposition hinder understanding; several unclear passages.

Suggestions for revision
- Specify all conditionals (Eqs. 3–4) precisely; justify the cross-layer temporal windowing versus same-layer recurrences.
- Replace or augment squared-error objectives with ELBO-based training and report predictive log-likelihood.
- Run with larger K and report sensitivity to K, N, and c; include stronger baselines (e.g., recurrent PF, deep exponential families).
- Clarify the connection to Fig. 3; fix figure readability.
- Move algebraic updates to the supplement; use main text for modeling choices and intuition.
- Substantially edit for grammar and style; prefer active voice in the abstract.

Writing and minor issues
- Lines 30–31: “fanatical data” is unclear; do you mean financial data or another domain? Please clarify.
- Examples of typos/usage to fix: “high-dimensional”; “factorize/factorization”; “property of the”; “Dirichlet”; “nested”; “visual representation”; avoid “transmission” for temporal dependency; fix incomplete sentences and missing articles (e.g., “a Dirichlet distribution as a prior”).
- Make Fig. 4 caption and axes legible.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject in current form. The idea is promising, but the paper needs clearer model specification, a principled training objective, stronger and more appropriate evaluations, and substantial editing.
