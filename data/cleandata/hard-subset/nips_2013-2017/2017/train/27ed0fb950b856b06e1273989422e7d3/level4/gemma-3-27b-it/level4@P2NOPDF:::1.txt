This paper introduces the deep dynamic Poisson factorization (DDPF) model, an extension of Poisson Factorization designed to capture temporal dependencies. Unlike prior work on dynamic PF, this approach utilizes a simplified recurrent neural network to model long-term relationships. Inference is performed via variational inference, incorporating an additional optimization step for the neural network parameters. The model is evaluated on five real-world datasets.

Overall, the core idea is potentially interesting, but significant improvements are needed before the paper would be suitable for acceptance at a conference like NeurIPS. I outline my concerns below.

The formulation presented in Eq. 4 does not fully represent a recurrent neural network (RNN) structure. The hidden state, h<sub>t</sub><sup>(n)</sup>, appears to depend only on top-layer memory vectors and lacks the recurrent connection to previous time steps within the same layer, which is characteristic of standard RNNs. The authors should justify this structural simplification.

Section 2 contains missing equations that hinder understanding. Specifically, the parameterization of the Gamma distribution in Eq. 4 is unclear, and the distribution from which θ<sub>t</sub> is generated in Eq. 3 is not defined. 

I disagree with the characterization of the distributions in lines 110-118 as “implicit.” Equations 9 and 10 provide explicit Gamma distributions with defined shape and rate parameters. Given that the distribution of θ<sub>tk</sub> (Eq. 9) can be computed in closed form conditioned on h<sup>(0)</sup>, it cannot accurately be described as implicit. The same applies to h in Eq. 10.

The statement in lines 30-31 regarding the method’s limitations with “fanatical data and disaster data” is unclear and requires elaboration. What specific characteristics of these data types pose a challenge for the proposed model?

The subsection in lines 133-155 is difficult to follow. The meaning of the “loss function” in Eq. 15 requires clarification.

Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Percentage Mean Squared Error (PMSE) are not ideal metrics for evaluating Poisson Factorization models. The results should be supplemented with predictive log-likelihood to provide a more appropriate assessment of model performance.

The value of K used in the experiments (line 207) appears insufficient. I recommend exploring larger values, such as K=100 or greater, to potentially improve performance.

The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and Figure 3 is unclear. The figure does not adequately support or illustrate the points being made.

The update equations for the variational inference procedure (Eqs. 11-14) can be moved to the supplementary material to streamline the main text.

The caption for Figure 4 is illegible and needs to be revised for clarity.

I strongly encourage the authors to review and address the frequent use of the passive voice throughout the paper, particularly in the abstract, to improve readability. 

The overall writing quality requires substantial improvement, and the paper contains numerous typos and grammatical errors. Examples include:

*   Line 15: “high-dimensional”
*   Line 22: “factorize”
*   Line 24: “property of the”
*   Lines 29, 34: “Dirichlet”
*   Line 34: “nested”
*   Fig. 1: “visual representation”
*   Fig. 1: “transmission” (the appropriateness of this term should be reconsidered)
*   Incomplete sentences (e.g., line 49)
*   Missing articles (e.g., “Although Dirichlet distribution is often used as prior distribution”)
