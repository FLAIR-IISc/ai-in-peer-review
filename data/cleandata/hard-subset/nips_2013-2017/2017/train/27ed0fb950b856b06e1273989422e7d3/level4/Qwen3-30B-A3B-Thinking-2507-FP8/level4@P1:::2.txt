

The authors propose Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a novel model for sequential count data analysis that integrates recurrent neural networks (RNNs) with Poisson Factor Analysis to capture complex temporal dependencies. Building on prior work like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis, DDPFA leverages RNNs to model implicit distributions over latent factors, enabling both short-term local relationships (via shallow layers) and long-term temporal dependencies (via deep structure). The model employs variational inference with a two-stage optimization: mean-field updates for latent variables and neural network parameter learning via a loss function inspired by variational autoencoders. Experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) demonstrate competitive performance against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA baselines, particularly in capturing multi-factor temporal patterns.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Technical Novelty (Originality):** The integration of RNNs to model implicit distributions over latent factors represents a meaningful advancement over prior shallow dynamic Poisson models. The deep structure explicitly addresses long-range dependencies—a limitation in PGDS (which assumes conditional independence between θ_{t-1} and θ_{t+1} given θ_t) and LSTM (which struggles with high-dimensional or long-sequence data). The use of Gamma distributions with RNN-approximated shape parameters is well-motivated for count data.  
2. **Empirical Rigor (Quality):** The evaluation covers diverse synthetic datasets (with varying temporal patterns) and five real-world applications, demonstrating robustness across domains. Results (Tables 1–2) show clear advantages over baselines in fitting and prediction tasks (e.g., DDPFA outperforms PGDS in Ebola/ASP datasets where PGDS fails to capture complex dependencies). The interpretability of learned factors (Figures 2–3) effectively supports the model’s value for domain analysis.  
3. **Clarity (Clarity):** The paper is well-structured, with clear mathematical formalization (Equations 1–18) and a logical progression from problem motivation to inference. The experimental setup (Section 4) is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Methodological Gaps (Quality):**  
   - The model assumes fixed scale parameters (β) for Gamma distributions (Equations 8–10), while only shape parameters (α) are RNN-approximated. This choice lacks justification and may limit flexibility—e.g., if scale parameters vary across time steps or features, the model’s expressiveness is constrained. A discussion of this assumption’s impact on performance (e.g., via ablation studies) is needed.  
   - The ablation study (Table 3) only tests layer counts (2 vs. 3 layers) and bottom-layer dimensionality, omitting critical comparisons: (a) single-layer DDPFA vs. standard PFA, and (b) varying window sizes (c) beyond the fixed c=4. Without these, it’s unclear whether the deep structure *necessarily* improves performance over simpler variants. The claim that "adding layers beyond two yields diminishing returns" (Table 3) is thus under-supported.  
2. **Presentation and Context (Clarity/Originality):**  
   - Equation 10 contains a critical typo (h^{(n)}_{tk} should be h^{(n)}_{t,k}), which disrupts comprehension. More generally, the presentation is rushed: Section 2’s description of the RNN-structured prior/posterior (Equations 3–4) lacks a clear diagram (Figure 1 is referenced but not described), making the architecture hard to visualize.  
   - The Introduction omits key recent work. Zhou et al.’s (JMLR 2016) *Augmentable Gamma Belief Networks* and Cong et al.’s (ICML 2017) *Deep Latent Dirichlet Allocation* both address deep Poisson factorization—explicitly comparing DDPFA to these would strengthen the novelty claim. The paper cites Zhou 2016 as [25] but fails to discuss its relevance.  
3. **Significance Limitations:** While the model shows strong empirical results, the "pruning" issue (where latent dimensions capture noise) and the observation that ladder-like architecture (Section 5) is necessary for performance (contrary to hierarchical VAEs) suggest unresolved challenges. The authors note this is due to mean-field VI but do not explore alternatives (e.g., MCMC), limiting the paper’s contribution to inference methodology.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Accept** (pro: strong novelty and results; con: unresolved methodological gaps and presentation issues). The core idea is compelling and well-executed for most datasets, but the lack of ablation studies for key design choices (layer depth, window size) and the omission of recent deep Poisson models weaken the case for acceptance. The technical contributions are significant but require more rigorous justification to fully convince the community.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
- **Pro:** Novel deep structure for long-range temporal dependencies in count data; strong empirical results across diverse datasets; clear interpretability of latent factors.  
- **Con:** Critical typo in Equation 10; insufficient ablation study (missing single-layer/layer-depth comparisons); inadequate discussion of fixed-scale parameter assumption; omission of key recent work (Zhou 2016, Cong 2017); rushed presentation.  

The paper makes a valuable contribution to dynamic count modeling but would benefit from addressing these points to strengthen its technical foundation and contextualize its novelty. With revisions, it could be an excellent fit for NIPS.
