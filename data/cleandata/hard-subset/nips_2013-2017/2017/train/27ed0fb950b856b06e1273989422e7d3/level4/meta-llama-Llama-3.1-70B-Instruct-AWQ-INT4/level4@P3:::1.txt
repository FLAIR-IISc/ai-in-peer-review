This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, a novel approach that extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) to capture temporal dependencies in sequentially observed count vectors. The proposed model leverages a recurrent neural network structure to represent implicit distributions and learn complicated relationships between different factors over time. Variational inference is employed to estimate the latent variables, and the parameters of the neural networks are learned through a loss function derived from the variational distribution.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the DDPFA model on four multi-dimensional synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets, including the Integrated Crisis Early Warning System (ICEWS), NIPS corpus, Ebola corpus, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population. The results show that the DDPFA model outperforms existing methods, such as Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), and PFA, in terms of fitting and prediction tasks.

Strengths of the paper:

1. The proposed DDPFA model provides a novel approach to capturing temporal dependencies in sequentially observed count vectors, which is a challenging problem in many real-world applications.
2. The use of a recurrent neural network structure to represent implicit distributions is a key contribution of the paper, allowing the model to learn complicated relationships between different factors over time.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the DDPFA model on various datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in comparison to existing methods.

Weaknesses of the paper:

1. The formulation of the DDPFA model in Eq. 4 does not correspond to a traditional RNN structure, as it only depends on the top-layer memory vectors and not on previous time steps for the same layer. The authors should clarify the reasoning behind this simplified structure.
2. Some model equations are missing or unclear, such as the parameterization of the Gamma distribution in Eq. 4 and the distribution that generates theta_t in Eq. 3. The authors should provide more details on these aspects.
3. The discussion on "implicit distributions" in lines 110-118 is misleading, as the distributions in Eqs. 9 and 10 are explicit Gammas with given shapes and rates.
4. The writing quality is poor, with many typos, incomplete sentences, and missing articles.

Suggestions for improvement:

1. Clarify the formulation of the DDPFA model in Eq. 4 and provide more details on the parameterization of the Gamma distribution and the distribution that generates theta_t in Eq. 3.
2. Revise the discussion on "implicit distributions" to accurately reflect the explicit nature of the distributions in Eqs. 9 and 10.
3. Improve the writing quality by addressing the numerous typos, incomplete sentences, and missing articles.
4. Consider including additional evaluation metrics, such as predictive log-likelihood, to provide a more comprehensive assessment of the DDPFA model's performance.

Arguments for acceptance:

1. The proposed DDPFA model provides a novel approach to capturing temporal dependencies in sequentially observed count vectors, which is a challenging problem in many real-world applications.
2. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the DDPFA model on various datasets, showing its potential for real-world applications.

Arguments against acceptance:

1. The paper requires significant improvements in terms of clarity, writing quality, and technical details to meet the standards of a top-tier conference like NIPS.
2. The authors should address the concerns raised regarding the formulation of the DDPFA model and the discussion on "implicit distributions" to ensure the accuracy and validity of the proposed approach.
