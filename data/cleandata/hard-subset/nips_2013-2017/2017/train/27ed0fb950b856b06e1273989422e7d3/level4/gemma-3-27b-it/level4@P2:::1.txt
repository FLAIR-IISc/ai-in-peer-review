This paper introduces the deep dynamic Poisson factorization model (DDPFA), a novel approach building upon Poisson Factorization (PF) to model temporal dependencies in sequential count data. Unlike prior work on dynamic PF, DDPFA utilizes a recurrent neural network structure – albeit a simplified one – to capture long-term dependencies. Variational inference is employed for parameter estimation, with an additional optimization step focused on the neural network parameters. The method is evaluated on five real-world datasets.

Overall, the core idea presented is potentially interesting, but the paper requires substantial refinement to meet the standards expected at NIPS. The presentation lacks clarity in several areas and the experimental evaluation could be significantly improved.

The formulation in Equation 4 does not fully align with the standard recurrent neural network (RNN) architecture. Specifically, h<sub>t</sub><sup>(n)</sup> depends only on the top-layer memory vectors and lacks the dependency on previous time steps *within the same layer* characteristic of typical RNNs. The motivation for this simplified structure should be clarified.

Section 2 contains incomplete model descriptions. For example, the parameterization of the Gamma distributions in Equation 4 is not specified, nor is the distribution generating θ<sub>t</sub> in Equation 3 clearly defined. Providing these details is crucial for reproducibility and complete understanding.

The authors' description of “implicit distributions” in lines 110-118 is inaccurate. Equations 9 and 10 define explicit Gamma distributions with clearly defined shape and rate parameters. Given that the distribution of θ<sub>tk</sub> (Eq. 9) can be computed in closed form conditional on h<sup>(0)</sup>, it cannot be rightfully termed “implicit.” A similar observation applies to h in Equation 10.

The statement in lines 30-31 regarding the limitations of previous methods when analyzing "fanatical data and disaster data" is vague and requires further elaboration. Providing concrete examples or explaining the specific challenges posed by these data types would strengthen the argument.

The subsection spanning lines 133-155 is unclear. The meaning and derivation of the "loss function" in Equation 15 are not sufficiently explained. A more detailed breakdown of the loss function's components and its role in the optimization process is needed.

The exclusive use of Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Predictive Mean Squared Error (PMSE) as evaluation metrics is questionable for a model of this type. Predictive log-likelihood is a more standard and informative metric for evaluating probabilistic factorization models. Including this metric would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation.

The choice of K (number of factors) in the experiments (line 207) appears small. Consider using larger values, such as K=100 or greater, to better assess the model's capacity and performance.

The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and Figure 3 is not readily apparent. Clarifying how Figure 3 illustrates the concepts described in the preceding text is crucial.

The detailed update equations for the variational inference procedure (Equations 11-14) can be relegated to the supplementary material, as they detract from the main flow of the paper.

The caption for Figure 4 is unreadable. Please revise it to provide a clear and informative description of the figure’s content.

The writing style could be improved by reducing the use of the passive voice, particularly in the abstract and introduction. A more active voice would enhance clarity and readability.

The paper suffers from poor writing quality and numerous typographical errors. Examples include: “high-dimensional” (line 15), “factorize” (line 22), “property of the” (line 24), “Dirichlet” (lines 29, 34), “nested” (line 34), "visual representation" (Fig. 1), "transmission" (Fig. 1), and numerous missing articles (e.g., “Although Dirichlet distribution is often used as prior distribution”). A thorough proofread is essential.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The idea of combining PF with deep learning for sequential count data is novel and potentially valuable.
*   The experiments demonstrate promising results on several real-world datasets, suggesting the model’s practical applicability.
*   The authors attempt to provide a comprehensive inference procedure via variational inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its formulation, explanation, and presentation.
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete and relies on potentially misleading metrics.
*   The implementation of the RNN structure is unconventional and lacks justification.
*   The writing quality is poor, hindering understanding and reproducibility.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Reject. While the core idea has merit, the current manuscript requires substantial revisions and improvements before it can be considered for acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to address the clarity issues, strengthen the experimental evaluation, and refine the presentation. I would be willing to reconsider a revised version of this paper.
