This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPF) model, which extends Poisson Factorization to incorporate temporal dependencies. Unlike previous dynamic PF models, DDPF employs a simplified recurrent neural network structure to capture long-term temporal patterns. The authors develop a variational inference algorithm, which includes an additional optimization step for the neural network parameters. The model is evaluated on five real-world datasets.

While the core idea of integrating a deep, recurrent structure into dynamic Poisson factorization is promising, the paper in its current form has significant weaknesses in its methodological description, experimental validation, and overall presentation. I believe substantial revisions are required before it can be considered for publication at NIPS.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Model Formulation and Clarity:** The description of the model is incomplete and contains several confusing or questionable statements.
    *   **RNN Architecture:** The formulation in Eq. 4 does not seem to correspond to a standard RNN. The hidden state at a given layer, h_t^{(n)}, depends on the memory vectors from the layer above but not on the hidden state from the previous time step at the same layer, h_{t-1}^{(n)}. This is a significant deviation from conventional RNNs. The authors should provide a clear justification for this simplified design choice and discuss its implications.
    *   **Incomplete Generative Process:** Key components of the model are not specified. For instance, in Section 2, it is unclear how the Gamma distribution in Eq. 4 is parameterized. Furthermore, the prior distribution for the topic proportions, theta_t in Eq. 3, is not defined. These omissions make it difficult to fully understand the generative process and reproduce the work.
    *   **Misuse of "Implicit Distributions":** I disagree with the discussion in lines 110-118 regarding "implicit distributions." Equations 9 and 10 define explicit conditional distributions for theta_tk and h, respectively. They are Gamma distributions with well-defined shape and rate parameters, which can be computed in closed form. The term "implicit distribution" is typically reserved for cases where one can only sample from a distribution but cannot write down its density, which is not the case here. This section should be corrected.
    *   **Unclear "Loss Function":** The subsection "Optimizing RNN Parameters" (lines 133-155) is confusing. It is not clear what the "loss function" in Eq. 15 represents or how it fits into the variational inference framework. Is it part of the ELBO, or is it a separate objective? The relationship between the variational updates and the optimization of the RNN parameters needs to be explained more formally and clearly.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup and results are not convincing enough to demonstrate the superiority of the proposed model.
    *   **Inappropriate Evaluation Metric:** The primary metric used is Mean Squared Error (MSE) / Per-word MSE (PMSE). These metrics are poorly suited for count data and models based on the Poisson distribution. A much stronger evaluation would include standard metrics for topic models, such as predictive log-likelihood or perplexity on a held-out test set.
    *   **Insufficient Model Capacity (K):** The number of latent factors was set to K=20 in the experiments. This value seems too small for the scale and potential complexity of the datasets analyzed. The authors should perform a sensitivity analysis with larger values of K (e.g., K=50, 100, 200) to ensure that the model capacity is not a limiting factor in performance.
    *   **Clarity of Results:** The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and the visualization in Figure 3 is not apparent. The authors need to explicitly guide the reader through the figure and explain how it supports their claims about the model's ability to capture temporal dynamics.

**Minor Concerns and Presentation Issues**

*   **Writing Quality:** The paper suffers from poor writing quality, with numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasings that impede readability. For example:
    *   Typos: "high-dimentional" (line 15), "factoraize" (line 22), "propaty of the" (line 24), "Dirichret" (lines 29, 34), "nasted" (line 34).
    *   Grammar: Many sentences are missing articles (e.g., "Although Dirichlet distribution is often used as prior distribution"), and some are incomplete (e.g., line 49).
    *   Clarity: The sentence "This method may have weak points in analyzing other data with different pattern long-time dependence, such as fanatical data and disaster data" (lines 30-31) is vague. The authors should elaborate on what specific patterns they are referring to.
*   **Presentation:**
    *   The variational update equations (Eqs. 11-14) are standard derivations and could be moved to the supplementary material to improve the flow of the main text.
    *   The caption for Figure 4 is illegible.
    *   The abstract and other sections overuse the passive voice. Rewriting in the active voice would make the paper more direct and engaging.
    *   In Figure 1, the word "transmission" seems inappropriate in this context; "propagation" or "evolution" might be better. The caption should also be corrected to "visual representation."

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting direction, it requires a major revision to address the fundamental issues in model description, experimental rigor, and overall clarity before it can meet the standards of a top-tier conference like NIPS.
