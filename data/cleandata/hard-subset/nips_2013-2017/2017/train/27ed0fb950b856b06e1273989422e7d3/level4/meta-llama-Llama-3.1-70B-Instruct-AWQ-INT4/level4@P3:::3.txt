The paper proposes a novel model, called Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model is an extension of the traditional Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and incorporates a deep structure to capture long-range temporal dependencies. The authors employ a recurrent neural network-like structure to model the temporal dependencies and use variational inference for parameter estimation.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the existing works on dynamic Poisson factorization models. The technical contents of the paper are good, and the authors provide a detailed description of the proposed model and its inference algorithm.

The experiments are comprehensive, and the authors evaluate the performance of the proposed model on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The results show that the proposed model outperforms existing methods, including Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. Firstly, the paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods, particularly dynamic PF with one level deep architecture. This would help to illustrate the utility of the proposed method with multiple levels of hierarchy. Secondly, the authors could provide more insights into the interpretability of the learned factors and how they capture long-range temporal dependencies.

Additionally, there are some grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper, which make it slightly difficult to read. These errors should be corrected to improve the overall clarity and readability of the paper.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper meets the criteria of quality, clarity, and originality. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated description of the proposed model, and the technical contents are sound. The experiments are comprehensive, and the results are well-presented. However, the paper could benefit from more insights into the interpretability of the learned factors and a more detailed comparison with existing methods.
