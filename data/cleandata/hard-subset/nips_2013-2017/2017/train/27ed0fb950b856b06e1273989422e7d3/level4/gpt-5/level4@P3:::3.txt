Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count data that augments Poisson factorization with a deep, recurrent structure to capture both short- and long-range temporal dependencies. The observation model is Poisson with loadings Φ and time-varying factor scores Θ, further modulated by feature-wise (Ψ) and component-wise (Λ) Gamma priors to promote sparsity and smoothness. The temporal dynamics are modeled via multiple latent layers H(0:N) with recurrent neural networks specifying implicit conditional Gamma distributions over each layer, and a “probability autoencoder” objective couples bottom-up and top-down networks. Inference proceeds in two stages: (i) mean-field variational updates exploiting Gamma–Poisson conjugacy with auxiliary latent counts; and (ii) gradient-based training of the recurrent networks via squared-error objectives motivated as surrogates to a (partially intractable) ELBO.

DDPFA is positioned relative to prior dynamic Poisson factorization and gamma–Poisson models (e.g., PGDS and related works) that use Markovian Gamma transitions, which may have limited ability to capture long-range dependencies. The paper also discusses connections to deep latent variable models (deep exponential families, deep PFAs, deep GPs), and to autoregressive neural models. Empirically, DDPFA is compared to PGDS, PFA, and LSTM on synthetic data and several real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population), with metrics focused on MSE and one-step PMSE; qualitative factor analyses are also provided.

Strengths
- Addresses an important problem: modeling temporal dependencies in high-dimensional sparse count data.
- Novel modeling idea combining conjugate Poisson factorization with deep, recurrently parameterized Gamma conditionals.
- Uses a practical two-stage inference scheme (conjugate mean-field updates + neural parameter learning) that scales to sizable datasets.
- Empirical results show improvements over PGDS, PFA, and LSTM on several tasks; qualitative analyses suggest more persistent factors.
- Discusses architectural considerations (e.g., ladder structure) and practical issues (e.g., “pruning”).

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Objective and inference clarity: The paper departs from a standard ELBO and optimizes squared-error losses (Eqs. 16–18) to train the recurrent networks. The connection to a coherent variational objective is not clearly derived. It is unclear whether these losses yield a valid lower bound or introduce bias/double-counting. Please define the exact ELBO and justify the surrogate losses or provide an alternative stochastic variational approach. Note that reparameterizations for Gamma variables (or implicit reparameterization) are available and could enable end-to-end VI.
- Identifiability and scaling: Including Φ, Θ, Λ, and Ψ yields scale non-identifiability (several positive matrices multiplying). The paper mentions absorbing Λ, Ψ into Θ but retains them for sparsity. Please clarify how priors or constraints resolve scaling ambiguity and how this affects interpretability and optimization.
- Evaluation metrics: For count models, predictive log-likelihood, Poisson deviance, or held-out perplexity are standard and more comparable than MSE/PMSE. Please add held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity and, if applicable, negative binomial deviance.
- Ablations to justify depth: While the paper argues for multi-layer temporal hierarchy, Table 3 suggests limited gains from adding layers, and the ladder variant helps most. Please add ablations that isolate depth versus recurrence, including: (a) a single-layer dynamic PF with the same RNN parameterization; (b) DDPFA with one layer versus two/three; and (c) a non-recurrent deep PF (to isolate temporal vs hierarchical effects).
- Baselines: Since [12] (Recurrent Poisson Factorization) is cited, including it as a baseline would strengthen the case that DDPFA’s gains come from the proposed hierarchical structure rather than simply using an RNN for dynamics.
- Experimental protocol: Hyperparameter tuning, early stopping, and fairness across methods are not clearly described. Please detail validation splits, tuning ranges for K, window c, network sizes, and convergence diagnostics. Runtime comparisons would also be informative.
- Bernoulli–Poisson link: The paper states b = 1(x > 1); typically b = 1(x > 0). Please confirm and correct if needed.
- References are incomplete and in several cases malformed; please provide full bibliographic details.

Quality
- The modeling component is technically interesting, but the training objective needs a clearer probabilistic grounding. The mean-field updates for conjugate parts are sound.
- The empirical results are promising, but lack stronger probabilistic metrics and key ablations to substantiate the depth claim.

Clarity
- The paper’s organization is reasonable, but grammar issues and inconsistent notation distract. The “Probability AutoEncoder” section needs a more careful derivation and clearer notation (e.g., what is optimized, where expectations are taken, and how gradients flow).

Originality
- The integration of deep recurrently parameterized Gamma conditionals within a PF framework is novel. The ladder-style coupling in a PF context is also interesting.

Significance
- If the objective is clarified and ablations confirm that depth materially improves long-range modeling, the approach could be impactful for sequential count data in topics, events, and recommendations.

Specific requests and questions
- Provide the exact ELBO and show how the learning objectives approximate or bound it. If using surrogates, justify why they target the desired posterior and discuss potential biases.
- Add held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity and Poisson deviance, and report confidence intervals or variability.
- Include a one-layer dynamic PF with RNN transitions as an ablation to isolate the benefit of multiple hierarchical layers.
- Clarify the Bernoulli–Poisson threshold and correct if necessary.
- Bold the best numbers in Table 2 and ensure consistent significant figures.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem with a creative modeling approach.
- Empirical gains on multiple datasets; qualitative analyses suggest more persistent factors.
- Practical inference leveraging conjugacy plus neural parameterization.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficiently justified training objective; unclear connection to a principled ELBO.
- Missing key ablations to support the hierarchical depth claim; some contradictory observations.
- Evaluation lacks standard probabilistic metrics; some factual/notation errors and incomplete references.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and the empirical results are encouraging, but the paper needs a clearer probabilistic objective, stronger and fairer evaluation (including held-out likelihood and ablations), and improved clarity/grammar. I encourage the authors to revise along these lines; with these improvements, the work could be a strong contribution.
