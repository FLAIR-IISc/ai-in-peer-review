

This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count data, extending Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with a deep neural network structure to capture long-term temporal dependencies. The authors propose using recurrent neural networks to model implicit distributions that capture complex temporal relationships, with variational inference used for parameter estimation. The paper compares DDPFA against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA on synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper makes a reasonable attempt to address an important problem in sequential count data analysis, which is relevant to NIPS. It builds on prior work in dynamic Poisson factorization (e.g., PGDS [2]) and deep probabilistic models (e.g., Deep Exponential Families [8], Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9-10]), though the novelty appears limited to the specific implementation of neural networks within the Poisson factorization framework.

Strengths:
- The model addresses a meaningful problem in analyzing sequential count data, which has applications in recommendation systems, text analysis, and time series analysis.
- The experimental section demonstrates the model on both synthetic and real-world datasets, including interesting cases like ICEWS, NIPS corpus, and Ebola outbreak data.
- The authors provide a clear motivation for why standard approaches (like PGDS) may not capture complex long-term dependencies.

Weaknesses:
1. The technical formulation contains significant issues. In Equation 4, the proposed model does not correspond to a standard RNN structure, as $h_t^{(n)}$ depends only on the top-layer memory vectors without incorporating previous time steps for the same layer, which is a fundamental aspect of RNNs. The authors should clarify why this simplified structure was chosen and how it differs from conventional RNN implementations.

2. The model equations are incomplete and confusing. For instance, Equation 3 lacks specification of how the Gamma distribution is parameterized, and Equation 4 does not clarify how the Gamma distribution is generated. The discussion of "implicit distributions" in lines 110-118 is misleading, as Equations 9 and 10 are explicitly Gamma distributions with closed-form parameters conditioned on latent variables.

3. The paper contains several unclear statements. The reference to "fanatical data and disaster data" (lines 30-31) is vague and unhelpful without specific examples. The discussion of "pruning" and "uselessness of adding network layers" (lines 224-232) lacks connection to the figures and results presented.

4. The evaluation metrics are inappropriate. Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Predicted MSE (PMSE) are not standard metrics for Poisson factorization models, which typically use predictive log-likelihood or other probabilistic metrics. The authors should report these more appropriate metrics to properly evaluate their model's performance.

5. The experimental setup has issues. The choice of K=3 for most datasets (line 207) appears unrealistically small for meaningful analysis of these datasets (e.g., NIPS corpus with V=14,036 features). The authors should justify this choice or conduct experiments with larger K values (e.g., K=100).

6. The writing quality is poor, with numerous grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper. Examples include "high-dimensional" (line 15), "factorize" (line 22), "property of the" (line 24), and missing articles (e.g., "Although Dirichlet distribution is often used as prior distribution" - line 34). The abstract should be rewritten to avoid passive voice.

7. The loss function in Equation 15 is not properly defined, and the derivation in lines 133-155 is unclear. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of how the neural network parameters are optimized.

8. Several figures and captions are problematic. The caption for Figure 4 is unreadable, and Figure 2's connection to the discussion in lines 224-232 is not clear.

Recommendation:
While the problem addressed is relevant to NIPS, the paper requires substantial revisions before it can be considered for publication. The technical formulation needs significant clarification and correction, the evaluation metrics should be changed to more appropriate ones for Poisson factorization, and the writing quality needs improvement. The novelty appears limited, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work on deep Poisson factorization models.

Pro: The problem addressed is relevant to NIPS and has practical applications. The experimental evaluation on multiple datasets is a positive aspect.

Con: The technical formulation contains serious errors, the evaluation metrics are inappropriate, the writing quality is poor, and the novelty is limited compared to prior work.

Given these issues, I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that with substantial revisions addressing the technical and presentation flaws, the paper could be reconsidered. The authors should focus on clarifying the model structure, using appropriate evaluation metrics, and improving the writing quality before resubmitting.
