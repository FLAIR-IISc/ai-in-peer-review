

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a novel approach for analyzing sequential count data by extending Poisson Factor Analysis with deep recurrent structures. The authors build upon previous work in dynamic Poisson models like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis, but introduce a deep hierarchical structure where recurrent neural networks model the implicit distributions governing temporal dependencies. The model uses variational inference for latent variables and gradient descent for neural network parameters, with the key innovation being the use of RNNs to capture complex long-term dependencies through a deep latent structure. The approach is evaluated on synthetic datasets and five real-world applications including ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA outbreak data, disaster events, and sheep population counts.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses an important problem in sequential count data analysis, where existing methods like PGDS and LSTM struggle with long-term dependencies. The DDPFA model demonstrates promising results on most datasets, particularly in capturing interpretable latent structures that reflect meaningful temporal patterns (e.g., the Israel-Palestinian conflict and six-party talks in ICEWS data). The model's ability to learn long-term dependencies through deep latent structure represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The experimental design is comprehensive, including both synthetic and real-world datasets, and the comparison with PGDS, LSTM, and PFA provides a strong benchmark.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the model description contains critical typos (e.g., Equation 10), which affects clarity and professionalism. Second, the authors fix the scale parameter in Gamma distributions while only updating shape parameters, a choice that requires more thorough justification. The draft review correctly points out that this parameterization decision needs detailed discussion, as it significantly impacts the model's flexibility and interpretability.

Third, the ablation studies are insufficient. The authors only test the model with two layers without comparing to single-layer models or exploring deeper architectures, making it difficult to assess the true impact of the deep structure. The paper also fails to systematically analyze how key hyperparameters (window size, number of factors) affect performance. The supplementary material (Table 3) hints at "pruning" and "uselessness of adding network layers" but doesn't provide adequate analysis of these phenomena.

Fourth, the paper doesn't sufficiently contextualize its contribution against recent deep Poisson factorization work. The draft review correctly identifies that papers like Zhou et al. (2016) and Cong et al. (2017) should be discussed in the introduction, as they represent more recent approaches to similar problems.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with a clear variational inference framework. However, the lack of ablation studies and insufficient parameter analysis weakens the quality assessment. The claim that deep structure improves performance needs stronger empirical support.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured but suffers from several typos and unclear explanations of the model architecture. A more detailed graphical illustration of the entire model would significantly improve clarity, as suggested in the draft review. The explanation of how the RNNs parameterize the Gamma distributions could be more precise.

**Originality:** The paper builds on existing work in Poisson factor analysis and deep latent models but provides a novel combination of deep structure with Poisson factorization. However, the contribution is somewhat incremental given recent work in deep Poisson factorization (e.g., Zhou et al., 2016; Cong et al., 2017), and the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with these approaches.

**Significance:** The model addresses a meaningful problem in sequential count analysis, with potential applications in recommendation systems, text analysis, and event prediction. The results on several real-world datasets demonstrate practical utility, though the poor performance on ICEWS prediction is concerning and needs explanation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses an important problem in sequential count data analysis
- Provides a novel combination of deep learning and Poisson factorization
- Demonstrates good performance on multiple real-world datasets
- Offers interpretable latent structures that capture meaningful temporal patterns

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Insufficient ablation studies to validate the deep structure's contribution
- Incomplete analysis of key hyperparameters and model design choices
- Inadequate comparison with recent deep Poisson factorization work
- Technical issues (typos, unclear parameterization) that affect credibility

**Recommendations:**
1. Fix all typos and clarify the Gamma distribution parameterization (shape vs. scale).
2. Conduct comprehensive ablation studies comparing single-layer, two-layer, and multi-layer models.
3. Systematically analyze the impact of key hyperparameters (window size, number of factors).
4. Expand the related work section to include and contrast with recent deep Poisson factorization models.
5. Provide a detailed graphical illustration of the complete model architecture.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to modeling sequential count data with deep latent structures. With the suggested revisions, particularly the additional ablation studies and improved contextualization of related work, this could become a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, the current version lacks sufficient evidence to support the claim that the deep structure provides significant advantages over simpler alternatives.
