This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a novel method for analyzing sequential count data. The model extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating a deep, hierarchical structure to capture complex temporal dependencies. Unlike previous dynamic PFA models, such as the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), which typically use Gamma-based transitions and assume Markovian properties, DDPFA employs recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to model the implicit conditional distributions of the latent temporal factors. This approach is designed to capture more complex, long-range dependencies in the time series. Inference is performed using a hybrid approach that combines coordinate-ascent variational inference for the latent variables with gradient-based optimization for the neural network parameters, structured in a manner similar to a variational autoencoder.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its novel approach to modeling long-range temporal dependencies in count data. By replacing the traditional Markovian assumptions in models like PGDS with a more flexible RNN-based structure, the authors propose a plausible and powerful method for capturing more intricate temporal patterns that exist in many real-world datasets. This addresses a well-known limitation of simpler dynamic models.

2.  **Technical Contribution:** The paper presents a non-trivial integration of Bayesian probabilistic modeling and deep learning techniques. The proposed inference scheme, which combines variational updates for the latent factors with a VAE-like loss function for training the neural network parameters, is technically interesting and appears sound.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct an empirical evaluation on a respectable range of synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the model's applicability to various domains, from international relations event data to text corpora. The results generally show favorable performance against several relevant baselines, including PFA, PGDS, and LSTM.

### Weaknesses (Arguments for Improvement/Rejection)

1.  **Insufficient Ablation and Experimental Justification:** The central claim of the paper is that the *deep* architecture is key to its improved performance. However, the experimental results do not sufficiently isolate and validate this specific contribution. A crucial missing baseline would be a "shallow" version of the proposed model (i.e., with only one hierarchical RNN layer). Comparing DDPFA against such a model would directly demonstrate the incremental benefit of the added depth. The current comparison to PFA shows the benefit of a dynamic model, and the comparison to LSTM is between different model families and inference paradigms, making it difficult to draw firm conclusions about the necessity of the hierarchical structure itself. The authors' own results in Table 3 suggest that adding layers does not always improve performance, which further complicates the justification for the deep architecture.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's clarity is significantly hampered by numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. This makes the manuscript difficult to follow in several sections and detracts from the technical quality of the work. A thorough proofreading by a native English speaker is strongly recommended. Some examples include:
    *   "The model based on the Poisson Factor Analysis method captures dependence among time steps by neural networks, representing the implicit distributions." (Awkward phrasing)
    *   "due to the including of feature-wise parameter"
    *   "excellent results are obtained in prediction and fitting tasks"
    *   "it is rough for these models to catch complicated time dependence"
    *   "recommend systems"

3.  **Choice of Evaluation Metrics:** For the text-based datasets (e.g., NIPS corpus), evaluating held-out perplexity would be a more standard and informative metric than Mean Squared Error (MSE). While MSE is acceptable for general count data, perplexity would provide a stronger and more direct comparison to the vast literature on dynamic topic models.

4.  **Minor Inaccuracies and Omissions:**
    *   The description of modeling non-count real-valued data (end of Section 2) could be more precise. The paper mentions a compound Poisson distribution, which is appropriate, but the language is slightly vague.
    *   For better readability and immediate comprehension of the results, the best-performing figures in the experimental tables should be highlighted (e.g., in bold).

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and promising direction for modeling dynamic count data. The core idea of using RNNs within a PFA framework to capture long-term dependencies is novel and significant. However, the paper is let down by weaknesses in its experimental validation and poor writing quality. The lack of a proper ablation study to justify the deep architecture is a major concern that undermines the central claim of the paper. Furthermore, the pervasive grammatical issues make the paper difficult to read and suggest a lack of care in its preparation.

While the technical ideas are valuable, the paper in its current form does not meet the high standards of NIPS for clarity and rigorous empirical validation. I would be willing to reconsider my score if the authors can address the experimental design issues (particularly by adding a single-layer baseline) and thoroughly revise the manuscript to improve its clarity and correct the grammatical errors.
