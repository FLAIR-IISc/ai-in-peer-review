Summary
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count data that extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with a deep, temporally structured latent hierarchy. Each observation xvt follows a Poisson likelihood with feature loadings Φ, time-varying factor scores Θ, and additional feature- and component-specific scale parameters Ψ and Λ. Temporal dependence is introduced via multiple latent layers H(n), where conditional distributions are parameterized by neural networks intended to capture short- and long-range dependencies. Inference proceeds in two stages: (i) mean-field variational inference (VI) using conjugacy for the Gamma–Poisson structure with auxiliary latent counts, and (ii) optimization of neural-network parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that resembles a VAE-style reconstruction loss. The paper presents experiments on synthetic data and five real-world datasets, comparing against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA using MSE/PMSE metrics.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on PFA and related Gamma–Poisson/NB process models for counts (e.g., Zhou et al.), and on temporal extensions such as PGDS and recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendations. It also connects to deep latent variable models for counts (deep exponential families; deep Poisson factorization with RBMs/SBNs) and variational autoencoder literature (including Ladder VAE). The stated goal is to combine the interpretability of PFA with the temporal modeling power of RNNs.

Strengths
- Tackles an important problem: modeling long-range temporal dependencies in sparse, high-dimensional count data.
- Integrates a deep temporal structure with a Poisson likelihood, aiming to retain interpretability of factors.
- Employs conjugate VI for the count model, which is computationally attractive.
- Empirical results indicate gains over baselines on several datasets; the qualitative analysis of factors is potentially useful.
- Discussion of practical issues (e.g., pruning, utility of deeper layers) is thoughtful.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Model specification is incomplete/ambiguous.
  - The conditional structures in Eqs. 3–4 do not clearly define an RNN. As written, h(n)t depends on h(n+1)t−c:t but not on h(n)t−1, which is atypical for RNNs. Please justify this design or revise the formulation to include same-layer temporal dependencies.
  - The parameterization of Gamma distributions is not fully specified in several places (e.g., shapes/rates for Eqs. 3–4). Explicitly state all conditional distributions with their parameters.
- Misuse of “implicit distributions.”
  - Eqs. 9–10 are explicit Gamma posteriors conditional on their inputs; calling them “implicit” is misleading. If “implicit” is intended to mean “parameterized by neural nets,” please state that clearly.
- Objective and inference are not well justified.
  - The “Probability AutoEncoder” loss (Eq. 15 onward) is under-specified. What is the precise objective being optimized? Is there an ELBO that the two-stage procedure maximizes or lower-bounds? Please derive a coherent variational objective and show how both stages jointly optimize it (or explain the approximation).
  - If the objective departs from a valid ELBO, discuss potential bias and provide empirical checks (held-out likelihood) to support the approach.
- Evaluation and metrics.
  - MSE/PMSE are not standard for Poisson models and can be misleading. Please report predictive log-likelihood (or held-out perplexity for text) and, where relevant, count-based scoring rules (e.g., negative binomial log-likelihood if overdispersion is modeled).
  - The choice of K appears small for some datasets. Please include results with larger K (e.g., 50–100) and sensitivity analyses.
  - The connection between the narrative (lines 224–232) and Fig. 3 is unclear; add clear captions and refer explicitly to the plotted factors and events.
  - Some visual elements (e.g., Fig. 4 caption) are unreadable; improve figure quality and labeling.
- Comparisons and scope.
  - Consider comparing against additional relevant baselines, such as hierarchical Poisson factorization (HPF), dynamic topic models, and recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation.
  - Discuss computational complexity and runtime vs. baselines, given the additional neural-network parameters.
- Writing and presentation.
  - The manuscript has numerous grammatical errors, typos, and missing articles, and overuses passive voice (especially in the abstract). Examples include “high-dimensional,” “factorize,” “Dirichlet,” “nested,” and several incomplete sentences. Please carefully proofread and revise for clarity.
  - Many variational-update details (Eqs. 11–14) can be moved to the supplement to improve flow.
  - Clarify ambiguous statements (e.g., “fanatical data and disaster data”; possibly “financial”?).

Quality
- Technical soundness is difficult to assess due to incomplete definitions of the conditional distributions and the lack of a clear ELBO. The Gamma–Poisson part is standard and sound; the neural network coupling needs a precise probabilistic interpretation and objective.

Clarity
- The paper is currently hard to follow. A thorough rewrite is needed: clearly define all distributions, unify notation for shape/rate, explicitly describe the temporal dependencies, and provide a principled inference objective.

Originality
- The idea of combining PFA with deep temporal parameterizations is interesting and timely. However, several related works already explore deep/temporal count models. The contribution would be stronger with a clear, principled objective and more thorough comparisons.

Significance
- Potentially significant if the model reliably improves predictive performance and interpretability at scale. As presented, the empirical gains are promising but not yet conclusive due to metric choice, small K, and unclear objective.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a formal ELBO and show how both stages of your algorithm optimize or bound it?
- Why does h(n)t not depend on h(n)t−1? Would adding same-layer temporal recurrence improve performance?
- How sensitive are results to K, window size c, and number of layers?
- Do Ψ and Λ materially improve performance/interpretability over absorbing them into Θ?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant problem and proposes a potentially useful hybrid of deep nets and Poisson factorization.
- Promising empirical performance on several datasets and interesting qualitative insights.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete model specification and unclear inference objective.
- Evaluation metrics are not well aligned with count modeling; limited baselines and small K.
- Writing quality and figure legibility issues hinder comprehension.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a complete probabilistic model with explicit parameterizations for all conditionals; clarify the temporal structure to align with RNN intuition or justify the chosen design.
- Derive and present a coherent ELBO (or alternative principled objective) and show how your algorithm optimizes it; justify the “Probability AutoEncoder” loss.
- Report predictive log-likelihood on held-out data; expand baselines and perform sensitivity analyses over K, layers, and c.
- Move routine VI updates to the appendix; improve figures and captions; thoroughly edit for grammar and clarity.
- Release code and scripts to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall, I see potential in the approach, but substantial clarification and strengthening of the modeling and evaluation are needed before it is ready for NIPS.
