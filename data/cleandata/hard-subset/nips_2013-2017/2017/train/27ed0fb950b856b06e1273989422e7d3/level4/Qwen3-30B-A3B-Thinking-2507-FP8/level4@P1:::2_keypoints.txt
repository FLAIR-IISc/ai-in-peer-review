- Proposal: DDPFA combines RNNs with Poisson Factor Analysis to model sequential count data, capturing short-term and long-term temporal dependencies.  
- Inference: Uses variational inference with two-stage optimization—mean-field updates for latent variables and neural-network parameter learning via a VAE-like loss.  
- Empirical evaluation: Tested on synthetic and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) and shows competitive performance versus PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.  
- Technical novelty: RNNs model implicit distributions over latent factors and RNN-approximated Gamma shape parameters provide a principled fit for count data.  
- Empirical rigor strength: Diverse datasets, clear improvement on complex-dependency cases (e.g., Ebola/ASP), and interpretable learned factors.  
- Clarity strength: Well-structured paper with clear math (Equations 1–18) and sufficiently detailed experimental setup for reproducibility.  
- Weakness — fixed scale (β) assumption: Scale parameters are fixed while only shape parameters are RNN-approximated; no justification or ablation on this choice.  
- Weakness — limited ablation studies: Ablation only tests layer counts and bottom-layer dimensionality; missing comparisons to single-layer DDPFA vs. PFA and varying window sizes (c).  
- Weakness — presentation issues: Critical typo in Equation 10 and insufficient architectural description/diagram make parts hard to follow.  
- Weakness — missing related work: Fails to adequately compare or discuss relevant recent deep Poisson models (e.g., Zhou 2016, Cong 2017).  
- Significance limitation: Pruning of latent dimensions and reliance on ladder-like architecture suggest mean-field VI limitations; alternatives like MCMC are not explored.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — promising and novel but needs more ablation, justification of design choices, and presentation/related-work fixes.  
- Pro/Con summary: Pro — novel deep structure, strong empirical results, interpretable factors; Con — typo, insufficient ablations, fixed-scale assumption, omitted key prior work, rushed presentation.
