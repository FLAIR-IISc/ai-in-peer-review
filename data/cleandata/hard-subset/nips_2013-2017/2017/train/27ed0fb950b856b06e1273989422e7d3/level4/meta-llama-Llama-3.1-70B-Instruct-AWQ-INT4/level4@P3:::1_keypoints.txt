- Introduces DDPFA, extending Poisson Factor Analysis to model temporal dependencies in sequential count vectors using an RNN-like structure and variational inference.  
- Uses a recurrent neural network to represent (claimed) implicit distributions and learn complex inter-factor temporal relationships.  
- Learns latent variables via variational inference and trains neural network parameters with a variational-loss-derived objective.  
- Evaluated on 4 synthetic and 5 real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) and reported better fitting and prediction than PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.  

Strengths:
- Novel approach for capturing temporal dependencies in sequential count data.  
- RNN-based representation of distributions is a key contribution for modeling complex temporal factor interactions.  
- Thorough empirical evaluation across multiple synthetic and real datasets demonstrating improved performance.  

Weaknesses:
- Eq. 4 does not follow a traditional RNN structure (depends only on top-layer memory vectors, not prior same-layer time steps); requires clarification/justification.  
- Several model equations are missing or unclear (Gamma parameterization in Eq. 4; generative distribution for theta_t in Eq. 3).  
- Claim of “implicit distributions” is misleading because Eqs. 9 and 10 specify explicit Gamma distributions.  
- Poor writing quality: many typos, incomplete sentences, and missing articles reduce clarity.  

Suggestions:
- Clarify and justify the Eq. 4 formulation and the simplified RNN design.  
- Provide full parameterizations for the Gamma distributions and explicitly state the distribution generating theta_t.  
- Revise the discussion on “implicit distributions” to accurately reflect the explicit distributions used.  
- Improve overall writing and consider adding evaluation metrics (e.g., predictive log-likelihood) for a more comprehensive assessment.  

Arguments for acceptance:
- Presents a novel, potentially impactful model for temporal count data with promising empirical results.  
- Demonstrated effectiveness across diverse datasets suggests real-world applicability.  

Arguments against acceptance:
- Significant issues in clarity, technical detail, and writing need resolution before acceptance.  
- Core methodological concerns (RNN formulation and the implicit vs explicit distribution claims) must be addressed to validate the approach.
