

The paper introduces Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for analyzing sequential count data by extending Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with deep recurrent structures to capture long-range temporal dependencies. Building on prior work like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) [2] and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9,10], DDPFA employs neural networks to model implicit distributions across layers, enabling deeper temporal modeling than shallow approaches. The authors propose variational inference for latent variables and neural network parameter optimization, demonstrating effectiveness on synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, etc.). The core contribution—using deep architecture to model long-term dependencies in count data—addresses a recognized gap in existing dynamic Poisson models.

**Strengths:**  
The experimental design is robust, covering diverse datasets (including high-dimensional real-world applications like ICEWS and NIPS corpus) and comparing against relevant baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA). The model achieves strong predictive performance on most datasets (Table 2), particularly where long-term patterns matter (e.g., EBOLA, ASP). The interpretability of latent factors (Fig. 2–3) is a notable strength, showing how DDPFA captures multi-factor temporal dynamics that PGDS misses (e.g., sustained Israel-Palestinian conflict patterns). The technical approach—leveraging Gamma-Poisson conjugacy within a deep variational framework—is well-motivated and aligns with recent advances in deep Bayesian models [8,23].

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Technical justification for depth:** The claim that "deep structure captures long-term dependence" lacks rigorous validation. The paper compares DDPFA to shallow PFA and PGDS but omits a critical comparison to a *single-layer deep* architecture (e.g., replacing the two-layer structure with one layer). This weakens the argument for *multiple* hierarchical layers. The LSTM comparison is valid but not directly comparable due to different learning paradigms (variational vs. gradient-based).  
2. **Evaluation metrics:** Relying solely on MSE/PMSE (Section 4.2) is insufficient for count data; held-out perplexity would better assess generative quality [12,13]. The ICEWS prediction failure (Table 2) is unexplained, though PGDS performs better here—suggesting potential limitations in the model’s adaptability.  
3. **Presentation issues:** The paper contains grammatical errors that impair clarity (e.g., "PFA factorized a count matrix" → "PFA factorizes"; "V-dimesnion sequentially" → "V-dimensional sequentially"; "due to the including of" → "due to the inclusion of"). These distract from the technical contribution. Section 4.1’s description of "SDS4" has inconsistent formatting.  
4. **Analysis of limitations:** The "pruning" issue (Table 3) is noted but not resolved; the authors acknowledge it stems from mean-field VI but offer no concrete mitigation. This undermines the quality assessment of the method.  

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**  
- Bold the best results in Table 2 for immediate readability.  
- Add a comparison against a single-layer deep PF variant to validate the *necessity* of depth (e.g., DDPFA with one layer vs. two layers).  
- Supplement MSE with perplexity on held-out data (e.g., last 20% of time steps) to better evaluate generative performance.  
- Correct grammatical errors (e.g., "Noticing the lack of capturing" → "Noting the lack of capture"; "other data with different pattern long-time dependence" → "data with diverse long-term dependence patterns").  
- Clarify the ICEWS prediction discrepancy: Is it due to noise, model capacity, or dataset-specific structure?  

**Recommendation:**  
This work makes a meaningful contribution to dynamic Poisson modeling by introducing a deep, interpretable framework for count data. While the technical approach is sound and experiments are thorough, the paper would benefit significantly from addressing the depth justification gap, improving evaluation metrics, and polishing language. The weaknesses are resolvable with minor revisions. **Weak Accept**—the paper advances the state of the art in modeling temporal count data but requires clarifications to fully justify its methodological claims and strengthen the evaluation.
