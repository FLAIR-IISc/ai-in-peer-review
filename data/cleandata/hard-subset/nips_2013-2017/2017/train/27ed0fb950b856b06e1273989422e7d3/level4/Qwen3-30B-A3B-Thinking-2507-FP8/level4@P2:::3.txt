

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model to analyze sequential count data by incorporating deep neural network structures to capture long-range temporal dependencies. The authors build upon Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and extend it with recurrent neural networks to model complex temporal relationships, addressing limitations in existing dynamic Poisson models like PGDS that struggle with long-term dependencies due to their shallow structure. The model uses variational inference with neural network parameter optimization for efficient training, and the authors evaluate it on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating improved performance in prediction and fitting tasks compared to baselines including PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The proposed DDPFA model effectively extends previous work by introducing a deep hierarchical structure that captures both short-term local relationships through lower layers and long-term temporal dependencies through higher layers. The variational inference approach is well-motivated and properly implemented, with appropriate use of Gamma-Poisson conjugacy to derive closed-form updates. The experimental results on multiple datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population) provide solid evidence of the model's effectiveness, particularly in capturing meaningful long-term patterns that simpler models like PGDS fail to detect. The model also shows good interpretability, as evidenced by the factor activations in Figure 2.

The paper's clarity is somewhat compromised by numerous grammatical errors and inconsistent phrasing (e.g., "V-dimesnion sequentially," "K-dimension latent variable," "due to the including of feature-wise"). While the technical content is sound, these writing issues hinder readability and reduce the paper's accessibility. The authors should carefully proofread the manuscript to address these issues.

The originality of the work is good. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous dynamic Poisson models by explicitly addressing the limitations of shallow temporal structures in capturing long-range dependencies. The incorporation of deep neural networks to model the temporal evolution of latent factors represents a meaningful extension of existing approaches. The authors have adequately referenced relevant prior work, including recent deep Poisson models and dynamic Bayesian factor analysis methods.

The significance of the contribution is substantial. Sequential count data analysis is important across multiple domains (recommendation systems, text analysis, network analysis, time series), and the ability to model complex temporal dependencies in such data has clear practical value. The model's demonstrated improvements over existing methods, particularly in capturing long-term patterns, suggest it could be valuable for practitioners. The paper's application to real-world datasets like ICEWS and EBOLA further underscores its relevance.

However, the paper could strengthen its contribution by addressing two key limitations. First, the comparison with a one-layer deep architecture would better demonstrate the necessity of multiple hierarchical layers, as the current comparison with LSTM (which uses a different learning algorithm) doesn't fully justify the need for depth. Second, while the paper mentions "pruning" as a limitation of their approach, it would be more convincing to provide more detailed analysis of this phenomenon and how it affects performance across different datasets.

The paper also misses an opportunity to use held-out perplexity as a stronger evaluation metric in Section 4.2, which would better reflect the model's ability to generalize to unseen data. While MSE/PMSE are acceptable, perplexity is a more standard metric for count data models and would strengthen the experimental evaluation.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of dynamic factor analysis for count data. The model effectively addresses a gap in existing approaches by incorporating deep structures to capture long-range temporal dependencies, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over baselines. The paper's main weaknesses are primarily in writing quality and some experimental design choices, but these are relatively minor compared to the technical contribution.

Strengths:
- Effective extension of PFA with deep structure for long-range temporal dependencies
- Solid experimental results on multiple datasets demonstrating improved performance
- Good interpretability of learned factors
- Appropriate use of variational inference for scalable training
- Clear distinction from previous work in the literature review

Weaknesses:
- Numerous grammatical errors and inconsistent phrasing that reduce readability
- Limited comparison with a one-layer deep architecture to justify the need for depth
- Incomplete evaluation with missing held-out perplexity metric
- Some claims about advantages could be better supported with additional analysis
- The "pruning" limitation is noted but not thoroughly analyzed

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of dynamic factor analysis for count data, with clear potential for application in multiple domains. The technical approach is sound and the results are compelling, though the writing quality needs improvement before publication. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the grammatical issues and strengthen the experimental evaluation.
