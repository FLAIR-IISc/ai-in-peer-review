This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a novel method for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model builds upon the Poisson Factorization Analysis (PFA) framework by introducing a deep structure composed of recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions over the latent temporal factors. The goal is to capture complex, long-range dependencies across time, an aspect where prior dynamic PFA models with Markovian assumptions (e.g., [1, 2]) may fall short. The authors develop a hybrid inference scheme combining coordinate-ascent variational inference for the latent variables with gradient-based optimization of the neural network parameters. The model's performance is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-known limitation in dynamic topic models and Poisson factorization—the difficulty of capturing long-range temporal dependencies. The proposed solution, which uses a deep, recurrent architecture to model the evolution of latent factors, is a novel and timely contribution to this area. It moves beyond the typical first-order Markov assumptions found in related models.
*   **Technical Approach:** The core idea of using neural networks to model the implicit conditional distributions within a hierarchical Bayesian model is technically sound. The paper presents a complete contribution, detailing the generative model, the inference procedure, and experimental validation.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's readability is significantly hindered by numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing. This makes it difficult for the reader to fully grasp the technical details. Examples include: "other data with different pattern long-time dependence", "Examples for this including", "V-dimesnion sequentially", "due to the including of feature-wise", and "recommend systems". A thorough round of proofreading is necessary.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments show promising results, the evaluation could be made more rigorous and convincing.
    *   The central claim is that a *deep* structure is beneficial for capturing long-range dependencies. However, the experiments do not sufficiently justify the need for multiple hierarchical layers. A critical baseline is missing: a comparison against a shallow, single-layer version of the proposed DDPFA. The current comparison with LSTM is less compelling because the learning algorithms and model families are fundamentally different, making it difficult to isolate the contribution of the deep architecture itself.
    *   For the text and event data experiments in Section 4.2 (e.g., NIPS, ICEWS), held-out perplexity is a more standard and informative metric for evaluating predictive performance than Mean Squared Error (MSE). While MSE is acceptable, perplexity would provide a stronger measure of the model's ability to generalize.
*   **Technical Correctness:** There may be an imprecise statement on lines 86-87 regarding the modeling of "non-count real-valued matrix". The text suggests linking to a latent count matrix via a compound Poisson distribution, but for modeling non-negative real values in this context, the Poisson-Randomized Gamma distribution is often used. This point may require clarification or correction.

**Minor Suggestions:**
*   To improve readability in Table 2, please consider making the best-performing result in each column bold.

In summary, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach for an important problem. However, its impact is currently limited by significant clarity issues and an experimental design that does not fully support the central claims about the utility of its deep architecture. Addressing these weaknesses would substantially strengthen the paper.
