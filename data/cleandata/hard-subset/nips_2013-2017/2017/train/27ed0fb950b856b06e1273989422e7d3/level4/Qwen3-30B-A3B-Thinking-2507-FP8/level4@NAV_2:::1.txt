

The paper introduces Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a novel model extending Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) to capture complex temporal dependencies in sequential count data via deep latent structures. Building on prior work like PGDS [2] and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9,10], DDPFA employs recurrent neural networks to model implicit distributions over latent factors, enabling both short-term complex relationships and long-term temporal dependencies. The approach uses variational inference with neural network parameter optimization, evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, etc.). 

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with well-justified use of Gamma-Poisson conjugacy and variational inference. However, the model formulation contains critical gaps: Eq. (4) does not represent a standard RNN (lacking intra-layer temporal dependencies), and key parameterizations (e.g., Gamma distributions in Eq. 4) are unspecified. The inference procedure (Eqs. 9–14) relies on explicit Gamma distributions, contradicting the "implicit distribution" claims in Lines 110–118. Additionally, MSE/PMSE is inadequate for count models; predictive log-likelihood should be reported. The small K (3–10) in experiments (Line 207) limits interpretability and may underrepresent model capacity, warranting larger values (e.g., K=100).

**Clarity**: The paper suffers from poor writing and numerous technical omissions. Missing articles ("Although Dirichlet..."), typos ("factorize," "high-dimensional"), and incomplete sentences (Line 49) undermine readability. Figure 1 caption is illegible, and the discussion of Fig. 3 (Lines 224–232) lacks connection to the visualization. The inference algorithm description (Lines 133–155) is unclear, particularly the loss function in Eq. (15), which is undefined. While Eq. (11)–(14) could be moved to supplement, their current presentation is ambiguous.

**Originality**: The core idea—integrating deep structures into dynamic Poisson factorization—is novel compared to shallow models like PGDS [2] and LSTM-based approaches. The ladder-like architecture (Table 3) addresses "pruning" in hierarchical models [25], offering a meaningful contribution. However, the neural network implementation is overly simplified (Eq. 4), and the connection to related work (e.g., Deep Exponential Families [8]) is superficial. The claim about "fanatical data" (Lines 30–31) lacks elaboration.

**Significance**: The model demonstrates strong empirical performance on synthetic data (Table 1) and interpretable factors on real-world datasets (Figs. 2–3), particularly for long-term patterns (e.g., Israel-Palestine conflict in ICEWS). It advances the state of the art in sequential count analysis by combining deep learning with probabilistic modeling. However, PGDS outperforms DDPFA on ICEWS prediction (Table 2), and LSTM fails on high-dimensional data (EBOLA, ASP), limiting generalizability. The "uselessness of adding layers" (Table 3) undermines the proposed depth advantage.

**Pro/Con Summary**:  
*Pros*: Conceptually novel integration of deep structures with Poisson factorization; strong synthetic data results; interpretable latent factors on real-world datasets.  
*Cons*: Incomplete model formulation (Eq. 4, Gamma parameterization); inadequate evaluation metrics (missing predictive log-likelihood); poor writing and technical omissions; small K values limit significance.  

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in sequential count analysis but requires substantial revision to meet NIPS standards. The core idea has merit but is undermined by technical shortcomings and presentation issues. I recommend rejection pending major revisions addressing the above points.
