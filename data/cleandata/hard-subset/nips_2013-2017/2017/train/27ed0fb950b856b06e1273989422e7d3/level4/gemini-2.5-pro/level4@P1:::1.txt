This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count data. The model extends Poisson Factorization (PF) to capture temporal dependencies by employing a deep, hierarchical structure where latent variables at each time step are conditioned on previous time steps. This temporal conditioning is modeled using recurrent neural networks that parameterize the prior distributions in a Gamma-Poisson hierarchy. Inference is performed using a hybrid approach that combines coordinate-ascent variational inference for the latent variables with gradient-based optimization for the neural network parameters. The authors evaluate the model on several synthetic and real-world datasets, reporting improvements in fitting and prediction error over baseline models.

Overall, I find the core idea of integrating deep neural networks into the generative process of a dynamic topic model to be potentially interesting. However, the paper requires significant improvements in its technical clarity, experimental validation, and overall execution before it can be considered for acceptance at NIPS. My main concerns are detailed below.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1.  **Model Formulation and Clarity:** The description of the model is ambiguous and at times incomplete.
    *   The structure described in Equation 4 does not seem to correspond to a standard Recurrent Neural Network (RNN). Specifically, the latent state `h_t^{(n)}` appears to depend only on the hidden states of the layer above it, `h^{(n+1)}`, but not on its own previous state, `h_{t-1}^{(n)}`. The authors should clarify this modeling choice and justify why this simplified structure is used instead of a conventional RNN.
    *   The model specification is missing key details. For instance, Equation 3 introduces `p(θ_t|...)` and Equation 4 introduces `p(h_t^{(n)}|...)`, but the functional forms of these distributions (e.g., how the Gamma distributions are parameterized by the neural network outputs) are not explicitly defined.

2.  **Inaccurate Terminology:** The characterization of the distributions in Equations 9 and 10 as "implicit" seems incorrect. These are explicit Gamma distributions with well-defined shape and rate parameters that are functions of other variables. A distribution is typically considered implicit only when its density function is intractable. Since the conditional `p(θ_tk | h^{(0)})` is a closed-form Gamma distribution, it is not implicit. This misuse of terminology is confusing and should be corrected.

3.  **Unclear Inference Procedure:** The description of the "Probability AutoEncoder" for learning the network parameters (lines 133-155) is difficult to follow.
    *   Equation 15 presents an objective to maximize an integral, which is not a standard loss function. It is unclear what this expression represents and how it connects to the subsequent L2-norm-based loss functions in Equations 16-18. A more rigorous derivation and justification for this objective are necessary.
    *   The claim in lines 30-31 that a prior method is weak for "fanatical data and disaster data" is vague. The authors should elaborate on the specific temporal patterns in these datasets that motivate their new model.

4.  **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup and evaluation are not fully convincing.
    *   Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Predicted MSE (PMSE) are not appropriate metrics for evaluating models of count data. A more standard and informative metric is the predictive log-likelihood on a held-out test set, which should be included for a meaningful comparison with other probabilistic models.
    *   The number of factors (K=3 or K=10) used in the experiments seems far too small, particularly for high-dimensional datasets like the NIPS corpus (V > 14,000). The model's performance and scalability should be tested with a more realistic number of factors (e.g., K=100).
    *   The connection between the qualitative discussion of the learned factors (lines 224-232) and the visualization in Figure 3 is unclear. The figure lacks the necessary labels and context to support the claims about capturing specific real-world events.

5.  **Writing and Presentation:** The paper's quality is significantly hindered by poor writing and organization.
    *   To improve readability, the standard variational update equations (Equations 11-14) could be moved to an appendix, allowing the main text to focus on the novel aspects of the model.
    *   The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing that impede readability. A thorough proofreading is essential. Examples include: "high-dimensional" (line 15), "factorize" (line 22), "nested" (line 34), "visual representation" (Fig. 1), incomplete sentences (e.g., line 49), and many missing articles ("Although a Dirichlet distribution is often used as a prior..."). The overuse of the passive voice, especially in the abstract, also makes the writing less direct.

In summary, while the paper presents a promising idea, its contributions are currently undermined by a lack of clarity in the model description and inference, weaknesses in the experimental evaluation, and poor overall writing quality. The paper is not ready for publication in its current form.
