

This paper proposes a novel approach to modeling long-term temporal dependencies in count data sequences by leveraging recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to parameterize the shape of gamma-distributed implicit posteriors. The key innovation lies in using RNNs to approximate the shape parameters of gamma distributions, which encode time dependencies, while incorporating information from both higher-layer priors and lower-layer likelihoods. The inference mechanism employs the expectation of latent variables to reduce variance from stochastic sampling, enhancing computational efficiency. The method presents a promising solution to the long-dependency challenge in dynamic count models, and the core idea is well-motivated. However, the manuscript requires significant refinement in presentation, technical justification, and experimental validation to meet NIPS standards. Below are specific concerns:

1.  **Critical Typos and Presentation Issues:** A notable typo appears in Equation (10), which disrupts readability. More fundamentally, the writing is rushed in several sections, leading to ambiguous phrasing (e.g., "implicit distributions" lacks precision). The authors should meticulously proofread the manuscript and clarify technical terms (e.g., explicitly state that the gamma distribution is used within a hierarchical Poisson-gamma model for count data).

2.  **Justification for Fixed Scale Parameter:** The manuscript fixes the scale parameter of the gamma distribution while only optimizing the shape parameter via the RNN. While this simplification is common (e.g., setting scale=1 for conjugacy), the authors provide no discussion of its implications. How does this choice impact the model's flexibility or expressiveness? Why is it preferable over learning the scale parameter? A brief theoretical or empirical justification is essential for methodological soundness.

3.  **Inadequate Ablation Studies:** The experiments only evaluate the model with a two-layer architecture. Crucially, the authors omit comparisons against single-layer and multi-layer variants (e.g., three or more layers). This omission prevents assessing the impact of architectural depth on performance, which is central to the proposed RNN-based approach. Similarly, the influence of hyperparameters—such as the temporal window size and the number of latent factors—remains unexplored. Ablation studies on these parameters are necessary to validate the model's design choices and demonstrate robustness.

4.  **Lack of Model Visualization:** The manuscript lacks a clear graphical illustration of the proposed model's structure, making it difficult for readers to grasp the flow of information between layers and the integration of RNNs with the gamma-Poisson hierarchy. A schematic figure, similar to Figure 1 in Chung et al. (NIPS 2015), would significantly enhance clarity. Such a figure should explicitly depict the RNN’s role in updating shape parameters, the hierarchical prior-likelihood structure, and the inference process.

5.  **Outdated Related Work Discussion:** The introduction fails to engage with recent advances in deep Poisson factor analysis. Specifically, the 2016 work by Zhou et al. (Augmentable Gamma Belief Networks) and the 2017 ICML paper by Cong et al. (Deep Latent Dirichlet Allocation with Topic-Layer-Adaptive SGD) are directly relevant. The authors should explicitly contrast their method with these approaches, highlighting key differences in modeling assumptions (e.g., gamma vs. Dirichlet priors), architectural choices (RNN vs. other deep structures), and performance. Omitting these works weakens the paper’s positioning within the current literature.

**Overall Assessment:** The core idea—using RNNs to model time-dependent shape parameters of gamma distributions for count data—is conceptually interesting and addresses a valid challenge. The computational efficiency gains from using latent variable expectations are a notable strength. However, the current manuscript’s technical gaps, insufficient experimental validation, and lack of contextualization within recent literature prevent it from being publication-ready. Addressing the points above—particularly the ablation studies, model visualization, and related work—would substantially strengthen the contribution.

**Recommendation:** Major Revision. The authors must provide rigorous justification for the fixed scale parameter, conduct comprehensive ablation studies on model architecture and hyperparameters, include a clear model diagram, and thoroughly discuss recent relevant work. With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field of dynamic count models.
