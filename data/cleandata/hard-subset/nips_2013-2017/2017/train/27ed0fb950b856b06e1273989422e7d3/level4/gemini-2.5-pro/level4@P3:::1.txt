This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count data. The proposed model extends the well-known Poisson Factorization Analysis (PFA) by incorporating a deep, hierarchical structure to capture complex temporal dependencies. Unlike prior work such as the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS) which assumes a first-order Markov structure, DDPFA uses recurrent neural networks to model the transition distributions between latent factors over time, aiming to capture longer-range dependencies. Inference is performed using a mean-field variational approach for the latent variables, combined with gradient-based optimization of the neural network parameters using a loss function inspired by variational autoencoders. The model's performance is evaluated on several synthetic and real-world datasets, where it is compared against PFA, PGDS, and LSTM baselines.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of modeling complex, long-term temporal dependencies in sequential count data, which is relevant to many applications discussed in the NIPS community.
*   The core idea of integrating deep, recurrent-like neural network structures into the PFA framework to learn flexible temporal dynamics is novel and conceptually interesting. It offers a potential path to move beyond the limitations of simpler Markovian assumptions present in models like PGDS.
*   The authors demonstrate the model's potential on a diverse set of five real-world datasets, showing its applicability to different domains.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while based on a promising idea, suffers from significant issues in its current form, primarily concerning clarity, technical soundness of the model and inference, and the experimental evaluation.

1.  **Clarity and Technical Precision:** The model and its inference procedure are not described with sufficient clarity for the work to be understood or reproduced.
    *   **Incomplete Model Specification:** The generative process is not fully specified. For example, the distribution `p(θ_t|...)` in Eq. 3 is not defined. In Eq. 4, it is stated that `h_t^(n)` is drawn from a distribution `p(·|h^(n+1) ...)` modeled as an RNN, but the exact form of this distribution (e.g., how the parameters of a Gamma distribution are derived from the RNN's output) is not provided.
    *   **Inconsistent Model Description:** The structure described does not appear to be a standard RNN. In Eq. 4, the latent state `h_t^(n)` at layer `n` depends on the states of the layer above it (`h^(n+1)`), but not on its own previous state `h_{t-1}^(n)`. This seems to be a hierarchical model rather than a recurrent one. The authors should clarify this design choice and justify why it is suitable for capturing temporal dependencies.
    *   **Misuse of Terminology:** The term "implicit distribution" (lines 110-118) appears to be used incorrectly. The conditional distributions in Eqs. 9 and 10 are specified as Gamma distributions whose parameters are computed by a neural network. These are explicit, parameterized distributions, not implicit ones where one can only sample but not evaluate the density.
    *   **Unclear Inference Objective:** The "Probability AutoEncoder" section and the loss function in Eq. 15 are confusing. The objective `∫ p(Θ|H(0))p(H(0)|Θ)dH(0)` is not standard. It is unclear how this relates to maximizing the evidence lower bound (ELBO), which is the standard objective in variational inference. The subsequent jump to an L2-norm-based loss function (Eq. 16) is not well-motivated and its connection to the probabilistic model is lost.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental methodology has several weaknesses that undermine the reported results.
    *   **Inappropriate Evaluation Metrics:** The paper relies on Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Predicted MSE (PMSE). These are not suitable metrics for evaluating probabilistic models on count data, as they do not measure how well the model captures the uncertainty in the data. The standard metric for such models is the predictive log-likelihood on a held-out test set, which should be included for a meaningful comparison.
    *   **Insufficient Model Complexity:** The number of latent factors (K) used in the experiments (K=3 or K=10) seems very small for high-dimensional datasets like NIPS (V=14036) and ICEWS (V=6197). This choice may artificially limit the performance of all factorization-based models and may not be representative of a realistic use case. It would be more convincing to show results for larger values of K (e.g., K=50 or 100).

3.  **Writing and Presentation:** The paper's quality of writing is below the standard expected for a top-tier conference, which hinders readability and comprehension. There are numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasings.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**
*   (Lines 30-31) The sentence "This method may have weak points in analyzing other data with different pattern long-time dependence, such as fanatical data and disaster data" is vague. Could you please elaborate on what specific patterns in this data are difficult for prior models to capture?
*   Could you please provide the full, explicit generative process for `θ_t` and `h_t^(n)`? For instance, if `θ_tk` follows a Gamma distribution, how are its shape and rate parameters computed from the outputs of the neural network?
*   Please clarify the objective function for training the neural network parameters. How is the loss function in Eq. 16 derived from the probabilistic model? Why was this chosen over directly optimizing the ELBO with respect to all variational and model parameters (e.g., using reparameterization tricks or score function estimators)?
*   The update equations for the variational inference (Eqs. 11-14) are standard and could be moved to a supplementary appendix to improve the flow and focus of the main paper.
*   (Lines 224-232 and Fig. 3) The connection between the text's discussion of "long-time activation of factors" and what is shown in Figure 3 is not clear. The figure should be better explained to support the claims made in the text.
*   The caption for Figure 1 uses the word "transmission", which seems inappropriate. Perhaps "dependency" or "generation" would be more suitable.
*   The writing requires substantial improvement. For example, the abstract uses passive voice extensively, and there are many missing articles throughout the paper (e.g., "a Gamma distribution is exploited in our model due to the including of..."). I strongly recommend a thorough proofreading by a native English speaker.

**Conclusion:**
The paper presents an interesting idea for modeling dynamic count data. However, the current manuscript has major shortcomings in terms of the clarity of the model and inference procedure, the appropriateness of the experimental evaluation, and the overall quality of writing. Due to these significant issues, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. A major revision addressing the points above would be necessary to make this a strong contribution.
