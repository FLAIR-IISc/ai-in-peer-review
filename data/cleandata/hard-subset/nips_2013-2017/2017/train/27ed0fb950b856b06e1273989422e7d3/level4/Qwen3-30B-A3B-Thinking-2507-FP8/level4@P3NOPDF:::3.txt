

This paper presents an interesting extension to dynamic Poisson factorization by incorporating long-range temporal dependencies through a deep recurrent-like architecture. The core contribution—using a neural network structure to model temporal dynamics beyond prior work—is promising and addresses a clear gap in the literature. The technical approach, combining Poisson factorization with variational inference for parameter optimization, demonstrates solid methodological grounding. However, the paper requires significant revisions to address grammatical errors, factual inaccuracies, and experimental clarity before it can be considered for publication.

**Major Technical Concerns:**  
The paper contains a critical factual error in its description of the modeling distribution. On lines 86–87, it incorrectly states that "nonnegative numbers are modeled using a Poisson distribution." This is inaccurate; the model employs the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution (a conjugate prior for Poisson likelihoods), not the standard Poisson distribution. This misstatement undermines the technical precision of the work and must be corrected immediately.  

Regarding experimental evaluation, the choice of metrics in Section 4.2 is suboptimal. While MSE/PMSE are acceptable for count data, held-out perplexity would provide a stronger, more interpretable measure of predictive performance for a Poisson-based model. The authors should replace or supplement these metrics with perplexity to better assess the model’s ability to generalize to unseen data.  

The comparison with LSTM (Section 4.3) is misleading. The authors claim their multi-level hierarchical structure outperforms LSTM, but the comparison is invalid because the learning algorithms differ fundamentally: LSTM uses backpropagation through time, while the proposed method uses variational inference. This discrepancy obscures whether the performance gain stems from the architecture or the optimization strategy. To credibly justify the need for multiple hierarchical levels, the authors should include a comparison against a single-level dynamic PF variant (e.g., a shallow architecture with identical inference procedure). The current comparison with LSTM fails to address this key question.

**Presentation and Clarity Issues:**  
The paper is marred by numerous grammatical errors and typos that impede readability and professionalism. Examples include:  
- "unit the data" → "unite the data"  
- "Noticing the lack of capturing" → "Noting the lack of capture"  
- "PFA factorized a count matrix" (tense inconsistency; should be "factorizes")  
- "other data with different pattern long-time dependence" → "data exhibiting different long-term dependence patterns"  
- "Examples for this including" → "Examples include"  
- "shows excellent results on predict and" → "shows excellent predictive results"  
- "However, it is rough for them" → "However, this is challenging for them"  
- "V-dimesnion sequentially" → "V-dimensional sequentially"  
- "K-dimension latent variable" → "K-dimensional latent variable"  
- "due to the including of feature-wise" → "due to the inclusion of feature-wise"  
- "recommend systems" → "recommendation systems"  

These errors are pervasive and distract from the technical contribution. The authors must conduct a thorough proofreading pass.  

**Additional Recommendations:**  
- **Table 2:** Bold the best-performing results in all columns to improve visual clarity and highlight key findings.  
- **Section 4.1:** Clarify how the "deep structure" (e.g., number of layers) is selected and its impact on performance. Without this, the benefit of depth remains unquantified.  
- **Limitations:** The paper acknowledges limitations of prior work (refs. 1, 2) but does not explicitly state how its approach overcomes them. A brief, focused discussion here would strengthen the motivation.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The core idea—leveraging deep recurrent structures for temporal dependencies in Poisson factorization—is novel and well-motivated. The technical execution is largely sound, but the paper’s current form is weakened by factual inaccuracies, inadequate metric choices, and poor editing. Addressing these issues would significantly elevate the work. The proposed method has clear potential for applications in time-series count data (e.g., user activity prediction), but the current presentation does not fully convey this value.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Reject** with a *strong recommendation for resubmission after major revisions*. The paper’s technical merits are promising, but the current version contains too many fundamental flaws to be accepted as-is. The authors must: (1) correct the distribution misstatement, (2) replace MSE/PMSE with held-out perplexity, (3) add a single-level dynamic PF comparison, (4) fix all grammatical errors, and (5) bold best results in Table 2. Without these changes, the paper risks being misunderstood or dismissed due to avoidable errors.  

**Strengths:**  
- Novel integration of deep learning with Poisson factorization for temporal modeling.  
- Clear problem statement addressing limitations of prior dynamic PF work.  
- Solid theoretical foundation with variational inference.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- Critical factual error in distribution description.  
- Inadequate experimental validation (metrics, comparison).  
- Poor language and numerous typos.  
- Unjustified claims about hierarchical advantages.  

This work has merit but requires substantial polishing to meet NIPS standards. With the proposed revisions, it could become a valuable contribution to the literature on structured time-series modeling.
