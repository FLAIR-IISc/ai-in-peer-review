Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), which extends Poisson factorization (PF/PFA) to sequential count data by introducing a multi-layer, RNN-inspired mechanism to capture temporal dependencies. Unlike prior dynamic PF approaches (e.g., PGDS), which impose Markovian Gamma–Poisson transitions and often struggle with long-range dependencies, DDPFA uses neural networks to parameterize distributions over latent factors across time and layers. Inference is performed by mean-field variational inference, combined with an additional optimization step for neural-network parameters using a VAE-like loss. The paper reports experiments on several synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population).

Strengths
- The idea of combining PF with neural networks to model longer-term dependencies is potentially interesting and addresses known limitations of shallow, Markovian dynamics in PGDS-like models.
- The approach uses variational inference and provides closed-form updates for many latent variables, which is computationally appealing.
- The model is evaluated on five real-world datasets, demonstrating the authors’ effort to assess practical utility.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- RNN structure (Eq. 4). The formulation in Eq. 4 does not correspond to a standard RNN: h(n) t depends only on higher-layer memory vectors and not on h(n) t−1,... within the same layer. This removes the temporal recurrence at each layer, which is central to RNNs. Please justify this simplified structure and discuss its implications for modeling temporal dependence.
- Missing model specifications. Section 2 omits essential details: how are the Gamma distributions parameterized in Eq. 4 (shape/rate)? What is the exact distribution generating θt in Eq. 3? These should be explicitly stated.
- “Implicit distributions.” I disagree with the description of Eqs. 9 and 10 as “implicit.” Both are Gamma distributions with known shapes and rates conditioned on their inputs; therefore they are explicit and tractable. Since p(θtk|h(0)) is available in closed form, it should not be called implicit. The same applies to h in Eq. 10.
- Unclear phrasing (lines 30–31). The sentence “This method may have weak points in analyzing other data with different pattern long-time dependence, such as fanatical data and disaster data” is unclear. Please clarify what “different pattern long-time dependence” and “fanatical data” mean, and provide concrete examples and references.
- Probability AutoEncoder subsection (lines 133–155). The objective in Eq. 15 is not clearly defined. What exact quantity is being maximized, under what approximation, and how does it relate to a well-defined ELBO or likelihood-based objective? Eq. 16 resembles a heuristic, VAE-like squared-error loss; please rigorously derive it or justify why this surrogate is appropriate. The notation and dependence assumptions for H(0)′ also need clarification.
- Evaluation metrics. MSE/PMSE are not well aligned with Poisson factorization’s probabilistic modeling goals. Please report predictive log-likelihood (or held-out perplexity for text), and clarify whether predictions are point estimates or derived from the predictive distribution.
- Model capacity (line 207). The choice of K appears too small for several datasets. Please include results with larger K (e.g., K = 100) to test whether the model is underparameterized.
- Figure/discussion mismatch. I could not connect the discussion in lines 224–232 with Fig. 3. Please clarify what the figure demonstrates relative to the claims made (e.g., which factors correspond to which events, and how long-term activation is quantified).
- Placement of technical updates. The variational-update equations (Eqs. 11–14) can be moved to the supplement to improve readability, keeping the main paper focused on modeling and key algorithmic choices.
- Figure quality. The caption in Fig. 4 is not legible. Please improve figure and caption readability.
- Writing and style. The manuscript would benefit significantly from editing for clarity and grammar, reducing passive voice (particularly in the abstract), and fixing typos. Examples:
  - Line 15: “high-dimensional”
  - Line 22: “factorize”
  - Line 24: “property of the”
  - Lines 29, 34: “Dirichlet”
  - Line 34: “nested”
  - Fig. 1: consider “visual representation” (and reconsider “transmission,” which may not be the right term)
  - Incomplete sentences (e.g., line 49)
  - Many missing articles (e.g., “Although the Dirichlet distribution is often used as a prior distribution”)

Pros and cons for acceptance
- Pros:
  - Conceptually interesting direction: injecting neural network structure into PF to capture longer-term dependencies.
  - Variational inference with closed-form updates for many variables is attractive.
  - Broad experimental coverage across five real datasets.

- Cons:
  - The proposed “RNN” structure is not truly recurrent within layers; key modeling choices are underspecified.
  - The “implicit distribution” terminology is inaccurate; several distributions are explicit.
  - The Probability AutoEncoder objective is not clearly derived; the loss appears ad hoc.
  - Evaluation metrics are misaligned with probabilistic modeling; missing predictive log-likelihood.
  - Likely underparameterization (small K); unclear figure–text alignment; figure readability issues.
  - Overall writing quality and numerous typos hinder comprehension.

Recommendation
The paper explores a promising idea but requires substantial clarification and revision before being suitable for NIPS. I recommend addressing the modeling specification issues (true temporal recurrence and explicit parameterizations), providing a principled objective for the neural components, strengthening the evaluation with predictive log-likelihood and larger K, improving figure quality, and thoroughly revising the writing.
