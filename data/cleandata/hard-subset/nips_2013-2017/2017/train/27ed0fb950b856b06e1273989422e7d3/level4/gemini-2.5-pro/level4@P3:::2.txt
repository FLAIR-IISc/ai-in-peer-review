This paper proposes a new generative model for sequential count data, termed the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA). The primary goal is to capture complex, long-range temporal dependencies that are often missed by traditional dynamic models with simpler Markovian assumptions. The core of the model is an extension of Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA), where the time-varying factor scores (`θ_t`) are governed by a deep, hierarchical latent structure. The key innovation lies in using Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) to parameterize the implicit conditional distributions that link latent variables across time and between hierarchical layers. Specifically, the shape parameters of the Gamma-distributed latent variables at each layer are determined by the outputs of RNNs, which process information from adjacent time steps and layers. Inference is performed using a two-stage procedure that combines mean-field variational inference for the latent variables with a gradient-based optimization of the RNN parameters using a loss function inspired by variational autoencoders. The model is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets, where it generally outperforms baselines like the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS) and standard LSTMs.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and compelling synthesis of deep learning techniques (RNNs) and Bayesian matrix factorization (PFA) for analyzing dynamic count data. Using neural networks to define flexible, non-linear conditional priors for the latent dynamics is a powerful idea that moves beyond the standard linear-Gaussian or conjugate-exponential assumptions in state-space models. This hybrid approach offers a principled and highly flexible framework that could have a significant impact on time-series modeling in various domains, including recommender systems, event analysis, and computational social science.

2.  **Technical Approach:** The proposed method for modeling temporal dependencies is elegant. The "probability autoencoder" structure, where separate RNNs learn top-down (prior) and bottom-up (approximate posterior) mappings between layers, is a clever way to structure the model and its inference. Furthermore, optimizing the network parameters based on the expected values of the latent variables is a pragmatic choice that likely improves computational efficiency and training stability compared to methods that rely heavily on sampling.

3.  **Empirical Performance:** The model demonstrates strong empirical results across several real-world datasets, outperforming both a related dynamic Bayesian model (PGDS) and a standard deep learning baseline (LSTM). This suggests that the proposed hybrid architecture is effective at capturing the complex dynamics present in these datasets.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript's clarity is a major concern and significantly hinders a full appreciation of the work.
    *   The mathematical exposition is often difficult to follow. For instance, the descriptions of the conditional distributions for the latent variables `h(n)tk` (Eq. 10 and 14) and the associated notation are confusing. The distinction between prior information (`α`) and posterior information (`γ`) and how they are combined needs to be explained more clearly.
    *   The "Probability AutoEncoder" section and the derivation of the loss functions (Eq. 15-18) are particularly opaque. The connection to the standard VAE framework is asserted but not clearly established, and the justification for using an L2 loss on the *expectations* of the distributions is not well-motivated.
    *   The paper would greatly benefit from a detailed graphical model diagram illustrating the precise dependencies between latent variables (`θ`, `h`), neural network parameters, and data across both time and the deep hierarchy. The current Figure 1 is too abstract to be helpful.

2.  **Missing Related Work:** The paper fails to discuss several highly relevant and recent publications. This is a critical omission that weakens the claims of originality and fails to properly situate the work within the current literature. Key missing references include:
    *   Chung et al., "A Recurrent Latent Variable Model for Sequential Data" (NIPS 2015), which provides a foundational framework for combining RNNs and variational inference for sequential data.
    *   Zhou et al., "Augmentable gamma belief networks" (JMLR 2016), which presents a closely related deep generative model for count data based on Gamma-Poisson conjugacy.
    *   Cong et al., "Deep latent Dirichlet allocation..." (ICML 2017), another recent deep topic model that tackles similar challenges.
    A thorough discussion and comparison with these methods are essential.

3.  **Technical Justification and Analysis:**
    *   A key modeling choice—fixing the rate parameter (`βh`) of the Gamma distributions for the hidden layers `h(n)` while learning the shape parameter via RNNs—is presented without any discussion or justification. This is a strong constraint that fundamentally affects the model's flexibility, and its motivation should be explained.
    *   The experimental results reveal important limitations that are not fully addressed. The authors honestly report that adding more layers does not improve performance and that the model suffers from a "pruning" effect where latent dimensions become inactive. While acknowledging these issues is good practice, they challenge the central motivation for a "deep" architecture. The paper would be stronger if it investigated these phenomena more deeply.
    *   The experimental evaluation could be more thorough. A proper ablation study on the impact of key hyperparameters, such as the context window size `c`, is missing.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper presents a novel, interesting, and potentially high-impact model that cleverly combines RNNs with Bayesian PFA. The core idea is elegant, and the empirical results are promising.
*   **For Rejection:** The paper is hampered by significant clarity issues, a critical lack of engagement with highly relevant prior work, and insufficient justification for key modeling choices. The experimental findings also raise questions about the practical utility of the "deep" aspect of the model, which is its main claim.

**Recommendation**

While the core idea of this paper is strong and promising, the execution in its current form falls short of the standards for NIPS. The issues with clarity, missing related work, and incomplete experimental analysis are too significant to overlook. Therefore, I lean towards rejection. However, I believe this is valuable work, and I would strongly encourage the authors to revise the paper to address the weaknesses outlined above for a future submission.
