This paper introduces the deep dynamic Poisson factorization (DDPF) model, extending Poisson factorization (PF) to incorporate temporal dependencies. Unlike prior work on dynamic PF, this approach employs a simplified recurrent neural network (RNN) structure to capture long-term dependencies. Inference is performed via variational inference, augmented with a step to optimize the neural network parameters. The authors demonstrate the model’s performance on five real-world datasets.

Overall, the idea presented is potentially interesting, but the paper requires significant improvements in execution before it would be suitable for acceptance at a conference like NeurIPS. My comments below detail areas for clarification and revision.

**Major Concerns:**

* **RNN Structure (Eq. 4):** The proposed formulation in Eq. 4 does not align with a standard RNN structure. The hidden state *h<sub>t</sub><sup>(n)</sup>* depends only on the top-layer memory vectors and lacks the temporal connection to previous time steps within the same layer characteristic of RNNs. The authors should justify this simplified structure or clarify its relation to traditional RNNs.
* **Model Specification (Section 2):** Section 2 contains incomplete model definitions. The parameterization of the Gamma distribution in Eq. 4 is not specified, and the distribution from which *θ<sub>t</sub>* is generated in Eq. 3 remains unclear. Providing these details is crucial for reproducibility and understanding.
* **“Implicit Distributions” (Lines 110-118):** I disagree with the characterization of Eqs. 9 and 10 as defining “implicit distributions.” These equations explicitly define Gamma distributions with known shape and rate parameters. Given that the distribution of *θ<sub>tk</sub>* (Eq. 9) can be computed in closed form conditioned on *h<sup>(0)</sup>*, it cannot be considered implicit. The same argument applies to *h* in Eq. 10.
* **Clarity of Subsection (Lines 133-155):** The purpose and derivation of the “loss function” in Eq. 15 are unclear. A more detailed explanation of its components and motivation is needed.

**Minor Concerns & Suggestions:**

* **Dataset Applicability (Lines 30-31):** The statement regarding the method’s potential weaknesses with “fanatical data and disaster data” lacks clarity. The authors should elaborate on the specific challenges these data types pose and why DDPF might struggle.
* **Evaluation Metrics:** Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Percentage Mean Squared Error (PMSE) are often inadequate metrics for evaluating PF-like models. I recommend including predictive log-likelihood as a primary evaluation metric to better assess the model’s ability to predict unseen data.
* **Value of K (Line 207):** The chosen value of K in the experiments appears small. I suggest exploring larger values, such as K=100 or greater, to assess the model’s performance with increased dimensionality.
* **Connection Between Text and Figure (Lines 224-232 & Fig. 3):** The discussion in lines 224-232 lacks a clear connection to Fig. 3. The authors should explicitly explain how the figure supports the claims made in the text.
* **Supplementary Material:** The update equations for the variational inference procedure (Eqs. 11-14) can be moved to the supplementary material to streamline the main text.
* **Figure Caption (Fig. 4):** The caption for Fig. 4 is unreadable and needs to be revised for clarity.
* **Writing Style:** The paper suffers from frequent use of the passive voice, particularly in the abstract. Active voice is generally preferred for clarity and conciseness. The authors should revise the manuscript accordingly.
* **Grammar and Typos:** The writing quality requires significant attention. Numerous grammatical errors and typos are present throughout the paper. Specific examples include:
    * Line 15: "high-dimensional"
    * Line 22: "factorize"
    * Line 24: "property of the"
    * Lines 29, 34: "Dirichlet"
    * Line 34: "nested"
    * Fig. 1: "visual representation"
    * Fig. 1: "transmission" (consider a more appropriate term)
    * Incomplete sentences (e.g., line 49)
    * Missing articles (e.g., "Although Dirichlet distribution is often used as prior distribution")



Addressing these concerns will substantially improve the clarity, rigor, and overall quality of the paper, making it more competitive for NeurIPS.
