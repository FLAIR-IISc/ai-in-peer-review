The paper proposes a new model, named Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model, for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model is based on Poisson Factor Analysis and captures dependence among time steps using neural networks, representing implicit distributions. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on both synthetic and real-world datasets, achieving good predicting and fitting performance with interpretable latent structure.

The paper relates to previous work in Bayesian factor analysis models, which have recently shown success in modeling sequentially observed count matrices. The authors discuss the limitations of existing models, such as the lack of ability to capture long-time dependence among time steps, and propose a new approach using a deep and dynamic structure over time steps.

The strengths of the paper include its ability to capture complex time dependence and its use of variational inference for efficient computation. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their model on various datasets, including a comparison with existing methods such as PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The writing is sometimes unclear, with grammatical errors and typos that make the paper difficult to read. Additionally, the authors could have provided more context and background information on the problem they are trying to solve and the relevance of their approach to the field.

Some specific suggestions for improvement include:

* Clarifying the statement on lines 86-87 regarding the modeling of nonnegative numbers.
* Making the best results bold in Table 2.
* Considering the use of held-out perplexity as a stronger indicator in the experiments in Section 4.2.
* Providing a comparison with dynamic PF with one level deep architecture to illustrate the utility of the proposed method with multiple levels of hierarchy.
* Correcting the grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a new and innovative approach to modeling sequential count vectors.
* The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their model on various datasets.
* The paper has the potential to contribute to the field of Bayesian factor analysis and deep learning.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The writing is sometimes unclear and contains grammatical errors and typos.
* The authors could have provided more context and background information on the problem they are trying to solve and the relevance of their approach to the field.
* The comparison with LSTM is useful, but the learning algorithms are different, and therefore, this comparison does not truly justify the need for multiple levels of hierarchy in the proposed method.
