This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, a novel extension of Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) that captures temporal dependencies in sequentially observed count data. The authors propose a hierarchical model that leverages recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions and learn complex relationships between factors over time. Variational inference is employed to estimate the latent variables and approximate the parameters of the neural networks.

The paper presents an interesting approach to modeling temporal dependencies in count data, and the experimental results on real-world datasets are promising. However, there are several areas that require improvement before the paper can be considered for acceptance in a conference like NIPS.

Firstly, the formulation of the model in Eq. 4 does not correspond to a standard Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) structure. The authors should clarify the reasoning behind this simplified structure and its implications on the model's ability to capture long-term dependencies.

Additionally, some model equations are missing or unclear, such as the parameterization of the Gamma distribution in Eq. 4 and the distribution that generates θ_t in Eq. 3. The authors should provide a more detailed and transparent presentation of their model.

The discussion on "implicit distributions" in lines 110-118 is also misleading. The distributions in Eqs. 9 and 10 are explicit Gammas with given shapes and rates. The authors should revise this section to accurately reflect the nature of these distributions.

The subsection on the loss function in Eq. 15 is unclear and requires further explanation. The authors should provide a more detailed derivation of the loss function and its connection to the variational inference procedure.

The experimental evaluation of the model relies solely on Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Predictive Mean Squared Error (PMSE) metrics, which may not be suitable for Poisson Factor Analysis-like models. The authors should consider including additional metrics, such as predictive log-likelihood, to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their model.

The value of K in the experiments (line 207) seems too small, and the authors may want to consider using larger values, such as K=100, to better capture the underlying structure of the data.

The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and Fig. 3 is unclear, and the authors should provide a more explicit explanation of how the figure supports their claims.

The update equations for the variational inference procedure (Eqs. 11-14) can be safely moved to the supplement to improve the paper's readability.

Finally, the writing quality of the paper requires significant improvement. The authors should revise the paper to address the numerous typos, grammatical errors, and unclear sentences.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel extension of Poisson Factor Analysis that captures temporal dependencies in sequentially observed count data.
* The experimental results on real-world datasets are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed model.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The model formulation and presentation require significant improvement to ensure clarity and transparency.
* The experimental evaluation relies on limited metrics and may not provide a comprehensive assessment of the model's performance.
* The writing quality of the paper requires significant improvement to meet the standards of a conference like NIPS.

Recommendation:

The paper has potential, but it requires significant revisions to address the issues mentioned above. The authors should focus on improving the model presentation, experimental evaluation, and writing quality before resubmitting the paper.
