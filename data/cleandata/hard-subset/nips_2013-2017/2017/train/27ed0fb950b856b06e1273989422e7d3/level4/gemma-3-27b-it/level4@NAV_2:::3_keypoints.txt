- Paper introduces DDPFA: a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization model adding RNN-like short-term and deep long-term structure to PFA with variational inference for learning.
- Technical soundness: builds on established PFA methods and uses appropriate variational inference and optimization.
- Novelty: integrating recurrent networks and deep hierarchy into Poisson factorization for sequential count data is a novel contribution.
- Experimental validation: results on synthetic and real datasets (compared to PGDS, LSTM, PFA) support the model’s effectiveness.
- Factual error: incorrect claim about modeling nonnegative numbers — Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution is the correct reference.
- Presentation issues: numerous grammatical errors and typos throughout the manuscript that reduce readability and professionalism.
- Table formatting: best results in Table 2 should be made bold for clarity.
- Evaluation metric: reliance on MSE/PMSE is limiting; held-out perplexity would be a stronger evaluation, especially for textual data.
- Comparative analysis: comparison with LSTM is useful but not fully conclusive because learning algorithms differ.
- Missing baseline: need a comparison with a single-layer (shallow) dynamic PF to isolate the benefits of the proposed deep hierarchy.
- Pruning and depth concerns: observed pruning and limited gains from increased depth suggest architectural/optimization issues needing investigation.
- Limitations of prior work: paper appropriately identifies and attempts to address limitations of PGDS and temporal Dirichlet processes.
- Strength–weakness balance: technically solid and novel but marred by factual, presentation, and comparative-analysis shortcomings.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on correcting factual errors, fixing grammar, adding requested comparisons/metrics, and addressing pruning/depth discussion.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound, empirically validated, and practically useful for recommendation/text/time-series tasks.
- Arguments for rejection: factual inaccuracies, poor presentation, insufficient comparative baselines, and incomplete treatment of pruning/depth issues.
