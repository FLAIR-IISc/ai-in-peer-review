

The paper introduces a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count data, extending previous work on dynamic Poisson factor analysis. The authors propose incorporating recurrent neural networks to model complex temporal dependencies across time steps, with a deep hierarchical structure designed to capture both short-term and long-term dependencies. This approach builds upon existing work in Poisson factor analysis (PFA) [1-2, 9-10] and dynamic Bayesian models [17-19], while addressing limitations in previous methods that rely on shallow temporal structures. The model represents the latent factor scores θt through a deep hierarchical structure where each layer's latent variables depend on neighboring time steps, modeled as a recurrent neural network. The authors employ variational inference for model fitting, with neural network parameters learned via a loss function based on the variational distribution.

The paper demonstrates the DDPFA model on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing its performance against PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA. The results show that DDPFA achieves better predictive performance and provides more interpretable latent structures, particularly for capturing long-term dependencies that other methods struggle with.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to modeling time-dependent count data by introducing a deep hierarchical structure that captures both short-term and long-term temporal patterns, addressing a clear limitation in existing approaches.
2. The model architecture is well-motivated, with clear explanations of how the deep structure captures dependencies across time steps.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic data to validate the model's ability to capture specific patterns and multiple real-world datasets from diverse domains (text, event data, epidemiological data, etc.).
4. The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the model's limitations, including the "pruning" problem and the diminishing returns of adding more layers, which demonstrates careful analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper contains several grammatical errors and inconsistent phrasing that undermine its clarity (e.g., "PFA factorized a count matrix" should be "PFA factorizes a count matrix," "V-dimesnion" should be "V-dimensional," "K-dimension" should be "K-dimensional," "due to the including of feature-wise" should be "due to the inclusion of feature-wise").
2. The comparison with existing methods is not fully convincing. While the paper compares with PGDS, LSTM, and PFA, the comparison with LSTM is problematic since LSTM uses a different learning algorithm (stochastic gradient descent) versus the variational inference used in DDPFA. This makes it difficult to attribute performance differences solely to the model structure.
3. The paper would benefit from a comparison with a single-layer deep structure (i.e., a shallow model with a single recurrent layer) to better demonstrate the value of the multi-layer architecture. The current comparison with PFA (which has no temporal structure) doesn't adequately isolate the contribution of the deep structure.
4. The evaluation metrics (MSE/PMSE) are acceptable, but the authors should have included held-out perplexity as a more standard metric for count data models to strengthen the evaluation.
5. The statement on lines 86-87 about "modeling nonnegative numbers" is inaccurate; it's the Poisson-Gamma distribution (not "Poisson Randomized Gamma") that is used for modeling nonnegative count data.

**Additional comments:**
- The paper would be strengthened by making the best results bold in Table 2 for easier comparison.
- The explanation of the "pruning" problem in the model is insightful, and the authors correctly identify it as potentially arising from the mean-field variational inference approach.
- The ladder-like architecture to address hierarchical structure limitations is a thoughtful addition that merits further exploration.

**Overall assessment:**
The paper presents a novel approach to modeling temporal dependencies in count data with a deep hierarchical structure. While the technical content is sound and the experimental results are promising, the paper's clarity suffers from grammatical errors and some methodological inconsistencies in the comparison with existing methods. The model's contribution is significant, but the presentation would benefit from addressing the grammatical issues and strengthening the comparison with relevant baselines. The work demonstrates good scientific merit and addresses an important problem in time series analysis of count data.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, but needs to address the grammatical errors and strengthen the comparison with relevant baselines to fully demonstrate the value of the deep structure. The authors have provided a solid technical foundation that could be valuable for the community with some refinements.
