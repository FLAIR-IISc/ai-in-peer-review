This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a model building upon Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) to capture temporal dependencies in sequential count data. The authors address limitations of previous dynamic PFA approaches by incorporating a recurrent neural network structure, aiming to model both short- and long-term dependencies. Inference is performed via variational inference, followed by optimization of the neural network parameters. The model is evaluated on four synthetic and five real-world datasets.

The core idea of leveraging neural networks within a PFA framework to model complex temporal dependencies is potentially interesting and relevant to the NIPS audience, particularly given the growing interest in sequential data analysis and deep probabilistic modeling. However, the paper’s execution requires substantial improvements before it would be considered for acceptance. While the authors identify related work in dynamic PFA and deep probabilistic modeling (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 8, 9, 12]), the presentation lacks clarity and technical rigor in several key areas.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a relevant problem:** Modeling sequential count data is important in many applications.
*   **Novel combination:** The integration of recurrent neural networks with PFA is a reasonable approach to address limitations of existing methods.
*   **Empirical evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model on a range of datasets, including real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **RNN Formulation:** The described structure in Equation 4 does not fully align with a standard Recurrent Neural Network (RNN), as the hidden state at time *t* does not depend on previous time steps within the same layer. The justification for this simplified structure is missing.
*   **Incomplete Model Specification:** Several model equations are incomplete, hindering full comprehension. The parameterization of the Gamma distributions in Eq. 4 and the distribution governing θ<sub>t</sub> in Eq. 3 are not clearly defined.
*   **Mischaracterization of "Implicit" Distributions:** The authors repeatedly refer to distributions as “implicit,” which is inaccurate. Equations 9 and 10 present explicit Gamma distributions with defined parameters, not implicit distributions.
*   **Unclear Explanations:** Certain passages lack clarity, for instance, the discussion regarding "fanatical data and disaster data" (lines 110-118) requires further elaboration. The subsection on the loss function (lines 133-155) needs significant clarification.
*   **Evaluation Metrics:** Reliance solely on MSE/PMSE is insufficient for evaluating probabilistic factor models. Predictive log-likelihood should be included as a standard evaluation metric.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of *K* (number of factors) appears small. Exploring a wider range of values (e.g., *K*=100) is crucial to assess model performance and sensitivity to this parameter.
*   **Figure Clarity:** The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and Figure 3 is unclear. Figure 4’s caption is illegible.
*   **Writing Quality:** The paper suffers from poor writing quality, numerous typos, and grammatical errors (examples provided in the original review). Frequent use of the passive voice weakens the presentation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The idea of combining neural networks with PFA is novel and could potentially lead to improved performance in modeling sequential count data.
*   The empirical evaluation demonstrates promising results on several datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The technical presentation is flawed, with incomplete equations and inaccurate descriptions.
*   The lack of clarity and rigor in several sections hinders a full understanding of the proposed method.
*   The evaluation is incomplete, lacking key metrics like predictive log-likelihood.
*   The writing quality is significantly below the standard expected for NIPS.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but its current state is insufficient for acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to address the technical shortcomings, improve the clarity of the presentation, and strengthen the empirical evaluation.  A major revision, addressing the points raised above, is required before reconsideration. Specifically, providing complete model specifications, justifying design choices, improving writing quality, and including more appropriate evaluation metrics are essential.
