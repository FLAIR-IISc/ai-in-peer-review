

This paper proposes a deep dynamic Poisson factorization (DDPF) model extending Poisson factorization (PF) to capture temporal dependencies via a simplified recurrent neural network (RNN) structure. The authors employ variational inference with an additional optimization step for neural network parameters, reporting results on five real-world datasets. While the core idea of leveraging neural networks for long-term temporal dependencies in PF is promising, the paper requires significant revisions to address methodological ambiguities, inadequate evaluation, and poor writing quality before it can be considered for acceptance at NIPS.

**Major Methodological Concerns:**  
The model's RNN structure (Eq. 4) is problematic. The hidden state \(h_t^{(n)}\) depends solely on top-layer memory vectors and lacks temporal recurrence (i.e., no dependence on \(h_{t-1}^{(n)}\)), contradicting standard RNN design. The authors should clarify the rationale for this simplification and justify why it captures long-term dependencies. Additionally, key model details are missing:  
- The parameterization of the Gamma distribution in Eq. 4 is unspecified.  
- The distribution generating \(\theta_t\) (Eq. 3) is not defined, making the model incomplete.  
The discussion of "implicit distributions" (lines 110–118) is incorrect; Eqs. 9–10 explicitly define Gamma distributions with closed-form parameters (given \(h^{(0)}\)), so they cannot be termed "implicit." This mischaracterization undermines the paper's technical credibility.

**Evaluation and Experimentation Deficiencies:**  
The experimental evaluation has critical flaws:  
- MSE/PMSE is inappropriate for PF-based models; predictive log-likelihood must be reported as the primary metric (standard in PF literature).  
- The choice of \(K=10\) (line 207) is unrealistically small for real-world datasets (e.g., K=100 is typical in PF works). Larger values should be tested to ensure model capacity.  
- The discussion of Fig. 3 (lines 224–232) is disconnected from the figure; the relationship must be explicitly explained.  
- The loss function in Eq. 15 (lines 133–155) is undefined; its components and derivation need clarification.

**Writing and Presentation Issues:**  
The paper suffers from severe writing problems:  
- Multiple grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "high-dimensional" [line 15], "factorize" [line 22], "property of the" [line 24], "Dirichlet" [lines 29, 34], "nested" [line 34], "visual representation" [Fig. 1], "transmission" [Fig. 1], incomplete sentences [line 49]).  
- Unclear phrasing (e.g., "fanatical data and disaster data" [lines 30–31] – clarify the intended meaning).  
- Overuse of passive voice (e.g., in the abstract), which violates NIPS writing standards.  
- Figure 4's caption is illegible, hindering interpretation.

**Recommendations for Revision:**  
1. **Clarify the RNN structure:** Either revise Eq. 4 to include temporal dependencies or provide a compelling justification for its omission.  
2. **Complete model specifications:** Define all distributions (Eq. 3, 4), parameterize the Gamma, and explicitly state the generative process.  
3. **Revise evaluation:** Report predictive log-likelihood alongside MSE, test \(K \geq 100\), and ensure all metrics align with PF conventions.  
4. **Fix writing issues:** Eliminate grammatical errors, clarify ambiguous statements (e.g., "fanatical data"), and replace passive voice with active constructions.  
5. **Improve figures:** Redraw Fig. 4 with a readable caption and ensure all figures (including Fig. 3) are referenced accurately in the text.  
6. **Move technical details:** The variational update equations (Eqs. 11–14) can be relocated to the supplement without loss of clarity.

**Conclusion:**  
The DDPF concept has potential, but the current implementation is underdeveloped and the presentation is unprofessional. The methodological gaps (especially the RNN design and missing model specifications) and the inadequate evaluation metrics (MSE instead of log-likelihood) are fundamental flaws for a NIPS submission. The writing errors further diminish the paper’s credibility. With substantial revisions addressing these points, the work could be strengthened. As is, it does not meet NIPS standards for technical rigor or presentation. I recommend rejection pending major revisions.
