The paper deals with incorporating long-range temporal dependence in a Poisson factorization model with deep structure. As opposed to previous works on dynamic PF, this paper employs a recurrent neural network-like structure for modeling this long-term dependency. Finally, inference is carried out using variational inference with optimization routines for finding the parameters of the neural network. The paper has strong technical content but contains some grammatical and factual inaccuracies. These are detailed below:

1. The statement made about modeling nonnegative numbers on lines 86-87 is incorrect. It is the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution that is used for modeling nonnegative numbers.
2. Please make the best results **bold** in Table 2. 
3. Held-out perplexity would have been a stronger indicator in the experiments in Section 4.2, though MSE/PMSE are acceptable.
4. The limitations of existing works for modeling long-range dependency (references [1, 2]) are adequately addressed in this paper. However, to illustrate the utility of the proposed method with multiple levels of hierarchy, a comparison with a dynamic PF with a single level deep architecture could have been useful. The comparison with LSTM is helpful, but the learning algorithms differ, and therefore, this comparison doesn’t fully justify the need for multiple levels of hierarchy in the proposed method.

**Review:**

This paper presents a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating recurrent neural networks to capture short-term dependencies between factors and a deep structure to model long-term dependencies. Inference is performed using variational inference and gradient descent. The authors evaluate their model on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating good predictive and fitting performance with interpretable latent structures.

The core idea of using recurrent neural networks to model the temporal dynamics in PFA is interesting and a natural extension of previous work. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing dynamic PFA models, such as the independence assumption between time steps in [1, 2], and the potential for limited expressive power in models like the Temporal Dirichlet Process [3]. The paper builds upon the existing literature of deep Bayesian models [4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12] and offers a novel combination of techniques tailored for sequential count data.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear motivation, the well-defined model formulation, and the thorough experimental evaluation. The use of both synthetic and real-world datasets is commendable, and the comparison with existing methods (PGDS, LSTM, and PFA) provides a good benchmark. The visualizations in Figures 2 and 3 help illustrate the interpretability of the learned latent factors and their temporal evolution.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The experimental setup could be strengthened by including held-out perplexity as an evaluation metric, particularly for the real-world datasets. While the comparison with LSTM is valuable, the fundamentally different learning algorithms make it difficult to directly attribute performance differences to the deep hierarchical structure. A comparison with a dynamic PF model incorporating only a single layer of depth could better demonstrate the benefits of the proposed multi-layered approach. Furthermore, the paper suffers from several grammatical errors and typos that impact readability. The factual inaccuracy regarding the modeling of nonnegative numbers should also be addressed. The discussion around the "pruning" phenomenon and the limitations of mean-field variational inference are insightful and highlight potential areas for future work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of PFA, recurrent neural networks, and deep learning for modeling sequential count data.
*   Strong experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Clear motivation and well-defined model formulation.
*   Interpretability of the learned latent factors.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper.
*   Factual inaccuracy regarding modeling nonnegative numbers.
*   Limited justification for the need for multiple levels of hierarchy beyond the comparison with LSTM.
*   Lack of held-out perplexity as an evaluation metric.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising approach to modeling sequential count data with long-range dependencies. Despite the identified weaknesses, the novelty of the proposed model, the thorough experimental evaluation, and the potential impact of the work outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the grammatical errors, factual inaccuracies, and suggestions for improving the experimental evaluation. I believe addressing these points would significantly enhance the clarity and impact of the paper.
