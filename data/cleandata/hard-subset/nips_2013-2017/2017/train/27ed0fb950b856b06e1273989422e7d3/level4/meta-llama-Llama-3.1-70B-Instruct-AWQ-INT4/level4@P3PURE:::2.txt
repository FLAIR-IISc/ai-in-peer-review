The authors propose a novel method to capture complex long-time dependencies over count vectors by leveraging recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions, specifically realized through the gamma distribution. The shape parameters of these gamma distributions, which capture time dependencies, are approximated by neural networks that attempt to incorporate information from both prior (higher layer) and likelihood (lower layer). The parameters in the neural network are updated based on the expectation of latent variables, which helps reduce the variance caused by sampling and increase computational efficiency. The model offers an interesting and intelligent approach to addressing the long dependency problem in dynamic models.

Although I find the proposed method compelling, I have some comments and suggestions for improvement. Firstly, there are some critical typos, such as in Equation 10, which need to be corrected. Secondly, the authors consider the scale parameter as fixed and only update the shape parameter in the gamma distributions, which warrants more detailed discussion and analysis. In particular, the authors should provide a clear justification for this design choice and explore its implications on the model's performance.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is limited, as it only presents the performance of the proposed model with two layers, without comparing it to models with a single layer or more layers. This makes it difficult to understand the influence of the number of layers on the model's performance. Additionally, other parameters, such as the size of the window and the number of factors, should be discussed and analyzed.

To facilitate better understanding, I suggest the authors provide detailed graphical illustrations of the entire model. For reference, the authors may consider Figure 1 in [1], which provides a clear and concise visualization of a similar model.

Lastly, the authors should discuss recent developments in deep Poisson factor analysis models, such as those proposed in [2] and [3], in the Introduction to provide a more comprehensive context for their work.
