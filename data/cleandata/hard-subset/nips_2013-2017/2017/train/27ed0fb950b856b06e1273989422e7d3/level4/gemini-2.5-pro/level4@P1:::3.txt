This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, a novel method for analyzing sequential count data. The model builds upon the Poisson Factorization (PF) framework by incorporating a deep, hierarchical structure to capture complex and long-range temporal dependencies. In contrast to previous dynamic PF models which typically rely on first-order Markov assumptions, DDPFA employs recurrent neural networks to represent the implicit temporal distributions of latent factors. Inference is conducted using a hybrid approach that combines mean-field variational inference for the latent variables with gradient-based optimization for the neural network parameters.

The paper presents a technically interesting model, but the manuscript in its current form has several weaknesses in its experimental validation and presentation.

**Strengths:**
*   The model effectively addresses a known limitation of prior work in dynamic topic modeling (e.g., [1, 2]), which often struggles to capture dependencies beyond a single time step. The integration of RNN-like structures into the generative process is a sound and well-motivated approach for modeling more complex temporal patterns.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Insufficient Justification for the Deep Architecture:** The central claim of the paper is that a multi-level deep structure is beneficial for capturing long-range dependencies. However, the experimental results do not provide direct evidence to support this claim. To demonstrate the utility of the proposed hierarchy, a critical ablation study is missing: a comparison against a "shallow" version of the DDPFA model with only a single hidden layer. While the comparison with an LSTM is included, it is not a fully convincing benchmark for this purpose, as the models rely on fundamentally different learning algorithms (variational inference vs. backpropagation through time). An ablation study would provide clearer evidence for the contribution of the deep architecture itself.

2.  **Choice of Evaluation Metrics:** The experiments rely on Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Predicted Mean Squared Error (PMSE) to evaluate performance. While these metrics are acceptable, a stronger and more standard indicator for probabilistic models of count data would be held-out perplexity or log-likelihood. These metrics provide a more direct measure of the model's predictive power on unseen data and would allow for a more rigorous comparison with related work.

3.  **Factual Correction:** The statement on lines 86-87 regarding the modeling of non-count, real-valued data appears to be inaccurate. The authors suggest linking a latent count matrix via a compound Poisson or Gamma belief network. It would be more precise to state that this is typically achieved using the Poisson-Randomized Gamma distribution, which is a key component of this framework.

4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper's clarity is hampered by numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasings that make it difficult to read. A thorough proofreading by a native English speaker is strongly recommended to improve the manuscript. Some examples include:
    *   "PFA factorized a count matrix" (incongruent tense)
    *   "other data with different pattern long-time dependence"
    *   "Examples for this including"
    *   "However, it is rough for them"
    *   "V-dimesnion sequentially"
    *   "K-dimension latent variable"
    *   "due to the including of feature-wise"
    *   "recommend systems" (should be "recommender systems")

5.  **Presentation of Results:** For better readability, I suggest highlighting the best-performing method for each metric in Table 2, for example, by using boldface.
