This paper introduces the deep dynamic Poisson factorization model, a model that builds upon Poisson Factorization (PF) to allow for temporal dependencies. In contrast to previous works on dynamic PF, this paper utilizes a simplified recurrent neural network to capture long-term dependencies. Inference is carried out via variational inference, with an additional step to maximize over the neural network parameters. The paper reports experiments on five real-world datasets.

Overall, I find the idea potentially interesting, but believe the paper requires improvements in its execution before it could be accepted at a conference like NIPS. My detailed comments are below.

The formulation in Eq. 4 does not correspond to a standard recurrent neural network (RNN) structure, as h<sub>t</sub><sup>(n)</sup> depends only on the top-layer memory vectors and not on previous time steps within the same layer, as is typical in RNNs. Is there a specific rationale for this simplified structure?

In Section 2, several model equations are incomplete (e.g., the parameterization of the Gamma distribution in Eq. 4 is not specified, nor is the distribution generating θ<sub>t</sub> in Eq. 3). Providing these details is crucial for reproducibility and clarity.

I disagree with the characterization of the distributions in lines 110-118 as "implicit." Equations 9 and 10 present explicit Gamma distributions with defined shape and rate parameters. Given that the distribution of θ<sub>tk</sub> (Eq. 9) can be computed in closed form conditioned on h<sup>(0)</sup>, it cannot accurately be described as implicit. A similar comment applies to h in Eq. 10.

Lines 30-31: The statement, "This method may have weak points in analyzing other data with different pattern long-time dependence, such as fanatical data and disaster data" is unclear. Elaboration on these limitations and examples would be beneficial.

The subsection in lines 133-155 lacks clarity. The meaning of the "loss function" in Eq. 15 requires further explanation.

Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Predictive Mean Squared Error (PMSE) are not ideal metrics for Poisson Factorization-like models. The results should include, at a minimum, predictive log-likelihood to better assess model performance.

The chosen value of K in the experiments (line 207) appears small. I suggest exploring larger values, such as K=100 or greater, to potentially capture more nuanced patterns in the data.

The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and Fig. 3 is unclear. Clarifying this relationship would improve understanding.

The update equations for the variational inference procedure (Eqs. 11-14) could be safely moved to the supplementary material to streamline the main text.

The caption in Fig. 2 is difficult to read clearly and needs improvement.

I strongly recommend addressing general writing quality and correcting the numerous instances of passive voice, especially in the abstract.

The paper contains numerous typos and grammatical errors. Examples include:
- Line 15: "high-dimensional"
- Line 22: "factorize"
- Line 24: "property of the"
- Lines 29, 34: "Dirichlet"
- Line 34: "nested"
- Fig. 1: "visual representation"
- Fig. 1: "transmission" (consider alternative phrasing)
- Incomplete sentences (e.g., line 49)
- Missing articles (e.g., "Although Dirichlet distribution is often used as prior distribution")

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The model addresses a relevant problem: modeling sequential count data with long-range dependencies.
*   The integration of neural networks with a Poisson factorization model is a novel approach.
*   The experimental results on real-world datasets demonstrate the potential of the proposed model.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The technical details of the model (e.g., parameterization of distributions) are incomplete, hindering reproducibility.
*   The theoretical justification for some design choices (e.g., the simplified RNN structure) is lacking.
*   The evaluation metrics are not well-suited for the model type.
*   The writing quality is poor and contains numerous errors.
*   The explanation of the results and the connection between the figures and the text require significant improvement.

**Overall Assessment:**

I currently recommend **rejection**. While the core idea holds promise, the paper requires substantial revisions to address the aforementioned issues before it would be suitable for acceptance at NIPS. The authors should focus on clarifying the technical details, improving the evaluation, and enhancing the overall presentation.
