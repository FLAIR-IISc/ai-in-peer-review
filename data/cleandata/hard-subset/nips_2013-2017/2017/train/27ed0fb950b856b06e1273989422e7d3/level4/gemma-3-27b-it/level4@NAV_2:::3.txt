The paper deals with incorporating long-range temporal dependence in a Poisson factorization model with deep structure. As opposed to previous works on dynamic PF, this paper employs a recurrent neural network-like structure for modeling the long-term dependency. Finally, the inference is carried out using variational inference with some optimization routines for finding the parameters of the neural network. The paper has good technical content but is also plagued with some grammatical and factual errors. These are listed below:

1. The statement made about modeling nonnegative numbers on lines 86-87 is incorrect. It’s the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution that is used for modeling nonnegative numbers.
2. Please make the best results **bold** in Table 2. 
3. Held-out perplexity would have been a stronger indicator in the experiments in Section 4.2, though MSE/PMSE are acceptable.
4. The limitations in existing works for modeling long-range dependency (references [1, 2]) are adequately addressed in this paper. However, to illustrate the utility of the proposed method with multiple levels of hierarchy, a comparison with dynamic PF with a single level deep architecture could have been useful. The comparison with LSTM is useful, but the learning algorithms differ; therefore, this comparison does not fully justify the need for multiple levels of hierarchy in the proposed method.

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating recurrent neural networks to capture short-term dependencies between factors and a deep structure to model long-term dependencies. Variational inference is used for latent variable estimation and network parameter learning. The authors demonstrate the model’s performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing it to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The model builds upon established techniques like PFA and incorporates deep learning effectively. The variational inference approach appears appropriate, and the described optimization steps are reasonable.
*   **Novelty:** The integration of recurrent neural networks and deep structure into a PFA framework for sequential count data is a novel contribution. The use of a recurrent structure is a natural extension to capture temporal dependencies, and the deep structure aims to address limitations in previous models.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets provide evidence of the model's effectiveness. The comparison with existing methods helps to establish its relative performance.
*   **Addressing Limitations of Prior Work:** The paper correctly identifies limitations in existing approaches like PGDS and temporal Dirichlet processes, motivating the development of DDPFA. The authors demonstrate how the proposed model attempts to overcome these limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Factual Error:** The incorrect statement regarding modeling of nonnegative numbers (point 1 above) indicates a lack of thorough understanding of related distributions.
*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The numerous grammatical errors and typos detract from the paper's readability and professionalism. While not fatal, these issues require careful attention during revision.
*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While comparisons with LSTM and PFA are provided, a comparison with a single-layer deep dynamic PF model would better isolate the benefits of the proposed deep architecture. The differing learning algorithms between LSTM and DDPFA make the comparison less direct.
*   **Evaluation Metric:** Reliance solely on MSE/PMSE is somewhat limiting. Including a metric like held-out perplexity, particularly for text-based datasets (e.g., NIPS corpus), would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
*   **Pruning and Network Depth:** The authors acknowledge issues with "pruning" and the limited benefit of increasing network depth. These observations suggest potential areas for further investigation and optimization of the model architecture.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to modeling sequential count data.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the model on diverse datasets.
*   The paper addresses limitations in existing methods and provides a compelling motivation for the proposed approach.
*   The model has the potential to be useful in various applications, including recommendation systems, text analysis, and time series analysis.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The presence of factual errors and grammatical issues detracts from the paper’s overall quality.
*   The comparative analysis could be strengthened by including a baseline with a single-layer deep dynamic PF model.
*   The limited discussion of the “pruning” phenomenon and the lack of clear solutions could raise concerns about the model’s robustness.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field, with a clear presentation of a novel model and convincing experimental results. However, the identified weaknesses – particularly the factual error, grammatical issues, and limited comparative analysis – need to be addressed before acceptance. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon the authors addressing the points outlined above. Specifically, correcting the factual error, thoroughly proofreading the manuscript for grammatical accuracy, and conducting the suggested comparative analysis would significantly improve the paper's quality.
