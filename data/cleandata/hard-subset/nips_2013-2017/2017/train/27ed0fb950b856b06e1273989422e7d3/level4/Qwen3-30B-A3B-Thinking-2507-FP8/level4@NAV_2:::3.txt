

The paper introduces a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model to capture long-term temporal dependencies in sequential count data, extending prior work on dynamic Poisson factorization (PFA) and Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS). It builds on recent NIPS contributions such as Deep Exponential Families [8] and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9,10], while addressing limitations in existing models that struggle with complex long-range dependencies due to shallow temporal structures. The authors propose a hierarchical neural network architecture to model implicit distributions across time steps, enabling both short-term complex relationships and long-term dependencies. Inference employs variational methods with neural network parameters optimized via a novel loss function derived from the variational distributions. The approach is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, ASP), showing competitive performance in prediction and fitting tasks compared to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA baselines.

Strengths: The technical approach is well-motivated and addresses a clear gap in modeling long-term temporal dependencies in count data. The integration of recurrent neural networks with Poisson factorization represents a meaningful extension of prior work. The experimental evaluation covers diverse datasets and demonstrates the model’s effectiveness in capturing interpretable latent structures (e.g., the sustained Israel-Palestinian conflict factor in ICEWS). The inference algorithm is soundly derived from variational principles, and the results on synthetic data (Table 1) convincingly show DDPFA’s superiority in complex temporal pattern learning. The discussion of limitations (pruning, layer redundancy) adds valuable transparency.

Weaknesses: The paper contains significant grammatical and technical errors that impair clarity. For instance, Line 86–87 incorrectly attributes the use of "Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution" for modeling nonnegative numbers (it is Gamma-Poisson). Several typos persist (e.g., "V-dimesnion," "K-dimension," "due to the including of feature-wise"). The comparison with single-layer dynamic PFA is absent, making it unclear whether the multi-layer structure provides tangible benefits over simpler hierarchical designs—this is a critical omission for establishing the model’s novelty. While the LSTM comparison is useful, the different training paradigms (variational inference vs. SGD) weaken the argument for the necessity of depth. The choice of MSE/PMSE over held-out perplexity (a standard metric for count data) in Section 4.2 is suboptimal for evaluating predictive performance. The ICEWS prediction results (Table 2) are notably weaker than PGDS, and the paper does not sufficiently explain this anomaly.

Pro: Strong technical foundation; clear problem formulation; competitive results on synthetic data; thorough real-world validation; insightful discussion of limitations.  
Con: Grammatical/technical errors reduce readability; missing comparison with single-layer hierarchy; insufficient justification for deep structure; suboptimal evaluation metrics; ICEWS performance gap unexplained.  

The paper makes a scientifically valid contribution to sequential count analysis, but grammatical issues and methodological gaps in the comparison undermine its quality. With revisions to address these points, it would be a strong fit for NIPS.
