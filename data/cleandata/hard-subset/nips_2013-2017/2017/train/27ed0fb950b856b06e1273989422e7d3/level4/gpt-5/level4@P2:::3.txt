Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a deep generative model for sequential count vectors. Building on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA), the method introduces a hierarchical, recurrent neural architecture to capture both short-term and long-range temporal dependencies in factor activations. The model augments standard PFA with feature-wise and component-wise Gamma variables (Ψ and Λ) to encourage sparsity and smoothness, and uses a variational inference scheme: a mean-field coordinate-ascent update for latent variables and an autoencoder-style objective to train the recurrent neural networks that parameterize implicit conditional distributions. Experiments on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE and PMSE.

This work relates to dynamic PF models such as PGDS, Gamma-NB processes, and Poisson-process-based dynamics, as well as deep latent variable models including Deep Exponential Families, deep PFA (e.g., RBM/SBN-based), gamma belief networks, and neural autoregressive models. The novelty lies in combining a deep, recurrent prior over factor scores with Poisson factorization and a VI procedure that couples bottom-up and top-down networks.

Strengths:
- Tackles an important limitation of shallow dynamic PF models by explicitly modeling long-range temporal dependencies.
- Integrates hierarchical structure with recurrent neural parameterizations, which is a plausible way to capture complex temporal patterns.
- Includes feature-wise and component-wise Gamma priors (Ψ, Λ) that improve sparsity and interpretability within a PFA framework.
- Broad empirical evaluation across synthetic and diverse real datasets; DDPFA often outperforms baselines in both fitting and prediction.
- Qualitative analysis suggests more persistent factor activations than PGDS, aligning with real-world expectations.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Evaluation metrics: For text-like and high-dimensional count data, held-out perplexity (or predictive log-likelihood) is a more standard and sensitive metric than MSE/PMSE. Please report held-out perplexity/log-likelihood for Section 4.2 in addition to MSE/PMSE.
- Ablation for depth: To justify the multi-level hierarchy, include a comparison to a one-layer dynamic PF (i.e., your model with a single latent layer or a non-deep recurrent prior). The LSTM comparison is informative but does not isolate the benefit of depth since the learning objectives differ.
- Clarity and correctness: There are several grammatical issues and a factual error that impede readability and could confuse readers (details below). A careful revision is needed.
- Neural training objective: The “probability autoencoder” objectives (Eqs. 15–18) appear heuristic and are only loosely connected to a principled ELBO. Clarify the derivation, assumptions, and the relationship to standard amortized VI (e.g., VAE-style reparameterization or IWAE), and discuss whether tighter bounds or stochastic gradient VI could be used.
- Inference assumptions: The mean-field factorization over all latent variables is strong and may contribute to pruning/underutilization. Comment on whether structured variational families or auxiliary-variable methods could mitigate this.
- Reporting: In tables, highlight the best results (e.g., bold the minima as suggested). Provide standard deviations or confidence intervals over multiple runs to quantify variability, especially for neural methods.

Quality:
- The modeling idea is technically sound and well-motivated. The Poisson–Gamma conjugacy and auxiliary count augmentation are correctly leveraged for VI updates.
- However, parts of the inference section are under-specified. Please provide clearer derivations or appendices for the Gamma posteriors in Eqs. 9–14, the precise parameterizations output by the RNNs (shape/rate or alternatives), and the optimization details (learning rates, scheduling, initialization, regularization).
- Discuss computational complexity, convergence behavior, and sensitivity to the window size c, number of layers, and K.

Clarity:
- The paper is difficult to follow in places due to grammar and style. Some examples to correct:
  - “unit the data”
  - “Noticing the lack of capturing …”
  - “PFA factorized a count matrix” (tense inconsistent; use present tense consistently)
  - “other data with different pattern long-time dependence …”
  - “Examples for this including …”
  - “shows excellent results on predict and …”
  - “However, it is rough for them …”
  - “V-dimesnion sequentially …”
  - “K-dimension latent variable …”
  - “due to the including of feature-wise …”
  - “recommend systems”
  Please perform a thorough language edit to improve readability, fix typos, and standardize notation.

Originality:
- Combining a deep, recurrent implicit prior with Poisson factorization is a novel and interesting direction relative to PGDS and earlier deep PFA variants. The paper would benefit from a clearer positioning versus related deep count models (e.g., hierarchical Poisson factorization, gamma belief networks, deep exponential families), explicitly contrasting how temporal dependencies are modeled and how your inference differs.

Significance:
- If robust and reproducible, the approach could be useful for sequential count modeling across domains. The empirical gains are promising, though the ICEWS prediction underperformance highlights that smoothing from PGDS can sometimes be advantageous. Stronger metrics, ablations, and uncertainty reporting would strengthen the case for adoption.

Specific comments:
- The statement about modeling nonnegative numbers (lines 86–87) appears incorrect. It is the Poisson randomized Gamma construction that models nonnegative counts via the Gamma–Poisson relationship. Please correct this passage and cite appropriately.
- Table 2: Please bold the best results.
- Consider releasing code and scripts for reproducibility, and specify all hyperparameters (architectures, optimizers, learning rates, early stopping criteria, number of VI iterations, random seeds).
- Discuss limitations (e.g., pruning, depth usefulness) and potential remedies (e.g., ladder connections, auxiliary variables, structured VI), possibly with a brief ablation if space permits.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important gap (long-range dependence) in dynamic Poisson factorization.
- Conceptually appealing integration of deep recurrent priors with conjugate updates.
- Broad empirical evaluation with generally strong results and interpretable factors.

Cons for acceptance:
- Clarity issues and a factual error need correction.
- Evaluation lacks standard predictive metrics and depth ablations.
- The neural objective is heuristic and its theoretical grounding could be clearer.

Overall, this is a promising contribution that would benefit from clearer exposition, stronger evaluation (perplexity/log-likelihood, ablations), and a careful language edit.
