This paper introduces a deep Poisson factorization model designed to capture long-range temporal dependencies in count data. The core contribution is the integration of a recurrent neural network-style architecture to model the evolution of latent factors over time, moving beyond the Markovian assumptions of previous dynamic Poisson factorization models. The authors develop a variational inference framework for model estimation, which includes specific optimization routines for the neural network parameters.

While the paper is technically sound and addresses an important problem, its current form is weakened by several issues ranging from factual inaccuracies to an incomplete experimental evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Motivation:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel approach to modeling temporal dynamics in count data. The proposed integration of a recurrent structure into a deep Poisson factorization model is a well-motivated and technically sound method for capturing long-range dependencies.
2.  **Addressing Prior Limitations:** The authors effectively address the limitations of prior work (e.g., references [1, 2]), which are often constrained by first-order Markov assumptions. The proposed model offers a more flexible and potentially more powerful alternative.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Factual Inaccuracy:** The statement on lines 86-87 regarding the modeling of non-negative numbers is incorrect. The Poisson distribution is for discrete counts. The model described appears to be the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution (or a similar compound Poisson model), which is used for modeling non-negative real-valued data. This should be corrected for technical accuracy.

2.  **Experimental Validation:** The experimental validation could be strengthened in two key areas to better support the paper's claims.
    *   **Evaluation Metric:** For the experiments in Section 4.2, held-out perplexity is a more standard and arguably stronger indicator of model fit for this class of probabilistic models than MSE/PMSE. Including perplexity would provide a more robust assessment of the model's performance.
    *   **Baseline Comparisons:** The justification for the model's deep, multi-level architecture is not fully substantiated by the current experiments. To demonstrate the utility of the proposed hierarchical structure, a more direct comparison would be highly beneficial. An ablation study comparing the proposed model against a simpler, single-level version of the same dynamic architecture would provide direct evidence for the added value of the deep structure. While the comparison against an LSTM is useful, its impact is limited because the models are trained with different learning algorithms. This makes it difficult to attribute performance differences solely to the architectural choices of the proposed model. A more controlled comparison is needed to isolate and validate the specific contribution of the deep hierarchy.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript would benefit significantly from a thorough proofreading. There are numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrases that hinder readability (e.g., "unit the data," "Noticing the lack of capturing," "due to the including of feature-wise," "recommend systems"). Inconsistent verb tenses also disrupt the narrative flow (e.g., "PFA factorized a count matrix" in a paragraph using the present tense). For improved clarity in the results section, I also suggest making the best-performing results in Table 2 bold.

In summary, the paper presents a promising and well-motivated model with a solid technical foundation. However, to be ready for publication, the authors should address the technical inaccuracy, strengthen the experimental validation with more appropriate metrics and baselines, and meticulously revise the manuscript to improve grammar and clarity.
