Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for modeling sequential count vectors. Building on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA), the model introduces a deep hierarchical set of latent variables h(n)t and uses recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to parameterize “implicit” conditional distributions over time. Specifically, Poisson observations are modeled as xvt ~ Poisson(∑k θtk φvk λk ψv). Temporal dependencies are captured by modeling θt and the deeper latent layers h(n)t as Gamma-distributed with shape parameters given by RNNs over sliding windows of past (and, in an auxiliary sense, future) latent states. Inference uses mean-field variational updates for conjugate Gamma-Poisson components and a two-stage procedure that alternates coordinate-ascent VI for latent variables with training of the RNN parameters via a “probability autoencoder” objective based on squared errors between distributional expectations.

The paper relates to several threads: dynamic PFA and Gamma–NB process models (e.g., PGDS), deep latent variable models for counts such as Deep Exponential Families and Gamma Belief Networks (GBN), and RNN-based latent variable models for sequences (e.g., VRNN). It also touches adjacent work on Recurrent Poisson Factorization for recommendations and neural autoregressive topic models. The paper’s main novelty is combining PFA with RNN-parameterized Gamma shape parameters in a deep hierarchy intended to capture both short-range and long-range temporal dependence.

Strengths
- Addresses an important setting: high-dimensional, sparse sequential count data common in text, events, and recommendations.
- Conceptually interesting integration of deep temporal structure with conjugate PFA, potentially improving long-horizon dependence modeling.
- Uses conditionally conjugate mean-field updates for most latent variables, which can be computationally efficient and stable.
- Empirical results on synthetic and diverse real datasets suggest improved fit/prediction over PFA and some advantage over PGDS/LSTM in several cases; qualitative ICEWS factors appear interpretable.

Weaknesses and detailed comments (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance)
Quality
- Inference objective for the neural networks is ad hoc. The “probability autoencoder” relies on squared errors between expectations of distributions (Eqs. 15–18) rather than optimizing a principled ELBO that includes the amortized parameters. There is no guarantee of a valid variational bound or convergence of the two-stage procedure. Please justify this objective theoretically or replace it with a proper ELBO (e.g., VRNN-style ELBO; implicit reparameterization for Gamma; or black-box VI with control variates).
- The model fixes/does not update the Gamma rate parameters while learning shape parameters via RNNs. This design choice can materially affect posterior geometry and identifiability. Provide analysis or ablations showing the impact of learning vs fixing rates, and discuss identifiability with the additional scaling variables λk and ψv (there is potential redundancy among λ, ψ, φ, and θ).
- Evaluation metrics use MSE/PMSE on counts. Predictive log-likelihood (or perplexity) on held-out data is more standard for probabilistic count models. Please report predictive log-likelihoods and calibration; clarify train/validation/test splits and hyperparameter tuning across baselines.
- Experimental comparisons omit closely related baselines such as Recurrent Poisson Factorization and deep count models (e.g., sequential variants of GBN/DEFs). Including these would better contextualize gains and novelty.
- Limited ablations. The paper largely reports results for two layers. Although Table 3 is mentioned, a systematic study of depth (1 vs 2 vs 3 layers), window size c, and number of factors K is important to understand when depth helps and to substantiate the discussion on pruning and ladder architectures.
- Complexity and scalability are not discussed (time per iteration, memory footprint vs V, T, K, layers), nor is code availability; both are important for practitioners.

Clarity
- The paper is difficult to follow due to numerous typos, inconsistent notation, and incomplete/misaligned references. Examples: Eq. 10 appears to have errors; several sentences contain grammar issues (“Natrually,” “thi figure,” “fanatical data”); many references are incomplete (missing venues/pages) and numbering is inconsistent. Please thoroughly proofread, fix Eq. 10, and standardize notation (shape/rate parameterization, expectations, window indexing).
- The generative story and inference flow would benefit from a clear plate diagram and a compact summary algorithm. A graphical illustration akin to PGDS Figure 1 would greatly aid understanding.
- The role of ffeed/fback RNNs, their architectures, and the ladder-style connections should be described more precisely, including inputs, outputs, and parameter sharing across time and layers.

Originality
- The paper’s core idea is promising, but the relationship to several very similar models requires clearer positioning. In particular, prior work on “Deep Poisson Factor Modeling with a recurrent structure” (cited as [12]) and Recurrent Poisson Factorization directly overlap in spirit. Please articulate the key methodological differences (e.g., hierarchical depth, Gamma parametrization, inference) and include empirical comparisons where feasible.

Significance
- If the inference is made more principled, and comparisons expanded, the approach could be impactful for sequential count modeling. As it stands, the empirical gains are encouraging but not yet conclusive due to missing baselines/metrics and the ad hoc training objective.

Specific suggestions
- Provide a principled ELBO and perform joint optimization of variational parameters and RNN parameters; consider implicit reparameterization tricks for Gamma or augmentation schemes to enable gradient-based VI; alternative: SG-MCMC for top layers with amortized proposals.
- Add predictive log-likelihood and calibration metrics; report uncertainty (e.g., standard errors over multiple runs).
- Include ablations: depth (1/2/3 layers), K, window size c, with training curves; analyze “pruning” quantitatively and demonstrate the ladder architecture’s effect.
- Normalize or constrain one of the scaling components (e.g., fix column sums of Φ or remove either λ or ψ) to mitigate identifiability.
- Expand related work discussion to include VRNN (Chung et al., 2015), GBN (Zhou et al., JMLR 2016), Deep LDA with SG-Riemannian MCMC (Cong et al., ICML 2017), and Recurrent Poisson Factorization; clarify differences.
- Add a plate diagram and an algorithm box summarizing the two-stage training; detail the RNN settings (hidden sizes, activations, optimization, learning rates).
- Release code and data splits.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with an appealing hybrid deep probabilistic approach.
- Empirical results are promising on varied datasets; factors appear interpretable.
- Conjugate updates keep parts of the inference efficient and scalable.

Cons for acceptance
- Inference objective for the neural networks is not a proper variational bound; theoretical justification and stability are unclear.
- Limited and sometimes non-standard evaluation; missing key baselines; insufficient ablations on depth and hyperparameters.
- Clarity and presentation issues (typos, notation, incomplete citations); missing graphical model.

Overall, this is an interesting direction with potential, but the current submission needs significant clarification, a more principled training objective, and stronger experimental evaluation to meet NeurIPS standards.

Minor issues and typos (non-exhaustive)
- Eq. 10 appears incorrect/inconsistent; please fix and standardize Gamma shape/rate notation throughout.
- Numerous typos and grammar errors: e.g., “Natrually,” “thi figure,” “fanatical data,” “strcuture,” “distrbution,” “is equal to maximize,” etc.
- Inconsistent references; please complete and verify all citations and numbering.
