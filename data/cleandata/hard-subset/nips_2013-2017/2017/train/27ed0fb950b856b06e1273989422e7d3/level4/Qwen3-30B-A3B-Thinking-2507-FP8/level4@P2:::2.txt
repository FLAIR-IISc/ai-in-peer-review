

The authors propose Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a novel model for analyzing sequential count data by integrating recurrent neural networks (RNNs) with Poisson factor analysis to capture complex long-term temporal dependencies. Building on prior work like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) [2] and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9, 10], DDPFA uses RNNs to parameterize the shape parameters of gamma-distributed latent factors, enabling hierarchical modeling of time dependencies. The inference employs variational methods with neural networks trained via a loss function balancing reconstruction and prior matching. The paper demonstrates strong performance on synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, etc.) compared to PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA.

**Strengths:**  
*Originality & Significance (1):* The integration of deep RNNs with Poisson factorization to model long-range temporal dependencies represents a meaningful extension beyond shallow dynamic models like PGDS [2] and standard PFA. The approach addresses a clear limitation in prior work—PGDS’s inability to capture complex long-term patterns (e.g., in EBOLA/ASP datasets) due to its Markovian assumptions. The interpretability of latent factors (Figure 2) and strong empirical results on diverse datasets (Tables 1–2) demonstrate significant practical value for sequential count analysis in applications like recommendation systems and event tracking.  
*Quality (1):* The technical approach is sound, with well-justified variational inference and a clear loss function (Eq. 18) for neural network training. The model’s ability to outperform baselines on complex synthetic patterns (SDS3–4) and real-world data (e.g., ICEWS factor dynamics in Figure 3) provides strong empirical support. The use of gamma distributions for conjugacy (Eq. 8) is appropriate for Poisson count data.  
*Clarity (1):* The paper is generally well-structured, with clear problem formulation and model description. The experimental design is thorough, covering multiple datasets and baselines.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Originality & Significance (2):* The introduction overlooks critical recent work on deep Poisson factorization, notably Zhou et al. [2, 3] (2016–2017), which directly addresses hierarchical modeling of count data using gamma belief networks and stochastic MCMC. This omission weakens the claim of novelty; the paper should explicitly contrast DDPFA with these methods (e.g., Zhou’s "augmentable gamma belief networks" [2] and "deep latent Dirichlet allocation" [3]).  
*Quality (2):* The handling of gamma distribution parameters requires deeper justification. The paper fixes the rate parameter β while only updating the shape parameter α (e.g., Eq. 9–10), but does not discuss why this choice is optimal or analyze the impact of β’s fixed value on model flexibility. This could introduce bias in latent factor estimation.  
*Quality (3):* The ablation study (Table 3) is insufficient. The authors test only two-layer models but omit comparisons with single-layer (PFA-like) and deeper architectures (e.g., 3+ layers), making it unclear whether the "ladder architecture" (Section 3) truly adds value beyond shallow models. The influence of hyperparameters (window size, K) is also not systematically explored.  
*Clarity (2):* The model’s graphical structure (Section 2) is described textually but lacks a clear diagram (as suggested in the draft review), hindering understanding of the RNN layers’ connections. Figure 1 is referenced but not provided in the draft, so its absence is a notable gap.  
*Quality (4):* The paper identifies "pruning" (Section 5) as a limitation but does not demonstrate how the ladder architecture mitigates it. Without empirical evidence (e.g., layer-wise latent activation patterns), this claim remains speculative.  

**Pro/Con Summary for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel RNN-based temporal modeling for count data; strong empirical results on complex patterns; interpretable latent factors; well-structured methodology.  
*Con:* Incomplete comparison with key recent work (Zhou et al. [2, 3]); insufficient ablation study on model depth/hyperparameters; inadequate justification for gamma parameterization; missing model diagram.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to sequential count modeling with clear empirical advantages over baselines. However, the omission of recent deep Poisson factorization literature [2, 3] and the underdeveloped ablation study undermine the novelty claim. To strengthen the submission, the authors should:  
1. Revise the introduction to explicitly contrast DDPFA with Zhou et al.’s work [2, 3] and justify their approach’s distinctiveness.  
2. Expand Table 3 to include single-layer (PFA) and deeper (e.g., 3-layer) results, plus ablation on window size and K.  
3. Add a model diagram (similar to [1]) and clarify the gamma parameterization choice.  
4. Address the "pruning" issue with empirical evidence (e.g., latent activation heatmaps).  

With these revisions, the paper would be a strong fit for NIPS. As submitted, it is technically competent but lacks sufficient novelty justification and experimental depth to warrant acceptance. **Recommendation: Weak Reject.**
