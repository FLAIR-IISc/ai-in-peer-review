Summary
This paper proposes a deep, recurrent extension of Poisson factorization to capture long-range temporal dependence in count data. Instead of the short-memory transitions common in prior dynamic PF models, the authors employ an RNN-like structure to propagate latent factors across time. Inference is performed via variational methods, with gradient-based optimization for the neural parameters.

Strengths
- Tackles an important limitation of dynamic PF—limited temporal memory—by introducing a recurrent mechanism.
- The hierarchical (multi-level) latent structure is well-motivated and could improve expressivity for complex temporal patterns.
- Empirical results suggest gains over several baselines, indicating practical value.

Main concerns and suggestions
1) Factual correction (lines 86–87): The claim about modeling nonnegative numbers is inaccurate. It is the Poisson randomized gamma (PRG) distribution that is used to model nonnegative real-valued data within this framework. Please revise the text accordingly and cite appropriately.

2) Evaluation metrics: For Section 4.2, held-out predictive likelihood or perplexity is standard and generally more informative than MSE/PMSE for count models and topic-type analyses. MSE/PMSE are fine as secondary metrics, but please add held-out perplexity (with a clear description of the held-out protocol across time and dimensions).

3) Depth ablation and baselines: To isolate the benefit of hierarchical depth, please include:
- A dynamic PF baseline with a single level (i.e., your model with depth=1 under the same training algorithm), and
- An ablation over depth (e.g., 1, 2, 3 layers), reporting both performance and parameter counts to control for capacity.
The comparison to LSTM is informative but not conclusive for justifying hierarchy, given different objective functions and learning algorithms; the above ablations would more directly demonstrate the contribution of depth.

4) RNN design and long-range dependence: Clarify the recurrent mechanism. If it is a simple RNN, explain how vanishing gradients are mitigated (e.g., gating, skip connections, residual paths, layer normalization, or temporal dilations). Report sensitivity to sequence length (e.g., varying truncation length or backprop-through-time horizon) to substantiate long-range modeling claims.

5) Variational inference details and reproducibility:
- Provide the explicit ELBO used, including the terms involving the recurrent transitions, and how expectations are computed.
- Specify the variational family, any amortization networks, reparameterizations (if applicable), and whether KL annealing or gradient clipping is used.
- Detail optimization settings (optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, number of iterations), initialization, and convergence diagnostics.
- Include computational complexity per iteration in terms of T, K, and V, and report wall-clock and memory usage where feasible.
- Consider releasing code or pseudocode to enhance reproducibility.

6) Data and experimental protocol:
- Describe data preprocessing, train/validation/test splits across time, and the held-out construction for perplexity.
- Include uncertainty estimates (e.g., standard errors over multiple runs) to assess robustness.
- Where possible, control for total parameter count across baselines to ensure fair comparisons.

7) Presentation and clarity:
- Table 2: Please boldface the best results in each column for quick reference.
- Improve the exposition of the generative process and inference procedure. A compact diagram or step-by-step listing would help readers follow the multi-level and recurrent structure.

Typos and language issues (suggested fixes)
- “unit the data” → “unite the data” or “until the data,” depending on intent; likely “unite the data” is not correct—consider “aggregate the data” or “combine the data.”
- “Noticing the lack of capturing …” → “Noting the lack of ability to capture …” or “Observing that existing models fail to capture …”
- “PFA factorized a count matrix” → Ensure tense consistency: “PFA factorizes a count matrix” (present) or “PFA factorized a count matrix” (past); prefer present to match surrounding text.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses a meaningful gap by endowing Poisson factorization with long-range temporal memory via a deep recurrent architecture. The idea is timely, technically interesting, and potentially impactful. The current draft, however, would benefit from (i) correcting the PRG-related statement, (ii) adding held-out perplexity and clearer experimental protocols, (iii) providing depth ablations and a dynamic PF (depth=1) baseline trained under the same algorithm, and (iv) improving clarity around the recurrent design and inference details. With these revisions and careful proofreading, the work would be significantly strengthened and easier to evaluate and reproduce.
