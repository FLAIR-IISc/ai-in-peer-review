This paper introduces the deep dynamic Poisson factorization model, a model that builds upon Poisson Factorization (PF) to allow for temporal dependencies. In contrast to previous work on dynamic PF, this paper uses a simplified recurrent neural network structure to capture long-term dependencies. Inference is carried out via variational inference, with an additional step to maximize the evidence lower bound with respect to the neural network parameters. The paper reports experiments on five real-world datasets.

Overall, I found the idea potentially interesting, but I believe the paper needs some improvements in its execution before it can be accepted at a conference like NIPS.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem – modeling sequential count data with complex temporal dependencies – which appears in many application areas.
*   The combination of PF with neural networks is a reasonable approach to capture both local and long-range dependencies.
*   The experimental evaluation on multiple datasets is commendable.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   The description of the model, particularly the recurrent structure in Eq. 4, is unclear and deviates from standard RNN implementations. The relationship between the different layers and how information flows through time isn't well explained.
*   Several model equations lack sufficient detail (e.g., parameterization of Gamma distributions in Eq. 4, distribution for θt in Eq. 3). This hinders understanding and reproducibility.
*   The terminology “implicit distributions” (lines 110-118) is misleading, as the distributions described (Eqs. 9 & 10) are explicitly defined Gamma distributions.
*   Certain explanations, like the one regarding “fanatical data and disaster data” (lines 30-31), lack clarity and justification.
*   The discussion of the loss function (lines 133-155) and Eq. 15 is unclear.
*   The evaluation metrics (MSE/PMSE) are not ideal for PF-like models. Predictive log-likelihood is a more appropriate metric.
*   The choice of *K* in the experiments (line 207) might be too small, potentially limiting the model’s representational capacity.
*   The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and Fig. 3 is weak and lacks clear explanation.
*   The detailed update equations for variational inference (Eqs. 11-14) could be moved to the supplementary material.
*   The writing quality is uneven and contains numerous typos and grammatical errors.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Eq. 4 and RNN Structure:** The formulation in Eq. 4 doesn't represent a standard RNN structure. *h<sub>t</sub><sup>(n)</sup>* depends only on the top-layer memory vectors and not on previous time steps within the same layer, as is characteristic of RNNs. What is the rationale for this simplified structure?

*   **Model Equations:** Please provide complete and clear definitions for all model equations. Specifically, clarify the parameterization of the Gamma distributions in Eq. 4 and the distribution that generates *θ<sub>t</sub>* in Eq. 3.

*   **“Implicit Distributions”:** The term "implicit distributions" (lines 110-118) is inaccurate.  Eqs. 9 and 10 define explicit Gamma distributions. Since the distribution of *θ<sub>tk</sub>* (Eq. 9) can be computed in closed form conditioned on *h<sup>(0)</sup>*, it cannot be considered implicit.  The same applies to *h* in Eq. 10.

*   **Clarity of Explanation:** Lines 30-31: “This method may have weak points in analyzing other data with different pattern long-time dependence, such as fanatical data and disaster data” is unclear. Please elaborate on why the temporal Dirichlet process might be less suitable for such data.

*   **Loss Function:** The subsection in lines 133-155 is not clear. Explain the “loss function” in Eq. 15 more thoroughly. What is being optimized, and what are the terms in the equation representing?

*   **Evaluation Metrics:** MSE/PMSE are not ideal for PF-like models. Include predictive log-likelihood as a primary evaluation metric.

*   **Value of K:** The value of *K* (line 207) seems small. Consider experimenting with larger values, such as *K* = 100, to assess the model's ability to capture more complex latent structures.

*   **Fig. 3 and Discussion:** The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and Fig. 3 is unclear. Explain how the figure illustrates the points being made.

*   **Variational Inference Equations:** Move the detailed update equations for variational inference (Eqs. 11-14) to the supplementary material.

*   **Fig. 4 Caption:** The caption in Fig. 4 is difficult to read. Revise for clarity.

*   **Writing Quality:** The writing quality needs significant improvement. Address the numerous typos, grammatical errors, and missing articles. Examples: line 15 ("high-dimensional"), line 22 ("factorize"), line 24 ("property of the"), lines 29, 34 ("Dirichlet"), line 34 ("nested"), Fig. 1 ("visual representation," "transmission"), incomplete sentences (e.g., line 49), and missing articles (e.g., "Although Dirichlet distribution is often used as prior distribution").

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The model proposes a novel combination of PF and neural networks for sequential count data.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the potential of the approach on a diverse set of datasets.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The model description is unclear in several places, hindering understanding and reproducibility.
*   The evaluation is incomplete, lacking a key metric (predictive log-likelihood).
*   The writing quality is poor, making it difficult to follow the paper’s arguments.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The core idea is promising, but the execution needs substantial improvements in terms of clarity, detail, evaluation, and writing quality. Addressing the comments above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential for acceptance.
