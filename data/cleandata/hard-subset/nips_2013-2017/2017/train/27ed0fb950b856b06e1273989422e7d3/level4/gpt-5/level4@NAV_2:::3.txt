This paper introduces a deep dynamic Poisson factorization framework to capture long-range temporal dependencies in sequential count data. Unlike prior dynamic PF approaches, it models temporal transitions via an RNN-style implicit distribution and uses a hierarchical (deep) structure to encode longer-term dependencies. Inference is performed via variational methods, with neural network parameters learned through a tailored optimization objective derived from the variational distributions. The technical contribution is solid, but several factual and writing issues remain, detailed below.

1. The assertion about modeling nonnegative numbers (lines 86–87) is incorrect. The appropriate distribution for this purpose is the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution.

2. Please highlight the best-performing results in Table 2 in bold for easier comparison.

3. For Section 4.2, held-out perplexity would be a stronger evaluation metric for text-like count data; MSE/PMSE are acceptable but less standard for this setting.

4. While the paper convincingly addresses the limitations of earlier dynamic PF models (e.g., refs 1, 2) in handling long-range dependencies, an ablation comparing against a dynamic PF with a single-level (shallow) architecture would better justify the benefits of the proposed hierarchical depth. The LSTM baseline is informative, but differences in learning frameworks make it less conclusive for validating the need for multiple layers in the proposed model.
