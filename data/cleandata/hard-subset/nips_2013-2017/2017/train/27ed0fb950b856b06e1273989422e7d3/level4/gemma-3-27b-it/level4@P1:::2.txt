The authors propose a novel method to capture complex long-term dependencies in sequential count data by exploiting recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions, parameterized by Gamma distributions. Specifically, the shape parameters of these Gamma distributions, which govern the temporal dependencies, are modeled using neural networks that integrate information from both prior (higher layers) and likelihood (lower layers). The neural network parameters are updated based on the expectations of latent variables, reducing variance and improving computational efficiency. The model presents an interesting and insightful approach to addressing the long-dependency problem in dynamic models. However, the manuscript feels somewhat rushed, and I have several comments below.

1. There are several typographical errors, such as in Equation 10.
2. The authors fix the scale parameter and only update the shape parameter in the Gamma distributions. This choice requires more detailed discussion and justification. Why was this simplification made, and what is its impact on model performance and expressiveness?
3. The experiments only evaluate the model with two layers. A more comprehensive analysis would include comparisons to models with a single layer and with more layers, to better understand the influence of network depth on performance. Further sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters, such as window size and the number of factors, is also needed.
4. For enhanced clarity, I recommend including a detailed graphical illustration of the entire model. The authors could draw inspiration from Figure 1 in [1] to effectively visualize the model structure and information flow.
5. Several recent deep Poisson factor analysis models have been proposed, including [2] and [3], which should be discussed in the Introduction to provide a more complete context for the presented work.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model builds upon Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and incorporates recurrent neural networks to capture short-term dependencies between factors and a deep structure to model long-term dependencies. Variational inference is used for inference, and network parameters are learned via gradient descent on a loss function derived from the variational distribution. The model is evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating good predictive and fitting performance with interpretable latent structures.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The combination of PFA, recurrent neural networks, and a deep hierarchical structure is a novel approach to modeling dynamic count data.
*   **Addressing Long-Term Dependencies:** The model explicitly addresses the challenge of capturing long-term dependencies, a weakness of previous PFA-based models.
*   **Variational Inference:** The use of variational inference provides a computationally efficient alternative to MCMC methods.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the potential effectiveness of the proposed model.
*   **Interpretability:** The authors highlight the interpretability of the latent structure learned by the model.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Typographical Errors:** The presence of typos detracts from the professionalism of the manuscript.
*   **Scale Parameter Fixation:** The decision to fix the scale parameter of the Gamma distributions without detailed justification is a limitation.
*   **Limited Experimental Analysis:** The experimental evaluation is incomplete, lacking a comprehensive exploration of the impact of network depth and hyperparameter settings.
*   **Clarity of Model Illustration:** The current description of the model could benefit from a clear and detailed graphical representation.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The Introduction omits recent relevant work in deep Poisson factor analysis.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The model presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to modeling sequential count data.
*   The experimental results are promising, demonstrating the effectiveness of the model on a variety of datasets.
*   The authors address a significant challenge in the field – capturing long-term dependencies – in a creative and well-motivated way.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The manuscript contains several minor flaws, such as typographical errors and a lack of thorough experimental analysis.
*   The justification for certain design choices, such as fixing the scale parameter, is lacking.
*   The omission of recent related work weakens the paper’s contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of dynamic count data modeling. However, the manuscript requires some revisions to address the identified weaknesses. I recommend **conditional acceptance**, contingent upon the authors addressing the reviewer comments, particularly regarding the justification for the scale parameter fixation, a more thorough experimental analysis, the inclusion of recent related work, and careful proofreading.
