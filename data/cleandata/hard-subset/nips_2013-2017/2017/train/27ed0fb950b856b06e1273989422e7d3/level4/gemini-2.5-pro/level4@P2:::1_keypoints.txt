- Introduces DDPFA: a novel generative Poisson factorization model using deep recurrent networks to capture long-range temporal dependencies in count data.
- Uses a two-stage inference: coordinate-ascent variational updates for latent variables and gradient-based optimization for neural network parameters.
- Empirically evaluated on five real-world datasets and shown to generally outperform PFA, PGDS, and LSTM baselines on MSE/PMSE.
- Core contribution is original and significant: integrating deep recurrent architecture within Bayesian factorization for sequential counts.
- Correctly identifies and attempts to overcome limitations of first-order Markov assumptions in models like PGDS.
- Initial experimental results are promising, suggesting improved temporal modeling over baselines.
- Model specification is incomplete: distributional forms (e.g., mapping RNN outputs to Gamma shape/rate) are not fully defined.
- The proposed RNN architecture is nonstandard and under-justified (layer depends on above layer but not its own previous state).
- Terminology misuse: calling closed-form conditionals "implicit distributions" is confusing and inaccurate.
- Inference objective is unclear and under-derived: the "Probability AutoEncoder" section and the L2 objective lack rigorous justification and derivation.
- Evaluation metrics (MSE/PMSE) are inappropriate for probabilistic count models; predictive log-likelihood on held-out data should be reported.
- Hyperparameter choices (small K values) are questionable; a wider K sweep (e.g., K=50,100) is needed to validate results.
- Qualitative analysis and visualizations (Figure 3) are not clearly explained or linked to the claims about long-term factor activation.
- Writing quality is poor with grammatical errors, typos, and illegible figure captions, which hinder comprehension.
- Presentation/organization could be improved by moving standard variational update equations to an appendix to streamline the main text.
- Conclusion: promising and novel idea but manuscript is not ready for publication without major revisions on clarity, inference rigor, experiments, and writing.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, significant problem, and promising empirical gains.
- Arguments for rejection: insufficient clarity/rigor in model and inference, weak experimental evaluation, and low writing quality.
