Summary
The paper proposes a dynamic count model that captures long-range temporal dependencies by using recurrent neural networks to parameterize the shape parameters of gamma-distributed latent variables. The higher-layer prior and lower-layer likelihood information are fused through the RNN to produce time-varying shape parameters, while the scale parameters are fixed. The authors update neural-network parameters using expectations of latent variables, which reduces gradient variance and improves computational efficiency. The approach is an interesting and thoughtful way to marry deep sequential modeling with count distributions, addressing the challenge of long-term dependencies in count data.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Using an RNN to parameterize the shape of gamma-distributed latent variables is a compelling idea for modeling dynamic counts with long-term dependencies.
- Variance reduction: Updating based on expected latent variables instead of raw samples is a practical design choice that likely improves stability and speed.
- Modeling choice: Gamma-Poisson structures can capture over-dispersion and align well with count data; the framework appears flexible enough to extend to deeper hierarchies.

Weaknesses and Suggestions
1) Typographical and clarity issues
- There are critical typos (e.g., Equation 10). Please thoroughly proofread and verify all equations, indices, and parameterizations (shape/scale vs. shape/rate).
- Clearly define notation early (e.g., whether you use “scale” or “rate,” and how priors/likelihoods are combined in the RNN outputs).

2) Fixed scale parameter
- The choice to fix the gamma scale and only update the shape needs a rigorous justification. Please discuss:
  - Identifiability: Are shape and scale not jointly identifiable under your likelihood or hierarchical structure?
  - Stability: Does fixing the scale improve training stability or reduce pathological solutions?
  - Expressiveness: What is lost by fixing scale? Could a learnable or time-varying scale improve fit or capture varying dispersion?
  - Sensitivity: Provide an ablation comparing fixed vs. learned scale and, if possible, a hybrid where scale is shared across time but learned per-layer.
  - Prior structure: If the fixed scale acts as an implicit prior, specify how it is chosen and whether it is tuned per dataset.

3) Experimental depth and ablations
- Depth: Evaluate 1-, 2-, and 3+-layer variants to understand how depth affects performance, stability, and computation.
- Window size and factors: Provide sensitivity analyses on the temporal window size, number of factors/topics, and hidden state dimensionality.
- Baselines: Compare against strong baselines, including:
  - Deep Poisson factorization and gamma belief networks [2], deep LDA with adaptive Riemannian MCMC [3].
  - Recurrent latent variable models for sequences with count likelihoods (e.g., VRNN-style models [1]) and non-recurrent baselines with temporal smoothing.
- Metrics and tasks: Report predictive log-likelihood/perplexity on held-out data, forecasting performance at multiple horizons, and qualitative analyses (e.g., factor interpretability).
- Efficiency: Include wall-clock comparisons and memory usage by sequence length and depth; discuss training stability (e.g., gradient norms, early stopping).

4) Model illustration and description
- Provide a clear graphical depiction of the full model, including layer-wise structure, temporal plates, and how prior (from higher layers) and likelihood (from lower layers) influence the RNN outputs. A figure in the style of [1] would greatly aid comprehension.
- Elaborate the update equations: how expectations of latent variables are computed (closed form vs. approximations), how they feed into the RNN, and how gradients are propagated. If the procedure is EM-like, clarify the E-step and M-step analogues and whether gradients remain unbiased.
- Explain how the model prevents issues like posterior collapse or degenerate solutions, and how positivity constraints are enforced when mapping RNN outputs to gamma parameters.

5) Relation to prior work
- Expand the related work to include recent deep Poisson/gamma-based models and hierarchical topic models:
  - Augmentable Gamma Belief Networks [2].
  - Deep Latent Dirichlet Allocation with topic-layer-adaptive SGR-MCMC [3].
  - Recurrent latent variable models for sequential data [1].
- Clarify the conceptual and algorithmic differences: how your use of RNNs for gamma shape parameters compares to the hierarchical propagation in [2] and the adaptive inference in [3], and why your approach is better suited to long-range temporal dependencies.

6) Additional considerations
- Negative binomial interpretation: Since a Gamma-Poisson compounding yields a negative binomial, discuss the implied dispersion modeling and whether your design can adapt dispersion over time and layers.
- Robustness: Comment on handling zero-inflation or very sparse counts, if relevant to your datasets.
- Reproducibility: Provide training details (optimizers, batch sizes, learning rates, initialization), early stopping criteria, and code if possible.

Minor comments
- Fix the typo in Equation 10 and double-check all numbering.
- Ensure consistent notation for indices (time t, layer l) and parameters (shape k, scale θ vs. rate β).
- Clarify the datasets used, preprocessing steps, and any data-specific hyperparameter choices.
- Improve writing for clarity and flow; several sentences read as rushed.
- Include a brief discussion of limitations and future directions (e.g., extending to learnable scales or alternative count likelihoods).

Overall assessment
The paper introduces a promising framework that leverages RNNs to parameterize gamma-distributed latent variables for dynamic count modeling. The idea is timely and well-motivated, and the emphasis on variance-reduced updates is practical. However, the paper would benefit from stronger empirical validation (depth and sensitivity ablations, broader baselines), a clearer exposition (model diagram and equations), a justification of the fixed-scale choice with ablations, and a more comprehensive related work section including [1–3]. With these improvements, the contribution would be significantly strengthened.
