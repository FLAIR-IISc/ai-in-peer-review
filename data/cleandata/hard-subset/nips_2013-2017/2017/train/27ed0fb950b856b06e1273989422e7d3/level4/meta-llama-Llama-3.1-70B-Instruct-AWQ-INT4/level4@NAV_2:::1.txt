This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, a novel approach that extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) to capture temporal dependencies in sequentially observed count data. The proposed model leverages a recurrent neural network structure to represent implicit distributions and learn complicated relationships between different factors over time. The authors employ variational inference for parameter estimation and neural network training.

The paper presents a thorough review of related work, highlighting the limitations of existing models in capturing long-time dependencies. The authors argue that their approach addresses these shortcomings by incorporating a deep structure to model complex temporal relationships. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model in comparison to existing methods, including Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), and PFA.

The strengths of the paper include its novel approach to modeling temporal dependencies in count data, its thorough evaluation on multiple datasets, and its ability to capture complex relationships between factors. However, there are several areas that require improvement.

Firstly, the formulation of the recurrent neural network structure in Eq. 4 does not conform to a standard RNN setup, as it only depends on top-layer memory vectors and not on previous time steps for the same layer. The authors should clarify the reasoning behind this simplified structure.

Secondly, some model equations are missing or unclear, such as the parameterization of the Gamma distribution in Eq. 4 and the distribution that generates theta_t in Eq. 3. The authors should provide a more detailed and transparent explanation of their model.

Thirdly, the discussion of "implicit distributions" in lines 110-118 is misleading, as Eqs. 9 and 10 are explicit Gamma distributions with given shapes and rates. The authors should revise this section to accurately reflect the nature of their model.

Lastly, the writing quality and clarity of the paper require significant improvement. There are numerous typos, incomplete sentences, and missing articles throughout the text. The authors should thoroughly proofread their work to ensure that it meets the standards of a top-tier conference like NIPS.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to modeling temporal dependencies in count data.
* The experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model in comparison to existing methods.
* The paper has the potential to contribute to the advancement of Poisson Factor Analysis and related fields.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper requires significant improvements in terms of clarity, writing quality, and transparency of the model.
* The formulation of the recurrent neural network structure is unclear and may not conform to standard RNN setups.
* The paper lacks a thorough evaluation of the proposed model's performance using metrics such as predictive log-likelihood.
