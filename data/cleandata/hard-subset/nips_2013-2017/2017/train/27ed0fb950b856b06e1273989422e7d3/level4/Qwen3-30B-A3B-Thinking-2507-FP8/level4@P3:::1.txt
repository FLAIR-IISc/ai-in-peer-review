

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count data. The authors build upon Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and extend it by incorporating deep neural networks to capture both short-term complex relationships and long-term temporal dependencies. The work relates to recent NIPS contributions on dynamic Bayesian models, including the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS) [2] and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9, 10], but introduces a novel neural network-based approach to model temporal dynamics rather than relying on Gamma-Poisson transition assumptions. The paper also draws connections to deep learning approaches like Deep Exponential Families [12] and Deep Gaussian Processes [6, 7].

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a novel approach to modeling temporal dependencies in count data, which represents a meaningful extension to existing dynamic factorization methods. The authors provide a clear motivation for their approach, highlighting limitations in previous work regarding long-term dependencies. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates the model's effectiveness across diverse applications including text analysis (NIPS corpus), disaster data (International Disaster), and epidemic tracking (EBOLA). The proposed inference method using variational inference combined with neural networks for parameter learning is technically sound, and the results in Table 2 show consistent improvements over baseline methods (PGDS, PFA, LSTM) on most datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Clarity:** The model formulation contains significant ambiguities. Equation (4) claims to represent a recurrent neural network structure, but the dependencies are not consistent with standard RNNs, as h^(n)_t depends only on the top-layer memory vectors rather than previous time steps for the same layer. The paper fails to clarify the parameterization of the Gamma distributions in Equations (3) and (4), and the "implicit distributions" referenced in the text (Lines 110-118) are actually explicit Gamma distributions with closed-form parameterizations (Equations 9-10).

2. **Experimental Design:** The choice of K=3 for many datasets (Lines 207-208) appears unrealistically small for meaningful factorization, particularly for high-dimensional datasets like NIPS (V=14,036). The authors should justify this choice or demonstrate performance with larger K values (e.g., K=100) as suggested by prior work [9, 10].

3. **Evaluation Metrics:** The paper relies solely on MSE and PMSE for evaluation, which are inappropriate metrics for Poisson factorization models. Predictive log-likelihood would provide a more appropriate assessment of model performance for count data.

4. **Writing Quality:** The paper contains numerous grammatical errors, missing articles, and inconsistent terminology (e.g., "factorize" instead of "factorization," "property of the" without a following noun). The abstract uses excessive passive voice, and several figures have unreadable captions (e.g., Figure 4). The claim about "fanatical data" (Lines 30-31) is unclear and requires elaboration.

5. **Critical Analysis:** The paper does not adequately address limitations of the approach. While Table 3 shows diminishing returns with additional layers, the authors do not connect this to known issues in hierarchical models [25] or provide a thorough analysis of the "pruning" phenomenon they observe.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

*For acceptance:* The paper addresses a meaningful problem in time series analysis of count data with a novel neural network-based approach that extends existing work. The model demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines on diverse real-world datasets, and the technical approach is well-motivated.

*Against acceptance:* The paper contains significant technical ambiguities that would make it difficult for readers to reproduce or build upon the work. The experimental design lacks appropriate metrics and justification for key parameter choices. The writing quality is poor with numerous grammatical errors and unclear explanations that undermine the paper's scientific contribution.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the core idea has potential, the paper requires substantial revision to address the technical ambiguities, improve experimental design, and correct writing errors. The current version does not meet the quality standards expected for NIPS.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Clarify the model structure to align with standard RNN terminology, or explicitly justify the simplified structure.
2. Provide complete parameterization details for all distributions, including the Gamma parameters in Equations (3) and (4).
3. Replace MSE/PMSE with predictive log-likelihood for evaluation.
4. Justify the choice of K values or run experiments with larger K (e.g., K=100).
5. Correct grammatical errors, improve writing quality, and clarify ambiguous statements (e.g., "fanatical data").
6. Expand the discussion of limitations, particularly regarding the "pruning" phenomenon and diminishing returns with additional layers.
7. Consider moving the variational inference update equations (Eqs. 11-14) to the supplement to improve readability.

The paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field of dynamic factor analysis, but it currently suffers from insufficient technical clarity and poor writing quality that would prevent it from being a strong NIPS submission. With significant revisions addressing the points above, the paper could become a compelling contribution.
