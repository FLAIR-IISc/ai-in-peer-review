- Paper proposes deep dynamic Poisson factorization (DDPFA), combining Poisson Factor Analysis with a simplified recurrent neural network to model temporal dependencies.  
- Inference uses variational inference with an extra maximization step over neural network parameters; experiments on five real-world datasets are reported.  
- The RNN formulation (Eq. 4) is nonstandard and simplified — hidden states depend only on top-layer memory vectors, not previous timesteps for the same layer; reason for this simplification is unclear.  
- Several model equations are missing or under-specified (e.g., Gamma parameterization in Eq. 4 and the distribution generating θ_t in Eq. 3), hurting clarity.  
- The paper’s claim about “implicit distributions” is incorrect: Eqs. 9 and 10 are explicit Gamma distributions and can be computed in closed form conditional on h^{(0)}.  
- An unclear sentence about limitations on “fanatical data and disaster data” needs context or elaboration.  
- The subsection around lines 133–155 is confusing, particularly the explanation and derivation of the loss function in Eq. 15.  
- MSE and PMSE are inadequate evaluation metrics for PFA-like models; predictive log-likelihood should be reported.  
- The latent dimensionality K used in experiments is too small; authors should evaluate with larger K (e.g., K ≥ 100).  
- The discussion in lines 224–232 is not clearly connected to Figure 3 and needs clarification of how text relates to the figure.  
- Detailed variational update equations (Eqs. 11–14) are not central and could be moved to the supplement.  
- Figure 4’s caption is hard to read and should be revised for clarity.  
- The abstract and paper overuse passive voice; follow general writing advice to improve clarity.  
- Writing quality is poor with many typos, missing articles, and awkward phrases; numerous line-specific examples are provided.  
- Strength: introduces a novel model combining PFA with an RNN-style component to capture temporal dependencies.  
- Strength: evaluates the model on multiple real-world datasets, offering empirical assessment.  
- Weakness: poor writing and many typos reduce readability and professionalism.  
- Weakness: missing model details and unclear loss/derivation hinder reproducibility and understanding.  
- Weakness: incorrect discussion of implicit vs. explicit distributions indicates conceptual mistakes.  
- Weakness: experimental settings (small K, limited metrics) are insufficient to fully assess model performance.  
- Acceptance argument: novel model idea and comprehensive dataset evaluation make it potentially interesting.  
- Rejection argument: current execution — writing quality, missing equations, incorrect claims, and weak experiments — warrants rejection until substantial revision.
