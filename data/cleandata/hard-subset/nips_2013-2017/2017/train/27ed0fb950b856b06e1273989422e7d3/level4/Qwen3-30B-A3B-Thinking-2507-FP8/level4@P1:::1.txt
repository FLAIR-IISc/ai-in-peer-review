

This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), extending Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with a deep recurrent structure to model temporal dependencies in count data. Building on prior work like PGDS [2] (which uses Gamma transitions) and Deep Exponential Families [8], DDPFA employs neural networks to capture short-term relationships through latent implicit distributions and leverages deep latent layers for long-term dependencies. Inference combines variational methods for latent variables with neural network parameter optimization via a novel loss function. The authors evaluate DDPFA on four synthetic datasets and five real-world applications (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population), comparing against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA baselines.

**Strengths**  
The paper addresses a meaningful gap in modeling complex temporal dependencies in high-dimensional count data (e.g., topic evolution, event sequences). The core innovation—using deep latent structures with RNNs to model both short-term and long-term patterns—aligns with recent trends in deep Bayesian models [8, 9]. Empirical results demonstrate clear advantages: DDPFA outperforms PGDS and PFA in fitting/prediction across most datasets (Table 2), particularly excelling in capturing multi-factor activations over time (Fig. 2), where PGDS fails to model concurrent factors (e.g., Israel-Palestinian conflict and six-party talks in ICEWS). The interpretability of learned factors (Fig. 3) is a significant strength, showing how latent structures align with domain knowledge. The method’s scalability to large datasets (e.g., NIPS corpus, V=14,036) and use of variational inference for efficient inference are well-justified.

**Weaknesses**  
*Technical execution and clarity*:  
- The model formulation (Eq. 4) does not conform to standard RNN structure. The recurrence for $h^{(n)}_t$ depends only on the top-layer memory ($h^{(n+1)}$) and ignores time-step dependencies within the same layer (e.g., $h^{(n)}_{t-1}$), contradicting typical RNN design. The authors should clarify why this simplified structure is used.  
- The claim that Eqs. 9–10 represent "implicit distributions" is misleading. These are explicit Gamma distributions with closed-form shapes/rates (e.g., $\theta_{tk} \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_{\theta_{tk}} + x_{v\cdot k}, \beta_\theta + \lambda_k \sum_v \psi_v \phi_{vk})$), not distributions requiring approximation. This mischaracterization undermines technical rigor.  
- Critical model details are missing: Gamma parameters in Eq. 4 are undefined, and the transition mechanism for $\theta_t$ (Eq. 3) lacks specification (e.g., how $\alpha_{\theta_{tk}}$ is derived from $h^{(0)}_{t-c:t}$).  
- The term "fanatical data" (lines 30–31) is unclear and likely a typo; the authors should clarify the intended reference (e.g., "fanatical" → "fanatical patterns" or "extreme event patterns").  

*Evaluation and presentation*:  
- MSE/PMSE is inappropriate for count data models; predictive log-likelihood (standard for generative models like PFA) should replace or supplement these metrics.  
- The small $K=3$ for high-dimensional datasets (e.g., ICEWS, $V=6197$) is questionable. Using $K=100$ (as in [9]) would better assess scalability.  
- Section 3.3 (lines 133–155) lacks clarity: Eq. 15’s loss function is not well-motivated, and the connection between Eq. 17 and the "ladder architecture" (Table 3) is under-explained.  
- Typos and grammatical errors persist (e.g., "factorize" → "factorization," missing articles like "the" before "Gamma distribution"), reducing readability. The abstract’s passive voice ("is proposed") should be revised to active ("we propose").  
- The critique of "pruning" (Table 3) is valid but lacks concrete solutions; the authors note it stems from mean-field VI but do not propose alternatives (e.g., MCMC).  

**Recommendation**  
The paper presents a promising approach with strong empirical results and clear interpretability, addressing a relevant problem in sequential count analysis. However, critical technical gaps in model formulation, evaluation, and presentation prevent it from meeting NIPS standards. The RNN structure (Eq. 4), "implicit distribution" misstatement, and MSE metric choice are particularly problematic. These issues must be resolved to validate the core claim that deep structures improve long-term dependency modeling.  

*Pro*: Novel architecture for temporal count data, strong empirical performance on diverse datasets, clear latent factor interpretation.  
*Con*: Inconsistent model description, inappropriate evaluation metrics, unaddressed technical flaws (RNN structure, Gamma parameterization), and poor writing quality.  

This work has potential but requires substantial revision to clarify technical contributions and strengthen empirical validation. **Weak rejection** pending major revisions.
