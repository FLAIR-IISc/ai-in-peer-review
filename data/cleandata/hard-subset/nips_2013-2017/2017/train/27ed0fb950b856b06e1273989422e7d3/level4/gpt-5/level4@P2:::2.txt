Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for modeling sequential count vectors. Building on Poisson factorization, the model introduces a deep hierarchical latent structure over time and parameterizes temporal dependencies via recurrent neural networks (RNNs) that output parameters of Gamma distributions. Specifically, the Poisson rates are factorized using Φ, Θ, and feature- and component-specific shrinkage variables Ψ and Λ. Temporal dependencies in Θ are captured through a multi-layer set of latent variables h(n)t, where both bottom-up (posterior) and top-down (prior) “implicit” conditional distributions are represented by RNNs that produce Gamma shape parameters. Inference alternates mean-field variational updates for conjugate parts (via auxiliary latent counts) with gradient-based learning of RNN parameters using a “probability autoencoder”-style loss derived from expectations under the variational distributions. Experiments on synthetic data and real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA to PGDS, PFA, and LSTM; the model generally shows improved fit and prediction, with interpretable factors.

The work connects to dynamic count factor models (e.g., Poisson/Gamma chains and PGDS), deep latent variable models for counts (deep exponential families; deep Poisson factorization with RBM/SBN), and RNN-based latent variable models for sequences. It is also related to recent deep count models such as Augmentable Gamma Belief Networks and deep LDA with SG-Riemannian MCMC.

Strengths:
- Tackles long-range temporal dependence in count data—an important and challenging setting—by marrying conjugate Poisson–Gamma structure with RNN-parameterized priors/posteriors.
- Exploits conjugacy and auxiliary-count augmentation for efficient mean-field updates, reducing variance relative to sampling-based schemes.
- The hierarchical temporal structure and ψ/λ shrinkage variables aim to balance sparsity, interpretability, and flexibility.
- Empirical results show strong performance on diverse datasets, with qualitative factor analyses suggesting meaningful temporal patterns.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:
- Clarity and presentation: The manuscript reads rushed. There are typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., Equation 10), and several definitions are difficult to parse on first read. A clear, complete graphical illustration (e.g., akin to Fig. 1 in [1]) would substantially improve readability.
- Parameterization choice: The RNNs appear to output only Gamma shape parameters while rate/scale parameters are fixed or shared. This design choice needs justification and empirical analysis. How does fixing the rate affect expressiveness, identifiability, and stability? Please include sensitivity analyses (e.g., learning both shape and rate, or different constraints).
- Inference objective: The “probability autoencoder” loss (Equations 15–18) is only loosely connected to a principled ELBO. The derivation relies on matching expectations and L2 reconstructions, which introduces bias and lacks guarantees. Please clarify the relationship to variational objectives (e.g., whether the loss lower-bounds a tractable ELBO, or is an auxiliary consistency objective), discuss convergence, and justify why reparameterization/score-function estimators were not used (beyond sampling cost). Also specify how positivity constraints on Gamma parameters are enforced in the RNN outputs.
- Ablations and model size: While the paper reports strong results, the influence of architectural choices is underexplored. A more systematic ablation is needed:
  - Depth: Compare single-layer vs multi-layer variants and quantify gains from each additional layer.
  - Window size c, number of factors K, and hidden sizes: Analyze their impact on fit/prediction and runtime.
  - Prior/posterior networks: Compare prior-only vs bidirectional (prior+posterior) RNNs.
- Baselines and related work: Recent deep models for count data (e.g., Augmentable Gamma Belief Networks [2], deep LDA with topic-layer-adaptive SGR-MCMC [3]) and RNN-based temporal PF variants deserve discussion and, where possible, empirical comparison. Cite and position against recurrent latent-variable models for sequences (e.g., [1]) and other deep count models.
- Evaluation metrics: MSE/PMSE are informative but not standard for count models. Please report held-out predictive log-likelihood or perplexity (and calibration metrics) to better assess probabilistic quality. Include runtime and memory comparisons.
- Scope and practical details: The Bernoulli–Poisson and compound-Poisson links are mentioned but not evaluated; either include experiments or move to an appendix. Clarify initialization, optimization settings, and computational complexity with respect to V, T, K, depth, and window size.

Quality:
- The modeling idea is sensible and technically plausible, combining conjugate updates with RNN-parameterized Gamma priors/posteriors. However, the loss function and training procedure need a clearer theoretical footing, and some choices (fixed rate) require analysis. The experimental section is promising but would benefit from stronger ablations and additional metrics.

Clarity:
- The paper would benefit from careful editing, consistent notation, a full generative story, and a comprehensive graphical model. Several equations (e.g., 9–10, 15–18) should be rewritten more clearly with precise conditioning and parameter definitions.

Originality:
- The combination of deep temporal Gamma-chain structure with RNN-parameterized implicit distributions within Poisson factorization is novel relative to PGDS and classical deep PF. The approach overlaps conceptually with variational autoencoders and deep exponential families but is distinct in using conjugacy and auxiliary counts.

Significance:
- If clarified and better validated, the method could be useful for a range of sequential count applications (text, events, recommender data). The empirical gains are encouraging, and the interpretability claims are appealing.

Specific suggestions:
1) Fix typographical errors and clean up notation (especially Eq. 10 and surrounding definitions). Add a graphical plate model and a clear generative process summary.
2) Justify and test the choice to fix Gamma rate parameters; consider learning them and report sensitivity.
3) Strengthen the inference section: connect the loss to an ELBO (or explicitly state it is an auxiliary objective), discuss bias, and describe constraints for positivity.
4) Add ablations on depth, window size c, K, and network architecture; include training time and memory.
5) Report predictive log-likelihood/perplexity in addition to MSE/PMSE; consider calibration plots.
6) Expand related work to include [1–3] and other recent deep count models; add comparisons where feasible.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem (long-range temporal dependence in counts).
- Clever integration of conjugate structure with RNNs.
- Strong empirical results and compelling qualitative factors.

Cons against acceptance:
- Clarity and exposition issues; some equations incorrect/ambiguous.
- Inference objective insufficiently justified; theoretical grounding needs work.
- Limited ablations and missing baselines/metrics.
