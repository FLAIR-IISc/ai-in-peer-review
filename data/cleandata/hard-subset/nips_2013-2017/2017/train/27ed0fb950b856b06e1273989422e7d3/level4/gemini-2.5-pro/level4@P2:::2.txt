This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a novel hierarchical model for analyzing sequential count data, such as document word counts over time or event data. The model builds upon the Poisson Factorization Analysis (PFA) framework. Its primary contribution is a deep, dynamic structure designed to capture complex, long-range temporal dependencies. This is achieved by introducing layers of latent variables where the temporal transitions are governed by implicit distributions. These distributions are cleverly modeled as Gamma distributions whose shape parameters are generated by Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). This approach contrasts with prior work like the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), which typically assumes a first-order Markov structure and may struggle with long-term patterns. Inference is performed using a two-stage hybrid approach, combining mean-field variational inference for the latent variables with gradient-based optimization for the RNN parameters.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance):**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of integrating RNNs into a hierarchical Bayesian model to parameterize the priors of dynamic latent variables is both novel and compelling. It provides a flexible and powerful way to move beyond the limitations of simple Markovian assumptions that are common in dynamic topic models and related factorization methods. This directly addresses a significant challenge in time-series modeling: capturing complex, long-range dependencies.

2.  **Model Design and Potential:** The proposed deep structure is well-motivated. By stacking layers of latent variables, the model has the potential to learn temporal patterns at different scales of abstraction, which is a powerful concept. The use of a "Probabilistic Autoencoder" structure for training the RNNs, which balances information from higher (prior) and lower (posterior) layers, is an interesting and pragmatic design choice.

3.  **Inference Scheme:** The two-stage inference procedure, which updates neural network parameters based on the expectations of the latent variables, is a sensible approach. It avoids the high variance that can arise from sampling-based updates in similar deep generative models and is likely more computationally efficient and stable.

**Weaknesses (Reasons for Rejection & Suggestions for Improvement):**

The paper presents a promising direction, but the manuscript in its current form has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. It appears to have been prepared in a rush, which detracts from the quality of the contribution.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is difficult to follow, which hinders a full appreciation and verification of the proposed method.
    *   **Lack of Graphical Model:** The model's structure is complex, involving multiple layers, temporal dependencies, and a mix of Bayesian and neural network components. A detailed graphical model illustration is essential for understanding the conditional dependencies between all variables (X, θ, h, Φ, etc.). The authors could refer to Figure 1 in Chung et al. (NIPS 2015) for an example of how to clearly illustrate a similar recurrent latent variable model.
    *   **Confusing Equations and Notation:** Some key equations are unclear or potentially incorrect. For instance, in Equation 10, `h(0)tk |Θ,h(1), β,X ∼ Gamma(αh(0)tk + γh(0)tk , βh)`, the rate parameter `βh` appears to be a fixed hyperparameter. This is a strong modeling assumption that implies the variance of the hidden states is fixed. This needs to be explicitly stated and justified. Why is the rate parameter not also learned or dependent on other variables, as it is for θ in Equation 9? This lack of detail makes the model specification ambiguous.
    *   **Inference Description:** The description of the "Probability Autoencoder" and the loss function in Equation 16 is terse and inadequately justified. The motivation for minimizing the L2 norm between expectations is not clearly connected to a standard variational objective. A more principled derivation or a clearer explanation of this objective function is needed.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while showing promising results, do not provide sufficient evidence to support the central claims about the benefits of the "deep" structure.
    *   **Ablation Studies:** The paper claims that a deep structure captures long-time dependence better than shallow models. To convincingly support this, the authors must compare their 2-layer DDPFA not only against external baselines but also against a 1-layer version of their own model. This crucial ablation would isolate the benefit of adding depth. While Table 3 compares 2-layer and 3-layer models, the lack of a 1-layer baseline is a major omission.
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The model has several important hyperparameters, such as the window size `c`, the number of factors `K`, and the architecture of the RNNs. The paper provides no analysis of how the model's performance varies with these choices. A sensitivity analysis is needed to understand the model's robustness and provide guidance for practitioners.

3.  **Missing Related Work:** The introduction and related work section overlook several highly relevant and recent papers on deep generative models for count data. This positions the work inadequately within the current literature. The authors should discuss and compare their approach with:
    *   **Augmentable Gamma Belief Networks (AGBN)** by Zhou et al. (JMLR 2016), which is another deep generative model based on Gamma-Poisson conjugacy.
    *   **Deep Latent Dirichlet Allocation (DeepLDA)** by Cong et al. (ICML 2017), which also explores deep hierarchical structures for topic modeling, albeit with different inference techniques.
    *   The authors also cite their own reference [23] (Hoffman, ICML 2017) as "Deep Latent Dirichlet Allocation uses stochastic gradient MCMC", but the actual title is "Learning Deep Latent Gaussian Models with Markov Chain Monte Carlo". This should be corrected, and the connection to their work should be clarified.

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a compelling and original model for dynamic count data analysis with strong potential. The integration of RNNs to shape the priors in a deep Poisson factorization model is a significant and interesting research direction. However, the current manuscript is not yet ready for publication. The acceptance of this paper should be conditional on a major revision that thoroughly addresses the significant issues in clarity, experimental rigor, and its positioning with respect to recent literature. If the authors can provide a clearer model description (with a graphical model), justify their modeling choices, and conduct more comprehensive experiments (especially ablation studies), this could be a strong contribution to the field.
