This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for sequential count vectors. Building on Poisson Factorization, the model introduces a deep, recurrent structure over time: the latent factor weights θt are given Gamma priors whose shape parameters are functions of a hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t, with temporal dependencies modeled by recurrent neural networks (RNNs). Inference proceeds in two stages: (i) mean-field variational inference (VI) with standard Poisson–Gamma conjugacy and multinomial data augmentation for the factorization, where the RNNs provide time-varying shape parameters; and (ii) training the RNN parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that matches expectations between encoder and decoder distributions and reconstructs X via the expected factors. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, Ebola, International Disaster, and Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA, showing improved fit and prediction in most cases and interpretable temporal factors.

Relation to prior work: The model is positioned between dynamic Poisson factor models such as PGDS and deep latent variable models (Deep Exponential Families, deep Poisson FA, Gamma Belief Networks), and is related to sequential VAEs with RNN latent dynamics (e.g., Chung et al., NIPS 2015). The claimed novelty is to couple Poisson factorization with a deep, RNN-defined hierarchy of Gamma priors to capture long-range temporal dependencies, and to train the neural components using an expectation-based, reconstruction-driven objective.

Strengths
- Addresses an important problem: modeling long-term dependencies in high-dimensional, sparse count time series.
- Combines interpretable count models (Poisson–Gamma) with flexible temporal priors via RNNs.
- Inference avoids high-variance gradient estimators by using expectations under Gamma variational distributions, potentially improving efficiency.
- Empirical results are promising: better MSE/PMSE than PFA and LSTM on several datasets; interpretable factors in ICEWS.
- The discussion of “pruning” and the limited benefits of added depth is candid and useful.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
1) Typos and clarity: The manuscript contains numerous typos and unclear statements that hinder understanding (e.g., Eq. 10 and surrounding text). The graphical model and computational flow are not sufficiently clear.

2) Shape-only updates: The Gamma distributions’ rate (or scale) parameters appear fixed while only the shape parameters are learned via RNNs. This design choice needs justification and analysis. How sensitive is performance to fixing rates? Could learning rates improve expressivity or stability? Please discuss identifiability, scaling ambiguities with λk and ψv, and the effect on posterior variance.

3) Inference objective: The “Probability AutoEncoder” loss (Eqs. 15–18) departs from standard ELBO-based training. The derivation is informal, and the role of auxiliary H′ variables and expectation matching is not rigorously justified. Please clarify:
- How this objective relates to a lower bound on the marginal likelihood or a well-defined divergence.
- Why differentiable variational Bayes or reparameterized gradients for Gamma variables are unsuitable here (beyond sampling cost).
- Whether the layer-wise training plus fine-tuning optimizes a coherent objective and how convergence is monitored.

4) Ablations and hyperparameters: While the paper mentions experiments with different sizes and a ladder architecture, a clearer and more comprehensive ablation is needed:
- Single-layer vs two-layer vs deeper DDPFA, with matched parameter counts.
- Effect of the window size c and number of factors K on fit and forecast.
- Effect of including/excluding λk and ψv (since they can be absorbed into Θ) on sparsity and performance.
- Sensitivity to the fixed rate/scale parameters and priors.
Please include training time and memory comparisons.

5) Baselines and related work: The introduction and comparisons should better situate recent deep Poisson factorization models and temporal count models. In particular, discuss Gamma Belief Networks (Zhou et al., JMLR 2016) and deep LDA with SG-Riemannian MCMC (Cong et al., ICML 2017), and sequential latent variable models (Chung et al., NIPS 2015). Consider including additional temporal count baselines where appropriate (e.g., recurrent Poisson factorization).

6) Reproducibility details: Please provide precise architectural specifications (RNN type, hidden sizes per layer, activation functions), optimization settings (learning rates, batch sizes, regularization), and stopping criteria. Clarify how missing counts or zeros are handled in updates, and how multinomial augmentation is implemented for large V,T.

Specific comments
1. Critical typos (e.g., Eq. 10) and several places with inconsistent notation (shape vs rate/scale) should be corrected.
2. Provide a detailed graphical illustration of the full hierarchical-temporal model (nodes for Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ, h(n), and RNN dependencies), analogous to figure-style presentations in PGDS.
3. Expand the discussion of the “ladder” variant and its impact (referencing Zhao et al., 2017), including quantitative gains and when deeper layers help.
4. In ICEWS forecasting, PGDS performs best; please analyze why DDPFA underperforms (e.g., overfitting, window length, horizon mismatch) and whether simple smoothing or regularization helps.
5. Provide credible intervals or uncertainty measures for forecasts, given the probabilistic nature of the model.

Evaluation
- Quality: The modeling idea is sound and the Poisson–Gamma conjugacy is used correctly; however, the training objective for the neural components needs a clearer derivation and justification. Empirical results are encouraging but require stronger ablations.
- Clarity: The paper is difficult to follow in places due to typos, notation issues, and missing figures. Substantial editing is needed.
- Originality: Combining deep RNN-defined Gamma priors with Poisson factorization is novel relative to PGDS and existing deep PF; the training approach is unconventional.
- Significance: Potentially significant for sequential count modeling in text, recommendations, and events; clearer evidence and analysis would strengthen the case.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem with a creative integration of deep temporal priors and count modeling.
- Strong empirical performance on several datasets with interpretable factors.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity and correctness issues (typos, notation, missing details).
- Insufficient theoretical justification for the training objective.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses; unclear benefits of depth across settings.

Overall, I find the idea promising, but I recommend revision to address clarity, provide a rigorous treatment of the learning objective, and include comprehensive ablations and discussions of design choices (especially the fixed rate parameters and depth). References
