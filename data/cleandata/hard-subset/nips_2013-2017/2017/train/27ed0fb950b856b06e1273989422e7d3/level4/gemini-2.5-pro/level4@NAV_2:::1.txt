This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a new model for analyzing sequential count data. The model extends Poisson Factorization (PFA) by incorporating a deep structure, implemented with recurrent neural networks, to capture complex and long-range temporal dependencies among the latent factors. This contrasts with previous dynamic PFA models that often assume Markovian dependencies. Inference is performed using a mean-field variational approach, where the neural network parameters are updated based on a reconstruction loss. The model's performance is evaluated on several synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing it against PFA, LSTM, and the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS).

The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of modeling dynamic count matrices. The core idea of leveraging deep neural networks to learn flexible, long-range temporal transitions within a PFA framework is novel and has the potential to be a significant contribution. However, the current manuscript has several major issues in its technical presentation, experimental evaluation, and overall clarity that prevent a recommendation for acceptance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution is the novel integration of a deep, neurally-parameterized dynamic structure into the Poisson factorization framework. This is a promising approach for moving beyond the simple Markovian assumptions of prior work (e.g., PGDS) and capturing more complex temporal patterns.
*   **Problem Relevance:** The paper tackles a well-motivated problem with broad applicability in areas like recommender systems, text analysis, and event data modeling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Clarity and Technical Soundness:**
    *   The model's generative process is not fully specified. For example, the distribution `p(θt|·)` in Eq. 3 is not defined, and it is unclear how the Gamma distribution in Eq. 4 is parameterized by the outputs of the neural network.
    *   The claimed RNN structure is questionable. The formulation in Eq. 4 suggests that `h_t^(n)` depends on `h^(n+1)` at surrounding time steps but not on `h_(t-1)^(n)`. This is not a standard recurrent structure, and the motivation for this specific architectural choice is not explained.
    *   The use of the term "implicit distributions" seems incorrect. The conditional posteriors for `θ_tk` and `h_tk` (Eqs. 9 and 10) are presented as standard Gamma distributions with defined parameters, making them explicit, not implicit.
    *   The inference procedure, particularly the "Probability AutoEncoder" section, is confusing. The objective in Eq. 15 is not well-defined, and the subsequent derivation of the loss functions in Eqs. 16-18 is difficult to follow and lacks sufficient justification.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The evaluation metrics (MSE and PMSE) are inappropriate for evaluating probabilistic models on count data. A proper assessment of model quality requires standard metrics like predictive log-likelihood or perplexity.
    *   The experimental setup may not be sufficient to demonstrate the model's full capabilities. The number of latent factors used in the experiments (e.g., K=10 for the NIPS dataset) seems very small. The authors should explore larger values of K (e.g., K=50 or K=100) to ensure the model's capacity is not a limiting factor.
    *   The discussion of the experimental results is not always convincing. For instance, the connection between the text in lines 224-232 and the qualitative results shown in Figure 3 is not clear.

*   **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The manuscript's writing quality is poor and requires substantial revision. There are numerous grammatical errors, typos (e.g., "factorize", "nested"), incomplete sentences, and missing articles, which severely impede comprehension.
    *   The overall organization could be improved. The variational update equations (Eqs. 11-14) are standard and could be moved to an appendix to improve the readability of the main text.
    *   Some figures and text are unclear. The caption for Figure 4 is illegible. The sentence in lines 30-31 regarding "fanatical data and disaster data" is vague and needs clarification. Using a more active voice, especially in the abstract, is also recommended.
