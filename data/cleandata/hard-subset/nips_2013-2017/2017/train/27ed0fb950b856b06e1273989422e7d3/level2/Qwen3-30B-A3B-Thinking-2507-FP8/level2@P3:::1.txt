

The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count data that extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) using deep recurrent neural networks to capture complex temporal dependencies. The core innovation lies in modeling the transition of latent factor scores θ_t through multiple layers of implicit distributions, where each layer's hidden states h^(n)_t are parameterized by RNNs. The inference employs variational methods with coordinate ascent for latent variables and a novel loss function for neural network training, framed as a probability autoencoder.

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with thorough experimental validation across four synthetic datasets and five real-world applications (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, ASP). The model demonstrates consistent improvements over PFA and PGDS in fitting and prediction tasks (Table 2), particularly on high-dimensional data (NIPS, EBOLA) where LSTM struggles. The variational inference derivation (Section 3) is theoretically grounded, leveraging conjugate priors for efficient updates. However, the ablation study (Table 3) reveals a critical weakness: adding layers beyond two yields diminishing returns, contradicting the claimed advantage of "deep" structure. The observed "pruning" phenomenon—where latent dimensions capture noise—suggests mean-field VI may be inadequate for hierarchical latent structures, as noted in related work (e.g., [24]). While the paper acknowledges this limitation, it lacks a concrete solution beyond "trying other inference methods," weakening the technical contribution.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with consistent notation and logical flow from problem statement to experiments. The mathematical formulations (Equations 1–18) are precise, and Figures 1–3 effectively illustrate the model architecture and interpretable factor activations (e.g., Figure 2 showing sustained Israel-Palestinian conflict patterns). The experimental setup is detailed, including hyperparameter choices and baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA). Minor improvements could be made to clarify the relationship between the ladder-like architecture and prior hierarchical models (e.g., [25]), as the paper cites [25] but does not explicitly contrast DDPFA’s approach.

**Originality**: The work extends existing dynamic Poisson models (e.g., PGDS [2], Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9, 10]) by introducing a deep latent structure for long-term dependencies. However, the novelty is partially obscured by insufficient differentiation from Gan et al. (2015) [9], which also employs deep structures for Poisson factorization via RBMs. The paper should clarify how DDPFA’s RNN-based implicit distributions differ from [9]’s RBM/SBN approach and why the deep structure is necessary for temporal dependence. While the probability autoencoder loss (Equation 18) is a reasonable adaptation of variational autoencoders, it is not sufficiently novel to warrant significant emphasis.

**Significance**: The model addresses a meaningful gap in sequential count data analysis, where shallow models (PGDS) fail to capture complex temporal patterns (e.g., EBOLA, ASP datasets). The interpretability of learned factors (Figure 3) and strong performance on diverse real-world tasks demonstrate practical relevance. The findings suggest that deep structures can enhance Poisson-based models beyond recurrent networks alone, advancing the state of the art in dynamic factorization. However, the marginal gains on ICEWS (Table 2) and the layer-pruning issue limit immediate applicability.

**Recommendation**: The paper presents a technically sound, well-executed model with clear empirical advantages over baselines in key scenarios. While the "deep" aspect is overstated due to diminishing returns (Table 3), the core contribution—using neural networks to model implicit temporal dependencies in Poisson factorization—remains valuable. The significance is strengthened by the real-world validation, though the originality is slightly reduced by overlap with [9]. The weaknesses (pruning, layer utility) are reasonable to address in revision, but not critical enough to reject the paper. Accept with minor revisions to clarify novelty vs. [9] and explicitly discuss the layer-pruning limitation.
