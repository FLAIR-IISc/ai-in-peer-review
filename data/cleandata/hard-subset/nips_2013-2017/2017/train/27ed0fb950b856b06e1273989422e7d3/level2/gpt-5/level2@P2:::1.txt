Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count vectors that extends Poisson factor analysis (PFA) with a deep hierarchical sequence of Gamma latent variables h(n)t whose conditional distributions across time are parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The observed counts xvt are modeled via a Poisson likelihood with per-feature and per-factor scale variables (ψv, λk), with θtk providing time-varying factor scores. Time dependence is modeled by conditioning θt on a sliding window of h(0)t−c:t, and higher layers by conditioning h(n)t on windows of h(n+1). The authors use a two-stage inference scheme: (i) mean-field coordinate ascent updates exploiting Gamma–Poisson conjugacy, treating the RNN outputs (e.g., shape parameters α·) as fixed; and (ii) training the RNN parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that penalizes squared deviations between expectations of encoder/decoder conditionals and between reconstructions and data. Experiments on synthetic and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, Ebola, International Disasters, Sheep population) compare DDPFA to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA using MSE/PMSE.

The work relates to dynamic count factorization with Poisson/Gamma constructions (e.g., PGDS), deep count models (deep exponential families; deep PFA with RBM/SBN; gamma belief networks), and neural autoregressive topic models. A closely related line is recurrent/dynamic Poisson factorization for recommendation and temporal topic models. The paper aims to capture both short-term and long-range dependencies via deep recurrent priors.

Strengths
- Ambitious attempt to combine conjugate Poisson–Gamma factor models with deep, recurrent dependencies, targeting long-range temporal structure.
- Uses explicit per-feature and per-factor scales (ψ, λ) to encourage sparsity and control factor importance.
- Empirical results suggest improvements over shallow PFA and PGDS on several datasets, with some qualitative interpretability (e.g., ICEWS factors).
- The paper candidly discusses training pathologies (“pruning,” limited benefit from added layers) and proposes a ladder-style variant to mitigate them.

Weaknesses (major)
- Inference objective is not theoretically grounded. The “Probability AutoEncoder” replaces ELBO maximization with ad hoc squared-loss matching between expectations of conditional distributions (Eqs. 15–18). No derivation shows this is a lower bound to the marginal likelihood or that optimizing it improves the variational objective used in the E-step. As a result, the overall alternating scheme is not optimizing a coherent objective, and convergence/consistency are unclear.
- Ambiguity in the probabilistic model. While the paper refers to “implicit distributions” parameterized by RNNs, the updates assume Gamma conditionals with shape terms α(·) produced by RNNs. This makes the priors explicit (Gamma) but data/latent-dependent via neural nets. The conditioning structure, the roles of α vs. γ in h(n) posteriors, and how rates are handled are insufficiently specified to reproduce or verify conjugacy. Equations (7–10, 13–14) contain inconsistencies (e.g., duplicated φ, missing ψ, mismatched indices).
- Evaluation metrics are misaligned with count models. Using MSE/PMSE to evaluate count data under Poisson likelihood is not standard; predictive log-likelihood or held-out perplexity would be more appropriate and allow fair comparisons to PGDS and deep count/topic models.
- Baselines and fairness. LSTM is not a calibrated probabilistic baseline for count prediction and is disadvantaged on high-dimensional sparse counts. More relevant baselines (e.g., dynamic topic models, recurrent Poisson factorization, neural autoregressive count models) are missing. Hyperparameter tuning appears uneven (e.g., fixed PGDS iterations, limited details on DDPFA architecture and tuning).
- Limited scalability evidence and ablations. Experiments use small K (≤10) and moderate sizes; no runtime or memory comparisons are reported. Ablations on the roles of Λ, Ψ, window size c, and depth N are limited; Table 3 is discussed but not fully shown.
- Clarity and presentation. The manuscript has numerous grammatical issues, undefined notation, and incomplete/malformed citations, which impede understanding and verification.

Quality
The modeling idea is interesting, but the theoretical underpinnings of the inference are weak. The paper does not present a clear ELBO for the full model nor gradients with respect to network parameters; the proposed squared-loss training objective lacks justification. Several equations contain errors/typos. Empirical evaluation, while suggestive, does not use proper scoring rules and omits key baselines.

Clarity
The paper is difficult to follow. The conditioning structure across layers and time should be formally specified; the exact parameterization (which parameters of the Gamma are output by which RNNs) must be stated; and the mean-field family q(·) needs precise definitions. Figures/tables are referenced but not fully included; references are incomplete.

Originality
Combining a deep hierarchical Gamma prior with RNN-parameterized dependencies in a Poisson factorization framework is a novel combination. Related dynamic count models and deep count models exist; the paper would benefit from positioning against recurrent PF for recommendation and dynamic topic models more thoroughly.

Significance
Potentially useful if made principled and scalable, but the current form lacks a sound inference objective and rigorous evaluation, limiting impact.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide a coherent variational objective. Derive the ELBO for the full model, including the RNN-parameterized priors, and optimize it (e.g., via variational EM with gradients through expectations; black-box VI with control variates; or reparameterization/approximation for Gamma). If using surrogate losses, prove they bound/improve the ELBO.
- Clarify the model: exact forms of p(θt|h(0)t−c:t) and p(h(n)t|·), which Gamma parameters are functions of RNN outputs, how rates are handled, and the role of γ terms. Fix Eq. (7) and related notation.
- Evaluate with predictive log-likelihood on held-out counts; report calibration (e.g., PIT, dispersion). On NIPS, compare to dynamic topic models; on recommendation-like data, to recurrent PF; on ICEWS, to PGDS with learned transitions and to dynamic Poisson processes.
- Detail architectures (RNN type, hidden sizes, depth), optimization (learning rates, schedules), and provide code for reproducibility. Report runtime and sensitivity to K, c, depth.
- Justify inclusion of Λ and Ψ with ablations; report their inferred posteriors and effect on sparsity.
- Strengthen qualitative analysis: show top features per factor over time (e.g., top words for NIPS), not just factor time series.
- Improve writing and complete citations (authors, venues, years). Compare to closely related works more carefully.

Minor comments
- Numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., “natrually,” “strcuture,” “fanatical data”). Proofread thoroughly.
- Specify whether the RNNs are LSTM/GRU/vanilla and whether the “window” conditioning uses bidirectional context (note that using future context may be inappropriate for forecasting).
- Clearly state whether future information (t+1:t+c) is used at training vs. prediction time.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem (dynamic modeling of sparse counts).
- Conceptually interesting blend of deep recurrent structure with conjugate count models.
- Some empirical gains and interpretability claims.

Cons for acceptance
- Inference not principled; unclear objective and convergence.
- Ambiguous model specification; errors in equations.
- Evaluation metrics and baselines are inadequate.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues.

Overall recommendation
I lean toward rejection in the current form due to the lack of a sound training objective and clarity issues, but the direction is promising; a revised version with a principled ELBO-based inference, clearer modeling, and stronger evaluation could be impactful.
