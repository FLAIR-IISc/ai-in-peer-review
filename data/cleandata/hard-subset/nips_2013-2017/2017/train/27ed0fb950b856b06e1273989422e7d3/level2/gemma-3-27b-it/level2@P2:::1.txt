This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors. The core idea is to combine Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with recurrent neural networks to capture both short- and long-term dependencies in the data. The authors use variational inference for parameter estimation and demonstrate the model’s effectiveness on synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing it to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of existing work in Bayesian factor analysis and deep learning for sequential data. The motivation for combining PFA with RNNs is well-explained, highlighting the limitations of previous approaches in capturing complex temporal dependencies. The authors correctly cite relevant work in both areas, including models like PGDS [2], Deep Gaussian Processes [6, 7], and Neural Autoregressive Density Estimators [11].  The inclusion of related work on deep Poisson factorization [9, 10] is also appropriate. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of PFA with deep recurrent neural networks represents a novel approach to modeling sequential count data.  While deep learning has been applied to similar problems (e.g., [12]), the specific integration with the PFA framework appears to be a new contribution.
* **Technical Soundness:**  The model formulation appears mathematically sound, building logically from PFA and incorporating RNNs in a principled way. The use of variational inference is also a reasonable approach given the complexity of the model.  The derivations in Section 2 and 3 are presented reasonably clearly, though more detailed explanation of the variational inference steps would be beneficial (see weaknesses).
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the potential of the DDPFA model. The comparison with baseline methods like PGDS, LSTM, and PFA provides a good benchmark for evaluating performance. The visualization of factors in Figure 2 and 3 is helpful for understanding the model's behavior.
* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem of analyzing sequential count data and motivates the need for a model that can capture both short- and long-term dependencies.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Variational Inference Details:** The description of the variational inference procedure in Section 3 is somewhat high-level. Key steps, such as the derivation of the variational distributions and the optimization procedure, are not fully explained.  More details regarding the coordinate-ascent method and the loss function optimization would be helpful.  The connection between equations (7-18) isn't always immediately clear without a deeper understanding of variational inference.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a thorough analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity. While the authors specify the hyperparameters used, they do not discuss how the performance of the model changes with different settings.  This is important because the choice of hyperparameters can significantly impact the results, particularly in deep learning models.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The authors mention "pruning" and the limitations of MF-VI, and acknowledge issues with adding layers. However, there is limited discussion of the computational complexity of the model and its scalability to very large datasets. Given the use of RNNs and variational inference, scalability could be a significant concern.
* **ICEWS Prediction Performance:** The authors note that the DDPFA model does not perform as well as PGDS on the ICEWS dataset for the prediction task. This discrepancy deserves further investigation.  It would be helpful to understand why PGDS outperforms DDPFA in this specific case and whether any modifications to the model could improve its performance.
* **Clarity of Notation:** Some of the notation is somewhat dense and could be simplified.  For example, the use of multiple subscripts and superscripts can make it difficult to follow the equations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The model presents a novel and potentially valuable approach to analyzing sequential count data.
* The experimental results demonstrate promising performance on a variety of datasets.
* The paper is technically sound and builds upon a strong foundation of existing work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The description of the variational inference procedure is insufficient.
* The analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity is lacking.
* The discussion of scalability is limited.
* The poor performance on the ICEWS prediction task raises concerns.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of sequential count data analysis. The DDPFA model is a novel and potentially powerful approach, and the experimental results are encouraging. However, the lack of detail in the variational inference explanation, limited hyperparameter analysis, and scalability concerns prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the weaknesses related to variational inference details and hyperparameter sensitivity. A more thorough explanation of the ICEWS results would also be beneficial.
