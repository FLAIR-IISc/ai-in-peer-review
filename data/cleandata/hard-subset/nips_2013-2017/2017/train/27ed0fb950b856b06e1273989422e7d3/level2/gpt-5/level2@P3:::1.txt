Summary
The paper introduces Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for modeling sequential count vectors. Building on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA), the work augments the temporal evolution of factor scores with a deep hierarchy of latent variables linked across time windows via recurrent neural networks. The temporal prior p(θt | h(0)t−c:t) and a multi-layer hierarchy p(h(n)t | h(n+1)t−c:t) are parameterized by RNNs that output Gamma parameters, aiming to capture short-range nonlinear dependencies and longer-range structure. Inference proceeds in two stages: (i) a mean-field variational inference (MFVI) scheme for the Gamma-Poisson core using standard auxiliary latent counts, alternating with (ii) training of the RNN parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that matches conditional expectations and reconstruction errors, inspired by (but not identical to) VAE/ladder architectures. Experiments on synthetic data and several real-world datasets (ICEWS events, NIPS corpus, Ebola, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA, reporting improvements in MSE/PMSE and offering qualitative interpretations of factors.

Relation to prior work
DDPFA is closely related to several lines: Poisson factorization and Gamma/Negative-Binomial process models for counts (e.g., Gopalan et al., UAI 2015; Zhou et al., NIPS 2012; JMLR 2016), dynamic Poisson/Gamma state-space models including PGDS (e.g., Schein et al., 2016), deep latent variable models for counts (Deep Exponential Families; Deep/Hierarchical PFA; Gamma Belief Networks), and recurrent/temporal Poisson factorization (e.g., Recurrent Poisson Factorization for Temporal Recommendation, 2017). The use of RNNs to parameterize Gamma conditionals for temporal dynamics and the ladder-like bidirectional links across layers resembles amortized inference and ladder VAEs, adapted to conjugate PF structure.

Strengths
- Interesting architectural idea: combining conjugate Poisson-Gamma factorization with RNN-parameterized Gamma conditionals to capture complex temporal dependencies, with a multilayer hierarchy for longer horizons.
- The inference scheme leverages standard PF conjugacy for stable updates, while using neural nets for structured priors/posteriors, potentially offering a pragmatic balance between flexibility and tractability.
- Empirical coverage across multiple domains with both predictive and fit metrics, and qualitative factor interpretations that speak to model interpretability.
- Discussion of practical issues (e.g., pruning, depth usefulness, ladder connections) shows thoughtful engagement with known challenges in hierarchical variational models.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Objective derivation is unclear. The “Probability AutoEncoder” losses (Eqs. 15–18) are not presented as a bound on the marginal likelihood or ELBO. The switch from KL/objective in Eq. 15 to squared-error matching of expectations in Eq. 16 is ad-hoc and lacks theoretical justification. This weakens guarantees about convergence and comparability. Essential: provide a precise objective for network parameters, ideally a single ELBO jointly over all variables with amortized/implicit conditionals, or clearly justify the surrogate loss (e.g., show it is a bound or provide empirical evidence that optimizing it improves held-out log-likelihood).
- Variational family and updates need more rigor. The model uses Gamma conditionals whose shapes are outputs of RNNs; clarify whether these enter the ELBO and how gradients are computed. If sampling-based gradients are avoided, discuss bias introduced by using expectations, and provide convergence diagnostics. Consider standard alternatives: reparameterization for Gamma (via approximation), score-function estimators with control variates, or conjugate-computation variational inference for conditionally conjugate blocks.
- Evaluation metrics rely on MSE/PMSE on counts; for count models, predictive log-likelihood or per-token perplexity (for text) is more standard and more comparable to prior work. Essential: report held-out predictive log-likelihoods; for text, per-word perplexity; for ICEWS, predictive event likelihood.
- Baselines and ablations could be strengthened. Consider dynamic topic models and recurrent PF baselines beyond LSTM and PGDS (e.g., variants with learned transition matrices, recurrent PF). Provide ablations for window size c, depth N, and with/without λ, ψ scales to quantify each design choice.
- Computational aspects are under-specified. Provide training time, parameter counts, and scaling behavior versus V, T, K. Discuss stability and sensitivity (e.g., to priors, learning rates).

Clarity
- The presentation suffers from notation overload and several typographical/ indexing errors (e.g., Eq. 7 normalization, repeated symbols; missing indices/rates; some inconsistent uses of φ/ψ/λ). Essential: carefully edit equations and define all symbols once; include a compact generative process and dependency diagram with clear indexing.
- The term “implicit distribution” is ambiguous; clarify whether these are explicit Gamma conditionals with parameters produced by nets, or implicit nonparametric distributions.
- Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues) and some relevant prior work is not cited or not precisely identified (e.g., PGDS citation). Essential: complete and correct the bibliography with standard venues/years.

Originality
- The overall combination is a meaningful extension, though adjacent to known ideas (deep PF, ladder/amortized inference, recurrent PF). The novelty lies in the specific hierarchical RNN-Gamma parameterization and the proposed training heuristic. To emphasize contribution, include comparisons to the closest neural dynamic PF/topic models and clearly articulate differences.

Significance
- The reported empirical gains suggest potential utility, but the lack of log-likelihood-based evaluation, limited ablations, and unclear optimization objective reduce confidence. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the objective would significantly improve impact.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses an important and challenging setting: sequential high-dimensional count data with long-range dependencies.
- Conceptually appealing architecture marrying conjugate PF with deep/recurrent priors; potentially interpretable and flexible.
- Broad empirical evaluation with qualitative interpretability.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- The training objective for neural components is insufficiently justified; absence of a clear ELBO or bound undermines theoretical soundness.
- Evaluation metrics are nonstandard for count models; stronger likelihood-based comparisons are needed.
- Presentation issues (notation, typos, incomplete references) and missing computational details hinder reproducibility.
- Limited analysis of when depth helps and how hyperparameters affect performance.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential for camera-ready:
- Provide a unified, principled objective (ELBO or a provable surrogate) for joint learning, or justify the surrogate with theory or thorough empirical validation (including predictive log-likelihood improvements).
- Report held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity alongside MSE, and expand ablations (depth, window size, with/without λ/ψ).
- Clean up notation, equations, and complete references. Add a succinct generative process block and clarify “implicit distribution.”

Nice-to-have:
- Add stronger baselines (dynamic topic models; recurrent PF variations).
- Report runtime and scaling; release code.
- Explore alternative inference variants to mitigate pruning (e.g., auxiliary variables, richer variational families).

Overall, the idea is promising and relevant, but the current form requires a clearer learning objective and stronger empirical validation to meet NeurIPS standards.
