- Proposes DDPFA: extends Poisson Factor Analysis with deep neural nets to model sequential count vectors, claiming short- and long-term dependency capture.
- Strength: addresses an important problem with clear motivation.
- Strength: comprehensive experiments on synthetic datasets (SDS1-4) and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, ASP).
- Strength: DDPFA outperforms PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA on most datasets, especially for complex temporal patterns.
- Strength: learned latent factors are interpretable; DDPFA shows multiple active factors over time (Figures 2–3) versus PGDS’s single-factor activation.
- Weakness: unclear novelty and insufficient distinction from prior deep Poisson factorization models (e.g., RBM/SBN-based approaches).
- Weakness: ladder-like architecture and addressed “pruning” issue seem ad-hoc rather than a principled novel contribution.
- Weakness: model limitations (pruning, adding layers not helping) are identified but no convincing solution is provided.
- Weakness: inference method is technically complex with dense, inconsistent notation, impairing clarity and reproducibility.
- Weakness: variational inference explanation (eqs. 11–14) is insufficient and loss function (eq. 18) looks like a heuristic without justification.
- Weakness: experimental comparisons are limited to three baselines and omit computational efficiency/training time comparisons.
- Weakness: no statistical significance testing reported for performance differences.
- Quality: technically reasonable but clarity suffers from inconsistent notation and inadequate inference details.
- Quality: experiments presented well but lack analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity and model robustness.
- Originality: contribution seems incremental; does not convincingly advance beyond existing deep Poisson factorization literature.
- Recommendation: reject—needs clearer novelty differentiation or stronger analysis and comparisons to justify the deep structure’s benefits.
