Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for modeling sequential count vectors. Building on Poisson factorization X ~ Poisson(ΦΘ^T) with additional feature-wise and factor-wise scalars (ψv, λk), the central novelty is a deep, time-dependent prior over the factor scores Θ. Specifically, Θ depends on a hierarchy of latent variables h(n)_t, with local temporal context via a window [t−c, …, t], and both “prior” and “auxiliary posterior” conditionals parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The latent variables are modeled with Gamma distributions whose shape parameters are outputs of these RNNs. Inference alternates between mean-field variational updates for Gamma-distributed latent variables (using standard Gamma–Poisson conjugacy conditional on neural network outputs) and training the neural networks with an autoencoder-like squared-error objective that encourages consistency between “prior” and “auxiliary posterior” predictions and reconstruction of the data via expected rates. Experiments on synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets (ICEWS events, NIPS word counts, Ebola cases, disaster counts, sheep population) compare DDPFA to PGDS (Poisson–Gamma Dynamical System), PFA, and LSTM; DDPFA is reported to perform best in most settings, with some discussion of interpretability and the effects of depth.

Relation to prior work: The paper is related to Poisson factorization and negative binomial process models (Zhou et al.), dynamic count models such as PGDS, deep factorization models for counts (Deep Exponential Families; deep Poisson factor analysis), and recurrent/intensity-based models for temporal recommendation (e.g., recurrent Poisson factorization). It also draws conceptual inspiration from autoencoders/VAEs and ladder-style architectures for bidirectional information flow.

Strengths
- Targets an important setting (temporal count data) where both long-range and local dependencies are relevant.
- Attempts to marry Poisson factorization with deep temporal structure via RNN-parameterized Gamma conditionals, which is a promising direction beyond linear-Gaussian or shallow Gamma transitions.
- Provides a practical coordinate ascent scheme for the conjugate parts and a separate neural network training stage; experiments cover multiple domains and include both fitting and one-step prediction.
- The paper reflects on limitations (depth not always helping; “pruning” phenomenon) and discusses architectural choices (ladder connections), which is useful to readers.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Objective and inference: The proposed “Probability AutoEncoder” training objective is ad hoc and not shown to be an ELBO, a lower bound on predictive likelihood, or any principled surrogate. Using squared error between expectations for both latent consistency and data reconstruction is mismatched to the Poisson likelihood and undermines probabilistic coherence. It is unclear what quantity is optimized end-to-end or whether the procedure yields a consistent estimator.
- Derivations: Several conditional/posterior forms (e.g., Eq. 10 with Gamma(α + γ, β) for h(n)) are stated without derivation from a joint model. Introducing an “auxiliary posterior network” and then using its output as an additive shape parameter needs justification or a clear reference (e.g., auxiliary-variable VAEs, ladder VAEs). As written, it is not clear that a joint probabilistic model exists for which the stated updates are exact or variationally justified.
- Evaluation metrics: MSE/PMSE for counts do not reflect the Poisson model; predictive log-likelihood, Poisson deviance, or perplexity would be more appropriate. Without likelihood-based metrics and uncertainty-aware evaluation, it is hard to assess whether the model actually advances state of the art in probabilistic modeling.
- Baselines and tuning: The fairness of comparisons is unclear. LSTM settings are minimal for some datasets; PGDS is run for 1000 Gibbs iterations versus DDPFA’s 100 VI iterations plus SGD (but no wall-clock/runtime). Hyperparameters for all methods are not tuned via validation, and early stopping or regularization are not discussed. Please provide validation procedures, sensitivity analyses (e.g., window size c, K), and runtime/memory comparisons.
- Missing details: The architectures (RNN type, hidden sizes per layer, activations to ensure positive Gamma parameters), optimization settings, and initialization strategies are not sufficiently specified for reproduction. Code availability would help.
- References are incomplete and in places ambiguous; some relevant work on amortized VI for non-reparameterizable distributions (e.g., auxiliary variable VAEs, rejection/implicit reparameterization gradients, score-function with control variates) could be cited and discussed to better motivate the chosen training strategy.

Clarity
- The paper would benefit from tightened notation and clearer exposition of the generative model and inference. Terms like “implicit distribution,” “probability autoencoder,” and the roles of α and γ need precise definitions and derivations. Some typos and inconsistencies (e.g., indices, switching φ/ψ) make it harder to follow.
- The training schedule (order of updates, convergence criteria, whether VI and network parameters are jointly optimized or alternated, and how many passes) should be clearly stated.

Originality
- The combination of Poisson factorization with RNN-parameterized deep temporal priors is interesting, but related ideas have appeared (deep exponential families, deep Poisson factor analysis, recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation, auxiliary-variable VAEs/ladder VAEs). The main novelty seems to be the specific ladder-like Gamma hierarchy and the ad hoc “probability autoencoder” training; the latter is insufficiently principled to be a strong originality contribution.

Significance
- Empirically, the reported gains are promising in some datasets, but the lack of likelihood-based evaluation, limited hyperparameter tuning of baselines, and unclear objective weaken the claim of advancing the state of the art. The method’s broader impact is limited until the probabilistic foundations and training are made more rigorous.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a clear joint generative model and derive an ELBO that includes the neural network parameters (amortized VI). If exact reparameterization of Gamma is challenging, consider auxiliary-variable VAEs, rejection-based/pathwise gradients for Gamma, or score-function estimators with strong control variates; cite and compare to such alternatives.
- Replace MSE with appropriate likelihood-based objectives for counts during network training (e.g., negative log-Poisson or NB) and evaluate with predictive log-likelihood and Poisson deviance.
- Justify or remove Eq. 10–14 unless derived from a coherent model; if adopting ladder-style auxiliary inference, connect to established formulations and cite accordingly, explaining how α and γ arise.
- Expand experimental evaluation: strong baselines (e.g., deep exponential families on counts; recurrent Poisson factorization), tuned via validation; ablations on window size, number of layers, K; computational cost; and interpretability analyses of λ and ψ sparsity.
- Improve clarity: complete references, define all variables, and provide full architectural/optimization details; release code if possible.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem (temporal count modeling) with an interpretable factorization framework.
- Interesting attempt to integrate deep temporal structure via RNNs and a ladder hierarchy.
- Broad empirical coverage across domains; includes discussion of failure modes.

Cons
- Inference objective is not principled; squared-error loss mismatched to Poisson modeling.
- Several update formulas lack derivation; unclear if a coherent probabilistic model underlies the method.
- Evaluation metrics and baseline tuning are insufficient to substantiate claims.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues; incomplete references.

Overall, while the direction is promising, the current submission needs a sound variational objective, clearer derivations, and stronger empirical validation before it is ready for publication at NIPS.
