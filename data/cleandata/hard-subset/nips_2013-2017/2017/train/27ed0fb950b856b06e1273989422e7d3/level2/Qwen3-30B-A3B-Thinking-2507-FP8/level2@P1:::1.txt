

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors, which extends Poisson Factor Analysis with deep neural networks to capture both short-term complex relationships and long-term temporal dependencies. The authors build upon existing work like PGDS and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis, but introduce a novel deep latent structure with recurrent neural networks to model the implicit distributions of latent variables across time steps.

Strengths:
The paper makes a technically sound contribution by combining Poisson Factor Analysis with deep learning for temporal count data. The model's two-stage inference approach (mean-field variational inference followed by neural network parameter learning) is well-motivated and implemented consistently. The experiments are comprehensive, covering both synthetic datasets (SDS1-4) and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, ASP), with appropriate baselines (PGDS, LSTM, PFA). The results demonstrate that DDPFA outperforms baselines in most cases, particularly in capturing long-term patterns (e.g., in ICEWS and ASP datasets where PGDS fails). The interpretability of latent factors is a notable strength, with Figure 2 showing meaningful temporal patterns that PGDS misses.

Weaknesses:
The paper would benefit from more detailed comparison with closely related work. The authors reference "Deep Poisson Factor Modeling with a recurrent structure based on PFA" [12] but do not clearly distinguish how DDPFA differs from this work or why their deep latent structure approach is superior. The claim of "deep" modeling is somewhat misleading, as the model has only two layers (N=2), and the experiments in Table 3 show diminishing returns from adding more layers (suggesting the "deep" aspect may not be necessary). The paper also fails to adequately address the "pruning" issue mentioned in the discussion (where some latent dimensions contain only noise), which could undermine the model's practical utility.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear mathematical formulations. However, some sections could be improved for clarity. For instance, the description of the neural network structure in Section 3 (particularly equations 3-4 and the subsequent text) could be more intuitive. The authors might benefit from a more detailed explanation of why a deep structure is needed for long-term dependencies rather than a single recurrent layer. The experimental setup for LSTM (hidden layer size 40) should be justified, as this is unusually small for LSTM networks.

Originality:
The core idea of combining deep learning with Poisson Factor Analysis for temporal data is novel, and the authors properly position their work relative to prior art. However, the "deep" aspect of their model is limited in practice, as shown in Table 3. The model would be more original if the authors could demonstrate that the deep structure provides significant advantages over a single-layer recurrent approach, rather than just incremental improvements.

Significance:
The problem of analyzing sequential count data is important for applications like recommendation systems, text analysis, and event prediction. The DDPFA model addresses a gap in the literature for capturing long-term dependencies in such data, and the empirical results support its effectiveness for this purpose. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the small number of real-world datasets tested and the fact that the model's performance gains over baselines are not always substantial (e.g., the poor performance on ICEWS prediction).

Specific suggestions for improvement:
1. Clarify the distinction between DDPFA and closely related work, particularly [12], and justify the need for a deep structure beyond two layers.
2. Provide more detailed analysis of the "pruning" issue and how it might be addressed (beyond just mentioning it as a limitation).
3. Include ablation studies comparing the deep structure to a single-layer recurrent approach to demonstrate the necessity of depth.
4. Explain the choice of LSTM hidden layer size (40) and consider testing larger sizes to ensure fair comparison.
5. Consider adding a discussion of computational complexity compared to baselines, as this would be important for practical adoption.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a clear contribution to the field of sequential count data analysis. The model shows promising results, particularly in capturing long-term dependencies that other methods miss. The paper would be strengthened by better distinguishing the novelty from related work and addressing the limitations of the deep structure. The authors have made a valuable contribution that advances the state of the art in modeling temporal count data, though the "deep" aspect could be more compelling with additional analysis. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
