- Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA): extends PFA with deep RNN-parameterized latent transitions for sequential count data.  
- Core innovation: multi-layer implicit latent factor transitions via RNNs and a probability autoencoder-style loss.  
- Inference: variational methods with coordinate ascent and conjugate priors yield theoretically grounded, efficient updates.  
- Experimental validation: thorough tests on four synthetic and five real-world datasets with detailed setup and baselines.  
- Empirical performance: consistently improves over PFA and PGDS, especially on high-dimensional datasets (NIPS, EBOLA), where LSTM underperforms.  
- Ablation weakness: adding layers beyond two shows diminishing returns, contradicting the claimed deep benefit (Table 3).  
- Pruning issue: latent dimensions capture noise ("pruning"), suggesting mean-field VI may be inadequate for hierarchical latents.  
- Limited mitigation: paper acknowledges the inference limitation but offers no concrete alternative beyond suggesting other methods.  
- Clarity: well-structured, precise notation, clear equations and informative figures; experimental details transparent.  
- Minor clarity fix: should better contrast the ladder-like architecture with prior hierarchical models (e.g., [25]).  
- Originality: extends dynamic Poisson models, but novelty is partly obscured by overlap with Gan et al. (2015) [9]; needs clearer differentiation from RBM/SBN approaches.  
- Autoencoder loss: reasonable adaptation but not highly novel and should not be overemphasized.  
- Significance: practical and interpretable improvements for sequential count modeling, though gains are dataset-dependent and layer-pruning limits applicability.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—clarify novelty vs. [9] and explicitly discuss/address the layer-pruning/inference limitation.
