This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a novel method for analyzing sequential count data. The model extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating a deep, hierarchical structure to capture complex temporal dependencies. The main contribution is the use of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to parameterize the implicit conditional distributions of latent factors over time. This approach aims to model more flexible and non-linear dynamics compared to previous work like the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), which relies on simpler conjugate transitions.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work is its well-motivated and novel combination of a classic probabilistic model (PFA) with the expressive power of deep learning. Using RNNs to learn the transition dynamics is an elegant way to move beyond the Markovian assumptions and linear relationships of prior models. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both synthetic and diverse real-world datasets. The results compellingly demonstrate that DDPFA generally outperforms relevant baselines, including PGDS and LSTM, in both fitting and prediction tasks. A significant positive aspect is the authors' candid discussion of the model's limitations, such as the "pruning" effect and the diminishing returns of adding more layers, which reflects a mature scientific approach.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is a lack of clarity and rigorous justification for the inference procedure used to train the neural network parameters. The paper introduces a "Probability AutoEncoder" and proposes a loss function (Eq. 16-18) based on squared error. This shift from the variational inference framework and the evidence lower bound (ELBO) to a deterministic reconstruction loss feels disconnected and ad-hoc. The motivation for this specific loss function is not clearly derived from the overall probabilistic objective, which undermines the technical soundness of the inference method. Furthermore, the paper omits key architectural details of the RNNs used, which would hinder the reproducibility of the results.

In summary, the paper introduces a promising and powerful model for a significant problem. The core idea is strong and the empirical results are impressive. However, the justification for the inference algorithm is a notable flaw. I recommend acceptance on the condition that the authors significantly revise the inference section to provide a more principled derivation of their learning objective for the neural networks, clearly linking it back to the overall variational framework.
