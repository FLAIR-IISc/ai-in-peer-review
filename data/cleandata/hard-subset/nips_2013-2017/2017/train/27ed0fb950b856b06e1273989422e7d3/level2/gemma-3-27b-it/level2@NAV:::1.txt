## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors, aiming to improve upon existing Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) methods by capturing both short- and long-term dependencies via recurrent neural networks. The core idea of leveraging deep learning to model the temporal evolution of latent factors within a PFA framework is promising, particularly for applications dealing with high-dimensional, sparse sequential data. 

The paper clearly outlines the motivation, model formulation, and inference procedure. The inclusion of recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions and a deep structure to capture long-term dependencies is a logical extension of prior work like PGDS and deep PFA models (references [2, 9, 10]). The variational inference approach is well-described, and the inclusion of a loss function for training the neural networks is a crucial element.  The experimental setup includes both synthetic and real-world datasets, alongside comparison to established methods like PGDS and LSTM, which provides a solid evaluation ground. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its novel combination of PFA with deep learning techniques. The experimental results on synthetic data demonstrate the ability of DDPFA to capture complex temporal relationships. The application to real-world datasets, like the ICEWS and NIPS corpus, is also valuable, and the analysis of factor activations (Figures 2 and 3) provides some insight into model interpretability.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the model is presented with mathematical rigor, the explanations of the neural network components feel somewhat superficial. The ablation studies examining the impact of network depth (Table 3) are insightful but highlight the limitations of the variational inference approach, specifically the "pruning" and "uselessness of adding network layers" phenomena. The discussion of these limitations feels somewhat brief; a more thorough analysis of the reasons behind these issues and potential solutions (e.g., alternative inference methods like MCMC) would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, while the results are generally positive, the performance gains over strong baselines aren’t always substantial, and the reasons for the DDPFA’s underperformance on the ICEWS prediction task aren’t sufficiently explained. The clarity could also be improved with more concise writing and potentially more visual aids to illustrate the model architecture.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to modeling sequential count data. The experimental results demonstrate the potential of DDPFA to outperform existing methods on certain datasets.
**Arguments against acceptance:** The paper suffers from some limitations related to the explanation of neural network components, insufficient discussion of limitations, and moderate performance gains in some cases. 

**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. While there are areas for improvement, the core idea is promising, and the experimental results are encouraging. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the clarity of the neural network explanations, a deeper discussion of the limitations, and exploring potential solutions to the observed issues with variational inference.
