

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) to capture complex temporal dependencies in sequential count data, extending Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with deep recurrent neural networks to model implicit distributions across time steps. The authors claim that their hierarchical structure enables both short-term pattern learning (via local implicit distributions) and long-term dependence modeling (via deep latent layers), outperforming baselines like PGDS, LSTM, and PFA on synthetic and real-world datasets.

Strengths include a well-motivated problem statement linking to foundational work (e.g., PFA [2], PGDS [1]), comprehensive experiments across 4 synthetic and 5 real datasets (including ICEWS, NIPS corpus, and EBOLA), and clear demonstration of interpretability via factor activation patterns (Figure 2). The variational inference framework is technically sound, with detailed algorithmic descriptions (Section 3) and reasonable ablation studies (Table 3) showing that ladder architectures improve performance over naive deepening.

However, the work suffers from critical limitations. The claims of superiority lack statistical rigor: no significance tests are provided for performance differences (e.g., MSE/PMSE in Tables 1–2), and the LSTM baseline appears poorly configured (hidden size 4 vs. 40 in real data, inconsistent with standard practice). The novelty is incremental; the deep structure resembles prior Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9,10] and Deep Exponential Families [8], with insufficient differentiation from these works. The "ladder" architecture justification (Table 3) is weak, as it merely replicates known hierarchical VAE issues [24,25] without novel solutions. Additionally, the ICEWS prediction failure (Table 2) undermines the core claim of robust long-term dependence modeling.

The paper acknowledges limitations like "pruning" (Table 3) but offers no concrete remedies beyond vague references to MCMC. For NIPS, the contribution is modest—addressing a niche problem with a plausible but unvalidated extension of existing methods. The significance is limited by the absence of theoretical guarantees and the lack of real-world deployment evidence. While the model is technically complete, its practical impact remains unproven. The authors should strengthen the statistical validation, justify architectural choices against prior deep count models, and clarify the LSTM baseline. Given the incremental novelty and methodological gaps, this paper is borderline for acceptance.
