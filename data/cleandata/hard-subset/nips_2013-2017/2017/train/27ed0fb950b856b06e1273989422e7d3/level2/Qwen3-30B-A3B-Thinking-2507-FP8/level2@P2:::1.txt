

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors, extending Poisson Factor Analysis with deep neural networks to capture complex temporal dependencies. The authors claim their model captures both short-term complicated relationships through local implicit distributions and long-term dependencies through deep latent structures.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses an important problem in sequential count data analysis with clear motivation. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including both synthetic datasets (SDS1-4) and five real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, ASP). The results demonstrate that DDPFA outperforms PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA on most datasets, particularly in capturing complex temporal patterns. The interpretability of the learned latent factors is a notable strength, as shown in Figures 2 and 3 where DDPFA captures multiple active factors over time, unlike PGDS which only activates one factor per time step.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness lies in the unclear novelty and insufficient comparison with prior deep Poisson factorization models. While the introduction cites "Deep Poisson Factor Analysis based on RBM or SBN" [9,10], it fails to adequately distinguish DDPFA from these approaches. The "ladder-like architecture" mentioned to address the "pruning" issue appears to be an ad-hoc solution rather than a novel contribution. The paper also identifies problems with their model ("pruning" and uselessness of adding layers) but doesn't provide a convincing solution, merely noting these issues as problems with variational inference.

The inference method is technically complex but lacks sufficient clarity. The mathematical notation is dense and inconsistent (e.g., θtk vs. θtk in equations), and the explanation of the variational inference procedure (equations 11-14) is insufficient for reproducibility. The loss function in equation (18) appears to be a heuristic approximation rather than a principled derivation, but the paper doesn't justify why this approximation is valid.

The experimental comparison is limited to three baselines, and the paper doesn't provide computational efficiency comparisons. For instance, it's unclear how the training time of DDPFA compares to LSTM or PGDS, which is crucial for practical applications. The paper claims "excellent results" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the performance differences.

**Quality and Clarity:**
While the model is technically sound in its approach, the paper's clarity suffers from inconsistent notation and insufficient explanation of the inference algorithm. The experimental results are well-presented, but the paper doesn't provide enough detail about hyperparameter sensitivity or model robustness. The claim that "adding more layers or variables in upper layers doesn't improve performance" (Table 3) is problematic as it suggests the deep structure doesn't provide benefits, undermining the core motivation of the paper.

**Originality and Significance:**
The paper's contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. The core idea of using deep structures for temporal dependencies in count data is not new, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how DDPFA advances beyond existing deep Poisson factorization approaches. The significance is limited by the lack of clear comparison with the most relevant prior work on deep Poisson factorization (e.g., [9,10,12]).

**Recommendation:**
The paper addresses an important problem with promising empirical results but suffers from insufficient novelty and unclear contribution. The model's limitations (pruning, useless layers) are not adequately addressed, and the paper fails to clearly distinguish itself from prior deep Poisson factorization methods. While the experimental results are generally positive, the lack of sufficient methodological justification and comparison with the most relevant prior work weakens the paper's scientific contribution.

I recommend rejection, as the paper does not make a sufficiently novel contribution to the field. The authors should either significantly strengthen the novelty claim by better differentiating from prior work or provide more rigorous analysis of why their deep structure is beneficial despite the observed "uselessness of adding layers" issue. The paper would benefit from a more focused comparison with the most relevant prior deep Poisson factorization models and a clearer explanation of how their approach advances the state-of-the-art.
