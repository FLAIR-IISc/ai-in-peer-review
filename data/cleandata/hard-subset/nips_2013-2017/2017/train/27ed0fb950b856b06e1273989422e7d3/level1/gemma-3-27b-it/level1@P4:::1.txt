The paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors. The core idea is to extend Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating recurrent neural networks to model the temporal dependencies in the latent factor scores, thereby capturing both short-term and long-term relationships. Variational inference is used for model fitting, and the method is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating competitive performance against baseline models like PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the limitations of existing dynamic count models in capturing complex temporal dependencies. The combination of PFA with recurrent neural networks is a reasonable approach and aligns with the current trend of utilizing deep learning techniques for Bayesian modeling. The experimental evaluation, including comparisons with several baselines on diverse datasets, provides a comprehensive assessment of the proposed method. The inclusion of visualizations (Figures 2 & 3) attempting to interpret the learned factors adds value.

However, several weaknesses hinder the paper's overall impact.  Firstly, while the model is presented clearly, the mathematical derivations, particularly concerning the variational inference steps, lack sufficient detail. Equations (11-18) are presented without adequate explanation of how the expectations are calculated or why the specific loss function is chosen. The reader familiar with variational autoencoders would benefit from a more detailed connection to established derivations. The claim of using a "mean-field" approximation is standard, but the implications for tractability and potential limitations aren't thoroughly discussed. 

Secondly, the experiments, while extensive, suffer from a lack of statistical significance testing.  Differences in MSE and PMSE are reported, but without p-values or confidence intervals, it’s difficult to assess whether the observed improvements are statistically meaningful or due to random chance. The hyperparameter settings are briefly described but lack justification. How were these values selected? Was a hyperparameter search performed?  The discussion of the "pruning" problem and the limitations of MF-VI is good, but feels somewhat tacked on at the end, rather than integrated into a discussion of the model's weaknesses and future directions earlier in the paper.

Thirdly, the comparison with related work is not exhaustive.  While the paper cites relevant papers, it omits discussion of more recent advances in dynamic topic modeling and sequential Bayesian modeling that could provide a stronger context for the proposed work. For example, the paper doesn't discuss approaches leveraging attention mechanisms in recurrent neural networks for capturing long-range dependencies, which are relevant to the problem addressed here. [1] and [2] provide alternative, recent approaches to dynamic topic modelling.

Finally, the choice of datasets, while varied, could be strengthened. The description of the datasets is relatively brief, and it’s not always clear why these specific datasets were chosen to evaluate the proposed method. More detailed analysis of the dataset characteristics and how they relate to the strengths and weaknesses of the DDPFA model would be beneficial. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The proposed model offers a novel combination of PFA and recurrent neural networks for dynamic count data analysis.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on a range of datasets.
* The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in sequential data modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The mathematical derivations are incomplete and lack sufficient detail.
* The experimental results lack statistical significance testing and thorough hyperparameter justification.
* The comparison to related work is not exhaustive.
* The discussion of limitations and future directions is somewhat belated.
