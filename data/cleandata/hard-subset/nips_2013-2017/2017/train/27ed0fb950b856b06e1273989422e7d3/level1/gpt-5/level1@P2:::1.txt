Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for modeling sequences of high-dimensional count vectors. Observations xvt are modeled via a Poisson likelihood with additive factors: xvt ~ Poisson(sum_k θtk φvk λk ψv). Temporal dependence is introduced through a deep hierarchical latent structure: the factor scores θt depend on a window of lower-layer hidden states h(0) via an RNN-defined “implicit” conditional, and multiple higher layers h(n) are stacked, each with forward (prior) and backward (auxiliary posterior) RNNs over time windows. Priors on φ, λ, ψ are Gamma to encourage sparsity and smoothness. Inference alternates: (i) mean-field updates for Gamma-distributed latent variables using auxiliary latent counts xvtk to retain conjugacy; (ii) a “Probability AutoEncoder” stage updating the RNN parameters by minimizing L2 losses between expectations of prior and auxiliary-posterior conditionals and between data and the model’s Poisson rate (a reconstruction term). Experiments on four synthetic datasets and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA to PGDS (Poisson-Gamma dynamical system), LSTM, and PFA, showing improved MSE/PMSE on most tasks and some qualitative interpretability.

The work relates to: Poisson factorization and the Gamma–Poisson/NB process literature (e.g., Zhou et al.), dynamic PF models such as PGDS, deep latent variable models for counts (Deep Exponential Families, Gamma Belief Networks), and recurrent/temporal topic or PF models (e.g., neural autoregressive topic models; recurrent PF for recommendations). The paper aims to combine deep hierarchical latent structure with recurrent connections to capture long- and short-range temporal dependencies.

Quality:
- Technical correctness: The mean-field updates for φ, λ, ψ, and θ using auxiliary counts are standard under conjugate Gamma–Poisson assumptions. However, the central contribution—learning RNN-defined “implicit” conditionals—relies on a heuristic “Probability AutoEncoder” objective (Eq. 16–18) that minimizes squared distances between expectations of conditional distributions and between observed counts and reconstructed rates. This is not an ELBO or a likelihood-based objective. There is no derivation tying the two-stage procedure to maximizing a coherent bound on the marginal likelihood, nor guarantees about convergence. Because the prior over θ depends on RNN parameters, the model is no longer conditionally conjugate in the usual sense; the mean-field updates implicitly treat the RNN-defined shape parameters as fixed, and then the networks are fit separately with the L2 losses. This alternating scheme may break variational consistency and can introduce bias.
- Missing or unclear details: The exact parameterization of the Gamma conditionals (shape/rate) output by RNNs, stability constraints (positivity), and how the backward “auxiliary posterior” is used in the MF updates are not fully specified. The bidirectional/windowed RNN structure, choice of c, and the ladder-like connections are described informally without exact architectures. The choice of MSE/PMSE as the main metrics for count data is suboptimal; predictive log-likelihood or perplexity would be more standard.
- Empirics: While the model outperforms baselines on several datasets, details on data preprocessing, train/validation/test splits, hyperparameter selection, and runtime are limited. Comparisons may be unfair across methods (e.g., different iteration budgets and no hyperparameter tuning description). The baseline suite omits closely related deep count models (e.g., DEFs on counts) and recent recurrent PF variants in some domains. There is no ablation of the “Probability AutoEncoder” loss terms, the window size, or the contribution of λ, ψ. Uncertainty is not evaluated.

Clarity:
The paper has numerous grammatical issues, typos, and inconsistent notation (e.g., switching between subscripts/superscripts; ambiguous use of α, β; Eq. 8–14 notational collisions and incomplete conditioning). Several references are incomplete. Key derivations (e.g., the objective in Eq. 15–18) are not properly justified, and the training algorithm is hard to follow. Figures and tables are referenced but not fully described here; interpretability claims would benefit from clearer factor visualizations (e.g., top words for NIPS topics).

Originality:
Using RNNs to define temporal priors over PF scores is not entirely new; related work has modeled dynamics with transition matrices or RNNs. The proposed combination of (i) deep hierarchical latent layers over time, (ii) both forward and backward RNN-defined conditionals, and (iii) an alternating MF + “Probability AutoEncoder” training scheme is a novel blend. However, the paper does not clearly differentiate its contributions from deep Poisson/gamma belief networks and prior recurrent Poisson factorization approaches, nor does it provide ablations to isolate which component drives gains.

Significance:
If made rigorous and clearly presented, a deep dynamic PF with scalable inference could be impactful for sequential count modeling with interpretability. Given the current heuristic training objective, unclear theoretical footing, and limited evaluation metrics, it is difficult to assess broader impact or reproducibility. The empirical gains are promising but not decisive without stronger baselines and likelihood-based evaluations.

Pros:
- Addresses an important problem: modeling high-dimensional temporal count data with long-range dependencies.
- Unified framework combining PF’s interpretability with deep/recurrent structure.
- Empirical improvements on several synthetic and real datasets; qualitative case studies suggest meaningful factors.
- Discussion of observed failure modes (“pruning”) and ladder connections is thoughtful.

Cons:
- Core training objective is heuristic and not tied to an ELBO or well-defined likelihood; correctness and convergence are unclear.
- Clarity issues: notation, missing derivations, incomplete references, and imprecise algorithmic details.
- Evaluation relies on MSE/PMSE rather than predictive log-likelihood; limited hyperparameter tuning and baseline coverage.
- Novelty over closely related deep/recurrent PF and deep count models is not crisply established.
- No analysis of computational cost or scalability.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a principled variational objective that includes RNN parameters (e.g., a single ELBO with reparameterization/implicit gradients or augmentation-based tricks for Gamma/NB), and optimize it end-to-end with stochastic VI.
- Clarify the model: exact parameterizations, positivity constraints, ladder connections, windowing, and the roles of forward/backward conditionals. Provide a complete algorithm box.
- Report predictive log-likelihood (or perplexity), calibration, and ablations on depth, window size, ladder vs. plain hierarchy, and the contributions of λ and ψ.
- Strengthen baselines (deep exponential families on counts, gamma belief networks, existing recurrent PF) and ensure fair tuning and compute budgets. Report runtime.
- Improve writing, fix notation, and complete citations; add more interpretability analyses (e.g., topic coherence, top features per factor over time).

Overall assessment:
Interesting direction with potentially useful ideas, but the methodological foundation and clarity are currently insufficient for NIPS. I recommend rejection in its present form; with a principled variational objective, clearer exposition, and stronger evaluation, this work could become a solid contribution.
