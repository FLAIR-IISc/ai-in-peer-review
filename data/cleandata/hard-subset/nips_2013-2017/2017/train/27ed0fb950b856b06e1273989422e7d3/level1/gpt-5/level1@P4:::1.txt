Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for modeling sequential count vectors. Observations xvt follow a Poisson model with rate ∑k θtk φvk λk ψv, where φvk are factor loadings, θtk are time-varying factor scores, and λk, ψv are component- and feature-specific scale variables. Temporal dependence is introduced through a deep hierarchical latent structure: θt depends on a lower latent layer h(0) over a window of size c, and higher layers h(n) form a multi-layer recurrent structure capturing longer-range dependencies. The conditional distributions p(θt|h(0)) and p(h(n)|h(n+1)) are “implicit” Gamma distributions whose shape parameters are outputs of recurrent neural networks (RNNs). Inference alternates between (i) mean-field variational updates exploiting Gamma–Poisson conjugacy with latent count augmentation and (ii) learning the RNN parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that aligns the expectations of prior/posterior conditionals and the reconstruction.

The work connects to Poisson/Gamma factorization models (e.g., Beta- or Gamma–negative binomial processes) and dynamic variants like the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS). It is also related to deep latent variable models for counts (Deep Exponential Families, deep Poisson factorization with RBM/SBN) and to recurrent/temporal count models (e.g., recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation). The use of ladder-style top–down and bottom–up networks echoes hierarchical VAEs.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: sequential count modeling is important in recommendation, text, and event data.
- Modeling counts natively: the Poisson factorization backbone with Gamma priors and latent count augmentation is appropriate and scalable.
- Attempt to capture long-range temporal dependencies: the multi-layer recurrent prior is a principled direction beyond first-order gamma transitions used in PGDS-like models.
- Empirical scope: comparisons on multiple synthetic and real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, disasters, sheep population). The model reportedly outperforms PFA and LSTM, and is competitive with PGDS, with qualitative interpretability claims.
- Partially conjugate VI: makes some updates efficient.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (technical soundness)
- Objective for learning the RNN priors is not derived from a principled bound. The “Probability AutoEncoder” loss replaces the ELBO with squared errors between expectations of conditional distributions and reconstruction terms (Eqs. 15–18). This departs from variational inference without justification of an upper/lower bound, consistency, or convergence. As a result, there is no guarantee the learned network parameters maximize an ELBO or correspond to a coherent posterior approximation.
- The implicit conditional distributions are insufficiently specified. For example, p(θt|h(0)) is said to be Gamma with shape αθt produced by an RNN, but the rate βθ is fixed; similarly, p(h(n)|h(n+1)) uses Gamma(α + γ, βh) (Eq. 10), where both α and γ come from networks. The probabilistic meaning of summing “prior” and “posterior” shape parameters is unclear, and the top-layer prior p(h(N)) is not fully specified (Eq. 4).
- Several notational and indexing inconsistencies in the variational family and updates raise concerns about correctness:
  - In Eq. 5, q(ψk|·) appears, while ψ is defined feature-wise (ψv).
  - In Eq. 8, the posterior rate for λk includes βθ instead of βλ, and the terms include θ·k ∑v ψv φvk; clarity on sums and rates is needed.
  - The multinomial allocation in Eq. 7 uses φv twice in the numerator (φv λ φv), which seems a typo; should be θtk φvk λk ψv normalized.
- The decision to avoid reparameterization or score-function gradients because “sampling all latent variables is high-cost and useless” is not technically justified; VI typically uses minibatch subsampling, and in 2017 there were viable gradient estimators for nonreparameterizable distributions. Without a proper stochastic gradient estimator of an ELBO, the claimed VI becomes a hybrid of coordinate-ascent updates and ad-hoc regression objectives.
- Evaluation metrics: using in-sample MSE (fit) and one-step-ahead MSE (prediction) is suboptimal for count models; Poisson log-likelihood or held-out perplexity are more appropriate and standard in the literature. The paper does not report uncertainty or calibration.
- Experimental protocol is under-specified: details on network architectures, activation functions (e.g., enforcing positivity via softplus), initialization, learning rates, batch sizes, and runtime are missing. The single-step prediction setup (“last time step as predicting target”) does not test longer-horizon forecasting and may conflate overfitting with predictive performance.

Clarity
- The manuscript has numerous grammatical errors, typos, and inconsistent notation that impede understanding. The generative process for the deep hierarchy should be laid out precisely, with all conditionals, parameterizations, and priors. An algorithm box describing the two-stage inference would help.
- Several references are incomplete (missing venues/years) and key related work on hierarchical VAEs and temporal count models could be cited more precisely.
- Figures/tables are referenced but not shown; the text should summarize exact numbers or trends clearly.

Originality
- Combining recurrent, multi-layer priors with Poisson factorization is a reasonable extension, but related ideas exist: DEFs for counts, deep Poisson factorization, recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation, and state space models with deep transitions. The paper’s novelty hinges on the specific ladder-style bidirectional Gamma hierarchy and the hybrid VI/autoencoder training; however, the latter is not theoretically grounded. The paper should more carefully distinguish its contributions relative to PGDS with transition matrices, DEFs, and recurrent PF models.

Significance
- If technically solid and clearly demonstrated, a deep dynamic Poisson factorization could impact sequential count modeling. However, given the ad-hoc training objective, unclear probabilistic semantics of the deep hierarchy, limited metrics, and modest baselines (e.g., no comparison to other deep count models or stronger temporal Poisson baselines), the current evidence does not convincingly establish state-of-the-art performance or broad utility.

Suggestions for improvement
- Derive and optimize a principled ELBO for the full model, using appropriate gradient estimators (reparameterization where possible, or score-function with variance reduction), and report ELBO values.
- Clarify and correct all conditional distributions, variational factors, and updates; ensure indices and parameters are consistent (e.g., ψv vs ψk; βλ vs βθ).
- Provide a complete generative model specification, including the top-layer prior and exact parameterizations produced by networks (with positivity constraints).
- Report standard likelihood-based metrics for count data and perform multi-step forecasting. Include stronger baselines (e.g., recurrent PF for temporal recommendation, dynamic topic models with deep transitions).
- Add ablations (window size c, number of layers, effect of λ and ψ), sensitivity to K, and runtime/memory analysis.
- Improve clarity, organization, and references; include algorithm pseudocode and architectural details.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem with a count-native model.
- Attempts to capture long- and short-term dependencies via a deep recurrent hierarchy.
- Employs partially conjugate updates for scalability.
- Broad set of datasets with qualitative interpretability claims.

Cons
- Training objective for the neural prior is not a valid variational bound; lacks theoretical guarantees.
- Several derivation/notation inconsistencies and ambiguities.
- Evaluation metrics and baselines are limited; experimental details are insufficient.
- Novelty relative to existing deep/recurrent count models is not clearly established.
