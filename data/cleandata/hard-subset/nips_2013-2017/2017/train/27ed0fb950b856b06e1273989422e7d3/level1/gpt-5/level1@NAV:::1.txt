This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) for sequential count vectors. Building on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA), it models temporal dependence in the factor scores via a hierarchy of latent Gamma variables whose conditional distributions are parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs) over a sliding window, aiming to capture both short-range nonlinear dependencies and longer-range structure through depth. Inference alternates between mean-field updates exploiting Poisson–Gamma conjugacy (with auxiliary latent counts) and optimization of neural parameters via a VAE-like “probability autoencoder” loss that aligns encoder/decoder expectations and reconstructs counts. Experiments on synthetic series and several real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, Sheep) compare against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM, with DDPFA generally strongest except on ICEWS prediction where PGDS excels. The authors discuss practical issues (latent “pruning,” depth utility) and propose a ladder-style architecture as mitigation. This connects to NeurIPS work on Poisson/negative-binomial processes and PFA (e.g., Zhou et al.), deep exponential families, amortized VI for sequences (e.g., VRNN, Deep Kalman Filters), and recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendations.

Quality: The conjugate MF-VI part is standard and sound. However, the neural component is trained with an ad hoc squared-error objective on expectations rather than a principled ELBO; there is no derivation or bound guaranteeing consistency or convergence. The “collapse” replacing Θ by X in the loss (Eq. 18) is insufficiently justified. Experimental protocol lacks likelihood-based metrics for count data (e.g., predictive log-likelihood/Poisson deviance) and statistical significance; LSTM and PGDS tuning details are sparse.

Clarity: The paper is challenging to follow: indexing/typos, incomplete references, and undefined design choices (e.g., parameterization of Gamma shape/rate, role of λ and ψ given possible absorption) hinder reproducibility. Figures/tables are referred to but not shown in the text provided.

Originality: Combining PFA with RNN-parameterized Gamma transitions and a ladder hierarchy is a promising idea; related to deep exponential families and recurrent PF but not identical. The “probability autoencoder” framing is novel but needs theoretical grounding.

Significance: Addressing long-range dependence in sparse count sequences is important. The empirical gains are suggestive but not conclusive without stronger evaluation and ablations (depth, window size, ladder vs. plain, K sensitivity, computational cost).

Pros (for acceptance):
- Interesting hybrid of conjugate count models with neural temporal priors
- Generally strong empirical results across diverse datasets
- Interpretable factors and thoughtful discussion of pitfalls (latent collapse)

Cons (against acceptance):
- Lacks a principled ELBO/objective for the neural component
- Clarity/reproducibility issues; incomplete citations and details
- Evaluation metrics and baselines insufficiently rigorous
- Limited ablations and analysis of depth/architecture benefits
