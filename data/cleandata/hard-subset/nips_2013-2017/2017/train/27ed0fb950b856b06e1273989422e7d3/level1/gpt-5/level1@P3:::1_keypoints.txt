- Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA): Poisson factorization with per-component λk and per-feature ψv scales and deep hierarchical latent states h(n)t with windowed recurrent connections.
- Uses Gamma conditional distributions with neural networks outputting shape parameters; inference alternates between MFVI for conjugate parts and a “Probability AutoEncoder” training of networks using L2 consistency losses on expectations.
- Evaluated on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE and one-step PMSE.

Strengths:
- Combines scalable Poisson factorization interpretability (Φ) with learned temporal priors from RNNs to capture complex dependencies.
- MFVI updates exploit Gamma–Poisson conjugacy and sparse auxiliary counts, suited for large sparse matrices.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over PFA and sometimes over PGDS/LSTM across diverse domains.

Major concerns:
- Probabilistic formulation and training objective are incoherent: network “posteriors” are not derived from the model likelihood and training uses L2 consistency losses, not an ELBO or evidence lower bound.
- MFVI updates for h(n) mix prior shape and auxiliary network outputs in an ad hoc way, deviating from standard conjugate posterior updates and making the approximated posterior unclear.
- Generative specification of the implicit RNN-based distributions is underspecified (positivity, stationarity, layer dependence, top-layer parents).
- Network training replaces likelihood-based terms with squared-error losses on expectations, risking biased learning and decoupling from true Poisson likelihood; no sensitivity analyses or justification provided.
- Evaluation metrics (MSE/PMSE) are suboptimal for count data; held-out Poisson log-likelihood or perplexity and a clear prediction protocol are missing.
- Baselines and tuning are inadequate: LSTM setup is weak for high-dimensional counts, PGDS tuning unclear, and important baselines like dynamic Poisson factorization and deep time-series VAEs are absent.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues: typos, inconsistent notation, missing RNN/activation/optimizer details, and unclear preprocessing.

Clarity and originality:
- High-level idea is understandable but derivations and the “Probability AutoEncoder” lack principled connection to variational inference; notation errors impede reproducibility.
- Using neural nets for temporal priors over Gamma states is natural with precedents; the ladder bidirectional prior/posterior twist is interesting but overall novelty is incremental.

Significance:
- Potentially impactful if made principled and reproducible, but current theoretical and experimental weaknesses limit practical influence.

Pros:
- Tackles modeling temporal dependencies in high-dimensional count data; uses scalable conjugate structure and reports qualitative interpretability and empirical gains.

Cons:
- Ad hoc inference objective without ELBO or guarantees; posterior forms mix neural outputs inconsistently with the model.
- Missing implementation details, suboptimal evaluation/baselines, limited ablations, and signs of instability.

Suggested improvements:
- Recast learning under a coherent variational framework (ELBO) or amortized inference with appropriate Gamma reparameterizations or augmentations.
- Use proper count metrics (held-out log-likelihood, deviance/perplexity), clearly define prediction protocol, and include stronger baselines (dynamic PF, VRNN/DMM).
- Provide full architectural and optimization details, ensure positive parameterizations, and add thorough ablations (depth, window, λk/ψv effects, K).
- Tighten writing and correct equations; release code for reproducibility.

Recommendation:
- Reject in current form due to unsound probabilistic/inference foundations, unclear experiments, and reproducibility issues; revise with principled objective and stronger evaluation to be competitive.
