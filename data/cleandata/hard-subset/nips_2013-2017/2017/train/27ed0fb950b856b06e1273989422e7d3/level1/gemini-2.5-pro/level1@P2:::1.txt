### Summary

This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a novel approach for modeling time-series of count vectors. The model extends the standard Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) framework by incorporating a deep, dynamic structure to capture temporal dependencies. Specifically, the latent temporal factors (denoted Θ) are not modeled with a simple first-order Markov assumption as in previous work like the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS). Instead, DDPFA uses a hierarchical stack of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to define implicit conditional distributions, aiming to learn complex, non-linear, and long-range dependencies across time steps. The inference procedure is a two-stage hybrid method: latent variables are updated using mean-field variational inference, while the neural network parameters are trained by minimizing a squared-error loss function inspired by autoencoders. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on several synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improved fitting and prediction performance compared to PFA, PGDS, and LSTM baselines.

### Review

The paper addresses an important problem and proposes an interesting and intuitive modeling idea. However, the work is severely undermined by a lack of methodological rigor in its inference procedure and a lack of clarity in its presentation.

**Quality**

The technical quality of the paper is mixed. The motivation for using a more expressive model for temporal dynamics is sound, and the experimental setup appears reasonable. However, the core technical contribution—the inference algorithm—is not well-founded and lacks theoretical justification.

*   **The inference procedure is ad-hoc and not principled.** The paper describes a two-stage process of alternating between mean-field VI for the latent variables (Φ, Ψ, Λ, H, Θ) and gradient-based optimization for the neural network parameters (W). It is not clear what global objective this hybrid procedure optimizes.
*   The "Probability AutoEncoder" and its loss function (Eq. 16) are the most problematic aspects. The derivation from the intractable integral in Eq. 15 to a simple squared-error loss is hand-wavy and not rigorous. This loss function is not derived from a proper probabilistic objective like the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO). It discards the crucial KL divergence term found in variational autoencoders, which regularizes the posterior. As a result, it is unclear what the model is actually learning.
*   The justification provided for avoiding standard differentiable variational inference ("only a few samples are drawn from one certain distrbution") is unconvincing and does not align with common practice for training deep generative models.
*   The authors are honest about the model's limitations, such as the "pruning" phenomenon (posterior collapse) and the limited benefit of adding more layers. While this transparency is commendable, it also points to fundamental issues with the proposed model or inference scheme that are left unresolved.

**Clarity**

The paper is poorly written and organized, making it very difficult to understand the proposed model and reproduce the work.

*   The model description in Section 2 is vague. The generative process involving the RNNs (Eqs. 3 and 4) is not clearly specified until the inference section. A clear, step-by-step description of the generative process is missing.
*   **A critical Figure 1 is referenced multiple times but is not included in the paper.** This omission makes it nearly impossible to visualize and understand the proposed hierarchical RNN architecture.
*   The inference section (Section 3) is particularly confusing. The relationship between the "prior" network (`ffeed`) and the "posterior" network (`fback`) is muddled and does not cleanly map to the standard encoder-decoder structure of a VAE.
*   The manuscript is replete with grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing (e.g., "fanatical data," "it is rough for these models to catch complicated time dependence"), which significantly impedes readability.
*   References are incomplete and poorly formatted.

**Originality**

The core idea of integrating a deep RNN structure to model the temporal dynamics in a PFA model is novel. While dynamic topic models and deep generative models for sequences exist, this specific architecture for count data time-series appears to be a new combination of existing techniques. However, the novelty of the technical contribution is diminished by the ad-hoc nature of the inference method. The paper mentions "Recurrent Poisson Factorization" [12], which seems highly related, but fails to adequately discuss the differences or improvements offered by DDPFA.

**Significance**

The problem of modeling dynamic count data is highly significant, with applications in many areas of interest to the NIPS community. A model that can effectively capture complex temporal dynamics would be a valuable contribution. The reported empirical improvements over strong baselines suggest that the proposed approach has potential. However, the significance of this work is severely limited by the lack of methodological rigor and clarity. Without a well-defined and theoretically-grounded inference algorithm, the results are difficult to trust, and the method cannot be easily built upon by other researchers.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in modeling sequential count data.
*   The core modeling idea—using deep RNNs for temporal dynamics in PFA—is novel and intuitive.
*   The model shows strong empirical performance across multiple datasets compared to relevant baselines.

**Con:**
*   The inference algorithm is ad-hoc, lacks theoretical justification, and is not derived from a principled probabilistic objective (e.g., the ELBO). This is a major technical flaw.
*   The paper is poorly written, with key details of the model and inference being unclear. A critical figure is missing, making the architecture incomprehensible.
*   The acknowledged issues of posterior collapse ("pruning") and limited benefit from depth suggest the proposed method is not a robust solution.

### Final Recommendation

While the paper tackles an important problem with a novel idea, the execution is critically flawed. The lack of a principled inference algorithm and the severe clarity issues prevent this paper from being a solid scientific contribution. The work is not in a state where it can be understood, reproduced, or confidently built upon by the community. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors are encouraged to reformulate their inference within a standard framework like amortized variational inference (i.e., a proper VAE) and to substantially revise the manuscript for clarity before resubmission.
