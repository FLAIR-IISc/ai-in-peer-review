

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count data, extending previous work on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and dynamic Poisson models. The authors build upon PGDS [2] and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9,10] by incorporating recurrent neural networks to model implicit distributions, aiming to capture both short-term complex relationships and long-term temporal dependencies. The model uses variational inference with neural network-based parameter learning, evaluated on synthetic and real-world datasets including ICEWS, NIPS corpus, and EBOLA.

The paper demonstrates several strengths. The model is technically sound with proper variational inference implementation and clear experimental design. The results show consistent performance improvements over PFA and PGDS on most datasets, particularly in capturing interpretable latent structures (e.g., Figure 2 shows DDPFA better captures multi-factor activations than PGDS). The significance is evident in real-world applications where complex temporal patterns are present, and the model provides interpretable factorizations. The clarity is good, with well-organized sections explaining the model architecture and inference procedure.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The originality is somewhat limited as it combines existing techniques (PFA + deep learning) rather than introducing fundamentally new concepts, and the authors acknowledge issues with "pruning" and unnecessary network layers (Table 3). The significance is weakened by inconsistent performance—DDPFA underperforms PGDS on ICEWS prediction, and the model's benefits diminish with additional layers. The evaluation could be strengthened by more thorough ablation studies on layer depth and network architecture.

Pro: Strong technical foundation, clear model description, good performance on most datasets, interpretable latent structures.
Con: Limited novelty, inconsistent performance across datasets, unaddressed issues with model architecture.

Overall, the paper makes a reasonable contribution to sequential count data analysis, but the incremental nature of the approach and unresolved architectural issues prevent it from making a transformative contribution to the field. While the technical execution is sound, the significance is somewhat constrained by the marginal gains over existing methods. I recommend rejection due to limited novelty and the presence of unresolved weaknesses that affect the model's practical utility.
