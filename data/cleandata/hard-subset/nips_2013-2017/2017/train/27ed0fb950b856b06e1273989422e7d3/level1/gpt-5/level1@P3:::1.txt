Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA) for modeling sequential count vectors. Observations xvt are modeled as Poisson with rate ∑k θtk φvk λk ψv, extending standard Poisson factorization with per-component (λk) and per-feature (ψv) scales to encourage sparsity and smoothness. Temporal dependence is introduced via a deep hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t, with a windowed recurrent structure: h(n)t depends on h(n+1)t−c:t, and θt depends on h(0)t−c:t. The conditional distributions are Gamma, with shape parameters produced by neural networks (“implicit distributions”). Inference proceeds in two alternating stages: (i) mean-field variational inference (MFVI) with conditional conjugacy for Φ, Λ, Ψ, Θ, H, given fixed network parameters; (ii) training the neural networks via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective that encourages consistency between top-down priors and bottom-up posteriors and a reconstruction term, using squared-error losses on expectations rather than an ELBO. Experiments on synthetic datasets and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA to PGDS, PFA, and LSTM, reporting MSE for fit and one-step prediction.

This work connects to a substantial literature on count factorization and temporal/deep generative models: Poisson/Gamma/Beta-negative binomial processes for counts (e.g., Zhou et al., NIPS 2012–2015), Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS), Hierarchical/Deep Poisson Factor Analysis and Gamma Belief Networks (Gan et al., Zhou et al.), dynamic Poisson factorization for recommendation (Gopalan et al., RecSys 2015), recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation, and deep latent variable time-series models (Deep Kalman Filters, Krishnan et al., NIPS 2015; VRNN; Deep Markov Models).

Quality
Strengths:
- The model combines the strengths of Poisson factorization (scaling to sparse counts, interpretability via Φ) with learned temporal priors parameterized by RNNs, potentially capturing complex short- and long-range dependencies.
- The MFVI updates for Φ, Λ, Ψ, and Θ leverage Gamma–Poisson conjugacy and sparse auxiliary counts, a practical choice for large, sparse matrices.
- The empirical section spans several distinct domains and shows consistent gains over PFA and sometimes PGDS/LSTM.

Concerns:
- The probabilistic formulation and inference objective are not coherent. The neural networks output Gamma shape parameters for priors and “posteriors,” but the latter are not derived from the model’s likelihood; instead, the model introduces a second network and uses L2 consistency losses between expectations (Eqs. 16–18). This is not an ELBO, nor is there a guarantee it lower-bounds log evidence, making convergence and statistical correctness unclear.
- The MFVI updates for h(n) (Eq. 14) with shape αh(n)tk + γh(n)tk combine a prior shape and an auxiliary “posterior” contribution from another network. This deviates from standard conjugate updates (which add observed or expected counts) and appears ad hoc. As a consequence, it is unclear what posterior is being approximated.
- The “implicit distributions” are said to be RNNs that output prior parameters, but the exact generative specification (e.g., parameterization ensuring positivity, stationarity, and dependence structure at each layer, especially for the top layer h(N) with past-only parents) is underspecified.
- The training objective for the networks replaces likelihood-based terms with squared errors on expectations, partly “collapsing” Θ into X via fixed factors (Eq. 18). This can bias learning and decouple network training from the true Poisson likelihood. No sensitivity or ablation is provided to justify these design choices.
- Experimental evaluation metrics (MSE for fit and one-step PMSE) are not ideal for count models; held-out Poisson log-likelihood/deviance or perplexity are standard. The prediction protocol (what history is conditioned on) is not fully specified.
- Baselines and tuning: LSTM on very high-dimensional count vectors without appropriate architectures (e.g., autoregressive decoders, count likelihoods) is a weak baseline. PGDS settings are taken from prior work, but no effort is described to tune across datasets. There is no comparison to dynamic Poisson factorization (Gopalan et al.) or recent deep time-series VAEs.
- Clarity/notation issues impede reproducibility: several typos and inconsistencies (e.g., Eq. 7 parameters, duplicated symbols; Eq. 8 references; inconsistent indices), missing architectural details (RNN type, activations, ensuring positive shape parameters), optimizer settings, and data preprocessing.

Clarity
The paper’s high-level idea is understandable, but the derivations are often informal, with ambiguous notation and errors in equations. The “Probability AutoEncoder” is central yet not presented with a clear, principled connection to variational inference. Figures and tables (mentioned but not shown here) seem to carry much of the interpretability argument; however, the text alone does not allow reproduction.

Originality
The idea of using neural networks to parameterize temporal priors over Gamma latent states in Poisson factorization is natural and has close precedents (recurrent Poisson factorization, dynamic Poisson factorization with state-space models, deep Poisson/gamma hierarchies). The specific ladder-style bidirectional networks for prior/posterior parameters is an interesting twist, but the training approach departs from established variational objectives without theoretical justification. Relative to prior deep temporal latent-variable models (DKF, VRNN/DMM), the novelty is incremental.

Significance
If made principled and reproducible, combining PFAs with learned temporal priors could be impactful for sequential sparse counts. The current empirical gains are promising but hard to interpret given metric choice, limited baselines, and algorithmic ambiguity. Without a sound objective or guarantees, it is difficult to see this version influencing practice.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem: modeling complex temporal dependencies in high-dimensional count data.
- Uses scalable conjugate structure for count factors.
- Reports improvements on several datasets; provides qualitative factor interpretability.

Cons
- Inference objective is ad hoc; no ELBO or theoretical guarantees.
- Posterior forms for latent variables mix neural outputs in ways that do not follow from the model.
- Clarity and notation issues; missing implementation details.
- Evaluation metrics/baselines are suboptimal; missing key comparisons (dynamic PF, deep Markov models).
- Limited ablations (e.g., effect of λk, ψv, window c, hierarchy depth), and reported issues (“pruning,” hierarchy ineffectiveness) suggest instability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Recast learning under a coherent variational framework: define an ELBO with amortized inference networks for h(n) and θt, using appropriate reparameterizations (e.g., generalized reparameterization gradients for Gamma, or augmentation-based gradients), and optimize it end-to-end. Alternatively, maintain conjugate MFVI for PF parameters and amortize only the temporal prior.
- Use proper metrics (held-out log-likelihood, Poisson deviance/perplexity) and clearly define the prediction protocol. Include stronger baselines: dynamic Poisson factorization, recurrent PF, Deep Markov Models/VRNN with Poisson likelihoods.
- Provide full architectural/optimization details and code. Clarify all distributions and ensure positivity constraints and parameterizations are explicit.
- Conduct thorough ablations: depth vs. window size, role of λk and ψv, different K, different RNNs, and assess uncertainty calibration.
- Tighten writing and correct equations.

Recommendation
Reject in its current form. The paper tackles a relevant problem and has promising empirical signals, but the probabilistic/inference foundations are insufficiently sound and the experimental evaluation does not meet NeurIPS standards. A revised version with a principled variational objective, clearer exposition, and stronger evaluation could be competitive.
