Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count vectors. Building on Poisson Factorization (PF), it introduces: (i) additional feature-level and component-level Gamma variables (ψv, λk) to encourage sparsity and smoothness, (ii) a deep hierarchy of time-evolving latent variables h(n) with a windowed recurrent structure to capture short- and long-range temporal dependencies, and (iii) a two-stage inference scheme: mean-field coordinate ascent updates for Gamma-Poisson conjugate parts and a “Probability AutoEncoder” that trains recurrent networks to parameterize the shape parameters of Gamma conditionals by matching moments via squared-error losses. Experiments on synthetic data and five datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, EBOLA, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA to PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE and one-step PMSE, and show improved performance in most settings and qualitatively interpretable factors.

The paper is related to dynamic Poisson factorization and topic models (e.g., PGDS, dynamic topic models), deep latent variable models (deep exponential families; deep Poisson factor analysis using RBM/SBN), and neural sequence models for counts (recurrent PF for temporal recommendation; deep Poisson factor modeling with recurrent structure and Bernoulli-Poisson link). The idea of using neural networks to parameterize conditional distributions sits close to neural autoregressive density models and neural topic models. The proposed deep hierarchical temporal structure aims to combine PF’s count modeling with RNNs’ capacity to capture complex temporal dependencies.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important setting: sequential, sparse count data across diverse domains.
- Combines PF’s interpretability with deep/recurrent structure to model long-range temporal dependence, a recognized limitation of shallow PF transitions.
- Inference leverages conjugacy for many variables, making the updates simple; the model design (Gamma priors) is compatible with PF machinery.
- Empirical results suggest improved MSE/PMSE vs. PGDS, PFA, and LSTM in several datasets; qualitative examples (ICEWS factors) indicate interpretability and multi-factor activation across time.
- Ablation observations (e.g., limited gains from deeper upper layers; benefits of ladder-like connections) are useful and honest.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation:

Quality:
- The derivation and justification of the “Probability AutoEncoder” objective are not rigorous. The training loss uses squared errors between means of conditional distributions (Eq. 16–18) rather than a principled ELBO or likelihood-based objective. It is unclear whether optimizing this loss maximizes a lower bound on the marginal likelihood, or even correlates with predictive log-likelihood. This raises concerns about consistency, bias, and convergence.
- The mean-field updates treat θtk and h(n)tk as Gamma with shape parameters coming from RNNs; however, the paper alternates between calling these “implicit distributions” and specifying them as Gamma. If they are Gamma, a proper ELBO with the RNN outputs as natural parameters could be derived, allowing gradients of the ELBO with respect to network parameters (e.g., conjugate-computation VI or amortized variational EM). The current moment-matching squared-error training seems ad hoc.
- Important modeling/inference details are missing or ambiguous: the exact RNN architecture (LSTM/GRU/vanilla), nonlinearities, parameterization of rate vs. shape, positivity enforcement, window size selection, number of layers per dataset, and computational complexity/time. Equations contain typos/inconsistencies (e.g., ψv vs. ψk; Eq. 8 “Φ,Φ”; Bernoulli-Poisson link b = 1(x > 1) should be 1(x > 0); normalization in Eq. 7). These issues hinder reproducibility.
- Evaluation metrics (MSE, PMSE) are not ideal for count data; held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity would be more appropriate. No uncertainty estimates or significance tests are reported. Comparisons to closely related neural/dynamic topic models (e.g., dynamic topic models; deep Poisson factor modeling with recurrent structure; neural variational topic/RNN approaches) are missing.
- The paper notes “pruning” and limited benefits from deeper upper layers, but the proposed ladder remedy is only briefly described; quantitative ablations and clarity on the ladder architecture are limited.

Clarity:
- The exposition is difficult to follow in several places, with grammatical issues, incomplete/misformatted references, and overloaded notation. The modeling section would benefit from a clear generative process, explicit distributions at each layer, and a consistent notation table. The inference section should cleanly separate (a) coordinate updates derived from a stated ELBO and (b) how the neural parameters are optimized with respect to that ELBO.
- Figures/Tables are referenced but not fully described; hyperparameter settings and preprocessing choices are under-specified.

Originality:
- While combining PF with deep/recurrent structures is not new (e.g., deep PF; recurrent PF; deep Poisson factor modeling with recurrent components), the particular hierarchy and bidirectional windowed RNNs for Gamma shape parameters across multiple latent layers are a novel combination. That said, the closest prior [12] (deep recurrent Bernoulli-Poisson PF) seems very close; the paper should clearly delineate differences and include direct comparisons.

Significance:
- Potentially useful if the method offers better long-range temporal modeling with interpretable factors. However, without a principled inference objective and stronger baselines/metrics, the current evidence is not yet compelling. The lack of likelihood-based evaluation and ablations limits the paper’s impact.

Questions and suggestions:
- Derive and optimize a principled ELBO. Given Gamma conditionals with natural parameters output by RNNs, you can use conjugate-computation VI (closed-form coordinate updates for q, backprop through expectations for network parameters) or black-box VI with control variates. Avoid ad-hoc squared-error objectives.
- Clarify the exact distributions: are p(θt|h(0)) and p(h(n)|h(n+1)) Gamma with shape from RNN and fixed rate? If so, specify both shape and rate parameterizations and ensure conjugacy is preserved.
- Report predictive log-likelihood/perplexity and compare to DTM, deep PF (RBM/SBN), neural topic models, and recurrent PF. Include error bars and runtime.
- Provide architectural details, complexity, and code. Explore window size c, layers N, and ablations (no Λ/Ψ, no ladder, no future context in inference).
- Fix typos and notation. Correct the Bernoulli-Poisson link and normalization.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem (dynamic counts) with an interpretable model.
- Empirical improvements reported on several datasets; qualitative factor analyses are promising.
- Novel combination of PF with deep bidirectional temporal structure.

Cons against acceptance:
- Inference objective is not principled; the proposed training loss lacks theoretical grounding.
- Clarity and rigor issues (typos, notation, missing details) hamper reproducibility.
- Evaluation metrics and baselines are insufficient; missing comparisons to closest related work.
- Ablations suggest limited benefits from depth; the ladder fix is not thoroughly evaluated.

Overall, the idea is interesting and relevant, but the paper needs a clearer, principled variational formulation, stronger empirical evaluation, and improved presentation before it can meet NIPS standards.
