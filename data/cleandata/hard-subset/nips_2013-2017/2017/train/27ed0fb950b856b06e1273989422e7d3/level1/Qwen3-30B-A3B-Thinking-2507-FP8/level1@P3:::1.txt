

# Peer Review: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

## Summary and Context
This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count vectors, building upon Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and extending it with deep neural network structures to capture complex temporal dependencies. The authors propose a hierarchical latent structure where recurrent neural networks (RNNs) model the implicit distributions between latent variables across time steps, aiming to better capture both short-term local relationships and long-term temporal dependencies. This work extends previous methods like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis, addressing limitations in capturing long-range dependencies through a deeper latent structure.

The paper relates well to recent NIPS work on deep Bayesian models (e.g., Deep Exponential Families [12], Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9, 10]) and hierarchical models (e.g., nested Chinese Restaurant Process [4], Deep Gaussian Processes [6, 7]). However, it doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from the "Deep Poisson Factor Modeling with a Recurrent Structure" [12], which also uses RNNs for temporal dependencies in Poisson factorization.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound model with a clear theoretical foundation. The variational inference approach is well-motivated, and the experimental design is appropriate, using both synthetic and real-world datasets. The authors demonstrate that DDPFA outperforms PGDS, LSTM, and PFA on most datasets, particularly where long-term dependencies are important. The interpretability of the learned factors (shown in Figures 2-3) is a significant strength.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the model description is generally accessible. The experimental section provides sufficient detail about datasets and evaluation metrics (MSE and PMSE), allowing for reproducibility.

**Significance:** The model addresses an important problem in time series analysis of count data, with applications in recommendation systems, text analysis, and network analysis. The ability to capture long-term dependencies while maintaining interpretable latent factors has practical value. The demonstration of improved performance on datasets where previous methods (like PGDS) fail (e.g., EBOLA, ASP) shows meaningful progress.

## Weaknesses

**Originality:** The core contribution is not sufficiently novel. The use of RNNs to model temporal dependencies in Poisson factorization has been explored in [12], which the authors cite but don't adequately distinguish from their work. The "ladder-like architecture" mentioned as a solution to the "pruning" problem isn't sufficiently justified or compared to existing hierarchical models (e.g., the ladder VAE [26] or hierarchical Bayesian models [27]). The paper doesn't clearly articulate how DDPFA differs from previous deep Poisson factorization approaches.

**Quality:** The experimental evaluation lacks statistical significance testing for the performance improvements. The paper mentions "pruning" as a problem but doesn't thoroughly investigate its causes or propose effective solutions beyond noting it's likely due to mean-field variational inference. The paper also doesn't address potential computational complexity or scalability to very large datasets.

**Clarity:** The model description could be clearer regarding the exact structure of the RNNs and how they interact with the hierarchical latent variables. The loss function (Equation 18) is complex and could benefit from more intuitive explanation.

## Specific Criticisms

1. The paper claims DDPFA captures "long-time dependence" but doesn't provide theoretical justification for why the deep hierarchical structure specifically improves long-term dependency modeling compared to simpler RNNs or LSTMs.

2. The comparison with LSTM is somewhat superficial. While the paper notes LSTM's limitations on SDS4 and high-dimensional data, it doesn't explore why LSTM fails in these cases or whether a more sophisticated RNN architecture (e.g., LSTM or GRU) would perform better than the simple RNNs used in DDPFA.

3. The "pruning" problem mentioned in Section 5 is acknowledged but not adequately addressed. The authors suggest it's due to mean-field VI, but don't explore how alternative inference methods (e.g., MCMC, more sophisticated variational methods) might alleviate this issue.

4. The ablation study in Table 3 shows diminishing returns from adding layers, which contradicts the core motivation of the deep model. The paper doesn't adequately explain this counterintuitive result.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- Addresses an important problem with practical applications
- Demonstrates improved performance over baselines on several datasets
- Provides interpretable latent structures
- Well-structured and clearly written

**Against Acceptance:**
- Core contribution lacks sufficient novelty compared to recent work
- Experimental evaluation lacks statistical rigor
- Fails to adequately address limitations of the model
- The ablation study undermines the motivation for deep structure

## Recommendation

While the paper presents a technically sound model that addresses a meaningful problem in time series analysis of count data, the novelty is limited by insufficient differentiation from prior work, particularly [12]. The experimental evaluation would be significantly strengthened with statistical significance tests and a more thorough exploration of the model's limitations. The ablation study showing diminishing returns from deeper layers also weakens the justification for the deep structure.

I recommend *weak acceptance* with the understanding that the paper has value in its application to count data analysis but requires substantial revisions to clarify its novelty and address the limitations noted above. The authors should more clearly differentiate their approach from [12], provide statistical significance testing for their results, and thoroughly address the "pruning" issue and the counterintuitive ablation results.

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field but doesn't significantly advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. With the suggested revisions, it could be a valuable addition to the literature.
