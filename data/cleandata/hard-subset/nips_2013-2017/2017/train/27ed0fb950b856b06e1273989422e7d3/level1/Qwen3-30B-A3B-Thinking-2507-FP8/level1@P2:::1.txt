

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count data. The authors build upon Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and extend it with deep neural networks to capture complex temporal dependencies. The model uses recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions for short-term relationships and a deep structure to capture long-term dependencies. The authors claim their approach outperforms existing methods (PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA) on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The paper relates well to previous work, citing relevant NIPS papers like Deep Exponential Families [8] and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9, 10]. It acknowledges limitations in prior work, particularly the shallow structure of existing dynamic Poisson models that struggle with long-term dependencies. The authors position their work as filling this gap through a deep learning approach.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound model that combines variational inference with deep learning for sequential count data. The mathematical formulation is mostly clear, and the inference algorithm is well-described. The authors provide synthetic and real-world experiments to support their claims. However, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis to justify the claimed advantages of the deep structure. The experimental setup has limitations: DDPFA uses only 100 iterations while PGDS uses 1000 Gibbs sampling iterations, potentially biasing the comparison. The paper also doesn't thoroughly explain why DDPFA performs poorly on the ICEWS prediction task compared to PGDS. The claim that "deep structure" captures long-term dependence isn't convincingly supported, as Table 3 shows minimal performance improvement with additional layers.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the figures (Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate the model structure and results. The experimental section clearly describes datasets and evaluation metrics (MSE and PMSE). However, some aspects of the variational inference procedure could be clarified, particularly the relationship between the neural network parameters and the variational distributions. The description of the probability autoencoder could benefit from more concrete implementation details.

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution is the integration of deep neural networks with Poisson factorization for sequential count data. While the combination of deep learning with Poisson factorization isn't entirely novel (citing [9, 10, 12]), the specific architecture using recursive neural networks to model implicit distributions for temporal dependencies is presented as novel. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related work like "Deep Poisson Factor Modeling with a recurrent structure" [12], which also uses recurrent structures for Poisson factorization. The paper's main weakness in originality is that Table 3 reveals the "deep" aspect provides minimal performance gains, suggesting the deep structure may not be as valuable as claimed.

**Significance:** The problem of analyzing sequential count data is important for applications like recommendation systems, text analysis, and time series analysis. The paper demonstrates that DDPFA can provide more interpretable latent structures than existing methods (evidenced by Figures 2-3). However, the significance is somewhat limited by the modest performance improvements over existing methods and the inconsistent results across datasets (e.g., DDPFA performs worse than PGDS on ICEWS prediction). The paper also doesn't address how the model scales to very large datasets, which is crucial for practical applications.

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated problem with clear real-world applications
- Good experimental design with both synthetic and real-world datasets
- Effective visualization of interpretable latent structures (Figures 2-3)
- Clear explanation of the model's relation to prior work

**Weaknesses:**
- Minimal performance gains from adding deep layers (Table 3)
- Inconsistent results across datasets (poor performance on ICEWS)
- Insufficient theoretical justification for the deep structure
- Unfair experimental comparison (DDPFA vs. PGDS with different iteration counts)
- Limited discussion of scalability to large datasets

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in sequential count data analysis with potential applications in multiple domains.
2. The model provides interpretable latent structures that outperform existing methods on some datasets.
3. The integration of deep learning with Poisson factorization is a reasonable approach that could inspire future work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1. The "deep" aspect of the model provides minimal performance gains, undermining the main contribution.
2. The experimental comparison with PGDS is unfair due to different numbers of iterations.
3. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from related work like [12].
4. The paper lacks theoretical analysis to support the claims about long-term dependency capture.

**Overall assessment:** While the paper presents a reasonable extension of Poisson factorization for sequential count data, the claimed advantages of the deep structure are not strongly supported. The performance gains over existing methods are modest and inconsistent, and the paper's main contribution (the deep structure) appears to provide limited benefits. The work is technically sound but lacks the significant advancement in the state-of-the-art that NIPS typically seeks. I would recommend rejection, as the contribution doesn't demonstrate sufficient novelty or impact to warrant publication at NIPS. The paper would benefit from either a stronger theoretical justification for the deep structure, more extensive experiments showing consistent performance improvements, or a clearer differentiation from similar prior work.
