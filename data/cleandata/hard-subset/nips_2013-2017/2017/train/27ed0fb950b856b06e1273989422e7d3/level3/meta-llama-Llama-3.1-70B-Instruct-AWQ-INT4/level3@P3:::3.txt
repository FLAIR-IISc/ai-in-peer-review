This paper proposes a deep dynamic Poisson factorization model (DDPFA) to analyze sequential count vectors. The model extends traditional Poisson factor analysis by incorporating a recurrent neural network to capture long-range temporal dependencies. The authors employ variational inference to learn the model's parameters, which is a significant improvement over traditional methods that rely on Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) or other approximations.

The technical content of the paper is strong, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the model's components and the inference algorithm. The use of a recurrent neural network to model the temporal dependencies is a novel approach that allows the model to capture complex patterns in the data. The authors also provide a detailed comparison with existing methods, including Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model in both synthetic and real-world datasets. The authors show that DDPFA outperforms existing methods in most cases, especially in capturing long-range temporal dependencies. The results also highlight the importance of using a deep architecture to model the temporal dependencies, as the performance of the model improves significantly when using multiple layers.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main concerns is the lack of clarity in the writing. There are several grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper, which makes it difficult to follow at times. Additionally, some of the sentences are worded in a way that makes them hard to understand.

Another limitation is the lack of emphasis on held-out perplexity as an evaluation metric. While the authors provide results for mean squared error (MSE) and predicted mean squared error (PMSE), held-out perplexity is a more informative metric that can provide insight into the model's ability to generalize to unseen data.

The paper could also benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods. While the authors provide a comparison with PGDS and LSTM, it would be helpful to include a baseline of dynamic Poisson factorization with a single-level deep architecture to justify the benefit of using multiple hierarchy levels.

Furthermore, the comparison with LSTM is not definitive, as the learning algorithms differ and do not fully justify the hierarchical depth. It would be helpful to include a more detailed discussion of the differences between the two models and how they relate to the results.

Finally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed model. While the authors mention some of the limitations, such as the "pruning" problem, it would be helpful to provide a more detailed discussion of these limitations and how they can be addressed in future work.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to modeling temporal dependencies in sequential count data. The use of a recurrent neural network to model the temporal dependencies is a significant improvement over traditional methods, and the authors provide a thorough discussion of the model's components and the inference algorithm.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of sequential data analysis. The proposed model has the potential to be applied to a wide range of applications, including recommendation systems, text analysis, and time series analysis.
