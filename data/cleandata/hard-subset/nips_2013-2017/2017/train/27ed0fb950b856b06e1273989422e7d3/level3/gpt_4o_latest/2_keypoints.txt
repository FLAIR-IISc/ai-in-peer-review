- Introduces Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA) combining RNNs and gamma distributions to model long-term dependencies in sequential count data.
- Integrates Poisson Factor Analysis with deep hierarchical structures to capture both short-term and long-term temporal dependencies.
- Uses variational inference and neural networks to approximate gamma shape parameters, incorporating prior and likelihood information effectively.
- Demonstrates superior predictive and fitting performance on synthetic and real-world datasets compared to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.
- Novel integration of RNNs and gamma distributions is a meaningful advancement over shallow models for long-time dependencies.
- Variational inference approach is technically sound and reduces variance via latent variable expectations, improving computational efficiency.
- Experiments show strong performance and interpretable latent factors, enhancing practical utility.
- Addresses a relevant and challenging problem with broad applications (recommendation, text analysis, disaster prediction).
- Paper has clarity issues, including critical typos (e.g., Equation 10) and lacks detailed architectural diagrams.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: missing comparisons with simpler PFA variants and systematic analysis of window size and factor count.
- Fixed scale parameters in the gamma distributions are not justified or analyzed, raising concerns about flexibility and robustness.
- Introduction lacks sufficient discussion of recent deep Poisson factor analysis work, weakening contextualization.
- Recommendations: fix typos, add detailed model diagrams, perform ablation and sensitivity studies, and analyze fixed scale parameters.
- Pros: novel, technically sound, strong empirical results, and tackles an important problem.
- Cons: presentation clarity issues, limited experimental comparisons and parameter analyses, and insufficient discussion of fixed parameters and related work.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on improving presentation and expanding experimental/contextual analyses.
