This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors, leveraging a simplified RNN-like structure within a variational inference framework. While the idea of combining Poisson Factor Analysis with deep learning to capture both short- and long-term dependencies is potentially interesting, the paper suffers from significant clarity, technical soundness, and experimental evaluation issues, requiring substantial revisions before being considered for NIPS.

The paper’s presentation is weak, particularly regarding technical details. Section 2 lacks crucial equations. For example, the parameterization of Gamma in Eq. 4 and the distribution generating θt in Eq. 3 are undefined, hindering understanding and reproducibility. The authors claim certain distributions are “implicit,” which is incorrect; Eqs. 9-10 explicitly define Gamma distributions with closed-form conditionals. Furthermore, the description of potential weaknesses concerning "fanatical data and disaster data" (lines 30-31) is vague and requires concrete explanation. The subsection surrounding lines 133-155 is also unclear. Eq. 15’s “loss function” is not adequately defined—it’s presented without sufficient context or justification.

The core modeling choice of simplifying the RNN structure (Eq. 4) requires strong justification. The authors state that h_t^(n) depends only on top-layer memory vectors, not previous same-layer time steps. This deviates from standard RNNs and the rationale for this architectural choice – its benefits and limitations – is not provided. Is this for computational efficiency? Does it address a specific issue with gradient flow? Without this justification, it appears arbitrary.

The experimental evaluation is also deficient. The use of MSE/PMSE for evaluating PF models is inappropriate. Predictive log-likelihood is a far more appropriate metric for assessing the model's ability to predict count data and should be reported. Moreover, the choice of K=3 (or K=10 in some cases) seems too small. Given the high dimensionality of some datasets (e.g., V=14036 in NIPS corpus), a larger value of K (e.g., K≈100) is warranted to adequately capture the underlying latent structure.  The results in Table 3 regarding the impact of network depth and width are inconclusive and require more thorough investigation. The discussion around Fig. 3 (lines 224-232) feels disconnected from the figure itself – it lacks specific references to the observations made in the figure and doesn't provide a clear interpretation of the results.

Several minor issues detract from the paper's quality. The variational update equations (Eqs. 11-14) could be moved to the supplementary material to improve the flow of the main text. The caption for Fig. 4 is unreadable. Writing quality is consistently poor; the paper suffers from frequent passive voice (especially in the abstract), typos (“factorize”), missing articles, misused terminology (“Dirichlet”), incorrectly labeled Fig. 1, and incomplete sentences throughout.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* **Technical Incompleteness:** Missing equations and unclear explanations hinder understanding and reproducibility.
* **Inappropriate Evaluation Metrics:** Using MSE/PMSE for count data is fundamentally flawed.
* **Insufficient Justification:** The simplified RNN structure lacks a compelling rationale.
* **Poor Writing Quality:** Numerous grammatical errors and unclear phrasing obstruct comprehension.
* **Weak Experimental Design:** Small latent factor size and inconclusive scaling results.

**Arguments for acceptance (weak):**

* **Novel Idea:** The combination of deep learning and Poisson Factor Analysis is a potentially promising direction.
* **Empirical Results:** The model demonstrates competitive performance on some datasets, albeit with caveats.

**Overall:**

This paper presents an interesting idea but is not currently suitable for publication at NIPS. The authors must address the technical issues, clarify the methodology, improve the experimental evaluation, and significantly revise the writing quality.  Substantial revisions and further experimentation are required before this work can be considered a strong contribution to the field. I recommend rejection in its current form.
