Summary
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count vectors that augments Poisson factorization with a deep, recurrent structure over time. Counts xvt are modeled via a Poisson likelihood with nonnegative factors Φ, temporal scores Θ, and feature-/component-specific scalings Ψ and Λ. To capture complex temporal dependencies beyond first-order Markov assumptions used in prior dynamic PF (e.g., PGDS), the authors introduce a hierarchy of latent variables h(n)t (n = 0..N) whose conditional distributions are parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs) over temporal windows. Inference uses a two-stage scheme: (i) mean-field variational inference (VI) with conjugate Gamma updates for the PF part, leveraging auxiliary latent counts xvtk; (ii) learning the RNN parameters via a “probability autoencoder” loss that aligns prior and auxiliary posterior networks and reconstructs Θ (and, in a collapsed form, X). Experiments on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA to PGDS, LSTM, and a static PFA. DDPFA typically attains lower MSE/PMSE and exhibits longer, non-local factor activations with plausible interpretability.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Gamma–Poisson factorization and negative-binomial process models for count data, dynamic PF with gamma chains and transition matrices (e.g., PGDS), and deep latent variable models such as Deep Exponential Families, deep Poisson FA (RBM/SBN), and recurrent PF for recommendations. The main novelty is the combination of PF with a deep, ladder-like temporal hierarchy whose conditional distributions are parameterized by RNNs, plus a VI/training scheme that couples mean-field updates with a deterministic “probability autoencoder” objective.

Strengths
- Addresses a clear limitation of prior dynamic PF models (short-range, first-order dynamics) by introducing a deep, flexible temporal hierarchy.
- Derives practical mean-field updates that exploit conjugacy for all PF variables and integrates them with neural parameter learning.
- Empirical results on diverse datasets indicate consistent gains over PFA/PGDS and competitive performance vs. LSTM, with interpretable factors that remain active over extended periods.
- Discussion acknowledges known issues in hierarchical VAEs (e.g., pruning) and proposes a ladder-like architecture to mitigate them.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Objective/derivation: The “probability autoencoder” losses (Eqs. 15–18) are heuristic and not shown to be lower bounds on the marginal likelihood. It is unclear whether training the RNNs improves a coherent ELBO or introduces bias relative to the mean-field stage. Please clarify the relationship to a joint ELBO, or provide theoretical justification/ablation showing that these losses improve predictive log-likelihood.
- Evaluation metrics: MSE/PMSE are scale-dependent and not standard for count models. Please include held-out predictive log-likelihood or perplexity (as in PGDS/DEMs) and, for recommendation-style data, ranking metrics (MAP/NDCG).
- Ablations and baselines: To isolate the value of depth, include (i) a single-layer dynamic PF with an RNN prior directly on θt (i.e., no higher h(n)), and (ii) a depth-1 hierarchical variant vs. the proposed multi-layer hierarchy. Report performance vs. N and window size c. This will substantiate the claim that multiple layers provide additional benefit.
- Comparisons: The LSTM baseline is informative but not definitive since it optimizes a different objective and lacks the PF inductive bias. Please also compare to recurrent Poisson factorization for temporal recommendation and dynamic topic models that use non-Markovian priors where applicable.
- Identifiability and scaling: With Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ all positive and multiplicative, the model has scaling indeterminacies. Consider column-normalizing Φ (or Θ) and absorbing scales, or adding constraints/priors that improve identifiability and interpretability. Report how factors are post-processed for interpretability (e.g., top words for NIPS).
- Complexity/scalability: Maintaining xvtk for all v,t,k is expensive for large sparse matrices. Describe sparse updates and memory/time complexity, and report runtimes for all methods.
- Experimental protocol: Justify choices of K across datasets and provide sensitivity analyses. Ensure hyperparameter tuning parity across baselines (e.g., number of epochs, early stopping) and include standard deviations/CI over multiple runs.
- Clarity/notation: Some definitions are ambiguous (e.g., parameterization of Gamma distributions; exact architecture of ffeed/fback; the role of βh vs βθ). Define all variables once, specify RNN types and hidden sizes per layer, and provide pseudocode for the two-stage training.
- Factual inconsistency: Around lines 86–87 there is a confusion regarding the distribution used to model nonnegative reals. The appropriate construct is the Poisson randomized Gamma (PRG) distribution; please correct the text and cite the proper references.
- Presentation: Table 2 should highlight best (and second-best) results, e.g., boldface. Figures should have readable axes and legends. Numerous grammatical errors and typos hinder readability; thorough proofreading is needed. Examples to fix include: “unit the data,” “Noticing the lack of capturing…,” “PFA factorized a count matrix,” “other data with different pattern long-time dependence,” “Examples for this including,” “shows excellent results on predict and,” “However, it is rough for them,” “V-dimesnion sequentially,” “K-dimension latent variable,” “due to the including of feature-wise,” “recommend systems.”

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The PF component and mean-field updates are technically sound. However, the training of the neural components departs from a standard ELBO; without a principled bound or empirical evidence using log-likelihood metrics, the soundness of the overall optimization is less certain.
- Clarity: The paper is generally understandable but hampered by grammatical issues, some notation ambiguities, and missing details about architectures and training. Improving these would substantially help.
- Originality: Combining a deep temporal hierarchy of RNN-parameterized Gamma conditionals with PF is novel relative to PGDS and deep PF works cited, though related to recurrent PF. The ladder-style refinement is a nice touch.
- Significance: If validated with stronger metrics and ablations, the approach could be impactful for sequential count modeling in text, events, and recommendation. Current evidence is promising but would benefit from the suggested additions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important limitation of dynamic PF and shows consistent empirical improvements on diverse datasets.
- Offers a flexible, interpretable framework that retains conjugacy where possible and leverages deep temporal structure.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of likelihood-based evaluation and ablations makes it hard to attribute gains to the proposed depth/hierarchy.
- The training objective for neural components is heuristic without a clear probabilistic justification.
- Clarity and presentation issues (typos, notation) impede careful assessment.

Overall recommendation
Promising idea and empirical results; strengthening the theoretical justification of the training objective, adding likelihood-based evaluations and ablations, and improving clarity would make this a strong contribution.
