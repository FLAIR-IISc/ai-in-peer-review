This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count data. The core idea is to augment Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with recurrent neural networks to capture both short- and long-term dependencies in the data. The authors employ variational inference for learning the latent variables and parameters of the neural networks. The work builds upon prior successes in Bayesian factor analysis and deep learning, aiming to address limitations of existing dynamic PFA models in modeling long-range dependencies.

The paper is well-motivated, positioning itself within a growing body of work applying deep models to sequential count data. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches like PGDS and temporal Dirichlet processes, particularly their inability to effectively capture long-term dependencies. The proposed DDPFA model is a logical extension of PFA, incorporating neural networks in a principled manner. The use of variational inference is also appropriate given the complexity of the model. The paper demonstrates a solid understanding of related work in Bayesian nonparametrics, deep learning, and count data modeling, referencing key papers in the field (e.g., [1, 2, 4, 8, 9]).

**Strengths:**

*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model appears technically sound, combining PFA with recurrent neural networks in a coherent framework. The variational inference procedure is well-defined, and the loss function formulation for training the neural networks seems reasonable.
*   **Addresses a relevant problem:** Modeling sequential count data is a challenging problem with applications in various domains, including recommendation systems, text analysis, and time series analysis.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of deep learning and PFA for dynamic count data is a novel contribution. The use of recurrent neural networks to model temporal dependencies within a PFA framework appears original.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors evaluate the model on both synthetic and real-world datasets, comparing its performance against several baseline methods (PGDS, LSTM, PFA).
*   **Interpretability:** The authors discuss the interpretability of the latent factors learned by the model, which is a valuable aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Factual Error:** The paper contains a factual inaccuracy on line 86-87, incorrectly stating the model used for modeling non-negative numbers. This should be corrected.
*   **Evaluation Metrics:** While MSE and PMSE are used for evaluation, the authors should consider reporting held-out perplexity, particularly for tasks like topic modeling (NIPS corpus). This would provide a more standard and informative evaluation metric.
*   **Insufficient Baselines:** The comparison with existing methods could be strengthened by including a baseline of dynamic PF with a *single* layer of deep architecture. This would help justify the benefit of multiple layers of hierarchy in DDPFA.
*   **LSTM Comparison:** The comparison with LSTM is informative, but the different learning algorithms and optimization strategies make a direct comparison somewhat difficult. The authors should acknowledge this limitation. The benefit of hierarchical depth isn't clearly demonstrated over a well-tuned LSTM.
*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors and typos, hindering readability. Examples include: "unit the data", "Noticing the lack of capturing ..", "PFA factorized a count matrix", "other data with different pattern long-time dependence ..", "Examples for this including ..", "shows excellent results on predict and ..", "However, it is rough for them ..", "V-dimesnion sequentially ..", "K-dimension latent variable ..", "due to the including of feature-wise", and "recommend systems."  A thorough proofreading is essential.
*   **Table Formatting:** Table 2 lacks clear indication of the best-performing results (e.g., using bold font).
*   **Ladder Architecture Justification:** While mentioning the problem of "pruning", the benefit of the ladder-like architecture needs further explanation and justification.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to modeling sequential count data, addressing a known limitation of existing dynamic PFA models.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   The paper is well-written (despite grammatical errors that need fixing) and provides a clear explanation of the model and its inference procedure.
*   The potential for interpretability of the learned latent factors is a significant strength.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The presence of a factual error and numerous grammatical errors detracts from the overall quality of the paper.
*   The evaluation could be strengthened by including a more comprehensive set of baselines and considering alternative evaluation metrics.
*   The benefit of the deep hierarchical structure is not fully justified, and the comparison with LSTM is not definitive.
*   The “pruning” issue needs further attention and discussion.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed DDPFA model is a novel and promising approach to modeling sequential count data. However, the presence of a factual error and numerous grammatical errors, combined with the need for stronger baselines and more rigorous evaluation, raise concerns. I recommend **weak accept** with the condition that the authors address the identified weaknesses, particularly correcting the factual error, thoroughly proofreading the manuscript, and providing a more robust experimental evaluation. Addressing these issues will significantly improve the clarity, credibility, and impact of the work.
