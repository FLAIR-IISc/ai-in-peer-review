

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors, extending previous work on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and dynamic Poisson models. The authors address the challenge of capturing long-time dependence in time series count data by introducing a deep hierarchical structure where recurrent neural networks (RNNs) represent implicit distributions over latent variables using gamma distributions. This builds on prior work such as Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) [2] and Deep Exponential Families [8], but introduces a novel approach to modeling complex temporal dependencies through a deep architecture that combines information from both prior (higher layer) and likelihood (lower layer) distributions.

The key technical contribution is the use of neural networks to approximate the shape parameters of gamma distributions, with the model's hierarchical structure allowing for the capture of both short-term patterns (through lower layers) and long-term dependencies (through higher layers). The inference algorithm employs variational inference with a mean-field approximation, where neural network parameters are updated based on expectations of latent variables to reduce sampling variance. This approach provides an interesting and smart solution to the long-dependency problem that plagues shallow dynamic models.

The paper demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improved performance over PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA in most experiments. The authors effectively illustrate how their model captures more meaningful long-term patterns in real-world data (e.g., the Israel-Palestinian conflict and six-party talks in the ICEWS dataset), where other models fail to capture the sustained activation of factors.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before acceptance:

1. **Critical typos and unclear exposition**: Equation 10 contains a critical typo (the notation is inconsistent with the preceding discussion), and the manuscript appears rushed with several unclear notations. The description of the model's architecture (particularly the relationship between layers) would benefit from a detailed graphical illustration similar to Figure 1 in Chung et al. (2015).

2. **Lack of justification for key assumptions**: The authors assume fixed gamma scale parameters and update only shape parameters without adequate discussion of this choice. This is a significant limitation, as the scale parameter's role in capturing temporal dynamics is not explored. The authors should provide theoretical justification or empirical analysis of this assumption.

3. **Limited experimental evaluation**: The experiments are restricted to a two-layer model without comparisons to single-layer or deeper architectures. The paper lacks analysis of hyperparameters (window size, number of factors) and fails to demonstrate whether deeper architectures provide consistent improvements. The results in Table 3 suggest that adding more layers may not improve performance, which requires more thorough investigation.

4. **Inadequate related work discussion**: The introduction does not adequately address recent related work in deep Poisson factor analysis, such as Zhou et al. (2016) and Cong et al. (2017), which would help position this contribution more precisely within the field.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper notes "pruning" and "uselessness of adding network layers" as problems but does not adequately explain these phenomena or discuss their implications for the model's design. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of these limitations.

The paper's quality is somewhat compromised by these weaknesses, though the core idea is promising. The clarity could be significantly improved with better organization, clearer notation, and additional graphical illustrations. The originality is moderate - the approach combines familiar techniques in a novel way, but the novelty is somewhat limited by the lack of thorough comparison to related deep Poisson factorization methods.

The significance of the work is promising but not fully demonstrated. While the model shows good performance on the tested datasets, the paper does not convincingly argue why this approach is superior to other methods for practical applications. The claim that the model "provides interpretable latent structure" requires stronger evidence.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting approach to modeling long-time dependence in sequential count data. The core idea has potential, but the current manuscript requires substantial revision to address the technical limitations, experimental shortcomings, and clarity issues. The authors should:

1. Fix critical typos and improve the model's exposition with detailed graphical illustrations
2. Justify the assumption of fixed gamma scale parameters
3. Expand experiments to include comparisons with single-layer and deeper models
4. Analyze hyperparameters (window size, number of factors)
5. Discuss recent related work in deep Poisson factor analysis
6. Provide deeper analysis of the "pruning" phenomenon and limitations

With these revisions, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field of dynamic probabilistic modeling. As it stands, the current version has significant weaknesses that undermine the claimed contributions. I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that the core idea has merit and could be substantially improved with the suggested revisions.
