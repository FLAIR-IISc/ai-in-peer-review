- Presents DDPFA, extending Poisson Factor Analysis with RNNs to model short- and long-term dependencies in sequential count data.
- Uses variational inference and gradient-based learning for neural network parameters.
- Evaluated on synthetic and real datasets, showing competitive performance.
- Positions work within Bayesian factor analysis and deep Bayesian models but lacks detailed comparison to existing RNN-based sequence models.
- Strength: tackles an important problem of modeling complex temporal dependencies in count data.
- Strength: novel combination of PFA and RNNs to capture multi-scale temporal structure.
- Strength: empirical validation across multiple datasets supports effectiveness.
- Strength: overall model structure and inference procedure are reasonably well-defined.
- Weakness: major technical clarity issues—key equations and parameterizations are omitted or unclear (e.g., Gamma parameterization, θ_t generation).
- Weakness: misuse of terminology (“implicit distribution”) and incorrect characterization of explicit Gamma equations.
- Weakness: unusual and under-justified RNN design that ignores same-layer time-step dependencies.
- Weakness: nonsensical or unclear motivation text (e.g., “fanatical data and disaster data”) needing clarification.
- Weakness: ambiguous loss function description and unclear derivation (notably around Eq. 15).
- Weakness: inappropriate evaluation metrics (MSE/PMSE) for a probabilistic model; predictive log-likelihood recommended.
- Weakness: insufficient experimental exploration of latent dimension K; should test much larger K (e.g., ≈100).
- Weakness: weak explanation linking text discussion to Figure 3.
- Weakness: poor writing quality, excessive passive voice, typos, grammatical errors, and inconsistent notation.
- Weakness: supplemental material and figure issues—Equations 11–14 and implementation details belong in supplement; Fig.4 caption unreadable.
- For acceptance: idea is potentially novel and could improve sequential count modeling; empirical results are competitive.
- Against acceptance: pervasive technical/clarity problems, unjustified RNN choice, wrong evaluation metrics, poor writing, and inadequate experiments.
- Overall: potentially interesting contribution but not conference-ready; recommend rejection with encouragement to resubmit after substantial revision.
