This paper proposes a novel deep dynamic Poisson factorization model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model leverages recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions, enabling the capture of complex long-time dependence among time steps. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, showcasing improved performance and interpretability compared to existing methods.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their model and its components. The use of variational inference for estimating latent variables and approximating parameters in neural networks is well-motivated, and the authors provide a detailed explanation of their approach. The experimental results are convincing, and the authors provide a thorough comparison with existing methods.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to capture complex long-time dependence among time steps, which is a challenging problem in sequential count data analysis. The use of recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions is a novel and effective approach to addressing this challenge. The authors also demonstrate the interpretability of their model, showcasing the ability to extract meaningful factors from real-world datasets.

However, there are some areas for improvement. The manuscript appears a bit rushed and could benefit from additional polishing. There are several typos, including a critical one in equation 10, which must be fixed. Additionally, the authors assume fixed gamma scale parameters and only update the shape parameters, which requires more detailed discussion and analysis.

The experimental evaluation is limited, with only two-layer models shown and no comparisons to single-layer or deeper models. The authors also do not provide an analysis of other hyperparameters, such as window size and number of factors. Furthermore, the manuscript could benefit from detailed graphical illustrations of the full model, similar to Fig. 1 in Chung et al. (2015), to improve clarity.

In terms of related work, the authors could benefit from discussing recent deep Poisson factor analysis work, such as Zhou et al. (2016) and Cong et al. (2017), in the introduction. This would provide a more comprehensive overview of the state-of-the-art in the field.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise presentation of their model and its components. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of sequential count data analysis and demonstrates the effectiveness of deep learning approaches in capturing complex long-time dependence.
