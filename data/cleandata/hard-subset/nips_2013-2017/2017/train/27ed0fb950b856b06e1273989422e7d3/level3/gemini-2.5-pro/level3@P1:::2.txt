This paper proposes a new model, the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), for analyzing sequential count data. The core idea is to extend the Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) framework to capture complex temporal dependencies. Unlike previous dynamic models like the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), which assume simple Markovian transitions between latent factors, DDPFA employs a deep, hierarchical structure where Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are used to represent implicit distributions over the temporal latent variables. Specifically, the model uses RNNs to generate the shape parameters of Gamma distributions for the latent factors at each time step, conditioned on a window of latent variables from a higher layer or from previous time steps. Inference is performed using a hybrid approach that combines mean-field variational inference for the latent variables and a gradient-based optimization for the RNN parameters, using a loss function reminiscent of a variational autoencoder.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution of this work is the novel combination of deep learning techniques (RNNs) with Bayesian probabilistic models (PFA) to capture long-range temporal dependencies. Using RNNs to parameterize the priors of the latent variables is an elegant and powerful way to move beyond the restrictive first-order Markov assumptions common in state-space models for count data. This approach has the potential to model much more complex and non-linear dynamics, which is a significant step forward for this class of models.

2.  **Technical Approach:** The proposed inference scheme is clever. By using the expectations of the latent variables (derived from the variational updates) as targets for training the neural networks, the authors avoid high-variance Monte Carlo sampling within the gradient updates for the network parameters. This makes the inference more computationally efficient and stable. The "probability autoencoder" formulation for training the feed-forward and backward-pass networks is an interesting and pragmatic solution for learning the parameters of the implicit distributions.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript appears rushed and would benefit significantly from careful polishing. The notation is dense, and the description of the model's generative process and inference could be made much clearer.
    *   A detailed graphical model illustration (e.g., in the style of Chung et al., 2015) showing the dependencies between variables across layers and time steps (e.g., `h(n)`, `h(n+1)`, `θ`, `X`) is essential for understanding the information flow. The current Figure 1 is too abstract.
    *   There are several confusing statements and potential typos. For instance, in the description of Equation 10, `γh(n)tk` is described as posterior information given by the `(n-1)`th layer, which seems correct. However, the update equation for `γh(N)t` uses information from future time steps (`t+c+1:t+1`), which is contradictory to a generative model. This requires clarification. Is `fback` part of the generative model or only an inference network? This distinction is critical.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Justification:** A key modeling choice appears to be fixing the scale (rate) parameters of the Gamma distributions (`βθ`, `βh`) and only learning the shape parameters via the neural networks. This is a strong assumption that significantly constrains the model's flexibility, as it fixes the variance-to-mean ratio of the latent variables. The paper provides no justification for this choice. A detailed discussion on the implications of this decision and an empirical analysis of its impact are necessary for the work to be considered technically complete.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while showing promising results, are not comprehensive enough to fully support the paper's claims about the benefits of the "deep" structure.
    *   The experiments only seem to evaluate a two-layer model. A crucial ablation study is missing: how does the proposed model compare against a single-layer version (i.e., using an RNN only on the `θ` layer)? This would isolate the benefit of the deep hierarchical structure. Furthermore, results for models with more than two layers should be presented to validate the "deep" aspect. The results in Table 3 are concerning, as they suggest adding layers provides little to no benefit, which undermines a central motivation of the paper.
    *   The analysis lacks a sensitivity study on key hyperparameters, such as the window size `c` and the number of factors `K`. How do these choices affect performance and computational cost?
    *   The comparison to related work could be stronger. The introduction should more clearly position the work against other recent deep Poisson factor models, such as those by Zhou et al. (2016, "Augmentable gamma belief networks") and Cong et al. (2017), to better highlight the specific novelties of the proposed approach.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a highly interesting and novel model for dynamic count data that smartly integrates RNNs into a probabilistic factorization framework. The core idea is significant and could inspire future work in this area.

However, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication. The weaknesses in clarity, the lack of justification for critical modeling assumptions, and the limited scope of the experimental validation are major concerns. The promising results are not yet supported by a rigorous and convincing evaluation.

I believe the core contribution is strong enough to warrant publication after a major revision. I would encourage the authors to significantly improve the clarity of the presentation (especially with a better graphical model), provide a thorough justification for their modeling choices (particularly the fixed scale parameter), and conduct a more comprehensive experimental evaluation that includes ablation studies on the model's depth and sensitivity analyses of key hyperparameters.
