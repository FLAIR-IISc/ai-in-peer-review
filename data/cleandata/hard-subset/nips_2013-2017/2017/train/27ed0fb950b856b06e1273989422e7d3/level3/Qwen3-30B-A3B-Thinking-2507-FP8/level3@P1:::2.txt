

The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a novel model for sequential count data analysis that integrates recurrent neural networks (RNNs) into Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) to capture complex long-time dependencies. Building on prior work like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) and Deep Exponential Families, DDPFA uses RNNs to represent implicit gamma distributions over latent factor scores, enabling hierarchical modeling of temporal relationships. The model employs variational inference with coordinate-ascent updates for latent variables and neural network parameter learning via a loss function derived from variational expectations. Experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disaster, ASP) demonstrate competitive performance in prediction and fitting tasks compared to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA baselines.

**Strengths:**  
The core innovation—using RNNs to model gamma shape parameters for implicit distributions—effectively addresses the long-dependency limitation in shallow dynamic models like PGDS. The approach intelligently combines prior (higher-layer) and likelihood (lower-layer) information through neural networks, as shown in the variational updates (Eqs. 13–14). The empirical results are compelling: DDPFA outperforms baselines on most datasets (e.g., NIPS, EBOLA, ASP), and the interpretable latent factors (Fig. 2) reveal meaningful temporal patterns (e.g., Israel-Palestinian conflict in ICEWS) that PGDS misses. The variational inference framework is technically sound, and the loss function (Eq. 18) cleverly reduces sampling variance by leveraging expectations of latent variables, improving computational efficiency over MCMC.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Critical manuscript issues:** The paper contains significant errors (e.g., Eq. 10 has incorrect indices) and appears rushed. A detailed graphical illustration of the full model architecture (similar to Fig. 1 in Chung et al. 2015) is missing, hindering clarity.  
2. **Unjustified assumptions:** The fixed gamma scale parameters (β=1 for all variables) lack theoretical or empirical justification. This choice impacts model flexibility and requires discussion (e.g., sensitivity analysis).  
3. **Inadequate experiments:**  
   - Only two-layer models are tested; critical comparisons to single-layer (PFA) and deeper architectures (e.g., three layers) are omitted.  
   - Hyperparameter analysis (window size, number of factors K) is superficial (Table 3 shows limited ablation).  
   - The ICEWS prediction failure (Table 2) isn’t analyzed—why does PGDS outperform DDPFA here?  
4. **Missing related work:** Key recent deep Poisson factorization methods (Zhou et al. 2016, Cong et al. 2017) are not cited or contrasted in the introduction, weakening the novelty claim.  
5. **Unaddressed limitations:** The "pruning" issue (latent dimensions becoming noise) is noted but not resolved. The authors attribute it to mean-field variational inference but don’t explore alternatives (e.g., auxiliary MCMC as in [24]), which is a significant gap for a quality assessment.  

**Quality & Significance Assessment:**  
The technical approach is sound, and results demonstrate meaningful progress in modeling long-time dependencies for count data. However, the fixed gamma parameters and incomplete experiments prevent a strong quality claim. The significance is moderate: while DDPFA advances sequential count analysis, the limited scope (only two layers, no deep architecture ablation) and unresolved pruning issue limit its impact. The model’s interpretability (Fig. 2) is a notable strength, but the lack of broader validation (e.g., other datasets with long-term patterns) weakens the significance argument.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Reject.** The paper presents a promising direction but suffers from critical flaws that undermine its scientific contribution. The unaddressed assumptions (gamma parameters), insufficient experiments (no ablation on model depth/hyperparameters), and manuscript errors prevent confident evaluation of the claimed novelty and effectiveness. While the core idea is interesting, the current work is incomplete and lacks the rigor expected for NIPS.  

**Key Fixes Required for Rebuttal:**  
1. Correct all typos (e.g., Eq. 10) and add a clear model diagram.  
2. Justify the fixed gamma scale parameters (β=1) via sensitivity analysis or theoretical rationale.  
3. Expand experiments to include:  
   - Ablation on model depth (1 vs. 2 vs. 3 layers)  
   - Analysis of window size and K on ICEWS/EBOLA  
   - Comparison to Zhou et al. 2016 and Cong et al. 2017.  
4. Discuss pruning and propose mitigation (e.g., auxiliary MCMC).  
5. Cite and contrast with recent deep Poisson factorization work.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel RNN-based implicit distribution for long-time dependence; strong empirical results on most datasets; interpretable latent factors.  
*Con:* Unjustified gamma parameter assumption; limited experiments (depth/hyperparameters); manuscript errors; missing related work; unresolved pruning issue.  

The paper’s potential is evident, but it falls short of NIPS’ quality bar due to incomplete evaluation and technical gaps. A revised version addressing these points would be suitable for consideration.
