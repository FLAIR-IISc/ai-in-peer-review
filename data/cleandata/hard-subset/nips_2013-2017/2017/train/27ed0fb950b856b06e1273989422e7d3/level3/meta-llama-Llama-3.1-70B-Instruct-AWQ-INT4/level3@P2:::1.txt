This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model is based on Poisson Factor Analysis and incorporates recurrent neural networks to capture complex temporal dependencies. The authors use variational inference to estimate the latent variables and optimize the neural network parameters. The model is evaluated on four synthetic datasets and five real-world datasets, and its performance is compared to that of three existing methods: Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), and Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA).

The main idea of the paper is to capture long-time dependence in time series count data using a deep and dynamic structure. The authors argue that existing models, such as PGDS and PFA, have limitations in capturing complex temporal dependencies. The DDPFA model addresses this limitation by using a recurrent neural network to model the implicit distributions of the latent variables.

The paper has several strengths. First, the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of analyzing sequential count vectors and the limitations of existing models. Second, the DDPFA model is well-motivated and has a clear theoretical foundation. Third, the authors provide a thorough evaluation of the model on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, the simplified RNN-like structure used in the model is not fully justified. The authors should provide more explanation for why this structure is sufficient for capturing complex temporal dependencies. Second, the model equations and parameterizations are not fully specified, which makes it difficult to understand the details of the model. Third, the evaluation metrics used in the paper (MSE and PMSE) are not suitable for Poisson Factor models, and the authors should report predictive log-likelihood instead. Fourth, the number of latent factors K used in the experiments is too small, and the authors should try larger values. Finally, the writing quality is poor, with many typos, grammatical errors, and unclear sentences.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a new model that combines Poisson Factor Analysis with recurrent neural networks. While the idea of using neural networks to capture complex temporal dependencies is not new, the specific application to Poisson Factor Analysis is novel. However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work and how the proposed model differs from existing approaches.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to contribute to the field of time series analysis and Poisson Factor models. The proposed model could be useful for analyzing complex temporal dependencies in count data, which is a common problem in many fields. However, the paper needs to be revised to address the weaknesses mentioned above before it can be considered for publication.
