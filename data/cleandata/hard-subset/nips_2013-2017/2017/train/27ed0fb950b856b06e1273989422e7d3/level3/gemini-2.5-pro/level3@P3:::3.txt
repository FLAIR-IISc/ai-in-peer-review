This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count data. The core idea is to leverage a hierarchical, recurrent neural network-like structure to model the temporal evolution of latent factors. This approach is designed to capture complex, long-range dependencies that are missed by traditional dynamic Poisson factorization models, which often rely on first-order Markov assumptions (e.g., PGDS). The model uses a two-stage inference procedure: coordinate-ascent variational inference is used to update the latent variables, while the parameters of the neural networks are learned via gradient descent on a loss function inspired by variational autoencoders. This work relates to previous research in dynamic topic modeling, deep generative models, and variational inference.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Technical Soundness:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and technically sound approach to modeling temporal dynamics in count data. Combining a deep hierarchical Bayesian structure with recurrent neural networks to define implicit temporal priors is a powerful idea. It directly addresses a significant limitation of prior dynamic Poisson factorization models, providing a more flexible and expressive framework for capturing complex, non-Markovian dependencies over time.
*   **Problem Motivation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for such a model. It clearly articulates the shortcomings of previous methods like PGDS in handling long-range dependencies and positions its contribution as a direct solution.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The model shows strong empirical results across a variety of synthetic and real-world datasets. It consistently outperforms relevant baselines, including a standard PFA and the dynamic PGDS model, which supports the central claim that the deep recurrent architecture is beneficial for both fitting and prediction tasks.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity and Writing Quality:** The most significant weakness of the paper is its clarity. The manuscript is hindered by numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward sentence constructions that make it difficult to follow. This detracts from the quality of the technical contribution and requires significant revision. For example, phrases such as "unit the data", "Noticing the lack of capturing ..", "PFA factorized a count matrix", "other data with different pattern long-time dependence ..", "Examples for this including ..", "shows excellent results on predict and ..", "However, it is rough for them ..", "V-dimesnion sequentially ..", "K-dimension latent variable ..", "due to the including of feature-wise", and "recommend systems" should be corrected. A thorough proofreading is essential for publication.
*   **Factual Accuracy:** The paper contains a factual error in lines 86–87. When discussing the modeling of non-negative, real-valued data, the text suggests using a compound Poisson distribution or a Gamma belief network. The more appropriate model in this context is the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution. The authors should correct this statement to ensure the technical accuracy of their related work discussion.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments are a good start, they could be strengthened in several ways:
    1.  **Evaluation Metric:** The evaluation relies on Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Predicted Mean Squared Error (PMSE). For a probabilistic model of this nature, held-out log-likelihood or perplexity is a more standard and informative metric for assessing predictive performance. Including and emphasizing perplexity would make the results more robust and comparable to other models in the literature.
    2.  **Missing Baseline:** To properly justify the benefit of the multi-level hierarchical structure, the authors should include a baseline consisting of a dynamic Poisson factorization model with a *single* deep layer (i.e., one RNN defining the temporal dynamics). This would help isolate the performance gain from the hierarchical depth itself, as opposed to the general benefit of using a non-linear recurrent transition function.
    3.  **Comparison to LSTM:** The comparison with an LSTM is informative but not definitive. The learning algorithms and objective functions are fundamentally different (variational inference on a generative model vs. backpropagation on a predictive loss), making a direct comparison for justifying architectural choices like hierarchical depth difficult. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and be more cautious in their conclusions.
    4.  **Presentation of Results:** For better readability, the best-performing results in Table 2 should be marked in bold.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The technical ideas are strong, but the overall quality is diminished by poor writing, a factual error, and gaps in the experimental validation.
*   **Clarity:** This is the weakest aspect of the paper. The manuscript requires substantial revision to improve readability and fix grammatical errors.
*   **Originality:** The core contribution—a deep recurrent model for Poisson factorization—is highly original and addresses a clear need in the field.
*   **Significance:** The proposed model has the potential to be very significant, offering a state-of-the-art method for analyzing complex sequential count data. However, its impact will be limited unless the clarity and experimental rigor of the paper are improved.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and promising model with strong technical ideas. However, it is not yet ready for publication in its current form. Significant revisions are needed to address the major issues with writing clarity, correct the factual error, and strengthen the experimental evaluation with more appropriate metrics and baselines. If these issues are thoroughly addressed, the paper could represent a valuable contribution to the field.
