## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors. The authors address the limitations of existing Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) methods, particularly their difficulty in capturing long-range temporal dependencies. The core idea is to incorporate recurrent neural networks within a PFA framework to model complex relationships between factors over time, and to leverage deep structure to capture these long-term dependencies. Inference is performed using variational inference and gradient descent. The model is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating good performance in prediction and fitting tasks.

The work builds upon a solid foundation of prior research in dynamic PFA [1, 2] and deep probabilistic modeling [4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12]. The authors correctly identify the shortcomings of traditional PFA methods which often rely on independence assumptions that limit their ability to model complex temporal dynamics. The introduction of recurrent neural networks is a natural progression, mirroring the success of similar approaches in other domains. This paper also appropriately references recent advancements in deep Poisson factor modeling [9, 10, 12] and integrates them into a dynamic framework. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model appears technically sound and logically constructed. The integration of recurrent neural networks into the PFA framework is a well-motivated approach to address the limitations of previous methods. The use of variational inference is a reasonable choice for the inference procedure, given the complexity of the model.
* **Addressing a Relevant Problem:** Analyzing sequential count data is a common problem across diverse fields, including recommendation systems, text analysis, and time series analysis. The limitations of existing methods in capturing long-range dependencies represent a significant challenge, and the proposed model offers a potentially effective solution.
* **Experimental Validation:** The authors present results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed model. The comparison with existing methods (PGDS, LSTM, PFA) provides a valuable benchmark for assessing its performance.
* **Interpretability:** The authors highlight the interpretable latent structure obtained from the model, which is a significant advantage in understanding the underlying dynamics of the data. The example provided in Figure 2 and 3 showcasing factor activation patterns is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Factual Error:** The paper contains a factual inaccuracy. The authors state a Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution is used (lines 86–87), but this does not align with the core principles of the proposed method or standard probabilistic modeling techniques. This suggests a misunderstanding of relevant distributions and necessitates correction.
* **Evaluation Metric:** While MSE and PMSE are common metrics, held-out perplexity would be a more appropriate and widely accepted metric for evaluating the model's predictive performance, especially for count data. Emphasizing perplexity alongside the existing metrics would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The comparison with other methods would be strengthened by including a baseline of dynamic PFA with a *single-level* deep architecture (i.e., a deep PFA without the recurrent component). This would help isolate the benefits of the recurrent structure and justify the added complexity of the proposed model.
* **Comparison with LSTM:** The comparison with LSTM, while informative, is not entirely conclusive. The learning algorithms used for DDPFA and LSTM are different, making a direct comparison difficult. Further analysis is needed to demonstrate the clear advantages of the hierarchical depth offered by DDPFA over LSTM's sequential modeling capabilities.
* **Clarity & Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing (examples provided in the key points). These issues detract from the clarity of the paper and make it more challenging to follow. Careful proofreading and editing are essential.  Phrases like "unit the data," "Noticing the lack of capturing..," and "PFA factorized a count matrix" require significant revision.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not explicitly address the sensitivity of the model's performance to hyperparameter settings. Exploring the impact of different hyperparameters would provide valuable insights into the robustness of the proposed model.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper proposes a novel and technically sound approach to modeling sequential count data.
* The authors address a relevant and challenging problem in the field.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model compared to existing methods.
* The model offers interpretable latent representations, which can aid in understanding the underlying dynamics of the data.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The presence of a factual error raises concerns about the authors’ attention to detail and understanding of the underlying principles.
* The lack of a strong baseline comparison and the limitations of the evaluation metrics weaken the strength of the experimental results.
* The numerous grammatical errors and typos significantly detract from the clarity of the paper.
* The sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter settings is not adequately addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of dynamic count data analysis. However, several weaknesses, particularly the factual error and clarity issues, need to be addressed before the paper can be considered for acceptance. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should carefully address the weaknesses outlined above, correct the factual error, improve the clarity of the writing, and strengthen the experimental validation. A revised version that addresses these concerns would be a significant improvement and potentially suitable for publication.
