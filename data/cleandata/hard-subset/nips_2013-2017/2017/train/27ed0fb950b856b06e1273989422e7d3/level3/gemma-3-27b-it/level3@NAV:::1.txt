## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count data, leveraging a simplified RNN-like structure within a Poisson Factor Analysis framework. The authors employ variational inference and gradient descent to learn model parameters and demonstrate the model’s performance on five real-world datasets. While the idea of combining deep learning with Poisson factorization for dynamic count data is potentially interesting, the paper suffers from significant clarity, technical soundness, and experimental rigor issues, preventing it from being conference-ready.

A key concern lies with the simplified RNN structure (Eq. 4). The authors do not adequately justify why the hidden state *h<sub>t</sub><sup>n</sup>* depends *only* on top-layer memory vectors and not on previous time steps within the same layer. This deviation from a standard RNN warrants more detailed explanation and a discussion of potential limitations. Furthermore, Section 2 lacks critical details regarding model parametrization. The distributions generating *θ<sub>t</sub>* (Eq. 3) and the Gamma parameter in Eq. 4 are insufficiently defined, hindering understanding and reproducibility. The claim of "implicit distributions" is also incorrect; Eqs. 9-10 explicitly define Gamma distributions, and their conditionals are well-known. 

The writing quality is notably poor. The abstract relies heavily on passive voice, and numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., “factorize,” missing articles, misuse of “Dirichlet”) throughout the manuscript detract from clarity. Specific sections, like lines 133-155 concerning the “loss function” in Eq. 15, are unclear and require substantial revision. The reference to “fanatical data and disaster data” (lines 30-31) is vague and needs elaboration. 

The evaluation methodology is also flawed. Using MSE/PMSE is inappropriate for Poisson Factor Models; reporting predictive log-likelihood is crucial for a proper assessment. Moreover, the choice of K=3 latent factors appears insufficient, and the authors should explore larger values (e.g., K≈100) to potentially reveal richer latent structures. The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and Figure 3 is unclear.  Moving the variational update equations (Eqs. 11-14) to the supplement would improve the paper's flow.  Finally, the caption for Fig. 4 is unreadable.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Lack of Clarity:** Significant portions of the paper are poorly written and lack necessary technical detail.
*   **Technical Weaknesses:** The simplified RNN structure requires justification, and the evaluation metric is inappropriate.
*   **Insufficient Experimentation:** The small value of K limits the potential insights from the model.
*   **Poor Writing Quality:** Numerous typos and grammatical errors hinder understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance (weak):**

*   The idea of combining deep learning with Poisson factorization is potentially novel. 
*   The model demonstrates some performance gains on the tested datasets, though not convincingly due to the aforementioned issues.



**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting idea, but its current form is significantly flawed. Substantial revisions addressing the clarity, technical soundness, and experimental design are required before it can be considered for publication. I recommend a strong rejection.
