

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factor Analysis (DDPFA) model that extends Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with recurrent neural networks to capture long-range temporal dependencies in sequential count data. The authors build upon prior work in dynamic Poisson factorization (e.g., PGDS [2]) and deep Bayesian models (e.g., Deep Exponential Families [8]), addressing a key limitation of existing methods: their inability to effectively model long-time dependencies due to shallow temporal structures. The core innovation lies in using a hierarchical neural network structure to represent implicit distributions over latent factors, with variational inference for parameter learning.

**Quality**
The technical content is strong and demonstrates a solid understanding of the problem space. The authors successfully address limitations of prior dynamic PF works by introducing a deep hierarchical structure to model long-range temporal dependencies, which is a meaningful contribution to the field. The model formulation is theoretically sound, and the variational inference approach is well-motivated. However, there are several concerns:
- A critical factual error appears in lines 86-87: the paper states "Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution" is used, but the correct model is actually the Poisson-Gamma distribution (the Poisson Randomized Gamma is not a standard model for count data).
- The experimental evaluation lacks held-out perplexity, which would be a more appropriate metric for count data than MSE/PMSE. Perplexity is standard for topic modeling and count data analysis, and its omission weakens the evaluation.
- The comparison with LSTM is informative but not definitive due to fundamental differences in learning algorithms (variational inference vs. backpropagation through time), which prevents clear attribution of performance gains to the hierarchical structure.
- A crucial baseline is missing: a dynamic PF model with a single-level deep architecture would help justify the benefit of multiple hierarchical levels.

**Clarity**
The paper suffers from numerous grammatical errors and typos that significantly hinder readability. Examples include "unit the data," "Noticing the lack of capturing," "PFA factorized a count matrix," "other data with different pattern long-time dependence," "Examples for this including," "shows excellent results on predict and," "However, it is rough for them," "V-dimesnion sequentially," "K-dimension latent variable," "due to the including of feature-wise," and "recommend systems." These errors make the paper difficult to follow and undermine its professionalism. Table 2 also needs improvement: the best results should be marked in bold to enhance readability.

**Originality**
The paper makes a clear contribution by extending PFA with deep hierarchical structure to model long-range temporal dependencies. While deep Bayesian models (e.g., Deep Exponential Families [8]) and recurrent models (e.g., Deep Poisson Factor Modeling [12]) have been explored, this work specifically addresses the limitation of shallow temporal structures in dynamic PF, which is novel for this domain. The hierarchical RNN approach for modeling temporal dependencies in count data is a meaningful advancement over prior work.

**Significance**
The model has potential significance for applications involving sequential count data (e.g., recommendation systems, text analysis, event prediction). The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance across multiple datasets, and the interpretable latent structure is a valuable asset. However, the significance is somewhat diminished by the missing baseline comparison for hierarchical depth and the suboptimal evaluation metrics.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance**

*Pro:*
- Strong technical contribution addressing a clear limitation in prior work
- Well-motivated hierarchical structure for long-range temporal dependencies
- Good experimental results on multiple real-world datasets
- Variational inference approach is appropriate for the problem

*Con:*
- Critical factual error regarding the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution
- Missing baseline for hierarchical depth comparison
- Incomplete experimental evaluation (lacks held-out perplexity)
- Numerous grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper
- Table formatting issues that reduce readability
- LSTM comparison is not definitive due to different learning algorithms

**Recommendation**

I recommend rejection of this paper in its current form. The core technical contribution is valuable, but the paper contains critical errors and omissions that prevent it from meeting NIPS quality standards. The factual error about the distribution is particularly concerning as it undermines the model's theoretical foundation. The missing baseline for hierarchical depth is a significant methodological gap that prevents proper evaluation of the paper's main claim. The grammatical errors and suboptimal evaluation metrics further reduce the paper's quality.

The authors should:
1. Correct the factual error regarding the distribution
2. Add a baseline for dynamic PF with a single-level deep architecture
3. Include held-out perplexity as a primary evaluation metric
4. Fix all grammatical errors and typos
5. Improve Table 2 formatting by bolding the best results

With these revisions, the paper could be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, however, the technical and presentation issues prevent it from being accepted.
