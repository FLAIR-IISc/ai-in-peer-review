This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count vectors that marries Poisson factorization with deep, recurrent priors. Observations xvt are modeled as Poisson with rate sum over factors θtk φvk λk ψv. Temporal dependence is introduced by defining θt (and higher-layer latent states h(n)t) to follow implicit conditional distributions parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs), which output Gamma shape parameters. A bidirectional “probability autoencoder” is used: forward networks produce prior shapes (ffeed), and backward networks produce “posterior” shapes (fback), both driving Gamma conditionals. Inference alternates mean-field updates for conjugate variables (using Poisson–Gamma augmentation and auxiliary latent counts) with gradient-based training of the RNN parameters via a squared-error loss on expectations, rather than a strict ELBO. Experiments on synthetic data and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, ID, ASP) compare against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA; DDPFA generally improves MSE/PMSE and yields interpretable, temporally persistent factors.

Relation to prior work: DDPFA extends classical Poisson factorization and dynamic PFA/PGDS by replacing shallow Markovian gamma transitions with deep RNN-based priors, akin in spirit to deep exponential families and gamma belief networks for count data, and to recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendations. The idea of using neural nets to parameterize latent conditional distributions has parallels with deep state-space models (VRNN/Deep Kalman Filters), though here tailored to counts with conjugate updates.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The conjugate MF-VI over Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ is standard and correct. The choice to let RNNs parameterize only Gamma shape parameters (with fixed rate parameters for h and θ) is computationally convenient but should be justified more carefully. For counts, the mean and variance are governed jointly by shape and rate; learning only shape may limit flexibility or exacerbate scale non-identifiability among Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ. Please analyze identifiability and the induced mean–variance behavior, perhaps via toy experiments and sensitivity to βh, βθ.
- The “probability autoencoder” objective (Eq. 16–18) is heuristic: it matches expectations via squared error rather than maximizing a principled ELBO (or predictive log-likelihood). This may explain stability but divorces training from a coherent probabilistic objective. A discussion or ablation comparing this loss to a true variational bound (e.g., using implicit reparameterization gradients for Gamma, score-function estimators with control variates, or auxiliary-variable bounds) would strengthen the contribution.
- Some derivations and indexing are unclear or contain errors (e.g., Eq. 10: h(0)tk |Θ,h(1), β,X ∼ Gamma(αh(0)tk + γh(0)tk, βh) appears to conflate prior and posterior contributions; missing indices and inconsistent notation occur elsewhere). The update for xvtk (Eq. 11) is standard, but please ensure symbols align with earlier definitions.

Clarity
- The manuscript reads rushed. It needs careful proofreading to fix typos (e.g., Eq. 10; inconsistent superscripts “shp/rte”; missing indices; “Poisson+(λ)” definition), unify notation (rate vs scale), and standardize time-window indexing and boundary conditions.
- A clear graphical depiction is essential: include a plate diagram for Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ with the deep temporal hierarchy, and a schematic showing the forward and backward RNNs and the windowed conditioning (akin to Chung et al., 2015 style figures for deep state-space models).
- Provide algorithmic pseudocode for the two-stage inference (coordinate-ascent MF updates and NN training), and specify NN details (RNN type, hidden sizes, activations, initialization, normalization).

Originality
- Using RNNs to parameterize Gamma shape parameters for dynamic Poisson factorization is a fresh and appealing design that targets long-range temporal dependencies while retaining conjugate updates. However, the Introduction should position the work more explicitly relative to recent deep Poisson/gamma models (e.g., Zhou et al., 2016; Cong et al., 2017) and recurrent PF variants (e.g., Hosseini et al., 2017), clarifying differences in temporal modeling, inference, and expressivity.

Significance
- Empirically, DDPFA often improves MSE/PMSE and provides interpretable, persistent factors. That said, the evaluation metrics are not ideal for counts; predictive log-likelihood, Poisson deviance, or perplexity (for text) would be more informative. The ICEWS case where PGDS predicts better is interesting—an analysis of when smoothing via transition matrices beats deep priors would be valuable. Scalability appears reasonable on ICEWS and NIPS; report runtimes, memory, and convergence diagnostics.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Conceptually neat: deep, RNN-parameterized Gamma priors for counts; retains conjugacy for efficient MF updates.
- Addresses long-range temporal dependence in a way that standard Gamma chains (PGDS) cannot.
- Demonstrates interpretability of factors and competitive performance on diverse datasets.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Heuristic “probability autoencoder” loss is not a principled variational objective; no guarantee it maximizes predictive probability.
- Limited ablations: only a two-layer setting demonstrated; missing systematic comparisons to single-layer and deeper models, and to alternative window sizes c and numbers of factors K; no analysis of Λ and Ψ’s roles.
- Clarity issues and critical typos; missing detailed figures and algorithmic description.
- Baselines could be stronger/more aligned with count likelihoods (e.g., recurrent Poisson regression, RPF; dynamic topic models for NIPS).

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand related work to include deep Poisson/gamma models (e.g., augmentable gamma belief networks; scalable deep PF), recurrent PF for recommendations, and deep state-space models with count emissions; clarify what DDPFA adds.
- Provide ablations: single-layer vs multi-layer; ladder vs non-ladder; vary c, K; learn rate parameters vs fixing them; include a study of scale identifiability and the effects of Λ and Ψ.
- Use predictive log-likelihood/Poisson deviance; report runtime and complexity. Consider implicit reparameterization gradients for Gamma to train under a proper ELBO, or justify empirically why the squared-error objective is preferable.
- Add a comprehensive figure and pseudocode; fix notation and typos (especially Eq. 10) and polish the writing.

Overall, this is an interesting and promising approach to dynamic count modeling with a thoughtful combination of conjugate structure and deep temporal priors. With stronger empirical analysis, clearer exposition, and a more principled training objective (or a justification thereof), the contribution would be significantly strengthened.
