- Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) to model sequential count data with a deep latent structure and neural implicit distributions.
- Claims to capture both short-term complex relationships and long-term dependencies, building on PGDS and Deep PFA with variational inference.
- Demonstrates results on synthetic and real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disaster, Sheep Population) versus PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.
- Core idea is relevant and potentially interesting for sequential count-data applications like recommendation and text analysis.
- Fundamental flaw: claimed recurrent/deep structure is not truly recurrent—h_t^(n) lacks dependence on previous time steps within the same layer.
- Contradiction: authors claim use of "implicit distributions" but derive explicit Gamma conditionals in Eqs. 9–10.
- Evaluation is inappropriate: uses MSE and PMSE instead of predictive log-likelihood, which is standard for count/Poisson models.
- Experimental design questionable: latent dimension K is unreasonably small (K=3 or 10) for real-world count data where K≈100 is typical.
- Key model components are under-specified: distribution generating θ_t (Eq. 3) and Gamma parameters in Eq. 4 are unclear.
- Several writing issues and unclear phrases (e.g., “fanatical data and disaster data”) lack context and harm clarity.
- Loss function (Eq. 15) and subsection around lines 133–155 are poorly explained and hard to follow.
- Figures and captions (notably Fig. 4) are poorly formatted and difficult to interpret.
- Variational update equations (Eqs. 11–14) disrupt flow and should be moved to supplementary material.
- Ladder architecture justification is weak and not well-evaluated; diminishing returns from extra layers are not explained.
- Novelty is limited: contribution appears to be a simplified neural extension of Poisson factorization without clearly differentiating from prior deep PFA work.
- Strengths: addresses a relevant problem, promising general approach, and shows some empirical gains on NIPS and EBOLA datasets.
- Weaknesses: RNN misconception, improper evaluation metrics, inadequate model explanation, poor writing, and insufficient experiments (small K, no ablations).
- Recommendation: major revisions needed—clarify model structure, correct RNN/implicit-distribution claims, use proper metrics, increase K, improve writing, and strengthen connections to prior work.
