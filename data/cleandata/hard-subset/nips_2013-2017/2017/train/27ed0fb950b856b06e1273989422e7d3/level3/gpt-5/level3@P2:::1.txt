Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count vectors. It factorizes counts via a Poisson likelihood with Φ (loadings), Θ (time-varying factor scores), and additional per-feature and per-factor scalars (Ψ, Λ) to encourage sparsity/smoothness. Temporal dependencies are introduced through a multi-layer “deep” structure where θt depends on a window of hidden states h(0)t−c:t, and higher-layer latent states h(n) are connected by neural networks intended to capture short- and long-range dependencies. Inference alternates between mean-field variational updates for conjugate parts and a separate optimization of neural network parameters via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective. The model is evaluated against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM on four synthetic and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population), using MSE/PMSE as metrics. The authors report improved fit and prediction in several settings and discuss “pruning” and hierarchy usage issues.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on Poisson factorization (e.g., Gopalan & Blei’s hierarchical PF), dynamic count models using Gamma–Poisson constructions and PGDS, and deep latent variable models for counts (DEFs; Gamma belief networks). It also relates to dynamic topic models (Blei & Lafferty), recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendations, and variants that combine RNNs with count likelihoods. The proposed use of neural networks to parameterize Gamma conditionals for temporal dependencies is a natural extension, but similar ideas have appeared in recurrent PF and neural autoregressive topic models. A clearer positioning relative to PGDS, RPF, DEFs (Poisson layers), and DTM would strengthen the contribution.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: modeling sparse, high-dimensional sequential counts is important (text, events, epidemiology).
- Principled count likelihood with interpretable factors; inclusion of per-feature and per-factor scales (Ψ, Λ) is reasonable for shrinkage.
- Empirical gains in several datasets; qualitative discussion of factor interpretability.
- Honest discussion of hierarchical latent variable issues (pruning; limited gains from deeper layers) and an attempt to ameliorate via a ladder-like architecture.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Core model specification is incomplete/ambiguous. Eq. 3 and Eq. 4 do not fully specify distributions or parameterizations. It is unclear whether the RNNs output Gamma shape, rate, or both; βh seems fixed in places. Please give explicit generative conditionals with parameter domains.
- The “RNN” over time in Eq. 4 is not a standard recurrent transition: h(n)t depends on h(n+1) within a local time window, not on h(n)t−1. This is more a top-down temporal smoothing than a true recurrence at the same layer. Please justify this simplified structure and discuss implications for long-range memory vs. conditioning on upper-layer windows.
- The repeated use of “implicit distribution” is inaccurate. Eqs. 9–10 are explicit Gamma conditionals; the implicitness seems to refer to NN parameterization. Please correct terminology and clarify which parts are generative vs. amortized/inference.
- The “Probability AutoEncoder” objective (Eqs. 15–18) is unclear and appears ad hoc. It mixes squared-error reconstruction terms in expectation with KL-like intuition but is not derived from an ELBO. If network parameters affect the generative conditionals, provide a single coherent ELBO (with amortized or structured variational families) and optimize it end-to-end, or clearly justify the two-stage heuristic and its guarantees.
- Evaluation metrics are not appropriate: MSE/PMSE are poor for count models. Please report held-out predictive log-likelihood (Poisson NLL), Poisson deviance, and for text corpora, per-word perplexity; for recommendation-style binary counts, log-likelihood with a Bernoulli–Poisson link or ranking metrics.
- Experiments use very small K (e.g., K=3–10) even for large corpora (NIPS, ICEWS). This likely underestimates capacity and biases comparisons. Please explore larger K (e.g., 50–200) with regularization and model selection.
- Baselines are limited. Include strong count baselines: DTM, DEF (Poisson), Gamma Belief Network, recurrent PF for recommendation, and a non-deep dynamic PF with richer temporal transitions. Also train LSTM with a Poisson/negative binomial loss rather than MSE.
- Several claims are not supported or are unclear (e.g., “fanatical data and disaster data” — presumably financial? please clarify). The discussion around lines 224–232 is not clearly tied to Fig. 3; specify which signals each factor captures and how long-range dependencies are measured.
- Identifiability: Φ, Λ, Ψ are all multiplicative and may be absorbed; without constraints or priors that break symmetry, interpretability and stability suffer. Please discuss constraints (e.g., normalization of Φ) and priors to address identifiability.

Clarity
- Writing requires substantial editing: many grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing; frequent passive voice in abstract; incomplete sentences. Figures (e.g., Fig. 4 caption) are unreadable; Fig. 1 labels are unclear. Provide a careful proofreading pass.
- Notation is overloaded (e.g., using · for multiple aggregations). Define all symbols once, specify shapes/dimensions, and keep consistent subscripts.
- Move the standard mean-field updates (Eqs. 11–14) to the supplement to improve flow; allocate space to model definition and training objective.
- Clearly separate the generative model, the variational family, and the training objectives for NN parameters, with a schematic of data flow.

Originality
- Combining PF with NN-parameterized temporal dependencies is interesting but incremental relative to PGDS, recurrent PF, DEFs with Poisson layers, and neural autoregressive topic models. The claimed deep temporal hierarchy would be more novel if the recurrence operated within-layer and if the training objective were principled.

Significance
- Potentially useful if clarified and strengthened: a scalable, interpretable sequential count model with improved predictive performance would be impactful. As written, unclear modeling and evaluation choices limit confidence in the findings.

Requests to authors and suggestions
- Provide a complete generative model with explicit conditional distributions and NN parameterizations (which parameters, activation functions ensuring positivity, window size c effects).
- Replace the Probability AutoEncoder heuristic with an ELBO that includes NN parameters; or justify and ablate the two-stage approach.
- Use appropriate likelihood-based metrics; include additional strong baselines; scale K; report sensitivity to c, depth, and priors. Ensure fair hyperparameter tuning across methods.
- Clarify the discussion around figures; fix captions/legends; improve readability.
- Address identifiability by normalizing Φ or absorbing Λ/Ψ, or justify their necessity empirically.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem (sequential counts) with an interpretable likelihood.
- Empirical gains reported on several datasets; some qualitative interpretability.
- Interesting attempt to combine deep temporal structure with PF.

Cons against acceptance
- Incomplete/ambiguous model specification; unclear/heuristic training objective.
- Evaluation metrics and baselines insufficient; small K undermines evidence.
- Clarity and writing issues; figures/captions not readable.
- Novelty limited relative to prior dynamic PF and deep count models.

Overall recommendation
Promising direction but the paper requires substantial clarification, a principled training objective, stronger and more appropriate empirical evaluation, and improved writing/figures before it is conference-ready. I recommend rejection in its current form, with the above as a roadmap for a stronger revision.
