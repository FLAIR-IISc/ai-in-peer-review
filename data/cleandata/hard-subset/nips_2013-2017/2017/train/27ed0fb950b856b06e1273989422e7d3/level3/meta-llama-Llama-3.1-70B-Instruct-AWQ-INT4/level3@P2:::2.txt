This paper proposes a novel method for analyzing sequential count vectors, named Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA). The model is based on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and incorporates recurrent neural networks to capture complex long-time dependence among time steps. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets.

The main idea of the paper is to model the implicit distributions over count vectors using gamma distributions, which are represented by recurrent neural networks. This allows the model to capture complex temporal dependencies and relationships between factors. The authors also propose a variational inference algorithm to approximate the latent variables and update the parameters of the neural networks.

The paper is well-organized and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed model, the inference algorithm, and the experimental results. The use of mathematical notation and equations is helpful in understanding the technical details of the model.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to capture complex long-time dependence among time steps. The authors demonstrate this through experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that their model outperforms existing methods such as PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The experiments are limited to a two-layer model, and the authors do not provide comparisons to single-layer or deeper models. Additionally, the analysis of other hyperparameters such as window size and number of factors is lacking. The authors also assume fixed gamma scale parameters and update only shape parameters, which requires detailed discussion and analysis.

The paper could benefit from more detailed graphical illustrations of the full model, similar to Fig. 1 in Chung et al. (2015). This would help to clarify the architecture of the model and make it easier to understand.

In terms of related work, the authors could discuss recent deep Poisson factor analysis work, such as Zhou et al. (2016) and Cong et al. (2017), in the Introduction. This would provide a more comprehensive overview of the current state of research in this area.

Overall, the paper proposes a novel and effective method for analyzing sequential count vectors. While there are some limitations to the paper, the authors demonstrate the potential of their approach through experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets.
