This paper proposes a novel method called Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model is based on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and uses recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to represent implicit distributions over count vectors via gamma distributions. This approach allows the model to capture complex long-time dependence among the count vectors.

The paper provides a clear and concise introduction to the problem of analyzing sequential count vectors and the limitations of existing methods. The authors also provide a thorough explanation of the proposed method, including the use of RNNs to approximate the gamma shape parameters and the update of neural network parameters based on expectations of latent variables.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in capturing complex long-time dependence and improving the performance of sequential count analysis. The comparison with existing methods, such as Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), shows that the proposed method outperforms these methods in most cases.

However, the manuscript appears to be a bit rushed and needs polishing. There are critical typos, such as in equation 10, that must be fixed. Additionally, the authors assume fixed gamma scale parameters and update only shape parameters, which requires detailed discussion and analysis.

The experiments are also limited, as only a two-layer model is shown, and comparisons to single-layer and deeper models are missing. Furthermore, the analysis of other hyperparameters, such as window size and number of factors, is lacking.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel method that combines RNNs with PFA to capture complex long-time dependence. The use of RNNs to approximate the gamma shape parameters is an interesting and smart solution to the long-dependency problem in dynamic models.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of recent related work on deep Poisson factor analysis, such as Zhou et al. (2016) and Cong et al. (2017). Additionally, the authors could provide more graphical illustrations of the full model to improve clarity.

Overall, the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of sequential count analysis and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method in capturing complex long-time dependence. However, the manuscript requires polishing, and the experiments could be improved by including more comparisons and analyses of hyperparameters.
