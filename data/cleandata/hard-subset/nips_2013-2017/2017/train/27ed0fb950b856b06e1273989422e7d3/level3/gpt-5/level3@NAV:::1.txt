This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), which augments Poisson factorization with a deep, RNN-like prior over Gamma latent factors to capture both short-term and long-range temporal dependencies in sequential count data. The model keeps conjugacy at the local layer (Poisson–Gamma), while using neural networks to output parameters of Gamma conditionals for θt and higher-layer states h(n)t. Inference alternates mean-field updates for conjugate blocks with gradient-based training of the neural nets via a reconstruction-style objective. Experiments on four synthetic and five real datasets compare DDPFA with PGDS, LSTM, and PFA. The work relates to Poisson factorization and Gamma–NB processes (e.g., Zhou et al., NeurIPS), deep PF/DEFs (Gan et al.; Ranganath et al.), recurrent PF for recommendation, and PGDS.

Strengths
- Interesting hybrid of conjugate PF and neural parameterization; potentially scalable and interpretable.
- Clear motivation to model long-range dependence in counts beyond first-order Markov PGDS.
- Empirical scope includes several real datasets and synthetic controls; interpretability of factors is discussed.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Eq. 4 is not a true RNN: h(n)t depends only on top-layer memory, not same-layer past states. Please justify this simplified architecture versus a standard temporal recursion within each layer; discuss expressivity and identifiability.
- Section 2 omits crucial parameterizations. Specify the exact distributions: the Gamma shape/rate producing θt in Eq. 3 and the Gamma(s) in Eq. 4 (what are α, β; which are NN outputs; constraints to ensure positivity/stability).
- “Implicit distributions” is a misnomer: Eqs. 9–10 are explicit Gamma conditionals with closed forms. Please correct terminology and explain how the NN enters as a hyperparameter generator.
- Lines 133–155: the “loss function” in Eq. 15 is undefined as a proper objective; the integral is intractable and the surrogate in Eq. 16 lacks a principled ELBO connection. Provide a clear derivation, units, and explain training–inference alternation. Consider reporting the ELBO and/or predictive log-likelihood during training.
- Evaluation metrics: MSE/PMSE are inappropriate for PF; report held-out predictive log-likelihood (or Poisson deviance) at minimum, with proper train/validation/test splits. Include uncertainty estimates.
- Capacity: K is very small (K=3–10). Try larger K (≈100) and report sensitivity; include ablations (no deep layers; no Λ/Ψ).
- Figures and writing: Fig. 4 caption is unreadable; Fig. 1 labels are unclear; many typos (e.g., “high-dimensional,” “factorization,” misuse of “Dirichlet”), passive voice in the abstract, and several incomplete sentences. Clarify the “fanatical data and disaster data” sentence. Lines 224–232 are not clearly tied to Fig. 3.
- Flow: move Eqs. 11–14 to the supplement; provide a self-contained model specification in the main text.

Pros for acceptance
- Potentially useful modeling idea combining conjugacy with learned temporal structure.
- Interpretability of factors and qualitative insights.

Cons for acceptance
- Underspecified model and objective; evaluation metrics are not standard for PF; limited capacity studies; significant clarity and presentation issues.

Overall: promising idea but requires substantial clarification, corrected terminology, stronger likelihood-based evaluation, and expanded experiments before being conference-ready.
