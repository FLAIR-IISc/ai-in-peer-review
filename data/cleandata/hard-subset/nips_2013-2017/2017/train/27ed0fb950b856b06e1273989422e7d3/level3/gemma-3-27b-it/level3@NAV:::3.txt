## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper proposes a novel deep learning approach, the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, for analyzing sequential count data. The core idea is to leverage recurrent neural networks within a Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) framework to capture both short and long-range temporal dependencies. This builds on prior work in dynamic PFA models which often struggle with long-term relationships due to independence assumptions. The authors utilize variational inference for learning the model parameters. 

The work addresses a relevant and challenging problem in sequential data analysis, applicable to diverse areas like recommendation systems and time series analysis. The technical approach is sound, and the attempt to combine the strengths of PFA with the representational power of deep learning is a logical step forward. The inclusion of a deep, hierarchical structure to model long-term dependencies is a clear contribution, distinguishing it from shallower dynamic PF models. The experimental evaluation includes both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating the model’s performance across different scenarios, and comparisons with methods like PGDS and LSTM are insightful. 

However, the paper suffers from several shortcomings. Firstly, a factual error exists regarding the use of the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution (lines 86-87) – it is actually the correct choice for modeling nonnegative numbers. Secondly, while MSE and PMSE are reported, held-out perplexity would offer a more robust and common metric for evaluating predictive performance, particularly in the context of sequential modeling. Table 2 requires formatting improvements; marking the best results in bold would significantly improve readability. 

Crucially, the comparison lacks a necessary baseline: a dynamic PF model *with* a single-level deep architecture. Establishing the benefit gained from the hierarchical depth requires demonstrating improvement over a simpler, yet still deep, alternative. The comparison to LSTM, while informative, is limited by the differing learning algorithms and doesn’t strongly justify the added complexity of the hierarchical approach.

Finally, the manuscript is riddled with grammatical errors and typos (“unit the data,” “PFA factorized a count matrix,” etc.). These significantly hinder clarity and detract from the professional quality of the work. Addressing these is critical.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of PFA and deep learning to model temporal dependencies.
*   Technically sound and addresses limitations of existing dynamic PF models.
*   Demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Factual error in the text.
*   Lack of a crucial baseline comparison (single-level deep dynamic PF).
*   Grammatical errors and typos detract from readability.
*   Reporting of perplexity would strengthen evaluation.



Overall, the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution. However, addressing the identified weaknesses – particularly the baseline comparison and the quality of writing – is essential for it to be considered for acceptance. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to resubmit after thorough revision.
