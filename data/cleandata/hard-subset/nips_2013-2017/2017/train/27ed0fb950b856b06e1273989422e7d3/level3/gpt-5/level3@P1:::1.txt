This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count vectors that augments Poisson factorization with a hierarchical temporal structure parameterized by neural networks. Each observation xvt is modeled via Poisson rates that factor into per-feature loadings φvk, per-time factors θtk, and additional per-component and per-feature scalars (λk, ψv) to encode sparsity and temporal smoothness. Temporal dependence is introduced by a multi-layer latent hierarchy h(n)t, where a forward network supplies prior “shape” parameters for Gamma distributions and a backward network supplies “posterior” information, with a windowed temporal context. Inference alternates between mean-field variational updates for Gamma–Poisson conjugate parts and gradient-based training of the neural networks via a “probability autoencoder” loss. The model is evaluated on four synthetic datasets and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population), compared to PGDS, PFA, and LSTM, reporting MSE/PMSE.

Relation to prior work: The paper is positioned against PGDS and related gamma–negative binomial temporal count models, and connects to deep latent variable models such as Deep Exponential Families, deep Poisson factorization via RBM/SBN, and amortized VI (VAE). Closely related contemporaneous lines include recurrent Poisson factorization for recommendation, gamma belief networks, and deep state space models/Deep Markov Models for sequences, which should be discussed and compared more explicitly.

Strengths:
- A timely attempt to combine Poisson factorization with deep temporal structure, aiming to capture both short- and long-range dependencies.
- Clear exploitation of Gamma–Poisson conjugacy for efficient mean-field updates; the auxiliary count decomposition is standard and appropriate.
- Empirical coverage across diverse datasets; some qualitative analysis of factor interpretability.
- The discussion of “pruning” and the ladder-like architecture shows awareness of pitfalls in hierarchical VAEs.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:

Quality:
- The temporal hierarchy in Eq. 4 is not a true RNN over h(n)t: h(n)t is conditioned on h(n+1) in a temporal window, not on h(n)t−1,... directly. This is closer to a top-down temporal smoothing than a recurrent state model. Please justify this design and compare to alternatives where h(n)t depends on h(n)t−1 (e.g., GRU/LSTM priors or Markovian Gamma transitions).
- Several key distributions are underspecified. Eq. 3 mentions p(θt|h(0) t−c:t) but the exact Gamma parameterization (shape/rate) and dependence on the network outputs are not defined until much later and remain ambiguous. Likewise for Eq. 4, please specify the exact Gamma distributions (e.g., h(n)tk ~ Gamma(αh(n)tk, βh)).
- The term “implicit distribution” is misused. Eqs. 9–10 are explicit Gamma conditionals. If “implicit” is meant to signal that parameters are given by neural networks, clarify this and avoid the established meaning of “implicit model” (no tractable density).
- The “probability autoencoder” objective (Eqs. 15–18) is unclear and appears ad hoc. The integral in Eq. 15 is not a likelihood or ELBO; the subsequent substitution with squared-error terms on expectations mixes generative and recognition networks without a principled bound. Please either (i) derive a valid ELBO with amortized VI, using reparameterization/score-function estimators for Gamma variables (e.g., generalized reparameterization or local reparameterization tricks), or (ii) provide a clear justification and analysis for the proposed surrogate (consistency, bias).
- Identifiability/scaling: introducing both λk and ψv on top of φ and θ exacerbates scale non-identifiability in Poisson factorization (ΦΘTΛΨ). Without explicit constraints (e.g., column normalization of Φ, priors with shape>1, or softmax/simplex constraints), learning can be unstable. Please discuss and add constraints or regularizers.
- Evaluation uses MSE/PMSE on counts, which is not a proper scoring rule for count models and is sensitive to scale. Report held-out predictive log-likelihood (Poisson/NB), per-token perplexity (for text), and for Bernoulli-Poisson tasks, AUC/precision-recall. Include uncertainty calibration if possible.
- Experiments fix very small K (e.g., K=3 or 10) even for high-dimensional datasets (NIPS V=14k). This likely underfits and obscures the benefits of a deep temporal model. Please sweep K to larger values (e.g., 50–200) and report model selection via validation likelihood.

Clarity:
- Several sections are hard to follow. The block around Eqs. 15–18 (lines ~133–155 in the submission) needs a precise objective, variable definitions, and training procedure (what is sampled, what is fixed in each phase).
- The claim about weaknesses on “fanatical data and disaster data” is unclear both linguistically and substantively; please clarify the data properties and why prior methods fail.
- The discussion around lines 224–232 does not clearly connect to Fig. 3. Spell out what signal each factor captures, how it is measured, and how “long-time activation” is quantified.
- Many typos, grammatical errors, and incomplete references detract from readability (see list below). Equations also have typos (e.g., Eq. 7’s parameter vector; check λk and ψv placement). The Bernoulli–Poisson link uses b=1(x>1); it should be b=1(x≥1).

Originality:
- The idea of neural-parameterized priors for count factorization over time is interesting, but related work on recurrent Poisson factorization and deep state-space models should be addressed. The proposed top-down temporal windowing is novel but needs stronger motivation and ablations.

Significance:
- Potentially useful if it demonstrably improves prediction and interpretability on large-scale sequential count problems. As presented, the experimental setup and metrics limit the strength of the claims. Include stronger baselines (e.g., RPF, Deep Markov Models), larger K, and proper likelihood-based evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a precise generative model with full parameterization of all Gamma priors/conditionals, and replace “implicit” with “neural-parameterized.”
- Rework the training objective to a principled ELBO, or clearly justify the surrogate and show ablations demonstrating its benefit over standard amortized VI.
- Report held-out predictive log-likelihood/perplexity; add binary link tasks with AUC/PR where appropriate.
- Increase K and conduct ablations: effect of λ/ψ; window size c; number of layers; ladder vs non-ladder; removing top-down connections; replacing window with true recurrent dependence.
- Compare to recurrent Poisson factorization, gamma belief networks with temporal links, and deep state-space models.
- Provide runtime/scalability, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and code.

Presentation issues and examples:
- Fix Fig. 4 caption (currently unreadable) and improve all figure labels (Fig. 1 arrows and layer indices ambiguous).
- Move derivation-heavy updates (Eqs. 11–14) to the supplement; keep main-text intuition and algorithm sketch.
- Editing: avoid passive voice in abstract; correct “factorize”→“factorization,” “high-dimensional,” article usage, “misused ‘Dirichlet’,” “feature-wise,” “Bernoulli–Poisson,” “International Disaster dataset,” “dimensions,” “structure,” “fanatical”→likely “financial” or “sporadic.”
- Complete references with authors, venues, years; verify numbering consistency.

Questions to authors:
- Why not condition h(n)t on h(n)t−1 directly? Any stability or data-driven reason for the chosen structure?
- How do you prevent scale indeterminacy among Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ?
- What is the exact training schedule between VI updates and network parameter updates? Any convergence guarantees?
- How are K and c selected? Any validation procedure?

Pros for acceptance:
- Interesting direction integrating deep temporal structure with count factor models.
- Uses conjugacy to keep parts of inference efficient.
- Some qualitative interpretability.

Cons for acceptance:
- Unclear and potentially unprincipled training objective; key distributions underspecified.
- Evaluation metrics inappropriate; experiments underpowered (small K, missing baselines).
- Significant clarity and writing issues; references incomplete.

Recommendation: Reject in current form. With a clearer, principled objective, stronger likelihood-based evaluation, larger-scale experiments, and improved exposition, this work could become a solid contribution.
