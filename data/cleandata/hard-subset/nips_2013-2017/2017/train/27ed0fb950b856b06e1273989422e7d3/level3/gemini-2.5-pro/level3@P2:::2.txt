This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a new model for analyzing sequential count vectors. The core idea is to extend the Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) framework to better capture complex and long-range temporal dependencies. Standard dynamic PFA models, such as the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), typically assume a first-order Markov structure on the temporal latent factors. To overcome this limitation, DDPFA introduces a deep hierarchical structure where the conditional distributions of latent factors at each time step are modeled implicitly by Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). Specifically, the shape parameters of the Gamma-distributed latent variables at each layer are functions of the latent variables from adjacent layers and previous time steps, as modeled by the RNNs. Inference is performed using a hybrid approach: coordinate-ascent variational inference is used to update the latent variables, while the parameters of the RNNs are trained by minimizing a loss function inspired by variational autoencoders, which operates on the expectations of the variational distributions.

### Strengths:

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this work is its novel and elegant approach to modeling long-term dependencies in dynamic count data. By using RNNs to parameterize the implicit conditional distributions of the temporal factors, the model moves beyond the restrictive first-order Markov assumption common in previous work like PGDS. This allows it to capture far more complex and non-linear dynamics over time. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of dynamic topic modeling and temporal point process analysis, and it is likely to be influential for future research in this area.

2.  **Pragmatic Inference Scheme:** The inference method is a smart combination of variational inference and gradient-based optimization. By updating the neural network parameters based on the *expectations* of the latent variables rather than on noisy samples (as in many stochastic gradient MCMC or black-box VI approaches), the authors propose a more stable and computationally efficient training procedure. This "probability autoencoder" approach is well-suited for this model structure.

### Weaknesses:

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript appears to be in a preliminary stage and would benefit greatly from thorough revision and polishing. The model description is difficult to follow, which hinders a full appreciation of the contribution.
    *   The explanation of the hierarchical structure and the flow of information is confusing. For instance, the role of the feedforward network (`ffeed`) and backward network (`fback`) in defining the prior and "posterior" information (`α` and `γ` terms) could be explained more clearly. The description of the top layer `h(N)` seems inconsistent with the general structure.
    *   There are potentially critical typos or unclear statements. For example, the text describes `γh(N)t` being calculated from future time steps, which seems contradictory for the top-most, purely autoregressive layer.
    *   **Suggestion:** A detailed graphical illustration of the full model, showing the dependencies between variables across both layers and time (e.g., similar to Figure 1 in Chung et al., "A Recurrent Latent Variable Model for Sequential Data," NIPS 2015), is essential and would significantly improve the paper's clarity.

2.  **Technical Justification:** The paper assumes that the scale parameters of the Gamma distributions for the temporal latent variables (`βθ`, `βh`) are fixed, while the RNNs only model the shape parameters. This is a strong modeling choice that restricts the flexibility of the posterior approximation. The authors provide no discussion or justification for this decision. Its impact on the model's expressiveness and performance should be analyzed and discussed.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** While the results are promising, the experimental section is not comprehensive enough to fully support the paper's claims about the benefits of the deep architecture.
    *   The experiments only report results for a two-layer model. A crucial comparison against a single-layer version of the proposed model (i.e., PFA with a single RNN layer for the temporal dynamics) is missing. This would be the most direct way to demonstrate the value of the "deep" component.
    *   The authors' own finding in Table 3 that adding more layers does not improve performance (and can even hurt it) is an important result that requires a much deeper analysis and discussion, rather than being briefly attributed to "pruning."
    *   The paper lacks an ablation study on key hyperparameters, such as the window size `c` or the number of factors `K`, which would provide valuable insights into the model's behavior.

4.  **Discussion of Related Work:** The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison with other recent deep generative models for count data. While several models are cited, the introduction does not clearly articulate how DDPFA's architecture and inference differ from, for example, the Augmentable Gamma Belief Networks (Cong et al., 2017) or other deep Poisson models (e.g., Gan et al., 2015). Providing this context would help to better situate the paper's contribution.

### Summary of Arguments:

**Pros for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and significant method for capturing complex, long-range temporal dependencies in count data.
*   The integration of RNNs to parameterize implicit Gamma distributions is a clever and powerful idea.
*   The inference strategy is pragmatic and computationally sensible.

**Cons for acceptance:**
*   The paper is poorly written and difficult to understand in its current form.
*   Key modeling choices (e.g., fixed Gamma scale parameter) are not justified.
*   The experimental validation is weak and lacks crucial comparisons and ablation studies needed to substantiate the claims.

Overall, the core idea presented in this paper is highly promising and innovative. However, the execution in terms of presentation, technical justification, and experimental rigor is currently below the standard for NIPS. The weaknesses, particularly the lack of clarity and the insufficient experimental validation, are significant. If the authors can substantially revise the manuscript to address these points, it would represent a strong contribution.
