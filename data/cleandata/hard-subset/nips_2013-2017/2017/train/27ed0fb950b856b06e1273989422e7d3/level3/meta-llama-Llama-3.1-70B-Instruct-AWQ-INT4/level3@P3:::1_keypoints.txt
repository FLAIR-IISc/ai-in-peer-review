- Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) that combines Poisson Factor Analysis with an RNN and variational inference for sequential count data.
- Main contribution: introduces a deep dynamic structure to capture long-term dependencies in sequential count data.
- Empirical claim: shows improved performance on synthetic and real datasets compared to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.
- Need justification for the simplified RNN in Eq.4 where h_t^(n) ignores same-layer previous time steps, which may lose temporal information.
- Provide full model equations and parameterizations (e.g., distribution generating θ_t in Eq.3 and the Gamma in Eq.4) to clarify assumptions.
- Clarify inconsistency: some distributions are claimed “implicit” but Equations 9–10 present explicit Gamma closed-form conditionals.
- Explain the vague remark about weaknesses on “fanatical data and disaster data”: define challenges and mitigation strategies.
- Subsection around lines 133–155 is unclear; explicitly define the loss in Eq.15 and provide derivations/explanations.
- Evaluation metrics MSE and PMSE are inappropriate for Poisson factorization; report predictive log-likelihood at minimum.
- Increase latent factor count K in experiments (e.g., K ≈ 100) to demonstrate scalability and robustness.
- Clarify the discussion in lines 224–232 and explicitly connect it to Figure 3 with more analysis.
- Improve writing quality: reduce passive voice, fix typos/grammar, and rephrase unclear sentences.
- Originality: novel combination of PFA and RNNs, but needs more related-work citations and clearer differentiation from existing methods.
- Significance: addresses an important problem and is promising, but should expand on potential impact and applications.
- Overall recommendation: paper has potential but requires substantial clarification, corrections, and stronger experiments before acceptance.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, demonstrated empirical gains, and tackles a challenging problem.
- Arguments against acceptance: methodological/details omissions, inappropriate evaluation, small K in experiments, and poor writing quality.
