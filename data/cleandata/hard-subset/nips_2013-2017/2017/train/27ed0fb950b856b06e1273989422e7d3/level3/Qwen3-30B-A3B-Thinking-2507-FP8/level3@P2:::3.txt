

# Review for "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

## Summary
This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA) to analyze sequential count vectors, extending Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with deep neural network structures to capture complex temporal dependencies. The authors build upon previous work in dynamic Poisson factorization (PGDS, PFA) and deep latent variable models (Deep Exponential Families, Deep Poisson Factor Analysis), addressing the limitation of shallow models in capturing long-range temporal dependencies. The model incorporates recurrent neural networks to represent implicit distributions across time steps, with a hierarchical structure to capture both short-term and long-term dependencies. Variational inference is used for parameter estimation, with neural network parameters optimized through a loss function derived from the variational distributions.

## Strengths
The paper makes a technically sound contribution that addresses a genuine limitation in prior work. The proposed DDPFA model successfully extends existing dynamic Poisson factorization approaches by incorporating deep hierarchical structures to capture complex temporal patterns that shallow models cannot. The theoretical foundation is well-grounded in Bayesian factor analysis and deep latent variable models, with appropriate references to related work. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates the model's effectiveness, particularly in capturing long-term dependencies where previous methods like PGDS and LSTM struggle. The paper provides a clear explanation of the model's structure and inference procedure, with sufficient mathematical formalization.

## Weaknesses
Several weaknesses require attention before publication:

1. **Technical Error**: The paper incorrectly states "Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution" (lines 86-87) when the correct model should be "Poisson-Gamma" or similar. This factual error undermines technical credibility.

2. **Experimental Evaluation**: The paper relies solely on MSE and PMSE for evaluation, which are not standard metrics for count data analysis. Held-out perplexity would be a more appropriate and stronger evaluation metric for this type of model. The authors should clarify why MSE/PMSE are preferred over more standard metrics for count data.

3. **Comparative Baselines**: The paper lacks a critical baseline: a single-layer deep architecture (without the hierarchical structure) to justify the benefit of multiple hierarchy levels. This comparison is essential to demonstrate that the deep structure provides meaningful improvements over shallow deep models.

4. **Method Comparison**: The comparison with LSTM is informative but not definitive, as the learning algorithms differ significantly. The paper does not sufficiently justify why the hierarchical depth of DDPFA is necessary rather than just a deeper neural network.

5. **Writing Quality**: The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "unit the data," "V-dimesnion," "K-dimension," "due to the including of feature-wise") that make it difficult to read and undermine the paper's professionalism.

6. **Table Presentation**: Table 2 should be formatted to highlight the best results in bold for easier comparison.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- The model addresses a genuine limitation in current dynamic Poisson factorization approaches (long-range temporal dependencies)
- The technical approach is sound and well-motivated
- The experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over baseline methods in several datasets
- The hierarchical structure provides interpretable latent features that capture meaningful long-term patterns (as shown in Figures 2-3)
- The paper provides sufficient mathematical formalization and inference details

**Con:**
- The factual error regarding the distribution type undermines technical credibility
- The experimental evaluation lacks appropriate metrics for count data
- The lack of a single-layer deep baseline makes it difficult to assess the true value of the hierarchical structure
- The writing quality is poor with numerous grammatical errors
- The comparison with LSTM is not sufficiently justified

## Recommendation
This paper presents an interesting extension to dynamic Poisson factorization with a deep hierarchical structure. The core idea is promising and addresses a genuine limitation in current methods. However, the technical error, inadequate evaluation metrics, missing baseline, and poor writing quality require significant revisions before publication.

I recommend **Major Revision**. The authors should:

1. Correct the factual error regarding the distribution type
2. Add held-out perplexity as an evaluation metric alongside MSE/PMSE
3. Include a single-layer deep architecture baseline to justify the need for multiple hierarchy levels
4. Fix all grammatical errors and typos throughout the manuscript
5. Format Table 2 to highlight the best results

With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field of dynamic factorization models for count data. The core idea is strong, and the experimental results demonstrate the potential value of the approach, but the current presentation requires significant improvement to meet NIPS standards.
