- Paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA): a deep, hierarchical temporal extension of Poisson factorization to model long-range dependencies in sequential count data.
- Strength: Novel idea to use a multi-layered deep structure for temporal dynamics in Poisson factorization, potentially capturing more complex patterns than shallow models.
- Strength: Tackles an important problem—modeling high-dimensional, sparse, temporally correlated count data.
- Strength: Evaluated on multiple synthetic and five real-world datasets, showing empirical improvements over PFA, PGDS, and LSTM.
- Major clarity issue: Model and inference are underspecified—key distributions (Eqs. 3–4) and parameterizations (e.g., how RNNs produce Gamma parameters) are missing.
- Major clarity issue: The inference objective ("Probability AutoEncoder") is confusing and inconsistently derived; the connection to VAEs is not rigorously established.
- Writing and presentation problems: Numerous grammatical errors, typos, unclear figures and captions, and vague examples that hinder understanding.
- Technical concern: The claimed "RNN" is misleading—architecture lacks within-layer recurrence and resembles a deep convolutional/temporal structure; this design choice is unjustified.
- Terminology error: Neural nets do not define implicit distributions here; they output parameters of explicit Gamma conditionals—calling them implicit is incorrect.
- Results interpretation is weak: Specific claims about learned factors/topics are not clearly supported or explained relative to baselines.
- Experimental flaws: Using MSE/PMSE is inappropriate for Poisson models; predictive log-likelihood on held-out data is the correct metric.
- Experimental flaws: Small numbers of latent factors (K=3,10) are insufficient for high-dimensional datasets; experiments should include larger K (e.g., 50–100).
- Actionable suggestions: Rewrite Sections 2–3 with full generative and inference equations, rigorously derive the neural loss, correct terminology, justify architectural choices, use predictive log-likelihood, expand K sensitivity, and improve prose and figures.
- Conclusion: Idea is promising but paper is not ready—substantial revisions needed for clarity, technical correctness, and experimental validity; recommendation: reject.
