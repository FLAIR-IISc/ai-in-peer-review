## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper proposes a novel model, the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), for analyzing sequentially observed count vectors. The core idea is to leverage recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to represent implicit distributions within a Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) framework, aiming to capture both short-term and long-term dependencies in the data. The model utilizes variational inference for latent variable estimation and gradient descent for learning network parameters. While the proposed approach is promising and addresses a relevant problem, the manuscript requires substantial polishing and clarification.

**Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of modeling dynamic count data, prevalent in areas like recommendation systems, text analysis, and time series analysis. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing PFA-based methods, particularly their struggle with long-range dependencies. They build upon the foundation of PFA, augmenting it with a deep, recurrent structure to address this shortcoming.  The motivation is well-explained, and the connection to previous work in Bayesian factor analysis and deep generative models is established. However, the related work section feels somewhat incomplete.  Key recent contributions in deep Poisson factor analysis, such as Zhou et al. (2016) and Cong et al. (2017), are missing from the discussion and should be explicitly addressed, especially given their relevance to the proposed model.  Furthermore, a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between utilizing RNNs and other sequence modeling approaches (e.g., Transformers) would strengthen the paper.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of PFA with deep recurrent networks is a novel approach to modeling dynamic count data. The use of RNNs to parameterize the Gamma distributions governing the temporal evolution of latent factors is a clever idea.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The model formulation appears technically sound, building upon established Bayesian principles and leveraging the properties of the Poisson and Gamma distributions. The variational inference approach is a reasonable choice for approximate posterior inference in this complex model.
* **Potential for Interpretability:** The factorization structure inherent in PFA, combined with the potential to learn meaningful representations in the latent space via the RNNs, offers the possibility of gaining insights into the underlying factors driving the observed dynamics. The authors highlight this with their analysis on the ICEWS dataset.
* **Good Experimental Setup:** The paper presents results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, allowing for a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed model. The inclusion of baseline methods (PGDS, LSTM, and PFA) provides a useful point of comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from a lack of clarity in several areas. The notation is occasionally confusing, and the description of the inference procedure could be more detailed. There are also numerous typographical errors (e.g., in equation 10) that detract from the overall quality of the presentation.  A clear, detailed diagram illustrating the entire model structure (similar to Fig. 1 in Chung et al., 2015) is crucial for understanding the model’s architecture.
* **Model Assumptions & Justification:**  A key design choice is the updating of only the shape parameters of the Gamma distributions while holding the scale parameters fixed.  The paper does not offer a sufficient justification for this decision, nor a detailed analysis of its implications. The impact of this assumption on the model's flexibility and performance should be discussed.
* **Limited Experimentation:** The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited. The authors primarily focus on a two-layer model.  A more thorough investigation of the impact of network depth (comparing single-layer, two-layer, and deeper models) is needed.  Furthermore, a sensitivity analysis exploring the influence of other hyperparameters (e.g., window size, number of factors) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Lack of Ablation Study:** It is unclear how much each component contributes to the overall performance. An ablation study comparing different configurations of the model (e.g., without the deep structure, without the RNNs) would help isolate the benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Discussion of Pruning:** The authors acknowledge the “pruning” issue—where some latent dimensions become noisy—but the discussion lacks depth. Connecting this to existing literature on similar phenomena in variational autoencoders and exploring potential solutions (e.g., auxiliary MCMC structures as mentioned) would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The model presents a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to modeling dynamic count data.
* The theoretical formulation is sound, and the proposed inference procedure is reasonable.
* The experimental results demonstrate promising performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The manuscript suffers from clarity issues, typographical errors, and a lack of detailed explanations.
* Key modeling choices (e.g., fixed Gamma scale parameters) are not adequately justified.
* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks a thorough sensitivity analysis.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field. However, the current manuscript requires significant revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. I believe the paper has the potential to be a strong addition to NIPS, but only after substantial polishing and clarification.

**Rating:**  Weak Reject.  I would be willing to reconsider a revised version addressing the concerns raised in this review.
