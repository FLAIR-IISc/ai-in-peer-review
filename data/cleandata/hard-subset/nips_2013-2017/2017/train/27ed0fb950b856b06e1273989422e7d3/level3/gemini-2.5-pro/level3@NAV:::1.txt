This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, a novel approach for analyzing sequential count data. The core idea is to extend Poisson Factorization by incorporating a deep, recurrent-like structure to capture complex, long-range temporal dependencies. The model uses variational inference for the latent variables and a separate optimization step for the neural network parameters. This work builds on the lineage of dynamic topic models and Poisson factorization methods (e.g., PGDS) but aims to provide more expressive temporal modeling through a deep architecture, akin to recent work in deep generative models.

The primary strength of this paper is its promising direction. Combining the interpretability of Poisson factorization with the expressive power of deep recurrent structures to model non-Markovian dynamics in count data is a well-motivated and potentially significant contribution. The evaluation across five real-world datasets demonstrates a serious attempt to validate the model's utility.

However, the paper requires a major revision due to significant weaknesses in its technical clarity, experimental evaluation, and overall presentation.

**Quality and Clarity:**
The paper suffers from a lack of clarity that undermines its technical claims.
*   **Model Specification:** Key details of the model are missing or confusing. Section 2 omits the parameterization of the distributions in Eq. 3 and Eq. 4 (e.g., the role of Gamma). The claim that certain posterior distributions (Eqs. 9–10) are "implicit" is incorrect; they appear to be standard, explicit Gamma distributions. The structure in Eq. 4 is not a true RNN, as a layer's state h_t^(n) only depends on the layer above it, not its own previous state h_{t-1}^(n). This simplification needs strong justification.
*   **Inference and Experiments:** The explanation of the loss function in Eq. 15 is opaque. The evaluation relies on MSE/PMSE, which is inappropriate for Poisson models; predictive log-likelihood is a standard and necessary metric for proper comparison. Furthermore, the number of latent factors used in experiments (K=10) seems too small for high-dimensional datasets like NIPS, potentially limiting the model's performance.

**Presentation:**
The writing quality is poor and hinders comprehension. The manuscript is replete with typos, grammatical errors, and pervasive use of the passive voice. Several sentences are unclear (e.g., the reference to "fanatical data and disaster data"), and figures have unreadable captions (Fig. 4). The discussion in lines 224–232 is disconnected from Figure 3.

In summary, while the central idea is intriguing, the paper is not ready for publication. The authors must substantially clarify their model and inference procedure, justify their architectural choices, and conduct a more rigorous and appropriate experimental evaluation. A thorough revision of the manuscript for clarity and correctness is essential.
