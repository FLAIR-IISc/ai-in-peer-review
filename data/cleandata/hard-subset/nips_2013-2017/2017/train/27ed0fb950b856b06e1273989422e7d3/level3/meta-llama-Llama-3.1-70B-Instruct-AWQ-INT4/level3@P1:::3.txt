This paper proposes a novel deep dynamic Poisson factorization model (DDPFA) for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model extends traditional Poisson factor analysis by incorporating a deep neural network structure to capture long-range temporal dependencies. The authors employ variational inference to learn the parameters of the neural network, which is a significant improvement over traditional methods that rely on MCMC or other approximate inference techniques.

The technical content of the paper is strong, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the limitations of prior dynamic Poisson factorization (PF) works in capturing long-range dependencies. The proposed model addresses these limitations by introducing a hierarchical structure that can learn complex temporal relationships between the count data.

The paper also presents an extensive experimental evaluation of the proposed model on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The results show that the DDPFA model outperforms several baseline methods, including PGDS, LSTM, and PFA, in terms of mean squared error (MSE) and predicted mean squared error (PMSE).

However, there are a few areas that require improvement. Firstly, the manuscript contains a factual error regarding the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution. The authors should correct this mistake to ensure the accuracy of the paper.

Secondly, the experimental reporting could be improved by emphasizing held-out perplexity as a stronger evaluation metric alongside MSE/PMSE. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the model's performance.

Thirdly, the formatting of Table 2 needs improvement, and the best results should be marked in bold for better readability.

Fourthly, the comparison with LSTM is informative but not definitive, as the learning algorithms differ and do not fully justify the hierarchical depth of the proposed model. The authors should consider including a baseline of dynamic PF with a single-level deep architecture to justify the benefit of multiple hierarchy levels.

Lastly, the paper contains numerous grammatical errors and typos that make it harder to read. The authors should proofread the manuscript carefully to correct these errors and improve the overall clarity of the paper.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to modeling sequential count data using a deep neural network structure. The idea of using a hierarchical structure to capture long-range temporal dependencies is innovative and has the potential to improve the state-of-the-art in this area.

Regarding significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the analysis of sequential count data, which is a common type of data in many applications, including text analysis, recommendation systems, and network analysis. The proposed model has the potential to improve the accuracy and interpretability of these analyses, making it a significant contribution to the field.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed model. With some minor improvements in formatting, experimental reporting, and grammar, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of machine learning and data analysis.
