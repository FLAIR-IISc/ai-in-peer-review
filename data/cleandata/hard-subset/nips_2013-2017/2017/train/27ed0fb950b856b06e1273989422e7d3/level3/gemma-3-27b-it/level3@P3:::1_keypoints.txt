- Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) combining Poisson factor analysis with a simplified RNN-like structure for sequential count data.  
- Presentation is weak: crucial equations and parameterizations (e.g., Gamma in Eq.4, θt in Eq.3) are missing, hindering understanding and reproducibility.  
- Authors incorrectly claim some distributions are “implicit” despite explicit Gamma conditionals in Eqs.9–10.  
- Vague and unexplained references to “fanatical data and disaster data” need concrete clarification.  
- Several subsections (e.g., lines 133–155) and Eq.15’s loss function are unclear and insufficiently justified.  
- The simplified RNN architecture (h_t depends only on top-layer memory, not same-layer previous steps) lacks rationale, benefits, or limitations.  
- No explanation whether architectural simplification is for efficiency, gradient issues, or other reasons, making it appear arbitrary.  
- Experimental evaluation is deficient: MSE/PMSE is an inappropriate metric for count data; predictive log-likelihood should be reported.  
- Latent dimensionality K is too small (K=3 or 10); high-dimensional datasets likely require much larger K (e.g., ~100).  
- Results on network depth/width (Table 3) are inconclusive and need deeper investigation.  
- Discussion of Fig.3 is disconnected from the figure and lacks specific, interpretable observations.  
- Minor presentation issues: variational updates (Eqs.11–14) could be moved to supplement, Fig.4 caption unreadable, and Fig.1 mislabeled.  
- Writing quality is poor: frequent passive voice, typos, missing articles, misused terminology (e.g., “Dirichlet”), and incomplete sentences.  
- Arguments for rejection: technical incompleteness, inappropriate evaluation metrics, insufficient architectural justification, poor writing, and weak experimental design.  
- Weak arguments for acceptance: idea of combining deep learning with Poisson factor analysis is promising and some empirical performance appears competitive.  
- Overall recommendation: reject in current form; substantial revisions to technical clarity, experiments, and writing required before reconsideration.
