Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a model for sequential count vectors. Building on Poisson Factorization (PF), DDPFA introduces a deep temporal latent structure where Gamma-distributed latent variables are connected across time and layers via “implicit” conditional distributions parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs). A ladder-like architecture supplies forward (“prior”) and backward (“posterior”) RNNs that output Gamma shape parameters for each layer. Inference proceeds in two stages: (1) mean-field variational inference (MFVI) leveraging Poisson–Gamma conjugacy when RNN outputs are treated as fixed parameters; (2) learning the RNN parameters via a “probability autoencoder” objective that couples a reconstruction loss with a consistency term between forward and backward networks. The model is evaluated on synthetic data and five real-world datasets (ICEWS events, NIPS corpus by year, Ebola time series, International Disasters, Sheep population) and compared with PGDS, PFA, and LSTM, reporting MSE and one-step-ahead PMSE.

This work is related to: dynamic Poisson-Gamma systems and negative-binomial-process factorization (e.g., PGDS; Zhou and coauthors’ BNP PFA variants), deep exponential families and deep PF (Ranganath et al.; Gan et al.), hierarchical Poisson factorization (Gopalan et al.), and recurrent PF for recommendation (Hosseini et al.). The use of neural parameterizations of conjugate conditionals resembles amortized/implicit variational models (e.g., DEFs) and bidirectional/ladder architectures.

Strengths
- Addresses a real limitation of shallow dynamic PF models in capturing long-range temporal dependencies, proposing a principled deep temporal structure.
- Retains conjugacy-based MFVI updates for count/Gamma components, which is computationally attractive.
- Empirical results on both synthetic and diverse real datasets are encouraging, with gains over PGDS and PFA on several tasks and interpretable factors shown qualitatively.
- The discussion of “pruning” and ladder architectures shows good awareness of pitfalls in hierarchical variational models.

Weaknesses
- The “probability autoencoder” objective is not derived as a valid ELBO or bound; it mixes squared-error terms with KL-like intuition without guarantees of consistent inference. No ELBO values or likelihood-based diagnostics are reported.
- Evaluation relies on MSE/PMSE; predictive log-likelihood/held-out perplexity (standard for PF/topic models) is missing, making comparisons to prior NIPS literature difficult.
- Limited ablations: depth/number-of-layers and “single-level dynamic PF with RNN prior” baselines are needed to isolate the benefit of hierarchy versus a shallow RNN-PF.
- Comparison to LSTM is informative but not definitive (different likelihoods and objectives; LSTM trained with MSE, not a count likelihood).
- Notational inconsistencies and grammatical errors impede clarity (examples below). Some equations appear to have typos (e.g., duplicated Φ in condition sets; indicator “x>1” instead of “x>0/≥1” in the Bernoulli–Poisson link).
- Identifiability/scaling issues among Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ are not addressed (could cause unstable learning unless constraints/normalization are imposed).
- A factual inaccuracy: the text conflates the proper mechanism for modeling nonnegative real-valued data via Poisson-compound constructions. The appropriate reference is the Poisson Randomized Gamma (PRG) distribution (Zhou, 2015), rather than the stated formulation.

Quality
- Soundness: The PF/MFVI part is standard and correct, assuming RNN outputs are treated as fixed. The joint training objective for RNNs lacks a principled variational justification, potentially yielding biased posteriors.
- Empirics: Results are promising but would be stronger with predictive log-likelihood, uncertainty, and ablations. No runtime or scalability analysis is provided.

Clarity
- The paper is difficult to read due to numerous grammatical errors, inconsistent notation, and ambiguous terminology (“implicit distribution,” “Probability AutoEncoder”). Figures/tables need clearer labeling; best results should be highlighted; architecture specifics and training details are scarce.

Originality
- The combination of PF with deep, bidirectional RNN-parameterized Gamma conditionals is novel relative to prior dynamic PF and DEFs. However, closer positioning w.r.t. recurrent PF (temporal recommendation) and DTM/PGDS would clarify incremental novelty.

Significance
- If validated with likelihood-based metrics and stronger ablations, the approach could be useful for long-horizon count time series. Current evidence suggests potential but falls short of a decisive advance.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide an explicit ELBO (or lower bound) that includes the RNN parameters, and report ELBO curves? If not, why is the L2 objective justified for probabilistic inference?
- How do you mitigate scaling/identifiability among Φ, Θ, Λ, Ψ? Do you normalize columns/rows or fix certain scales?
- What is the wall-clock/runtime and complexity scaling with V, T, K, layers?
- How sensitive are results to window size c, number of layers N, and hidden sizes?
- Can you report predictive log-likelihood/perplexity on held-out counts (e.g., word tokens for NIPS) and include standard deviations over multiple runs?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: (i) shallow dynamic PF with a single RNN prior; (ii) recurrent PF for recommendation; (iii) DTM when applicable.
- Evaluate with predictive log-likelihood/perplexity and provide uncertainty (mean±std).
- Derive and optimize a principled objective (e.g., variational bound with amortized inference; consider reparameterized Gamma/Weibull or implicit reparameterization).
- Improve table/figure formatting (bold best, consistent units, readable legends). In Table 2, mark best results in bold.
- Fix factual/notation errors and grammar. Examples to correct: “unit the data,” “Noticing the lack of capturing,” “PFA factorized a count matrix,” “different pattern long-time dependence,” “Examples for this including,” “shows excellent results on predict,” “it is rough for them,” “V-dimesnion,” “K-dimension,” “due to the including of feature-wise,” “recommend systems.” Also, use b=1(x>0) or ≥1 for the Bernoulli–Poisson link, and cite PRG for nonnegative real-valued data.
- Clarify architectural details (RNN type, hidden sizes, activations), optimization settings (optimizer, LR, batch size), initialization, and release code.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and plausible modeling idea for long-range temporal structure in PF.
- Encouraging empirical gains on several datasets and interpretable latent factors.

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of a principled variational objective for learning the RNN parameters; no ELBO or likelihood-based evaluation.
- Missing ablations to justify the benefit of depth; limited and partially unfair baselines.
- Clarity, notation, and factual issues.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Confidence: High
