

# Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"

This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count data, extending previous work on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and Dynamic Poisson-Gamma Systems (PGDS). The authors build upon the foundational work of Zhou et al. (2016) and Cong et al. (2017) in deep Poisson factorization, but introduce a novel deep structure using recurrent neural networks to capture complex long-time dependence in sequential count vectors. The model represents the implicit distributions over latent factors through neural networks, with the key innovation being the use of neural networks to approximate gamma shape parameters based on expectations of latent variables. This approach differs from previous methods like PGDS, which use simpler transition structures, and LSTM, which relies on recurrent neural networks for sequence modeling but lacks the explicit probabilistic structure.

**Quality:** The technical approach is generally sound, with a well-structured variational inference algorithm. The authors provide theoretical derivations for the variational updates and demonstrate the model's performance on both synthetic and real-world datasets. However, the paper makes a significant simplifying assumption by fixing the gamma scale parameters (β) and only updating the shape parameters (α), without sufficient justification for this choice. This is a critical limitation, as the scale parameters could be crucial for modeling complex temporal dependencies. The experimental evaluation is limited: the authors only test a two-layer model without comparing against single-layer variants or deeper architectures, and they fail to analyze the sensitivity of performance to key hyperparameters like window size and number of factors. The manuscript also contains several typos (e.g., in Equation 10), which detract from the paper's professionalism.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized but suffers from a rushed presentation. The mathematical notation is sometimes confusing, particularly with respect to the deep structure and the relationship between layers. The manuscript lacks a clear graphical illustration of the full model (e.g., similar to Figure 1 in Chung et al. 2015), which would significantly improve understanding. The explanation of how the neural networks approximate the gamma parameters could be more precise, and the connection between the variational inference and the neural network training could be better articulated.

**Originality:** The core idea of using neural networks to represent implicit distributions for capturing long-time dependence in count data is novel and addresses a real limitation in current approaches. However, the paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from recent work on deep Poisson factor analysis, such as Zhou et al. (2016) and Cong et al. (2017), which also explore hierarchical structures for count data. The authors should more explicitly discuss how their approach differs from these related works and why their specific neural network architecture provides advantages.

**Significance:** The problem of analyzing sequential count data is important across multiple domains (recommendation systems, text analysis, epidemiology), and the authors demonstrate promising results on several real-world datasets. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the restricted experimental evaluation and the lack of comparison to more recent deep learning approaches for sequence modeling. The paper's claim that "deep structure is included to capture long-time dependence" is not fully substantiated with comprehensive experiments showing the benefit of depth versus simpler alternatives.

**Strengths:**
- Provides a clever solution to the long-dependency problem in dynamic count data analysis
- Combines neural networks with probabilistic modeling in an interesting way
- Demonstrates good performance on multiple real-world datasets
- Offers interpretable latent structures that capture meaningful temporal patterns (e.g., in the ICEWS dataset)

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited experimental evaluation (only two-layer model tested, no hyperparameter sensitivity analysis)
- Critical assumption about fixed gamma scale parameters lacks justification
- Manuscript contains typos and requires polishing
- Fails to adequately distinguish from recent related work in deep Poisson factorization
- Lacks clear graphical representation of the model architecture

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem with a novel approach, the limited experimental evaluation, lack of proper justification for key modeling choices, and insufficient differentiation from recent related work prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for a high-quality contribution. The authors should substantially revise the paper to address these concerns before resubmission. Specifically, they should: (1) provide a thorough comparison of single-layer, two-layer, and deeper models; (2) analyze sensitivity to hyperparameters including window size and number of factors; (3) justify their assumption about fixed gamma scale parameters; (4) discuss recent related work more comprehensively; and (5) include a clear graphical illustration of the full model architecture.

The paper presents a promising direction for modeling sequential count data, but the current version does not sufficiently demonstrate its advantages over existing methods or provide enough evidence to support the claims made. With the suggested revisions, this work could become a strong contribution to the field.
