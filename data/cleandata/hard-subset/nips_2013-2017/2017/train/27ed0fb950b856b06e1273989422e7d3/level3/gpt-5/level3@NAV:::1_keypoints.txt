- Proposal: Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) augments Poisson factorization with a deep, RNN-like prior over Gamma latent factors to capture short- and long-range temporal dependencies.  
- Inference: alternates mean-field updates for conjugate Poisson–Gamma blocks with gradient-based training of neural nets that output Gamma conditional parameters.  
- Strength: interesting hybrid combining conjugate PF with neural parameterization, potentially scalable and interpretable.  
- Strength: clear motivation to model long-range dependence beyond first-order Markov models like PGDS.  
- Strength: empirical scope includes four synthetic and five real datasets and discusses factor interpretability.  
- Major architectural concern: Eq. 4 is not a true RNN since h(n)t depends only on top-layer memory, not same-layer past states—justify this simplification versus standard temporal recursion and discuss expressivity/identifiability.  
- Model specification gaps: Section 2 omits exact parameterizations (Gamma shape/rate for θt and γs in Eq. 4), which parameters are NN outputs, and constraints for positivity/stability.  
- Terminology error: “implicit distributions” is incorrect—Eqs. 9–10 are explicit Gamma conditionals; clarify that the NN produces hyperparameters.  
- Objective unclear: the “loss” (Eqs. 15–16) is not a defined ELBO; the integral is intractable and the surrogate lacks principled justification—derive the objective, show units, and explain training–inference alternation.  
- Evaluation metric issues: MSE/PMSE are inappropriate for PF; report held-out predictive log-likelihood or Poisson deviance with proper train/validation/test splits and uncertainty estimates.  
- Capacity and ablations: K is very small (3–10)—evaluate larger K (≈100), report sensitivity, and include ablations (no deep layers; no Λ/Ψ).  
- Presentation problems: figures (Fig.1, Fig.4) and captions are unclear, many typos/misuses (e.g., “Dirichlet”), passive voice, and several incomplete or unclear sentences.  
- Flow and placement: move derivations (Eqs. 11–14) to supplement and provide a self-contained model specification in the main text.  
- Pros for acceptance: promising modeling idea combining conjugacy with learned temporal structure and interpretable factors.  
- Cons for acceptance: underspecified model and objective, nonstandard evaluation metrics, limited capacity studies, and significant clarity/presentation issues.  
- Overall verdict: promising concept but requires substantial clarification, corrected terminology, likelihood-based evaluation, and expanded experiments before conference readiness.
