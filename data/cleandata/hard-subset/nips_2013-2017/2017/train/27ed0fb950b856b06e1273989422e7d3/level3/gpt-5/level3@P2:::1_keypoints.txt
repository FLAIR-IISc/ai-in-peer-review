- Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA): Poisson factorization for sequential count vectors with deep temporal structure and per-feature/per-factor shrinkage scalars (Ψ, Λ).
- Temporal dependencies modeled via multi-layer latent states where θt depends on a window of hidden states and higher layers are connected by neural nets to capture short/long-range dependencies.
- Inference alternates mean-field variational updates for conjugate parts and separate neural network optimization via a “Probability AutoEncoder” objective.
- Evaluated against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM on four synthetic and five real datasets using MSE/PMSE; reports improved fit/prediction in several settings.
- Builds on prior work (Poisson factorization, PGDS, Gamma belief networks, dynamic topic models, recurrent PF); similar NN-parameterized Gamma conditionals have appeared before.
- Positioning relative to PGDS, RPF, DEFs, and DTM is unclear and should be clarified.
- Strength: tackles a relevant problem (sparse, high-dimensional sequential counts) with interpretable count likelihood and shrinkage scalars.
- Strength: shows empirical gains and offers qualitative factor interpretability.
- Strength: candid discussion of hierarchical issues (pruning, limited benefit from deeper layers) and attempt at ladder-like fixes.
- Core model specification is incomplete/ambiguous (Eq.3–4): unclear which NN outputs (Gamma shape/rate), parameter domains, and some fixed parameters like βh.
- The “RNN” is nonstandard: higher-layer dependence within a local window, not true within-layer recurrence; implications for long-range memory are not justified.
- Misuse of “implicit distribution” terminology; generative vs. amortized/inference parts are not clearly delineated.
- The Probability AutoEncoder objective is unclear, appears ad hoc, and is not derived from a coherent ELBO; two-stage heuristic lacks justification or guarantees.
- Evaluation metrics (MSE/PMSE) are inappropriate for counts; should report held-out predictive log-likelihood, Poisson deviance, perplexity, or appropriate ranking metrics.
- Experiments use very small K (3–10) even for large corpora, likely underestimating model capacity and biasing comparisons; larger K should be explored.
- Baselines are limited; missing strong count/dynamic baselines (DTM, DEF Poisson, Gamma Belief Network, recurrent PF) and LSTM trained with count-appropriate loss.
- Several claims and dataset descriptions are unclear or unsupported; figures and discussion (e.g., lines 224–232 vs Fig.3) need clearer ties to results.
- Identifiability concerns: multiplicative factors (Φ, Λ, Ψ) can be absorbed without constraints; normalization/prior choices should be discussed or applied.
- Writing and presentation need substantial editing: grammar, notation consistency, figure readability, and clearer separation of model/variational/training details.
- Suggest moving routine mean-field updates to supplement and reallocating main text to model definition and principled training objective.
- Originality is incremental: combining PF with NN temporal parameterization is interesting but similar to existing recurrent PF and neural topic models; deeper novelty would require within-layer recurrence and principled training.
- Potentially significant if clarified: scalable, interpretable sequential count model would be impactful with stronger empirical support.
- Requests: provide full generative conditionals and NN parameterization details (positivity, activations, window c effects).
- Requests: replace or justify PAE with an ELBO including NN parameters, or provide ablations and guarantees for two-stage training.
- Requests: use likelihood-based metrics, stronger baselines, larger K, sensitivity analyses for c/depth/priors, and fair hyperparameter tuning.
- Requests: fix figures/captions, clarify factor interpretations, and address identifiability via normalization or priors.
- Pros for acceptance: relevant problem, interpretable likelihood, reported empirical gains, and attempt to combine deep temporal structure with PF.
- Cons against acceptance: ambiguous model specification, heuristic training objective, inappropriate evaluation and baselines, small K, poor writing/figures, and limited novelty.
- Overall recommendation: promising but requires substantial revision—principled objective, clearer model, stronger evaluation, and improved presentation; recommend rejection now with roadmap for revision.
