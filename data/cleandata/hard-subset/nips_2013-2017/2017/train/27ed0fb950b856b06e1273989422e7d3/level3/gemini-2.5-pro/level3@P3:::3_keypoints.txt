- Proposal: Introduces Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a hierarchical RNN-style model for temporal evolution of latent factors in sequential count data.
- Inference: Uses two-stage inference — coordinate-ascent variational updates for latent variables and gradient descent for neural network parameters (VAE-style loss).
- Relation to prior work: Connects to dynamic topic models, deep generative models, and variational inference; positions itself vs. first-order Markov dynamic PFAs like PGDS.
- Originality/technical soundness: Novel and technically sound combination of deep hierarchical Bayesian structure with recurrent networks to model non-Markovian, long-range temporal dependencies.
- Problem motivation: Clearly motivates need by highlighting limitations of prior models (e.g., PGDS) for long-range dependencies.
- Empirical performance: Shows strong results on synthetic and real datasets, outperforming baselines including standard PFA and PGDS.
- Writing/clarity: Paper suffers from poor writing, many grammatical errors and awkward phrases; requires thorough proofreading and revision.
- Factual accuracy: Contains an incorrect claim about modeling non-negative real data — should reference the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution instead of compound Poisson or Gamma belief network.
- Evaluation metric: Relies on MSE/PMSE; should include held-out log-likelihood or perplexity as standard probabilistic evaluation metrics.
- Missing baseline: Needs a single-layer dynamic Poisson factorization baseline to isolate benefits of hierarchical depth.
- LSTM comparison: Comparison to LSTM is inconclusive because objectives and learning algorithms differ; conclusions should be more cautious.
- Presentation: Results table should highlight best-performing entries (e.g., bold top results) for readability.
- Overall quality: Strong technical ideas but diminished by poor writing, a factual error, and insufficient experimental rigor.
- Clarity rating: Clarity is the paper’s weakest aspect and needs substantial revision.
- Originality/significance: Highly original with potential significant impact if clarity and experimental issues are addressed.
- Recommendation: Promising work but not ready for publication without major revisions to writing, factual corrections, and strengthened experiments.
