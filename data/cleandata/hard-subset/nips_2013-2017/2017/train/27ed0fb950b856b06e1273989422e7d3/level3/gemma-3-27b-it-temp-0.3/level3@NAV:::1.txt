## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count data. The core idea is to extend Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) by incorporating recurrent neural networks to capture temporal dependencies, aiming to model both short-term and long-term relationships within the data. The model employs variational inference for latent variable estimation and gradient descent for neural network parameter learning. The authors evaluate DDPFA on five real-world datasets, comparing its performance against PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

The motivation for addressing the limitations of existing PFA models (like PGDS) in capturing complex temporal dependencies is sound, and the attempt to leverage deep learning for this purpose is a potentially interesting direction. The application to diverse datasets, including event data and text corpora, demonstrates a broad scope. However, the paper suffers from significant clarity, technical soundness, and experimental design issues.

A major concern is the simplified RNN structure (Eq. 4). The authors fail to adequately justify why `h_t^(n)` depends *only* on the top-layer memory vectors and not on previous time steps within the same layer. This deviates from a standard RNN and necessitates a stronger rationale. Furthermore, Section 2 lacks crucial details regarding parameterizations, specifically the Gamma distribution parameter (Gamma in Eq.4) and the distribution generating θ_t in Eq.3, hindering reproducibility. The claim of "implicit" distributions (Eqs. 9-10) is incorrect; these are explicit Gamma distributions with well-defined conditionals. 

The discussion surrounding "fanatical data and disaster data" (lines 30-31) is vague and requires substantial elaboration. The description of the loss function (around lines 133-155, Eq. 15) is unclear. More importantly, the evaluation metric (MSE/PMSE) is inappropriate for Poisson Factor Models; predictive log-likelihood is essential for proper model assessment. The choice of K=3 (or 10) seems small, and exploring larger values (e.g., K≈100) is crucial. The connection between the discussion in lines 224-232 and Fig. 3 is unclear. The variational update equations (Eqs. 11-14) could be moved to the supplementary material. Fig.4’s caption is unreadable.

Finally, the writing quality is poor, riddled with typos, grammatical errors, and passive voice. Numerous specific errors throughout the paper detract from its readability and professionalism.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Potentially interesting idea of combining PFA with RNNs.
*   Evaluation on a diverse set of real-world datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Significant clarity and technical issues (unjustified RNN structure, missing parameter details, incorrect claims).
*   Inappropriate evaluation metrics.
*   Poor experimental design (small K).
*   Poor writing quality and numerous errors.



**Overall:** This paper presents a potentially interesting idea but requires substantial revision, clarification, and stronger experimental validation before it is suitable for publication at a competitive conference like NIPS. I recommend **rejection** in its current form.
