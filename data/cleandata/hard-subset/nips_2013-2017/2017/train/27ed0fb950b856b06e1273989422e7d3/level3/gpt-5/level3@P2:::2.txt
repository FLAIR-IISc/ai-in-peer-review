Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a generative model for sequential count vectors that combines Poisson factorization with a deep, dynamic latent structure. Counts xvt are modeled as Poisson with rate ∑k θtk φvk λk ψv, where φ, θ are factor loadings and time-varying factor scores, and λk, ψv are component-/feature-level scales. Temporal dependence is introduced via multiple latent layers h(n) whose conditional distributions are modeled implicitly using RNNs; in particular, RNNs parameterize Gamma distributions (primarily the shape parameters) both top-down (prior) and bottom-up (auxiliary posterior), resembling a ladder/autoencoding architecture. Inference alternates between mean-field updates for conjugate parts (using Poisson–Gamma augmentation with latent xvtk) and training the RNN parameters with a “probability autoencoder” loss that matches expectations from prior and posterior networks and encourages reconstruction of Θ (and, in a collapsed variant, X).

The approach builds on Poisson factorization for counts (e.g., Gopalan et al. 2015; Zhou et al. on Beta/Negative Binomial processes and PFA), dynamic gamma-Poisson transitions (PGDS), and deep latent variable models such as deep exponential families and deep Poisson factor models. The dynamic deep structure and the use of RNNs to parameterize Gamma conditionals relate to recurrent deep generative models (e.g., VRNN/Chung et al. 2015) and ladder-style variational autoencoders, adapted here to Poisson factorization. The paper compares against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM and reports improved fitting and forecasting on several synthetic and real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, Ebola, disasters, sheep population).

Strengths
- Novel integration of RNNs with Poisson factorization: using RNNs to parameterize Gamma shape parameters for time-varying factors provides a flexible way to capture short- and long-range temporal dependencies while retaining conjugacy for efficient mean-field updates.
- Clever ladder-style design: combining top-down priors and bottom-up auxiliary posteriors via two RNNs per layer is an interesting and practical solution to the long-dependency problem and to the well-known under-utilization of higher layers in deep latent variable models.
- Computationally pragmatic: training the networks against expectations of latent variables reduces sampling variance and avoids difficult reparameterization for Gamma variables.
- Empirical results: on diverse datasets, DDPFA generally improves MSE/PMSE over PGDS/PFA and performs competitively with LSTM, with qualitative evidence of more persistent/overlapping factor activations.

Weaknesses
- Inference objective is under-specified and potentially ad hoc. The “probability autoencoder” losses (Eqs. 15–18) are not shown to be a lower bound to the log evidence; they mix reconstruction of Θ/X with squared-error matching of expectations from prior/posterior networks. It is unclear what quantity is being optimized globally, whether the procedure is guaranteed to improve ELBO, or how it interacts with the MF updates. A derivation, or at least a discussion of what is sacrificed relative to a joint ELBO (e.g., as in amortized VI), is needed.
- Fixed Gamma rates and updating only shape parameters: the paper fixes β for θ and h and only learns α via RNNs. This choice is central but not justified. It may limit expressivity, impact identifiability, and bias uncertainty. A theoretical or empirical analysis (ablation comparing fixed vs learned rates, impact on fit/prediction, posterior calibration) is important.
- Over-parameterization and identifiability: introducing both λk and ψv alongside φ, θ may create scaling redundancies (ΦΘTΛΨ). Without constraints or priors that break scaling, interpretability and stability can suffer. Please clarify any normalization or shrinkage mechanisms and their practical effects.
- Clarity and polish: the manuscript appears rushed. There are critical typos and inconsistent notation (see below), and core components (e.g., exact forms of ffeed/fback, RNN architectures, windowing) are insufficiently specified for reproduction.
- Limited ablations: although Table 3 is mentioned, the paper largely shows only a two-layer model and does not systematically compare single-layer vs deeper variants, different window sizes c, factor numbers K, or different choices of λ/ψ. A more thorough sensitivity analysis is needed to understand when depth helps and when it prunes/under-utilizes layers.
- Baselines and fairness: consider additional strong PF baselines (e.g., hierarchical/HPF, deep Poisson factorization variants by Gan et al.; augmentable gamma belief networks by Zhou et al.) and modern recurrent PF for recommendations (Hosseini et al.). Report training time and data preprocessing so the comparison to PGDS/LSTM is fair.

Quality
- The model design is sound and the conjugate updates are standard. However, the learning objective for the RNNs is not theoretically grounded, and convergence properties are unclear. Experimental coverage is promising but incomplete (limited ablations; some datasets tiny or idiosyncratic), and claims of interpretability lack quantitative metrics (e.g., topic coherence for NIPS).

Clarity
- The paper is readable but needs significant editing. Derive or reference the complete conditional forms precisely; define all symbols once; provide a full graphical model and a temporal plate diagram (e.g., akin to Fig. 1 in Chung et al. 2015). Detail RNN architectures, hidden sizes, activations, parameterizations of Gamma (shape/rate vs shape/scale), and training schedules.

Originality
- The specific combination—Poisson factorization with time dynamics via RNN-parameterized Gamma conditionals and a ladder-style auxiliary posterior—is novel and nontrivial, even if it draws on known pieces from PGDS, deep PF, and recurrent VAEs.

Significance
- If made rigorous and better evaluated, the approach could be impactful for dynamic count modeling in text, events, and recommendations. The current empirical evidence is encouraging but not yet definitive.

Specific suggestions and questions
- Provide a single, consistent ELBO for the whole model, or clearly argue why the proposed two-stage objective is preferable; if possible, connect Eqs. 15–18 to a bound (e.g., via amortized VI with auxiliary variables).
- Analyze the effect of fixing β (rates) vs learning them. Include ablations.
- Add sensitivity to c (window size), K, number of layers; report pruning statistics; try skip connections or residual RNNs to mitigate pruning.
- Expand related work: discuss deep PF (Gan et al. 2015/2016), augmentable gamma belief networks (Zhou et al., JMLR), and other deep temporal count models. Clarify differences from PGDS and recurrent PF (Hosseini et al.).
- Provide a full graphical illustration (plates over time, layers, latent counts), and a training algorithm box with all updates.
- Include runtime, convergence plots, and seeds; release code if possible.
- Consider quantitative interpretability (e.g., topic coherence for NIPS, event factor labeling for ICEWS).

Typos/notation issues (non-exhaustive)
- Eq. 7: denominator and factors appear to repeat φv; please correct. Also “λ φv” likely a typo.
- Eq. 8: conditioning sets repeat Φ; subscripts for x aggregates (xv·k, x··k, etc.) need precise definitions.
- Eq. 9–10: mixing α and γ for Gamma shapes is unusual; clarify the generative vs auxiliary roles and ensure consistent parameterization (shape/rate). The text says Eq. 10 has issues; please fix.
- Throughout: use consistent notation for expectations ⟨·⟩, digamma Ψ(·), and rate vs scale.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Interesting and potentially impactful modeling idea for dynamic counts.
- Elegant use of RNNs to parameterize Gamma conditionals with top-down/bottom-up interactions.
- Generally strong empirical results across diverse datasets; qualitative interpretability signals.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Objective for training the implicit distributions lacks theoretical grounding; unclear if it optimizes a valid bound.
- Important design choices (fixed rates, over-parameterization) not justified or analyzed.
- Clarity and correctness issues (typos, missing details) hinder reproducibility.
- Limited ablations and baseline coverage; some datasets are small.

Recommendation
Leaning weak reject in current form due to the unclear learning objective and missing analyses, but I am positive about the core idea. With a clearer variational formulation (or stronger justification of the current loss), corrected notation, and more thorough ablations and baselines, this work could be a strong contribution.

Medium.
