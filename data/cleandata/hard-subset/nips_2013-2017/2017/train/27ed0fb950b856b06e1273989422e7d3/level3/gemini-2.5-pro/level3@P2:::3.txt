### **Review of "Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model"**

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), a novel method for analyzing sequential count data. The model extends the standard Poisson Factorization Analysis (PFA) framework by incorporating a deep, recurrent structure to capture complex temporal dependencies. The core idea is to model the priors of the time-varying latent factors using a hierarchy of recurrent neural networks, allowing the model to learn long-range temporal patterns that are often missed by traditional dynamic PFA models which make stronger Markovian assumptions (e.g., PGDS). Inference is performed using a hybrid approach that combines mean-field variational inference for the latent variables with a probabilistic autoencoder-style training objective to learn the parameters of the neural networks. The authors evaluate DDPFA on several synthetic and real-world datasets, showing improved performance in fitting and prediction tasks compared to PFA, PGDS, and LSTM baselines.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a key limitation of existing dynamic count data models: the difficulty of capturing long-range temporal dependencies. The proposed solution—integrating a deep, recurrent neural network architecture to define the implicit temporal priors within a Bayesian PFA model—is a novel and significant contribution. This combination of deep generative models and Bayesian nonparametrics is a promising research direction, and this work provides a strong example of its potential.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed DDPFA model is technically sound. The generative process is well-defined, and the motivation for using a deep structure to overcome the limitations of shallow models is clearly articulated. The inference scheme, while complex, is a reasonable and practical approach for such a model, cleverly combining coordinate-ascent variational inference with a gradient-based method for the neural network components. The paper correctly identifies and builds upon relevant prior work in dynamic topic models and deep generative models.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness across a good range of synthetic and real-world datasets. The comparison with PGDS is particularly valuable, as it highlights the proposed model's superior ability to capture non-local temporal dynamics. The qualitative analysis of the learned factors in Figure 2 and Figure 3 provides useful, interpretable insights into the model's behavior.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance / Suggestions for Improvement)**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from numerous grammatical errors, typos, and awkward phrasing that significantly impede readability and detract from the overall quality of the submission. A thorough proofreading is essential. Examples of problematic phrases include: "PFA factorized a count matrix", "other data with different pattern long-time dependence", "due to the including of feature-wise", and "recommend systems". Furthermore, the presentation of results could be improved; for example, in Table 2, the best-performing result for each metric should be highlighted in bold for easier comparison.

2.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation, while good, could be strengthened in several ways:
    *   **Evaluation Metrics:** For a probabilistic model of count data, held-out perplexity is a more standard and powerful metric for evaluating predictive performance than Mean Squared Error (MSE). The inclusion of perplexity scores would make the comparison to other probabilistic models like PGDS more direct and convincing.
    *   **Ablation Study:** To justify the "deep" aspect of the model, a crucial baseline is missing: a "shallow" version of DDPFA with only a single hierarchical layer. This comparison would directly demonstrate the performance gain achieved by adding multiple levels of hierarchy, which is a central claim of the paper.
    *   **LSTM Comparison:** The comparison to LSTM is interesting but not definitive. The learning algorithms and objectives are fundamentally different (variational inference for a generative model vs. backpropagation for a discriminative predictor). As such, it is difficult to attribute performance differences solely to the model architecture. This comparison does not fully justify the benefits of the proposed hierarchical Bayesian structure over a standard neural network approach.

3.  **Minor Technical Inaccuracy:** There appears to be a minor error on lines 86-87 regarding the extension to non-count real-valued data. The text suggests using a compound Poisson distribution, but the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution is a more common and appropriate choice for this task. This point should be clarified or corrected.

**Overall Recommendation**

This paper presents a novel, technically strong model that makes a significant contribution to the field of temporal count data analysis. The core idea of using deep recurrent structures to model temporal dynamics in a PFA framework is both original and promising. However, the paper is held back by significant issues with clarity and writing, as well as some gaps in the experimental evaluation. The weaknesses are addressable, and I would be willing to raise my score if they are thoroughly addressed. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to undertake a major revision of the manuscript to correct the language issues and strengthen the experimental section as suggested above.
