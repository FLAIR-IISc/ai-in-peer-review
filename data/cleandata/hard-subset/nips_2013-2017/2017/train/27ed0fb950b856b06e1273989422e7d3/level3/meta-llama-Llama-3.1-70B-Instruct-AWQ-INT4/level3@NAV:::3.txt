This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for analyzing sequential count vectors. The model is based on Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and incorporates recurrent neural networks to capture complicated temporal dependence. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on both synthetic and real-world datasets, outperforming existing methods such as Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks.

The technical content of the paper is strong, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their model and its components. The use of variational inference for learning the neural network parameters is well-justified, and the authors provide a detailed derivation of the inference algorithm.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to capture long-range temporal dependencies, which is a challenging task in sequential data analysis. The authors demonstrate this capability through their experimental results, which show that their model outperforms existing methods in terms of mean squared error (MSE) and predicted mean squared error (PMSE).

However, there are some limitations to the paper. Firstly, the authors make a factual error in their description of the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution, which is used for modeling non-negative numbers. Additionally, the experimental reporting could be improved by emphasizing held-out perplexity as a stronger evaluation metric alongside MSE/PMSE.

Furthermore, the authors could improve the formatting of Table 2 by marking the best results in bold. It would also be beneficial to include a baseline of dynamic PFA with a single-level deep architecture to justify the benefit of multiple hierarchy levels.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques, including PFA and recurrent neural networks. However, the authors could provide more context on how their work differs from previous contributions in the field.

The significance of the paper lies in its ability to address a difficult problem in sequential data analysis, namely capturing long-range temporal dependencies. The authors demonstrate the importance of their model through their experimental results, which show that it outperforms existing methods in terms of MSE and PMSE.

However, there are some grammatical errors and typos throughout the paper that need to be corrected. For example, "unit the data" should be "unite the data", and "Noticing the lack of capturing .." should be "Noticing the lack of capturing".

In conclusion, this paper proposes a novel and effective model for analyzing sequential count vectors. While there are some limitations and areas for improvement, the paper demonstrates the significance and originality of the proposed model.
