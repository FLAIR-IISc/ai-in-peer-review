This paper proposes the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model, a novel method for analyzing sequential count data. The core contribution is a deep, recurrent neural network-like architecture built upon Poisson Factorization (PF) to capture complex, long-range temporal dependencies. The model employs variational inference to learn the parameters of the neural networks representing the latent distributions. This work extends prior dynamic PF models, such as the Poisson-Gamma Dynamical System (PGDS), which typically rely on simpler Markovian assumptions and struggle with long-range patterns. The approach fits within the broader trend of integrating deep learning structures into Bayesian probabilistic models to enhance their flexibility and expressive power.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses a significant and well-known limitation of existing dynamic PF models. The proposed combination of a deep hierarchical structure with recurrent dynamics is technically strong and represents a novel contribution to modeling time-series count data. The motivation is clear, and the method is well-situated within the relevant literature. The authors have effectively identified a key weakness in prior art and proposed a technically sound solution.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite its promising direction, the manuscript has several weaknesses that need to be addressed.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from numerous grammatical errors and typos, which significantly impede readability. Phrases such as "unit the data," "Noticing the lack of capturing," and "recommend systems" are examples that require correction. A thorough proofreading is essential. Additionally, for clarity, Table 2 should be reformatted to mark the best-performing results in bold.

2.  **Factual Accuracy:** There appears to be a factual error on lines 86–87. The description of modeling non-negative numbers corresponds to the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution, not the model explicitly stated in the text. This correction is critical for technical accuracy.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup could be strengthened.
    *   While MSE/PMSE are reported, held-out perplexity is a more standard and robust metric for evaluating probabilistic sequence models and should be emphasized for a more comprehensive assessment.
    *   The comparison to baselines is insufficient to justify the model's complexity. A crucial missing baseline is a dynamic PF with a single-level deep architecture. Without it, the benefit of the multiple hierarchical levels is not empirically demonstrated.
    *   The comparison with LSTM, while interesting, is not definitive. The models are trained with different learning algorithms, making a direct comparison of architectural benefits difficult and not fully justifying the hierarchical depth.

In summary, the paper presents a strong core idea but requires significant revision in its writing, factual details, and experimental validation to meet the standards for publication.
