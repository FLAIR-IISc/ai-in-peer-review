

The paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model to analyze sequential count data, extending Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with a deep latent structure using neural networks to capture long-term temporal dependencies. The core idea—employing a simplified recurrent-like structure over latent layers (Eq. 4) to model time dependencies—builds on prior work like PGDS [2] and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis [9,10]. However, the model’s technical execution and evaluation require significant clarification.

**Strengths**  
The paper addresses a relevant problem (modeling time-dependent count data) and explores a promising direction by integrating deep structures into Poisson factorization. The experiments cover diverse datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, etc.), and the interpretability claim (Fig. 2) suggests potential value in real-world applications where multiple topics co-occur over time. The variational inference approach is well-motivated for scalability.

**Weaknesses**  
*Quality*:  
- The model’s architecture is fundamentally flawed. Eq. 4 claims a "recurrent" structure but h^(n)_t depends *only* on top-layer vectors (h^(n+1)_t−c:t), not on prior same-layer time steps. This is not an RNN and lacks temporal recurrence—authors must justify this simplification or correct the formulation.  
- Key model equations are incomplete: The Gamma prior in Eq. 4 is undefined, and the "implicit" distributions (Eqs. 9–10) are explicitly Gamma with closed-form conditionals, contradicting the paper’s claim.  
- Evaluation metrics are invalid. MSE/PMSE are inappropriate for count data; predictive log-likelihood (standard for Poisson models) must be reported.  
- Latent factors K is unrealistically small (K=3 for ICEWS, K=10 for others), ignoring established practices (e.g., K≈100 in topic models [9,12]). Larger K should be tested to assess scalability.  

*Clarity*:  
- Writing is poor with grammatical errors ("factorize" vs. "factorization"), missing articles, and passive voice (e.g., "it is shown that" in abstract).  
- Critical sections (lines 133–155, Eq. 15) are unclear: The "loss function" in Eq. 15 is intractable, and the justification for Eq. 16–18 is insufficient.  
- Fig. 4 caption is illegible; Fig. 1 labels are inconsistent.  
- The "fanatical data" reference (lines 30–31) is undefined and must be removed.  

*Originality & Significance*:  
- The "deep" aspect is overstated. The structure is not hierarchical (per [25]) and lacks true depth—adding layers (Table 3) yields marginal gains, contradicting the claim that deep structure captures long-term dependencies.  
- Results are underwhelming: DDPFA outperforms baselines *only* on synthetic data (Table 1); on real data (Table 2), it fails on ICEWS (PGDS wins) and EBOLA/ASP (PGDS/LSTM fail). The "interpretability" claim (Fig. 2–3) is not rigorously validated.  
- The paper does not advance the state-of-the-art—similar deep Poisson models exist (e.g., [8,12]), and DDPFA’s novelty is obscured by technical flaws.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper has a promising *concept* but is currently **not suitable for acceptance** due to critical technical errors, inadequate evaluation, and poor exposition. The core model structure is misdefined, metrics are invalid, and experiments lack rigor. To be competitive at NIPS, the authors must:  
1. Correct the architecture (Eq. 4) to implement a true RNN or justify the simplification.  
2. Report predictive log-likelihood (not MSE) and test larger K values.  
3. Rewrite unclear sections (Eqs. 9–10, 15–18), fix typos, and clarify Fig. 1/4.  
4. Remove unsupported claims (e.g., "fanatical data," "implicit distributions").  

Without these changes, the paper risks misleading readers about its contributions. The significance is limited by weak results and unresolved technical issues. While the idea merits exploration, the current submission is not a complete or sound scientific contribution. **Reject**.
