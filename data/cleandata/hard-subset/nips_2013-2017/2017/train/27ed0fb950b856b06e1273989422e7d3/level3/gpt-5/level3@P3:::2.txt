Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a probabilistic model for sequential count vectors that augments Poisson factorization with a deep, recurrent latent hierarchy. The key novelty is to represent time-varying factor scores via implicit conditional distributions parameterized by recurrent neural networks (RNNs). Specifically, the latent counts follow a Poisson model with Gamma priors; the conditional posteriors over time are Gamma distributions whose shape parameters are produced by RNNs that aggregate (i) top-down “prior” information from higher latent layers and (ii) bottom-up “likelihood/posterior” information from lower layers within a finite window. Training alternates between mean-field variational inference for latent variables and a “probability autoencoder” objective that updates network parameters using expectations of latent variables, thereby reducing sampling variance and improving efficiency. The paper compares against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM on synthetic and real datasets.

DDPFA relates to deep Poisson factor models (e.g., deep exponential families; deep Poisson factorization with RBMs/SBNs) and deep gamma belief networks, as well as recurrent latent-variable models (e.g., VRNN/Chung et al., 2015). Unlike PGDS, which uses linear transitions, DDPFA uses RNNs over Gamma shape parameters to capture complex short- and long-range dependencies while keeping conjugate Gamma-Poisson structure for efficient VI.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Using RNNs to parameterize implicit conditional Gamma distributions over count factors is elegant and well-motivated for capturing complex long-term dependencies without abandoning conjugacy (for latent-variable updates).
- Clear modeling intuition: Combining top-down (prior) and bottom-up (likelihood) information via α (prior) and γ (posterior) shape terms is a neat and practical design for ladder-style deep latent models on counts.
- Variance reduction and scalability: Updating neural-network parameters from expectations rather than samples is a sensible way to stabilize training and reduce variance/cost compared to reparameterized stochastic gradient VI for Gamma variables.
- Empirical promise: On multiple synthetic and real datasets, DDPFA often outperforms PFA and PGDS in both fitting and one-step prediction, demonstrating usefulness in long-dependency regimes where linear transitions struggle.

Weaknesses:
- Technical clarity: The manuscript feels rushed. There are notational inconsistencies, missing definitions, and at least one critical typo (e.g., Eq. 10’s conditioning/indices). shp/rte vs α/β notation is mixed, some sums (e.g., θ·k) are not consistently defined, and boundary handling for the window c is unclear. The paper needs thorough proofreading and polishing.
- Objective justification: The “probability autoencoder” losses (Eqs. 16–18) are heuristic and not an explicit ELBO. It would help to clarify the connection to established bounds (e.g., VAE/auxiliary inference) or to articulate what is optimized and why it suffices.
- Fixed Gamma scales: The model largely fixes Gamma rate/scale parameters and learns only shapes via the RNNs. This choice can reduce variance but limits flexibility and can confound dispersion control and identifiability with Λ and Ψ. A detailed discussion and ablation are needed (e.g., learning both shape and rate, or parameterizing mean/dispersion explicitly).
- Experimental scope: Only a two-layer configuration is emphasized, and ablations are limited. A principled exploration of depth (single-layer vs 2 vs ≥3), window size c, number of factors K, and other hyperparameters (e.g., βh, βθ) is missing. Comparisons to deeper or recurrent Poisson factor alternatives and to variants with learned scales would strengthen claims.
- Missing figure/diagram: A full graphical illustration of the model (plate diagram plus unrolled prior/recognition RNNs, akin to Chung et al. 2015 Fig. 1) would significantly improve clarity.

Quality:
- The modeling idea is technically sound at a high level, but the training objective needs clearer derivation or justification. Empirical results are positive but not exhaustive; broader baselines and ablations are needed to fully support the long-dependency claims. Some implementation details (optimizer, learning rates, initialization, runtime) are too sparse for full reproducibility.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally understandable but needs careful editing. Please fix typos (esp. Eq. 10), unify notation, define all symbols on first use, clarify the ladder connections (α and γ paths), and detail boundary conditions for the windowed RNNs.

Originality:
- The combination of RNN-parameterized Gamma shapes for Poisson factor dynamics and a ladder-style top-down/bottom-up scheme is new and interesting, distinct from PGDS and earlier deep Poisson/gamma belief networks, and from VRNNs that focus on real-valued data.

Significance:
- If stabilized and better validated, this approach could be useful for practitioners modeling sequential counts (events, text, recommendations). The potential impact is meaningful, though current evidence is somewhat limited by missing ablations and objective justification.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a complete graphical depiction: plate diagram of Φ, Λ, Ψ, Θ, H(n), and an unrolled view of the prior and recognition RNNs across time and layers (cf. Chung et al., 2015).
- Fix typos and notational issues; ensure Eq. 10 and related equations have correct indices and conditionals. Standardize α/β vs shp/rte and define all aggregated notations (xv·k, θ·k, etc.).
- Justify and analyze the fixed-scale choice: discuss identifiability with Λ/Ψ, impact on overdispersion, and whether learning rates improves fit. Provide ablations: shape-only vs shape+rate vs mean/dispersion parametrization.
- Expand experiments:
  - Compare single-layer dynamic model (no deep hierarchy) and deeper variants (3–4 layers).
  - Ablate window size c, number of factors K, and hidden sizes.
  - Include NB-link variants and compare against recent deep Poisson/gamma models with temporal structure.
  - Report wall-clock time, memory, and convergence behavior; show that expectation-based updates reduce variance vs sampling.
- Related work: Strengthen the Introduction with recent deep Poisson factor and gamma belief network literature, e.g., Zhou et al. (2016, Augmentable Gamma Belief Networks), Cong et al. (2017), and other deep count models; clarify differences and advantages.

Minor comments and questions:
- Clarify how boundary times t < c are handled and whether padding/causal masking is used.
- Explain the role of Λ and Ψ vs ΦΘ in identifiability; are there constraints or normalizations?
- Provide pseudocode for the alternating MF-VI and network updates; specify optimizer, batch sizes, and stopping criteria.
- Consider reporting calibration (e.g., predictive log-likelihood) in addition to MSE/PMSE.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and clever mechanism to capture long-term dependencies in counts via RNN-parameterized Gamma shapes.
- Maintains conjugate updates for latent variables; efficient and scalable.
- Promising empirical results and interpretable factors.

Cons against acceptance:
- Heuristic objective without clear variational bound; limited theoretical grounding.
- Insufficient ablations and limited experimental scope.
- Clarity issues, critical typos, and missing figures hinder understanding and reproducibility.
- Fixed-scale assumption not analyzed.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept if the authors can fix clarity/typos, add a comprehensive model diagram, and include key ablations (depth, window size, shape vs rate), or otherwise weak reject. The core idea is compelling and deserves further development and evaluation.
