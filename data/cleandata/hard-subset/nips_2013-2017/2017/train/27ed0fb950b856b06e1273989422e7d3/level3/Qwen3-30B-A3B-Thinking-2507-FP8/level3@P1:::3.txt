

The paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a novel model for analyzing sequential count data by integrating Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) with deep recurrent structures to capture long-range temporal dependencies. Building on prior work like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis, DDPFA uses neural networks to model implicit distributions across hierarchical latent layers, enabling more flexible representation of complex time patterns than shallow approaches. The core innovation lies in its deep architecture (via recurrent neural networks) for capturing both short-term local relationships and long-term dependencies, with variational inference for scalable learning.  

**Strengths.** The technical contribution is strong: the model effectively addresses a key limitation of prior dynamic PF methods (e.g., PGDS) in modeling long-range temporal dependence due to their shallow structure. The variational inference framework is well-motivated and implemented consistently, with clear derivations of conjugate posteriors and neural network-based parameter updates. Experiments demonstrate clear advantages over PGDS, PFA, and LSTM on synthetic datasets (SDS1–4) and real-world data (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, ID, ASP), particularly in capturing interpretable long-term factor activations (e.g., sustained Israel-Palestinian conflict patterns in ICEWS). The interpretability of learned factors is a significant practical contribution, as shown in Figures 2–3. The model’s performance on high-dimensional, sparse data (e.g., NIPS corpus, V=14,036 features) further underscores its applicability.  

**Weaknesses.** The paper contains critical technical errors that undermine its validity. Line 86–87 incorrectly states the model uses a "Poisson Randomized Gamma" distribution, but the actual mechanism relies on Gamma-Poisson conjugacy (standard in PFA literature; see [13–16, 23]). This mischaracterization misrepresents the model’s foundation and must be corrected. The experimental evaluation is also suboptimal: reporting only MSE/PMSE (not held-out perplexity, a standard metric for count data) weakens the significance claim. The comparison with LSTM is informative but incomplete—LSTM’s lack of hierarchical depth is not the sole reason for its inferiority, and a single-layer deep PF baseline is missing to justify the multi-layer design (e.g., Table 3 shows minimal gains from extra layers). Table 2’s formatting lacks bolded best results, reducing readability. Numerous grammatical errors ("V-dimesnion," "unit the data," "due to the including of") impair clarity and must be fixed.  

**Originality & Significance.** The work is original in applying deep hierarchical structures to dynamic Poisson factorization, distinct from prior shallow models (PGDS) or non-temporal deep PF variants (e.g., [9, 10]). It advances the state of the art by enabling interpretable long-range dependency modeling in count data, with clear applications in text analysis (NIPS), disaster tracking (EBOLA), and event prediction (ICEWS). The significance is reinforced by the model’s practical performance on real-world datasets where PGDS fails (e.g., EBOLA’s complex patterns). However, the significance is partially offset by the ICEWS prediction weakness (Table 2) and the unexplained "pruning" issue (Table 3), where latent dimensions capture noise.  

**Quality & Clarity.** The technical quality is high (sound inference, thorough experiments), but the Gamma distribution error is a serious flaw. The writing requires substantial editing to fix grammatical errors and improve flow (e.g., "Noticing the lack of capturing..." → "The lack of capturing..."). The model description (Section 2) is precise, but Section 3’s variational inference details are overly dense without clear motivation for key choices (e.g., Gamma priors over Dirichlet).  

**Recommendation.** The paper presents a technically sound, novel approach to dynamic count data analysis with strong empirical results. However, the factual error in Section 1 (line 86–87), the suboptimal evaluation metrics, and grammatical issues must be addressed. The missing single-layer baseline and insufficient justification for depth (beyond Table 3’s limited analysis) weaken the contribution. With revisions to correct the Gamma distribution misstatement, add perplexity metrics, include a single-layer deep PF baseline, and fix language errors, this work would be a strong candidate for NIPS. As-is, the technical flaws and evaluation gaps prevent acceptance.  

**Pro/Con Summary.**  
*Pro:* Novel deep architecture for long-range temporal dependence; strong empirical results on diverse datasets; interpretable factors; technically rigorous inference.  
*Con:* Critical Gamma distribution error; insufficient evaluation metrics; missing single-layer baseline; grammatical errors; unexplained "pruning" issue.
