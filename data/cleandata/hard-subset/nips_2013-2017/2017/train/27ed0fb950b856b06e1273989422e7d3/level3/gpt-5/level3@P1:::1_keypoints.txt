- Proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA): Poisson factorization with a hierarchical, neural-parameterized temporal structure for sequential count vectors.
- Observation model: x_vt ~ Poisson with rates factorized into φ_vk, θ_tk and sparsity/scale scalars λ_k, ψ_v.
- Temporal dependence via multi-layer latent hierarchy h^(n)_t with top-down neural networks using a temporal window for prior/posterior parameterization.
- Inference alternates mean-field variational updates for Gamma–Poisson parts and gradient-based training of neural networks using a “probability autoencoder” surrogate loss.
- Evaluated on four synthetic and five real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS, EBOLA, International Disasters, Annual Sheep Population) against PGDS, PFA, and LSTM using MSE/PMSE.
- Paper positioned relative to PGDS, gamma–negative binomial models, Deep Exponential Families, RBM/SBN Poisson factorization, amortized VI, and deep state-space models.
- Related work (recurrent Poisson factorization, gamma belief networks, Deep Markov Models) needs more explicit discussion and comparison.
- Strength: timely integration of Poisson factorization with deep temporal structure to capture short- and long-range dependencies.
- Strength: leverages Gamma–Poisson conjugacy and auxiliary count decomposition for efficient mean-field updates.
- Strength: diverse empirical datasets and some qualitative factor interpretability.
- Strength: awareness of hierarchical VAE pitfalls shown by pruning and ladder-like architecture discussion.
- Weakness: temporal hierarchy is not a true RNN; structure is top-down smoothing rather than recurrent—needs justification and comparison to Markovian/GRU/LSTM alternatives.
- Weakness: several key distributions and Gamma parameterizations are underspecified and ambiguous in the text.
- Weakness: misuse of the term “implicit distribution”; neural-parameterized conditionals should be clearly described.
- Weakness: the “probability autoencoder” objective is unclear, ad hoc, and not a principled ELBO; requires derivation or justification.
- Weakness: scale/identifiability issues with φ, θ, λ, ψ are not addressed; constraints or regularizers are needed.
- Weakness: evaluation metrics (MSE/PMSE) are inappropriate for count models; held-out predictive likelihood, perplexity, and classification metrics should be reported.
- Weakness: experiments use very small K (e.g., 3–10) for high-dimensional data, likely underfitting; K should be swept to larger values (50–200).
- Clarity issue: sections around Eqs. 15–18 are hard to follow; objectives, variable definitions, and training schedule need precision.
- Clarity issue: unclear language on specific dataset weaknesses and connection between text and figures (e.g., Fig. 3 signal interpretation).
- Writing issue: many typos, grammatical errors, unreadable figure captions, and incomplete references detract from readability.
- Originality: neural-parameterized temporal priors are interesting but related literature must be addressed; the top-down windowing is novel but under-motivated.
- Significance: potential value if predictive performance and interpretability improve on large-scale tasks, but current experiments and metrics limit claims.
- Suggestions: provide a precise generative model, replace “implicit” with “neural-parameterized,” and fully specify Gamma conditionals.
- Suggestions: rework training objective to a principled ELBO or justify the surrogate and show ablations comparing methods.
- Suggestions: report held-out log-likelihood/perplexity, add binary link tasks with AUC/PR, and include uncertainty calibration.
- Suggestions: increase K, run ablations (λ/ψ, window size c, layers, top-down vs non-top-down, recurrent dependence), and add stronger baselines (RPF, DMM).
- Suggestions: provide runtime/scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, code, and a validation procedure for selecting K and c.
- Presentation fixes: improve figure captions/labels, move derivations to supplement, fix equation typos, and thoroughly edit text and references.
- Questions for authors: why not condition h^(n)_t on h^(n)_(t−1) directly; how is scale indeterminacy prevented; what is the exact training schedule and convergence behavior; how are K and c selected?
- Pros for acceptance: interesting direction, uses conjugacy for efficiency, some interpretability.
- Cons for acceptance: unclear/principled training objective, underspecified distributions, inappropriate evaluation, underpowered experiments, and poor exposition.
- Recommendation: reject in current form; suggest resubmission after principled objective, likelihood-based evaluation, larger-scale experiments, and improved exposition.
