This paper proposes a deep dynamic Poisson factorization model (DDPFA) to capture long-range temporal dependencies in sequential count data. The core idea is to leverage recurrent neural networks within a Poisson factorization framework to model complex relationships between factors over time, and to use variational inference for learning. While the technical content is generally strong and addresses limitations of prior work, the manuscript suffers from several issues regarding correctness, experimental reporting, clarity, and presentation.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) and its dynamic extensions [1, 2]. These models typically assume Gamma distributions for smoothing factor transitions. The authors correctly identify the limitations of these approaches in capturing long-range dependencies due to independence assumptions. They position DDPFA as a deep extension, drawing parallels to deep models used in Bayesian learning [4, 5, 6, 7] and neural network-based approaches to factor analysis [8, 9, 10, 11, 12]. The use of recurrent neural networks to model factor dependencies is a sensible direction, aiming to overcome the limitations of shallower models. The inclusion of variational inference is also a standard approach for such models.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The overall model formulation appears technically sound and the derivation of the variational inference procedure seems reasonable. The attempt to integrate deep learning and Bayesian nonparametrics is a valuable direction.
* **Addressing Long-Range Dependency:** The paper clearly identifies and attempts to address a key limitation of prior dynamic PF models – the inability to capture long-range dependencies. The use of recurrent networks is a logical approach.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors evaluate the model on a diverse set of datasets, including both synthetic and real-world examples, which is commendable.
* **Interpretability Focus:** The analysis of factors and visualization in Figure 2 and 3 are a positive aspect, attempting to provide insight into the learned representations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Factual Error:** A critical error exists regarding the Gamma distribution used. The paper states (lines 86-87) the Gamma distribution is used, however it’s actually the *Poisson Randomized Gamma* distribution that is required for modeling nonnegative numbers. This substantially affects the validity of the methodology.
* **Incomplete Evaluation:** While MSE and PMSE are reported, held-out perplexity would be a far more appropriate and informative metric for evaluating the generative capabilities of this model, especially given its probabilistic nature. Emphasizing perplexity alongside the reported metrics would significantly strengthen the evaluation.
* **Table Formatting:** Table 2 lacks clear formatting. The best results should be clearly indicated using bold font to facilitate comparison.
* **Missing Baseline:** A baseline comparison with a dynamic PF model that utilizes a single-level deep architecture (e.g., a simple feedforward network) is missing. This comparison is crucial to justify the benefit of the proposed hierarchical depth. Without it, it's unclear if the improvement stems from the depth or other aspects of the model.
* **LSTM Comparison:** The comparison to LSTM is informative but not definitive.  Different learning algorithms are used, and the hierarchical depth is not inherently justified by simply showing that DDPFA performs comparably to LSTM. A more direct comparison, perhaps with a hierarchical LSTM, would be more compelling.
* **Clarity & Writing Quality:** Numerous grammatical errors and typos significantly hinder readability.  Examples include: "unit the data", "Noticing the lack of capturing ..", "PFA factorized a count matrix", "other data with different pattern long-time dependence ..", "Examples for this including ..", "shows excellent results on predict and ..", "However, it is rough for them ..", "V-dimesnion sequentially ..", "K-dimension latent variable ..", "due to the including of feature-wise", "recommend systems". These errors need to be meticulously addressed.
* **Limited Discussion of 'Pruning' and Ladder Architectures:** While acknowledging these problems, the discussion is brief and lacks depth. The authors mention the potential benefits of a ladder-like architecture and addressing pruning issues via alternative inference methods, but do not elaborate on potential implementation strategies or expected improvements.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The model addresses an important problem in sequential count data analysis: capturing long-range dependencies.
* The technical approach of combining recurrent neural networks with Poisson factorization is novel and potentially promising.
* The experimental evaluation, despite limitations, covers a range of datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The factual error regarding the Gamma distribution is significant and undermines the technical correctness of the paper.
* The writing quality and clarity are substantially below the standard expected for NIPS.
* The evaluation is incomplete, lacking a crucial baseline and an appropriate generative metric (perplexity).
* The justification for the hierarchical depth is weak.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea with a solid technical foundation, but it is currently not ready for publication in its current state. The factual error, writing quality issues, and incomplete evaluation are significant concerns. Addressing these issues, particularly the factual error and improving the clarity of the manuscript, is crucial for improving its quality.

**Recommendation:** Reject. Major revisions are required. Specifically, the authors must: (1) Correct the error regarding the Gamma distribution; (2) Thoroughly proofread and revise the manuscript for grammatical errors and clarity; (3) Include a baseline comparison with a single-level deep dynamic PF model; (4) Report held-out perplexity as an evaluation metric; (5) Improve the formatting of Table 2; and (6) Expand the discussion regarding the “pruning” issue and ladder architecture.
