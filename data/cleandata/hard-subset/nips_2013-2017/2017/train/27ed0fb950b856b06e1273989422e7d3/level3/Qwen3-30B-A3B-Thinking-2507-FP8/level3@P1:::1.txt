

This paper proposes a Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA) model for sequential count data analysis, extending Poisson Factor Analysis with a deep latent structure to capture complex temporal dependencies. The authors incorporate neural networks to represent implicit distributions, aiming to model both short-term complicated relationships and long-term dependencies in time series count data. The model builds upon previous work like Poisson-Gamma Dynamical Systems (PGDS) and Deep Poisson Factor Analysis, but introduces a novel deep structure with variational inference for parameter estimation.

The paper demonstrates the model's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, Ebola outbreak data, International Disaster data, and Annual Sheep Population), comparing it against PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA. The authors claim their model achieves better prediction performance and interpretable latent structures.

The core idea is potentially interesting and addresses a relevant problem in analyzing sequential count data, which appears in applications like recommendation systems and text analysis. However, the paper suffers from significant issues that substantially weaken its contribution.

The most serious concern is the model's formulation. The proposed deep structure (Eq. 4) does not implement a true recurrent structure as claimed, as h_t^(n) depends only on top-layer memory vectors and not on previous time steps within the same layer. This fundamental misunderstanding of RNNs requires justification or substantial revision. The authors claim to use "implicit distributions" but Eqs. 9-10 explicitly derive Gamma distributions with closed-form conditionals, contradicting their claim.

The evaluation methodology is problematic. The paper uses MSE and PMSE for evaluation, which are inappropriate for count data models that should report predictive log-likelihood. This misalignment with standard evaluation practices for Poisson models undermines the reported performance claims. The authors also use unreasonably small values for K (the number of latent factors), with K=3 or 10 for most datasets when K≈100 would be more appropriate for real-world count data.

Several key aspects of the model are inadequately explained. The distribution generating θ_t (Eq. 3) and the Gamma parameters in Eq. 4 are not clearly defined. The reference to "fanatical data and disaster data" in lines 30-31 lacks elaboration and context. The loss function in Eq. 15 is not properly explained, and the subsection around lines 133-155 is particularly unclear.

The paper's writing quality is poor, with numerous grammatical errors, passive voice usage, and typos throughout. Figures and captions (particularly Fig. 4) are poorly formatted and difficult to interpret. The variational update equations (Eqs. 11-14) could be moved to the supplement to improve the main text flow.

While the authors correctly note that hierarchical latent variable models may not fully utilize structure (citing [25]), their proposed "ladder architecture" solution is not sufficiently justified or evaluated in the paper. The observation that adding more layers provides diminishing returns (Table 3) is not adequately connected to the model's design.

The most significant contribution of this paper appears to be the application of a simplified neural network structure to Poisson factorization, but the paper fails to clearly articulate how this differs from previous deep Poisson factorization approaches [8-12]. The connection to related work is insufficient, and the novelty claim is not strongly supported.

Strengths: The problem addressed is relevant, and the general approach of extending Poisson factorization with deep structures is promising. The empirical results on some datasets (particularly NIPS corpus and EBOLA) show reasonable performance compared to baselines.

Weaknesses: Fundamental misunderstandings of RNN structure, inappropriate evaluation metrics, inadequate model explanation, poor writing quality, and insufficient experimental design (too small K values, no ablation studies on network depth). The paper lacks sufficient technical depth to justify its claims.

The paper would benefit from substantial revisions: clarifying the model structure and addressing the RNN misconception, using appropriate evaluation metrics, increasing the number of latent factors, improving the writing quality, and providing clearer connections between the model design and experimental results. As presented, the technical contributions are not sufficiently novel or well-supported, and the paper would not meet the quality bar for NIPS.

The authors' claim that their model "captures complex time dependence" is not convincingly demonstrated given the methodological issues. The paper appears to have significant technical and presentation flaws that would require major revisions before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
