## Review of Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model

This paper proposes a novel deep learning approach, the Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization Model (DDPFA), for analyzing sequential count data. The core idea is to leverage recurrent neural networks within a Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) framework to capture both short- and long-range temporal dependencies. The authors address a known limitation of prior dynamic PFA models – their difficulty in modeling long-term relationships – by introducing a hierarchical, recurrent structure. The model is learned via variational inference, and evaluated on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

The technical contribution is strong. The paper clearly articulates the motivation for a deep, recurrent approach to dynamic PFA and provides a detailed mathematical formulation. The use of variational inference is appropriate given the complexity of the model, and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying statistical principles. The comparison to existing methods like PGDS, LSTM, and standard PFA is valuable, highlighting the potential benefits of the proposed approach. The inclusion of both fitting and prediction tasks in the experimental evaluation is also commendable.

However, the manuscript suffers from several weaknesses. First, a factual error exists regarding the Poisson-Gamma distribution (lines 86-87), which should be the Poisson Randomized Gamma distribution. Second, while MSE and PMSE are reported, held-out perplexity would be a more informative metric, particularly for sequence modeling, and should be emphasized. Table 2 lacks clear formatting; the best results should be bolded for easy comparison. The comparison to LSTM, while interesting, isn’t fully convincing, as the differing learning algorithms make a direct comparison of hierarchical depth difficult. Crucially, the paper lacks a baseline comparison to a dynamic PF model *without* the deep architecture – this is essential to justify the added complexity of multiple hierarchy levels. 

Furthermore, the manuscript is riddled with grammatical errors and typos (e.g., “unit the data”, “PFA factorized a count matrix”). These significantly hinder readability and detract from the overall quality of the work. The authors should carefully proofread the manuscript before submission.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and addresses a relevant problem in sequential count data analysis.
*   Novel combination of PFA and deep recurrent neural networks.
*   Demonstrates promising results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Factual error regarding the Poisson-Gamma distribution.
*   Lack of a crucial baseline comparison (single-level dynamic PF).
*   Numerous grammatical errors and typos.
*   Limited justification for the benefits of hierarchical depth compared to LSTM.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field. However, the identified weaknesses, particularly the factual error, missing baseline, and poor writing quality, prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns outlined above. A thorough proofreading and clarification of the model’s advantages over simpler alternatives are essential.
