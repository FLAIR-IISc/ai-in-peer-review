Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes Deep Dynamic Poisson Factorization (DDPFA), a hierarchical, RNN-like extension of Poisson Factor Analysis (PFA) for sequential count data. The observation model is Poisson with nonnegative latent factors, and temporal dependence is captured by a deep stack of latent variables whose conditional distributions are parameterized by recurrent neural networks. Inference alternates mean-field variational updates for conjugate Gamma–Poisson substructures with gradient-based training of the neural parameterizations via a probability autoencoder-style objective. Experiments on synthetic data and several real datasets (ICEWS, NIPS corpus, Ebola, International Disaster, Annual Sheep Population) compare DDPFA to PGDS, LSTM, and PFA.

The work builds on dynamic Poisson factorization (e.g., PGDS) that models Markovian transitions in Gamma–Poisson state-space models, and on deep latent variable models (deep exponential families, deep PFA, deep gamma belief networks) and neural temporal models (NADE/DocNADE, LSTMs). The key novelty is combining a Poisson likelihood for counts with a deep recurrent prior over multiple latent layers to capture longer-range dependencies than first-order gamma transitions.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: prior dynamic PF methods struggle with long-range temporal dependencies; the deep recurrent prior directly targets this limitation.
- Technically sound core: the use of conjugacy to retain closed-form variational updates for φ, ψ, λ, θ, while learning neural mappings for the shape parameters, is well-grounded. The probability autoencoder objective is a reasonable surrogate when reparameterized gradients are impractical.
- Empirical breadth: includes both synthetic stress tests and diverse real datasets. Qualitative analyses (factor trajectories) help interpretability.
- Addresses known issues: the discussion of hierarchy utility and pruning is thoughtful, with ladder-style connections to mitigate posterior collapse.

Weaknesses and requests for revision:
- Factual error: Lines 86–87 incorrectly describe the distribution for modeling nonnegative real-valued data. The suitable construction is the Poisson Randomized Gamma (PRG) distribution, which mixes a Gamma with a Poisson count to model nonnegative reals. Please correct the statement and cite appropriate sources.
- Evaluation metrics: MSE/PMSE do not fully reflect probabilistic fit for count models. Please add and emphasize held-out predictive log-likelihood and per-token perplexity (for text) or normalized predictive deviance. For recommendation-like settings, ranking metrics (MAP/NDCG) are also informative.
- Baselines for hierarchy: To justify the benefit of multiple latent layers, include a dynamic PF baseline with a single deep level (i.e., your recurrent prior but N=1), and an ablation across depths (N=1,2,3) under identical training budgets. This is more diagnostic than comparing only to shallow PFA.
- LSTM comparison: Useful but not definitive. LSTMs are trained with different likelihoods/objectives and do not ensure count calibration. Consider adding an RNN-Poisson GLM baseline (Poisson output with log link) trained on the same loss as DDPFA’s decoder. Clarify that the LSTM comparison does not, by itself, justify hierarchical depth.
- Reporting and formatting: Table 2 should bold the best results per row and include standard errors over multiple runs. Please report wall-clock time and compute budget for all methods to contextualize the additional complexity of DDPFA.
- Notational/derivation issues to fix:
  - Eq. 7 appears to repeat φ; it should read proportional to θtk φvk λk ψv.
  - In Eq. 5, q(ψk|·) should be q(ψv|·) (ψ is feature-wise).
  - Index ranges: use k ∈ {1,…,K}.
  - Clarify shape/rate parameterizations consistently and specify priors for βt, βh.
  - Provide the exact RNN architectures, window size c choices, and how α, γ are constrained to be positive (e.g., softplus).
- Experimental design: Provide sensitivity analyses for K, window size c, ladder connections on/off, and dataset-specific hyperparameters. For text (NIPS), report topic coherence and/or held-out perplexity to assess interpretability beyond factor trajectories.
- Writing quality: Numerous grammatical errors/typos make the paper hard to follow and sometimes ambiguous.

Quality:
- Technical content is largely solid and original in its combination of Poisson factorization with a deep recurrent prior and a tailored VI/autoencoder training loop. Some derivations need clearer notation and parameterization details. Claims about long-range dependence are supported qualitatively and by synthetic/real results, but would be stronger with probabilistic predictive metrics and ablations.

Clarity:
- Currently below NIPS standards due to grammar and typos; organization is acceptable but several equations and algorithmic details need clarification (see above). With careful editing and expanded experimental details, clarity can be brought to standard.

Originality:
- The integration of deep hierarchical RNN priors with conjugate PF and a probability autoencoder training objective is novel relative to PGDS, deep PF without dynamics, and standard neural topic models.

Significance:
- If validated with stronger baselines and probabilistic metrics, the approach could be impactful for sequential count modeling in text, events, and recommender systems.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, relevant technical idea tackling an important limitation (long-range dependence) in dynamic PF.
- Conjugate VI + neural parameterization is elegant and computationally reasonable.
- Promising results and interpretability.

Cons for acceptance:
- Writing quality and notational issues.
- Missing key baselines/ablations to isolate the benefits of hierarchy.
- Incomplete evaluation metrics; lack of held-out log-likelihood/perplexity.

Recommendation: Weak accept, conditional on fixing the factual error, improving clarity, adding probabilistic evaluation (perplexity/log-likelihood), marking best results in Table 2, and including ablations comparing N=1 vs deeper hierarchies (and, ideally, an RNN-Poisson baseline).
