This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The central idea is to address the shortcomings of existing adaptive sampling schemes, which are either computationally prohibitive (requiring full gradient information) or lack theoretical guarantees of improvement over simpler, static sampling distributions. The authors formulate the problem of choosing a sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem. Given only efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes, the method finds a sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case convergence bound. This "safe" distribution is proven to be superior to fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform sampling). The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute this distribution in each iteration and demonstrate its application to Generalized Linear Models (GLMs). Experimental results on several real-world datasets are presented to support the claims.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The primary strength of this work is its rigorous theoretical foundation. The formulation of the sampling problem as a min-max optimization problem (7) is elegant and provides a principled way to operate under uncertainty about the true gradient. The resulting guarantee in Theorem 3.2, which proves that the proposed sampling is always better than Li-based sampling, is a significant contribution. This "safety" net addresses a critical weakness of many heuristic adaptive methods, which can sometimes underperform compared to simple uniform sampling.

2.  **Methodological Contribution:** The derivation of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the non-trivial min-max problem is a solid technical contribution. This makes the theoretically motivated sampling scheme practically feasible.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It effectively motivates the problem by discussing the limitations of existing static and adaptive sampling methods. The theoretical development is presented logically, progressing from the full-information case to the limited-information setting.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Overhead:** The main practical concern is the O(n log n) computational overhead introduced in *every single iteration*. While the authors argue this is manageable when the feature dimension `d` is large (d = Ω(n)), this is not always the case. For many modern machine learning problems, particularly with sparse data, `n` (the number of coordinates or data points) can be much larger than `d`. In such scenarios, this per-iteration cost could easily dominate the O(d) cost of a single coordinate gradient computation, making the method slower in wall-clock time than simpler baselines, despite better iteration complexity. The timing results in Figures 6 and 9 are promising but do not cover the full range of possible `n` vs. `d` regimes.

2.  **Limited Empirical Comparison:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating an advantage over uniform sampling and showing proximity to the (impractical) optimal sampling, is incomplete. A crucial comparison is missing: the proposed method is not benchmarked against other *practical* adaptive sampling schemes from the literature (e.g., [23, 36]), which often use stale gradients or simpler upper-bound-based heuristics. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed method's complexity and rigorous formulation translate into a tangible performance benefit over existing, simpler adaptive approaches.

3.  **Underwhelming SGD Performance:** The authors candidly note that for SGD, the performance gap between optimal sampling and uniform sampling is small on the tested problems. Consequently, their proposed method offers only a marginal improvement. This significantly reduces the claimed impact of the work, as SGD is arguably the more prevalent optimization framework in large-scale machine learning and deep learning. The contribution appears much more relevant for CD than for SGD, based on the evidence provided.

4.  **Novelty of Gradient Bounds:** The method for obtaining the safe gradient bounds in Section 4 is based on standard techniques (e.g., Cauchy-Schwarz inequality). While sufficient for demonstrating the framework, it is a very loose approximation. The paper's core novelty lies in what to do with the bounds, not how to get them. The performance of the entire scheme is critically dependent on the quality of these bounds, and the paper does not explore the trade-off between the tightness of the bounds and the cost of maintaining them.

**Summary of arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a methodologically sound and novel min-max formulation for adaptive sampling under uncertainty.
*   It provides a strong theoretical guarantee of improvement over standard fixed importance sampling, a property many adaptive heuristics lack.
*   An efficient algorithm is provided to implement the proposed sampling strategy.

**Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical utility is questionable due to the non-trivial O(n log n) per-iteration overhead, which may not be justified in all problem settings.
*   The empirical evaluation is missing comparisons to other established, practical adaptive sampling methods, making it difficult to judge its state-of-the-art performance.
*   The demonstrated benefit for SGD, a key target application, is marginal, limiting the overall significance of the contribution.

**Conclusion:**

This paper introduces a theoretically rigorous and well-motivated approach to adaptive importance sampling. The "safety" guarantee is a welcome property in a field with many heuristics. However, the work is let down by concerns about its practical overhead and an empirical evaluation that, while clean, fails to compare against relevant adaptive baselines and shows limited gains for SGD. The contribution is solid from a theoretical standpoint but feels more incremental than transformative in its practical impact. It is a good paper, but its significance for the broader NIPS community is tempered by these limitations.
