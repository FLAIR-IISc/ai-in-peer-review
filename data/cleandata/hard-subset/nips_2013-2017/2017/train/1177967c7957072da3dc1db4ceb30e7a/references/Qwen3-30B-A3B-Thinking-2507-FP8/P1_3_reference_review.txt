

# Peer Review: "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address the challenge that while gradient-based adaptive sampling theoretically outperforms fixed sampling (potentially by a factor of n), it is computationally infeasible to compute the optimal sampling distribution in practice. Their key contribution is a method to approximate the optimal sampling distribution using safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information, while guaranteeing that the approximation is never worse than fixed importance sampling. The authors prove theoretical guarantees for their method, demonstrate how to efficiently maintain gradient bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs), and provide empirical evidence of significant performance improvements over fixed sampling strategies.

This work builds on the established literature on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., Zhao and Zhang, 2015; Shalev-Shwartz and Zhang, 2013) but makes a novel contribution by introducing a "safe" approximation that maintains theoretical guarantees, unlike previous approaches that relied on upper bounds or stale values.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution with Theorem 3.2, which proves that the proposed safe sampling is never worse than fixed importance sampling. This is a significant improvement over previous adaptive sampling methods that couldn't guarantee such a property. The authors' approach to formulate the sampling problem as an optimization over a bounded gradient set is elegant and well-justified.

2. **Computational Efficiency**: The authors demonstrate that the proposed method can be implemented with O(n log n) computational overhead per iteration, which is comparable to the cost of computing a single gradient component for many machine learning problems. The algorithm (Algorithm 4) is carefully designed and the complexity analysis is thorough.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experimental results are comprehensive, covering multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20), loss functions (square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss), and regularization types (L1, L2). The results consistently show that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in terms of convergence speed and total computation time.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The paper provides a clear demonstration of how to maintain safe gradient bounds for GLMs, which is a practically important class of problems in machine learning. This shows that the theoretical framework can be implemented efficiently in real-world settings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Empirical Validation**: While the paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness for GLMs, it doesn't explore more complex models where gradient bounds might be harder to maintain. The claim that "our approach can immediately be applied when the tracking of the gradient is delegated to other machines in a distributed setting" lacks empirical support.

2. **Inconsistent Performance Across Methods**: The paper shows significant improvements for CD but only modest gains for SGD (Figure 7-9). The authors don't adequately explain why the method is more effective for CD than SGD, which weakens the claim of general applicability. This is a notable gap given that SGD is more commonly used in practice for large-scale problems.

3. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper doesn't compare with other recent adaptive sampling methods (e.g., the "Non-Uniform Stochastic Average Gradient Method" by Schmidt et al. (2015) or "Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling" by Perekrestenko et al. (2016)), despite citing them. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution.

4. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The theoretical analysis assumes coordinate-wise L-smoothness, which is a strong assumption for many machine learning problems. The paper doesn't discuss how violations of this assumption might affect the practical performance.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization for large-scale machine learning. The theoretical guarantees are sound, the method is computationally efficient, and the empirical results demonstrate clear practical benefits. The "safe" aspect of the proposed sampling, which ensures the method never performs worse than fixed sampling, is particularly noteworthy and addresses a key limitation of previous adaptive sampling approaches.

The main weaknesses are the limited scope of the empirical validation and the inconsistent performance between CD and SGD. However, these do not undermine the core contribution of the paper, which is a theoretically grounded and practically efficient method for adaptive importance sampling with provable guarantees.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. It makes a clear theoretical contribution that addresses a practical problem in large-scale optimization, with sound theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field. While the empirical results for SGD could be strengthened, the overall contribution is substantial and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.

## Key Points for Authors

1. Consider adding a discussion of why the method performs better for CD than SGD, and whether this can be addressed in future work.

2. Include a comparison with more recent adaptive sampling methods to better position your work in the literature.

3. Expand the empirical evaluation to include more complex models beyond GLMs to demonstrate broader applicability.

4. Clarify the relationship between the theoretical assumptions (coordinate-wise L-smoothness) and practical machine learning problems.

This paper represents a strong contribution to the field of optimization for large-scale machine learning, with clear theoretical guarantees and practical implementation considerations. It advances the state of the art in adaptive importance sampling by providing a method that is both theoretically sound and computationally efficient.
