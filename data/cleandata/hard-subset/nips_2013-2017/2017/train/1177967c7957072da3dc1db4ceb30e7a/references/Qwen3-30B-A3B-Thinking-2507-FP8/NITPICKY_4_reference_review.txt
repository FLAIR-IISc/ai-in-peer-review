

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors argue that adaptive sampling strategies theoretically outperform fixed importance sampling but are often computationally infeasible. Their approach uses safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information to construct a sampling distribution that is provably better than fixed sampling, while being efficiently computable.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

The paper builds upon existing work on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) but introduces a novel approach to computing sampling distributions under limited gradient information. Previous work (e.g., [36]) typically uses the current gradient estimates to construct sampling probabilities, but these approaches can sometimes perform worse than uniform sampling. The authors claim their method is "safe" in that it provably never performs worse than fixed sampling.

The paper makes four main contributions: (1) theoretical justification of gradient-based sampling in CD; (2) a generic adaptive sampling strategy; (3) integration with CD and SGD for structured problems; and (4) empirical evidence on real data.

## Strengths

The paper identifies a genuine problem in adaptive importance sampling: the potential degradation in performance when using approximate gradient information. The proposed "safe" approach is theoretically sound, with the key result being Theorem 3.2 showing the sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling. The empirical results on GLMs are promising, particularly for CD methods.

## Weaknesses and Critical Errors

Despite its promising theoretical framework, the paper contains numerous critical errors and inconsistencies that undermine its main claims:

1. **Fundamental Notational Error**: In Example 2.2, the authors claim "αk(punif) = 1Ln" when Li = L for all i. This is mathematically incorrect. If Li = L for all i, then Tr[L] = nL, so αk(pL) = 1/Tr[L] = 1/(nL), not 1/Ln. This error is critical as it misrepresents the baseline performance.

2. **Inconsistent Update Rule**: The paper states γk = αk[pk]^{-1}_{ik} (Equation 2), but then writes the update as "xk+1 := xk − αk[pk]ik∇ikf(xk)eik". This is inconsistent with the proposed stepsize; it should be "xk+1 := xk − αk[pk]^{-1}_{ik}∇ikf(xk)eik" to match the definition of γk.

3. **Critical Mathematical Error in Example 2.3**: The authors claim "αk(p?k) = ‖∇f(xk)‖22 ‖√L∇f(xk)‖2" which is ambiguous and likely incorrect. It should be "αk(p?k) = ‖∇f(xk)‖_2^2 / ‖√L∇f(xk)‖_1" or similar, but the paper's notation suggests multiplication rather than division.

4. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses "L" to denote both a scalar (e.g., Lmin) and a matrix (L = diag(l)), creating confusion. The notation "vector l = (L1, . . . , n)>" should be "vector l = (L1, . . . , Ln)>".

5. **Undefined Symbols**: The paper uses "p?k", "p̂k", and "p̃?k" without defining them in the notation section.

6. **Algorithmic Inconsistency**: In Algorithm 4, the initialization uses "u = 1" and "` = n", which are confusing as "u" and "`" are used both as vectors and scalars.

7. **Confusing Formatting**: In Equation (3), "αk [pk]ik (∇ikf(xk)) 2" should be "αk / [pk]_ik (∇ikf(xk))^2" to match the proposed update rule.

8. **Theoretical Claim Unsupported**: The paper claims "the speed-up can reach a factor of the dimension n" but doesn't provide a clear proof or example where this occurs.

9. **Empirical Misrepresentation**: In Section 5, the authors state "real-sim' and rcv1'" with apostrophes instead of prime symbols, which is inconsistent with mathematical notation.

10. **Terminological Inconsistency**: The paper uses both "efficiently be computed" (Section 1) and "efficiently computed" (Section 3.1), with the latter being the correct form.

## Overall Assessment

While the core idea of "safe" adaptive importance sampling is interesting and potentially valuable, the paper is marred by fundamental mathematical errors and notational inconsistencies that undermine its technical credibility. The critical error in Example 2.2 (misrepresenting the baseline stepsize) is particularly damaging as it misleads readers about the performance gains claimed for the proposed method.

The paper also fails to provide a clear explanation of why O(n log n) computational cost per iteration is "no extra cost" when the gradient computation itself is O(d) (which is often larger than O(n log n) for large datasets). The claim that "the scheme can be implemented at no extra computational cost" is therefore misleading.

The paper's theoretical claims are not supported by proper mathematical notation or derivations, and the empirical results, while promising, are presented with insufficient detail to verify the claims.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper's fundamental mathematical errors and notational inconsistencies are too severe to overlook, even though the core idea is potentially valuable. The authors need to thoroughly revise the paper to correct these errors and clarify the mathematical foundations before it could be considered for publication. The errors are not merely typographical but undermine the paper's main claims about the performance of the proposed method.

The paper's contribution is not sufficiently novel or technically sound to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical claims are based on mathematical errors, and the presentation is marred by inconsistent notation and formatting. While the problem addressed is important, the paper fails to properly establish its claims.
