- Proposes "safe adaptive importance sampling" for coordinate descent and SGD that uses maintained safe upper/lower gradient bounds to build sampling distributions efficiently.
- Formulates adaptive sampling as a convex optimization over safe gradient bounds, differing from prior stale-value or heuristic approaches.
- Proves the proposed sampling is always at least as good as fixed importance sampling and derives competitive ratio bounds (Theorem 3.2, Lemmas 3.5–3.6).
- Provides an efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) with claimed computational overhead O(n log n) per iteration and practical cost small relative to computing a coordinate gradient O(d).
- Demonstrates how to maintain safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models with L1/L2 regularization in linear time O(n) per update.
- Empirical results on rcv1, real-sim, and news20 for squared hinge, logistic, and square loss show significant speedups for CD and modest gains for SGD.
- Strength: strong theoretical foundation with novel provable guarantee for adaptive importance sampling over fixed sampling.
- Strength: practical implementation appears computationally feasible and well-designed.
- Strength: clear and well-structured presentation with complete proofs in the appendix.
- Strength: concrete method and insights for maintaining safe gradient bounds in GLMs.
- Weakness: empirical evaluation limited to GLMs with L1/L2 regularization, limiting demonstrated applicability.
- Weakness: adaptive sampling yields less significant improvements in SGD than in CD, with no clear theoretical explanation provided.
- Weakness: lacks comparison to several recent adaptive sampling methods that might be competitive in efficiency or guarantees.
- Weakness: O(n log n) complexity claim may be optimistic in practice due to sorting overheads for very large n.
- Weakness: theoretical guarantees are much stronger for CD than for SGD, weakening generality claims.
- Recommendation: accept — novel, rigorous, and practically valuable contribution despite limited empirical scope and weaker SGD theory.
- Pro: first computationally efficient, theoretically guaranteed adaptive importance sampling; significant CD improvements; small computational overhead; applicability to both CD and SGD.
- Con: limited empirical validation, weaker empirical/theoretical gains for SGD, missing comparisons to some adaptive methods, and weaker SGD guarantees.
