## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for both Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to address the computational and theoretical drawbacks of existing adaptive strategies by introducing a "safe" approximation. This approximation relies on readily available upper and lower bounds on gradient information, allowing for efficient computation while guaranteeing performance at least as good as uniform sampling. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improvements over fixed sampling (up to a factor of the dimension *n* in CD) and show how the scheme can be integrated into practical algorithms. Empirical validation on GLMs using real-world datasets supports the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a crucial problem:** Adaptive importance sampling is known to improve optimization speed, but often suffers from computational cost and weaker theoretical guarantees. This paper directly tackles these issues.
* **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis showing the benefits of the proposed method compared to fixed sampling, and establishes a competitive ratio against the optimal (but computationally prohibitive) gradient-based sampling. The connection to coordinate-wise Lipschitz continuity is well-established.
* **Practical Algorithm & Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is computationally efficient (O(n log n) per iteration) making it applicable in practical settings, particularly when compared to the cost of computing full gradients.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough, including comparisons to both uniform sampling and the optimal sampling strategy (when feasible), across multiple datasets and with different loss functions.  The inclusion of iteration complexity and timing results is valuable.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the key ideas and technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited empirical advantage in SGD:** While theoretically sound, the empirical results for SGD show only a marginal improvement over uniform sampling. This is a significant gap, given the paper’s broader claims of benefit. The authors acknowledge this, but a deeper investigation into the reasons behind this disparity would be valuable.
* **Dependence on accurate bounds:** The performance of the method hinges on the quality of the upper and lower bounds on the gradients. The paper demonstrates a simple method for obtaining these bounds, but more sophisticated or adaptive bounding techniques could further enhance performance.  The sensitivity of the method to the tightness of these bounds isn’t thoroughly explored.
* **Assumptions about Gradient Access:** While the authors highlight efficient tracking of bounds in GLMs, the generalizability to other model types isn't fully addressed.  The reliance on being able to track gradient *changes* is a significant limitation for some models.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some notation could be clarified (e.g., the definition of 'l' and 'L').  A more detailed explanation of the connection between Lemma 3.5 and Lemma 3.6 would be helpful.



**Assessment based on NIPS criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported. The experimental results, while not uniformly positive, are presented honestly and provide evidence of the method's potential.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized. However, as noted above, some minor clarifications could improve readability.
* **Originality:** The proposed “safe” adaptive sampling approach is a novel contribution, building on existing work in importance sampling and coordinate descent. The formulation as a convex optimization problem and the efficient algorithm for solving it are original ideas.
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant, particularly for applications where gradient computation is expensive. The method offers a practical way to accelerate optimization without requiring full gradient information. However, the limited advantage in SGD reduces the overall impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a well-motivated and important problem in optimization.
* Presents a strong theoretical foundation with provable guarantees.
* Offers a computationally efficient and practical algorithm.
* Provides empirical evidence of effectiveness, particularly in the context of Coordinate Descent.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited empirical advantage in the SGD setting.
* Reliance on the quality of gradient bounds introduces a potential weakness.
* Generalizability to model types beyond GLMs is not fully explored.



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper, with minor revisions. The work represents a significant contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling, offering a practical and theoretically sound approach. While the empirical results for SGD are less compelling, the overall quality of the paper, the strength of the theoretical analysis, and the originality of the ideas justify acceptance. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version, particularly by providing a more in-depth analysis of the SGD results and exploring the sensitivity of the method to the quality of gradient bounds.
