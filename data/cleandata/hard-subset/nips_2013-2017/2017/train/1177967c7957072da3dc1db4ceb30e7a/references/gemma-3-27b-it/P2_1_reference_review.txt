## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for both Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling in a computationally efficient manner, while still providing theoretical guarantees of improvement over uniform or fixed importance sampling. The authors achieve this by formulating the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem, solvable with a proposed efficient algorithm.  They demonstrate both theoretical advantages (speedups up to the dimension 'n' for CD) and empirical performance on real-world datasets using generalized linear models.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.  The analysis demonstrates provable improvements over static sampling strategies and links the proposed approach to optimal gradient-based sampling when full gradient information is available. The lemmas and proofs seem rigorous (though a deeper dive would be needed for full verification).
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm offers a compelling balance between performance and computational cost. The authors demonstrate that the sampling distribution can be computed efficiently (O(n log n)), making it practical for large-scale problems.
* **Generality:** The method is presented as being applicable to both CD and SGD, broadening its potential impact.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors address a key issue in adaptive sampling: the need for safe bounds on gradient information, and provide a practical method for obtaining these bounds within the context of GLMs.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real-world datasets with GLMs provide strong evidence that the proposed method performs well in practice, and often outperforms fixed importance sampling.  The inclusion of timing results is a plus.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **SGD Results Less Compelling:** While the theoretical analysis extends to SGD, the empirical results demonstrate a less significant improvement compared to CD. This discrepancy isn't thoroughly investigated.  A deeper analysis of why the benefits don't translate as strongly to SGD would be helpful.
* **GLM Focus:** The empirical evaluation is heavily focused on generalized linear models. While a good starting point, it limits the generalizability of the conclusions. Demonstrating performance on other problem domains (e.g., deep learning, non-convex optimization) would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Comparison to Existing Adaptive Methods:** The paper compares favorably to uniform sampling and fixed importance sampling, but a more direct comparison to existing adaptive sampling methods (e.g., those based on duality gaps, or stale gradients) would be valuable.
* **Assumption of Safe Bounds:** The reliance on safe gradient bounds is a key aspect, but the paper doesn't fully address the sensitivity of the performance to the tightness of these bounds. More analysis on the impact of inaccurate or loose bounds would be beneficial.  Could the performance degrade significantly with poor bounds?
* **Complexity Analysis Caveats:** The claim of O(n log n) computational complexity for the algorithm relies on sorting. While correct, it's important to acknowledge that this could become a bottleneck for *extremely* high-dimensional problems.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed method addresses a crucial problem in large-scale optimization—efficient adaptive sampling—and offers a theoretically sound and computationally practical solution. The experimental results are encouraging. While the SGD results and the limited scope of the empirical evaluation are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  Strong theoretical foundations and guarantees.
*  Practical and efficient algorithm.
*  Demonstrated improvements over established methods (uniform/fixed sampling) in a relevant setting.
*  Clear and well-written presentation.
*  Addresses a significant problem in large-scale optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  SGD results are less compelling than CD results.
*  Empirical evaluation primarily limited to GLMs.
*  Lack of direct comparison to other adaptive sampling methods.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  8/10. Technically sound, well-supported by analysis, and generally complete.
* **Clarity:** 8/10.  Well-written and organized, but some sections could benefit from further elaboration.
* **Originality:** 8/10.  The combination of convex optimization for sampling distribution selection, coupled with the focus on safe bounds, feels novel and provides a valuable contribution.
* **Significance:** 7/10.  The potential impact is significant, particularly for problems where gradient computation is expensive.  Expanding the empirical evaluation would bolster this further.



**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Investigate and discuss the reasons for the weaker performance of the method in the SGD setting.
2.  Consider including a comparison to other existing adaptive sampling techniques.
3.  Explore the sensitivity of the performance to the accuracy of the safe gradient bounds.
4.  Acknowledge the potential bottleneck of sorting in the complexity analysis for extremely high-dimensional problems.
