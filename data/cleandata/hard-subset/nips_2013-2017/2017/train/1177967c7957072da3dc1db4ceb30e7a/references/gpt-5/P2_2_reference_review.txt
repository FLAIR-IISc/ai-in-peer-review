Summary
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). With full information, the paper revisits and sharpens the known result that sampling coordinates proportionally to √Li|∇if(x)| (CD) or components by ‖∇fi(x)‖ (SGD) minimizes a standard one-step smoothness upper bound and can yield up to n-fold speedups over uniform/fixed importance sampling. The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive sampling scheme when only bounds on the per-coordinate (or per-component) gradients are available. The authors formulate a min–max problem over the probability simplex and a box Ck defined by safe lower/upper bounds on the (unknown) current gradients, and show:
- The resulting sampling pk is the best worst-case choice given the bounds (Theorem 3.2(i)).
- It is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling based on Li (Theorem 3.2(ii)).
- It recovers the full-information gradient-based sampling when the bounds collapse to the true gradient.
They give an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4 / Theorem 3.4) that computes the optimal pk and an associated safe stepsize αk = 1/vk, where vk is the min–max value. They also discuss how to maintain safe gradient bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs), and present experiments on rcv1, real-sim, and news20 for CD and SGD.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The one-step progress analysis for CD is standard and correct: with coordinate-wise L-smoothness, choosing γk = αk/[pk]ik yields E[f(xk+1)|xk] ≤ f(xk) − αk‖∇f(xk)‖2 + (αk)2/2 ∑i Li(∇if)2/[pk]i. Minimizing over α for fixed p leads to the objective V(p,c)/‖c‖2, with c the (unknown) gradient vector; minimizing over p yields p∝√Li|ci| when c is known. The min–max formulation with safe bounds Ck is natural. The properties in Theorem 3.2 follow directly by comparing to the feasible fixed-importance solution pL. The O(n log n) solver is a “water-filling”-like thresholding procedure consistent with the KKT conditions; although details are in the appendix, the construction appears correct and the complexity claim is plausible (sorting dominates).
- Claims vs. evidence: The paper’s main guarantee—never worse than fixed importance sampling with respect to the smoothness model—is well supported. Experiments demonstrate substantial gains for CD, and modest gains for SGD, aligning with the theory and candidly discussed.
- Scope and completeness: Convergence rates are sketched (one-step descent) and referenced to appendices. A full end-to-end rate theorem for the safe sampling variant (e.g., for strongly convex objectives) in the main text would strengthen the presentation, but the ingredients are standard once the one-step bound and vk bounds are established.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with clear motivation and algorithmic summaries (Algorithms 1–4). However:
  - Notation is overloaded: n is used as ambient dimension for CD and as dataset size for SGD; d is the other dimension. Consider clarifying early and being consistent across sections.
  - Lemma 2.1 / Eq. (4): the expression “− αk(pk) 2 ‖∇f‖2” appears ambiguous; it should be “−(αk(pk)/2)‖∇f‖2,” consistent with minimizing a quadratic in α.
  - The practical maintenance of safe bounds for SGD relies on bounds for ∇2hi along a segment; please state the assumptions (e.g., known curvature bounds of hi) more explicitly and how they translate into computable numeric bounds in practice.
  - A brief, intuitive sketch of Algorithm 4 in the main text (water-filling/thresholding interpretation) would help readability.

Originality
- Adaptive sampling is well studied, but the paper’s specific safe min–max formulation under uncertainty sets, the proof that this choice is optimal in the worst case given bounds, and the efficient O(n log n) algorithm appear novel for both CD and SGD contexts. Prior works using stale/upper-bounded gradients (e.g., Csiba et al., Perekrestenko et al., Zhao & Zhang) did not provide the “never worse than fixed importance” guarantee under bounded uncertainty. The connection to minimax (Sion/Komiya) and the geometric characterization are also nice touches.

Significance
- For CD on common ML objectives (GLMs with L1/L2 regularization), the method yields substantial practical speedups and a compelling guarantee: with only safe bounds, one can approximate gradient-based sampling in a principled way and never underperform fixed importance sampling with respect to the standard smoothness model. This is valuable for large-scale settings where full gradients are infeasible.
- For SGD, the empirical gains are modest; nonetheless, the framework is general and the conservative guarantees may still be attractive in practice when safe bounds can be made tight.
- The per-iteration overhead is O(n log n) to compute pk plus O(n) to update bounds, which is significant in some regimes. The paper argues this is acceptable when the cost of computing one gradient coordinate is Θ(d) with d=Ω(n). This is an important caveat: in problems with very large n and small d (or very cheap per-step updates), the overhead may dominate; discussing variants that amortize or sparsify the updates (e.g., updating pk every few iterations, or maintaining a heap/incremental updates) would broaden applicability.

Pros
- Clear motivation and principled formulation; optimal worst-case sampling given uncertainty bounds.
- Strong and interpretable guarantees; recovers full-information optimal sampling.
- Efficient, implementable algorithm with O(n log n) complexity.
- Empirical results on real datasets showing substantial gains for CD; honest reporting for SGD.

Cons
- Overhead may be non-negligible when d ≪ n or per-iteration costs are otherwise tiny.
- Some details for maintaining tight safe bounds (especially for SGD) are deferred and could be clearer; the Cauchy–Schwarz-based bounds may be quite loose.
- Convergence rate theorems for the full algorithm (beyond one-step bounds) are not presented in the main text.
- Minor clarity/notation issues.

Questions/Suggestions for authors
- Can pk be updated less frequently (e.g., once per epoch) with similar gains, reducing O(n log n) overhead?
- For SGD, can tighter safe bounds be obtained cheaply (e.g., via Lipschitz Hessian bounds or variance reduction contexts)?
- Please clarify assumptions on hi for Lemma 4.1 and how to translate ∇2hi(a>ix̃) into computable bounds.
- Provide a short global convergence rate result in the main text (e.g., for μ-strongly convex f), using the vk bounds.

Recommendation
- Overall: Accept (7/10). The paper offers a clean, novel, and practically useful approach with solid theory and promising empirical results for CD. While some implementation details and clarity can be improved, the contribution is significant and relevant to the NeurIPS community.

Confidence: 0.8
