## NIPS 2017 Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for both Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The authors address the drawbacks of existing adaptive sampling techniques – often lacking theoretical guarantees or being computationally expensive – by proposing a method that is both provably better than uniform/fixed importance sampling and efficiently computable. The core idea is to formulate the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem based on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient, ensuring robustness even with imperfect gradient information. The paper demonstrates theoretical advantages, particularly a potential speed-up factor of the dimension 'n' in CD, and validates the approach empirically on real-world datasets with generalized linear models.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds on a rich history of importance sampling in optimization, referencing seminal papers like those by Nesterov, Richtárik, and Zhao & Zhang. It directly tackles the limitations highlighted in works discussing the theoretical shortcomings of adaptive strategies (e.g., Csiba et al., 2015) and the computational cost of gradient-based sampling (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, 2015). It distinguishes itself by providing a *safe* adaptive strategy with provable guarantees, filling a gap between computationally expensive optimal sampling and potentially suboptimal fixed sampling.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides strong theoretical justification for the proposed method, demonstrating its superiority over static sampling distributions. The analysis of the convergence rate improvements, especially the potential n-fold speedup in CD, is compelling.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is designed to be computationally feasible, addressing a key limitation of previous adaptive methods. The use of safe bounds allows for efficient computation of the sampling distribution.
* **Generality:** The scheme is presented as a generic improvement applicable to both CD and SGD, demonstrating its broad applicability.
* **Practical Relevance:** The empirical evaluation on real-world datasets with GLMs demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach in a practical setting. The ability to derive safe gradient bounds for GLMs is a valuable contribution.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear notation and a logical flow of arguments.  Algorithms are presented in a concise and understandable manner.
* **Safety Guarantee:** The method's “safe” nature, guaranteeing performance at least as good as fixed sampling, is a significant strength, offering a robust alternative.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Empirical Results (SGD):** While the CD results are promising, the performance gains in the SGD experiments are less pronounced. The authors acknowledge this, but further investigation into why the benefits are less apparent in the SGD setting would be valuable. It’s unclear if the algorithm is less suited to SGD or if the experimental setup doesn’t fully showcase its advantages.
* **Gradient Bound Assumptions:** The performance relies on accurate and efficiently maintainable safe gradient bounds. While the authors provide a method for GLMs, the generalizability to more complex models and the robustness of the bounds to noisy data or poorly conditioned problems aren’t fully addressed.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the overall computational cost is reasonable, the algorithm involves a sorting step (O(n log n)) and potentially more complex updates for the bounds, which could add overhead in certain implementations. This wasn’t explicitly evaluated in the timing experiments.
* **Discussion of Competitive Ratio:** While the authors present bounds on the competitive ratio, its practical implications are not fully explored. A deeper discussion of how the quality of the gradient bounds affects the competitive ratio and overall performance would be useful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are focused primarily on GLMs. Evaluating the performance on a wider range of optimization problems (e.g., non-convex optimization, deep learning models) would strengthen the claims of generality.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling.
* It addresses a significant limitation of existing methods – the trade-off between theoretical guarantees and computational cost.
* The empirical results, particularly for CD, demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed scheme.
* The “safe” nature of the algorithm provides a robust and reliable improvement over fixed sampling.
* The method is generally applicable to a wide range of optimization problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical gains for SGD are not as significant as for CD, raising questions about the algorithm’s performance in different settings.
* The reliance on accurate safe gradient bounds could limit the applicability to certain models or datasets.
* The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of the competitive ratio.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions to the field of adaptive importance sampling. While the empirical results for SGD are less compelling, the overall rigor, generality, and safety guarantees of the proposed method are highly commendable.  The paper clearly advances the state of the art and has the potential to be influential in the optimization community.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Investigate further the reasons for the limited gains in the SGD experiments.
2.  Discuss the robustness of the gradient bounds to noisy data and poorly conditioned problems.
3.  Provide a more in-depth analysis of the practical implications of the competitive ratio.
4.  Consider broadening the scope of the experimental evaluation to include more diverse optimization problems.




**Confidence:** 8/10
