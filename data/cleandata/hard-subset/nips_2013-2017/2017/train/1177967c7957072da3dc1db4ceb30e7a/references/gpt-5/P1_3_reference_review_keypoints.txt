- Paper tackles adaptive importance sampling for CD and SGD using per-coordinate gradient magnitude bounds to form a safe min–max sampling rule.
- Proposes a min–max formulation that yields a closed-form thresholding solution computable in O(n log n) and recovers full-information sampling when bounds are tight.
- Connects to prior work on fixed importance sampling, adaptive gradient-based sampling, adaptive CD, and safe screening, while introducing a novel safe min–max perspective.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation and elegant principled min–max formulation bridging optimal and computationally feasible sampling.
- Solid one-step progress analysis for CD with interpretable criterion V(p,c) and optimal p ∝ |√L c|.
- The safe distribution p̂ is provably worst-case optimal over the uncertainty set and dominates Li-based fixed sampling.
- Efficient algorithmic contribution: an O(n log n) sorting-based procedure with insightful geometric interpretation.
- Practicality: provides easy-to-compute safe bounds for GLMs (via Cauchy–Schwarz) applicable to CD and SGD.
- Empirical results: substantial speedups for CD and modest improvements for SGD, highlighting the benefit of adaptive step sizes.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Computational overhead: O(n log n) per-update for p̂ and O(n) bound updates may be prohibitive when n is large and d ≪ n; need reporting of update frequency and timing.
- Global convergence complexity: main text lacks explicit end-to-end iteration complexity theorems comparing to fixed sampling.
- Min–max interchange and optimality: reliance on minimax interchange is delicate; needs more intuition and a self-contained proof sketch in main text.
- Empirics for SGD limited: gains modest and comparisons to state-of-the-art adaptive/stale sampling and variance-reduction baselines are missing.
- CD baselines: lacks comparisons to strong adaptive CD baselines like gap-based sampling and Gauss–Southwell rules or approximations.
- Practical details: inconsistent timing setups, need standardized wall-clock evaluations and breakdown of time spent on bounds/sampling/updates.
- Sensitivity and notation: sensitivity to λ and bound tightness not fully explored; some typographical and notation clarity issues (e.g., equation refs, sign/abs).

Quality:
- Theoretical development is thoughtful and mostly sound, with main concerns on minimax justification and computational feasibility in very high-dimensional settings.

Clarity:
- Overall well organized but some proofs should be summarized in the main text and computational assumptions and implementation details clarified.

Originality:
- Novel safe min–max formulation over gradient bounds and efficient solver with guarantee of never being worse than fixed importance sampling.

Significance:
- High practical relevance for CD on GLMs with promising gains; less clear impact for SGD unless overhead and amortization are addressed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide explicit convergence-rate theorems for Algorithm 2 in convex/strongly convex settings and compare rates to fixed sampling.
- Include stronger baselines (gap-based, Gauss–Southwell, stale-gradient methods) and standardized wall-clock comparisons.
- Evaluate amortized/periodic updates of p̂ and explore data structures or incremental methods to reduce per-iteration cost.
- Expand main-text sketch of the minimax interchange and KKT-based derivation of the thresholding solution for clarity.
- Add sensitivity analyses for bound tightness, alternatives to Cauchy–Schwarz, and dependence on λ.

Pros:
- Principled, novel min–max formulation; closed-form characterization and efficient algorithm; strong CD guarantees.

Cons:
- Potentially high per-iteration overhead for large n; limited SGD gains and incomplete baseline/complexity comparisons.

Recommendation:
- Weak accept: promising and novel work with solid CD results but needs stronger empirical baselines and clearer treatment of computational practicality and convergence rates.
