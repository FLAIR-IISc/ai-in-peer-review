This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD), aiming to improve upon existing static and adaptive sampling methods. The core idea is to formulate the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem, leveraging safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to ensure a guaranteed level of performance. The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach, demonstrating potential speed-ups over fixed sampling in CD and showing competitive ratios with optimal sampling. Empirical evaluation on GLMs supports the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly in the context of coordinate descent.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing guarantees on the performance of the proposed sampling scheme relative to fixed sampling and optimal sampling. The Lemma 2.1 and Theorem 3.2 are particularly strong contributions, providing quantifiable improvements.
* **Practicality:** The proposed algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient, with the overhead introduced by adaptive sampling being relatively modest (O(n log n) per iteration). This makes it potentially applicable to large-scale problems.
* **Safety Guarantee:** The use of safe gradient bounds ensures that the adaptive sampling strategy will never perform worse than fixed sampling, which is a valuable property in practical applications.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are reasonably easy to follow.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on GLMs demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in practice, highlighting the benefits of adaptive sampling and adaptive stepsize selection.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Improvement:** While the theoretical and empirical results are positive, the improvements over existing methods, particularly in the SGD setting, appear somewhat modest. The performance gain in SGD is less pronounced than that observed for coordinate descent, raising questions about the broader applicability of the method.
* **Reliance on Safe Bounds:** The performance of the proposed scheme critically depends on the quality of the safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient. While the paper discusses how to obtain these bounds for GLMs, it does not address the challenges of obtaining accurate bounds for more complex models or non-convex optimization problems. The derivation of these bounds may be a significant bottleneck in other scenarios.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is focused primarily on GLMs with relatively simple datasets. It would be beneficial to evaluate the performance of the proposed method on a wider range of datasets and optimization problems, including those with high dimensionality or more complex objective functions.
* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the theoretical complexity is reasonable, the implementation of the optimization problem for sampling (Algorithm 4) could be non-trivial in practice, particularly for very large-scale problems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically viable adaptive sampling scheme that provides guaranteed improvements over fixed sampling.
* The approach is applicable to both coordinate descent and stochastic gradient descent, making it a potentially valuable tool for a wide range of optimization problems.
* The empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method on real-world datasets.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The improvements over existing methods are relatively modest, particularly in the SGD setting.
* The reliance on safe gradient bounds may limit the applicability of the method to certain types of models and optimization problems.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am cautiously positive about this paper. The theoretical contributions are significant, and the proposed method addresses a relevant and challenging problem in optimization. However, the modest improvements observed in the experiments and the reliance on safe gradient bounds raise some concerns. While the paper doesn’t represent a groundbreaking advance, it offers a solid contribution to the field with a clearly articulated and justified approach. I believe that the paper is of sufficient quality to be accepted to NIPS, but I would encourage the authors to address the concerns raised above in a revision. Specifically, exploring the sensitivity to the quality of the gradient bounds, and demonstrating performance on more diverse and challenging datasets would be beneficial. I would rate this as a **weak accept**. It is a solid, technically sound piece of work, but does not present a major breakthrough.
