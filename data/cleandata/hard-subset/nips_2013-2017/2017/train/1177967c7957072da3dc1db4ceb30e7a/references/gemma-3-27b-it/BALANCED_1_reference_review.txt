This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling in a way that’s computationally efficient and comes with theoretical guarantees. The authors demonstrate that their scheme provably outperforms uniform or fixed importance sampling, and recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting.  The work builds on prior research in adaptive sampling techniques [22, 5, 26, 10, 21, 23], acknowledging their drawbacks in terms of theoretical guarantees and computational cost. It contrasts with existing approaches by focusing on a *safe* approximation, ensuring performance is never worse than static sampling, and providing a characterization of the best possible approximation given limited information.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method, establishing bounds on the expected progress and demonstrating improvements over static sampling strategies, particularly in the context of CD. The analysis connecting the proposed method to optimal sampling (Example 2.3) is clear and insightful.
* **Practicality & Efficiency:** The authors address the computational limitations of full gradient-based sampling by introducing a practical algorithm (Algorithm 2) with a reasonable computational cost (O(n log n) per iteration). The efficient computation of safe bounds on the gradient (Section 4) adds to the practicality of the method.
* **Generality:** The scheme is presented as generic and applicable to both CD and SGD methods, increasing its potential impact.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The extensive experimental results on real-world datasets (RCV1, real-sim, news20) with various GLMs and regularization techniques provide strong empirical evidence supporting the theoretical claims.  The inclusion of plots showcasing the evolution of `vk` provides valuable insight into the behavior of the proposed adaptive stepsize.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The notation is standard, and the main contributions are explicitly stated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Theoretical Sections:** While theoretically sound, Sections 2 and 3 are dense and require significant effort from the reader to fully grasp.  More intuitive explanations or intermediate steps could improve clarity.
* **SGD Performance Gap:** The experimental results show only a slight performance improvement with SGD compared to uniform sampling, a contrast to the more significant gains observed in CD.  The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of this discrepancy. Why does the theory translate less effectively to SGD in practice?
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on GLMs. Exploring the method's performance on other optimization problems (e.g., non-smooth, non-convex) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The efficiency of the algorithm relies on having good upper and lower bounds on the gradient. While Section 4 discusses how to obtain these bounds for GLMs, the generalizability of this approach to other problems isn't thoroughly explored. The sensitivity of the method to the quality of these bounds warrants further investigation.
* **Comparison to Existing Adaptive Methods:** While the paper references existing adaptive sampling methods [22, 5, 26], a more direct empirical comparison to state-of-the-art techniques would be beneficial.  For instance, a comparison to more recent variance reduction techniques could clarify the unique contributions of the proposed method.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically efficient adaptive importance sampling scheme with provable guarantees.
* It addresses a relevant and challenging problem in optimization, particularly in the context of large-scale machine learning.
* The extensive experimental validation demonstrates the effectiveness of the method on real-world datasets.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical sections are quite dense and potentially challenging for some readers.
* The performance gains in SGD are less pronounced than in CD, raising questions about the broader applicability of the method.
* The experiments are primarily focused on GLMs and could benefit from broader coverage.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed adaptive importance sampling scheme is theoretically well-motivated, practically efficient, and empirically validated. While the theoretical sections could benefit from some simplification, and the SGD results are less compelling than the CD results, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically:

1. **Clarify Theoretical Sections:**  Add more intuitive explanations or intermediate steps to improve the readability of Sections 2 and 3.
2. **Discuss SGD Performance:** Provide a more detailed discussion of why the performance gains in SGD are less significant than in CD. Investigate potential factors that might limit the effectiveness of the method in the SGD setting.
3. **Expand Experimental Evaluation:** Consider exploring the performance of the method on a broader range of optimization problems (e.g., non-smooth, non-convex).
4. **Sensitivity Analysis:**  Investigate the sensitivity of the method to the quality of the gradient bounds.
5. **Comparison to SOTA:**  Include a more direct empirical comparison to existing state-of-the-art adaptive sampling and variance reduction techniques.
