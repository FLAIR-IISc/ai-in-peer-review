

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors claim to provide an efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling that is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling, while being computationally tractable without requiring full gradient computation in each iteration.

The paper's main contribution is a method that uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to approximate the optimal adaptive sampling distribution. The authors formulate this as a convex optimization problem and prove theoretical guarantees showing their method is never worse than fixed importance sampling. They demonstrate their approach on generalized linear models, claiming the computational overhead is only O(n log n) per iteration, which they argue is negligible compared to gradient computation.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper provides clear theoretical justification for adaptive importance sampling, showing that it can offer significant theoretical improvements over fixed sampling (e.g., up to n-fold speedup in some cases).
2. The proposed method does have a provable guarantee that it is never worse than fixed sampling, which is a meaningful theoretical contribution.
3. The empirical evaluation covers multiple datasets and loss functions, showing consistent improvements for CD methods.
4. The paper carefully addresses the computational complexity of their approach and provides a clear algorithm for computing the proposed sampling distribution.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Theoretical claims are limited in scope:** The "safe" guarantee only holds relative to fixed importance sampling, not relative to the optimal adaptive sampling (which requires full gradient computation). The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5-3.6) shows the method can be up to Lmin/Lmax worse than the optimal sampling, which could be significant in practice. The paper fails to demonstrate how large this gap is in actual applications.

2. **Empirical results are incomplete:** The method shows strong improvements for CD but only marginal improvements for SGD, which is surprising given the paper's claims of generality. The authors provide no convincing explanation for this discrepancy, making it difficult to assess the method's true value for SGD applications.

3. **Computational complexity claims are misleading:** The paper claims the overhead is "comparable with the cost to evaluate a single component of the gradient," but this is only true when d = Ω(n). For typical machine learning problems where d << n (e.g., text classification), the O(n log n) overhead would dominate the O(d) gradient evaluation cost, making the method computationally expensive.

4. **Lack of comparison with recent work:** The paper does not compare with recent adaptive sampling methods like "Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling" (Perekrestenko et al., AISTATS 2017) or "Non-Uniform Stochastic Average Gradient Method" (Schmidt et al., AISTATS 2015), which also aim to improve sampling efficiency with theoretical guarantees. This omission makes it difficult to assess the novelty and relative merit of the proposed approach.

5. **The "safe" aspect is questionable:** The paper claims their method is "safe" because it's never worse than fixed sampling, but this is a very weak guarantee. Many existing methods also have this property, and the paper fails to demonstrate that their method provides a meaningful improvement over these existing "safe" methods.

**Recommendation:**
The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution with limited practical significance. While the theoretical guarantee that the method is never worse than fixed sampling is valid, this is a weak benchmark in the context of adaptive sampling methods. The marginal empirical improvements for SGD and the computational overhead in typical machine learning scenarios undermine the practical value of the proposed method. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive comparison with recent adaptive sampling approaches, a clearer explanation of the CD vs. SGD performance discrepancy, and a more realistic assessment of computational complexity for typical machine learning problems.

Given the paper's limited novelty compared to recent work, the weak empirical evidence for SGD, and the misleading computational complexity claims, I cannot recommend acceptance. The method appears to be a marginal improvement over existing approaches with limited practical impact, particularly for the increasingly important SGD applications. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their empirical evaluation and theoretical claims to justify acceptance at NIPS.

This paper is on the borderline of acceptability but falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS due to the lack of compelling evidence for significant improvement over existing methods and the questionable practical relevance of the proposed approach.
