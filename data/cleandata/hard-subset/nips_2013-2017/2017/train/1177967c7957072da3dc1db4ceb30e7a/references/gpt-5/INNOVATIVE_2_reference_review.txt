Summary
This paper tackles adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) under limited information. The authors first show that the “full-information” gradient-based sampling (probabilities ∝ √Li |∇i f| for CD and ∝ ‖∇fi‖ for SGD) yields strictly better expected progress than fixed (L- or uniform-based) sampling, with potential speedups up to a factor n. The main contribution is a safe adaptive scheme when full gradients are unavailable: given per-coordinate safe lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (or per-component gradient norms), they formulate a convex min–max problem minp maxc∈C V(p,c)/‖c‖2 (where C is the box defined by the bounds). This yields a sampling distribution p̂ that:
- is optimal in the worst case over all c consistent with the bounds,
- provably never underperforms fixed importance sampling (and strictly improves it unless degenerate),
- recovers the full-information gradient-based sampling when bounds tighten to the true c.

They provide an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting-based) to compute p̂ and the associated stepsize α̂ = 1/v (where v is the min–max value), along with a geometric characterization and competitive ratio bounds. They also show how to maintain safe bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs) via simple updates (using Cauchy–Schwarz) and demonstrate empirical gains for CD on several datasets; gains for SGD are smaller.

Relation to prior work
Builds on and unifies lines of work on non-uniform/importance sampling for CD/SGD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Zhao & Zhang; Needell et al.; Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.). Prior adaptive schemes either require full gradients or rely on stale/upper bounds without worst-case guarantees and can underperform uniform sampling. The novelty here is the min–max “safe” formulation with a constructive O(n log n) algorithm and a guarantee of dominance over fixed sampling under arbitrary valid bounds.

Strengths
- Novel, principled min–max formulation that bridges full-information optimal sampling and practical limited-information settings, with a clear safety guarantee.
- Efficient algorithm with a simple structure (sorting and thresholding per coordinate) and clean geometric interpretation.
- Generic and modular: applies to both CD and SGD; recovers the full-information distribution; comes with a stepsize policy tied to the same objective.
- Theory is careful: expected one-step progress bounds, dominance over fixed sampling, competitive ratio analysis, and discussion of α coupling.
- Empirical evidence shows substantial improvements for CD over fixed sampling on multiple GLMs; the measured v-values corroborate theory (adaptive α is critical).

Weaknesses and questions
- Per-iteration cost and scalability: Maintaining safe bounds for all coordinates/components is stated as O(n) per iteration, and computing p̂ adds O(n log n). This is substantial for large n and undermines the usual appeal of CD/SGD (O(1) or data-dependent cost per update), especially on sparse data (common in GLMs). The experiments use reduced subsets; it is unclear how the method scales to full-scale sparse regimes where d ≪ n or nnz per sample is small. Can the authors:
  - amortize bound maintenance (lazy/partial refresh, periodic updates), or maintain p̂ incrementally without full resorting each iteration?
  - exploit sparsity to reduce the O(n) bound updates (only touch coordinates interacting with the active feature)?
  - provide wall-clock comparisons on full datasets without subsampling and detail nnz and per-iteration costs?
- Baselines: Comparisons are mainly against uniform/fixed and “optimal” full-gradient sampling. Missing are strong adaptive baselines that use stale gradients/duality gaps (e.g., Perekrestenko et al., Csiba et al., gap-safe rules) to contextualize practical gains at similar costs.
- Tightness of bounds: The proposed safe bounds via Cauchy–Schwarz can be very loose, especially on sparse high-dimensional text. While safety is preserved, loose bounds can make v close to Tr[L], reducing gains and potentially not justifying the extra overhead. An ablation on bound tightness vs. performance would help.
- SGD results show modest gains; discussion suggests limited benefit in that setting. Could the method be combined with variance reduction (SAGA/SVRG) and minibatch importance sampling to amplify gains?
- Convergence rates: The main text emphasizes one-step progress; full convergence statements are deferred to the appendix. A succinct theorem in the main body (rates in expectation with the adaptive p̂, α̂) would improve clarity.

Quality
Conceptually and theoretically solid, with correct use of minimax (Sion/Komiya), well-posedness, and algorithmic details. The largest technical concern is computational practicality in large, sparse regimes; the paper acknowledges costs but does not fully resolve them.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The derivations are clear, and the geometric view helps. I recommend moving a concise convergence theorem to the main text and expanding the practical discussion on bound maintenance and incremental updates.

Originality
High. The safe min–max formulation with a constructive solution and guaranteed dominance over fixed sampling appears novel. It meaningfully advances adaptive sampling beyond heuristic/stale approaches.

Significance
Potentially significant. If the computational overhead can be reduced or amortized in practice, this “safe adaptivity” could be a new default for CD/SGD where full gradients are unavailable. Even as a conceptual framework, it sets a useful benchmark and may inspire efficient variants.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and novel conceptual advance (safe min–max adaptive sampling).
- Strong theoretical guarantees and recovery of full-information optimum.
- Generic applicability (CD and SGD) with a unified stepsize scheme.
- Empirical gains for CD that match the theory.

Cons against acceptance
- Practical overhead (O(n)+O(n log n)) per iteration is high; scalability on large sparse problems is questionable.
- Limited baselines and modest SGD improvements.
- Heavy reliance on loose bounds; limited ablation on bound quality.

Recommendation
Accept (7/10). Despite practical concerns, the core idea is fresh, principled, and has the right kind of guarantee. I encourage the authors to strengthen the empirical section with full-scale sparse experiments, more adaptive baselines, and a discussion/implementation of amortized updates to make the approach immediately impactful.

Confidence
3/5 (reasonable familiarity with adaptive sampling and stochastic optimization).
