

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of adaptive importance sampling in coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors argue that while gradient-based adaptive sampling can theoretically outperform fixed sampling strategies, previous approaches either require full gradient computation (which negates computational benefits) or use approximations that may not be guaranteed to be better than uniform sampling. Their key contribution is a "safe" adaptive sampling strategy that uses only upper and lower bounds on gradient information to compute an adaptive sampling distribution. This approach is provably better than fixed importance sampling, computationally efficient (O(n log n) per iteration), and can be applied to both CD and SGD. The paper demonstrates theoretical guarantees, efficient computation of the sampling distribution, and empirical validation on several machine learning tasks.

The work builds upon and extends prior research on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) and safe screening techniques (e.g., [14, 15]), but introduces a novel formulation that provides theoretical guarantees for the adaptive sampling without requiring full gradient computation. It relates to recent work on non-uniform sampling in coordinate descent (e.g., [3, 5, 23]) and stochastic gradient methods (e.g., [6, 25, 33]).

## Strengths
1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper provides rigorous proofs of the key theoretical guarantees, particularly showing that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2) and deriving bounds on the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5, 3.6). The connection between the proposed method and the ideal gradient-based sampling is well-established.

2. **Practical computational efficiency**: The authors demonstrate that their method requires only O(n log n) time per iteration, which is comparable to the cost of computing a single coordinate of the gradient (when d = Ω(n)). This makes the method truly practical for large-scale problems.

3. **Comprehensive empirical validation**: The experiments cover multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20), loss functions (square loss, hinge loss, logistic loss), and regularization types (L1, L2), showing consistent improvements over fixed sampling strategies. The results for CD show particularly strong performance gains, while the SGD results also demonstrate meaningful improvements.

4. **Clear exposition**: The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, intuitive explanations, and well-structured algorithms. The geometric interpretation of the optimization problem (Appendix B) provides valuable insight.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited theoretical explanation for CD vs. SGD performance difference**: While the empirical results show that adaptive sampling provides much larger improvements for CD than for SGD, the paper lacks a thorough theoretical explanation for this discrepancy. This is a significant gap, as it would help practitioners understand when to apply this method.

2. **Insufficient comparison with related adaptive sampling methods**: The paper focuses on comparing with fixed importance sampling and uniform sampling, but does not thoroughly compare with other adaptive methods that use gradient bounds (e.g., [36, 22, 23]). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the case for the novelty and superiority of the proposed approach.

3. **Practical implementation details for gradient bounds**: While the paper demonstrates how to compute gradient bounds for GLMs, the description of the practical implementation could be more detailed. For example, how the bounds are initialized, how often they are updated, and how they behave in non-GLM settings would be helpful for practitioners.

4. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper does not adequately discuss scenarios where the proposed method might fail or where the gradient bounds might be too loose to provide meaningful improvements. The authors claim "very loose safe gradient bounds are sufficient," but do not quantify when this might not hold.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough empirical validation. The proofs in the appendix are rigorous and address the key claims. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The key contributions are well-explained, and the algorithms are presented in a way that makes them easy to implement.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a novel way to compute adaptive sampling distributions using safe gradient bounds while providing theoretical guarantees. While building on prior work, the "safe" approach is distinct and addresses a key limitation in the literature.

**Significance**: The method has clear practical value for large-scale optimization problems, and the improvements in convergence rates demonstrated in the experiments suggest it could be widely adopted. The method's computational efficiency makes it particularly attractive for real-world applications.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical implications for optimization in machine learning. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated across multiple settings. While the paper has some weaknesses in explaining the CD vs. SGD performance difference and in comparing with other adaptive methods, these do not undermine the overall contribution.

**Decision**: Accept.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the theoretical discussion to better explain why the adaptive sampling provides larger improvements for CD than for SGD. This could involve analyzing the specific structure of the optimization problems where the method excels.

2. Include a more comprehensive comparison with other adaptive sampling methods in the experiments, particularly those that use gradient bounds (e.g., [36, 22, 23]).

3. Provide more details about the practical implementation of the gradient bound tracking, including initialization strategies and performance characteristics in non-GLM settings.

4. Add a brief discussion of the method's limitations, particularly in scenarios where the gradient bounds are extremely loose or where the problem structure doesn't allow for efficient bound tracking.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, and with these minor improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
