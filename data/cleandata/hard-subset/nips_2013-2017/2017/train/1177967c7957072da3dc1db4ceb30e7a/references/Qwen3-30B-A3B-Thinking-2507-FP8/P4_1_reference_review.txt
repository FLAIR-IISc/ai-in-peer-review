

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address the computational infeasibility of optimal gradient-based adaptive sampling (which requires full gradient computation) by introducing a novel optimization formulation that uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information. Their key insight is to formulate the problem of finding the best sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem with respect to these bounds, providing theoretical guarantees that the resulting sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling. The approach builds on prior work on adaptive sampling (e.g., [22, 23, 36, 37]) but overcomes the main limitation of previous methods by providing a computationally efficient approximation with provable guarantees.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors prove key properties of their sampling scheme (Theorem 3.2), including that it is always better than fixed importance sampling (Li-based sampling) and that it achieves the best worst-case guarantee (i.e., minimizes the maximum possible error). The complexity analysis shows that the proposed method adds only O(n log n) overhead per iteration, which is comparable to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component for many problems. The empirical evaluation on multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with various loss functions (squared hinge, logistic) and regularization (L1, L2) demonstrates consistent improvements over fixed sampling, with clear convergence rate benefits.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with logical flow from theoretical motivation to algorithmic development to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the key contributions are clearly stated upfront. The pseudocode for algorithms (1-4) is sufficiently detailed for experts to reproduce the method. The paper adequately explains the connection between the theoretical guarantees and practical implementation, particularly in Section 4 where it demonstrates how to compute safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by proposing a safe optimization formulation for adaptive importance sampling that provides theoretical guarantees not available in previous approaches. While adaptive sampling has been studied previously (e.g., [22, 36]), this paper is the first to show that a practical approximation can be guaranteed to be better than fixed sampling. The key insight of formulating the problem as a min-max optimization (7) with respect to the gradient bounds is novel and addresses a significant limitation in the literature. The paper thoroughly reviews related work, including recent NIPS/ICML papers on adaptive sampling, and clearly articulates how their approach differs.

**Significance:** The method provides a practical solution to a well-known problem in optimization: how to implement adaptive importance sampling efficiently without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. The approach can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations with minimal overhead, making it immediately applicable to a wide range of machine learning problems. The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements in convergence speed and computational efficiency over fixed sampling, with the proposed method approaching the performance of the theoretically optimal (but computationally infeasible) gradient-based sampling.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Empirical Scope:** While the paper demonstrates the approach on generalized linear models (GLMs), it does not evaluate the method on more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks) where adaptive sampling might be particularly valuable. This limits the perceived applicability of the method to the broader machine learning community.

**Theoretical Assumptions:** The analysis assumes coordinate-wise Lipschitz continuity of gradients, which may not hold for all machine learning problems. While the paper acknowledges this by stating it applies to "a large class of objective functions," it doesn't explore the limitations of this assumption in depth.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper cites relevant prior work but doesn't directly compare with the most recent adaptive sampling methods (e.g., Perekrestenko et al. [Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling, AISTATS 2017]). A more direct comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**For Acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical contribution with provable guarantees for a practical adaptive sampling scheme
- Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets and problem types
- Clear, well-organized presentation that enables reproducibility
- Practical computational complexity that makes the method applicable to real-world problems
- Addresses a significant limitation in the adaptive sampling literature

**Against Acceptance:**
- Limited empirical evaluation on only generalized linear models
- Theoretical assumptions might not generalize to all machine learning problems
- Does not directly compare with the most recent adaptive sampling methods

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, addressing a significant gap in adaptive importance sampling. The proposed "safe" sampling method provides the first computationally efficient approximation with provable guarantees that it is better than fixed sampling. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical results are convincing, and the method is presented in a clear, reproducible manner. While the empirical scope could be broader, the paper's contributions are substantial and directly address a well-known problem in the optimization literature. The method has clear potential for immediate application in a wide range of machine learning tasks. The paper meets all four criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance) at a high level, with only minor limitations in the scope of the empirical evaluation. I recommend acceptance.
