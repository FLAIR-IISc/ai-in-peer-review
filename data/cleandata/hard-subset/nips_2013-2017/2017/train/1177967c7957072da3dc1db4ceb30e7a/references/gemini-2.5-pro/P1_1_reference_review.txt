### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The key motivation is to bridge the gap between fixed importance sampling, which is computationally cheap but suboptimal, and optimal gradient-based adaptive sampling, which is often computationally prohibitive as it requires full gradient information at each iteration.

The authors' main contribution is a principled method for dealing with partial or uncertain gradient information. They assume access to lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes for each coordinate (in CD) or component function (in SGD). Instead of using these bounds heuristically, they formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem: find the distribution that minimizes the worst-case theoretical progress, where the worst case is taken over all possible gradient vectors consistent with the known bounds. This "safe" approach guarantees that the resulting sampling strategy is provably at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform or L-based sampling), thus overcoming a major drawback of prior adaptive methods.

The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this minimax problem. It also demonstrates how the required gradient bounds can be efficiently maintained for an important class of problems: Generalized Linear Models (GLMs). Finally, comprehensive experiments on several real-world datasets show that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling schemes in both iteration count and wall-clock time, and performs nearly as well as the impractical optimal sampling scheme.

### Review

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is sound, moving from the motivation for adaptive sampling (Section 2) to the novel minimax formulation for safe sampling (Section 3). The main theoretical result, Theorem 3.2, establishes the crucial "safety" guarantee: the proposed sampling is provably better than Li-based fixed sampling in the worst case. The derivation of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the non-trivial optimization problem in (7) is a strong contribution, making the overall approach practical.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed. The authors compare their method against relevant baselines (uniform sampling, fixed importance sampling) and an oracle "optimal" method. The experiments cover both CD and SGD, multiple standard datasets, and various loss functions and regularizers. The inclusion of wall-clock time plots is crucial and convincingly demonstrates that the computational overhead of the proposed method is small enough to yield real-world speedups.

#### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, and the progression of ideas is logical. The distinction between the "ideal" but impractical optimal sampling (Algorithm 1) and the proposed "safe" practical sampling (Algorithm 2) is well-articulated. The notation is standard and consistent.

A minor point for improvement would be the description of Algorithm 4. While the core idea is presented, the description is quite condensed and the role of the variables `u` and `l` (as indices) could be confused with the bound vectors `u` and `l`. A slightly more detailed explanation or a clearer pseudocode would enhance readability for readers wishing to implement the method.

#### Originality

The paper makes several original contributions. While the idea of using gradient information for adaptive sampling is not new (e.g., [22, 23, 36]), previous works often relied on approximations (like using stale gradients or only upper bounds) that lacked theoretical guarantees of improvement over fixed sampling.

The primary novelty lies in the minimax formulation (Equation 7). This provides a principled and robust way to handle uncertainty in the gradient information and is, to my knowledge, a new approach in this context. It directly leads to the "safety" property, which is a significant conceptual advance. The algorithm developed to solve this problem is also novel and non-trivial. The paper clearly situates its contributions with respect to prior work and highlights its unique advantages.

#### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners in large-scale machine learning. The problem of efficient data selection in stochastic optimization is of fundamental importance. This work provides a practical, theoretically-grounded, and empirically-verified solution that improves upon standard methods.

The "safe" property is particularly important, as it lowers the barrier to adopting adaptive methods; practitioners no longer need to worry that an adaptive strategy might underperform a simpler baseline. The fact that the method can be applied as a generic "add-on" to existing CD and SGD solvers for GLMs further increases its potential for broad adoption. The paper also opens up interesting avenues for future research, such as developing tighter and more general schemes for maintaining gradient bounds for more complex models.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The minimax optimization framework for choosing a sampling distribution under uncertainty is elegant, novel, and provides a robust solution to a key problem in adaptive sampling.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The proposed method is provably better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), addressing a major shortcoming of previous adaptive schemes.
3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm for the sampling procedure and demonstrates how it can be integrated into existing solvers for GLMs with low overhead.
4.  **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** Extensive experiments on real data convincingly show that the method is effective in practice, leading to significant speedups in both iterations and wall-clock time over standard baselines.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection/revision):**

1.  **Limited Scope of Bound Maintenance:** The practical method for maintaining gradient bounds is demonstrated only for GLMs. The applicability and performance of the approach on other important model classes (e.g., neural networks) remains an open question.
2.  **Underwhelming SGD Results:** The empirical gains for SGD are much less pronounced than for CD. The paper notes this but offers little discussion as to why this might be the case. This could be explored further.
3.  **Analysis of Competitive Ratio:** The analysis of the competitive ratio (how close the safe sampling is to the true optimal sampling) in Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6 is somewhat preliminary and relies on either a non-convex problem or strong assumptions.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically-sound, and practical method for adaptive importance sampling. The core idea of "safe" sampling via a minimax formulation is a significant contribution to the field. Despite minor weaknesses regarding the scope of the bound estimation techniques and the less pronounced gains in the SGD setting, the strengths of the paper in its novelty, theoretical guarantees, and empirical results for CD are overwhelming. This work advances the state of the art in stochastic optimization and is a clear fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
