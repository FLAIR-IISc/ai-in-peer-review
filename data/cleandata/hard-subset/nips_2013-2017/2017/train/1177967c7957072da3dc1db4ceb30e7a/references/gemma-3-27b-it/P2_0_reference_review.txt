## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address the drawbacks of existing adaptive methods – often lacking theoretical guarantees or being computationally expensive – by proposing a strategy based on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient. They demonstrate theoretical improvements over fixed sampling, particularly in CD, and present an efficient algorithm for computing the adaptive sampling distribution.  The approach is generic and readily applicable to various optimization problems.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established field of importance sampling for reducing computational cost in large-scale optimization. It acknowledges existing adaptive sampling techniques based on gradients and duality gaps, but highlights their limitations in terms of theoretical guarantees and computational burden. It connects to recent work on accelerated coordinate descent and stochastic optimization, and positions itself as a more practical and theoretically sound alternative. The paper correctly cites relevant work in the area, including contributions from Nesterov, Richtárik, and others.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method, demonstrating its superiority over fixed sampling strategies. The analysis in Section 2 regarding the speed-up achievable in CD is particularly insightful.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The algorithm is designed to be computationally tractable, avoiding the need for full gradient computations. The efficient computation of the sampling distribution (Theorem 3.4) is a significant contribution.
* **Generality:** The approach is applicable to both CD and SGD, and can be integrated into a wide range of optimization problems.
* **Safe Adaptation:** The use of safe gradient bounds ensures that the adaptive sampling is *never worse* than fixed sampling, addressing a key concern with adaptive methods.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed scheme, showcasing improvements in convergence speed and overall performance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and explanations of the key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practicality of Gradient Bounds:** While the authors discuss how to obtain safe gradient bounds in the context of GLMs (Section 4), the generalizability to more complex models might be challenging. The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of these bounds.
* **SGD Results Less Convincing:** The empirical results for SGD show only marginal improvements over uniform sampling, which weakens the overall impact of the work. The authors acknowledge this, but further investigation into why the benefits are less pronounced in the SGD setting would be valuable.
* **Complexity of Notation:** The mathematical notation is dense, particularly in Section 2, and might require careful reading to fully grasp the arguments. While rigorous, it could be made more accessible.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are primarily focused on GLMs. Evaluating the method on a wider range of problems, including deep learning applications, would strengthen the empirical support.




**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the experimental results are convincing (especially for CD). It represents a complete and well-executed piece of work.
* **Clarity:** The paper is reasonably clear, although the notation is somewhat dense. The organization is logical, but some sections could benefit from more concise explanations.
* **Originality:** The combination of safe gradient bounds with a convex optimization formulation for adaptive sampling is novel and represents a significant contribution. While building on existing techniques, it offers a unique and valuable approach.
* **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in large-scale optimization – balancing computational efficiency with theoretical guarantees. The proposed method has the potential to improve the performance of a wide range of optimization algorithms, making it a significant contribution to the field.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and proven improvement over fixed sampling.
* Efficient and practical algorithm.
* Generality and applicability to a wide range of problems.
* Clear presentation of the method and its theoretical properties.
* Convincing experimental results (particularly for CD).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on the availability of safe gradient bounds, which might be difficult to obtain in some cases.
* Less pronounced benefits in the SGD setting.
* Dense mathematical notation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The authors address a significant challenge with a novel and well-supported approach. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017, with the suggestion that the authors clarify the challenges of obtaining safe gradient bounds in more complex models, investigate the reasons for the limited gains in the SGD experiments, and potentially simplify the notation where possible.
