

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" technique for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors argue that gradient-based adaptive sampling can theoretically outperform fixed importance sampling but is computationally expensive due to the need for full gradient computation. Their key contribution is a method to efficiently approximate the optimal adaptive sampling while maintaining theoretical guarantees that the sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling. The approach formulates the sampling problem as an optimization over safe upper and lower bounds of gradient information, with an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling distribution. The method is demonstrated on generalized linear models (GLMs) and shows empirical improvements over fixed sampling methods, particularly for CD.

## Strengths
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a provably safe approximation of adaptive importance sampling. The key strength is the theoretical guarantee that the proposed method is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), a property not established for previous adaptive sampling approaches. The computational complexity analysis is strong, showing the overhead is O(n log n) per iteration, which is comparable to gradient computation cost in many settings. The empirical evaluation on multiple datasets and loss functions (L1/L2 regularization, square loss, hinge loss, logistic loss) demonstrates clear practical benefits, especially for CD methods. The paper is well-structured, with clear notation and good organization.

## Weaknesses
The main limitation is the narrow scope of the empirical evaluation, which is limited to generalized linear models (GLMs) and does not test the method on more complex machine learning problems like deep learning or non-linear models. The theoretical guarantees are primarily established for GLMs, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address how the method would generalize to other problem classes. The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5-3.6) is technically sound but lacks intuitive explanation that would help readers grasp the practical implications. The paper cites relevant literature but could better contrast with recent advances in adaptive sampling (e.g., Perekrestenko et al. 2016), particularly regarding the computational trade-offs.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement
1. Extend the empirical evaluation to at least one non-GLM problem (e.g., deep learning on MNIST/CIFAR) to demonstrate broader applicability.

2. Provide more intuitive explanation of the competitive ratio ρk (equation 10) - perhaps with a simple example showing how it relates to practical performance.

3. In Section 4, elaborate on the limitations of the safe gradient bounds approach for non-GLM problems. What would be required to extend the method to more complex models?

4. Compare the computational overhead more directly with other adaptive sampling methods (e.g., gradient-based sampling with stale gradients [36, 37]) to better contextualize the efficiency claims.

5. In the related work section, explicitly discuss how the "safe" aspect of the proposed method differs from recent approaches like [Perekrestenko et al., AISTATS 2016] that also use adaptive importance sampling.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution with clear practical benefits for CD methods. The "safe" aspect of the sampling (always better than fixed sampling) is a significant advance over previous work. While the empirical evaluation is limited to GLMs, the theoretical foundation is robust and the method is well-motivated.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel approach to adaptive sampling (Originality), and offers practical improvements for optimization methods (Significance). The limited scope of the empirical evaluation is a weakness, but the strong theoretical contribution and clear practical benefits outweigh this limitation. The paper would be strengthened by the suggested extensions, but these are not necessary for acceptance at NIPS.

The authors have made a valuable contribution to the understanding of adaptive importance sampling that advances both theory and practice. The method is generalizable to other settings beyond GLMs, and the safe sampling framework could inspire future work in optimization with adaptive sampling strategies.
