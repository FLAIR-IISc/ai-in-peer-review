- Proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for CD and SGD that uses gradient bounds to approximate optimal sampling and is guaranteed to outperform fixed sampling.  
- Extends prior adaptive sampling work by providing an efficient, provably better alternative to fixed (uniform/importance) sampling.  

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical foundations: proves the method is never worse than fixed sampling (Theorem 3.2) and analyzes competitive ratio.  
- Practical implementation: achieves O(n log n) overhead per iteration (comparable to a single gradient component) with thorough complexity analysis.  
- Comprehensive experiments: extensive evaluation across datasets, losses, and both CD/SGD showing consistent improvements, even with loose gradient bounds.  
- Clear novelty and differentiation: shows the method is optimal given limited information and fills a gap in prior work lacking guarantees.  

Weaknesses:
- Limited analysis of SGD: empirical gains for SGD are modest and the paper lacks a deep explanation for this discrepancy.  
- Assumption on gradient bounds: efficient computation of safe bounds is demonstrated only for GLMs and broader applicability (e.g., neural networks) is not discussed.  
- Limited wall-clock overhead analysis: theoretical O(n log n) (and O(log n) in some regimes) is given, but concrete timing/speedup comparisons versus fixed sampling are not provided.  

Overall assessment:
- Strong, well-executed theoretical and empirical contribution that addresses a key limitation in adaptive sampling and is broadly applicable.  

Recommendation:
- Recommend acceptance; significant, novel, and practically relevant work suitable for NeurIPS.
