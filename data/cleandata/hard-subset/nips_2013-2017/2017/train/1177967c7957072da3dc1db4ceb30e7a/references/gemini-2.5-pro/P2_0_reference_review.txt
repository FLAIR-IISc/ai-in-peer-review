This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core problem it addresses is that optimal adaptive sampling (e.g., gradient-based) is often computationally prohibitive as it requires full gradient information at each iteration. Existing approximations can lack theoretical guarantees and may perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The main idea is to work with efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient information (magnitudes for CD, norms for SGD). Instead of heuristically using these bounds, the authors formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. This finds the distribution that minimizes the worst-case objective progress, considering all possible true gradient vectors that lie within the given bounds. The key contributions are: (1) a theoretical justification for gradient-based sampling in CD, (2) the "safe" sampling framework based on the minimax formulation, (3) an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute this sampling distribution, and (4) empirical validation showing significant speedups over fixed sampling strategies.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The central contribution—formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax problem (Equation 7)—is highly original and elegant. It provides a principled way to handle the uncertainty inherent in having only partial gradient information (the bounds `l` and `u`). This is a significant departure from prior work that often relies on heuristics, such as using stale gradients or simply sampling based on the upper bounds, which lack guarantees.

2.  **Strong "Safety" Guarantee:** The most compelling aspect of this work is the provable guarantee that the proposed sampling is never worse than standard fixed (Li-based) importance sampling (Theorem 3.2 (ii)). This "safety net" is a crucial practical feature, as it removes the risk of performance degradation that has plagued other adaptive methods. This makes the proposed method a much more reliable drop-in improvement for existing solvers.

3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve their novel optimization problem. This is critical; without it, the method would be a purely theoretical curiosity. The paper also clearly demonstrates how the required gradient bounds can be efficiently maintained for an important class of problems (GLMs). The complexity analysis correctly notes that for problems where the feature dimension `d` is on the order of `n` or larger, the overhead of computing the sampling is marginal compared to the cost of a single coordinate update.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The comparison against both uniform sampling and the theoretically optimal (but infeasible) gradient-based sampling clearly situates the performance of the proposed method. Crucially, the authors report results in terms of both epochs and wall-clock time, confirming that the theoretical gains in convergence rate translate to real-world speedups despite the computational overhead. The inclusion of plots for the `vk` value is an excellent addition, as it provides empirical insight into the theoretical mechanism driving the acceleration.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Dependence on Bound Tightness:** The performance of the method is fundamentally dependent on the quality (tightness) of the lower and upper bounds `l` and `u`. The paper uses a simple Cauchy-Schwarz based method to maintain these bounds for GLMs, which can be quite loose. While the method is guaranteed not to be worse than fixed sampling, its advantage over fixed sampling will diminish as the bounds widen. A more in-depth discussion or analysis of how the performance (e.g., the value `vk`) degrades with looser bounds would strengthen the paper. For instance, what happens when `l_i -> 0` and `u_i -> infinity` for many coordinates? The method should gracefully degrade to Li-based sampling, but illustrating this would be valuable.

2.  **Comparison to Other Practical Adaptive Methods:** The experiments compare against a lower bound (fixed sampling) and an upper bound (infeasible optimal sampling). While this is effective, the paper would be more impactful if it included comparisons against other *practical* adaptive importance sampling schemes. For example, methods that use stale gradient information [22] or those that sample proportionally to the upper bounds `u_k` [36, 23]. Such a comparison would better position the proposed "safe" approach against the existing state-of-the-art practical alternatives.

3.  **Overhead in the `d << n` Regime:** The argument that the O(n log n) overhead is negligible relies on the assumption that `d` is reasonably large. However, in many machine learning problems, one might have a very large number of data points `n` but a much smaller feature dimension `d`. In this `d << n` setting, the O(n log n) sorting cost per iteration could potentially dominate the O(d) update cost, making the method slower in wall-clock time despite faster convergence in epochs. The authors could strengthen their claims by acknowledging this limitation and perhaps suggesting strategies to mitigate it, such as recomputing the sampling distribution only every `m` iterations.

### Detailed Evaluation

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs provided in the appendix. The derivation of the optimal sampling distribution via the minimax problem is solid. The experiments are thorough and support the theoretical claims.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. It logically progresses from the ideal full-information setting to the practical limited-information case, motivating the need for the proposed solution. The notation is consistent and the algorithms are clearly presented. The "geometric interpretation" provides helpful intuition.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. To my knowledge, the formulation of finding a sampling distribution via a minimax problem over an uncertainty set defined by gradient bounds is novel. It provides a new perspective on how to design robust adaptive optimization algorithms.
*   **Significance:** The results are significant. The paper presents a practical, efficient, and theoretically-grounded method that can accelerate fundamental optimization algorithms like CD and SGD. The provable "safety" guarantee makes it a compelling choice for practitioners. This work is likely to be built upon and could influence the design of future stochastic optimization methods.

### Summary and Recommendation

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, principled, and practical method for adaptive importance sampling. Its main strengths are the robust theoretical guarantees (especially the "safety" property) and the efficient algorithm that makes the approach viable in practice. While a more detailed analysis of the impact of bound tightness and a comparison to other practical adaptive methods would further improve the work, the current contribution is already of high quality and significance. I recommend acceptance.
