

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors argue that adaptive sampling strategies theoretically outperform fixed sampling, with potential speedups up to a factor of the dimension n. Their key contribution is an efficient approximation of the optimal gradient-based sampling that requires only safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, rather than the full gradient. The authors claim this method is always better than fixed importance sampling, has computational complexity O(n log n) per iteration, and can be integrated into CD and SGD methods.

The paper builds on prior work on adaptive importance sampling [22, 36, 37] and coordinate descent methods [18, 19, 2], but aims to address the computational inadmissibility of computing the optimal adaptive sampling distribution. The authors' approach differs from previous methods by formulating the sampling problem as an optimization problem over the safe bounds, rather than using heuristics like using the upper bound or stale values.

## Strengths

The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution by providing a method that is provably better than fixed sampling. The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is rigorous, with clear guarantees about the performance of the proposed method relative to fixed sampling. The experimental section demonstrates practical benefits on real datasets, showing that adaptive sampling significantly outperforms fixed sampling in terms of convergence speed and total computation time. The computational complexity analysis is sound, and the O(n log n) complexity claim is reasonable given the sorting operation in Algorithm 4.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical gaps and unclear notation**: The paper makes several claims without sufficient justification. For example, in Example 2.3, the expression for αk(p?k) is written as "‖∇f(xk)‖22 ‖√L∇f(xk)‖2", but the denominator should be squared for consistency with the numerator. The notation "‖∇f(xk)‖22" is ambiguous (is it the square of the norm, or the norm squared?).

2. **Missing appendix content**: The paper references "Section A.1 of the appendix" multiple times but does not include the appendix in the provided text, making it impossible to verify the proofs of key claims.

3. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for the Lipschitz constants. It defines "l = (L1, ..., n)>" but then uses "L = diag(l)", which is confusing since the dimension n is used as a component of the vector. The paper should use a different symbol for the dimension.

4. **Incomplete experimental analysis**: The paper reports only slight improvements for SGD compared to CD, but fails to adequately explain this discrepancy. The theoretical guarantees are primarily for CD, but the SGD section is much less developed, with minimal discussion of why the method works better for CD than for SGD.

5. **Algorithmic implementation details**: Algorithm 2 states "Update `, u (compute safe sampling)" but does not explain how the bounds are updated in practice. Section 4 claims that bounds can be updated in O(n) time, but does not provide the necessary details to verify this claim.

6. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims "the proposed sampling is the best possible with respect to the limited information at hand" but does not rigorously prove this optimality. The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5) makes claims that are not sufficiently supported.

7. **Formatting issues**: The paper contains minor formatting inconsistencies, such as inconsistent use of "p?k" vs "p^k" in different sections, and inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents an interesting approach to adaptive importance sampling with solid theoretical foundations. The core idea is sound, and the method appears to be a practical improvement over previous approaches. However, the paper's presentation contains several technical inconsistencies and gaps that undermine the credibility of the theoretical claims. The lack of appendix content makes it impossible to verify key proofs, and the experimental section does not adequately address the limitations of the approach.

The paper's main contribution is valuable, but the presentation issues and theoretical gaps prevent it from being a strong submission to NeurIPS. The authors would need to address the notation inconsistencies, provide complete proofs, and more thoroughly analyze the SGD case to make this a compelling contribution.

## Recommendation

While the paper contains a novel and potentially useful method, the technical inconsistencies and theoretical gaps prevent it from meeting NeurIPS standards. The paper would benefit from significant revision to address these issues, particularly by clarifying the mathematical notation, providing complete proofs, and thoroughly analyzing the SGD case.

I recommend rejection due to the significant technical inconsistencies and theoretical gaps that prevent the reader from fully evaluating the claims. While the core idea is promising, the paper as written does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims.
