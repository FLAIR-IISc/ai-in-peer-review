### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The key challenge in adaptive sampling is that computing the optimal sampling distribution often requires full gradient information, which negates the computational benefits of stochastic methods. Prior work has relied on approximations that lack theoretical guarantees and can perform worse than simple fixed sampling schemes.

The main idea of this work is to address this challenge in a principled way. Instead of using a point estimate of the gradient, the proposed method assumes access to provably correct lower and lower bounds (`l`, `u`) on the gradient components (for CD) or their norms (for SGD). Given this uncertainty set for the true gradient, the authors formulate the problem of choosing the sampling distribution as a minimax problem: find the distribution that minimizes the worst-case expected progress over all possible gradients consistent with the bounds.

The authors make the following key contributions:
1.  They show that this minimax problem can be solved efficiently in O(n log n) time.
2.  They prove that the resulting "safe" sampling distribution is guaranteed to perform at least as well as standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., Li-based sampling), thus overcoming a major drawback of previous adaptive methods.
3.  They demonstrate how to efficiently compute and maintain the required gradient bounds for an important class of problems, Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).
4.  They provide empirical results on several real-world datasets showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time, and performs comparably to the optimal (but computationally expensive) full-information adaptive sampling.

This work relates to a significant body of literature on importance sampling for stochastic optimization (e.g., [18, 33, 5, 23, 36]). It distinguishes itself by introducing a robust, worst-case optimization formulation to handle the uncertainty in gradient information, thereby providing a "safety" guarantee that was absent in prior adaptive schemes using heuristics or approximations.

### Strengths and Weaknesses (Pros and Cons)

**Arguments for Acceptance (Strengths):**

*   **Originality & Significance:** The central idea of formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax problem over a set of possible gradients defined by bounds is highly novel and theoretically elegant. It provides a principled and robust framework for adaptive sampling under limited information, addressing a critical and practical problem in large-scale optimization. The "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2), which proves the method is never worse than a standard fixed sampling scheme, is a significant theoretical contribution that distinguishes this work from prior art.
*   **Quality & Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically strong. The theoretical analysis of the minimax problem is sound, and the derivation of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to find the optimal safe distribution is a crucial result that makes the approach practical. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical proofs (provided in the appendix) and extensive empirical evaluations.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established, starting from the ideal but impractical "optimal" sampling and logically progressing to the proposed practical and "safe" alternative. The use of separate algorithms (1, 2, 3) to frame the discussion is very effective. The exposition is clear enough for an expert to understand the method's details and potential for implementation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental section is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method against relevant baselines (uniform, fixed, and optimal sampling) on several real datasets and for different loss functions and regularizers. The results clearly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method, not only in reducing the number of iterations but also in improving wall-clock time, confirming that the computational overhead is manageable.

**Arguments for Rejection (Weaknesses):**

*   **Practicality Hinges on Bound Quality:** The effectiveness of the method relies heavily on the ability to compute and maintain tight lower and upper bounds (`l`, `u`). The method proposed in Section 4 for GLMs uses a simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which can yield very loose bounds, particularly if data features are not normalized or are nearly orthogonal. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on the method's sensitivity to the quality of these bounds. If the bounds are consistently loose (e.g., `l` is near zero and `u` is large), the safe sampling may not offer much advantage over fixed sampling.
*   **Limited Impact in SGD Experiments:** As the authors correctly observe, the performance gains in the SGD experiments are much less pronounced than in the CD experiments. In fact, for the chosen problems, even the optimal full-information sampling provides only a marginal benefit over uniform sampling. This might temper the enthusiasm for the method's application to SGD and raises questions about the settings in which it is most beneficial. A deeper discussion on why this discrepancy between CD and SGD performance occurs would be valuable.
*   **Scalability of O(n log n) Overhead:** While the O(n log n) cost for computing the sampling distribution is often acceptable, it could become a bottleneck for problems where `n` (the number of coordinates or data points) is extremely large and the cost of a single stochastic update (e.g., O(d) for sparse data) is low. The paper briefly touches on this when `d = O(n)`, but a more detailed characterization of the regimes where the overhead is negligible versus where it might dominate would be useful for practitioners.

### Detailed Comments

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practical solution to a well-known problem in stochastic optimization. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. The following are suggestions for potential improvement:

1.  **On the Tightness of Bounds (Section 4):** It would be beneficial to add a short discussion or even a small experiment exploring how the performance of "Safe Sampling" degrades as the bounds become looser. For instance, one could artificially relax the bounds by a multiplicative factor and plot the resulting performance. This would provide insight into the robustness of the approach.
2.  **Competitive Ratio Analysis (Section 3.2):** The analysis of the competitive ratio `ρ_k` feels less impactful than the main results. Lemma 3.5 depends on solving a non-convex problem for `w_k`, and Lemma 3.6 relies on a strong assumption that may not hold in practice. While interesting, this section could be clarified to state that these are primarily theoretical tools and not essential for the algorithm's practical performance.
3.  **On the SGD Performance:** A brief hypothesis on the limited gains in the SGD setting could be included. For instance, is it because the variance across `||∇fi(x)||` is typically lower than the variance across `|∇if(x)|` in these specific problems? Or is it because the inherent variance of the stochastic gradient estimate in SGD overshadows the variance reduction from importance sampling? Answering this could guide future work.

Despite these minor points, the paper represents a solid and valuable contribution. The proposed "safe" sampling framework is a clear step forward for adaptive methods in large-scale optimization.
