- Paper proposes safe adaptive importance sampling for CD and SGD using per-coordinate gradient bounds to approximate full-information optimal sampling.  
- Shows full-information gradient-based sampling (p ∝ √Li|∇i f| or ‖∇fi‖2) maximizes an upper-bound on one-step expected progress and can be up to n× faster than fixed sampling.  
- Casts sampling choice as a convex min–max problem minimizing worst-case V(p,c)/‖c‖2 over c consistent with bounds and proves the solution p̂ is worst-case optimal given available info.  
- Proves p̂ is never worse than Li-based or uniform sampling in the upper-bound model and recovers full-information sampling when bounds are tight.  
- Provides an O(n log n) sorting + water-filling-like algorithm to compute p̂ and a safe stepsize α̂ = 1/v̂.  
- Describes inexpensive bound maintenance for GLMs (both CD and SGD) via linearized updates and Cauchy–Schwarz.  
- Experiments show substantial gains for CD across datasets/losses and modest positive gains for SGD; observed stepsizes indicate use of extra information.  
- Technical derivations and minimax justification are sound and standard; proofs are in the appendix and main claims are well supported.  
- Caveat: per-iteration overhead (O(n log n) for sampling, O(n) for bound updates) can dominate in SGD or sparse CD and may explain limited empirical SGD gains.  
- Clarity is good overall with clear structure; minor typos and a confusing statement about overhead reduction when d = Ω(n).  
- Need to explicitly state dependence of guarantees on compactness of the bound set Ck and clarify handling of u0 = ∞ in experiments/initial iterations.  
- Novelty: safe min–max adaptive sampling with provable worst-case guarantee and efficient solver appears new compared to prior full-information or heuristic approaches.  
- Significance: practically relevant and impactful for CD/GLMs; limited immediate impact for large-n SGD without amortization or incremental updates.  
- Strengths: clear theoretical motivation, worst-case optimality under bounds, reduction to full-information, efficient algorithm, practical GLM recipes, strong CD empirical results.  
- Weaknesses/questions: high overhead for SGD/sparse CD, initialization with infinite bounds, missing comparisons to Gauss–Southwell/approximate steepest CD and other adaptive heuristics, and limited main-text convergence-rate statements.  
- Pros for acceptance: conceptually clean, theoretically grounded, efficient and implementable, strong CD results.  
- Cons for acceptance: per-iteration overhead limits applicability, missing key adaptive baselines and clarity on initialization/overhead trade-offs.  
- Suggested improvements: compare to Gauss–Southwell/approximate steepest and stale-gradient heuristics, propose amortized/lazy updates (recompute every T steps), clarify initialization and compactness requirements, move main-rate statements to main text, and provide concrete FLOP/wall-clock overhead analysis.  
- Overall recommendation: solid and original contribution with a leaning toward acceptance if authors address comparisons and practical overhead clarifications.
