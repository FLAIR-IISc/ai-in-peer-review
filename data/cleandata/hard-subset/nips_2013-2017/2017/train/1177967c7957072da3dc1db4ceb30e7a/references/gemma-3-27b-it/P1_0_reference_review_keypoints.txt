- Presents a novel safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for CD and SGD that uses bounds on gradients to compute sampling distributions.  
- Shows theoretically that adaptive sampling can speed up CD substantially (up to factor n) and is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling.  
- Proposes a practical, computationally efficient approximation (Algorithm 4) that avoids full gradient computation.  
- Formulates an optimization problem using safe upper/lower gradient bounds to guarantee performance with limited information.  
- Provides empirical validation on real-world datasets supporting the theoretical claims for both CD and SGD.  
- Positions the work within prior importance/adaptive sampling literature and randomized optimization, addressing limitations of gradient-based and gap-based methods.  
- Distinguishes itself by offering a “safe” approximation with provable guarantees without computing full gradients.  
- Strong theoretical foundation, with compelling analysis and competitive ratio bounds (Lemmas 3.5 & 3.6).  
- Practical algorithm design that is computationally efficient and suitable for large-scale problems.  
- Provable guarantee of being at least as good as fixed sampling is a key advantage.  
- Method is general, applicable to both coordinate descent and stochastic gradient descent.  
- Experimental results are supportive, especially for CD, and include multiple datasets and settings.  
- Paper is generally well-written and clearly presents theory and algorithms.  
- Notation is dense and can be hard to follow for some readers.  
- Limited discussion of the tightness and practical impact of the safe gradient bounds; sensitivity to loose bounds is underexplored.  
- Empirical gains in the SGD setting are less pronounced than in CD and need further analysis.  
- Lacks specific implementation and parameter-tuning details, hindering reproducibility.  
- Assumptions about availability/quality of safe bounds need a more nuanced discussion.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important optimization problem, offers theoretical and computational benefits, and shows empirical effectiveness.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dense notation, weaker SGD improvements, and insufficient analysis of bound quality.  
- Overall assessment: significant contribution to optimization; strengths outweigh minor weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions (simplify notation, discuss bound-quality impact, expand SGD analysis, add implementation/tuning details).
