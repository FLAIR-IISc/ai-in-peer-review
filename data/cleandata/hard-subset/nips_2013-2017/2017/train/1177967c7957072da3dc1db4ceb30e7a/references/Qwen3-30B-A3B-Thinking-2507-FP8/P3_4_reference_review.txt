

# Peer Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address a key limitation of existing adaptive importance sampling strategies: while theoretically promising, they often require expensive full gradient computations. The paper's core contribution is a novel approach that uses safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information to compute an adaptive sampling distribution efficiently, with theoretical guarantees that it never performs worse than fixed importance sampling. The authors prove that their method can achieve up to an n-fold speedup over uniform sampling in CD, and demonstrate its practical effectiveness on GLM problems.

This work builds on the literature on adaptive importance sampling (citing [5, 10, 22, 23, 26, 36, 37]), but makes a critical distinction by ensuring the sampling is "safe" - meaning it provably outperforms fixed sampling without requiring full gradient computations. Previous approaches either used approximations that could be worse than uniform sampling [36, 37] or required expensive computations [22]. The paper's theoretical contributions (Lemmas 2.4, 3.2, 3.4) establish the mathematical foundation for the safe sampling approach, while the empirical results on real-world datasets validate its practical utility.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees, proving that the proposed safe sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2) and deriving precise computational complexity bounds (O(n log n) per iteration). The authors carefully address the theoretical gap between optimal sampling (which requires full gradient information) and practical adaptive sampling.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The proposed method has minimal computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration) compared to the cost of computing a single gradient component (O(d)), making it suitable for large-scale problems where d = Ω(n). The paper demonstrates this by showing the overhead reduces to O(log n) per iteration in many practical scenarios.

3. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations, well-structured algorithms (Algorithms 1-4), and a logical flow from theoretical motivation to practical implementation to empirical validation.

4. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation**: The experiments cover multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20), loss functions (square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss), and regularization types (L1, L2), providing strong empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope for SGD**: While the paper claims generality for both CD and SGD, the empirical results for SGD show minimal improvement over uniform sampling, whereas CD benefits significantly. The paper doesn't adequately explain this discrepancy or provide guidance on when the method is particularly beneficial for SGD.

2. **Narrow Application Scope**: The safe gradient bounds are derived specifically for generalized linear models (GLMs) using Cauchy-Schwarz bounds. While the authors acknowledge this is a starting point, they don't provide concrete examples of how to extend this to more complex models (e.g., neural networks), limiting the perceived applicability of the method.

3. **Comparison with Alternative Adaptive Methods**: The paper doesn't directly compare with other adaptive sampling methods that might have similar theoretical guarantees but different computational costs. For instance, [22] proposes gradient-based sampling with stale gradient approximations, which might be more comparable to the proposed method than fixed sampling.

4. **Overlooked Practical Consideration**: The paper assumes that the safe bounds (`k, uk) can be computed efficiently for the GLM case, but doesn't address how these bounds might behave in practice (e.g., how quickly they tighten during optimization).

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of adaptive optimization. The "safe" aspect of the proposed sampling is novel and addresses a critical gap in the literature. The method is theoretically sound, practically implementable with minimal overhead, and empirically validated across multiple standard machine learning tasks.

The paper excels in clarity and theoretical rigor, making it accessible to the NIPS community. The empirical results are convincing for CD applications, though the limited impact on SGD is a notable limitation. The authors acknowledge this limitation and provide a reasonable explanation (theoretical vs. practical differences between CD and SGD).

The significance of this work lies in its practical applicability to large-scale machine learning problems. For CD methods, which are widely used in structured optimization problems (e.g., Lasso, logistic regression), the proposed method offers a theoretically guaranteed improvement with minimal computational cost. While the SGD results are less impressive, the CD results are strong enough to establish the method's value.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper. It makes a clear, well-supported contribution to the field that addresses a practical problem with theoretical depth and empirical validation. The method has the potential to be widely adopted in machine learning practice, particularly for CD-based optimization. The minor limitations (primarily regarding SGD performance and broader applicability) don't detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

The paper is a strong example of how theoretical insights can lead to practical improvements in optimization algorithms, aligning well with NIPS's focus on both theoretical and practical advances in machine learning.
