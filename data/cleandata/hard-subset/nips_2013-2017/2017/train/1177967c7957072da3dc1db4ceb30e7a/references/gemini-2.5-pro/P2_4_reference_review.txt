### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The core problem addressed is that optimal adaptive sampling (e.g., gradient-based) is computationally prohibitive as it requires full gradient information at each iteration, while existing approximations can perform worse than simple uniform sampling and lack theoretical guarantees.

The authors' key idea is to work with efficiently computable safe lower and upper bounds (`l`, `u`) on the gradient components (or their norms). Instead of using a point estimate from these bounds, they formulate a minimax optimization problem: find a sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case convergence bound over all possible gradients consistent with the `l`, `u` bounds. The paper provides a provably efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that the resulting "safe" sampling distribution is guaranteed to perform at least as well as, and typically better than, standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., Li-based or uniform sampling). The authors demonstrate how to derive and maintain the required bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide extensive empirical validation on several real-world datasets, showing significant improvements in convergence time over fixed sampling schemes.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled and Novel Approach:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization (Equation 7) is the paper's core strength. It is a highly original and principled way to handle the uncertainty inherent in using gradient bounds. This moves beyond heuristic approximations (like using stale gradients or just the upper bounds) and provides a robust solution with strong theoretical backing.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The main theoretical result (Theorem 3.2) is significant. It proves that the proposed sampling scheme is *never worse* than standard fixed importance sampling. This "safety net" is a crucial advantage over prior adaptive methods that could degrade performance in certain scenarios, making this method a more reliable choice for practitioners.

3.  **Efficiency and Practicality:** The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the optimal safe sampling distribution. For many problems where the cost of a single coordinate update is O(d) and `d` is large, this overhead is negligible, making the method highly practical. The experiments effectively demonstrate that the wall-clock time is also improved, not just the iteration count.

4.  **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem by first presenting the "ideal" but impractical optimal sampling (Section 2) and then developing the "safe" and practical approximation (Section 3). The comparison between Algorithms 1, 2, and 3 effectively frames the contribution.

5.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering both CD and SGD, multiple real-world datasets, different loss functions, and both L1/L2 regularization. The inclusion of plots for the `v_k` term, which directly relates to the theoretical improvement over fixed sampling, is an excellent addition that connects the theory to the observed performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality and Applicability:** The method's performance is fundamentally tied to the quality of the gradient bounds `l` and `u`. While the paper provides a reasonable strategy for GLMs using Cauchy-Schwarz, these bounds can be quite loose. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the tightness of these bounds. Furthermore, the applicability to other major model classes, particularly deep neural networks where maintaining such bounds efficiently is non-trivial, is not discussed. This potentially limits the scope and impact of the work.

2.  **Computational Overhead in Certain Regimes:** While the O(n log n) overhead is often acceptable, it can be a bottleneck for problems where `n` (number of coordinates/data points) is very large and `d` (the feature dimension) is small. In such cases, the cost of computing the sampling distribution could dominate the cost of the actual optimization step. The paper acknowledges this trade-off but could benefit from a more detailed analysis of these computational regimes.

3.  **Less Pronounced Gains for SGD:** The authors are commendably honest about the empirical results for SGD, where the improvement over uniform sampling is much less significant than for CD. While this is an important scientific finding, it suggests that the "safe" sampling mechanism may be less impactful for SGD. The paper could speculate on why this might be the case—perhaps the inherent variance in SGD from data sampling dominates the variance from coordinate selection.

### Detailed Evaluation Against NIPS Criteria

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs provided in the appendix. The formulation of the problem is sound, and the proposed algorithm is correct. The empirical evaluation is thorough and honestly presents both positive and less positive results (for SGD). This is a complete and well-executed piece of research.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear. The writing is precise, the notation is consistent, and the flow from motivation to theory to experiments is logical. The use of motivating examples (e.g., Example 3.1) and figures helps build intuition. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.

*   **Originality:** The paper is highly original. While adaptive importance sampling is an existing field, the idea of formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax problem over an uncertainty set defined by bounds is novel. It provides a new and powerful perspective on how to design robust adaptive schemes. It is clearly differentiated from prior work that relies on stale or heuristic gradient approximations.

*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant for the large-scale optimization community. CD and SGD are cornerstone algorithms in machine learning. This paper provides a practical, efficient, and theoretically-grounded "drop-in" improvement that can accelerate convergence without the risk of performance degradation. Given its applicability to a wide range of GLMs, it is likely that other researchers and practitioners will use and build upon these ideas. The main limitation to its significance is the open question of its applicability beyond the class of models (like GLMs) where gradient bounds are easy to maintain.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent paper presenting a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. It addresses a clear shortcoming of existing adaptive importance sampling methods by providing a "safe" scheme with strong guarantees. The combination of a principled new formulation, solid theory, and convincing empirical results makes it a standout submission. I strongly recommend acceptance.
