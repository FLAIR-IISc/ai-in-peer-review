This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling by leveraging safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients, addressing the computational challenges associated with directly computing the optimal sampling distribution. The authors provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating improvements over uniform or fixed importance sampling and showcase empirical performance on generalized linear models.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon existing work in adaptive importance sampling and highlighting its drawbacks (computational cost, theoretical guarantees). The contribution lies in providing a practical and theoretically sound algorithm that balances adaptive sampling benefits with computational efficiency. The analysis demonstrating theoretical improvements over fixed sampling (Section 2) is a strong point. The clear connection to existing CD and SGD frameworks makes the proposed method readily applicable. The presentation is generally clear, with a good balance between mathematical notation and explanation. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering various loss functions and regularization techniques on multiple datasets. The inclusion of plots illustrating the values of `vk`, providing insights into the stepsize adaptation, is a nice touch.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the authors demonstrate improvements over fixed sampling, the gains in the SGD experiments are relatively modest compared to the CD results. The explanation for this discrepancy could be strengthened. The discussion on how to obtain the safe gradient bounds (Section 4) is somewhat high-level. While the GLM example is illustrative, expanding on how this approach can be generalized to other model classes would increase the paper’s impact. The notation is occasionally dense; for example, the introduction of `l` and `L` requires careful tracking. Some of the proofs are deferred to the appendix, making it difficult to fully assess the technical details without significant effort. The connection between the proposed method and existing adaptive sampling techniques like those based on duality gaps (mentioned in the introduction) could be further elaborated. While the authors acknowledge the computational overhead, a more detailed complexity analysis, particularly concerning the sorting step in Algorithm 4, would be valuable.  The competitive ratio analysis (Section 3.2) relies on conditions ([Ck]i ∩ γ[Ck]i = ∅ and γ−1[Ck]i ∩ [Ck]i = ∅) that may not always hold in practice; a discussion of the implications would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical soundness:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method, demonstrating improvements over existing techniques.
*   **Practical applicability:** The algorithm is readily applicable to widely used optimization methods like CD and SGD.
*   **Empirical validation:**  Comprehensive experiments on real datasets confirm the effectiveness of the method, particularly for coordinate descent.
*   **Novelty:** The approach of formulating the sampling problem as a convex optimization problem with safe gradient bounds offers a novel perspective.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Modest gains in SGD:** The improvements observed in SGD experiments are not as significant as those in CD.
*   **Limited discussion of safe bound computation:** The explanation of how to obtain safe gradient bounds could be more detailed and general.
*   **Notational density:** The paper could benefit from more careful use of notation and clearer explanations of mathematical concepts.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, especially for Algorithm 4, is needed.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed algorithm is practical, and the empirical results are encouraging, especially for coordinate descent. However, the somewhat modest gains in SGD and the limited discussion on obtaining safe gradient bounds prevent me from giving a strong recommendation for acceptance at this time. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above and resubmit in the future. Specifically, a deeper investigation into the performance of the method in SGD settings, a more detailed discussion of safe gradient bound computation, and a more comprehensive complexity analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. The clarification of the connection to existing work and the competitive ratio analysis would also improve the paper’s overall quality.
