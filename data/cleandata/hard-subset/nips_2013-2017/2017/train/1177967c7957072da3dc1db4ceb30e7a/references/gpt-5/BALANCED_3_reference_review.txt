Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to use two-sided safe bounds on per-coordinate gradient magnitudes (for CD) or component gradient norms (for SGD) to choose, at each iteration, a sampling distribution p and a stepsize α that optimize the model-based expected one-step progress in the worst case over all gradients consistent with those bounds. This leads to a convex min–max formulation; the authors derive structural properties, show that the resulting scheme is never worse (in the model) than fixed Li-based importance sampling, and recovers full gradient-based sampling when exact gradients are known. They also give an O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling distribution and stepsize from the bounds, along with examples for maintaining safe gradient bounds efficiently for GLMs. Experiments on several text datasets show substantial gains for CD (close to “optimal” sampling that uses the full gradient) and smaller gains for SGD.

The work is closely related to prior importance sampling and non-uniform CD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.; Nesterov; Allen-Zhu et al.), gradient-based sampling for SGD (Zhao & Zhang; Needell et al.), and adaptive schemes based on gaps or violations (Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.; gap-safe screening). Unlike those, which either require full gradient information or use heuristic/upper-bound proxies, this paper formulates a principled minimax selection over an uncertainty set induced by safe bounds, with a clear worst-case guarantee: never worse than fixed Li-sampling and strictly better when L is uniform. It also relates to approximate steepest coordinate descent (Stich et al.) by targeting distributions that align with the magnitude-weighted gradient.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clean min–max formulation that chooses p (and α) optimally given uncertainty sets on gradients. The “safety” guarantee (never worse than fixed sampling under the model) is compelling and distinguishes this from heuristic adaptive samplers.
- Theory: Clear one-step progress analysis; Theorem 3.2 (best worst-case guarantee and dominance over Li-sampling), plus a constructive characterization (Theorem 3.4) and an efficient O(n log n) algorithm. The argument for recovering gradient-based sampling with full information is sound.
- Practicality: Concrete recipes to maintain safe bounds for GLMs with separable regularizers for both CD and SGD; updates are O(n) per iteration using simple Cauchy–Schwarz bounds.
- Experiments: On multiple datasets for CD, the method tracks the “optimal” sampler closely in iteration counts and yields wall-clock gains over fixed sampling. The study of vk values provides useful insight on stepsize adaptivity.

Weaknesses
- Baselines and breadth: Empirical comparisons are limited mainly to uniform/Li-based and the “optimal” full-information sampler. Notably absent are recent adaptive CD samplers based on duality gaps/violations (e.g., AdaSDCA, gap-based sampling, Perekrestenko et al.), which would strengthen claims of practical superiority.
- End-to-end complexity: The per-iteration overhead is O(n log n) for sampling plus O(n) to update bounds, which can dominate when n is large and d (feature dimension in GLMs) is smaller, or when access to inner products is expensive/sparse. The paper argues overhead is negligible when d = Ω(n), but this does not hold universally; more nuanced runtime analysis and sparsity-aware implementations are needed.
- Convergence guarantees in the main text: The paper focuses on one-step progress; full convergence rates and complexity bounds are deferred to the appendix and are not fully contrasted with best-known rates for alternative samplers.
- Dependence on bound quality: While Lemma 3.6 offers a competitive ratio under relative-accuracy assumptions and experiments suggest loose bounds already work, the theoretical picture remains pessimistic when bounds are very loose. A clearer characterization of performance degradation vs. bound tightness would be valuable for practitioners.
- SGD impact: The gains for SGD are modest in experiments, and conditions under which the method gives substantial improvements (e.g., variance regimes, curvature heterogeneity across components) are not clearly delineated.

Quality
The technical development appears correct and is presented with appropriate references (including Sion’s minimax theorem for min–max interchange). The algorithm for computing the saddle-point solution is well specified and analyzable. Empirical evidence supports the theoretical story for CD. Some claims (e.g., overhead negligible) would benefit from broader and sparser settings and profiling.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized, though dense. A few typos/wording issues (“inadmissible”), and some crucial results (global convergence rates) are only in the appendix. It would help to more explicitly state assumptions for Li and for safe bound maintenance in the main text.

Originality
Good. The minimax “safe” adaptive sampling with constructive solution and provable dominance over fixed sampling appears novel relative to existing adaptive/non-uniform sampling methods.

Significance
Moderate to high for CD on convex problems; the approach could have impact in practice where gradient information can be bounded cheaply. Significance for SGD seems more limited based on current experiments.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Principled, novel formulation with strong worst-case guarantee
- Efficient algorithm with clear structure
- Solid empirical gains for CD and insightful analysis of stepsize adaptivity
- Practical recipes for safe bounds in GLMs

Cons:
- Missing comparisons to strongest adaptive CD baselines (duality-gap/violation methods)
- Overhead may be non-negligible in important regimes; limited sparsity discussion
- Limited theoretical/empirical understanding of when SGD benefits
- Convergence analysis not emphasized in main text

Suggestions for improvement
- Add empirical comparisons against AdaSDCA, gap/violation-based CD samplers, and recent non-uniform sampling schemes (including accelerated variants).
- Provide wall-clock breakdowns (sampling/bounds/updates vs. gradient compute) and sparse implementations; explore amortizing the O(n log n) step (e.g., updating p̂ less frequently).
- Include main-text convergence theorems and discuss how the rates translate into end-to-end speedups vs. uniform/Li sampling.
- Analyze sensitivity to bound tightness (ablation varying `, u), and explore tighter bounds beyond Cauchy–Schwarz (e.g., data-dependent cached inner products, block structures).
- Clarify when SGD benefits (e.g., heteroscedastic component gradients), possibly with synthetic studies.

Recommendation
Borderline accept (weak accept). The theoretical contribution and practical algorithm for “safe” adaptive sampling are novel and well-motivated, with strong results for CD. Strengthening empirical comparisons and runtime analysis would further solidify the case. Confidence: medium.
