### Paper Summary

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for stochastic optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The authors first motivate the use of adaptive sampling by showing that the ideal, full-information "optimal" sampling (based on current gradient magnitudes) can theoretically offer a speed-up of up to a factor of *n* over fixed or uniform sampling. Recognizing that computing this optimal distribution is computationally prohibitive (as it requires the full gradient in each step), they tackle the core problem of designing a practical and provably effective approximation.

The key contribution is to formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem. Instead of relying on a single (potentially inaccurate) estimate of the gradient, the method assumes access to safe lower and upper bounds (`l` and `u`) on the gradient component magnitudes. It then finds the sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case objective function upper bound, over all possible gradients consistent with these bounds. The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this min-max problem. A crucial theoretical result is that the resulting "safe" sampling distribution is provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., sampling proportional to Lipschitz constants), and it gracefully recovers the optimal sampling as the bounds tighten. The authors demonstrate how to derive and maintain such bounds efficiently for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide extensive experiments on several real-world datasets, showing significant performance gains over fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time.

### Review

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. It identifies a clear and important trade-off in adaptive importance sampling—between theoretical optimality and practical feasibility—and proposes an elegant, principled solution. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and supported by both strong theory and compelling experiments.

**Quality: Excellent**

The paper is technically very strong. The theoretical development is rigorous and the claims are well-supported.
-   The formulation of the sampling problem as a min-max optimization (Equation 7) is the core technical insight and is well-justified. It provides a robust way to handle uncertainty in the gradient information, which is a significant step up from prior approaches that might use upper bounds as point estimates in a more heuristic fashion.
-   The main theoretical result (Theorem 3.2), which guarantees that the proposed sampling is provably better than Li-based sampling, is a powerful "safety" guarantee that has been missing from many previous adaptive schemes. This addresses a major practical concern: that an adaptive strategy might, in some cases, perform worse than a simple, fixed one.
-   The paper provides a complete and non-trivial algorithm (Algorithm 4) for solving the key optimization problem and correctly analyzes its complexity at O(n log n), which is practical for many problem settings.
-   The authors are careful and honest in their evaluation. They clearly state the assumptions (availability of safe bounds `l`, `u`) and provide concrete, practical examples for GLMs in Section 4. The experiments compare against the most relevant baselines (uniform sampling and the impractical optimal sampling), providing a clear picture of where their method sits. The inclusion of the plots for the stepsize parameter `vk` is a nice touch, as it empirically validates the theoretical advantage of the adaptive scheme.

A minor weakness is that the applicability of the method hinges on the ability to efficiently compute and update tight bounds `l` and `u`. While the paper does an excellent job for GLMs using a simple Cauchy-Schwarz based update, the tightness of these bounds can be data-dependent. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion on how the performance (e.g., the competitive ratio `ρk`) degrades as the bounds `u_i / l_i` become very loose. Furthermore, the extension to more complex models like neural networks is not straightforward, which somewhat limits the immediate applicability of this specific bound-generation technique, although the core sampling framework remains general.

**Clarity: Very Good**

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The logical flow from the ideal full-information setting (Section 2) to the practical limited-information setting (Section 3) is effective and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, and the contributions are stated explicitly. The notation is standard and consistent.

The one area that could be improved is the presentation of Algorithm 4. The description in the main text is very brief, and the algorithm itself is somewhat dense. While the proof and details are deferred to the appendix, a more intuitive, high-level description in the main body of how the algorithm works (e.g., explaining that it iteratively determines which coordinates must lie on their bounds based on a running threshold `m`) would improve readability for those not digging into the proof.

**Originality: Excellent**

The paper is highly original. While adaptive importance sampling is a well-studied area, this work introduces a genuinely novel approach. The key original contributions are:
1.  **The Min-Max Formulation:** To my knowledge, framing the choice of a sampling distribution as a robust optimization problem that hedges against the worst-case gradient within known bounds is a new and powerful idea in this context. It shifts the paradigm from using *estimates* of importance to reasoning about *sets* of possible importances.
2.  **Provable Safety Guarantee:** Deriving an adaptive method that comes with a formal proof of superiority over standard fixed sampling is a significant advance. This bridges the gap between the empirical success and the often-weaker theoretical guarantees of previous adaptive methods.

The paper does a good job of positioning its contributions relative to prior work (e.g., [22, 23, 36]), clearly articulating how it improves upon them by providing stronger guarantees without sacrificing computational efficiency.

**Significance: Excellent**

The results of this paper are important and likely to have a substantial impact. The problem it addresses—making adaptive sampling both fast and reliable—is of central importance to practitioners and researchers in large-scale machine learning.
-   The proposed method is practical. The O(n log n) overhead is often negligible compared to the cost of gradient computations, especially when the feature dimension *d* is large, as confirmed by the timing experiments.
-   The "safety" guarantee makes it a much more attractive option for real-world use, as it removes the risk of the adaptive strategy backfiring.
-   The framework is generic and can be applied to both CD and SGD. While the paper focuses on GLMs for deriving the bounds, the sampling algorithm itself is general. This work could inspire further research into deriving efficient bounds for other important model classes, thus broadening the impact.

Given its strong theoretical foundation and practical utility, this method has the potential to become a standard tool for accelerating CD and SGD methods on a wide class of problems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a novel and principled min-max formulation for adaptive importance sampling.
2.  Provides the first (to my knowledge) practical adaptive sampling method with a formal proof that it is always better than standard fixed importance sampling.
3.  The proposed method is computationally efficient (O(n log n)) and shown to be effective in terms of wall-clock time on real datasets.
4.  The paper is a complete work, containing strong theory, a practical algorithm, and thorough empirical validation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The practical applicability is most clearly demonstrated for GLMs; its utility for other major model classes (e.g., deep neural networks) is less clear and depends on future work for deriving suitable gradient bounds.

**Conclusion:**
The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, elegant, and practical solution to an important problem in large-scale optimization. The combination of theoretical novelty, strong guarantees, and empirical effectiveness makes this a standout paper. I strongly recommend acceptance.
