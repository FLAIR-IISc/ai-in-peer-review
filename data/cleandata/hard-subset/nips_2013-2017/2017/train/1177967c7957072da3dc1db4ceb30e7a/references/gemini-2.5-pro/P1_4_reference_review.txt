### Summary

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for stochastic optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The key challenge in adaptive sampling is that computing the optimal sampling distribution often requires full gradient information at each iteration, which is computationally prohibitive. Existing approximations can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors' main contribution is a method that leverages efficiently computable "safe" lower and upper bounds on the gradient components (or norms). Instead of naively using these bounds to estimate the optimal distribution, they formulate the problem of choosing a sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem. The goal is to find the distribution that performs best in the worst-case scenario, given the uncertainty defined by the bounds. The authors prove that the resulting "safe" sampling distribution is guaranteed to be at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., based on Lipschitz constants). They provide an elegant and efficient algorithm that computes this distribution in O(n log n) time. The paper demonstrates how to derive the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provides extensive empirical evidence on real-world datasets, showing that their method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time, and performs nearly as well as the optimal (but computationally infeasible) full-gradient sampler.

This work builds upon a rich literature of adaptive sampling for CD and SGD (e.g., Csiba et al., 2015; Perekrestenko et al., 2017; Zhao and Zhang, 2015). Its primary novelty lies in the robust min-max formulation, which provides a theoretical "safety" guarantee that was absent in previous methods based on approximations or stale information.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Theoretically Sound Formulation:** The core idea of formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a min-max problem (Equation 7) is highly original and addresses a critical flaw in prior approximate adaptive sampling schemes. The resulting "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2), which proves the method is never worse than standard fixed importance sampling, is a significant theoretical contribution. This provides practitioners with confidence that using this adaptive scheme will not unexpectedly degrade performance.

2.  **Efficient and Practical Algorithm:** The paper provides a practical algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the min-max problem with a time complexity of O(n log n). This is crucial, as it makes the method computationally feasible. The authors correctly analyze the overall complexity, noting that for many problems where the feature dimension `d` is large, this overhead is negligible compared to the cost of a single gradient update.

3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors test their method on both CD and SGD, across multiple real-world datasets and for various loss functions and regularizers. The inclusion of the full-information optimal sampler as a baseline is particularly effective, as it clearly demonstrates that the proposed method approaches the best possible performance achievable by this class of samplers. The timing plots (Figures 6 and 9) are essential and confirm that the theoretical speed-ups translate into practical gains in wall-clock time.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and structured. It begins with a clear motivation, logically progresses from the full-information setting to the practical limited-information case, and presents its theoretical results and algorithms clearly. The contributions are well-articulated and supported throughout the manuscript.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Quality of Bounds:** The practical performance of the method hinges on the ability to maintain reasonably tight safe bounds (`l`, `u`) on the gradients. The paper provides a solid example for GLMs using a Cauchy-Schwarz-based relaxation. However, these bounds can be quite loose, especially for unnormalized data. The paper would benefit from a discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the tightness of these bounds. Furthermore, the applicability to more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks), where deriving such per-coordinate or per-component bounds is non-trivial, remains an open question. While this is acknowledged as future work, it is a primary limitation on the method's immediate broader impact.

2.  **Computational Overhead in a Specific Regime:** While the O(n log n) overhead is often acceptable, it could become a bottleneck in problems where `n` (number of coordinates/data points) is very large and `d` (feature dimension) is small. In such cases, the cost of computing the sampling distribution could dominate the cost of the gradient update itself. A more explicit discussion of this `n` vs. `d` trade-off would be helpful for practitioners to understand when the method is most suitable.

3.  **Under-explored SGD Results:** The authors observe that the performance gains for SGD are less pronounced than for CD. This is an interesting result, but the paper offers little insight into why this might be the case. Is it a characteristic of the chosen problems, where the variance of gradient norms is inherently lower than the variance of individual gradient coordinates? Or does it suggest a more fundamental difference in how adaptive sampling benefits CD versus SGD? A short discussion on potential reasons for this discrepancy would strengthen the analysis.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The "safe" adaptive sampling framework is a novel and elegant solution to a well-known problem. The combination of strong theoretical guarantees, a practical and efficient algorithm, and compelling empirical results makes this a complete and high-quality piece of work. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to the scope of applicability and areas for future exploration rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed method. The paper is well-written, original, and the ideas presented are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. I strongly recommend acceptance.
