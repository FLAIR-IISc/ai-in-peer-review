### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core problem addressed is that while adaptive sampling (e.g., based on gradient magnitudes) can significantly accelerate convergence, computing the optimal sampling distribution is often computationally prohibitive, typically requiring a full gradient calculation at each step. Existing approximations based on stale information or simple bounds can perform worse than non-adaptive (fixed) importance sampling and lack theoretical guarantees of improvement.

The authors' main contribution is a principled method to construct a sampling distribution from inexact gradient information. They assume access to safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes (for CD) or norms (for SGD). Instead of using a heuristic to pick a distribution, they formulate a minimax problem: find the sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case one-step progress bound, where the worst case is taken over all possible gradients consistent with the given bounds. This "safe" distribution is provably at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., based on Lipschitz constants) in expectation. The paper provides a non-trivial and efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this minimax problem. The authors demonstrate how to maintain the required gradient bounds efficiently for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide strong empirical evidence on several real-world datasets, showing that their method outperforms fixed sampling in both iteration complexity and wall-clock time, while performing nearly as well as the computationally expensive optimal sampling. This work builds upon the literature of both fixed importance sampling [18, 25] and adaptive sampling [5, 22, 23, 36], but offers a novel, robust formulation that provides theoretical safety guarantees previously absent in approximate adaptive schemes.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Approach:** The central idea of formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem (Eq. 7) is highly novel. It provides a principled and robust way to handle the uncertainty inherent in using approximations of the gradient. This contrasts sharply with prior heuristic approaches, such as sampling based on upper bounds [36, 23] or using stale information [22], which lack formal guarantees of improvement over static sampling.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees (Quality):** The paper provides a key theoretical result (Theorem 3.2) proving that the proposed "safe" sampling scheme is guaranteed to perform at least as well as, and typically better than, standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., Li-based or uniform sampling). This "safety net" is a significant contribution, as it removes the risk of performance degradation that can make practitioners hesitant to adopt adaptive methods. The theoretical analysis is sound and well-presented.

3.  **Computational Efficiency (Significance):** The theoretical appeal of the minimax formulation is matched by a practical algorithm (Algorithm 4) that solves it in O(n log n) time. The authors correctly argue that for many problems (e.g., when the feature dimension `d` is large), this overhead is negligible compared to the cost of the gradient computation itself. This makes the proposed method a practical drop-in improvement for existing CD and SGD solvers.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically structured. It begins by motivating the potential of optimal adaptive sampling (Section 2), then clearly presents the problem of limited information and introduces its robust solution (Section 3). The inclusion of a concrete application for GLMs (Section 4) and thorough experiments (Section 5) makes the work complete and convincing.

5.  **Empirical Validation (Quality & Significance):** The experiments are comprehensive, covering multiple datasets, loss functions, and regularizers for both CD and SGD. The results strongly support the theoretical claims, demonstrating significant improvements over fixed sampling in both number of iterations and wall-clock time. The performance is shown to be very close to that of the optimal (but impractical) gradient-based sampling, validating the effectiveness of the safe approximation.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The practical performance of the method is contingent on the availability of reasonably tight lower and upper bounds on the gradient information. The paper demonstrates one method for GLMs using the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which can be quite loose. While the results are strong even with these simple bounds, the paper would benefit from a discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the quality of the bounds. It is unclear how the method would perform if the bounds were uninformatively wide (e.g., `l=0`, `u=∞`).

2.  **Limited Gains in SGD Experiments:** As honestly reported by the authors, the empirical improvement for SGD is notably less pronounced than for CD. In the SGD experiments, the gap between uniform sampling and optimal sampling is small to begin with. While this is a valuable scientific observation, it may temper the perceived impact of the method for the SGD community. An analysis of why this occurs (e.g., are the gradient norm bounds for SGD inherently looser or less informative than the coordinate-wise bounds for CD?) would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Utility of Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The competitive ratio analysis in Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6, while theoretically interesting, feels somewhat disconnected from the main contribution. The bounds derived are difficult to compute in practice (Lemma 3.5 requires solving a non-convex problem) and their practical implications are not clearly demonstrated. This part of the analysis adds less value compared to the core results on the safety and computation of `p̂`.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The formulation of adaptive sampling as a robust optimization problem is a powerful idea that elegantly resolves a key tension between the speed of adaptive methods and the safety of static ones. The work is technically sound, supported by strong theoretical guarantees, an efficient algorithm, and compelling empirical results. The weaknesses identified are minor and represent opportunities for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution. The paper is clearly written and its ideas are likely to be influential and useful to both researchers and practitioners working on stochastic and coordinate descent methods.
