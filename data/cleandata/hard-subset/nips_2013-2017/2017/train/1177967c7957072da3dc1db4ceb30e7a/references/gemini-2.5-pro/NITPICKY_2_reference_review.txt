**Summary**
This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling method, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to improve upon computationally expensive gradient-based sampling by using efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. The main contribution is a novel minimax formulation that finds a sampling distribution which is optimal under the worst-case realization of the gradient within these bounds. The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this minimax problem in each iteration and prove that the resulting sampling is guaranteed to be at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling. The method is evaluated on several real-world datasets for generalized linear models, demonstrating significant empirical improvements, particularly for CD.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization problem (Equation 7) is elegant and novel. It provides a principled way to handle uncertainty in gradient information, and the resulting "safe" guarantee (Theorem 3.2) that the sampling is provably better than fixed `Li`-based sampling is a significant theoretical contribution over prior heuristic approaches that use gradient bounds.
2.  **Efficient Algorithm:** The paper proposes a non-trivial O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the per-iteration optimization problem. This makes the theoretically appealing formulation computationally practical, with an overhead that is often negligible compared to the gradient computation itself.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results for CD:** The experimental validation for coordinate descent is thorough and convincing. The proposed method consistently and substantially outperforms uniform sampling, closing much of the gap to the impractical "optimal" sampling that requires full gradient knowledge. The plots for the adaptive step-size parameter `vk` effectively illustrate the mechanism behind the observed speedup.
4.  **Comprehensive Scope:** The paper addresses both CD and SGD within a unified framework, demonstrates how to derive the necessary gradient bounds for a relevant class of problems (GLMs), and provides both theoretical and empirical analyses.

**Weaknesses**
The paper, while containing strong core ideas, suffers from a notable number of minor flaws and a lack of polish that detract from its overall quality and clarity.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   **Typos and Formatting:** The manuscript contains several typographical errors (e.g., "deep leaning", "practical preformance") and formatting inconsistencies. The footnote on the first page ("1st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...") appears to be a template artifact and is highly unprofessional. The reference list is poorly formatted with inconsistent capitalization and missing venue information for some entries (e.g., [1], [3]). These issues suggest a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   **Algorithm Description:** The description of Algorithm 4, which is central to the method's practicality, is overly terse. The variables `u` and `l` are confusingly used as indices, shadowing their primary role as bound vectors. The logic is non-trivial, and the brief description in the main text is insufficient for the reader to confidently grasp its correctness without a deep dive into the appendix. A more intuitive explanation or a proof sketch in the main body would be beneficial.
    *   **Motivation for CD Stepsize:** The proposed stepsize `γk = αk[pk]−1ik` for CD is non-standard. While it is justified as being "directionally-unbiased" akin to SGD, the paper does not sufficiently discuss the theoretical implications of this choice for coordinate descent methods, which typically rely on stepsizes related to the coordinate-wise Lipschitz constants (`1/Lik`) to guarantee descent. While it works well empirically, the motivation feels somewhat ad-hoc.

2.  **Technical Depth:**
    *   **Quality of Bounds:** The entire method relies on the availability of safe bounds `l, u`. The examples in Section 4 use a standard but potentially very loose Cauchy-Schwarz inequality. The analysis does not explore how the quality (i.e., tightness) of these bounds impacts the performance of the sampling scheme. The competitive ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) provides some insight, but the resulting bounds (`vk/wk`, `γ^4`) appear loose and the conditions for Lemma 3.6 may be difficult to satisfy in practice.
    *   **SGD Results:** The authors honestly report that the benefit of their method is marginal for SGD on the tested problems. However, the paper lacks a deeper discussion or investigation into *why* this is the case. Is this a fundamental limitation, a property of the chosen datasets/models, or an artifact of the specific gradient norm bounds used? A more thorough analysis here would have significantly strengthened the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core technical contribution—the "safe" minimax formulation for adaptive sampling—is novel, theoretically sound, and addresses a practical problem in large-scale optimization.
*   The provided O(n log n) algorithm makes the approach practical.
*   The method is shown to be highly effective for coordinate descent, a widely used optimization technique.
*   The guarantee of being provably better than fixed sampling is a strong selling point over previous adaptive methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript suffers from a lack of care in its presentation, including multiple typos, inconsistent references, and a template artifact on the first page, which undermines confidence in the work's rigor.
*   The explanation of the core algorithm (Algorithm 4) is not sufficiently clear in the main text.
*   The theoretical justification for the non-standard CD stepsize is thin.
*   The analysis of how bound quality affects performance is superficial, and the weak SGD results are not adequately explained.

**Decision**
While the paper presents a significant and interesting core idea, the execution in the current manuscript is flawed. The combination of unprofessional formatting errors, lack of clarity in explaining the central algorithm, and superficial treatment of some key technical aspects (bound quality, SGD performance) leads me to recommend **rejection**. The work has clear potential, but it requires a substantial revision to meet the high standards of NIPS. The authors should be encouraged to thoroughly polish the manuscript, improve the clarity of their technical descriptions, and deepen their analysis before resubmission.
