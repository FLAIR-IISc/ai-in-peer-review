- Introduces a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for CD and SGD that avoids full-gradient computation using safe gradient bounds.  
- Method has O(n log n) per-iteration complexity and is provably better than any fixed importance sampling strategy.  
- Uses a minimax formulation to find the optimal sampling distribution under gradient bounds and recovers gradient-based sampling in full-information settings.  
- Provides practical implementation details for GLMs with L1/L2 regularization and an efficient sampling algorithm.  
- Empirical results show strong improvements over fixed sampling for coordinate descent, but only modest gains for SGD.  
- Builds on prior adaptive sampling work but fills the gap of avoiding full-gradient costs while providing theoretical guarantees.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: theorem guaranteeing superiority over fixed sampling and a novel minimax formulation.  
- Practical computational complexity: O(n log n) makes the method feasible for large-scale problems.  
- Clear connection between theory and practice with explicit procedures for computing safe bounds and implementing the sampler.  
- Comprehensive empirical validation across datasets, losses, and regularizers for GLMs.  
- Well-structured and clearly written presentation with consistent notation.  
- Limited empirical scope: experiments confined to GLMs, no demonstration on non-GLM or more complex models.  
- Unexplained performance discrepancy: method yields large gains for CD but only modest gains for SGD without sufficient analysis.  
- Lacks comparison to other computationally efficient adaptive sampling methods (e.g., prior works [22,36,37]).  
- Unclear sensitivity to the quality/looseness of gradient bounds; no sensitivity analysis presented.  
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to convex functions with coordinate-wise Lipschitz gradients; non-convex applicability is not explored.  
- Suggested improvements: test on at least one non-GLM task, analyze CD vs SGD discrepancy, compare to other adaptive methods, add sensitivity analysis for bounds, and clarify theoretical limitations.  
- Overall assessment: significant, well-executed contribution that is technically sound and practically relevant, though empirical generality is limited.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Pro acceptance: first computationally efficient (O(n log n)) theoretically guaranteed adaptive sampler that avoids full gradients and shows strong CD performance.  
- Con acceptance: limited empirical generality, unclear SGD gains, lack of comparisons to other adaptive methods, and limited discussion of non-convex settings.
