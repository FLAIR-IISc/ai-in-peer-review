## NIPS 2017 Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenge of efficiently implementing adaptive importance sampling (AIS) for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. While AIS often outperforms static sampling, it's hampered by computational cost and theoretical guarantees that can be worse than uniform sampling. The authors propose a novel, "safe" AIS scheme that can be computed efficiently in each iteration, provides provable improvements over fixed sampling, and recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting. They achieve this by formulating the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem based on *safe* bounds on the gradient, and proposing an efficient algorithm for solving it.  The paper rigorously analyzes the theoretical properties of their approach and demonstrates its effectiveness through experiments on real-world datasets.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on a rich history of work on stochastic optimization and importance sampling. It correctly situates itself within the existing literature on uniform sampling [27, 29, 7, 8], fixed importance sampling [18, 19, 2, 33], and adaptive sampling techniques like gradient-based sampling [22, 5, 26, 10, 21, 23]. It acknowledges the drawbacks of previous adaptive methods regarding computational cost and theoretical guarantees [23, 3]. The connection to duality gap-based sampling [5, 23] for CD and the work by Zhao and Zhang [36] on SGD with gradient-based sampling is clearly established. The novelty lies in the safe, computationally tractable adaptation strategy, and the provable guarantees it offers.  The paper also draws on the broader literature of coordinate descent [9, 11, 35, 27] and related optimization techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of the proposed method. The lemmas, theorems, and proofs convincingly demonstrate the improvements over fixed sampling and the recovery of gradient-based sampling in the full-information scenario.  The analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) is particularly insightful.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient, requiring only O(n log n) time per iteration, which is comparable to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component in many scenarios. This addresses a significant practical limitation of previous adaptive sampling approaches.
* **Practicality & Safety:** The concept of "safe" sampling, relying on upper and lower bounds on the gradient, is a clever and practical approach. The paper demonstrates how these bounds can be efficiently maintained for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs), making the method readily applicable to a wide range of machine learning problems.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation on multiple datasets and with different loss functions (squared hinge, logistic, square loss) provides strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness.  The inclusion of plots showing the evolution of *vk*, highlighting the adaptive stepsize, is particularly valuable.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the concepts are explained in a concise and accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **GLM Focus in Empirics:** While the demonstration of safe gradient bound maintenance for GLMs is valuable, the experimental evaluation is heavily focused on these models.  It would be beneficial to see results on a broader range of problems, perhaps including those with more complex data structures or non-convex objectives, to assess the method’s generalizability.
* **Limited Discussion of Bound Tightness:** The performance of the method is sensitive to the quality of the gradient bounds. While the paper acknowledges this, there's limited discussion of strategies for obtaining tighter bounds beyond the simple approach based on scalar products.  Exploring more sophisticated bound estimation techniques could further improve performance.
* **SGD Results are Less Dramatic:** The experiments show less substantial improvements with SGD compared to CD. While the paper acknowledges this, a more in-depth analysis of why the benefits are less pronounced in the SGD setting would be useful.  Perhaps the benefits are masked by other factors (e.g., step size selection) in SGD.
* **Complexity of Algorithm 1:** Although theoretically optimal, Algorithm 1 (full gradient sampling) is impractical for large datasets. The paper rightly focuses on the practical Algorithm 2, but the contrast highlights the significant gap between theoretical optimality and computational feasibility. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs involved could be included.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed safe adaptive sampling scheme is a novel and significant contribution to the field of stochastic optimization.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The rigorous theoretical analysis provides strong guarantees about the method’s performance.
* **Practicality:** The computational efficiency and the ability to maintain safe gradient bounds make the method practical for real-world applications.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on relevant datasets.
* **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental challenge in stochastic optimization and has the potential to improve the performance of a wide range of machine learning algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Scope of Empirical Validation:** The reliance on GLMs for the majority of the experimental evaluation is a minor concern.
* **SGD Results are Less Convincing:** The relatively small improvements observed with SGD might raise questions about the method’s applicability to all stochastic optimization scenarios.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The proposed safe adaptive sampling scheme is well-motivated, theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically validated. While there are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I **strongly recommend acceptance** to NIPS 2017.  The authors have tackled an important problem and provided a compelling solution that is both theoretically rigorous and practically relevant.



**Confidence:** 9/10
