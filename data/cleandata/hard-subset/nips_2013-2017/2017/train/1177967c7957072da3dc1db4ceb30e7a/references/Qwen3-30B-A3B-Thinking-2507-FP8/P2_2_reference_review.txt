

# Review: "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address a critical limitation in adaptive importance sampling: while optimal gradient-based sampling can theoretically provide significant speedups over fixed sampling strategies, it's often computationally infeasible to compute the optimal distribution in each iteration. Their key insight is to formulate the problem of finding the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem using safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information. The proposed method guarantees that the sampling distribution is always better than fixed importance sampling while being efficiently computable in O(n log n) time per iteration. The authors demonstrate their approach on generalized linear models where they can efficiently track the necessary gradient bounds, and provide empirical results showing consistent improvements over fixed sampling strategies.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical guarantees. The authors prove that their proposed sampling distribution (p̂k) is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), which is a significant contribution as previous adaptive methods lacked such guarantees. The proof techniques are rigorous, and the authors provide a detailed appendix for the proofs. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, showing that the overhead of the proposed method (O(n log n) per iteration) is comparable to the cost of computing a single gradient component (O(d) for GLMs), making it practical for large-scale problems.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20), loss functions (squared hinge, logistic), and regularization types (L1, L2). The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms fixed importance sampling in both convergence speed and computational efficiency. The authors also provide insights into the "theoretical sampling quality" by tracking the vk values, which demonstrate the method's ability to leverage gradient information effectively.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The key contributions are stated upfront, and the notation is consistently defined. The mathematical presentation is precise, and the algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-4) are easy to follow. The authors effectively use examples to illustrate their points (Examples 2.2 and 2.3), which helps readers grasp the theoretical concepts. The empirical results are presented with clear figures showing convergence behavior, iteration complexity, and timing versus accuracy.

One minor improvement would be to include a brief comparison with recent adaptive sampling methods (e.g., Perekrestenko et al., "Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling," AISTATS 2017) in the related work section to better position their contribution. The paper could also benefit from a more detailed explanation of why adaptive sampling works better for CD than for SGD in their experiments, as this is a notable finding that isn't fully explained.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of adaptive sampling. While adaptive importance sampling has been studied before (e.g., [22, 36, 37]), previous approaches either lacked theoretical guarantees or were computationally expensive. The authors' key innovation is using safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information in a novel way to formulate an optimization problem that guarantees the sampling is never worse than fixed importance sampling.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work in the introduction and discussion. For instance, they note that previous methods like [36, 37] use upper bounds (e.g., uk) to approximate the optimal sampling, but these can sometimes be worse than uniform sampling. In contrast, their method provides a theoretical guarantee that the sampling will always be better than fixed sampling. This is a substantial conceptual advance over previous approaches.

## Significance
The proposed method has significant practical implications for large-scale machine learning. The computational overhead is minimal (O(n log n) per iteration, which is often dominated by the cost of computing a single gradient component), making it highly applicable to real-world problems. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages in convergence speed without significant computational penalty.

The paper addresses a fundamental practical limitation in adaptive importance sampling that has been a barrier to its widespread adoption. The authors show that their method can be applied to a broad class of problems (GLMs) with efficient gradient bound tracking, and they note that the approach can be extended to other problem types. The results on real datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with varying dimensions and sparsity patterns demonstrate the method's robustness.

## Strengths
- Clear theoretical guarantees that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling
- Practical algorithm with efficient O(n log n) implementation
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and problem types
- Demonstrated computational efficiency (overhead is small compared to gradient evaluation)
- Well-structured paper with clear mathematical presentation

## Weaknesses
- The empirical evaluation is limited primarily to generalized linear models; it would be stronger to demonstrate the approach on more diverse problem types (e.g., non-linear models, deep learning)
- The paper doesn't thoroughly explain why adaptive sampling works better for CD than for SGD in their experiments, which is a notable finding
- The comparison is primarily against fixed sampling, not against other adaptive sampling methods that might have different computational trade-offs
- The connection to recent work (e.g., Perekrestenko et al., 2017) could be more explicitly discussed

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The theoretical guarantees are novel and important, and the computational approach is practical for large-scale applications. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages over fixed sampling strategies. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded to more diverse problem types, the current results are sufficient to demonstrate the method's value for a broad class of optimization problems.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria well. It is technically sound, clearly written, original in its approach, and has significant practical implications for large-scale machine learning. The proposed method addresses a real limitation in adaptive importance sampling that has been a barrier to its adoption, and the authors provide a practical solution with theoretical guarantees.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
