### Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

#### Summary

This paper addresses a key challenge in large-scale optimization with methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD): how to perform adaptive importance sampling efficiently and reliably. While adaptive sampling (e.g., based on gradient magnitudes) is known to offer significant theoretical speedups over fixed or uniform sampling, its practical application is often hampered by two issues: (1) the high computational cost of calculating the optimal sampling distribution at each step, and (2) the risk that approximations used to reduce this cost can lead to performance that is worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors propose a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme that elegantly resolves this dilemma. The core idea is to work with efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient information (`l_k`, `u_k`) rather than exact values. Instead of using a point estimate from these bounds (e.g., the upper bound `u_k`), the authors formulate the problem of choosing a sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. Specifically, they seek the distribution `p_k` that minimizes the worst-case value of a progress metric, where the worst case is taken over all possible gradient vectors consistent with the known bounds.

The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It provides a principled minimax formulation for finding a robust sampling distribution given uncertain gradient information.
2.  It proves that the resulting "safe" sampling scheme is guaranteed to be no worse than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., `L_i`-based sampling), thus justifying the "safe" moniker.
3.  It develops an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm to solve this minimax problem, making the scheme practical.
4.  It demonstrates how to maintain the required gradient bounds efficiently for an important class of problems: Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).
5.  It provides strong empirical evidence on several real-world datasets showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling and closely tracks the performance of the impractical "optimal" sampling that uses full gradient information.

#### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical foundation is sound. The minimax formulation (Eq. 7) is a principled and novel way to handle uncertainty in the gradient information. The main theoretical results (Theorems 3.2 and 3.4) are solid and directly support the paper's central claims of safety and efficiency. The proofs, deferred to the appendix, appear correct. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare their method not only against standard baselines (uniform sampling) but also against the "optimal" full-information adaptive sampler, which serves as a powerful oracle benchmark. The inclusion of both iteration-wise and time-wise performance plots demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the method, showing the `O(n log n)` overhead is well-justified.

*   **Weaknesses:** The method for maintaining the gradient bounds for GLMs (Section 4), while effective, relies on a simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequality which can be loose. The paper's framework is generic and works with any safe bounds, but the performance is naturally tied to the tightness of these bounds. While the experiments show that even these simple bounds are sufficient for a significant speedup, a brief discussion on the sensitivity to bound quality could strengthen the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   **Strengths:** The narrative flows logically from the motivation (Section 1-2), to the core theoretical contribution (Section 3), to practical implementation details (Section 4), and finally to empirical validation (Section 5). The problem is clearly motivated, and the proposed solution is well-explained. The notation is consistent and easy to follow. Algorithms 1-4 are clearly presented, and the figures are informative, effectively visualizing the performance gains. The inclusion of the plot showing the evolution of `v_k` is particularly insightful, as it directly connects the empirical speed-up to the theoretical stepsize improvement.

*   **Weaknesses:** No significant weaknesses in clarity. A minor point is that the exposition of Algorithm 4 could benefit from a slightly more intuitive walk-through in the main text, but the current description is sufficient for an expert reader.

**Originality:** The paper presents a significant and original contribution to the field of stochastic optimization.
*   **Strengths:** The central idea of using a minimax framework to derive a robust sampling distribution from interval bounds on the gradient is, to my knowledge, novel. Previous works on adaptive sampling either assumed access to full information [22], used stale information [22, 1], or used point estimates from bounds [36, 23], none of which came with a formal guarantee of being better than fixed sampling. This paper's "safety" guarantee is a key differentiator and a major conceptual advance. The `O(n log n)` algorithm for solving the specific optimization problem is also a novel technical contribution.

*   **Weaknesses:** The paper builds upon a well-established line of research in adaptive importance sampling. However, it clearly distinguishes its contributions and addresses a well-defined and important gap in the existing literature.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant and likely to be impactful.
*   **Strengths:** The paper provides a practical "drop-in" improvement for a wide range of CD and SGD methods. The problem it addresses is fundamental to large-scale machine learning. The proposed solution is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, offering a rare combination of strong guarantees and practical speedups. Because the framework is generic (requiring only safe bounds `l, u`), it opens up avenues for future research into maintaining such bounds for other complex models (e.g., neural networks), potentially broadening its impact significantly. Practitioners using CD/SGD for GLMs can immediately benefit from this work.

#### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, principled, and robust method for adaptive importance sampling with a "safety" guarantee that was missing in prior art.
*   **Pro:** The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs and an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm.
*   **Pro:** The method is shown to be practically effective through extensive experiments on real-world datasets, demonstrating significant improvements in both iteration count and wall-clock time over standard baselines.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and addresses a problem of high significance to the NIPS community.

#### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   **Con (minor):** The practical demonstration focuses on GLMs. The extension to more complex models like deep neural networks is mentioned as future work but remains an open question, as maintaining tight and cheap gradient bounds in that setting is non-trivial. This, however, does not detract from the value of the current contribution.

#### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization. It solves an important practical problem with a theoretically elegant and novel solution. The "safe" guarantee is a powerful feature that makes adaptive sampling a much more attractive and reliable tool. The paper is well-executed in all aspects: theory, algorithm design, and empirical validation. I strongly recommend acceptance.
