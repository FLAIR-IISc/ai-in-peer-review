This paper proposes a new adaptive importance sampling method, "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to address the impracticality of optimal gradient-based sampling, which requires full gradient information at each iteration. Instead of using point estimates or stale values for the unknown gradient components, the authors assume access to safe lower and lower bounds (`l_k`, `u_k`) on the gradient magnitudes.

The main contribution is a novel formulation for choosing the sampling distribution. The authors propose solving a minimax problem: find the sampling distribution `p` that minimizes the worst-case expected progress, where the worst case is taken over all possible gradient vectors `c` consistent with the bounds `l_k <= c <= u_k`. This approach yields a sampling strategy that is provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., based on Lipschitz constants). The paper provides an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm to solve this minimax problem at each iteration. The authors demonstrate the method on generalized linear models (GLMs), showing how simple bounds can be maintained efficiently. Empirical results on several real-world datasets suggest strong performance for CD, nearly matching the impractical optimal sampling and significantly outperforming uniform sampling.

### Strengths:

1.  **Principled Theoretical Formulation:** The minimax formulation for choosing the sampling distribution under uncertainty is elegant and theoretically sound. It provides a robust way to handle the lack of full gradient information, which is a central challenge for adaptive methods.

2.  **Provable "Safety" Guarantee:** A key strength is the theoretical guarantee (Theorem 3.2) that the proposed sampling is never worse than a standard fixed importance sampling scheme (Li-based sampling). This "safety" net addresses a significant drawback of previous adaptive heuristics, which can sometimes underperform even uniform sampling.

3.  **Efficient Implementation:** The authors provide an `O(n log n)` algorithm to compute the proposed sampling distribution. This makes the approach computationally feasible, as the overhead is often manageable compared to the cost of a single coordinate gradient evaluation, especially in high-dimensional settings (`d >> n`).

4.  **Strong Empirical Performance for Coordinate Descent:** The experimental results for CD are compelling. On a variety of problems, the proposed method demonstrates a substantial improvement in convergence speed (both in iterations and wall-clock time) over uniform sampling and performs nearly as well as the impractical optimal sampler that uses the full gradient.

### Weaknesses:

1.  **Crucial Dependence on Bound Quality:** The practical performance of the entire method is critically dependent on the tightness of the lower and upper bounds (`l`, `u`). The paper derives these bounds for GLMs using a simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which can be very loose. The paper lacks a thorough discussion or analysis of how the method's performance degrades as the bounds become looser. If the bounds are not informative, the "safe" sampling may offer little to no advantage over fixed sampling, negating its purpose.

2.  **Unconvincing Results for SGD:** While the method is presented as being generic for both CD and SGD, the empirical results for SGD are weak. In Figures 7-9, the proposed method offers only a marginal improvement over uniform sampling. This is in stark contrast to the significant gains observed for CD. The paper does not offer a satisfactory explanation for this discrepancy. This raises questions about the general applicability of the approach and suggests its benefits might be confined to CD-type methods.

3.  **Insufficient Baselines in Experiments:** The experiments compare the proposed method primarily against uniform sampling and the *impractical* optimal sampling. A crucial comparison is missing against other *practical* adaptive importance sampling schemes, such as those that use stale gradient information [22] or those that use the upper bounds directly as a proxy for the gradient magnitudes [36, 23], which the authors themselves criticize in the introduction. Without demonstrating superiority over these established practical heuristics, the significance of the proposed method is not fully established.

4.  **Overhead Justification:** The claim that the `O(n log n)` overhead is negligible is only true under certain data regimes (e.g., `d = Omega(n)`). For many problems where `n >> d`, this overhead could be substantial compared to the `O(d)` cost of a coordinate update. A more detailed analysis of the computational trade-offs would strengthen the paper.

### Summary of Recommendation:

The paper presents a theoretically appealing and novel approach to adaptive importance sampling with a valuable safety guarantee. The strong empirical results for coordinate descent are a clear highlight. However, the work is let down by a lack of rigorous experimental validation. The unconvincing performance for SGD undermines the claim of generality, and the absence of comparisons to other practical adaptive methods makes it difficult to assess the true state-of-the-art contribution. Furthermore, the performance is heavily tied to the quality of gradient bounds, a topic that deserves a more in-depth treatment. While the core idea is strong, the paper in its current form feels incomplete due to these experimental and analytical gaps.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and principled minimax formulation for adaptive sampling under uncertainty.
*   Provides a "safety" guarantee, provably outperforming fixed importance sampling, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
*   Demonstrates very strong empirical performance for Coordinate Descent methods.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Lack of comparison against relevant and practical adaptive sampling baselines.
*   Weak empirical results for SGD, questioning the method's claimed generality.
*   Insufficient analysis of the method's dependency on the quality of the gradient bounds, which is central to its practical utility.
