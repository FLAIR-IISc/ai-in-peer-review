### Paper Summary

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling". The core problem addressed is that optimal adaptive sampling (e.g., gradient-based) is computationally expensive as it requires full gradient information at each step, while approximations can be unstable and lack theoretical guarantees, sometimes performing worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors' key idea is to operate with efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient information (`l_k` and `u_k`). Instead of using these bounds naively (e.g., sampling proportional to `u_k`), they formulate the problem of choosing the sampling distribution `p_k` as a minimax optimization problem. Specifically, they seek the distribution `p_k` that minimizes the worst-case convergence bound, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradient vectors consistent with the bounds `l_k` and `u_k`. The main contributions are: (1) a theoretical analysis showing the potential speedup of gradient-based sampling for CD; (2) the novel "safe" sampling formulation and proof that it is guaranteed to be better than fixed importance sampling; (3) an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve for this sampling distribution; and (4) strong empirical validation, particularly for CD methods on real-world datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The central contribution—formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax problem (Eq. 7)—is highly original and elegant. It provides a robust way to handle uncertainty about the full gradient, directly addressing a key limitation of prior work that relied on potentially poor approximations (e.g., stale gradients or using only upper bounds). This robust optimization perspective is a significant conceptual advance in the area of adaptive sampling.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees ("Safety"):** A major strength is the provable "safety" of the proposed scheme. Theorem 3.2(ii) formally establishes that the method's worst-case performance is always better than or equal to that of standard fixed `L_i`-based importance sampling (which includes uniform sampling as a special case). This is a crucial guarantee that has been missing from previous practical adaptive methods and makes the proposed scheme highly reliable.

3.  **Efficient Implementation:** The theoretical contribution is paired with a practical and efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) for solving the minimax problem in O(n log n) time. The authors correctly argue that for many problems where the data dimension `d` is large, this overhead is negligible compared to the cost of a single coordinate update. This makes the method genuinely practical.

4.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation for CD:** The experiments for coordinate descent are thorough and convincing. The method is compared against appropriate baselines (uniform, optimal full-information sampling) on several real datasets. The results in Figures 3-6 clearly demonstrate that the proposed method (Algorithm 2) closely tracks the performance of the oracle-like optimal sampling (Algorithm 1) and significantly outperforms fixed sampling, both in terms of iteration count and wall-clock time. The inclusion of the `v_k` plot is also very insightful, as it visually confirms that the adaptive step sizes are much more aggressive and effective than the conservative fixed ones.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Less Compelling SGD Results:** While the framework is presented for both CD and SGD, the empirical results for SGD (Figures 7-9) are much less impressive. The authors honestly report that for their experiments, even the optimal full-information sampling provides only a marginal benefit over uniform sampling. While this honesty is commendable, the paper would be strengthened by a deeper discussion of *why* this might be the case. Is there a fundamental difference in the variance of gradient norms (`||∇f_i(x)||`) versus coordinate-wise gradient magnitudes (`|∇_i f(x)|`) on these sparse datasets? Providing some intuition or analysis here would add significant value and help practitioners understand when to expect large gains from this method.

2.  **Simplicity of Bounding Technique:** The proposed method for obtaining the safe bounds in Section 4, while practical and effective, is based on a simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequality. This can be quite loose, especially for data with low feature correlation. While the framework is general and can accommodate any safe bounds, the paper's performance claims rest on this specific, simple technique. A brief discussion on the potential impact of bound tightness or pointers to more sophisticated bounding techniques would have been a useful addition.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the theoretical development is logical, and the notation is consistent. The progression from the full-information setting (Section 2) to the limited-information setting (Section 3) is very effective for didactic purposes.
*   **Quality:** The technical quality is very high. The proofs appear sound, and the connection between the minimax problem (Eq. 7), its geometric interpretation, and the efficient solver (Algorithm 4) is a complete and satisfying piece of theoretical work. The experiments are well-designed and support the main claims of the paper for the CD case.
*   **Originality and Significance:** The work is highly original. While adaptive importance sampling is a known concept, the "safe" robust optimization approach is novel and solves a critical problem in the field. The significance is high, as this method provides a "drop-in" replacement for standard sampling in CD/SGD that offers potentially large speedups with a guarantee of no performance degradation. It has the potential to become a new standard for importance sampling in first-order methods.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper with a novel, theoretically sound, and practically relevant contribution. The proposed "safe" sampling scheme elegantly solves the dilemma between expensive but optimal adaptive sampling and cheap but potentially unstable approximations. The paper combines rigorous theory with an efficient algorithm and compelling empirical evidence (especially for CD). The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work. This paper represents a clear advance for the optimization community and is an excellent fit for NeurIPS.
