### Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The central idea is to make adaptive sampling "safe," meaning it is provably at least as good as a fixed, non-adaptive importance sampling scheme (e.g., uniform or Li-based sampling). The authors argue that while optimal gradient-based sampling can offer significant speedups, it is computationally prohibitive. Existing approximations based on stale values or bounds lack theoretical guarantees and can be worse than fixed sampling. To address this, the authors frame the selection of the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem. Given only lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes for each coordinate (or component), they seek a distribution that minimizes the worst-case variance of the update over all possible gradients consistent with those bounds. The main contributions are this min-max formulation, a proof that the resulting sampler is indeed "safe," an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute this distribution, and a demonstration of how to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs). Experimental results on several real-world datasets show that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling and performs comparably to the impractical optimal sampling scheme.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The core idea of formulating the sampling problem as a min-max optimization problem (Equation 7) is novel and elegant. It provides a principled way to handle uncertainty (represented by the bounds `l` and `u`) and directly leads to the "safety" guarantee, which is a significant conceptual advance over previous heuristic approximations.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a key theoretical result (Theorem 3.2) proving that the proposed sampling scheme `p̂` is provably better in the worst-case than the standard Li-based sampling. This "safety net" is a crucial property that has been missing from many prior adaptive sampling methods.

3.  **Efficient Algorithm:** The theoretical contribution is complemented by a practical and efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) for solving the min-max problem in O(n log n) time. This makes the proposed method computationally feasible, with an overhead that is often negligible compared to the gradient computation itself, as argued in Section 4.

4.  **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method for CD. The authors compare against relevant baselines (uniform and optimal sampling) and show strong performance in both iteration count and wall-clock time. The inclusion of the `vk` plots provides valuable insight into the behavior of the adaptive step sizes. The honest reporting that the gains are less pronounced for SGD is a sign of careful scientific evaluation.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, despite its strong core ideas, suffers from a significant number of technical inaccuracies, clarity issues, and formatting errors that detract from its quality and call its immediate reproducibility into question.

1.  **Critical Inconsistencies in Algorithm Definitions:** There appears to be a fundamental error in the update steps presented in the paper.
    *   Equation (2) defines the CD update as `xk+1 = xk − γk∇ikf(xk)eik`, with the stepsize `γk = αk[pk]−1ik`. This implies the update is `xk+1 = xk - (αk / [pk]ik) ∇ikf(xk) eik`.
    *   However, Algorithms 1, 2, and 3 all state the update as `xk+1 := xk − αk[pk]ik∇ikf(xk)` (or variants with hats/stars). This is a completely different update rule where the stepsize *increases* for higher-probability coordinates, which is the opposite of the standard importance sampling mechanism. Furthermore, the vector `eik` is missing from the update equations in all three algorithm boxes in Figure 1. This is a major technical flaw that makes the presented algorithms inconsistent with the analysis.

2.  **Incorrect Gradient Update Formula:** The method for maintaining gradient bounds in Section 4 for CD is based on Equation (11): `∇if(xk+1)−∇if(xk) = γk〈ai,aik〉 , ∀i 6= ik`. This formula seems incorrect. For a least-squares objective, the change in `∇if(xk)` depends on the step taken, `-(step_size) * ∇ikf(xk)`, and the Hessian term `〈ai,aik〉`. The formula presented omits the `∇ikf(xk)` term and uses an undefined `γk`. This error undermines the entire procedure for maintaining the safe bounds `l` and `u`, which is a cornerstone of the paper's practical application.

3.  **Lack of Clarity:**
    *   The presentation of Algorithm 4 is difficult to follow. The variables `u` and `l` are used as indices, which is confusingly similar to the notation for the upper and lower bound vectors. The description of the update `m := ‖c‖22 · ‖ √ Lc‖−11` as being "as in (9)" is circular, as (9) defines `c` in terms of `m`. The logic of the algorithm is not self-evident from the main text.
    *   The condition in Lemma 3.6 for the competitive ratio is obscure and possibly contains a typo. The conditions `[Ck]i ∩ γ[Ck]i = ∅` and `γ−1[Ck]i ∩ [Ck]i = ∅` are highly unusual. A more standard condition would relate the width of the interval, e.g., `[uk]i ≤ γ [`k]i`. The current phrasing is difficult to interpret and apply.

4.  **Minor but Numerous Presentation Flaws:**
    *   The reference list is poorly formatted, with many entries containing empty fields (e.g., `[1] Alain et al., , , ,`). This indicates a lack of care in the final manuscript preparation.
    *   There is a typo in the Notation section: `l = (L1, . . . , n)>` should be `l = (L1, . . . , Ln)>`.
    *   The first page footer contains a leftover template string: "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017), Long Beach, CA, USA."
    *   In Equation (3), the parenthetical `(1)` seems to be a misplaced reference to Equation (1).

**Recommendation**

While the core conceptual contribution of this paper—a principled, safe, and efficient min-max approach to adaptive sampling—is significant and promising, the manuscript in its current form is marred by several critical technical errors and a general lack of polish. The inconsistencies in the core update equations and the gradient tracking formula are particularly concerning as they invalidate key parts of the proposed method and its analysis. Due to the number and severity of these issues, the paper is not ready for publication. I recommend **rejection**, with strong encouragement for the authors to thoroughly revise their work. A corrected and polished version of this paper would likely be a strong contribution to the field.
