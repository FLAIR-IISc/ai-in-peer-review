**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling method, "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithms. The key challenge addressed is that while adaptive sampling (e.g., based on gradient magnitudes) can theoretically offer significant speedups over fixed or uniform sampling, it is often computationally prohibitive as it requires full gradient information at each iteration. Existing approximations that use stale or bounded information can perform worse than simple uniform sampling and lack theoretical guarantees.

The authors' main contribution is to formalize the problem of choosing a sampling distribution under uncertainty. Assuming access to efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient information, they propose a minimax optimization problem. This problem finds a sampling distribution that optimizes the worst-case expected progress over all possible true gradients consistent with the bounds. The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem and, crucially, prove that the resulting sampling distribution is *always* at least as good as standard fixed (Li-based) importance sampling. This "safeness" guarantee is the paper's central feature. The authors demonstrate how to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide extensive empirical results on real-world datasets, showing significant improvements in convergence time over fixed sampling schemes.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax problem (`min_p max_c ...`) is highly novel and elegant. It provides a principled way to handle uncertainty in gradient information, which is a fundamental problem in adaptive stochastic optimization. This moves beyond heuristics used in prior work.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The main theoretical result (Theorem 3.2), which proves that the proposed sampling is provably better than or equal to fixed Li-based importance sampling, is a major strength. This "safeness" guarantee is practically very important, as practitioners can adopt this method without risking a degradation in performance compared to standard baselines.
3.  **Efficiency and Practicality:** The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the safe sampling distribution. For many problems where the data dimension `d` is large, this overhead is negligible compared to the cost of a single coordinate gradient update (O(d)), making the method highly practical. The experiments on timing (Figures 6 and 9) confirm this.
4.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It effectively motivates the problem by first analyzing the "ideal" but impractical full-information setting, and then developing the proposed "safe" approximation. The work is self-contained, covering theory, an efficient algorithm, a practical application case (GLMs), and thorough experiments.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Bound Maintenance:** The practical applicability of the method relies heavily on the ability to compute and maintain tight lower and upper bounds (`l`, `u`) on the gradient information. The paper provides a clear and simple method for GLMs using the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, but these bounds can be quite loose. The discussion on how this approach would extend to other important model classes, such as deep neural networks, is absent. The performance of the method is likely sensitive to the quality of these bounds.
2.  **Underwhelming SGD Performance:** The empirical results for SGD (Figures 7-9) are much less compelling than for CD. The authors note that for their chosen problems, even the "optimal" full-information sampling offers only a marginal benefit over uniform sampling. While this is an interesting finding in itself, it somewhat diminishes the demonstrated impact of the proposed method for SGD. More analysis or discussion on *why* this occurs would strengthen the paper. For instance, is the variance from the data subsampling in SGD inherently dominant over the potential gains from importance sampling?
3.  **Clarity of Algorithm 4:** The pseudocode for Algorithm 4 is quite dense and could be made clearer. The use of `u` and `l` as both vectors of bounds and integer counters is confusing. Furthermore, the pseudocode implicitly assumes that the original indices of the sorted arrays are tracked, which should be made explicit. A more detailed explanation or a clearer presentation of this core algorithm would improve the paper's reproducibility.

**Recommendation**

I recommend **acceptance**.

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The formulation of the safe sampling problem is principled and elegant, and the resulting theoretical guarantee of being provably better than fixed sampling is a strong and important result. It addresses a key practical concern with existing adaptive sampling methods. The weaknesses identified, such as the limited scope of the bound maintenance techniques and the less impressive SGD results, are avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed framework. The core contribution is sound, significant, and likely to be influential for researchers and practitioners working on large-scale optimization.

**Detailed Comments for the Authors**

*   Regarding the SGD experiments: Could you offer more insight into why the gap between uniform and optimal sampling is so small for SGD in your experiments? This is an interesting result and understanding its cause would be valuable.
*   Regarding the quality of the bounds: Have you considered the impact of the looseness of the Cauchy-Schwarz bounds? It would be interesting to see an ablation study on how performance changes as the bounds (`u-l`) tighten, if this can be simulated.
*   In Section 2.1, you propose the stepsize `γk = αk[pk]−1ik` and present it as a new proposal for CD. This form is common for creating unbiased updates in stochastic methods. Could you clarify its novelty in the context of randomized CD methods specifically and perhaps cite relevant precursors if they exist?
*   Please consider revising the pseudocode in Algorithm 4 for clarity. Specifically, I suggest using different variable names for the counters and the bound vectors, and making the tracking of original indices explicit. For example, instead of `sort_asc(√L⁻¹l)`, one might use a structure that stores `(value, original_index)` pairs.
*   Minor typo in the introduction: "deep leaning" should be "deep learning".
