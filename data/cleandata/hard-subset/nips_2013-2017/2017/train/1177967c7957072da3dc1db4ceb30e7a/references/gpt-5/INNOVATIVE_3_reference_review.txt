Summary
The paper tackles adaptive importance sampling for first-order methods, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors first show that full-information, gradient-based sampling yields strictly better expected progress than fixed importance sampling in CD (potentially up to an n× speedup when Li are equal). They then address the central practicality issue: full gradient information is too expensive per iteration. The core contribution is a “safe” adaptive sampling rule computed from per-coordinate lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (or component gradient norms for SGD). They formulate choosing the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization that yields a distribution p̂ and a stepsize α̂ = 1/v minimizing the worst-case quadratic upper bound among all gradients consistent with the bounds. They prove p̂ is never worse than fixed Li-based sampling, give a fast O(n log n) algorithm to compute it, and show it recovers the full-information optimal rule when bounds are tight. They also present simple, cheap ways to maintain safe bounds in GLMs and provide experiments on linear models demonstrating strong gains for CD and modest gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
There is extensive literature on non-uniform and adaptive sampling for CD/SGD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč, Nesterov, Zhao & Zhang, Perekrestenko et al., Csiba et al., Osokin et al.). Prior adaptive methods either need full gradients, stale/approximate surrogates, or lack guarantees that they beat fixed sampling. Safe screening literature provides bounds to eliminate coordinates, but not to build sampling distributions with performance guarantees. The paper’s min-max “safe” construction and O(n log n) solver appear novel and fill a gap between theory-optimal adaptive sampling and practical, guaranteed-better-than-fixed rules.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Casting adaptive sampling with partial gradient information as a min-max problem and deriving a distribution that is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling is elegant and new.
- Clear theoretical guarantees: Theorems 3.2 and 3.4 give a worst-case dominance over Li-based sampling, bounds Lmin ≤ v ≤ Tr[L], and an efficient computation procedure. The competitive ratio discussion is informative.
- Practical algorithm: The O(n log n) algorithm that uses sorted thresholding to enforce the KKT-like structure in Eq. (9) is clean, easy to implement, and bridges theory and practice.
- Coupled stepsize: The paper proposes a principled stepsize αk tied to the sampling p, improving expected progress over classical fixed stepsizes; empirically important.
- Integrations: Concrete recipes to maintain safe bounds for GLMs for both CD and SGD with O(n) updates per iteration; simple and general.
- Empirical evidence: On several real datasets, the safe sampling approaches the full-information method and outperforms fixed sampling for CD in both iterations and wall-clock time.

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of “safe bounds”: For least squares, bounds are truly safe. For general GLMs, the derivations rely on curvature terms (∇2h or h''), but the paper only briefly mentions using Cauchy–Schwarz and does not spell out the conditions (e.g., known bounds on h'') that make the overall bounds provably safe. Please clarify explicit assumptions ensuring safety for logistic/squared hinge across the optimization path.
- Proximal/nonsmooth regularizers: The CD update rule is presented in a gradient form. With nonsmooth separable regularizers (e.g., L1), one typically uses proximal/coordinate-wise shrinkage. How does the proposed αk and p̂k integrate with proximal CD precisely? Any theoretical guarantees in that setting?
- Overhead and scalability: Recomputing p̂k each iteration costs O(n log n). For very large n and sparse, high-dimensional data where d ≪ n or coordinate costs are tiny, this overhead could dominate. A discussion and experiments with less frequent refresh (e.g., update p̂ every T steps) or incremental maintenance would be valuable.
- Baselines: The experiments compare to uniform/fixed Li-based sampling and the full-information oracle. Missing are practical adaptive baselines that use stale gradients or cheap surrogates (e.g., cached gradient norms, duality-gap based rules, adaptive SDCA/OSCAR variants). Including such baselines would strengthen the empirical case.
- SGD results: Improvements over uniform in SGD are modest. Some analysis of when the min-max rule helps in SGD (e.g., heteroscedastic gradients, non-uniform curvature) would help set expectations and guide usage.
- Dependence on initial bounds: Experiments initialize with [0, ∞). While this is fair and yields graceful warm-up, an ablation on tighter initial bounds (e.g., from a cheap pass) would show how benefits scale with bound quality.
- Competitive ratio: Lemma 3.6 assumes per-coordinate multiplicative separation γ that may be hard to ensure. Discussion on how realistic this is with the proposed bound maintenance would help interpret the ratio bounds.
- Clarity/notation: The paper uses both n and d (coordinates vs data dimension) and switches between CD and SGD quickly. A small section unifying notation and giving explicit pseudocode for the SGD variant would improve readability.

Minor comments
- Please give explicit curvature bounds for logistic and squared hinge to justify safe updates (e.g., h'' ∈ [0, 1/4] for logistic).
- Discuss memory and data structures for the O(n log n) solver; a partial-sorted structure or heap may allow amortized o(log n) updates when bounds evolve slowly.
- Report wall-clock including the sampling computation separately to quantify overhead.
- Consider ablations on refresh frequency of p̂ and sensitivity to the stepsize α̂ = 1/v.
- A brief comparison against Gauss–Southwell (exact/approximate) would help contextualize the n× potential speedup claim.

Overall evaluation
This paper introduces a genuinely novel and principled approach to adaptive importance sampling under limited information, with clean theory, an efficient algorithm, and convincing CD experiments. Despite some missing empirical baselines and clarity gaps on safe bounds beyond least squares, the core idea is strong and likely to influence subsequent work on practical adaptive sampling rules with guarantees. I lean toward acceptance.

Recommendation: Accept (strong novelty and soundness outweigh the moderate empirical/clarity weaknesses)
