### Summary

This paper introduces a "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core problem addressed is that optimal adaptive sampling (e.g., gradient-based) is computationally expensive as it requires full gradient information at each iteration, while common approximations can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors' main contribution is a novel, principled approach to this problem. Instead of using a point estimate for the unknown gradient components, they assume access to safe lower and upper bounds. They then formulate the problem of choosing the best sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem: find the distribution that minimizes the worst-case objective progress over all possible gradients consistent with the bounds. The key results are:
1.  A proof that this "safe" sampling strategy is guaranteed to be at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., L-based or uniform).
2.  An efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm to solve the minimax problem and compute the sampling distribution.
3.  A demonstration of how to derive and efficiently update the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).
4.  Empirical validation showing significant speedups for CD on real-world datasets, achieving performance close to the full-information (oracle) optimal sampling.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The central idea of framing the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax game against an adversary choosing the worst-case gradient within given bounds (Eq. 7) is highly original and theoretically sound. This is a significant conceptual advance over previous adaptive methods that rely on heuristics, stale values, or overly optimistic point estimates (e.g., using only the upper bounds), which lack performance guarantees.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The "safety" property (Theorem 3.2) is the paper's most compelling feature. It formally proves that the proposed method is never worse than standard fixed importance sampling. This guarantee resolves a major practical and theoretical obstacle for the adoption of adaptive sampling, as practitioners no longer need to fear that the overhead and approximation errors of an adaptive strategy will lead to worse performance than a simple baseline.

3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The theoretical elegance of the minimax formulation is matched by a practical algorithm (Algorithm 4) for its solution. The `O(n log n)` complexity makes the method computationally feasible for large `n`. The authors also provide a concrete and simple mechanism for maintaining the required gradient bounds for the important class of GLMs, with a per-iteration update cost of `O(n)`. This demonstrates the end-to-end viability of the proposed framework.

4.  **Convincing Empirical Results for CD:** The experimental evaluation for coordinate descent is strong. The proposed method ("safe adaptive") consistently and significantly outperforms uniform sampling in both iteration complexity and wall-clock time. More impressively, its performance closely tracks that of the oracle "optimal" sampling, which requires the full gradient. This provides excellent evidence that the safe bounds, even when loose, are sufficient to capture most of the benefits of full-information adaptation. The plots showing the evolution of the stepsize parameter `v_k` are also very insightful.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Empirical Gains for SGD:** The empirical results for SGD are far less convincing than for CD. In the provided experiments (Figures 7-9), the oracle "optimal" sampling itself offers only a marginal benefit over uniform sampling. Consequently, the proposed method also shows a very limited speedup. While the authors report this honestly, it tempers the paper's claim of being a generic improvement for both CD and SGD. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of why this discrepancy between CD and SGD performance exists. It might be related to the problem structure, or it could suggest that norm-based sampling for SGD is inherently less potent than gradient-based sampling for CD in these settings.

2.  **Applicability Beyond GLMs:** The practicality of the method hinges on the ability to efficiently maintain tight gradient bounds. While the paper does an excellent job for GLMs, the applicability to other major classes of models, particularly deep neural networks, is not discussed and appears challenging. For a general non-linear model, tracking how a single SGD update affects the gradients of other loss components may not be possible without significant computation, potentially negating the benefits. This limitation on the scope of applicability should be more explicitly acknowledged.

3.  **Quality of Bounds:** The performance of the method is directly tied to the quality (i.e., tightness) of the bounds `l_k` and `u_k`. The paper uses a simple Cauchy-Schwarz based bound, which may be quite loose. The paper shows this is sufficient for GLMs, but it is unclear how performance degrades as the bounds become looser. An ablation study on the tightness of the bounds would have provided valuable insight.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the theoretical development is logical and easy to follow, and the connection between theory and practice is well-established.
*   **Novelty:** In Section 2.1, the authors propose a new stepsize rule `γ_k = α_k / [p_k]_i` for CD. This differs from the standard `γ_k = 1/L_{i_k}` and is key to their unified analysis. This is a nice, subtle contribution in itself.
*   **Algorithm 4:** The algorithm for solving the minimax problem is clever. The intuition behind it, based on the optimality conditions in Eq. (9), is not immediately obvious from the main text but is a crucial component that makes the work practical.
*   **Competitive Ratio:** The analysis in Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6 is a nice theoretical addition, providing further guarantees on how the proposed sampling compares to the ex-post optimal one.

### Recommendation: Accept

This paper presents a high-quality contribution to the field of large-scale optimization.

*   **Quality & Originality:** The work is technically sound, and the minimax formulation for safe adaptive sampling is a novel and significant idea that elegantly addresses the shortcomings of prior work.
*   **Significance:** The paper provides a practical adaptive sampling method with a highly desirable safety guarantee, which could make adaptive methods much more attractive for widespread use, especially in the context of coordinate descent. The approach is likely to inspire follow-up research into deriving better bounds for different model classes and applying the minimax concept to other areas of stochastic optimization.

While the empirical gains for SGD are modest and the applicability to non-GLMs is an open question, the strength of the core theoretical idea, the safety guarantee, and the outstanding results for coordinate descent make this a strong paper worthy of acceptance at NIPS.
