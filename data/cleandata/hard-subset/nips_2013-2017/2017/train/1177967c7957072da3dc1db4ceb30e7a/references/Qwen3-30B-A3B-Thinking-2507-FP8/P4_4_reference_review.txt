

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors argue that gradient-based adaptive sampling can theoretically provide significant speedups (up to a factor of dimension n) over fixed sampling methods, but computing the optimal adaptive distribution requires the full gradient, which is computationally expensive. To address this, they develop a "safe" approximation that uses lower and upper bounds on gradient information, which can be computed efficiently. Their method guarantees that the sampling distribution will never be worse than fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform sampling or Lipschitz-based sampling), while still providing significant improvements in practice. The authors demonstrate how to efficiently compute these safe bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs) and provide empirical results on real datasets showing that their method outperforms fixed sampling strategies.

The work relates to previous NIPS papers on adaptive sampling (e.g., "Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling" by Perekrestenko et al., "Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent with Adaptive Probabilities" by Csiba et al.), but distinguishes itself by providing theoretical guarantees that the sampling is never worse than fixed sampling, unlike previous approaches that relied on approximations that could be worse.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for their approach. The authors derive the optimal sampling distribution for the full-information case, then develop a safe approximation using lower and upper bounds on gradient information. They prove that their method is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2) and provide tight bounds on the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5-3.6). The proofs are well-structured and deferred to the appendix.

2. **Computational Efficiency**: The proposed method has a computational complexity of O(n log n) per iteration, which is comparable to the cost of computing a single gradient component (O(d) for d-dimensional data). This makes the method practical for large-scale optimization problems.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The paper provides a concrete example (GLMs) of how to efficiently compute safe gradient bounds, demonstrating that these bounds can be updated in O(n) time per iteration. This makes the method applicable to a broad class of machine learning problems.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The authors provide extensive empirical results on real datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with various loss functions and regularization terms. The results consistently show that their method outperforms fixed sampling strategies in terms of convergence speed and computational efficiency.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Empirical Results for SGD**: While the method shows significant improvements for CD, the empirical results for SGD show only modest improvements over uniform sampling. This is surprising given the theoretical advantages, and the authors do not provide a thorough explanation for this discrepancy. The paper states that "the effect of the proposed sampling is less pronounced in the three SGD experiments," but doesn't explore why this might be the case.

2. **Scope of Applicability**: The paper focuses on generalized linear models as the primary example for computing safe gradient bounds. While the authors acknowledge that "there are many other ways to track safe gradient bounds," they don't provide a comprehensive analysis of the scope of applicability. It's unclear how broadly this method can be applied to other problem classes beyond GLMs.

3. **Theoretical Complexity**: The theoretical development involves several non-trivial mathematical concepts (e.g., the optimization problem in (7)). While the paper is well-written, the complexity of the theoretical development might make it challenging for some readers to follow without additional intuition.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound, with well-supported theoretical claims and empirical validation. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, and the theoretical guarantees are rigorous and well-motivated.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The main ideas are presented logically, and the notation is consistent. The appendix provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The empirical results are presented clearly with appropriate visualizations.

**Originality**: The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by providing a method that is provably better than fixed sampling and computationally efficient. The key innovation is the "safe" optimization formulation that guarantees the sampling will not be worse than fixed sampling, which differentiates it from previous adaptive sampling approaches.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a practical challenge in large-scale optimization with significant potential impact. The method could be widely applicable to many machine learning problems that use CD or SGD. The empirical results demonstrate that the method provides meaningful improvements in practice, with only a small computational overhead.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro**:
- Provides a theoretically sound method that is provably better than fixed importance sampling
- Computationally efficient (O(n log n) per iteration)
- Demonstrated empirical improvements on real datasets across multiple problem types
- Clear differentiation from previous work on adaptive importance sampling
- Well-structured theoretical development with rigorous proofs

**Con**:
- Empirical results for SGD show only modest improvements compared to uniform sampling
- Limited analysis of the scope of applicability beyond GLMs
- Theoretical development is somewhat complex for some readers

## Recommendation

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization with a theoretically sound and practically efficient method for adaptive importance sampling. The theoretical guarantees are strong, the empirical results are convincing, and the method has clear potential for broad application. While the empirical results for SGD could be more compelling, the paper's strengths outweigh this limitation. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
