

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address a key challenge in adaptive importance sampling: while theoretically optimal gradient-based sampling can significantly outperform fixed sampling (with potential speedup factors of dimension n), computing the optimal distribution is often computationally prohibitive. The paper introduces a novel approach that uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to compute an adaptive sampling distribution that is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling, while being efficiently computable in each iteration. The authors provide theoretical guarantees, an efficient algorithm for computing the sampling distribution, and empirical validation on generalized linear models.

The work builds on prior literature on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 23, 36]) but introduces a novel "safe" optimization formulation that guarantees theoretical performance improvements over fixed sampling. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of how to efficiently compute the required gradient bounds for generalized linear models.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with well-justified theoretical claims. The authors prove that their proposed sampling distribution is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), and provide tight bounds on the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5, 3.6). The optimization formulation for finding the safe sampling distribution (Equation 7) is well-motivated and the proposed algorithm (Algorithm 4) has a clear theoretical complexity analysis (O(n log n) per iteration).

The experimental evaluation is thorough, with results on multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) and different loss functions (square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss). The authors demonstrate that their method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in CD settings while maintaining reasonable computational overhead. The experimental design effectively supports the theoretical claims, including the comparison of vk values (which determine the stepsize) across different sampling methods.

One potential weakness is the limited theoretical comparison of the convergence rates between the proposed method and fixed sampling. While the paper shows the proposed method is always better than fixed sampling, it doesn't provide a clear theoretical bound on how much better it can be in terms of convergence rates. The experimental results show clear improvements, but a stronger theoretical analysis would strengthen the paper.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly establishes the problem, the motivation for adaptive sampling, and the limitations of previous approaches. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The key contributions are clearly enumerated at the beginning, and each section builds logically on the previous one.

The algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode (Algorithms 1-4), and the explanation of how to compute the safe sampling distribution is particularly well-structured. The experimental section is also well-organized, with results presented in a logical sequence that supports the main claims.

The only minor clarity issue is that the paper doesn't fully explain why the adaptive sampling shows less improvement for SGD than for CD, despite the theoretical guarantees being applicable to both methods. A brief theoretical discussion of this difference would enhance clarity.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of adaptive optimization methods. While adaptive importance sampling has been studied before (e.g., [22, 23, 36]), the "safe" approach presented here is novel in that it guarantees the sampling will never be worse than fixed sampling while still being efficiently computable. Previous methods either used approximations without guarantees or required full gradient computation.

The key insight—formulating the problem as an optimization over the worst-case gradient bounds—represents a meaningful contribution. This approach differs substantially from previous work that used either fixed upper bounds (e.g., [36]) or stale gradient information (e.g., [22]) without theoretical guarantees.

The paper also provides a useful framework for how to compute the necessary gradient bounds for a broad class of problems (generalized linear models), which extends the applicability of the method.

## Significance

The paper addresses a practical problem of significant importance in large-scale machine learning: how to efficiently implement adaptive importance sampling without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. The proposed method has the potential to significantly improve the performance of CD and SGD algorithms across a wide range of applications.

The experimental results demonstrate clear practical benefits, with the method outperforming fixed sampling in terms of both iterations and total computation time. The paper also shows that even very loose gradient bounds are sufficient to achieve significant improvements, making the method applicable in many settings without requiring specialized gradient tracking.

The computational overhead of O(n log n) per iteration is minimal compared to the cost of computing a single gradient component (O(d)), making the method practical for many machine learning problems. The paper also demonstrates that the method can be implemented at "no extra computational cost" on many problems, which is a significant practical advantage.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical analysis is sound, the proposed method is novel and well-motivated, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to NIPS readers.

The main limitations are the limited theoretical comparison of convergence rates and the lack of explanation for why the method shows less improvement in SGD than in CD. However, these do not significantly undermine the paper's contributions.

I believe this paper makes a substantial contribution to the field and would be of interest to NIPS readers working on optimization algorithms for machine learning. It addresses a practical problem with a theoretically grounded solution that has clear empirical benefits.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It meets all four NIPS criteria well and makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The paper is technically sound, well-written, original, and significant for the machine learning community.
