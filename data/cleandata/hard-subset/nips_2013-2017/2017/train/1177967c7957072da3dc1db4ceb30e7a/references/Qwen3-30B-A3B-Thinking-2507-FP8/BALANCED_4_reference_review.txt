

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors argue that gradient-based adaptive sampling theoretically provides significant speedups over fixed importance sampling, but is computationally intractable in practice. Their key contribution is a method to compute a sampling distribution using safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information that is provably better than fixed sampling and can be computed efficiently (O(n log n) per iteration). The paper establishes theoretical guarantees, provides a practical implementation, and demonstrates empirical improvements on real datasets for CD. The work relates to prior work on adaptive sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) but distinguishes itself by providing theoretical guarantees that their approximation is never worse than fixed sampling.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Quality**: The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2) that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling, a significant contribution that previous adaptive sampling methods lack. The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5, 3.6) is particularly valuable.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a clear algorithm (Algorithm 4) for computing the safe sampling distribution efficiently, with a detailed discussion of computational complexity (O(n log n) per iteration). This is a major practical contribution, as it addresses the key barrier to implementing adaptive sampling.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The empirical evaluation on real datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) demonstrates significant improvements over fixed sampling for CD, with the proposed method often performing nearly as well as the theoretically optimal (but computationally intractable) gradient-based sampling. The results are well-presented and support the theoretical claims.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear notation, logical flow, and well-structured algorithms. The connection between theory and practice is effectively explained.

## Weaknesses
1. **SGD Results Lack Explanation**: The empirical results for SGD (Figures 7-9) show much less improvement than for CD, but the paper doesn't adequately explain why this is the case. This is a significant gap, as the paper claims their method is "completely generic" and applicable to both CD and SGD.

2. **Limited Discussion of Safe Bounds**: While the paper provides examples of how to compute safe bounds for GLMs (Section 4), it doesn't thoroughly discuss when and how these bounds can be efficiently computed for more complex models beyond GLMs. This limits the perceived scope of the method.

3. **Comparison to Other Adaptive Methods**: The paper doesn't compare against other adaptive sampling methods that might be more efficient than the proposed approach (e.g., [22, 36, 37]), making it difficult to assess the relative advantages of their method.

4. **Theoretical-Practical Gap**: While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the paper doesn't explicitly connect the competitive ratio ρk (Lemma 3.5) to the observed empirical performance, which would strengthen the argument.

## Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:**
- Strong theoretical contribution with provable guarantees that previous adaptive sampling methods lack
- Practical implementation with manageable computational overhead (O(n log n))
- Empirical validation on real datasets demonstrating significant improvements over fixed sampling for CD
- Well-written and clearly organized paper

**Con:**
- Limited empirical results for SGD with insufficient explanation
- Insufficient discussion of when and how safe gradient bounds can be computed for non-GLM problems
- Lack of comparison to other adaptive sampling methods
- Theoretical guarantees don't fully explain the empirical performance gap between CD and SGD

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The theoretical guarantees are novel and valuable, and the practical implementation is well-motivated and efficient. The paper is well-written and provides a useful tool for practitioners.

The main limitations are the lack of explanation for the weaker SGD results and insufficient discussion of the applicability beyond GLMs. These are significant but not fatal flaws. The paper could be significantly strengthened with additional experiments or analysis for SGD, and a more thorough discussion of the applicability to broader classes of problems.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field with strong theoretical guarantees and practical implementation. The weaknesses identified are not fundamental to the contribution but could be addressed with additional analysis or discussion. The work is of high quality, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the area of adaptive sampling in optimization.
