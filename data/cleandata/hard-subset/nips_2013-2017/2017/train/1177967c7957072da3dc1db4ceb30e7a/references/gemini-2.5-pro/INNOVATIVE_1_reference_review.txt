This paper addresses the problem of adaptive importance sampling for large-scale optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The authors first reiterate the significant theoretical advantage of optimal gradient-based sampling, which can offer speedups of up to a factor of the dimension `n`. However, they correctly identify the critical drawback: computing this optimal distribution is prohibitively expensive, as it requires full gradient information at each step. Existing approximations, which rely on stale information or upper bounds, lack guarantees and can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The main contribution of this work is a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme. The core idea is to work with efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. Instead of heuristically using these bounds (e.g., sampling based on the upper bounds), the authors frame the selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. The goal is to find the distribution that minimizes the worst-case convergence bound over all possible true gradients that lie within the given lower and upper bounds. This principled approach yields a sampling strategy that is provably at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling, while adapting to the information gathered during optimization. The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this minimax problem and demonstrate how to derive and update the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs). Experiments on several real-world datasets show significant performance improvements over fixed sampling schemes.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and conceptual elegance. The formulation of the adaptive sampling problem as a minimax optimization (Equation 7) is a fundamentally new and powerful idea in this domain. It moves beyond heuristic approximations and provides a principled, worst-case optimal solution given the available information. This is a significant conceptual leap that addresses the most critical flaw of prior adaptive sampling methods: the lack of a "safety net."

The theoretical guarantees that stem from this formulation are a major contribution. Theorem 3.2, which proves that the proposed sampling is always better than standard `L_i`-based sampling (which includes uniform sampling as a special case), is the key result. This "safety" guarantee makes adaptive sampling a far more reliable and attractive tool, removing the risk of performance degradation that has hindered its widespread adoption.

The paper is theoretically sound. The authors not only propose the novel optimization problem but also provide an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm to solve it (Algorithm 4). This demonstrates that the approach is not just a theoretical curiosity but a practically viable method.

The empirical evaluation, particularly for Coordinate Descent, is compelling. The results clearly show that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time. The plots tracking the stepsize parameter `v_k` provide excellent insight, demonstrating that the method effectively learns a much more aggressive stepsize than the fixed baseline, approaching the performance of the oracle-like optimal sampler.

**Weaknesses:**

While the core idea is excellent, the method's performance is naturally dependent on the tightness of the gradient bounds `l_k` and `u_k`. The paper proposes a straightforward method for GLMs based on the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which can be quite loose. While the experiments show that even these simple bounds are sufficient for a large performance gain, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the impact of bound quality or potential avenues for deriving tighter bounds. However, this is a minor point, as the framework itself is general and the demonstration with simple bounds is convincing.

The computational overhead of `O(n log n)` per iteration, while often negligible compared to a single coordinate gradient evaluation (typically `O(d)`), could become a bottleneck for problems where `d` is small and `n` is extremely large. The paper acknowledges this and the timing experiments suggest the overhead is well-managed in practice, but it remains a limitation for certain problem structures.

The empirical gains for SGD were notably less pronounced than for CD. The paper reports this honestly, but a deeper analysis of why this might be the case would have strengthened the experimental section.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It introduces a bold, elegant, and highly novel approach to a well-known problem in large-scale optimization. The "safe" minimax formulation provides the crucial theoretical guarantee that was missing from previous adaptive sampling schemes, making it a significant advance in the field. While there are minor limitations regarding the tightness of bounds and computational overhead in certain regimes, these do not detract from the importance of the core contribution. This work prioritizes a significant new concept over incremental refinement, and it provides a strong foundation that other researchers are very likely to build upon. This is precisely the kind of thought-provoking and impactful research that NIPS should champion.
