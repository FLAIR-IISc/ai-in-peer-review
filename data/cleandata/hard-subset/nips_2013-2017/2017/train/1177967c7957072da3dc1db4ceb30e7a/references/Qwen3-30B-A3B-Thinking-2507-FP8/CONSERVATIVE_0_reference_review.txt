

**Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"**

The paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that claims to provide a computationally efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling. The authors argue that their method is theoretically better than fixed importance sampling, provably never worse than static sampling, and recovers optimal gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting. The key innovation is an optimization formulation that uses safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information to compute sampling probabilities efficiently.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear theoretical argument for adaptive sampling being superior to fixed sampling in certain cases (Theorem 2.1, Example 2.3).
- The proposed "safe" sampling approach (Algorithm 2) is theoretically grounded, with Theorem 3.2 demonstrating that the method is always better than fixed importance sampling.
- The computational complexity analysis shows the overhead is O(n log n) per iteration, which may be comparable to gradient computation in many cases.
- The empirical evaluation on GLMs demonstrates improved convergence over fixed sampling, providing practical validation of the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art adaptive methods:** The paper claims to improve upon gradient-based sampling but fails to compare with other adaptive sampling approaches that have been previously proposed (e.g., [22, 23, 26, 36, 37]). The empirical results only compare with uniform and fixed importance sampling, not with other adaptive methods. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed method offers a meaningful improvement over existing adaptive approaches.

- **Weak theoretical justification for the bound approach:** The paper assumes safe gradient bounds can be efficiently computed (Section 4), but the argument for GLMs relies on very loose Cauchy-Schwarz bounds. The theoretical guarantee that the method is better than fixed sampling (Theorem 3.2) doesn't depend on the tightness of these bounds, making the theoretical claim somewhat vacuous. The paper states that "the measured value vk was always significantly below the worst case estimate," but doesn't provide concrete evidence of how much better the method performs in practice compared to the theoretical worst case.

- **Limited scope of empirical evaluation:** The paper claims the method is "completely generic," but the empirical evaluation is restricted to GLMs with L1/L2 regularization. There's no evidence that the approach works well on other problem classes (e.g., deep learning, non-GLM problems), which limits the significance of the claimed generality.

- **Overstated novelty:** The paper claims to be the first to use safe bounds in a "novel way," but the idea of using bounds for adaptive sampling is not new (e.g., [36, 37] use upper bounds for gradient-based sampling). The key contribution appears to be the specific optimization formulation (equation 7), but this isn't sufficiently distinguished from prior work.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a technically sound method with some practical benefits, but falls short of being a significant contribution to the field. The main issue is the lack of thorough validation against state-of-the-art adaptive sampling methods, which is critical for a paper claiming to improve upon existing adaptive approaches. The theoretical guarantees are weakened by the use of loose bounds, and the empirical results don't convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method provides a substantial advantage over existing approaches.

While the paper makes a minor improvement to an established technique, the claims of theoretical and practical superiority are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The method appears to be a reasonable practical implementation of adaptive sampling that may work well in specific contexts, but it doesn't represent a significant advancement over existing methods that have been used in practice for years.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
*Pros:* Theoretical justification for adaptive sampling, efficient implementation with O(n log n) complexity, empirical results showing improvement over fixed sampling.

*Cons:* Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art adaptive methods, weak theoretical guarantees without tight bounds, limited empirical evaluation, overstated novelty.

**Recommendation:** Weak reject. The paper makes a minor improvement to an established technique, but the evidence provided does not support the claims of significant theoretical and practical advantages over existing methods. The lack of comparison with the most relevant prior work (other adaptive sampling methods) is particularly problematic for a paper claiming to advance the state of the art.
