Summary and contributions
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). With full-information, the authors show that sampling coordinates/components proportionally to √Li|∇i f(x)| (CD) or to ‖∇fi(x)‖ (SGD) optimizes a natural quadratic upper bound on expected one-step progress, potentially yielding up to an n-fold speed-up over uniform/fixed importance sampling. The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive alternative that does not require the full gradient each iteration: given per-coordinate safe lower/upper bounds on the unknown gradient magnitudes (or component gradient norms), they formulate a robust min–max problem over the uncertainty set Ck to choose pk and stepsize αk. The resulting distribution p̂k is shown to optimally minimize the worst-case ratio V(p, c)/‖c‖2 over c ∈ Ck, guarantees progress at least as good as Li-based (fixed) sampling, and recovers the full-information optimum as the bounds tighten. They give an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting-based) to compute p̂k via KKT structure, and discuss how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for GLMs using simple updates (e.g., Cauchy–Schwarz) for both CD and SGD. Experiments on standard text datasets show strong improvements for CD and modest gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
- Fixed non-uniform sampling for CD is well-studied (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.). Adaptive sampling for CD based on duality gaps/violations exists (Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.), and for SGD based on gradient norms (Zhao & Zhang; Needell et al.). Safe screening ideas (Liu et al.; Ndiaye et al.) also produce bounds but for feature elimination, not sampling.
- The robust min–max formulation over safe uncertainty sets, the guarantee of never being worse than fixed importance sampling, and the efficient algorithm to compute the distribution appear novel. The connection to robust optimization and Sion’s minimax theorem is appropriate.

Quality and correctness
- The per-iteration expected decrease analysis for CD is standard and sound. With αk chosen to minimize the quadratic model, the decrease depends on V(pk, ∇f(xk)). The “optimal” full-information pk matches known forms.
- The robust sampling is defined via minp maxc∈Ck V(p, c)/‖c‖2. The claimed properties (best worst-case guarantee; always ≤ Tr[L]·‖c‖2 and hence never worse than Li-based sampling) follow from including the Li-based distribution as a feasible comparator and a valid min–max interchange (cited). The geometric reformulation √vk = maxc∈Ck ⟨√l, c⟩/‖c‖2 is insightful.
- The O(n log n) algorithm leveraging the thresholding structure [ci]=clip([√Lim]) is plausible; the pseudo-code is clear enough, though it reuses variable names (u, l) for both vectors and indices which should be cleaned up.
- Convergence rates are sketched via “standard techniques” and deferred to the appendix. In the main text, the paper stops at one-step progress guarantees. A clean global convergence theorem (e.g., for L-smooth convex and µ-strongly convex cases) with explicit constants for the robust sampling would strengthen the paper.

Significance
- The robust “safe” approximation to full-information adaptive sampling directly addresses a key practical blocker (computational cost) and gives a strict worst-case improvement over fixed distributions. This is of broad interest for large-scale optimization and could be readily incorporated into CD solvers; for SGD, benefits are dataset/setting dependent.
- The ability to trade information and safety under uncertainty via a principled min–max formulation is conceptually appealing and likely to inspire follow-up work (e.g., blocks, minibatches, distributed settings).

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized; the motivation, formulation, and properties are clear. The notation occasionally confuses (e.g., “∇2h(Ax)=In” refers to Hessian w.r.t. Ax, not x). Algorithm 4 should avoid reusing l,u for both bounds and indices. Minor typos and formatting issues exist in equations.
- The complexity discussion could be more nuanced: per-iteration overhead O(n log n) to recompute p̂k and O(n) to update bounds may be significant when a single CD/SGD update is O(nnz of a column) or O(d). The authors note amortization when d=Ω(n), but many sparse problems have d≫n or vice versa.

Empirical evaluation
- CD results show large practical gains and that even very loose bounds can help; this supports the method’s robustness. The comparison to an oracle “optimal sampling” is informative.
- For SGD, gains are modest; the paper acknowledges this. However, maintaining bounds with O(n) work per iteration undermines SGD’s appeal in large-n regimes; practical variants that refresh p̂k less frequently or maintain coarse summaries would be useful.
- Missing baselines: duality gap/violation-based adaptive CD (e.g., AdaSDCA, adaptive probabilities), Gauss–Southwell/approximate steepest CD, and other adaptive SGD samplings. Including these would better establish empirical significance.
- Timing results are on different machines; for fairness, re-run on the same hardware and report overhead breakdowns. Ablations on bound quality (e.g., exact inner products vs. Cauchy–Schwarz; refresh frequency) would be insightful.

Originality
- The robust min–max construction of safe adaptive sampling with a tight O(n log n) algorithm and formal guarantee of outperforming fixed importance sampling is, to my knowledge, new.

Pros
- Clear motivation; principled robust formulation with worst-case optimality.
- Always at least as good as fixed importance sampling; recovers the full-information optimum as bounds tighten.
- Efficient algorithm with simple implementation; constructive guidance to maintain bounds for GLMs.
- Solid improvements for CD across diverse losses and regularizers.

Cons
- Overhead O(n log n)+O(n) per iteration may be prohibitive in many practical sparse or SGD settings.
- Global convergence rates for the robust scheme are not presented prominently with explicit constants.
- Experimental baselines are limited; timing comparisons not strictly controlled; modest SGD benefits.
- Some clarity/notation issues; practical guidance on refresh frequency and amortization is limited.

Questions for authors
1) Can you present explicit convergence rates (convex/strongly convex) for the safe scheme in the main text, not just one-step progress?
2) How often must p̂k be recomputed in practice? Can you amortize by updating every T iterations, and what is the trade-off?
3) For general smooth h with nontrivial Hessian, how do you maintain safe bounds without O(n d) work? Any structured cases beyond least squares?
4) Can the method be extended to block CD, proximal CD, or composite objectives with non-smooth regularizers?
5) Please compare to gap-based/adaptive-probability CD methods and Gauss–Southwell variants.

Minor comments
- Clean up Algorithm 4 variable naming; fix equation formatting (e.g., (4)).
- Clarify the role of absolute values in CD bounds and how signs are handled.
- Provide code and reproducibility details (hardware, libraries, seeds).

Recommendation: Weak accept. The robust, safe adaptive sampling formulation is novel and well-motivated, with solid theory and promising CD results. Addressing the experimental baselines, clarifying complexity/practical variants, and presenting explicit convergence rates would further strengthen the paper. Confidence: high.
