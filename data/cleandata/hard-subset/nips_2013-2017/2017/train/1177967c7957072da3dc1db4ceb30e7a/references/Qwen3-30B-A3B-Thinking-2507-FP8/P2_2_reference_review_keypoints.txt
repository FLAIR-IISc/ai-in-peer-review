- Summary: Proposes "safe adaptive importance sampling" using safe upper/lower gradient bounds to formulate a convex problem that yields a sampling distribution provably better than fixed importance sampling and computable in O(n log n) per iteration; demonstrated on GLMs with empirical improvements.

- Quality: Technically sound with rigorous proofs (Theorem 3.2) and thorough complexity analysis; empirical evaluation on multiple datasets, losses, and regularizers shows consistent gains.

- Clarity: Well-organized and precisely written with clear notation and algorithms; would benefit from a brief comparison to recent adaptive sampling work and more explanation for CD vs SGD behavior.

- Originality: Novel use of safe gradient bounds to guarantee sampling never worse than fixed importance sampling, distinguishing it from prior adaptive methods that lacked such guarantees.

- Significance: Practical for large-scale ML due to low overhead and broad applicability (GLMs and extensible), addressing a major barrier to adaptive sampling adoption.

- Strengths: Provable improvement over fixed sampling, efficient O(n log n) algorithm, comprehensive experiments, small computational overhead, and clear mathematical presentation.

- Weaknesses: Experiments focused mainly on GLMs, limited comparisons to other adaptive methods, and insufficient explanation of why adaptive sampling helps CD more than SGD.

- Overall assessment: Strong, novel, and practical contribution with solid theory and empirical support; recommended for acceptance.

- Recommendation: Accept.

- Pro acceptance arguments: Novel theoretical guarantee; practical, low-overhead algorithm; comprehensive empirical validation; clear presentation; broad applicability.

- Con acceptance arguments (minor): Limited empirical scope beyond GLMs; sparse comparison to other adaptive methods; CD vs SGD performance difference not fully explained.
