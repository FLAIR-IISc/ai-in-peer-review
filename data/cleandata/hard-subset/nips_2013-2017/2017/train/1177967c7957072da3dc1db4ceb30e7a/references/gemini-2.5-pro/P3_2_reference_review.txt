This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core problem with existing adaptive sampling schemes is that computing the optimal, gradient-based distribution is often as expensive as a full gradient step, while heuristic approximations can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors' main contribution is a principled approach to handle this issue. They assume that instead of the exact gradient, one has access to efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the magnitude of each gradient component. Based on these bounds, they formulate the choice of the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem: find the distribution that minimizes the worst-case one-step progress over all possible gradients consistent with the bounds. The key results are: (1) a proof that the resulting "safe" sampling is guaranteed to perform better than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., based on Lipschitz constants), (2) a non-trivial and efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this min-max problem, and (3) a practical demonstration of how to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs). The method is evaluated extensively on several large-scale datasets, showing significant improvements in both iteration complexity and wall-clock time over fixed sampling schemes, and performance close to that of an oracle using the full gradient information.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Principled Formulation:** The min-max formulation for choosing the sampling distribution (Eq. 7) is the paper's most significant contribution. It is an elegant and robust way to handle uncertainty in the gradient information. This contrasts sharply with prior work that often relies on using the upper bounds as proxies for the true gradient, a heuristic that lacks theoretical guarantees and can fail in practice (as shown in Example 3.1). The resulting "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2)—that the method is provably better than fixed importance sampling—is a strong and highly desirable property for an adaptive scheme.
*   **Strong Theoretical Underpinnings:** The paper is technically sound and provides solid theoretical justification for the proposed method. The derivation of the efficient O(n log n) solver (Algorithm 4) for the non-trivial min-max problem is a key technical achievement. Furthermore, the analysis of the competitive ratio provides useful insights into the quality of the approximation relative to the true, unknown optimal sampling.
*   **Practicality and Generalizability:** The proposed framework is general, applying to both CD and SGD. The authors provide a concrete method for maintaining the gradient bounds for the important class of GLMs with only an O(n) overhead per iteration. The overall O(n log n) cost for computing the sampling distribution is practical for many machine learning problems, especially when the feature dimension is large relative to the number of samples/coordinates.
*   **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering different loss functions, regularizers, and several large, real-world datasets. The results compellingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. It consistently and significantly outperforms fixed sampling and comes close to the performance of the oracle (full-information) sampler. The inclusion of plots showing the value of `vk` (which determines the step size) provides excellent intuition for the method's adaptive nature and its advantage over static schemes.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Discussion on Gradient Bound Tightness:** The method for maintaining gradient bounds in Section 4 for GLMs relies on a potentially loose application of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality. While the experiments clearly show that even these simple bounds are effective, the paper would be stronger if it discussed the impact of bound tightness. For instance, could tighter bounds be derived by exploiting data sparsity, and would this lead to further performance gains?
*   **Explanation of SGD Results:** The experiments show that the benefit of adaptive sampling (both optimal and safe) is much less pronounced for SGD than for CD. The paper notes this but does not offer a detailed explanation. This is a missed opportunity for deeper insight. A discussion on why this might be the case—perhaps due to differences in how updates affect the gradient geometry in CD versus SGD for these problems—would add significant value.
*   **Clarity of Algorithm 4's Derivation:** The main text presents Algorithm 4 without much intuition, deferring the justification to the appendix. A more high-level, intuitive explanation of why the sorting and iterative updating of the candidate solution `c` works would improve the paper's readability and help the reader appreciate this core technical contribution.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The proposed CD step size `γk = αk/[pk]ik` is presented as novel. This form, which makes the expected update direction align with the full gradient, is related to standard techniques in stochastic optimization. It would be helpful to clarify the contribution here and relate it more explicitly to prior work on importance sampling for CD (e.g., Richtárik and Takáč, 2016).
*   In Section 4, the method for maintaining bounds for general smooth functions `h` involves terms like `∇²h(Ax̃)`. For non-quadratic losses like logistic regression, this Hessian is not constant. Please clarify how the bounds were computed for the logistic regression experiments, as this seems non-trivial.
*   The O(n log n) overhead is compared to the O(d) cost of a coordinate gradient evaluation. For datasets like rcv1 (n≈20k, d≈47k), `n log n` can be larger than `d`. While the wall-clock time results are still favorable, a brief discussion on the per-iteration time cost for such "tall" data matrices would be welcome.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the literature on stochastic and coordinate descent methods. The proposed "safe" adaptive sampling method is novel, theoretically well-founded, and practically effective. It solves a real problem with existing adaptive methods in a principled way. The technical quality is high, and the experimental results are convincing. Despite some minor weaknesses in the discussion and presentation, the strengths of this work are overwhelming. The paper is well-written, original, and its ideas are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. I strongly recommend acceptance.
