### Summary of the Paper
This paper tackles a well-known challenge in stochastic optimization: adaptive importance sampling is powerful in principle but often impractical. Optimal, gradient-based sampling requires computing the full gradient at each iteration, which defeats the purpose of using a stochastic method like CD or SGD. Existing approximations that use stale information or upper bounds can perform worse than simple uniform sampling and lack theoretical guarantees of improvement.

The authors introduce a novel and elegant solution they term "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling". The core idea is to work with efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient components. Instead of heuristically picking a value within these bounds, the authors formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. They seek a distribution that minimizes the worst-case convergence bound over all possible true gradients consistent with the current bounds. This approach yields a sampling strategy that is (i) provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform or Li-based), (ii) captures the optimal gradient-based sampling if full information is available, and (iii) can be computed efficiently in `O(n log n)` time. The authors demonstrate how to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models and provide empirical results showing significant speedups, particularly for Coordinate Descent.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and conceptual elegance. The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax game (Equation 7) is a significant and original contribution. It moves beyond heuristics and provides a principled, robust way to handle the uncertainty inherent in having only partial gradient information. This is a bold new direction for adaptive sampling methods.

The theoretical "safety" guarantee is a major result. The fact that the proposed sampling is provably at least as good as fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2) is a crucial property that has been missing from prior work on approximate adaptive sampling. This guarantee could significantly increase the practical adoption of adaptive methods, as it removes the risk of performance degradation.

The work is theoretically sound and complete. The authors not only propose a novel formulation but also provide an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve it, demonstrating that their idea is practical. The analysis is clear and the connection between the optimization problem and the convergence bounds is well-established.

The proposed method is generic, applicable to both Coordinate Descent and Stochastic Gradient Descent, broadening its potential impact.

**Weaknesses:**

The method for maintaining the safe gradient bounds presented in Section 4 is based on a simple application of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality. While sufficient to demonstrate the potential of the framework, these bounds may be quite loose in practice. The performance of the entire scheme is contingent on the quality of these bounds, and the paper does not explore the impact of their tightness. However, this can be seen as a promising avenue for future work rather than a fundamental flaw of the proposed sampling framework itself.

The `O(n log n)` computational overhead per iteration, while often manageable, could be a bottleneck for problems where `n` is very large and the per-coordinate gradient cost `d` is small (i.e., `d << n log n`). In such regimes, the proposed method might be slower in wall-clock time than simpler sampling schemes, despite its superior iteration complexity. The authors acknowledge this limitation.

The empirical results for SGD show less dramatic gains compared to CD. While the authors are transparent about this, it does suggest that the practical benefits might be more problem- or algorithm-dependent than the unified theory suggests.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Pro:** The minimax formulation for robust sampling is a highly novel and significant conceptual contribution to the field of stochastic optimization.
*   **Pro:** The paper provides a "safety" guarantee, proving its method is an improvement over fixed sampling. This is a key theoretical result that addresses a major weakness of prior art.
*   **Pro:** The proposed idea is supported by a solid theoretical analysis and a practical, efficient algorithm.
*   **Pro:** The approach is bold and introduces a new way of thinking about adaptive sampling, prioritizing robustness to uncertainty. This is precisely the type of foundational work that can inspire follow-up research.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
*   **Con:** The method used to derive the gradient bounds is simplistic, and the overall performance relies heavily on the quality of these bounds.
*   **Con:** The `O(n log n)` overhead per iteration may limit practicality for certain problem structures.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is an excellent paper that I would argue strongly for accepting. It introduces a genuinely new and powerful idea to a mature area of research. The "safe" sampling concept, backed by a robust minimax formulation, is a fundamental step forward. While the experimental validation could be broader and the specific implementation of the gradient bounds is simple, these are minor points. The core contribution is the novel framework itself, which is both elegant and theoretically sound. This work prioritizes a bold new direction over perfecting an incremental improvement, and its potential impact on how we design and trust adaptive optimization methods is significant. This paper is a clear and valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
