

# Peer Review: "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address the key limitation of existing adaptive importance sampling approaches, which require full gradient computation in every iteration (making them computationally expensive), and the theoretical weakness of approximate methods that may not outperform uniform sampling.

The paper makes four key contributions: (1) it theoretically demonstrates that adaptive sampling can provide up to n-fold speedups over fixed sampling in CD; (2) it proposes a generic, efficient adaptive sampling strategy that works with limited gradient information; (3) it shows how to integrate this strategy into CD and SGD for structured optimization problems; and (4) it provides empirical validation on real-world datasets.

The authors' key insight is to formulate the problem of finding the optimal sampling distribution as an optimization problem that accounts for safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, rather than trying to approximate the full gradient. This leads to a method that is provably better than fixed importance sampling and can be computed efficiently in O(n log n) time per iteration. The method is particularly well-suited for CD methods, where the authors show significant empirical improvements, though the benefits are more modest for SGD.

This work builds on previous contributions in adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37]), but distinguishes itself by providing theoretical guarantees that the approximation is never worse than fixed sampling, which previous approaches could not establish.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper provides rigorous proofs for the key claims, including the theoretical advantage of adaptive sampling over fixed sampling (Theorem 3.2) and the computational complexity of the proposed method (Theorem 3.4). The optimization formulation in Section 3.1 is particularly elegant.

2. **Practical contribution**: The method is designed to be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations with minimal computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration), which is comparable to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component. This addresses a real practical problem in adaptive sampling.

3. **Clear empirical validation**: The paper provides comprehensive experimental results across multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) and loss functions (squared hinge, logistic, square loss) with both L1 and L2 regularization. The results clearly demonstrate the advantage of the proposed method over fixed sampling.

4. **Careful distinction from prior work**: The paper clearly articulates how its approach differs from previous work on adaptive importance sampling, particularly by providing theoretical guarantees that the approximation is never worse than fixed sampling (which previous methods could not establish).

5. **Practical relevance**: The paper demonstrates how to efficiently compute the necessary safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs), which are widely used in machine learning, making the method immediately applicable to many common problems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited theoretical explanation for CD vs. SGD performance difference**: The empirical results show significantly better performance for CD than for SGD, but the paper does not provide a theoretical explanation for this difference. This is a notable gap, as it would help practitioners understand when to apply the method.

2. **Limited scalability discussion**: While the paper mentions the computational complexity, it doesn't thoroughly discuss how the method would scale to extremely large datasets (e.g., with billions of features). The O(n log n) complexity is reasonable for the datasets used, but the behavior on much larger problems is unclear.

3. **Lack of comparison with more recent adaptive methods**: The paper cites relevant prior work, but could have compared with more recent adaptive sampling methods that might have addressed some of the limitations the authors identify (e.g., [5, 23, 36, 37] are cited, but there may be more recent work).

4. **Implementation details**: The empirical section could provide more details about how the safe gradient bounds were computed for the different datasets, including how the initial bounds (`0 = 0n, u0 = ∞n`) were handled in practice.

5. **Limited theoretical convergence rate analysis**: While the paper demonstrates the advantage of adaptive sampling in terms of expected progress, it doesn't provide a detailed convergence rate analysis for the proposed method, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

## Specific Comments

1. **Section 2.1 (CD with Adaptive Importance Sampling)**: The connection between the theoretical analysis and the proposed algorithm is clear, but the explanation of why the adaptive stepsize αk(p?k) can be up to n times larger than the fixed sampling stepsize could be made more intuitive.

2. **Section 3.1 (Optimization Formulation)**: The optimization formulation is elegant, but the explanation of why the worst-case guarantee approach is better than using extreme bounds (like ck = `k or ck = uk) could be strengthened with a simple geometric argument.

3. **Section 4 (Example Safe Gradient Bounds)**: The demonstration of how to compute safe gradient bounds for GLMs is excellent, but the paper could have included a brief discussion of how this approach might extend to more complex models beyond GLMs.

4. **Figures 2-6 (CD Results)**: The figures effectively demonstrate the advantage of the proposed method, but the "theoretical sampling quality" plots (bottom of each figure) could have included a comparison with the theoretical worst-case bounds to better illustrate how close the method is to the optimal.

5. **Section 5 (Empirical Evaluation)**: The SGD results (Figures 7-9) are somewhat underwhelming compared to the CD results, and the paper doesn't adequately explain why this difference exists. A more thorough discussion of this discrepancy would strengthen the paper.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The theoretical guarantees are rigorous, the method is practical and easy to implement, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over fixed sampling methods. The paper addresses a real problem in stochastic optimization and provides a solution that is both theoretically sound and computationally efficient.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses (particularly regarding the CD vs. SGD performance difference), these do not undermine the overall quality and contribution of the work. The method has clear potential for widespread adoption in machine learning practitioners' toolkits.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a solid theoretical contribution with practical implications, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a clear distinction from prior work.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Novel approach to adaptive importance sampling that avoids full gradient computation while maintaining theoretical guarantees
- Provably better than fixed sampling, a guarantee not established for previous methods
- Practical implementation with reasonable computational complexity (O(n log n) per iteration)
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and loss functions
- Clear and well-structured presentation of ideas

## Arguments for Rejection

- The performance difference between CD and SGD is not adequately explained (though this is a minor point)
- Could have included more comparisons with recent adaptive sampling methods
- Limited discussion of scalability to extremely large datasets

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and it makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning.
