- Proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for CD and SGD that uses maintained lower/upper gradient bounds to compute sampling distributions efficiently and provably improves over fixed sampling.
- Theoretical guarantee: Theorem 3.2 shows the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling; competitive ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5–3.6) bounds worst-case performance.
- Practical algorithm: Algorithm 4 computes the safe sampling distribution in O(n log n) per iteration, comparable to a single gradient component.
- Empirical validation: Experiments on rcv1, real-sim, news20 with square/hinge/logistic losses and L1/L2 regularizers demonstrate significant convergence improvements and analyze adaptive stepsize behavior.
- Clear technical distinction between ideal full-gradient adaptive sampling and the efficient safe approximation, with preserved guarantees.
- Limited empirical scope: evaluations focus on generalized linear models and do not test more complex models like deep neural networks despite claiming generality.
- SGD gap unexplained: the method's effect is much less pronounced on SGD than CD, and the paper does not adequately explain why.
- Incomplete comparisons: does not directly compare against several recent adaptive sampling methods (e.g., [3, 21, 23]).
- Missing practical timings: claims about reduced overhead (e.g., O(log n) when d = Ω(n)) lack concrete timing measurements on large-scale datasets.
- Overall assessment and recommendation: strong theoretical and practical contribution, with manageable limitations; recommendation: Accept.
