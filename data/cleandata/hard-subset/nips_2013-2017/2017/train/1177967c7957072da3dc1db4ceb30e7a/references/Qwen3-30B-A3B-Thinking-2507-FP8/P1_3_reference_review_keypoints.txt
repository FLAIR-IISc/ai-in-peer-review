- Proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method that approximates the optimal sampling distribution using safe lower/upper gradient bounds.  
- Key theoretical guarantee (Theorem 3.2): the safe sampling is never worse than fixed importance sampling.  
- Formulates sampling as an optimization over a bounded gradient set, providing a novel and well-justified approach.  
- Method can be implemented with O(n log n) overhead per iteration; Algorithm 4 and complexity analysis are provided.  
- Shows how to efficiently maintain safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs).  
- Empirical results on rcv1, real-sim, news20 and multiple losses/regularizers show significant speedups over fixed sampling in convergence and compute time.  
- Strength: combines theoretical soundness, computational efficiency, and practical implementation details.  
- Weakness: empirical validation limited to GLMs; no experiments on more complex models or distributed gradient tracking despite claims.  
- Weakness: inconsistent gains—large improvements for coordinate descent (CD) but only modest gains for SGD, with insufficient explanation.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical comparison to several recent adaptive sampling methods cited in the literature.  
- Weakness: relies on coordinate-wise L-smoothness, a strong assumption not analyzed for practical violations.  
- Recommendation: accept — paper makes a substantive, well-supported contribution to large-scale optimization.  
- Key suggestions for authors: explain CD vs SGD performance gap, compare to more recent adaptive methods, broaden experiments beyond GLMs, and clarify the impact of coordinate-wise L-smoothness.
