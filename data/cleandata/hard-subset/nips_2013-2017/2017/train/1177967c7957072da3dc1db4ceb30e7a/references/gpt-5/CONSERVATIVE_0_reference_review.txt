Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It argues that fully gradient-based sampling (probabilities proportional to |∇i f|√Li in CD, and to ‖∇fi(x)‖ in finite-sum SGD) can be theoretically superior to fixed (data-dependent but static) importance sampling and uniform sampling. Because full-gradient information is typically unavailable at each iteration, the authors propose a “safe” adaptive scheme: maintain per-coordinate lower/upper bounds on the relevant gradient magnitudes, and choose a sampling distribution by solving a minimax problem that minimizes the worst-case quadratic upper bound on one-step progress over all gradients consistent with the bounds. They derive a closed-form characterization and an O(n log n) algorithm to compute the distribution and an associated stepsize 1/vk. They provide theoretical guarantees that, under the model, the proposed sampling is never worse than fixed Li-based sampling and approaches the optimal gradient-based distribution when the bounds tighten. They illustrate how to maintain safe bounds for GLMs and present experiments on three text datasets for CD and SGD, showing substantial gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD.

The work relates to prior non-uniform sampling for CD and SGD (Nesterov; Richtárik & Takáč; Qu & Richtárik; Zhao & Zhang; Csiba et al.), Gauss-Southwell and steepest-coordinate rules (Nutini et al., Stich et al.), and safe screening (Liu et al., Ndiaye et al.). The minimax formulation is reminiscent of robust optimization and uses minimax theorems (Sion, Komiya).

Quality
– Theory: The one-step progress analysis and choice of pk and αk is standard. The minimax problem over gradient uncertainty sets is clearly formulated, and the O(n log n) procedure (Algorithm 4) is plausible given the thresholding structure. The guarantee V(p̂, c) ≤ Tr[L]‖c‖2 for all c in the uncertainty set yields “never worse than fixed Li-based sampling” at the model level. However:
  • Convergence rates of the full algorithm with inexact, evolving bounds are deferred to the appendix and not emphasized; end-to-end complexity vs. uniform/fixed baselines under standard convex/strongly convex assumptions is not clearly established in the main text.
  • The safety guarantee critically hinges on truly safe bounds. For GLMs, the update formulas require Hessian bounds along a segment; the paper sketches arguments but rigorous conditions for each loss (squared hinge, logistic) are not fully spelled out, especially with separable regularizers. The use of Cauchy–Schwarz bounds can be extremely loose; the analysis does not quantify the impact on vk and practical progress.
  • For SGD, the method incurs O(n log n) per iteration to refresh pk in the worst case, which is often prohibitive when a single component gradient costs O(d) with d ≪ n; theoretical justification for practicality in finite-sum settings is limited, and experiments also show only minor gains.

– Experiments: The empirical section shows consistent CD gains on three datasets, but the evidence is limited:
  • Baselines are weak. There is no comparison to Gauss–Southwell/steepest CD (even approximate), to adaptive non-uniform CD with stale gradients (e.g., AdaSDCA/Quartz variants), or to recent approximate steepest-coordinate methods. For SGD, more competitive importance sampling with variance reduction or stale updates is missing.
  • Overhead is not rigorously profiled. While some timing plots are provided, they are on different machines, and there is no breakdown of time spent in bound maintenance and sorting. The O(n log n) overhead per iteration is consequential and should be quantified across regimes (varying n, d, sparsity).
  • Dataset setup choices (binary features, same λ across datasets, no normalization) may bias difficulties and sampling advantages; no sensitivity or ablations (e.g., varying bound tightness, initialization, regularization) are shown.
  • No statistical variability (error bars), and evaluation focuses on training objective not test performance.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written but with some notational and expository issues:
  • The statement “∇2 h(Ax) = In” for least squares is ambiguous; for h(z)=1/2‖z−b‖2, ∇2 h(z)=I in z, while with respect to x the Hessian is A^T A. This should be clarified around Eq. (11).
  • Overloaded notation for ℓ, u and indices in Algorithm 4 is confusing; careful variable naming would help.
  • Key convergence results are pushed to the appendix; a concise theorem in the main text would strengthen the story.

Originality
The robust/minimax formulation to choose a sampling distribution from safe gradient bounds, with a concrete O(n log n) algorithm, appears novel in this context. The idea of using bounds is related to safe screening and stale/upper-bound-based sampling, but the “safe and optimal under uncertainty” framing is a nice contribution. The stepsize coupling with pk is a minor but sensible twist.

Significance
For CD on convex GLMs, the method could be of practical interest if the overhead is amortized by large d and if safe bounds can be maintained cheaply; the theoretical “never worse than fixed” result is attractive. However, the limited experimental validation, lack of strong baselines, and unclear practicality for SGD reduce the impact. Without more comprehensive evidence and tighter analysis, I am cautious about endorsing this as a significant advance.

Pros
- Clear formulation of a safe adaptive sampling problem via a convex minimax and an efficient solver.
- Sound one-step progress analysis and guarantees relative to fixed importance sampling.
- Practical bound-maintenance strategies for GLMs and empirical gains for CD.

Cons
- Convergence guarantees and end-to-end complexity are not fully established in the main text.
- Reliance on potentially very loose Cauchy–Schwarz bounds; no quantification of bound quality vs. performance.
- O(n log n) overhead each iteration; insufficient profiling and scaling analysis, especially for SGD.
- Missing comparisons to stronger adaptive/greedy CD and SGD baselines; limited datasets and ablations.
- Some clarity/notation issues.

Questions for the authors
1) Please provide a main-text convergence theorem for Algorithm 2 under standard convex/strongly convex assumptions, including dependence on vk sequences.
2) For logistic and squared hinge losses, what explicit Hessian bounds are used to ensure safe updates in Eq. (12)? Are the bounds guaranteed for all iterates with L1 regularization?
3) How often do you recompute/sort to update p̂k in practice? Can you reuse orderings or use incremental updates to reduce O(n log n) to near-linear?
4) Can you profile per-iteration time into gradient computation, bound maintenance, and sampling computation across datasets?
5) How does performance vary with the tightness of bounds? Can you simulate different γ in Lemma 3.6 and show ρk empirically?
6) Why omit comparisons to Gauss–Southwell (and approximate versions) and to AdaSDCA/Quartz-like methods?

Recommendation
Lean reject. The core idea is interesting and grounded in established analysis, and the “safe” guarantee is appealing. However, the work falls short on empirical rigor and comparative evaluation, and important practical and theoretical details are not convincingly substantiated in the main text. Strengthening the experimental section with strong baselines, overhead profiling, and ablations, and clarifying convergence and bound assumptions, would substantially improve the paper.
