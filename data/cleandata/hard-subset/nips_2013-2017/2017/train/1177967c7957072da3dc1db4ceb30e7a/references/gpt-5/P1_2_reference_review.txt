Summary
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for stochastic optimization, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors first show that “gradient-based sampling” (probabilities proportional to √Li |∇if(x)| in CD, and to ‖∇fi(x)‖2 in SGD) maximizes an upper-bound model of the one-step expected progress and can be up to n times faster than fixed (Li-based or uniform) sampling. Since computing these “optimal” probabilities requires full gradient information each iteration, they propose a safe approximation based on per-coordinate lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (or per-component gradient norms for SGD). They cast the sampling choice as a convex min–max problem that selects probabilities p̂ minimizing the worst-case ratio V(p, c)/‖c‖2 over all c consistent with the bounds, where V(p, c) = ∑i Li c2i /pi. They prove: (i) p̂ has the best worst-case guarantee given the available information; (ii) it is never worse than Li-based (uniform) sampling in this model; (iii) when bounds collapse to the true gradient, p̂ recovers the full-information optimal sampling. They provide an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting and a water-filling-like thresholding) to compute p̂ and the associated safe stepsize α̂ = 1/v̂. They describe how to maintain safe bounds cheaply for GLMs in both CD and SGD via simple linearized updates and Cauchy-Schwarz. Experiments on several text datasets show substantial improvements over uniform/fixed sampling for CD, and smaller but positive gains for SGD; the observed stepsizes indicate the method leverages information beyond fixed sampling.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation of the expected progress bound, the optimal full-information sampling for CD (Example 2.3), and the min–max formulation under limited information are standard and carefully executed. The use of Sion/Komiya minimax to justify interchanging min and max and the construction in Theorem 3.4 are plausible; the algorithmic solution via sorted thresholds is elegant and efficient. The guarantee “never worse than fixed sampling” is clearly stated in terms of the upper-bound model, not necessarily the true objective decrease.
- Completeness: Proofs are deferred to the appendix, and the main claims are well supported. The experimental section validates benefits for CD across losses and regularizers, and illustrates the behavior of vk.
- Caveats: The practical computational overhead is nontrivial. Computing p̂ is O(n log n) per iteration and maintaining bounds is O(n), which can dominate per-iteration costs in SGD or sparse CD. The paper argues the overhead is small when d = Ω(n) because a coordinate update costs Θ(d), but this comparison could be more precise and is data-dependent. For SGD, the method’s overhead can be prohibitive when n is large, which may explain the modest empirical gains.

Clarity
The paper is well written overall, with clear notation and structure. The separation between full-information results (Sec. 2), the safe min–max formulation (Sec. 3), how to obtain bounds (Sec. 4), and experiments (Sec. 5) is helpful. Minor issues:
- Some typos/grammar: performance, “inadmissible” might be rephrased, a few run-on sentences.
- The statement “overhead reduces to O(log n) per iteration when d = Ω(n)” is confusing; it seems to refer to the overhead factor versus gradient cost, not absolute overhead.
- The dependence of all guarantees on compactness of the bound set Ck should be explicit; experiments begin with u0 = ∞, which violates compactness—clarify how the algorithm handles this in the first iterations.

Originality
The safe adaptive sampling via a min–max optimization over gradient bounds, with a provable worst-case guarantee relative to fixed importance sampling and an efficient O(n log n) solver, appears novel. Prior work considered full-information gradient-based sampling (e.g., Zhao & Zhang; Richtárik & Takáč and follow-ups) or heuristics using stale gradients/upper bounds; the paper provides a conceptually clean and principled alternative. The stepsize choice α = 1/v̂ aligned with the chosen sampling is also a nice contribution for CD.

Significance
For CD on GLMs and related problems, the approach is practically relevant: it offers an adaptive scheme with guarantees and empirically strong performance, sometimes close to steepest CD, yet without computing full gradients. The idea of safe min–max sampling could be reused in other settings (block-CD, minibatch SGD). For SGD on large n, the current per-iteration overhead limits impact; still, the conceptual contribution may inspire amortized/infrequent updates, incremental data structures, or distributed trackers.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical motivation: full-information optimal sampling can yield up to n-fold gains.
- Novel safe adaptive sampling under partial information with a best-possible worst-case guarantee, and reduction to full-information when bounds are tight.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm with a simple thresholding structure.
- Practical recipes to maintain safe bounds in GLMs.
- Solid empirical gains for CD across datasets and losses; stepsize adaptation is important in practice.

Weaknesses and questions
- Overhead for SGD and sparse CD: O(n log n) sampling plus O(n) bound updates per iteration can be prohibitive. Can p̂ be updated every T iterations with little loss? Any incremental O(log n) updates using heaps or partial scans?
- Assumptions on bounds: Theoretical guarantees require finite [ℓ, u]; experiments start with u0 = ∞. How is p̂ defined then, and do guarantees still apply? Please clarify initialization and the first-epoch behavior.
- Comparisons: It would strengthen the case to compare against other adaptive baselines beyond uniform/fixed: Gauss–Southwell and approximate steepest CD (e.g., Stich et al. 2017), stale-gradient sampling, gap-based CD (e.g., Csiba et al.). Also compare against the heuristic p ∝ u or p ∝ √L u to illustrate the value of the min–max formulation.
- Convergence rates: The paper mentions standard rate derivations in the appendix. Summarizing main-rate theorems in the main text (convex/strongly convex) would aid readers.
- Practical guidance: When is the method most beneficial (n vs d, sparsity, curvature heterogeneity)? Any guidance on when gains offset overhead?

Pro/Con for acceptance
Pros:
- Conceptually clean, theoretically grounded method with guarantees stronger than prior adaptive heuristics under limited information.
- Efficient and implementable algorithm, with clear construction.
- Strong empirical results for CD; practical utility for important ML models.

Cons:
- Per-iteration overhead limits applicability to SGD and very high-n regimes; empirical SGD gains are modest.
- Missing comparisons to several strong adaptive/CD baselines; unclear practical trade-offs vs approximate steepest/Gauss–Southwell.
- Some clarity issues regarding initialization and overhead discussion.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to Gauss–Southwell/approximate steepest CD and other adaptive sampling heuristics.
- Provide an amortized or lazy update variant (e.g., recompute p̂ every T steps) and report sensitivity.
- Clarify initialization when u0 = ∞ and the conditions required for the min–max guarantees.
- Move a compact convergence-rate statement to the main text.
- Tighten the overhead discussion with concrete FLOP counts and wall-clock on the same hardware.

Overall, this is a solid and original contribution with clear theory and good practical benefits for CD. I lean toward acceptance, especially if the authors address comparisons and clarify practical overheads.
