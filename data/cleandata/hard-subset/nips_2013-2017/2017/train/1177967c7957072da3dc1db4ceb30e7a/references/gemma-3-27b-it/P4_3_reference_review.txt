This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling, ensuring theoretical guarantees and computational feasibility. The authors demonstrate that the proposed scheme outperforms fixed sampling and provides a provable improvement over uniform sampling, with empirical validation on real datasets.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents its contributions. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the drawbacks of existing adaptive strategies. The theoretical analysis in Section 2 provides a strong justification for adaptive sampling, particularly for CD methods, demonstrating a potential speed-up factor of the dimension *n*. The proposed safe adaptive sampling strategy in Section 3, along with its efficient computation via Algorithm 4, is a significant contribution. The discussion on tracking gradient bounds for GLMs in Section 4 is practical and relevant. The empirical evaluation in Section 5, although limited in scope, provides evidence supporting the theoretical claims.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper offers a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed approach, building on existing work in adaptive importance sampling and coordinate descent. The analysis demonstrating the speed-up potential for CD is particularly strong.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is designed to be computationally tractable, addressing a major limitation of existing adaptive strategies. The O(n log n) complexity for computing the sampling distribution is reasonable, and the authors acknowledge its impact.
*   **Safety Guarantee:** The “safe” aspect of the adaptive sampling is a crucial strength, ensuring that the scheme never performs worse than fixed sampling, even with imperfect gradient bounds.
*   **Generality:** The approach is applicable to both CD and SGD, increasing its broad applicability.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation primarily focuses on GLMs with specific regularization techniques. Evaluating the scheme on a wider range of problems, including deep learning models and more complex datasets, would strengthen the claims.
*   **SGD Results are Less Convincing:** While the authors report positive results for CD, the improvement observed in the SGD experiments is less pronounced. A more in-depth analysis of the performance differences between CD and SGD, and potential reasons for the weaker results in SGD, would be valuable.
*   **Gradient Bound Tracking:** The method relies on maintaining safe gradient bounds. While the discussion in Section 4 is helpful, a more detailed analysis of the challenges and limitations of tracking these bounds in various scenarios would be beneficial.  Specifically, how sensitive is the algorithm to the tightness of the bounds ` and `u`?
*   **Competition with Existing Techniques:** The paper positions itself within the existing body of work on adaptive sampling. However, a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art adaptive sampling techniques, beyond the mentioned references, would be helpful.  For instance, how does it compare to more recent advancements in variance reduction techniques for SGD?
*   **Notation:** While generally clear, the notation could be more consistent. For example, introducing `l` as a vector and then using it within the diagonal matrix `L` requires careful attention.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant challenge in optimization: efficient adaptive sampling.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong justification for the proposed approach.
*   The proposed algorithm is computationally tractable and offers a safety guarantee.
*   The empirical evaluation, while limited, supports the theoretical claims.
*   The contribution is novel and advances the state-of-the-art in adaptive importance sampling.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
*   The performance gains in SGD are less convincing than those in CD.
*   The reliance on safe gradient bounds introduces a potential practical limitation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed algorithm is efficient and safe, and the empirical results are promising. While the empirical evaluation could be more extensive and the SGD results are less compelling, the overall quality of the paper warrants acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS 2017.
