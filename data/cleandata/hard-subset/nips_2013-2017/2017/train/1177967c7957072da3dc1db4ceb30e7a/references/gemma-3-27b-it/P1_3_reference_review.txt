## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the theoretical benefits of adaptive sampling but also acknowledging its computational challenges and potential for instability. Their key contribution is a practical, computationally efficient adaptive sampling strategy that provides provable guarantees – it's better than uniform/fixed sampling and recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting. The approach relies on efficiently computable, safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient entries, and formulating the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem.  Empirical validation on GLMs demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds directly upon existing work on importance sampling, particularly adaptive variants like gradient-based sampling (Zhao & Zhang, 2017) and duality gap-based sampling (Csiba et al., 2015).  It acknowledges the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and computational cost inherent in adaptive methods. The authors correctly position their work as a bridge between fully adaptive schemes requiring full gradient information and static sampling, offering a more practical and theoretically sound alternative.  They reference relevant work on coordinate descent (Nesterov, 2012), stochastic gradient descent (Needell et al., 2014), and screening methods (Liu et al., 2014).

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides clear theoretical justification for their approach, demonstrating improvements over fixed sampling in terms of expected progress, and establishing a connection to optimal gradient-based sampling.  The guarantees of the proposed method are a significant strength.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is designed to be computationally tractable, avoiding the full gradient computations required by many existing adaptive methods. The authors demonstrate how to efficiently compute the safe sampling distribution using sorting (O(n log n) complexity).
* **Practicality & Generality:** The method is applicable to both CD and SGD, and the framework of safe gradient bounds makes it adaptable to a wide range of problems, as demonstrated by the GLM examples.  The authors provide concrete examples of how to derive these bounds in practice.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on real-world datasets (RCV1, real-sim, news20) show significant performance gains, both in terms of iteration complexity and overall computation time. The comparison with existing methods is comprehensive.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear notation and a logical flow of ideas. The algorithms are presented in a concise manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The performance of the algorithm heavily relies on the quality of the safe gradient bounds ` and `u`. While the authors provide examples for GLMs, it's not immediately clear how easily these bounds can be obtained for more complex models.  The sensitivity of the method to inaccuracies in these bounds warrants further investigation.
* **SGD Results less Dramatic:** While the theoretical analysis covers SGD, the empirical results show a less pronounced improvement compared to CD. The authors acknowledge this, but a deeper exploration of the reasons for this difference would be beneficial. Is it due to the specific datasets used, or inherent limitations of the approach in the SGD setting?
* **Competitive Ratio Complexity:** The analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) is somewhat complex and might be difficult for some readers to follow. While mathematically rigorous, a more intuitive explanation of the meaning and implications of these results would enhance understanding.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons with uniform sampling and gradient-based sampling are included, it would be valuable to compare the proposed method with more recent adaptive sampling techniques, if any exist, and/or with other advanced optimization algorithms.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in large-scale optimization – balancing the benefits of adaptive sampling with computational efficiency.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees about the performance of the proposed method.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the approach.
* The generality of the method makes it applicable to a wide range of machine learning problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on safe gradient bounds could be a limiting factor in certain applications.
* The results for SGD are not as striking as those for CD.
* The complexity of the competitive ratio analysis might hinder accessibility.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed adaptive importance sampling scheme is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically effective.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not outweigh the significant strengths of the work. I believe this paper is **highly suitable for acceptance** to NIPS.

**Rating:**  8.5/10 (Strong Accept)

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*  Expand the discussion of how to obtain safe gradient bounds for a broader range of models beyond GLMs.
*  Investigate the reasons for the less pronounced improvement in SGD performance.
*  Provide a more intuitive explanation of the competitive ratio analysis.
*  Consider including comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art optimization algorithms.
