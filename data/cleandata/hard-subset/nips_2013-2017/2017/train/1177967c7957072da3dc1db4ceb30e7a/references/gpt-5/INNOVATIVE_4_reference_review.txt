Summary
This paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling without computing full gradients by maintaining per-coordinate safe lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (or component gradient norms for SGD). Given these bounds, the authors formulate a minimax problem over sampling distributions that (i) optimizes the worst-case one-step progress bound over all gradients consistent with the bounds, and (ii) provably never performs worse than any fixed (non-adaptive) importance sampling. They provide an O(n log n) algorithm to compute the safe distribution and a stepsize αk = 1/vk derived from the same minimax problem. They also show that, in a full-information regime, the scheme recovers optimal gradient-based sampling. For CD, they prove that gradient-based sampling can improve convergence by up to a factor n over classical fixed sampling. They show how to maintain safe bounds cheaply for generalized linear models using simple inequalities, and they present empirical results on several datasets showing substantial gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
There is a substantial literature on non-uniform and adaptive sampling for CD/SGD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč, Qu et al., Zhao & Zhang, Csiba et al., Perekrestenko et al.), often using Li-based or gradient/gap-based sampling, sometimes with stale or approximated gradients. This paper’s main difference is the safety/worst-case guarantee with limited information: the min-max formulation produces a sampling distribution that is provably no worse than fixed sampling and the best possible given only bounds. The authors connect to Sion’s minimax theorem and provide an efficient construction of the solution. The safe bound maintenance viewpoint also echoes “safe screening” for sparsity, but here it influences sampling rather than variable elimination.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: Casting adaptive sampling with partial information as a minimax problem and deriving a computable O(n log n) solution with a dominance guarantee over fixed importance sampling is, to my knowledge, new and impactful.
- Theory with clear takeaways: (i) clean one-step progress bounds and stepsize selection; (ii) explicit performance dominance over fixed sampling; (iii) recovery of gradient-based sampling under full information; (iv) potential n-fold speedups for CD.
- Practicality: The algorithm only needs safe bounds, not full gradients. The proposed bound maintenance for GLMs is simple. The method is generic and plugs into CD and SGD.
- Empirical evidence: On several datasets and losses, safe adaptive sampling significantly outperforms fixed sampling for CD, approaching the full-information baseline in iterations while maintaining favorable wall-clock times.

Weaknesses and questions
- Computational overhead and scalability: The per-iteration O(n log n) sampling update plus O(n) bound updates can be heavy when n is large and per-coordinate gradient evaluation is cheap (e.g., very sparse problems where nnz(aik) is small). Please clarify regimes where this overhead is amortized (e.g., d ≳ n) and whether you can update p̂k less frequently or incrementally maintain the sorted structures to reduce amortized cost.
- Applicability to composite/proximal CD: The paper states general ψi regularizers, including L1, but the CD update described is a gradient step. In L1 settings one typically uses proximal updates. How does the sampling/stepsize αk interact with proximal CD? Which gradient component (smooth part vs full subgradient) should be used for bounds and for (3)? Some discussion or a proximal variant would strengthen the paper.
- Competing adaptive baselines: Experiments compare mainly to uniform/fixed and to the (infeasible) full-gradient sampling. It would be useful to include practical adaptive baselines such as gradient-norm sampling with stale values, gap-based sampling (e.g., SDCA with adaptive probabilities, AdaSDCA), or approximate steepest CD, to contextualize gains under similar cost budgets.
- SGD results: Gains are modest. It would help to elaborate on when adaptive sampling should help SGD (e.g., heterogeneity in component gradients), and whether the bound-based construction may be too loose in practice for SGD unless tighter bounds are available.
- Tightness of safe bounds: The Cauchy–Schwarz-based bounds can be very loose. Any guidance on constructing tighter yet cheap bounds (e.g., precomputed coherence/leverage, block structure, Hessian diagonals) would increase practical relevance.
- Competitive ratio: Lemma 3.6’s condition is nontrivial. Can you give more intuition, measure ρk empirically, or provide looser but interpretable bounds that indicate dependence on bound tightness?
- Clarity/notation: The exposition occasionally mixes dimensions (d vs n) and sometimes glosses over the nonsmooth case. Cleaning up these parts, and explicitly stating assumptions for each theorem (smoothness, composite structure), would improve readability.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a new, principled, and broadly applicable approach (minimax-safe adaptive sampling) with clear theoretical guarantees and an efficient algorithm.
- Demonstrates meaningful empirical gains in CD with low overhead.
- Bridges “safe” ideas and adaptive sampling; likely to inspire follow-up work on better bounds, block variants, and distributed settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Overhead may limit applicability in very high-dimensional, very sparse problems unless further engineering (incremental updates) is done.
- Limited comparison to other practical adaptive sampling methods.
- Treatment of nonsmooth/proximal settings is not fully developed.

Overall assessment
I view this as a strong and novel contribution. The safety guarantee under limited information, the minimax formulation, and the efficient solver are conceptually fresh and practically relevant. While some empirical and scope limitations exist, they are not fundamental and are outweighed by the new direction and clean theory.
