This paper tackles the important problem of adaptive importance sampling, a cornerstone of efficient optimization in large-scale machine learning. The core idea – developing a computationally efficient and theoretically grounded adaptive sampling strategy – is highly promising and aligns well with NIPS’s focus on impactful algorithmic advances. The authors correctly identify the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees, computational cost, and practical performance in existing methods. The proposed “safe” adaptive sampling, building on bounds on gradient information, is a clever approach to address these challenges. The theoretical analysis, particularly the connection to gradient-based sampling and the derivation of convergence rates, is a significant strength. The empirical evaluation on GLMs is well-executed and provides compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness, especially in the coordinate descent setting.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed “safe” adaptive sampling strategy is a genuinely novel contribution. It offers a practical way to leverage adaptive sampling benefits without the full gradient computation requirement, which is a key bottleneck in many applications. The formulation as a convex optimization problem and the efficient algorithm for its solution are also novel.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The analysis comparing the performance to fixed sampling and gradient-based sampling is convincing, and the convergence guarantees are valuable. The demonstration of at least as good performance compared to existing methods is important.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The applicability to both CD and SGD, and the focus on efficiently computable bounds makes this work immediately relevant to a broad range of machine learning problems.  The demonstration on GLMs is a good starting point.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the derivations are mostly easy to follow. The algorithms are presented in a concise and understandable manner.
* **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. Efficient adaptive sampling can significantly reduce the computational cost of training large-scale models, making it a valuable tool for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

* **SGD Results Less Convincing:** The empirical results for SGD are less compelling than those for CD. The improvement over uniform sampling is modest, and the authors acknowledge this. Further investigation into why the benefits are less pronounced in the SGD setting would be valuable. Perhaps the gradient bounds are less effective in this context.
* **Bounded Gradient Assumption:** The reliance on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients is a potential limitation. While the authors demonstrate how to obtain these bounds for GLMs, it’s not immediately clear how easily this generalizes to more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks).  A discussion of the challenges and potential solutions for obtaining these bounds in more complex settings would strengthen the paper.
* **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The competitive ratio analysis in Section 3.3 feels a bit disconnected from the practical performance evaluation. While theoretically interesting, its relevance to the empirical results isn’t fully clear.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** While GLMs are a good starting point, extending the empirical evaluation to more diverse datasets and models (e.g., deep learning tasks) would further solidify the claims of the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* This paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling.
* The proposed method addresses a critical challenge in large-scale machine learning – balancing computational cost and performance.
* The empirical results, particularly for CD, are compelling and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas and contributions.
* The theoretical guarantees, even with the reliance on bounds, are significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited improvement over uniform sampling in the SGD setting raises some concerns about the generality of the method.
* The dependence on safe gradient bounds could be a limitation for some applications.
* The competitive ratio analysis feels somewhat theoretical and disconnected from the experimental results.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor weaknesses, I strongly believe this paper deserves acceptance to NIPS. The novelty, theoretical soundness, and practical relevance of the proposed method are compelling. The authors have identified a significant challenge in large-scale optimization and have developed a promising solution. While further work is needed to address the limitations, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and has the potential to significantly impact future research in adaptive sampling and optimization algorithms. I rate this paper as **Accept**. I would encourage the authors to expand the empirical evaluation and further investigate the performance in the SGD setting as future work.
