- Proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for CD and SGD that uses bounds on gradients to avoid full-gradient computation.  
- Shows gradient-based sampling can theoretically speed up CD by up to a factor of n over fixed sampling.  
- Formulates finding the best adaptive sampling under gradient bounds as a convex problem (Equation 7) and solves it in O(n log n) time (Theorem 3.4).  
- Demonstrates safe gradient bounds can be efficiently computed for generalized linear models (GLMs).  
- Provides theoretical guarantees that the safe method is never worse than fixed importance sampling.  
- Offers comprehensive proofs for key claims (Lemma 2.4, Theorems 3.2 and 3.4).  
- Empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, losses, and regularizers shows substantial gains for CD with small overhead.  
- Includes iteration- and time-based comparisons and analysis of adaptive stepsizes (vk).  
- Acknowledges that adaptive sampling yields less pronounced gains for SGD than for CD and discusses observed behavior.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-organized, and connects theory to implementation effectively.  
- Mathematical notation and algorithms are clear and consistent.  
- Some explanations (e.g., Example 3.1 intuition and relation between theory and practice) could be clearer.  
- Key proofs and details (e.g., inequality (*) in Equation 8) are relegated to the appendix, reducing accessibility.  
- Original contribution is the practical, theoretically guaranteed "safe" approach that is computationally efficient compared to prior adaptive sampling work.  
- Method is significant for large-scale ML because O(n log n) overhead is small relative to component gradient cost O(d).  
- Strengths: strong theoretical foundation, practical implementation, thorough experiments, GLM applicability, good presentation.  
- Weaknesses: limited theoretical explanation for weaker SGD gains, sparse competitive-ratio analysis, focus mainly on GLMs, some key math deferred to appendix.  
- Recommends acceptance based on novelty, theoretical rigor, and empirical validation.  
- Suggests adding discussion on why SGD benefits less than CD, per-iteration time comparisons, illustrative examples for ρk, geometric intuition for Section 3.2, and brief justification for no-gradient-initialization claim.
