

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a novel approach to adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The key insight is to formulate adaptive sampling as a convex optimization problem using safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, rather than relying on approximations or full gradient computations. The authors prove that their method is provably better than fixed importance sampling while maintaining computational efficiency (O(n log n) per iteration), making it practical for large-scale optimization.

The paper's novelty is significant. Previous adaptive sampling methods either required full gradient computations (making them inadmissible for large-scale problems) or lacked theoretical guarantees. The authors' "safe" approach—using convex optimization to determine sampling probabilities within the bounds of available gradient information—provides a theoretically grounded alternative that is both efficient and provably superior to static sampling. This is a bold conceptual shift from the typical approach of approximating optimal sampling distributions.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The theoretical contributions are strong, with careful proofs establishing that the proposed method:
1) Is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2)
2) Recovers the gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting
3) Can be computed efficiently (Theorem 3.4)

The empirical evaluation demonstrates practical benefits, particularly for coordinate descent. The results show that adaptive sampling significantly outperforms fixed sampling in terms of convergence speed and computational efficiency across multiple datasets and loss functions. The paper's claim that "very loose safe gradient bounds are sufficient" is well-supported by the experiments, with the method performing nearly as well as the optimal (but computationally infeasible) sampling after just one epoch.

The authors effectively differentiate their approach from related work. They properly cite key papers on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) and clearly explain how their method differs from previous approaches that use approximations or stale values. The comparison with gradient-based sampling (which requires full gradient computation) is particularly compelling.

While the theoretical guarantees are strong, I note that the empirical results for SGD are less impressive than for CD. The paper acknowledges this, suggesting it may be due to the nature of the problems rather than the method itself. This honest discussion of limitations is a strength.

The paper's main weakness is that it focuses primarily on generalized linear models (GLMs) for demonstrating the gradient bound computation. While the authors claim their approach is generic, the paper doesn't sufficiently explore how the method would perform on more complex models where gradient bounds might be harder to compute. The theoretical framework is sound, but the practical applicability beyond GLMs isn't fully demonstrated.

Despite this limitation, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The "safe" adaptive importance sampling framework is a novel conceptual advance that could influence how adaptive sampling is implemented in various optimization algorithms. The computational efficiency (O(n log n) per iteration) is particularly appealing, as it's comparable to the cost of evaluating a single coordinate of the gradient.

The paper successfully addresses a fundamental challenge in adaptive sampling: how to obtain the benefits of adaptive methods without the computational cost. The authors have provided a theoretically sound and practically efficient solution that could become a standard component in optimization algorithms.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper highly compelling. The conceptual framework is fresh, the theoretical guarantees are strong, and the method has clear practical value. While the empirical results for SGD are somewhat limited, this doesn't undermine the paper's core contribution, which is primarily a theoretical and methodological advance.

The paper meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS submission:
- Quality: Strong theoretical foundation with careful proofs
- Clarity: Well-organized and clearly written
- Originality: Novel approach to adaptive importance sampling
- Significance: Potential to impact a wide range of optimization algorithms

I recommend acceptance. This paper introduces a significant new direction in adaptive sampling that addresses a fundamental limitation in the field. The method is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically validated. While the SGD results could be more comprehensive, the core contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant publication at NIPS.
