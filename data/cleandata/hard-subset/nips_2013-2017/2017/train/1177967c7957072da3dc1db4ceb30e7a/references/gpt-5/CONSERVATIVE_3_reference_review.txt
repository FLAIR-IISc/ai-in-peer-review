Summary
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors (i) show that gradient-based sampling for CD can yield up to an n-fold speedup over fixed (Li-based) sampling under the standard quadratic upper-bound model; (ii) propose a “safe” adaptive scheme that, given per-coordinate lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes, chooses probabilities and a step-size via a min–max problem; (iii) give an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting-based) to compute the sampling; (iv) argue how to maintain safe bounds for GLMs; and (v) present experiments on GLMs showing clear gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD. A key theoretical statement is that the proposed sampling is never worse than fixed importance sampling (under the model), and recovers the ideal gradient-based sampling when full information is available.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of the sampling choice as an optimization problem and an elegant min–max formulation that uses only safe bounds.
- Sound and interpretable guarantee: the proposed sampling dominates Li-based sampling with respect to the quadratic upper-bound model; reduces to gradient-based sampling with exact gradients.
- Efficient algorithm to compute the sampling in O(n log n), with a constructive characterization of the worst-case vector achieving the min–max value.
- The experimental results for CD on GLMs suggest the method can approach the oracle sampling with modest overhead; the stepsize adaptation is empirically important.
- The work connects and consolidates several lines of prior work on non-uniform/importance sampling in CD/SGD, and highlights the pitfalls of stale/approximate distributions.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality and soundness
- The “never worse than fixed sampling” guarantee is with respect to the surrogate quadratic model (equation (1)), not necessarily the true objective. The paper sometimes reads as if this were an absolute guarantee; the distinction should be emphasized more carefully.
- Global convergence and complexity bounds of CD with the proposed adaptive probabilities and step-sizes are only sketched (“standard techniques”/appendix). A formal theorem in the main paper comparing iteration complexity to fixed sampling would strengthen the case.
- The practical viability is questionable: maintaining safe bounds is O(n) per iteration and computing probabilities is O(n log n) per iteration, which can dwarf the O(nnz of column) cost of a CD update in sparse settings. The paper argues this is negligible when the per-update gradient cost is Θ(d) and d = Ω(n), but this is not typical in the sparse, high-dimensional problems used in the experiments. The wall-clock improvements shown on subsets do not convincingly address the O(n log n) overhead for n up to 10^5–10^6.
- Assumptions in Section 4 rely on twice-differentiability for deriving safe updates; yet experiments include squared hinge loss, which is not twice differentiable. The paper does not explain how the safe-bounds machinery applies in that case.
- The approach hinges on “safe” bounds that are often very loose (e.g., Cauchy–Schwarz). While the method can still help, the theoretical benefit then becomes less informative; bounds tighter than trivial C–S would likely be necessary in practice, yet no systematic treatment or analysis is provided.

Clarity
- The exposition is generally clear but dense. Several key proofs (min–max interchange, Theorem 3.4 derivation) are deferred to the appendix. The algorithmic cost discussion could be more transparent, explicitly contrasting dense vs sparse regimes and when the O(n log n) sorting each iteration is acceptable.
- Some terminology (“inadmissible”) and small typos detract slightly.

Originality
- The core idea—choosing a sampling distribution via a worst-case optimization over safe intervals—appears novel in this precise form. The formulation and the O(n log n) construction are interesting. However, the underlying building blocks (gradient-based sampling; non-uniform CD/SGD; safe screening/bounds) are established, and several recent works propose adaptive probabilities or related “gap-based” heuristics. The paper would benefit from a more thorough empirical comparison to those practical baselines.

Significance
- Potentially useful for practitioners of CD if the overhead can be controlled or amortized. However, without stronger evidence of scalability and broader comparisons, the impact is uncertain.
- For SGD, the gains are modest in the presented experiments, limiting practical significance there.

Experimental validation
- Comparisons lack several relevant baselines: Gauss–Southwell/GS-r variants, adaptive probabilities based on stale gradients (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, Csiba et al.), and recent adaptive/gap-based CD methods (e.g., Perekrestenko et al.). Comparing mainly to an oracle and to uniform/fixed sampling does not establish superiority among practical methods.
- Scalability concerns: results on full-scale news20 are not shown; subsets are used. It is unclear how often the probabilities are recomputed; exploring less frequent updates or incremental maintenance would be informative.
- Non-smooth losses and safe-bounds applicability should be clarified and validated.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean “safe” formulation with a constructive, efficient algorithm and clear theoretical dominance over fixed sampling (under the model).
- Strong empirical performance for CD on GLMs with large potential speedups.

Cons for acceptance
- Practical overhead and sparse-regime applicability not convincingly addressed; experiments avoid the most challenging scales.
- Limited comparisons to competing adaptive sampling heuristics.
- Some theoretical aspects that matter in practice (global rates with proposed αk, treatment of non-smooth losses) are not fully developed in the main paper.

Questions for the authors
1. How often are the probabilities recomputed in practice? Can you amortize the O(n log n) sorting, or update p̂ incrementally?
2. How do you handle squared hinge (non C2) within the safe-bounds framework? What constants are used?
3. Can you provide end-to-end convergence rates (not just one-step progress) comparing iteration complexity to Li-based sampling for CD with your αk?
4. Please include comparisons to Gauss–Southwell(-Lipschitz) and to adaptive/stale-gradient sampling baselines.
5. Can you report results on truly large n (without subsetting), including wall-clock breakdown of overhead vs update cost?

Recommendation
Score: 5 (borderline/weak reject)
Confidence: 0.7

While the safe min–max sampling is a neat and potentially impactful idea, the paper, in its current form, does not sufficiently establish practical viability or superiority over existing adaptive baselines, especially given the per-iteration overhead and reliance on loose bounds. Strengthening the empirical section, clarifying assumptions for non-smooth losses, and providing fuller convergence results would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
