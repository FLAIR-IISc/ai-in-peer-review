This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, aiming to improve computational efficiency while providing theoretical guarantees. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling by leveraging safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients, formulating the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem solvable in O(n log n) time. The authors demonstrate theoretical improvements over fixed sampling, and empirically validate their approach on GLMs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a clear theoretical justification for adaptive sampling, specifically demonstrating improvements over fixed sampling for CD methods (factor of *n* speedup). The proposed safe sampling strategy and its associated guarantees are well-defined.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient, with a complexity of O(n log n) per iteration, making it potentially applicable to large-scale problems. The method for tracking safe gradient bounds in GLMs is practical and well-motivated.
*   **Generality:** The method is presented as applicable to both CD and SGD, broadening its potential impact.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on GLMs demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach, showing faster convergence compared to fixed sampling. The plots illustrating the evolution of *vk* provide valuable insight into the algorithm’s behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Sloppiness:** The notation is occasionally inconsistent and imprecise. For example, the definition of *l* in the notation section is initially a vector, then appears as a diagonal matrix *L* with the same symbol. The use of `∇if` to denote both the i-th component of the gradient and the gradient of the i-th coordinate is confusing and demands more careful notation.
*   **Ambiguous Assumptions:** The assumption of having access to safe upper and lower bounds `l` and `u` is presented without sufficient discussion of its practical limitations. While an example is provided for GLMs, the applicability to other models requires further investigation. The explanation of how to maintain these bounds during optimization lacks sufficient detail; simply stating “using Cauchy-Schwarz” is insufficient without clarifying the practical implications and potential for instability.
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experimental evaluation is primarily focused on GLMs. While this provides a valuable proof-of-concept, the lack of results on more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks) limits the generalizability of the findings. The fact that SGD experiments did *not* show significant benefits, but are presented in full detail, is a concerning observation and requires more thoughtful discussion.
*   **Algorithm 1's Practicality:** Algorithm 1, the optimal sampling with full gradient computation, while theoretically sound, is presented as a benchmark but is likely impractical for large-scale problems. The paper doesn't adequately address the cost implications of full gradient computation and its relationship to the proposed approximation.
*   **Missing Proof Details:** Several claims are made without full proof details, referencing “Section A.1” and “Appendix B”. These appendices are not provided in the submitted paper, making it impossible to verify the correctness of the theoretical results. This is a critical flaw.
*   **Minor Formatting Issues:** Throughout the paper, there are minor formatting issues (e.g., inconsistent spacing, use of italics) that detract from its overall polish. For example, the definition of ∆n needs consistent notation and formatting.
*   **Overly Strong Claims:** The claim of "provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling" is too strong without qualification. The improvement is conditional on the accuracy of the bounds `l` and `u`. A more nuanced statement acknowledging this dependence is warranted.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in large-scale optimization: reducing computational cost while maintaining theoretical guarantees.
*   The proposed safe adaptive sampling scheme is novel and potentially impactful.
*   The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the benefits of adaptive sampling.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The numerous minor flaws (notational inconsistencies, ambiguous assumptions, incomplete proofs, limited empirical scope, formatting issues) significantly detract from the overall quality of the paper.
*   The lack of appendices renders the theoretical claims unverifiable.
*   The limited scope of empirical evaluation raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
*   The performance of SGD with the proposed approach is underwhelming, casting doubt on its widespread applicability.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a promising approach to adaptive importance sampling, the numerous flaws and limitations prevent me from recommending acceptance. The lack of complete proofs and the limited scope of the empirical evaluation are particularly concerning. The paper reads as incomplete and requires significant revision before it could be considered for publication at NIPS. The level of meticulousness expected for NIPS is simply not met here. I recommend **rejection**.
