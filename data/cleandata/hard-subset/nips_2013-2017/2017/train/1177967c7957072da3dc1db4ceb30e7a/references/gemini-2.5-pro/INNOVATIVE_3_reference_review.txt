### Summary
The paper addresses the challenge of making adaptive importance sampling practical for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. While optimal, gradient-based sampling can offer significant speedups over fixed or uniform sampling, it is computationally prohibitive as it requires full gradient information at each iteration. Existing approximations that use stale information or simple bounds can perform worse than non-adaptive methods and lack theoretical guarantees.

The authors' main contribution is a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme. The core idea is to work with efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. Instead of heuristically picking a value within these bounds, the authors formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. Specifically, they find the sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case expected progress, where the worst-case is taken over all possible gradients consistent with the known bounds. The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem and proves a crucial "safety" guarantee: the resulting sampling strategy is provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., Li-based or uniform sampling). The authors demonstrate how to maintain the required bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs) and provide empirical results showing significant performance improvements over non-adaptive methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development, moving from the full-information oracle (Section 2) to the proposed limited-information framework (Section 3), is logical and well-motivated. The derivation of the minimax problem in (7) is a clever way to handle the uncertainty in the gradient, and Theorem 3.2 provides the key "safety" guarantee that distinguishes this work from prior heuristics. The algorithm for solving this problem (Algorithm 4) is non-trivial and its O(n log n) complexity makes the approach practical. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against the appropriate baselines (fixed sampling and the full-information oracle) and demonstrating effectiveness on real-world datasets for both iteration complexity and wall-clock time. The authors are also honest about the limitations, for instance, noting the less pronounced gains for SGD in their experiments.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, and the key ideas are presented in a structured manner. The distinction between the ideal (but impractical) sampling and the proposed safe approximation is sharp and compelling. The inclusion of Figure 1 and the pseudo-code for all three compared algorithms effectively summarizes the core differences in the approaches.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is its strongest asset. The idea of using importance sampling that adapts to the optimizer's state is not new. However, previous attempts to make it computationally feasible often relied on heuristics (e.g., using stale gradients or only upper bounds) that could fail in practice and lacked guarantees. The central contribution here—framing the problem of choosing a sampling distribution under uncertainty as a minimax game—is a bold and highly novel conceptual leap. This provides a principled way to derive a robust sampling strategy, moving the field beyond ad-hoc approximations. The resulting safety guarantee is a significant theoretical advance that addresses a critical shortcoming of prior work.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant. Improving the efficiency of fundamental optimization algorithms like CD and SGD is of paramount importance to the machine learning community. This work makes adaptive importance sampling substantially more practical and trustworthy. By providing a method that is both efficient and guaranteed to not perform worse than standard methods, the authors lower the barrier to adoption for practitioners. While the specific bounding techniques discussed are for GLMs, the "safe sampling" framework itself is generic and could inspire a new line of research into designing optimizers that explicitly model and hedge against uncertainty in gradient information, potentially with applications in distributed optimization or for more complex models.

A minor weakness is that the bounding method for GLMs is relatively straightforward (based on Cauchy-Schwarz), and the performance gains are less dramatic for SGD than for CD. However, these points do not detract from the core conceptual contribution. The paper's main achievement is the framework itself; demonstrating its efficacy even with simple bounds is a powerful proof of concept.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **High Novelty:** The minimax formulation for robust adaptive sampling is a genuinely new and powerful idea that provides a principled solution to a well-known problem.
*   **Key Theoretical Guarantee:** The "safety" proof (provably better than fixed sampling) is a major contribution that addresses a critical flaw in prior approximate adaptive schemes.
*   **Practicality and Performance:** The proposed method is computationally efficient (O(n log n)) and the experiments show it delivers significant wall-clock time speedups, nearly matching the performance of the impractical oracle method.
*   **Potential for High Impact:** The work makes a powerful optimization technique safer and more accessible, and the core idea could influence the design of other adaptive optimization algorithms.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The empirical gains for SGD are modest compared to the strong results for CD.
*   The paper only explores a simple mechanism for maintaining gradient bounds; the method's utility for more complex models where such bounds might be looser or harder to compute remains an open question.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper. The central idea is bold, elegant, and novel. It solves a real, practical problem with a theoretically sound and empirically validated approach. The weaknesses are minor and represent interesting avenues for future work rather than flaws in the current contribution. This work introduces a significant new direction for adaptive optimization methods and is a clear accept.
