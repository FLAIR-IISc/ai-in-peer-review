## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for both Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The authors motivate the approach by highlighting the theoretical benefits of gradient-based sampling but acknowledging its practical limitations due to computational cost.  Their contribution is a practical, "safe" adaptive sampling strategy that provably performs at least as well as fixed importance sampling, and offers potential speedups, while being computationally efficient. The key idea is to formulate the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem, solvable in O(n log n) time, given safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient.  The paper provides theoretical analysis demonstrating performance guarantees and empirical validation on real-world datasets, showcasing improvements in both iteration count and runtime.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a practical problem:** Adaptive importance sampling is theoretically sound but often computationally prohibitive. This paper directly tackles this issue.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The work provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing bounds on performance relative to fixed importance sampling and the optimal gradient-based sampling.  The connection to existing literature on CD and SGD is well-established. The proof of safety (never worse than fixed sampling) is a significant strength.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm is computationally efficient (O(n log n) per iteration) and can be integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations with relative ease.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the core ideas are clearly explained.  The inclusion of pseudocode (Algorithms 1-4) is helpful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed scheme, showing improvements over standard sampling methods. The analysis on the bounds (vk) provides useful insights.
* **Connection to Bounds:** The authors clearly articulate how readily available bounds on gradients, or approximations thereof, can be used within their framework, making it practically applicable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Theoretical Analysis:** While thorough, some of the theoretical analysis (particularly in Section 2.1 and Lemma 2.4) is dense and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with the specific mathematical tools used. More intuitive explanations could be beneficial.
* **SGD Results Less Compelling:** The empirical results for SGD are less dramatic than those for CD.  The authors acknowledge this, but it raises the question of whether the method is as impactful in the SGD setting. More analysis of the reasons for this difference would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are solid, they focus primarily on GLMs. Expanding the evaluation to other types of machine learning models and datasets would strengthen the claims of generality.
* **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:**  The performance heavily relies on the quality of the safe gradient bounds (` and `u).  A more thorough discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm to the accuracy of these bounds would be useful. How much performance degradation occurs with looser bounds?
* **No Comparison to Other Adaptive Methods:** The paper primarily focuses on comparing against fixed sampling.  A comparison to *other* existing adaptive sampling techniques (beyond gradient-based) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.




**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound and presents a complete, well-supported contribution.  The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results are convincing. (Score: 8.5/10)
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally clear, although some sections are mathematically dense.  The organization is logical, and the pseudocode aids understanding. (Score: 7.5/10)
* **Originality:**  The approach of formulating the adaptive sampling problem as a convex optimization with safety constraints is novel and represents a significant contribution. (Score: 9/10)
* **Significance:**  The results are important as they bridge the gap between theoretically optimal adaptive sampling and practical implementation.  The method has the potential to be widely adopted in CD and SGD algorithms. (Score: 8/10)

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a significant practical challenge in adaptive optimization.
* Strong theoretical guarantees and practical algorithm.
* Demonstrated empirical improvements on real-world datasets.
* Clearly presented and well-motivated.

**Con:**

* Theoretical analysis can be challenging for some readers.
* SGD results are less compelling than CD results.
* Limited scope of experimental evaluation.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The authors have identified a practical problem, developed a theoretically sound and computationally efficient solution, and provided convincing empirical validation. Addressing the minor weaknesses listed above (e.g., providing more intuitive explanations of the theoretical analysis, exploring the sensitivity to gradient bounds, expanding the experimental evaluation, and comparing against more adaptive sampling methods) would further strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance contingent upon these revisions.
