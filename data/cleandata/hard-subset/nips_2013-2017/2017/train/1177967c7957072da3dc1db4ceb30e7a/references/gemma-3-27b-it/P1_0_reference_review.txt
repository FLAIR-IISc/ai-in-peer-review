## NIPS 2017 Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for both Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The authors demonstrate theoretically that adaptive sampling can offer a speedup over static sampling, particularly in CD, potentially reaching a factor of the dimension *n*. They propose a practical approximation to gradient-based sampling that is computationally efficient and provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling. The key contribution lies in formulating an optimization problem to determine the sampling distribution based on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients, guaranteeing performance even with limited information.  The authors back up their theoretical results with empirical validation on real-world datasets.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on a substantial body of work on importance sampling and adaptive sampling techniques for optimization. It correctly identifies the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and computational cost associated with existing methods like gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37] and duality gap-based sampling [5, 23].  The work distinguishes itself by focusing on a *safe* approximation that provides provable guarantees without requiring full gradient computation, addressing a critical limitation of previous approaches. It correctly positions itself within the landscape of randomized optimization techniques like SGD and CD [9, 11, 35, 27] and connects to theoretical foundations provided in [18, 19, 2].  The paper acknowledges and builds upon existing work on coordinate descent and its convergence properties [18, 19, 2, 34, 20].

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method, demonstrating improvements over fixed sampling both in terms of convergence rates and computational efficiency. The analysis for CD is particularly compelling, with the potential for significant speedups.
* **Practicality:**  The proposed algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient, avoiding the full gradient calculations required by earlier adaptive sampling methods. The method for deriving safe upper and lower bounds, and efficiently computing the sampling distribution (Algorithm 4) is a significant contribution.
* **Provable Guarantees:**  The approach provides a provable guarantee of being at least as good as fixed sampling, a crucial advantage over other adaptive methods. The analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemmas 3.5 & 3.6) provides further insights into performance.
* **Generality:** The method is applicable to both CD and SGD, expanding its potential impact.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed sampling strategy, supporting the theoretical claims.  The inclusion of experiments on both CD and SGD further strengthens the evaluation.
* **Clear Exposition:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear presentation of the theoretical results and the proposed algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Notation:** While generally clear, the notation is dense and can be challenging to follow at times, particularly for readers unfamiliar with the specific optimization concepts.
* **Limited Insight into Bound Quality:**  The paper mentions tracking safe upper and lower bounds but doesn’t thoroughly discuss the tightness of these bounds and how they influence the performance of the algorithm. The impact of inaccurate bounds on the competitive ratio (and therefore practical performance) isn't fully explored.
* **SGD Results Less Dramatic:** The empirical improvement of the proposed method over uniform sampling in the SGD experiments is less pronounced than in the CD experiments.  More analysis could be done to understand why this is the case.
* **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper lacks specific details regarding the implementation, such as specific software used or parameter tuning details (beyond λ).  This makes it difficult to fully assess the reproducibility of the results.
* **Discussion of Assumptions:** The discussion of the assumption that safe bounds are available (Section 4) could be more nuanced. What happens when the bounds are significantly loose? How sensitive is the algorithm to the quality of these bounds?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in large-scale optimization – the computational cost of adaptive sampling.
* The proposed solution is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and provides provable guarantees.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on real-world datasets.
* The work is well-positioned within the existing literature and offers a novel contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The notation is dense and might hinder understanding for some readers.
* The empirical gains in the SGD setting are less compelling than in CD.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the quality of the safe bounds and their impact on performance.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The authors have successfully developed a practical and theoretically sound adaptive importance sampling scheme that overcomes the limitations of previous approaches.  While there are some minor weaknesses regarding notation and the discussion of bound quality, the strengths of the paper far outweigh these concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider:

*   Simplifying the notation where possible.
*   Providing a more detailed discussion of the impact of bound quality on performance.
*   Expanding the analysis of the SGD experiments to understand the observed results.
*   Providing more details regarding the implementation and parameter tuning.
