Summary
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It starts from the “ideal” gradient-based sampling that maximizes a one-step upper-bound decrease but is impractical because it needs full gradient information at every iteration. The core contribution is a “safe” approximation: given per-coordinate lower/upper bounds on the unknown gradient magnitudes (or per-component gradient norms for SGD), choose the sampling distribution and stepsize by solving a minimax problem that optimizes the worst-case one-step progress over all gradients consistent with the bounds. The authors show:
- For CD, the ideal gradient-based sampling can be up to n times faster than fixed (Li-based) importance sampling.
- With only bounds `, u, their proposed distribution p̂ and stepsize α̂ = 1/v minimize a convex min-max objective and are provably never worse than any fixed Li-based sampling, and coincide with the ideal sampling when full information is available.
- They give an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting-based) to compute p̂ and α̂ and demonstrate how to maintain safe bounds cheaply for generalized linear models (GLMs).
- Experiments on several datasets show substantial gains for CD and modest gains for SGD, with low overhead.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on classical CD/SGD analyses and non-uniform/importance sampling schemes (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Zhao & Zhang), and on adaptive rules such as gradient-based sampling and duality gap-based sampling. The novelty is the safe minimax formulation that, given inexact gradient information, returns a provably optimal (best worst-case) distribution relative to those bounds, with a constructive O(n log n) algorithm. This differs from prior “stale” or upper-bound heuristics, which can be worse than uniform and lack a guarantee.

Quality
- Theory: The one-step expected decrease bound is standard and sound. Lemma 2.4 and the resulting “ideal” sampling are consistent with prior literature. The safe scheme (Theorem 3.2) gives two key guarantees: best worst-case over Ck and dominance over fixed Li-sampling. The min-max interchange appears to rely on Sion’s theorem; this is appropriate if the stated convexity/compactness conditions hold (they should, given ∆n and a box Ck). The constructive characterization (Theorem 3.4) and O(n log n) algorithm are clear and useful. The competitive ratio analysis further contextualizes performance. Convergence rates are claimed and deferred to the appendix; it would help to surface at least one main rate in the paper body, but the ingredients are standard (expected descent + convexity).
- Algorithms/complexity: Computing p̂ costs O(n log n). Maintaining safe bounds in GLMs is argued to be O(n) per iteration using Cauchy–Schwarz. This is the main practical caveat: per-iteration O(n) work can dominate CD when a single coordinate update is cheap (e.g., sparse data), and even more so when n ≫ d. The authors partly address this by noting that if computing a gradient coordinate costs Θ(d) with d = Ω(n), the overhead amortizes to O(log n). This assumption may not hold in many sparse, high-dimensional settings (e.g., text), where CD often scales due to O(nnz(a_i)) updates; touching all coordinates each step can be prohibitive. This tension deserves deeper discussion and perhaps variants that update bounds less frequently or maintain them incrementally for active sets.
- Empirics: The CD results are convincing: p̂ is close to the ideal sampling and clearly outperforms uniform/fixed sampling in both iterations and time. The SGD gains are small, which aligns with prior observations that component-wise variance reduction from importance sampling is often modest. However, the experimental comparison set is limited: there is no head-to-head with other adaptive schemes (e.g., duality gap-based sampling for CD, AdaSDCA-style adaptive probabilities, or stale-gradient approximations). Also, the timing experiments are not all on the same machine; this weakens time-based claims. It would help to provide overhead breakdowns (time for bound updates and sorting vs. compute), and an ablation on bound tightness/frequency of recomputation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear narrative from ideal sampling to safe approximation, theory, and application to GLMs. The proofs are largely in the appendix but are sufficiently sketched in the main text to understand the ideas. A few suggestions:
- State the exact conditions for the min-max interchange in Theorem 3.2 in the main text (e.g., invoke Sion explicitly).
- Clarify the data model and costs for CD: when are updates O(d) vs. O(nnz(a_i)), and what is the practical cost of O(n) bound updates on sparse data?
- Provide the SGD variant of Algorithm 2 explicitly.
- Clean up minor typos/formatting in algorithms and references.

Originality
The safe minimax formulation and its efficient solution under gradient bounds appear novel and nontrivial. The reduction to a tractable O(n log n) procedure and guarantees that strictly dominate fixed importance sampling differentiate this work from prior adaptive sampling methods based on stale or upper bounds.

Significance
For CD on convex smooth objectives, a practically computable adaptive sampler with provable advantages over fixed sampling is valuable. The technique is modular and could influence how practitioners design adaptive sampling when full gradient information is unavailable. The impact for SGD seems smaller (as acknowledged), but the framework still offers a principled alternative to heuristics. The main limitation to broader impact is the O(n) per-iteration maintenance of bounds in some settings; demonstrating scalable variants or more efficient bound maintenance would strengthen significance.

Pros
- Clear theoretical contributions; safe dominance guarantee over fixed sampling.
- Elegant formulation and efficient algorithm with constructive characterization.
- Good empirical gains for CD; close to ideal sampling.
- Generic framework applicable to CD and SGD, with concrete GLM instantiation.

Cons
- Maintaining safe bounds at O(n) per iteration can be heavy; practical scalability unclear when n is large and updates are sparse/cheap.
- Limited experimental baselines and some timing results on different hardware.
- Convergence rates are not highlighted in the main text.
- SGD benefits are modest; scope of applicability could be better delineated.

Questions for authors
- Can the safe bounds be updated less frequently (e.g., every T steps) or on subsets to reduce O(n) overhead while retaining guarantees?
- How do results change with tighter bounds (beyond Cauchy–Schwarz) or with data-dependent precomputation (e.g., approximate nearest-neighbor inner products)?
- Could the sorting step be maintained incrementally across iterations to reduce O(n log n) to near-linear expected time?
- Any comparisons to Gauss–Southwell or approximate steepest CD, and to AdaSDCA/optimality-violation sampling?

Recommendation: Weak Accept
The paper presents a principled and practically implementable adaptive sampling method with solid theory and promising results for CD. Addressing the scalability caveat of bound maintenance and expanding empirical comparisons would further strengthen the contribution.
