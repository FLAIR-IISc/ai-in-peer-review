This paper presents an adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, aiming to improve computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees compared to static sampling. The core idea revolves around approximating gradient-based sampling by utilizing safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients, formulated as a convex optimization problem solvable in O(n log n) time. The authors demonstrate theoretical improvements over fixed sampling in the CD setting, and present empirical validation on several datasets with generalized linear models.

While the paper addresses a relevant problem and proposes a seemingly well-motivated approach, a meticulous examination reveals a concerning number of flaws, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to substantial gaps in the theoretical justification and experimental evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** Adaptive importance sampling is a crucial topic for scaling machine learning algorithms to large datasets.
*   **Theoretical Motivation:** The paper builds on existing theoretical foundations of adaptive sampling and attempts to provide theoretical guarantees for the proposed method.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm offers a computationally tractable approximation to gradient-based sampling.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors provide empirical results demonstrating performance gains on real datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Imprecision:** The notation is inconsistent. For example, the definition of `[x]i` as `<x, ei>` clashes with later statements implying coordinate-wise Lipschitz continuity, where `∇if` should be a scalar, not a vector. Similarly, the statement regarding coordinate-wise L-smoothness and the subsequent definition of `l` and `L` are unclear and potentially incorrect given the established notation. There’s a lack of clarity distinguishing coordinate-wise Lipschitz continuity from standard Lipschitz continuity – this is critical for the stated theoretical guarantees.
*   **Theoretical Gaps:** The claim of a speedup of up to a factor of `n` in CD methods (Section 2.1) feels overstated and isn’t convincingly demonstrated. The derivation feels rushed and lacks rigor, particularly the jump from Lemma 2.1 to the discussion of αk. The connection between αk and the speedup isn't clearly articulated. While Lemma 2.4 is stated, its proof is deferred to the appendix which is inaccessible for review.
*   **Optimization Formulation & Solution:** The optimization problem (6) is well-motivated, but the discussion surrounding its solution (Theorem 3.4) is insufficient. Algorithm 4, while presented, lacks a clear justification for its correctness beyond referencing Theorem 3.4. The claim of O(n log n) complexity requires a more detailed explanation, especially concerning the inequality checks within the algorithm. Also, the statement about the algorithm being the *best possible* with respect to limited information needs stronger justification – it’s a bold claim.
*   **Experimental Limitations:** The empirical evaluation is inadequate. The datasets used are standard, but the experimental setup lacks detail. The choice of parameters (e.g., regularization parameter λ = 0.1) is arbitrary and not justified. More importantly, the comparison to baselines is insufficient. While the results show improvement over fixed sampling, a comparison to state-of-the-art adaptive sampling methods (beyond the idealized gradient-based sampling) is missing. The discussion of the SGD results is particularly weak, stating that the improvement is “slight” without a quantitative analysis. The time complexity analysis is hampered by conducting experiments on different machines.
*   **Presentation:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased or grammatically incorrect. Figures are poorly labeled and lack sufficient context. The discussion feels overly verbose and repetitive in certain sections.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a theoretically and practically important problem.
*   The proposed algorithm is novel and offers a computationally efficient approximation to gradient-based sampling.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous technical inaccuracies and inconsistencies undermine the credibility of the theoretical results.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the claims of significant performance improvements.
*   The presentation is lacking in clarity and rigor.
*   The overstatement of the theoretical speedup and the weak justification for the "best possible" claim are concerning.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the presented idea, the numerous flaws, particularly the lack of rigor in the theoretical analysis and the insufficient experimental validation, lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper requires substantial revisions to address the identified weaknesses before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. Specifically, the authors must: (1) Correct the notational inconsistencies and clarify the theoretical derivations, (2) Provide a complete and rigorous proof of all claims, including those deferred to the appendix, (3) Conduct a more comprehensive experimental evaluation with a detailed description of the experimental setup and a comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and (4) Revise the presentation to improve clarity and rigor.
