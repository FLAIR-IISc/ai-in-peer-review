- Problem: Proposes a safe, efficient adaptive importance sampling (AIS) scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and SGD using convex optimization over safe gradient bounds.  
- Novelty: Introduces a computationally tractable, “safe” AIS that provably improves over fixed sampling and recovers gradient-based sampling in full-information settings.  
- Theoretical contribution: Provides rigorous lemmas/theorems and insightful competitive-ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6).  
- Algorithmic efficiency: Presents an algorithm with O(n log n) time per iteration, comparable to evaluating one gradient component.  
- Practicality: Shows how to maintain safe upper/lower gradient bounds efficiently for GLMs, making the method applicable in practice.  
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments on multiple datasets and losses (squared hinge, logistic, square) demonstrate effectiveness; plots of vk show adaptive stepsize behavior.  
- Writing and organization: Clear, well-structured presentation with precise notation.  
- Relation to prior work: Properly situates contributions within uniform/fixed importance sampling, gradient-based/adaptive sampling, duality-gap sampling, and prior SGD work.  
- Strength — theoretical soundness: Strong proofs and guarantees supporting the proposed method.  
- Strength — practicality & safety: The safe-bounds approach is clever and practically motivated.  
- Strength — computational practicality: Addresses the high cost of prior adaptive methods with an efficient scheme.  
- Weakness — empirical scope: Experiments focus heavily on GLMs, limiting evidence of generalizability to other models or non-convex settings.  
- Weakness — bound tightness: Limited discussion and strategies for obtaining tighter gradient bounds beyond simple scalar-product methods.  
- Weakness — SGD results: Improvements for SGD are modest; more analysis is needed to explain the limited gains (e.g., step-size effects).  
- Weakness — algorithmic practicality gap: Algorithm 1 is theoretically optimal but impractical for large datasets; trade-offs between optimality and feasibility could be discussed more.  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, theoretically solid, practical, empirically validated, and significant for stochastic optimization.  
- Rejection arguments: Limited empirical diversity (GLMs) and less convincing SGD improvements may temper broad applicability.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong paper; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.  
- Reviewer confidence: 9/10.
