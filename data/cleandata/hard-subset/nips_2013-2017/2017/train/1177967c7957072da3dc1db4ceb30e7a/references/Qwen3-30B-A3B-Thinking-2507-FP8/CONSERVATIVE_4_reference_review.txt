

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors claim to provide an efficient approximation of gradient-based adaptive sampling that can be computed in every iteration while enjoying theoretical guarantees that it is provably better than fixed importance sampling. The key innovation is an optimization formulation to compute the sampling distribution using safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, which the authors claim is "provably never worse than static sampling."

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear proofs establishing that the proposed method is provably better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2).
- The paper provides a clear algorithmic formulation (Algorithm 4) for computing the sampling distribution efficiently in O(n log n) time.
- The empirical results demonstrate improvements over fixed sampling in coordinate descent experiments, which aligns with the theoretical claims.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate notation and mathematical formalism.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper suffers from several critical shortcomings that undermine its contribution and significance:

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison to state-of-the-art methods:** The paper compares primarily to uniform sampling and fixed importance sampling, but fails to benchmark against existing adaptive methods that have been proposed in the literature (e.g., [22, 23, 26, 36, 37]). For example, Zhao and Zhang [36] already proposed gradient-based sampling with theoretical guarantees, and the paper does not demonstrate how their "safe" method improves upon this prior work in either theory or practice. The claim that their method "recovers the gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting" is not sufficient to establish novelty.

2. **Underwhelming empirical results for SGD:** The paper acknowledges that "SGD with the optimal sampling performs only slightly better than uniform sampling," which directly contradicts the paper's central claim that adaptive sampling provides significant improvements. The authors do not adequately explain this discrepancy or provide a meaningful comparison to other adaptive SGD methods.

3. **Exaggerated claims of novelty:** The paper claims to be "the first to develop adaptive strategies that can efficiently be computed in every iteration and that come with theoretical guarantees," but this is contradicted by prior work such as [22, 23, 36, 37] which already proposed gradient-based adaptive sampling methods with theoretical guarantees. The paper's key contribution appears to be the "safe" aspect (using bounds instead of full gradients), but even this is not entirely novel as [36, 23] discuss similar approaches.

4. **Insufficient validation of computational overhead:** While the paper claims the overhead is "O(n log n) per iteration," it fails to demonstrate that this overhead is negligible compared to the cost of gradient evaluation. For many problems, computing a single gradient component takes O(d) time (where d is the feature dimension), and when d = Ω(n), the overhead becomes O(log n) per iteration. However, the paper does not show that this overhead is small enough to be worth the theoretical gains in practice.

5. **Lack of thorough analysis of the competitive ratio:** The paper introduces a competitive ratio (ρk) but does not provide meaningful bounds on it for practical scenarios. Lemma 3.6 requires stringent conditions ([Ck]i ∩ γ[Ck]i = ∅) that are unlikely to hold in practice, and the paper does not provide empirical evidence that the competitive ratio is actually favorable in real-world settings.

**Significance concerns:**
The paper's contribution is incremental at best. The "safe" aspect is not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant publication at NIPS, especially when the empirical results do not convincingly demonstrate significant advantages over existing methods. The paper's claim that "adaptive importance sampling significantly outperforms fixed importance sampling" is supported only by comparisons to fixed sampling, not to other adaptive approaches that are commonly used in practice.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a theoretically sound method with a clear algorithmic formulation, it fails to adequately demonstrate the significance of the contribution relative to existing work. The empirical results are insufficient to justify the claim that this method provides meaningful advantages over state-of-the-art adaptive sampling approaches. The paper would need to substantially strengthen its empirical validation by comparing against relevant adaptive methods from the literature and providing clearer evidence of practical advantages.

Given the skeptical stance of this reviewer toward new methods without extensive validation, I cannot recommend acceptance. The paper represents a reasonable incremental improvement to the field but falls short of the high bar for NIPS in terms of significance and empirical validation. The theoretical contribution is sound but not sufficiently novel or impactful to warrant publication at NIPS.
