This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD), aiming to improve computational efficiency over fixed sampling methods. The core idea is to formulate the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem, leveraging safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to ensure provable performance guarantees. The authors claim their method is provably better than uniform/fixed sampling, efficiently computable, and recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, presenting a logical progression from motivation to proposed method to theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The authors correctly identify the drawbacks of existing adaptive strategies – computational cost and theoretical guarantees – and attempt to address them. The connection to established methods like coordinate descent and SGD is clearly articulated. However, despite the clear presentation, I find the contributions, while technically sound, lack the necessary novelty and practical impact to warrant acceptance to NIPS.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide theoretical justification for their approach, demonstrating improvements over fixed sampling under certain conditions. The analysis of the convergence rates and the derivation of the optimal sampling distribution are commendable.
*   **Safety Guarantee:** The emphasis on "safe" sampling, ensuring performance is never worse than fixed sampling, is a valuable contribution. This addresses a critical concern with adaptive methods.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper addresses the computational feasibility of the proposed method, providing an algorithm for efficient computation of the sampling distribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on GLMs demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over fixed sampling in both iterations and runtime.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Advance:** While the proposed method offers improvements over existing techniques, the advancement feels incremental rather than transformative. The core idea of leveraging bounds on gradients for adaptive sampling isn’t entirely novel; previous work (cited, but not sufficiently contextualized in terms of their limitations) has explored similar concepts.
*   **Strong Assumptions:** The method relies on the availability of "safe" upper and lower bounds on the gradient, which, while achievable in some cases (as demonstrated with GLMs), may not be easily obtainable or maintainable for more complex models or datasets. The discussion of these limitations is somewhat superficial.
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation focuses primarily on GLMs with relatively simple datasets. While the results are positive, they don't provide strong evidence of generalizability to more challenging machine learning tasks (e.g., deep learning, non-convex optimization). The relatively small performance gain in SGD experiments is concerning.
*   **Overly Optimistic Framing:** The authors frequently state "provably better," which can be misleading. The theoretical improvements are often contingent on specific conditions (e.g., accurate gradient bounds, coordinate-wise Lipschitz continuity) that may not hold in practice. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of these limitations.
*   **Related Work:** The related work section, whilst extensive, lacks critical evaluation. The authors do not sufficiently differentiate their contribution from prior work on adaptive importance sampling, particularly the variations based on gradient estimation. A more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between different approaches is needed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and well-analyzed approach to adaptive importance sampling.
*   The safety guarantee is a valuable contribution that addresses a key concern with adaptive methods.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on relevant benchmark tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental and lacks significant novelty.
*   The method relies on strong assumptions that may limit its applicability.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and doesn't provide strong evidence of generalizability.
*   The framing of the results is overly optimistic, and the discussion of limitations is superficial.
*   The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from existing work in the field.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution, but ultimately falls short of the bar for acceptance to NIPS. While the technical aspects are well-executed, the lack of significant novelty, the reliance on strong assumptions, and the limited scope of the empirical evaluation outweigh the strengths. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should consider further expanding the empirical evaluation to more challenging tasks and providing a more comprehensive comparison with existing adaptive sampling methods. A more honest acknowledgement of the limitations of the method, especially regarding the practical difficulties of obtaining and maintaining accurate gradient bounds, would also be beneficial.
