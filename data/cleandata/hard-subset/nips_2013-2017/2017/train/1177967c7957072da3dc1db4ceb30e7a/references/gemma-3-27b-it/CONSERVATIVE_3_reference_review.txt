This paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, aiming to balance the benefits of adaptive sampling with computational tractability and theoretical guarantees. The core idea is to formulate the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem constrained by available gradient bounds, rather than relying on full gradient information. 

The paper is well-structured and the motivation is clear. Adaptive importance sampling is a valuable technique, and the authors correctly identify the challenges related to computational cost and theoretical guarantees. The connection to existing work in coordinate descent and SGD is well established, referencing key papers in the field. The theoretical analysis, while dense, appears sound and the proposed algorithm is presented with sufficient detail. 

However, despite a solid presentation, I find the contributions incremental and the empirical evaluation, while extensive, doesn’t fully justify the theoretical complexity. My main concerns revolve around the practicality and significance of the proposed approach. 

**Strengths:**

* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous analysis, demonstrating theoretical improvements over fixed sampling strategies, particularly for coordinate descent. The derivation of the bounds and the optimization formulation are well-executed.
* **Practicality Focus:** The “safe” aspect, utilizing gradient bounds instead of full gradient computation, is a reasonable approach to address the computational challenges of adaptive sampling.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the key ideas accessible.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:**  The empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and loss functions provides a reasonable assessment of the method’s performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Contribution:** While technically sound, the core idea of using bounds to approximate gradient-based sampling isn’t entirely novel. Similar ideas have been explored previously (though perhaps not with the same level of theoretical rigor). The added value of the proposed approach feels limited.
* **Overly Conservative Bounds:** The reliance on potentially loose gradient bounds (as illustrated in the examples where the proposed sampling isn’t significantly better than uniform sampling) diminishes the potential gains. The performance is heavily dependent on the quality of these bounds, and the paper doesn’t fully address how to obtain tight bounds in practical settings beyond the GLM example.
* **Limited Performance Gains in SGD:** The empirical results show only marginal improvements in the SGD setting, which weakens the overall impact of the work. The authors themselves acknowledge this.  Why does the method perform so much worse in the SGD setting?
* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors claim the overhead is reasonable, the O(n log n) complexity for computing the sampling distribution in each iteration, while potentially acceptable for coordinate descent with a large ‘d’ (dimension), remains a concern, particularly for very large ‘n’ (number of data points).
* **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While comparisons to uniform sampling and optimal sampling are made, there is a lack of comparison to other, simpler adaptive sampling schemes, such as those based on moving averages of gradient norms.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically motivated approach to adaptive importance sampling.
* The analysis provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost.
* The extensive empirical evaluation demonstrates the method’s effectiveness in certain scenarios.

**Con:**

* The contribution is incremental and doesn’t represent a major breakthrough in the field.
* The performance gains, particularly in the SGD setting, are modest.
* The reliance on gradient bounds introduces a potential bottleneck and limits the achievable performance.
* The implementation complexity may be a barrier to adoption.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the paper is technically competent and presents a reasonable approach to a relevant problem, the incremental nature of the contribution and the limited practical gains prevent me from recommending a strong acceptance. The theoretical analysis is valuable, but the empirical results don’t fully justify the increased complexity. I believe the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and a more compelling demonstration of its practical impact.  Further work on obtaining tighter gradient bounds and exploring alternative approximations could significantly enhance the value of this research. The paper feels like a good stepping stone toward something more impactful, but isn’t quite there yet.
