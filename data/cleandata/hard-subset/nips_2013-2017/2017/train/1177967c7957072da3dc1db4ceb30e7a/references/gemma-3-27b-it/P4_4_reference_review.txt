This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling by leveraging safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient, avoiding the computationally expensive full gradient calculation. The authors theoretically establish advantages over uniform sampling and provide an efficient algorithm for computing the sampling distribution. Empirical results on GLMs demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation, proposed approach, and experimental setup. It builds upon existing work in adaptive importance sampling, specifically addressing the computational drawbacks of previous methods. The theoretical analysis providing guarantees on the performance compared to uniform sampling is a significant strength. The inclusion of an efficient algorithm for computing the sampling distribution is also valuable. The empirical evaluation, while limited in scope (focusing on GLMs), provides supporting evidence for the claims made. 

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. The notation is dense and could benefit from more explanation. While the authors provide a good overview of related work, they could more explicitly differentiate their contributions from existing adaptive sampling techniques beyond just computational efficiency.  The assumption of having safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient is a critical one, and the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of its limitations and practical considerations. While the authors demonstrate how to compute these bounds for GLMs, it’s unclear how easily transferable this approach is to other models.  The experimental evaluation could be broadened to include more complex datasets and models to demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method. The improvement in SGD, as reported in the results, is relatively modest, requiring further investigation into the interplay between adaptive sampling and the stochastic nature of SGD. Finally, the writing, while clear, sometimes lacks conciseness. For example, the lengthy discussion of the theoretical gains in the CD case (Section 2) feels somewhat redundant, as the main contribution lies in the practical approximation presented later.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical results demonstrating the advantage of the proposed adaptive sampling scheme over uniform sampling.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm allows for efficient computation of the sampling distribution, making it practical for large-scale optimization problems.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the key ideas in a clear and understandable manner.
*   **Relevance:** The problem of efficiently handling large datasets is highly relevant to the NIPS community.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notation:** The notation is dense and could be more accessible.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation focuses primarily on GLMs and could be expanded to other models and datasets.
*   **Modest SGD Improvement:** The performance gains observed in SGD are relatively small.
*   **Assumption of Safe Bounds:** The dependence on safe upper and lower bounds requires careful consideration and further discussion of its practicality.
*   **Redundancy:** Some sections, like the detailed CD theoretical analysis, feel somewhat redundant.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant problem in large-scale optimization.
*   The theoretical contributions are significant and provide valuable insights into the benefits of adaptive sampling.
*   The proposed algorithm is computationally efficient and offers a practical solution for approximating gradient-based sampling.
*   The empirical results provide supporting evidence for the claims made.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited scope of the experimental evaluation may not fully demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method.
*   The modest improvement in SGD performance raises concerns about the practical impact of the method in certain settings.
*   The assumption of having safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient may limit the applicability of the method.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The theoretical guarantees and efficient algorithm make it a promising approach for accelerating optimization algorithms. However, the limited scope of the experimental evaluation and the assumption of safe gradient bounds are potential concerns.  Given the strengths and weaknesses, I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the weaknesses by expanding the experimental evaluation, providing a more detailed discussion of the safe gradient bound assumption, and streamlining the presentation.
