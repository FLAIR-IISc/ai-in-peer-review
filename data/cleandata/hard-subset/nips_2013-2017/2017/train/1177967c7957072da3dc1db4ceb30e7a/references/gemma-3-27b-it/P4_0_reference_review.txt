This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling, offering theoretical guarantees of improvement over uniform/fixed importance sampling while remaining computationally tractable. The authors present a novel optimization formulation to determine sampling probabilities based on available gradient bounds, demonstrating its practicality and benefits through theoretical analysis and empirical validation.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. It builds upon existing work in adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 5, 23]) by addressing the computational cost and theoretical limitations of prior approaches. The introduction effectively highlights the trade-offs between efficiency, theoretical guarantees, and computational feasibility. The connections to established methods like CD and SGD are clearly articulated. The proposed method is presented in a logical manner, with a clear separation of theoretical development and experimental evaluation. The notation is standard and consistent. The related work section is comprehensive, citing relevant publications in the field.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed scheme, demonstrating its improvement over static sampling via Lemma 2.1 and Theorem 3.2. The analysis of the optimal sampling distribution and the derivation of bounds on its performance are rigorous.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm offers a practical solution for adaptive sampling, addressing the computational challenges of previous methods.  The analysis of computational complexity (Section 4) and the development of Algorithm 4 are significant contributions.
*   **Safety Guarantee:** The concept of using safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients ensures that the adaptive sampling strategy is never worse than fixed sampling, providing a valuable practical advantage.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showcasing its performance on GLMs with both L1 and L2 regularization.  The comparative analysis with uniform sampling and the optimal sampling method is insightful.
*   **Generality:**  The scheme's applicability to both CD and SGD enhances its broad relevance and impact.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The method relies on the availability of safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient. While the authors provide examples for GLMs, the generality and ease of obtaining such bounds for more complex models could be a limitation. The discussion on this point is somewhat brief and further investigation of how to obtain such bounds is required.
*   **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** While Lemma 3.5 and 3.6 provide bounds on the competitive ratio, these bounds might be loose in practice.  A more refined analysis of the competitive ratio, considering specific problem structures, could be valuable.
*   **SGD Results:** The empirical results for SGD show only a slight improvement over uniform sampling. This disparity between the theoretical benefits and the observed performance requires further investigation. The authors acknowledge this, but a deeper analysis of the underlying reasons would be beneficial.
*   **Clarity of Algorithm 4:** While Algorithm 4 is well-defined, the explanation of its steps could be more detailed, potentially including a small example to illustrate the process.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling.
*   The proposed method addresses a significant challenge in large-scale optimization: balancing efficiency, theoretical guarantees, and computational feasibility.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the scheme on real datasets.
*   The work has the potential to impact a wide range of machine learning applications that rely on CD and SGD.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on safe gradient bounds might limit the applicability of the method in certain scenarios.
*   The modest improvement observed in the SGD experiments raises questions about the practical benefits in some settings.
*   The analysis of the competitive ratio could be further refined.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed algorithm is efficient, and the empirical results are promising. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. The work is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance to NIPS.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised in the weaknesses section, particularly regarding the discussion of gradient bounds and the analysis of the SGD results.
