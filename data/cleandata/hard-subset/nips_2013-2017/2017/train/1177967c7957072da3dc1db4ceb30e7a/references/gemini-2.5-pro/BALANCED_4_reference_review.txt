### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of designing efficient and theoretically sound adaptive importance sampling strategies for randomized optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). While existing adaptive methods can outperform static sampling, they often face a dilemma: either they are computationally too expensive (e.g., requiring a full gradient computation at each step) or their approximations lack guarantees and can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors propose a "Safe Adaptive Importance sampling" scheme to overcome this. The core idea is to maintain computationally cheap lower and upper bounds on the gradient information (magnitudes for CD, norms for SGD). Instead of using a point estimate from these bounds, the authors formulate a minimax problem: find the sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case expected progress, where the worst-case is over all possible gradients consistent with the current bounds. The key contributions are:
1.  A theoretical analysis of gradient-based sampling for CD with a directionally-unbiased stepsize, showing a potential speedup of up to factor `n`.
2.  The novel minimax formulation for deriving a "safe" sampling distribution, which is proven to be always at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling.
3.  An efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this minimax problem at each iteration.
4.  A demonstration of how to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs), with experimental validation showing significant improvements in both iteration count and wall-clock time over fixed sampling schemes.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem (Eq. 7) given uncertain gradient information—is highly novel and elegant. It provides a principled way to handle the trade-off between using outdated/approximate information and computational cost. The resulting "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2), which proves the method is never worse than standard Lipschitz-based sampling, is a significant theoretical advance over prior heuristic adaptive methods and makes the proposed scheme much more reliable for practical use. This work is a solid contribution to the active and important area of accelerating large-scale optimization.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the optimal sampling distribution from the minimax problem is rigorous, and the proposed O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) for its computation is efficient and well-analyzed. The application to GLMs in Section 4 demonstrates the practical viability of the approach. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using multiple real-world datasets, different loss functions, and both L1/L2 regularization. The inclusion of wall-clock time comparisons is crucial and convincingly shows that the method is not just better in theory but also faster in practice.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It effectively motivates the problem by first presenting the ideal "full information" case and then transitioning to the practical "limited information" setting. The inclusion of concise algorithm boxes (Algs. 1-3) helps clarify the differences between the methods. The authors are also honest about the limitations observed in the SGD experiments, which strengthens the credibility of the work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Clarity on Bound Maintenance:** The description of how the safe bounds `l` and `u` are maintained (Section 4) is overly brief. For the method to be reproducible, more detail is needed. For instance, in the CD case, the update relies on bounding `|〈ai,aik〉|`. The paper suggests using Cauchy-Schwarz, but this can be very loose. A more detailed pseudo-code or explicit update equations for `l` and `u` from step `k` to `k+1` would be very helpful. It would also be useful to discuss the effect of bound quality: how do the bounds evolve over time? Do they become progressively looser, and if so, is a periodic reset (e.g., by a full gradient computation) ever necessary? The experiments start with `l0=0, u0=∞`, and seem to work well without a reset, which is encouraging but warrants a brief discussion.
*   **Discussion of SGD Results:** The authors correctly note that the empirical gains for SGD are much less pronounced than for CD. This is an interesting finding. The paper would be strengthened by a short discussion on potential reasons for this discrepancy. Is it because the variance inherent in data sub-sampling for SGD is the dominant factor, making the choice of component `i` less critical? Or are the gradient norm bounds derived for the SGD case (Lemma 4.1) inherently looser than their CD counterparts? Providing some intuition here would add valuable insight.
*   **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The analysis of the competitive ratio `ρ_k` in Section 3.2 is a nice addition, but its practical implications could be clearer. Lemma 3.5 depends on solving a non-convex problem for `w_k`, and Lemma 3.6 relies on a strong assumption on the tightness of the bounds. It would be beneficial to connect this theoretical analysis back to the practical bounding strategy from Section 4. For example, can anything be said about the likely value of `γ` in Lemma 3.6 when using the Cauchy-Schwarz bounding approach?
*   **Minor Point on Novelty:** The paper introduces a stepsize rule `γk = αk[pk]−1ik` for CD and presents it as a proposal of "this work". While this is less common in the classical CD literature, similar "unbiased" update schemes are standard in the broader stochastic optimization literature (e.g., in SDCA). The claim of novelty might be slightly overstated; rephrasing to acknowledge its roots while highlighting its importance to the paper's specific framework would be more precise.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea of a "safe" adaptive sampling scheme via a minimax formulation is a novel, significant, and principled contribution.
*   The method is supported by solid theoretical guarantees, including a proof that it cannot perform worse than standard fixed importance sampling.
*   An efficient O(n log n) algorithm is provided to implement the scheme.
*   The experimental results, especially for Coordinate Descent, are strong and demonstrate clear practical benefits in both iteration complexity and wall-clock time.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The description of how the gradient bounds are practically maintained is sparse, which impacts the clarity and reproducibility of a key component of the method.
*   The empirical gains for SGD are modest, and this difference compared to the CD results is not explored.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong core contribution. The proposed "safe" adaptive sampling method is novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective. It elegantly addresses a key shortcoming of previous adaptive sampling schemes. While there are minor weaknesses, primarily related to the level of detail in the description of the bound updates, these are addressable and do not detract from the significance of the main results. The paper represents a clear step forward for adaptive methods in large-scale optimization. I recommend acceptance.
