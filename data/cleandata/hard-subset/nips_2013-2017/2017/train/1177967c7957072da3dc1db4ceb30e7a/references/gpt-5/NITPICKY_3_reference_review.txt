Summary
The paper proposes an adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that is “safe” in the sense of never being worse (in a precise bound sense) than fixed importance sampling based on L_i. The key idea is a per-iteration min–max problem over probability vectors p and unknown gradient magnitudes c constrained to lie in a box [ℓ,u], derived from cheap-to-maintain safe lower/upper bounds on gradient entries (CD) or component gradient norms (SGD). The optimal sampling p̂ solves min_p max_c V(p,c)/||c||^2 with V(p,c)=∑ L_i c_i^2/p_i. The authors prove p̂ achieves a worst-case value v_k ∈ [L_min, Tr(L)], guaranteeing V(p̂,c) ≤ Tr(L)||c||^2 for all c in the box, hence never worse than fixed Li-based sampling. They provide an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting-based thresholding) to compute p̂, and show that when full gradients are available, the method recovers classical gradient-based optimal sampling. They argue how to maintain safe bounds ℓ,u efficiently for GLMs using simple Cauchy–Schwarz estimates and present empirical results showing substantial gains over fixed sampling for CD, and modest gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to non-uniform/importance sampling for CD/SGD (Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.; Zhao & Zhang; Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.) and screening/safe rules (Liu et al.; Ndiaye et al.). The min–max “robust” formulation under interval uncertainty appears novel in this context. However, the paper does not empirically compare against recent adaptive-probability CD methods (e.g., Csiba–Qu–Richtárik 2015; Perekrestenko et al. 2017), nor against Gauss–Southwell and approximate steepest CD (Nutini et al.; Stich et al. 2017), which also leverage gradient information adaptively.

Strengths
- Clean and principled min–max formulation with clear safety guarantee: p̂ is optimal in the worst case over the uncertainty set and provably never worse than fixed Li-importance sampling.
- Constructive O(n log n) algorithm for computing p̂ with a simple geometric interpretation.
- Unifies the picture: recovers gradient-based optimal sampling when full information is available.
- Empirical gains in CD on several text datasets; ablation on step-size shows sensible behavior.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and completeness
- Nonsmooth objectives: Much of the CD analysis assumes coordinate-wise L-smoothness and uses ∇_i f and step x_{k+1}=x_k−γ_k ∇_{i_k}f e_{i_k}. Yet experiments include L1 regularization where ∇ f does not exist; standard approaches use proximal CD (soft-thresholding). The paper does not present a proximal extension of the analysis/algorithm (expected-progress bound, stepsize α_k, min–max formulation with subgradients or composite model). This is a major gap given the experiments.
- Squared hinge loss is not twice differentiable; Lemma 4.1 assumes twice differentiable h_i to derive the bound update for SGD. The handling of kinks/non-smooth points is not addressed.
- SGD theory is underspecified: the safe min–max formulation is stated to “also arise,” but no explicit theorem analogous to Theorem 3.2 (safety guarantee vs fixed) is provided for SGD, nor convergence rates. Claims rely on citation rather than carrying through the safe-bound setting.
- Stepsizes: The proposed γ_k=α_k/p_{k,i} with α_k=1/v_k is chosen from the worst-case bound. Guarantees are only in expectation and via the model (1). There is no discussion of stability or monotonicity, nor of the effect of loose ℓ,u on conservativeness of α_k. For compositional/nonsmooth cases this is more delicate.

Algorithmic/complexity issues
- Per-iteration overhead O(n log n) to compute p̂ plus O(n) to update bounds can dominate CD’s O(nnz of selected coordinate), especially on very sparse high-dimensional problems where per-iteration cost is tiny. The paper argues overhead is acceptable if d=Ω(n), but this is dataset-dependent; many practical regimes have d ≪ n or vice versa. No systematic timing breakdown is provided; Figure captions mention different machines for different figures, making timing evidence weak.
- Memory overhead for maintaining ℓ,u ∈ R^n is nontrivial for million-scale n; not discussed.
- The approach assumes known L_i. For GLMs this is feasible for smooth losses, but for L1 regularization L_i is undefined; a proximal analogue is needed. For general h, how L_i is computed or bounded is not detailed.

Empirical evaluation
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to strong adaptive baselines (Gauss–Southwell/approximate GS, Csiba et al. adaptive SDCA probabilities, Perekrestenko et al.), and to stale-gradient or upper-bound-based approximations used in practice.
- Reproducibility: No code, limited implementation details, and timing experiments not run on the same machine. Choice of λ=0.1 for all datasets without tuning may bias difficulty. Subsampling variants (rcv1’, real-sim’, news20’) may distort sparsity structure and cache behavior.
- SGD results show small gains; the paper acknowledges this but does not analyze when the method helps/hurts, nor provides theory explaining the gap vs CD.

Presentation and clarity
- Numerous typos and notation issues hinder readability: e.g., “vector l = (L1, . . . , n)>”, “2.4” in Eq (8) likely refers to Lemma 2.4; overloading symbols (u used as both vector and integer index in Algorithm 4; backtick ` as both variable and index); inconsistent bold/hat/tilde notation; “st Conference…” header artifact; “inadmissible” misused; punctuation/grammar throughout.
- Algorithm 4 pseudocode is hard to follow: u, ` both redefined; ct implicit; index bookkeeping unclear. A careful, unambiguous description is needed.
- Some claims are only stated with “proofs in appendix,” but the main paper should include enough to assess correctness (e.g., precise conditions to invoke Sion’s minimax theorem, convex–concave properties, domain compactness).

Pros
- Conceptually neat robust-optimization view of adaptive sampling under uncertainty.
- Safety guarantee relative to fixed sampling is appealing and novel.
- Constructive algorithm with interpretable solution structure.
- Strong improvements for CD on tested datasets.

Cons
- Theoretical framework does not cover nonsmooth/proximal settings used in experiments.
- Heavy per-iteration overhead and memory not convincingly justified empirically.
- Missing key baselines; timing comparisons on different hardware.
- SGD part underdeveloped theoretically and empirically.
- Many presentation/notation/formatting issues.

Questions for the authors
1. How does your theory and algorithm extend to proximal CD for composite objectives (e.g., L1)? Can you provide explicit expected progress bounds and a proximal analogue of V(p,c)?
2. For squared hinge and L1, how do you ensure the assumptions of Lemma 4.1 and Section 2 hold?
3. Can you provide per-iteration timing breakdowns (bound update, sorting, sampling, gradient, update) on the same machine, and experiments where nnz per coordinate is small?
4. How do you compute/estimate L_i in your experiments with L1 regularization?
5. Can you compare against Gauss–Southwell and approximate steepest CD, and adaptive probability methods (Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.)?
6. What is the memory footprint for ℓ,u on the largest dataset, and how often do you recompute/sort? Any amortization strategies?

Recommendation
While the core idea is interesting and potentially useful, the current submission has significant theoretical gaps (nonsmooth/proximal), incomplete empirical validation, and many clarity/formatting issues. I lean to reject in its current form. With a proximal extension, stronger baselines/timings, and improved presentation, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
