- Paper studies adaptive importance sampling for CD and SGD, showing full-information gradient-based sampling maximizes one-step expected decrease under a smooth quadratic model.
- Main contribution: a “safe” adaptive sampling via a min–max problem using per-iteration gradient interval bounds that never worsens fixed importance sampling.
- The safe sampler matches the full-information optimal when bounds are tight and can be solved in O(n log n) via a thresholding/sorting algorithm (Algorithm 4).
- The authors show how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for GLMs and provide experiments with substantial CD gains and smaller SGD improvements.
- Builds on prior non-uniform/importance sampling and adaptive strategies, but novelty lies in the interval-based min–max safety guarantee and efficient exact solution.
- One-step expected improvement analysis and derived optimal p? are correct under the smooth quadratic assumption.
- The “safety” guarantee (Theorem 3.2) and geometric view with the O(n log n) algorithm (Theorem 3.4) are elegant and useful.
- Comparison to hindsight-optimal distributions is informative, though competitive ratio bounds can be loose and rely on relative accuracy conditions.
- Some technical details (justification for swapping min/max, convergence rates for Algorithm 2) should be moved to or summarized in the main text for clarity.
- Empirically, CD benefits are consistent and track full-information sampling closely; SGD gains are modest and problem-dependent.
- Presentation improvements suggested: state minimax theorem assumptions, convergence rate for Algorithm 2, and clarify initialization with u0 = ∞ and Algorithm 4 behavior.
- Need clearer discussion of computational model: per-iteration O(n) bound updates and O(n log n) sampling may dominate in sparse CD regimes; suggest amortization and lazy updates.
- The safe min–max formulation and exact efficient solution appear novel compared to heuristics using stale gradients or loose bounds.
- For CD the contribution is practically and theoretically significant; for SGD the impact seems limited but potentially useful in heterogeneous settings.
- Strengths: principled min–max formulation, strong guarantees, efficient algorithm, GLM bound maintenance, and validated CD empirical gains.
- Weaknesses: per-iteration overhead may hinder scalability; more discussion and ablations on recomputation frequency and sparsity exploitation needed.
- Experiments lack comparisons to Gauss–Southwell/approximate steepest CD and gap-based/adaptive-probability CD baselines.
- SGD experiments should be tempered or extended to tasks with highly skewed component gradients and mini-batching comparisons.
- Need explicit statements that Ck is a compact box and V(p,c)/‖c‖2 convex in p, concave in c for the min–max swap validity.
- Clarify Algorithm 4 handling of ties, infinite bounds, numerical stability in computing m and norms.
- Minor: fix typos/formatting issues and missing footnote markers.
- Pro/Con summary: novel, theory-backed, and practically effective for CD but questions remain on scalability, SGD impact, and missing baseline comparisons.
- Recommendation: Weak accept — solid conceptual contribution and CD improvements justify acceptance, but address scalability and comparisons to strengthen the paper.
