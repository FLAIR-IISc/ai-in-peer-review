Summary
The paper proposes an adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and SGD that aims to approximate gradient-based sampling without computing full gradients each iteration. The central idea is to maintain safe lower and upper bounds on per-coordinate gradient magnitudes (for CD) or per-component gradient norms (for SGD) and to compute a sampling distribution p̂k by solving a min–max problem that minimizes a quadratic upper-bound model of expected decrease under all gradients consistent with the bounds. The authors show that: (i) in a full-information setting, gradient-based sampling can be up to n times better than fixed importance sampling; (ii) with only safe bounds available, the proposed p̂k is optimal for the worst case over the uncertainty set and is guaranteed not to be worse than fixed (Li-based) sampling under the model; (iii) p̂k can be computed in O(n log n) time by a sorting-based algorithm; and (iv) for GLMs, simple updates based on Cauchy–Schwarz yield safe bounds updatable in O(n) time per iteration. Experiments on several text datasets show notable gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work: The paper is positioned among non-uniform/importance sampling for CD/SGD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.; Csiba et al.; Zhao & Zhang; Perekrestenko et al.) and Gauss–Southwell/steepest-coordinate methods (Nutini et al.; Stich et al.). The novelty is the “safe” min–max formulation that, given bounds, yields a distribution guaranteed to be no worse than fixed importance sampling under the same quadratic model, and an efficient algorithm to compute it.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: full gradient-based sampling is often impractical; safe approximations with guarantees are desirable.
- The min–max formulation is well-motivated, and the geometric characterization plus the O(n log n) algorithm are elegant and likely of independent interest.
- Theoretical properties: p̂k is optimal for the worst-case c in the uncertainty set; guarantee V(p̂, c) ≤ Tr[L]‖c‖2 holds, implying “never worse than fixed Li-based” under the model.
- Empirical results indicate substantial improvements for CD on GLMs, with small overhead.
- The paper is generally well-written and situates itself among relevant adaptive sampling literature.

Weaknesses
- Practical scalability of maintaining safe bounds: The approach requires O(n) updates of bounds plus O(n log n) sampling computation every iteration. Classical CD leverages O(1) (plus cost of a single coordinate gradient) complexity per step; adding O(n) overhead per step can be prohibitive in truly large-n settings. The argument that when d = Ω(n) the overhead is “only” O(log n) relative to computing one gradient coordinate is not convincing for common sparse settings where nnz per column is small and d and n can be very different.
- Guarantees are on a surrogate model, not on actual function decrease. The “provably better than fixed” claim holds for the quadratic upper bound (and in expectation), but the paper does not provide end-to-end convergence rates demonstrating uniformly better global complexity than fixed sampling under standard assumptions. The full-information section shows potential n-fold gains, but for the safe scheme there is no analogous complexity theorem beyond worst-case constants (vk ∈ [Lmin, Tr L]).
- Handling of nonsmooth regularizers in CD is unclear. The CD update (2) uses gradients, yet several experiments include L1 regularization. It is not clear whether a proximal coordinate step is used and how the analysis extends when ψi are nonsmooth. The safe-bound tracking (11) also implicitly assumes differentiability; please clarify.
- Bound quality is very loose with simple Cauchy–Schwarz estimates. The method relies critically on the informativeness of bounds. The experiments suggest benefits even with loose bounds, but there is no systematic ablation on bound tightness or on more sophisticated bound maintenance methods. The competitive ratio bounds are mostly worst-case and offer limited practical guidance.
- Empirical scope and baselines are limited. Experiments focus on GLMs with bag-of-words features and include truncated datasets. There is no comparison to strong adaptive CD baselines such as Gauss–Southwell (Nutini et al.), approximate GS (Stich et al., 2017), or practical gradient-stale heuristics (e.g., Zhao & Zhang; Papa et al.). For SGD, reported gains are marginal, undermining the generality claim.
- Fairness of timing experiments: some timing results are from different machines; this complicates claims about overhead. More thorough, controlled timing studies are needed.

Quality
The theoretical development is mostly sound, and the algorithmic piece is neat. However, the main guarantee is with respect to a surrogate model and worst-case uncertainty; the paper lacks clear convergence improvements over fixed sampling in the limited-information setting. Practical complexity concerns are not fully addressed. The treatment of nonsmooth regularizers should be clarified.

Clarity
Generally clear, though several notational conventions (e.g., Tr[L] vs ∑ Li) and the use of gradients with nonsmooth ψi could be improved. Some derivations are deferred to the appendix that would help readers in the main text (e.g., explicit convergence rates for CD/SGD with p̂k). Figures would benefit from more detailed captions and controlled timing setups.

Originality
The safe min–max design and its efficient solver appear novel relative to prior adaptive sampling via bounds/stale gradients. That said, much of the surrounding framework builds on established ideas in CD/SGD and importance sampling.

Significance
For CD on GLMs, the approach may be useful when one can cheaply maintain informative bounds (e.g., via caching inner products) and when n is not too large relative to per-iteration gradient cost. The broader impact is less clear given the overhead and weaker empirical gains for SGD.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide explicit convergence rates (convex/strongly convex) for CD and SGD with p̂k, and conditions under which they are strictly better than fixed Li-based sampling.
- Clarify how the method handles nonsmooth ψi (proximal CD? subgradients?) and adapt the analysis accordingly.
- Assess the cost of maintaining bounds in sparse settings; consider amortizing or updating bounds less frequently; ablate update frequency vs. performance.
- Compare against Gauss–Southwell and approximate GS, and against adaptive probabilities with stale gradients.
- Evaluate on denser datasets and larger n to stress-test O(n) overhead, with careful timing on a single machine.
- Study the sensitivity of performance to bound tightness; include stronger bound-maintenance strategies as ablations.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, theoretically principled safe sampling design.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm for the min–max subproblem.
- Solid improvements for CD on GLMs.

Cons for acceptance
- Overhead per iteration undermines scalability relative to standard CD.
- Guarantees are model-based; no demonstrated superior global complexity in the limited-information regime.
- Unclear handling of nonsmooth regularizers; limited empirical scope and baselines.
- Marginal gains for SGD.

Recommendation: Weak reject.
