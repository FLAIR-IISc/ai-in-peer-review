- Proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for CD and SGD that uses gradient upper/lower bounds to compute an adaptive sampling distribution.
- Guarantees the adaptive sampling is never worse than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2).
- Provides tight competitive-ratio bounds on performance (Lemmas 3.5, 3.6).
- Formulates the safe sampling as an optimization problem (Equation 7) and gives an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4).
- Develops practical methods to compute required gradient bounds for generalized linear models.
- Empirically validates on multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) and losses (square, squared hinge, logistic), showing substantial gains over fixed sampling in CD.
- Demonstrates the method often has minimal computational overhead and can be practical in large-scale settings.
- Shows even loose gradient bounds can yield significant improvements, increasing applicability.
- Builds on prior adaptive sampling work but introduces a novel worst-case/bounds-based "safe" formulation with theoretical guarantees.
- Paper is technically strong, well-written, and clearly organized with good pseudocode and experimental design.
- Limitation: lacks a clear theoretical bound on how much faster convergence can be compared to fixed sampling.
- Minor clarity issue: does not fully explain why improvements are smaller for SGD than for CD despite theoretical applicability to both.
- Overall assessment: strong, original, and significant contribution; recommendation is to accept.
