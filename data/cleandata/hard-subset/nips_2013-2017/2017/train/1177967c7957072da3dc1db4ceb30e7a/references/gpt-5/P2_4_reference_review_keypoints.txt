- Paper addresses adaptive importance sampling for CD and SGD, showing full-information optimal CD sampling p ∝ √Li |∇i f| improves one-step progress and can yield up to n-fold speedups in extremes.
- Main contribution: a “safe” adaptive sampling via a min–max program using per-coordinate gradient bounds, provably never worse than fixed Li sampling and matching the full-info rule when bounds are tight.
- Provides an O(n log n) sorted-threshold algorithm to compute the safe distribution and shows O(n) mechanisms to maintain gradient bounds for GLMs.
- Empirical results: substantial improvements for CD on text datasets; modest gains for SGD; step-size adaptation matters and method tolerates loose bounds.
- Novelty: the safe min–max design leveraging interval bounds plus an efficient constructive algorithm appears new relative to prior adaptive importance-sampling work.
- Strength: clear theoretical motivation demonstrating the advantage of gradient-based sampling over fixed schemes.
- Strength: theorem guaranteeing the safe distribution dominates fixed Li-based sampling and recovers optimality with tight bounds.
- Strength: efficient constructive algorithm (Theorem 3.4) with geometric interpretation.
- Strength: practical recipes for maintaining safe bounds in GLMs with O(n) per-iteration cost.
- Strength: empirical support showing iteration and wall-clock improvements for CD.
- Weakness: per-iteration overhead is high (O(n) bound updates + O(n log n) to compute p̂), conflicting with O(1)/O(log n) goals in many CD/SGD setups.
- Suggested mitigation: evaluate less frequent p̂ updates, incremental data structures (heaps/buckets) for amortized sublinear updates, and sparsity/block exploitation to reduce bound-update cost.
- Weakness: lack of a global convergence rate analysis for the safe scheme beyond one-step progress and dominance claims.
- Weakness: limited empirical scope—few GLM/text datasets, modest SGD gains, missing comparisons to other adaptive heuristics (stale gradients, dual-gap, Gauss–Southwell) and missing ablations on bound tightness and recomputation frequency.
- Weakness: notation and clarity issues (n used ambiguously; A ∈ R^{d×n} swaps roles); Algorithm 4’s variables could map more clearly to original indices.
- Evaluation—quality: technically sound core but practical cost model and missing global analysis weaken applicability in large-n settings.
- Evaluation—clarity: generally well written with good intuition, but notation and algorithmic details need streamlining.
- Evaluation—originality: safe min–max approach and its efficient solution are novel in this context.
- Evaluation—significance: impactful for CD when gradient bounds are cheap and n is moderate; less clear benefit for SGD in reported experiments.
- Pros: novel safe adaptive sampling with dominance guarantee, efficient algorithm, strong CD gains and recovery of full-info optimal rule.
- Cons: potentially heavy per-iteration cost, limited experimental breadth and ablations, and missing explicit global convergence results.
- Recommendation: Weak accept, conditional on addressing cost/benefit trade-offs (infrequent updates, sparsity-aware implementations) and expanding experiments or convergence analysis.
