Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for randomized optimization, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). With full information, the authors show that gradient-based sampling (probabilities proportional to √Li |∇i f| for CD, and to ‖∇fi(x)‖ for SGD) maximizes a standard quadratic upper-bound model of expected one-step progress and can yield up to an n-fold speedup vs fixed Li-based sampling. The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive sampling scheme for the limited-information setting: given per-coordinate safe lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (or component gradient norms), the sampling distribution is chosen by solving a convex min-max problem that minimizes the worst-case model-based suboptimality over all gradients consistent with the bounds. The solution enjoys two key properties: (i) it is the best worst-case choice given the bounds; (ii) it is never worse than fixed importance sampling (and reduces to gradient-based sampling when bounds are exact). The paper provides an O(n log n) algorithm (via sorting and clamping) to compute the distribution and step size each iteration, and shows how to maintain the necessary safe bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs). Experiments on several text datasets show substantial gains for CD, with smaller gains for SGD.

This work builds on and connects to: nonuniform and adaptive sampling for CD and SGD (e.g., Nesterov, Richtárik & Takáč; Zhao & Zhang on gradient-based sampling for SGD; Perekrestenko et al. and Csiba et al. on adaptive/duality-gap schemes), Gauss-Southwell and steepest-coordinate rules (Nutini et al.; Stich et al.), and safe screening ideas for bounding coordinates (Liu et al.; Ndiaye et al.). The core novelty is the min-max “safe” formulation and its efficient solver, along with the guarantee that the adaptive scheme is never worse than fixed sampling.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for adaptive sampling and a principled formulation that bridges full-information optimal sampling and limited-information settings.
- Solid theoretical results: expected decrease derivation, characterization of the optimal probabilities, min-max optimality, non-worseness relative to Li-based sampling, and a competitive-ratio analysis. The geometric interpretation is insightful.
- An efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling/stepsize given bounds, with a clean clamping structure; reduces to optimal gradient-based sampling when bounds are tight.
- Practical integration for GLMs with simple, safe bound updates; empirical results show strong gains for CD across losses and regularizers.
- Writing is generally clear and the paper is well-positioned within relevant literature.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Computational overhead and practicality: Computing p̂k and vk in O(n log n), plus O(n) bound updates every iteration, can be significant when n is large and per-iteration CD work is O(1)–O(nnz(aik)) via residual updates. The paper argues the overhead is acceptable when d = Ω(n), but many modern sparse problems have d ≪ n (e.g., text classification with >10^6 features), where O(n log n) per iteration can dominate. The experiments use reduced subsets in some cases; more detail on wall-clock overhead and amortization strategies is needed.
- Limited baselines: For CD, comparisons are primarily to uniform/Li-based and to the full-information optimal sampling. Missing are strong adaptive baselines such as: Gauss-Southwell(q)/approximate steepest coordinate descent (e.g., Nutini et al., Stich et al.), duality-gap-driven sampling for coordinate/SDCA variants (Csiba et al., Qu et al.), and prior “upper-bound-based” adaptive probabilities (e.g., Zhao & Zhang; Schmidt et al.; Perekrestenko et al.). For SGD, the improvements are minor; a broader set of datasets or settings where component gradients vary more would clarify when the method helps.
- Some technical exposition issues: There appears to be a notational typo in the definition of V(p, x): minimizing the expected bound in Eq. (3) yields V(p, x) = ∑i Li x_i^2 / p_i, which matches Lemma 2.4 and the √Li|x_i| optimal solution; the text shows multiplication by [p]i in places. Equation (3) would also benefit from a more explicit derivation of the expectation. The competitive-ratio condition in Lemma 3.6 is somewhat opaque; more intuition/examples would help.
- Safe bounds for general h: The GLM section relies on Cauchy–Schwarz and, for non-quadratic h, on Hessian bounds along a segment. For logistic/squared hinge, concrete safe constants (e.g., bounds on h′′) and practical guidance on tightness would improve reproducibility. Sparse implementations and storage/maintenance of residuals and norms should be discussed.
- Experimental details: The choice to binarize features and fix λ = 0.1 may impact generality. It would be helpful to report the frequency of recomputing p̂k (every iteration vs periodically), actual per-iteration timings broken down into sampling vs update vs gradient, and ablations on bound tightness. For the largest dataset, the feasibility of O(n log n) per iteration should be clarified.

Assessment by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The theoretical core is sound and carefully argued, with meaningful guarantees. Practicality concerns (per-iteration O(n log n)) and limited baselines reduce the empirical strength.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but a few formula typos and some missing derivation details. More implementation details would help.
- Originality: The safe min-max formulation and its efficient solver are novel and interesting. The stepsize coupling to the sampling is also well-motivated.
- Significance: For CD, the approach appears impactful and could influence adaptive sampling design. For SGD, significance is less evident from the current experiments. Adoption may hinge on addressing the computational overhead.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and clearly restate V(p, x) and the derivation from Eq. (2)–(3); include a short proof in the main text.
- Provide concrete recipes and constants for safe bounds for common h (logistic, squared hinge), including how to bound ∇^2h along the segment, and discuss sparse data structures.
- Address computational overhead: consider amortizing p̂k computation (e.g., recomputing every T steps), incremental maintenance of the sorted lists, or bucketed approximations with provable degradation bounds. Report detailed timing breakdowns and sensitivity to n/d and sparsity.
- Expand baselines: compare to Gauss-Southwell(q)/approximate steepest CD (Nutini et al.; Stich et al. 2017), duality-gap/prox-violations-based sampling (Csiba–Qu–Richtárik; Perekrestenko et al.), and upper-bound/stale-gradient approaches (Zhao & Zhang; Schmidt et al.). For SGD, include settings with larger gradient-norm heterogeneity and mini-batch variants (e.g., importance sampling for minibatches).
- Empirical ablations: show how performance varies with bound tightness (e.g., looser vs tighter Cauchy–Schwarz vs data-dependent inner product bounds), and the evolution of the competitive ratio proxy (vk and an estimate of wk).
- Clarify whether p̂k is recomputed every iteration and how this scales on the largest datasets.

Arguments pro acceptance
- Novel, principled safe adaptive sampling with clear theory and an efficient solver.
- Strong improvements for CD; practical integration for GLMs.
- Guarantees that it is never worse than fixed sampling are appealing.

Arguments against acceptance
- Potentially prohibitive O(n log n) per-iteration overhead without amortization; unclear practicality at large n.
- Missing comparisons to several strong adaptive baselines.
- Limited gains for SGD and limited exploration of when it helps most.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
The theoretical contribution is solid and novel, and the CD results are promising. Addressing the practicality and baseline gaps would strengthen the paper further.
