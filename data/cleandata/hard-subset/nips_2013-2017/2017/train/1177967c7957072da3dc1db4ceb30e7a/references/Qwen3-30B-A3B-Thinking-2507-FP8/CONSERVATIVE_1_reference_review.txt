

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling technique for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors argue that while gradient-based adaptive sampling theoretically outperforms fixed importance sampling, computing the optimal adaptive distribution is computationally expensive (requiring full gradient computation). Their solution uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to compute an approximate sampling distribution that is guaranteed to be at least as good as fixed sampling. The theoretical contribution includes proofs showing their method outperforms fixed sampling, with empirical results on generalized linear models with L1/L2 regularization.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with clear theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2) and a well-structured presentation. The "safe" aspect of their method, which ensures the sampling distribution is never worse than fixed importance sampling, is a valuable theoretical contribution that addresses a significant limitation in prior work. The algorithms are clearly presented with pseudocode and the computational complexity analysis is thorough.

However, as a reviewer with skepticism toward new approaches without extensive validation, I have several concerns. The empirical evaluation is limited to generalized linear models with L1/L2 regularization on three datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20), which represents a narrow scope. The paper claims significant improvements over fixed sampling, but the magnitude of these improvements is modest in the experiments (Figures 3-6), and the paper fails to compare against more recent adaptive sampling methods that have appeared in the literature since the cited works.

Crucially, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contribution from prior work. It cites "Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent with Adaptive Probabilities" (Csiba et al., ICML 2015) but fails to acknowledge that this work already proposed adaptive importance sampling with theoretical guarantees and practical implementation. The paper also doesn't engage with "Non-Uniform Stochastic Average Gradient Method" (Schmidt et al., AISTATS 2015), which developed a similar approach for SGD. The claim that their method "provably performs better than any fixed importance sampling" is theoretically valid, but this guarantee is also established in prior work (e.g., Csiba et al., 2015).

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong, but the practical significance is unclear. The computational overhead is O(n log n) per iteration, which is acceptable for many problems but doesn't demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods that already provide similar guarantees. The empirical results show that adaptive sampling improves over fixed sampling, but the paper doesn't show how their method compares to the best adaptive sampling approaches currently in use.

I'm also concerned about the paper's claim that "the introduced computational overhead reduces to O(log n) per iteration" (Section 4). This claim depends on d = Ω(n), which isn't always true in practice. For many machine learning problems, d << n (e.g., in text classification), making the overhead potentially significant.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Comparing against more recent adaptive sampling methods (e.g., Perekrestenko et al., AISTATS 2016)
2. Expanding the empirical evaluation to more diverse machine learning problems beyond GLMs
3. Providing clearer evidence of the practical significance of the theoretical improvements
4. Better differentiating their contribution from prior work on adaptive importance sampling

While the theoretical contribution is valid and the method is well-motivated, the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate why this approach is necessary or superior to existing methods in practice. The empirical validation is limited in scope and doesn't compare against the most relevant baselines in the field. The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution but falls short in demonstrating its practical significance and clear differentiation from prior work.

Strengths: Theoretical guarantees are sound, the "safe" aspect is a valuable contribution, the paper is well-written and clearly organized.

Weaknesses: Limited empirical validation, insufficient comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, unclear practical significance, insufficient differentiation from prior work.

Overall, while the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theory of adaptive importance sampling, the empirical evidence doesn't sufficiently support the claim that this method offers a significant practical advantage over existing approaches. The paper would benefit from broader empirical validation and a more thorough comparison with recent methods in the field.
