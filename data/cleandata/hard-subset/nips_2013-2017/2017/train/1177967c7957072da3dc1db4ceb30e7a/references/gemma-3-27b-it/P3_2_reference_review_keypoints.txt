- Proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for CD and SGD that approximates gradient-based sampling while guaranteeing improvements over uniform/fixed sampling.
- Core idea: use safe upper and lower bounds on gradients to formulate and efficiently solve an optimization for the sampling distribution.
- Provides theoretical analysis showing up to n-fold speedup in coordinate descent and guarantees of being provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling.
- Derives optimal stepsize strategies and analyzes competitive ratio, offering solid theoretical foundations.
- Presents an efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) for approximating the optimal sampling distribution with O(n log n) complexity.
- Discusses efficient gradient-bound tracking for GLMs, enhancing practical applicability.
- Method is general and can be integrated into existing CD and SGD algorithms.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and supported by helpful algorithms and notation.
- Empirical results demonstrate strong gains for CD on real datasets; comparisons to uniform and optimal sampling are thorough.
- SGD experiments show only modest improvements, and the paper lacks deeper analysis explaining this gap.
- Method’s effectiveness depends on the quality of safe gradient bounds; obtaining tight bounds for complex models is not addressed.
- Competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.6) feels underdeveloped and relies on specific bound conditions without practical guidance.
- Experiments are mainly limited to GLMs, leaving broader applicability to other problem classes untested.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically effective for CD.
- Arguments against acceptance: modest SGD gains and reliance on accurate gradient bounds may limit applicability.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with rigorous theory, efficient algorithm, and promising results; recommended for acceptance.
- Recommendation: Accept; Confidence: 8/10.
