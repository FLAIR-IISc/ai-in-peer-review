This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The key challenge in adaptive sampling is that computing the optimal, gradient-based sampling distribution is often as expensive as a full gradient step, negating the benefits of a stochastic method. Existing approaches often rely on approximations or stale information, which can lack theoretical guarantees and sometimes perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The main idea of this work is to use efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient components (or their norms). Instead of using these bounds heuristically, the authors formulate a min-max optimization problem to find a sampling distribution that is robust to this uncertainty. Specifically, the chosen distribution minimizes the worst-case value of a performance metric over all possible gradient vectors consistent with the bounds. The authors present an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm to solve this problem. The resulting "safe" sampling scheme is provably at least as good as standard (fixed) importance sampling. The paper demonstrates how to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provides empirical results on several real-world datasets, showing significant improvements for CD and more modest gains for SGD.

**Strengths:**
1.  The core contribution—formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a min-max problem (7) over an uncertainty set—is novel and theoretically well-founded. It provides a principled way to derive a robust sampling scheme from partial information.
2.  The main theoretical result (Theorem 3.2) establishes a "safety" guarantee: the proposed method is provably better than or equal to Li-based fixed importance sampling in the worst case. This is a significant advantage over many heuristic adaptive methods that lack such guarantees.
3.  The empirical evaluation for coordinate descent is compelling. The proposed method ("safe sampling") consistently performs nearly as well as the impractical "optimal sampling" (which requires full gradient information) and substantially outperforms uniform sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time.
4.  The paper is upfront about the limitations observed in the SGD experiments, where the gains over uniform sampling were marginal. This honest reporting is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while containing promising ideas, suffers from a significant number of flaws in its execution, ranging from minor presentational issues to potentially critical technical errors.

1.  **Technical Correctness and Clarity:**
    *   **Gradient Update for CD:** Equation (11), which describes the update of gradient entries for inactive coordinates in CD, appears to be incorrect for the least-squares case discussed. The change `∇if(xk+1)−∇if(xk)` should depend on the inner product `<ai, aik>` multiplied by the step taken, which is `-γk * ∇_ik f(x^k)`. The formula presented is missing the `∇_ik f(x^k)` term. This is a crucial formula for maintaining the gradient bounds `l` and `u`, and if it is incorrect, the entire experimental validation for the CD methods is cast into doubt.
    *   **Algorithm 4 Description:** Algorithm 4 is central to the paper's contribution, but its description is opaque and seems to contain typos. The initialization `u = 1, l = n` is confusing, as `u` and `l` are also used for the bound vectors; presumably these are meant to be indices into sorted arrays. More critically, the update for `m` in line 11 is presented as a simple assignment, but the formula for `m` in the text (`m = ‖c‖22 · ‖ √ Lc‖−11`) depends on `c`, whose components in turn depend on `m` (as per Eq. (9)). This suggests a fixed-point iteration is required, but the algorithm does not specify this. As written, the algorithm is not clear enough to be implemented.
    *   **Min-Max Objective Motivation:** The derivation of the objective function `V(p, c) / ‖c‖22` in (6) is abrupt. While the resulting formulation is sound (optimizing for a guaranteed rate of progress with a stepsize `α = 1/v_k`), the paper does not clearly explain this motivation, leaving the reader to wonder why it differs from the objective of maximizing the one-step optimal progress, which would involve minimizing `V(p, c) / ‖c‖42`.
    *   **Confusing Notation:** The condition in Lemma 3.6, `[Ck]i ∩ γ[Ck]i = ∅`, is non-standard and difficult to parse. It seems intended to describe a bound on the relative width of the uncertainty interval, but this should be stated more clearly.

2.  **Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   The manuscript is littered with typos and formatting errors that detract from its quality. Examples include "deep leaning" (p. 1), "preformance" (p. 1), `(L1, . . . , n)>` instead of `(L1, . . . , Ln)>` (p. 1), and a leftover template string "st Conference..." on the first page.
    *   In Figure 1, the update rules in all three algorithm boxes (`Alg. 1`, `Alg. 2`, `Alg. 3`) are missing the standard basis vector `e_ik`.
    *   The statement in the conclusion that the scheme can be implemented "at no extra computational cost" is an overstatement. There is an `O(n log n)` overhead, which is non-negligible, especially when `d` is not significantly larger than `n log n`.

3.  **Experimental Details:**
    *   The paper states that for all datasets, "we set unnormalized features with all the non-zero entries set to 1". This data preprocessing step is unusual, not well-motivated, and makes it difficult to reproduce the results or compare them to other work using these standard datasets.
    *   The paper provides no analysis or discussion for why the adaptive methods provided very little benefit in the SGD setting. An investigation into the distribution of `‖∇fi(xk)‖` norms could have provided valuable insight.

**Summary of arguments:**

*   **For acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound min-max framework for robust adaptive sampling with strong "safety" guarantees. The empirical results for CD are very promising.
*   **Against acceptance:** The paper suffers from a number of significant flaws. There appears to be a critical technical error in the gradient update equation for CD (Eq. 11), the key algorithm for computing the sampling is opaquely described (Alg. 4), and the manuscript has a high number of typographical and notational errors. These issues collectively undermine confidence in the work's correctness and reproducibility.

**Recommendation:**
Reject.

While the core idea of this paper is strong and potentially of high impact, the current manuscript is not ready for publication. The combination of a likely technical error in a key formula, the incomprehensible description of the central algorithm, and a general lack of polish and care in the writing and presentation is too substantial to overlook. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise their work—verifying their formulas, clarifying their algorithms, and proofreading the text—and resubmit to a future conference. A corrected and polished version of this paper could be a very strong contribution.
