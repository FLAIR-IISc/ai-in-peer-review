Summary
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). With full gradient information, the authors re-derive the “gradient-based” optimal sampling that minimizes a quadratic upper bound on the expected one-step decrease. The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive scheme when only bounds on per-coordinate (CD) or per-component (SGD) gradients are available. They formulate a min-max problem over probability vectors and feasible gradients constrained by lower/upper bounds, prove that the resulting sampling p̂ is never worse (in the bound) than fixed Li-based sampling, and provide an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute p̂ each iteration via a sorting-and-thresholding procedure. They further discuss how to maintain safe bounds cheaply for generalized linear models (GLMs), and provide empirical results on several datasets indicating that the proposed method outperforms fixed sampling and approaches the performance of full-information gradient sampling, with larger gains for CD than for SGD.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to importance/non-uniform sampling for CD/SGD [Nesterov, Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.; Allen-Zhu et al.; Zhao & Zhang; Needell et al.] and adaptive schemes using gradient magnitudes or dual gaps [Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.; Osokin et al.]. The “safe bounds” angle is reminiscent of safe screening [Liu et al.; Ndiaye et al.], but applied to sampling distributions. The authors position their approach as providing worst-case guarantees against fixed sampling under bounded gradient information, and as a practical proxy for full gradient-based sampling.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting sampling under limited information as a min-max problem is elegant and yields a clean worst-case guarantee: V(p̂,c) ≤ Tr[L]·||c||^2 for all feasible c.
- Algorithmic contribution: The O(n log n) sorting/thresholding procedure to compute p̂ is simple and reminiscent of water-filling, with a constructive characterization (Theorem 3.4).
- Theoretical insights: Derivations for CD with “directionally-unbiased” steps γk = αk/pk, and recovery of gradient-based sampling in the full-information limit. The bounds Lmin ≤ vk ≤ Tr[L] and the competitive ratio considerations are useful.
- Practicality: Discussion on maintaining safe gradient bounds in GLMs; experiments show improvements in CD settings with minimal engineering.

Weaknesses / detailed issues
Technical correctness and clarity
- Notation inconsistencies that may hide errors:
  - In Example 2.3, αk(p*): the derivation from Eq. (3) implies α* = ||g||_2^2 / V(p,g) with V minimized to ||√L g||_1^2, hence α* = ||g||_2^2 / ||√L g||_1^2. The text writes “‖√L∇f‖^2” without norm subscript; multiple places omit the 1-subscript, potentially suggesting an L2 norm. Please fix and ensure consistency.
  - Lemma 2.1’s bound seems to be missing a factor 1/2 (expected decrease should be α*/2 · ||∇f||^2 from minimizing a quadratic). The formatting “αk(pk) 2” appears to intend “αk(pk)/2”. Throughout, fractions like “1Tr[L]” are missing slashes.
  - V(p,x) convexity in p and the min-max interchange (inequality (∗)) rely on Sion’s theorem; but the assumptions (convexity in p, concavity in c/compactness of Ck) are not cleanly stated for the ratio V/||c||^2. Please make explicit the function’s convex-concave structure and compactness to justify swapping min and max.

Assumptions for safe bounds
- Section 4’s “safe bounds” require assumptions on h’s curvature (e.g., bounds on ∇^2h) and on 〈ai, aik〉. For SGD (Lemma 4.1), safety of bounds on ||∇fi||2 requires known bounds on ∇^2hi over the relevant range; these are not stated. Without explicit boundedness assumptions, the “safe” guarantee is unclear.
- Initialization with `0=0, u0=∞ is not operational: Algorithm 4 cannot handle ∞ in sorting and thresholding. The paper should specify a fallback (e.g., Li-based sampling) until finite bounds are available and explain how/when u becomes finite.

Complexity and practicality
- The per-iteration overhead O(n log n) to recompute p̂ (plus O(n) to update bounds) is substantial for large n (news20 has >10^6 features). The paper does not discuss incremental or amortized updates to avoid sorting each iteration, nor data structures to maintain the threshold efficiently as bounds change. Claiming “no extra computational cost” when d=Ω(n) is not compelling for sparse high-dimensional problems where per-iteration gradient access is O(nnz(aik)).
- Bound updates in Section 4 for general h require evaluating or bounding 〈ai, ∇^2h(Ax̃) aik〉; naively this is expensive unless ∇^2h is simple (e.g., scaled identity). The paper uses Cauchy–Schwarz with precomputed norms, which can be very loose; the empirical section uses only very naïve bounds, yet claims generality.

Experimental evaluation
- Baselines: The experiments compare to uniform/fixed Li-based and the intractable “optimal” full gradient scheme. Missing are comparisons to existing practical adaptive samplings (e.g., stale gradient-based, gap-based sampling, SDCA with adaptive probabilities, or approximate steepest coordinate descent), which weakens the empirical case.
- Reporting: Only optimization curves are shown. No sensitivity to λ, no sparsity effects, no ablations (e.g., cost breakdown of O(n log n) overhead), and wall-clock times are not comparable across machines (Figures 6 vs 9). For news20-scale n, an O(n log n) per-iteration routine would be a major cost; the paper should provide timings to substantiate feasibility.
- Data preprocessing: “all non-zero entries set to 1” is unusual and may skew results; justify or provide results with standard normalization.
- Reproducibility: No code link; many implementation choices (e.g., handling of ∞ bounds, numeric stability in Algorithm 4) are unspecified.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous typos and formatting issues impede readability: missing fraction slashes (1/Tr[L]), inconsistent notation for norms, misuse of transpose “(L1, …, n)>”, dangling “st Conference…” line, footnote markers (“2.3”) out of place, grammar (“chose” vs “choose”), and overloaded symbols in Algorithm 4 (using ` and u as both vectors and indices). References are incomplete/inconsistent.
- Some claims are overstated without caveats: “no extra computational cost” and “provably better than fixed sampling” should be qualified (the latter holds under correct safe bounds; the former depends heavily on n and data sparsity).

Pros
- Novel and principled formulation of sampling under bounded information.
- Clear worst-case guarantee relative to Li-based sampling.
- Simple constructive algorithm with a nice geometric interpretation.
- Empirical gains for CD suggest practical usefulness.

Cons
- Sloppy notation/formatting and a few derivational ambiguities.
- Heavy per-iteration overhead not convincingly addressed.
- Incomplete assumptions for “safe” bounds; initialization/edge cases unclear.
- Limited and not fully convincing experiments; missing relevant baselines.

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially impactful for CD, but the paper in its current form has too many technical and presentation issues, and the empirical validation and practicality claims are not yet convincing. With corrected derivations/notation, clarified assumptions, stronger baselines, and a more compelling complexity treatment (or amortized implementation), this work could be much stronger.

Confidence: 4/5
