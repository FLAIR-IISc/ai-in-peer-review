Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for stochastic first-order methods, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and SGD. With full gradient information, the authors show that sampling coordinates proportionally to |∇i f|√Li (with a paired stepsize) maximizes an upper bound on expected one-step decrease and can yield up to an n-fold improvement over fixed (Lipschitz-based) sampling. The main contribution is a practical “safe” adaptive sampling that uses per-iteration lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (or per-component gradient norms for SGD) to compute a distribution p̂ by solving a convex min–max problem over an uncertainty set Ck. They prove: (i) p̂ is optimal for the worst case c ∈ Ck; (ii) it is never worse (in the model-based decrease bound) than fixed importance sampling; and (iii) it recovers the oracle distribution when bounds are tight. They provide an O(n log n) algorithm to compute p̂ and stepsize α̂=1/vk, and show how to maintain safe bounds cheaply for GLMs. Empirically, on several text datasets and losses (square, logistic, squared hinge) with L1/L2 regularization, the safe adaptive sampling accelerates CD substantially (iterations and wall time), and gives modest gains for SGD.

This builds on and contrasts with prior non-uniform/fixed importance sampling for CD/SGD (e.g., Nesterov, Richtárik & Takáč, Qu et al., Zhao & Zhang), adaptive probabilities based on dual residuals/gaps (e.g., Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.), and greedy/GS rules (e.g., Nutini et al., Stich et al.). Unlike prior adaptive heuristics that use stale/approximate gradients or loose bounds without guarantees, this paper gives a worst-case optimal distribution under uncertainty that still dominates fixed sampling in the same model.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical motivation: demonstrates potential n× speedups for oracle gradient-based sampling in CD and connects to existing SGD results.
- Novel min–max formulation over gradient uncertainty sets, with an efficient O(n log n) algorithm and interpretable structure (thresholding via √Li m).
- Safety guarantee: never worse than fixed importance sampling under the model; recovers oracle with full information.
- Practicality: simple bound-maintenance schemes for GLMs; per-iteration overhead modest; empirical gains for CD are consistent and sizable.
- Careful stepsize design coupled to the sampling distribution; evidence that adaptive stepsizes matter.

Weaknesses
- Experimental comparisons omit strong adaptive baselines: Gauss–Southwell (and approximate GS), gap-based/adaptive-probability CD (e.g., Csiba–Qu–Richtárik; Perekrestenko et al.), and heuristic gradient-norm sampling with stale updates.
- Empirical scope is narrow (text GLMs with bag-of-words features set to 1); unclear performance on dense data, non-GLM models, or composite/proximal settings.
- Overhead analysis could be more nuanced: O(n log n) per iteration may be nontrivial when partial gradients are extremely cheap (very sparse settings); the claim that it becomes O(log n) relative overhead when d = Ω(n) deserves more careful scenario analysis.
- The “safety” guarantees are with respect to the quadratic upper-bound model (L-smoothness). A discussion of model misspecification and robustness would be useful.
- Some implementation details are missing: handling of infinite initial bounds (u0=∞), update frequency of p̂, memory and constant factors, and extension to mini-batches.

Assessment by criteria
Quality: The theoretical development appears solid. The expected decrease bound, min–max optimality via Sion’s minimax theorem, and derivation of the O(n log n) algorithm are plausible and well-argued, with proofs deferred to the appendix. The coupling of pk and αk is handled cleanly. The safe bound maintenance for GLMs is correct in spirit (Cauchy–Schwarz and Hessian-based line-segment arguments), though tighter bounds or constants for specific losses would strengthen the practical claims. Empirical results support the CD gains; the more modest SGD gains are honestly reported.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written and organized. Notation is standard; algorithms are explicit. Some sections could be clearer: clarify how Algorithm 2 handles u0=∞; explicitly state assumptions on h or hi (bounds on second derivatives) needed for safe bounds; bring a main convergence theorem (beyond one-step) into the main text rather than relegating to the appendix.

Originality: The safe min–max formulation for sampling under bounded gradient information and the explicit algorithm to compute p̂ are, to my knowledge, novel in this context. The result that p̂ is guaranteed to dominate fixed importance sampling under the model is a clean contribution. The stepsize coupling is natural yet thoughtfully executed.

Significance: For CD on convex GLMs, the method seems practically valuable, offering consistent gains with modest overhead and without requiring full gradient computation. The idea of uncertainty-aware sampling could influence subsequent adaptive sampling designs. Impact for SGD appears smaller in the presented setups; broader demonstrations would help.

Constructive suggestions
- Add comparisons to: Gauss–Southwell and approximate GS; adaptive/gap-based CD sampling (e.g., Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.); and a heuristic using stale gradient magnitudes. This would better situate the gains.
- Evaluate on denser datasets and non-text features, and include composite/proximal variants (e.g., L1 with proximal CD), where adaptive sampling is commonly used.
- Discuss and quantify the effect of bound tightness: ablate bound quality, update frequency, and show how ρk or proxies evolve.
- Clarify handling of initial infinite bounds and propose a practical warm start; consider amortizing p̂ updates (e.g., every T steps) and report the trade-off.
- Provide a main-text convergence theorem (rates under strong convexity) highlighting the dependence on vk relative to Tr[L].
- Discuss mini-batch extensions and distributed settings (the method seems well-suited); include a small-scale experiment if possible.
- Tighten the GLM bound derivations for specific losses (e.g., logistic: bound ∇2h) and report constants used.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a principled and efficient adaptive sampling method with a compelling safety guarantee and strong empirical gains for CD. While the experimental comparison set is limited and the SGD benefits are modest, the theoretical contribution and practical viability for CD merit acceptance. Strengthening baselines and clarifying some implementation details would further improve the work.
