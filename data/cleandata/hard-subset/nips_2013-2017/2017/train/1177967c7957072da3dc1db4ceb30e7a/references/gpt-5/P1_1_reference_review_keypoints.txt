- Paper proposes adaptive importance sampling for CD/SGD that bridges full-gradient sampling and practical limited-information schemes.
- Main theoretical result: full-information optimal CD sampling p* ∝ √L_i |∇_i f(x)| with adaptive stepsize minimizing a quadratic model.
- Introduces a safe adaptive min–max formulation using interval bounds on gradients that provably dominates fixed L-based sampling and recovers p* when bounds are tight.
- Provides an O(n log n) sorting-based algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the min–max and compute the recommended stepsize α̂ = 1/v_k, with a geometric interpretation and competitive-ratio analysis.
- Derives simple, O(n)-updateable safe gradient bounds for GLMs with separable regularizers via mean-value/Cauchy–Schwarz expansions.
- Empirical results: substantial iteration and time gains for CD on several losses/datasets, and modest gains for SGD; approach approaches full-information optimal sampling in practice.
- Builds on extensive prior work in non-uniform sampling and adaptive CD/SGD, but adds a novel worst-case safe construction using interval bounds and explicit algorithmic solution.
- Theory quality: one-step optimality and min–max guarantees are solid; solver and geometric characterization are valuable; competitive-ratio bounds are loose but informative.
- Experimental quality: supports main claims with visualizations of v_k and comparisons to uniform and full-information sampling, but has limitations.
- Clarity: paper is well-organized; should emphasize per-iteration computational costs earlier, add a convergence statement for the adaptive stepsize, and clarify initialization/stability for u = ∞.
- Originality: the safe min–max adaptive sampling and constructive O(n log n) solver appear novel and provide worst-case dominance over fixed sampling.
- Significance: method could bring most benefits of gradient-based sampling without full gradients if overhead is controlled; guarantee of never being worse than fixed sampling is attractive.
- Major limitation: per-iteration overhead O(n) for bound updates plus O(n log n) for sampling can be prohibitive for sparse high-dimensional problems where per-iteration CD cost is tiny.
- Limited baselines: comparisons lack Gauss–Southwell/approximate steepest CD, adaptive SDCA/dual adaptive methods, and common heuristic bound-based samplers.
- Theoretical limitation: only one-step progress and worst-case dominance shown; no end-to-end global convergence rates that quantify effect of bound looseness.
- Practical bound concern: GLM bound updates via Cauchy–Schwarz can be loose; memory, sparsity, and lazy/amortized maintenance strategies are not deeply explored.
- Pros summarized: principled min–max formulation, constructive algorithm, provable dominance, applicability to CD/SGD, and empirical CD gains.
- Cons summarized: potentially large runtime overhead, limited baselines and small SGD gains, missing global rates, and some subsampled experiments.
- Suggested improvements: experiments on full-scale sparse datasets, comparisons to greedy/adaptive baselines, amortized/lazy update strategies, formal convergence rates involving v_k, and clarification of initialization/stability.
- Recommendation: Weak accept—solid, original contribution that needs stronger empirical validation and further analysis of computational overhead.
