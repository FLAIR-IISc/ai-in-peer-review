Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles adaptive importance sampling for randomized optimization methods, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It first shows that, under coordinate-wise smoothness, an “optimal” gradient-based sampling for CD—probabilities proportional to √Li|∇if(x)|—yields strictly better expected one-step progress than fixed (Li-based) sampling, with potential speedups up to a factor n in extreme cases. It then addresses the practical obstacle that full-gradient information per iteration is infeasible: the main contribution is a safe adaptive scheme that uses per-iteration lower/upper bounds on coordinate gradients (or per-component gradient norms in SGD) to compute a sampling distribution via a min–max formulation. The proposed distribution is guaranteed to be never worse than fixed importance sampling and recovers the full-information optimal rule when bounds are tight. An O(n log n) algorithm computes this distribution using sorted thresholding; the paper also gives examples of how to maintain safe bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs) in O(n) per iteration. Experiments on standard text datasets show clear improvements for CD (less so for SGD).

This sits amid a substantial literature on importance sampling and adaptive selection in SGD/CD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Zhao & Zhang; Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.), as well as Gauss–Southwell and screening rules. The novelty here is the “safe” min–max design that leverages interval bounds to obtain a distribution with a provable dominance guarantee over fixed sampling, plus an efficient algorithm to compute it.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical motivation: the full-information gradient-based sampling for CD improves one-step progress and can offer up to n-fold gains over uniform in special cases.
- Safe adaptive sampling via a convex min–max program: the chosen distribution p̂ minimizes the worst-case ratio V(p, c)/||c||^2 over feasible gradient intervals. Theorem 3.2 guarantees that p̂ is never worse than fixed Li-based sampling and often strictly better; it also coincides with the optimal gradient-based rule when bounds collapse.
- Efficient computation: Theorem 3.4 provides an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting-based thresholding) to compute p̂ and the associated step size, with a clean geometric interpretation.
- Practicality for GLMs: concrete mechanisms (via Cauchy–Schwarz) to maintain safe bounds on gradients or component norms in O(n) time per iteration are provided.
- Empirical support: on multiple datasets and losses, the method substantially outperforms fixed sampling for CD in both iterations and wall-clock; stepsize adaptation is important; the scheme works even with very loose bounds.

Weaknesses and questions
- Per-iteration overhead can be high. The bound updates cost O(n) and computing p̂ costs O(n log n) per iteration. Many CD/SGD setups aim for O(1) or O(log n) cost per iteration; scanning n coordinates each step is a strong assumption, and the argument that the overhead is negligible when d = Ω(n) will not hold in many sparse or high-n regimes. Can the authors:
  - Update p̂ less frequently (every T steps) and show the trade-off?
  - Maintain incremental data structures (heaps/buckets) to achieve amortized sublinear updates under mild coordinate changes?
  - Exploit sparsity (nnz per column) or block structures to reduce the O(n) bound-update cost?
- Convergence rates for the safe scheme are not fully developed in the main text. The paper gives one-step progress guarantees and dominance over fixed sampling, but a global convergence rate (e.g., expected suboptimality vs. iterations) under standard assumptions, explicitly comparing constants to fixed sampling, would strengthen the case.
- Empirical scope. Results are limited to GLMs on a few text datasets with simple feature preprocessing; SGD benefits are modest. It would help to include:
  - Comparisons to alternative adaptive schemes based on stale/full gradients or dual-gap/violation heuristics (e.g., Csiba et al., Perekrestenko et al., Gauss–Southwell variants).
  - Ablations varying the tightness of bounds and frequency of recomputation, to show robustness and cost/benefit trade-offs.
  - Larger-scale or distributed settings, given the motivation and the reference to distributed tracking.
- Notational overload and clarity. n is used for both number of coordinates and components; A ∈ R^{d×n} swaps roles across CD/SGD sections. Clarifying dimensions and costs per method would improve readability. Algorithm 4’s variables (`sort, usort, indices) could use a more explicit mapping to original indices.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The technical core appears sound. The min–max formulation, use of Sion’s theorem/minimax interchange, and the O(n log n) construction are convincing. The guarantee of never being worse than fixed sampling is clear and valuable. However, the practical cost model (O(n) + O(n log n) per iteration) raises concerns in typical large-n CD/SGD settings; more empirical or theoretical support for amortization or infrequent updates would help. The global convergence analysis for the safe scheme could be expanded beyond one-step progress.
- Clarity: Generally well-written, with good intuition (geometric view) and a clean separation of CD/SGD cases, but notation could be streamlined and the algorithmic details could be explained a bit more.
- Originality: The safe min–max approach and its efficient solution are, to my knowledge, novel relative to prior adaptive importance sampling using stale/full info or heuristic bounds.
- Significance: For CD on problems where gradient bounds are cheaply maintainable and n is not prohibitively large, this can be practically impactful. For SGD, gains are smaller in the reported experiments. The theoretical guarantee “never worse than fixed” is conceptually strong and may influence subsequent work.

Pros
- Novel safe adaptive sampling with dominance guarantee over fixed sampling.
- Efficient constructive algorithm; elegant theory with clear intuition.
- Strong empirical gains for CD; recovers optimal rule with full info.

Cons
- Potentially heavy per-iteration cost; limited guidance on reducing it.
- Limited experimental breadth; modest gains in SGD; missing ablations.
- Global convergence results for the safe scheme could be more explicit.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution and the safe, efficiently computable sampling rule are interesting and relevant to the NeurIPS audience. Addressing the cost/benefit trade-offs more thoroughly (e.g., less frequent updates, sparsity-aware implementations) and expanding experiments would strengthen the paper further.
