This paper tackles the important problem of adaptive importance sampling, a technique crucial for scaling machine learning to large datasets. The central idea – developing a “safe” adaptive sampling strategy that provably outperforms uniform sampling without requiring full gradient information – is genuinely novel and addresses a significant gap in the existing literature. The theoretical analysis establishing the guarantees and the connection to gradient-based sampling is strong, and the extension to both coordinate descent and SGD is a definite plus. The proposed algorithm and its efficiency analysis are well-presented.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The “safe” adaptive sampling approach is a genuinely new contribution. Existing adaptive methods often rely on full gradient information or suffer from theoretical weaknesses. This work offers a practical and theoretically grounded alternative.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides clear theoretical guarantees, demonstrating improvement over fixed sampling strategies. The analysis of convergence rates (although details deferred to the appendix) is a crucial strength.
* **Generality:** The applicability to both coordinate descent and stochastic gradient descent broadens the impact of the work.
* **Practicality:** The algorithm is efficient and the proposed method for computing safe gradient bounds (demonstrated for GLMs) suggests real-world applicability. The O(n log n) computational overhead is reasonable.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Empirical Evaluation Depth:** While the experimental results are encouraging, they feel somewhat limited in scope. The datasets used are standard, but the comparisons could be more extensive. The SGD results are less compelling than the CD results.  It's not entirely clear *how much* improvement is typically achieved in practice.
* **Safe Bound Assumptions:** The reliance on “safe” upper and lower bounds on gradients is a critical assumption. The paper provides a reasonable example for GLMs, but a more thorough discussion of how to obtain these bounds in more complex scenarios (e.g., deep neural networks) would be valuable.  The applicability hinges on the feasibility of obtaining these bounds.
* **Appendix Reliance:** A significant amount of the theoretical detail is relegated to the appendix. While this is not necessarily a flaw, it makes it harder for a quick assessment of the paper's technical contributions.
* **Competitive Ratio Focus:** The detailed analysis of the competitive ratio, while interesting, feels slightly tangential to the main contribution. While important for understanding the worst-case performance, it might not be the most relevant metric in practice.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling.
* The "safe" sampling guarantee is a significant contribution, addressing a key limitation of existing methods.
* The generality of the method to both CD and SGD increases its impact.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents its ideas.
* The potential for practical application is high.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The reliance on safe gradient bounds is a potential limitation that needs further discussion.
* The level of detail in the appendix is high, requiring a significant effort to fully understand the technical contributions.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a valuable new technique for adaptive importance sampling. The novelty, theoretical rigor, and generality of the approach are particularly impressive. While the empirical evaluation could be more extensive and the assumptions about safe gradient bounds require further consideration, these weaknesses are outweighed by the strengths of the work. I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance.  I value bold ideas and theoretical guarantees and the authors have delivered on both. The relatively minor flaws are acceptable given the significant potential impact of this work.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
