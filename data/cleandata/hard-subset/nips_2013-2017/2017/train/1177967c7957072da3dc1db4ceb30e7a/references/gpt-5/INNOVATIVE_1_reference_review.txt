Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for both coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The central idea is to select sampling probabilities using only cheaply maintainable lower/upper bounds on per-coordinate (CD) or per-component (SGD) gradients, rather than the full gradient information required by classic gradient-based adaptive sampling. The authors pose sampling as a min–max problem: choose probabilities p to minimize the worst-case quadratic upper-bound V(p,c)/‖c‖^2 over all c consistent with the current bounds, and set a matching step-size α = 1/v, where v is the worst-case value. They prove the resulting distribution p̂ is (i) optimal under the given information in a worst-case sense and (ii) never worse than fixed (L_i-based) importance sampling. When the bounds collapse to exact gradients, the method recovers the known optimal gradient-based sampling. They also give an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute p̂ via a thresholding characterization and provide competitive-ratio bounds. For GLMs, they show how to maintain safe gradient bounds in O(n) per iteration via simple curvature arguments (and Cauchy–Schwarz), and present experiments on several text datasets for CD and SGD.

This relates to a rich literature on non-uniform sampling for CD/SGD: fixed importance sampling w.r.t. L_i (Richtárik & Takáč; Nesterov), gradient-based sampling for SGD (Zhao & Zhang), duality-gap-based sampling for CD (Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.), and screening/safe rules (Liu et al.; Ndiaye et al.). The novelty here is the robust min–max formulation to synthesize a sampling distribution directly from safe bounds, along with a provable “never-worse-than-fixed” guarantee and an efficient sorting-based solver.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual contribution: casting adaptive sampling under partial information as a convex min–max problem, with a closed-form structure (thresholding) and efficient O(n log n) solver.
- Strong “safety” guarantee: p̂ is provably never worse than fixed L_i-based sampling, and matches optimal gradient-based sampling in the full-information limit.
- Clear connection to practice: bound maintenance for GLMs is simple and cheap; experiments show substantial gains for CD over uniform/fixed sampling with modest overhead.
- Sensible step-size coupling: α chosen to match p via the same min–max objective, leading to directionally unbiased updates and practical speedups.
- Competitive-ratio analysis and geometric interpretation add insight into when the approach helps (e.g., when bounds are tight or align with L).

Weaknesses and questions
- Scope of applicability: The safe-bound machinery is demonstrated for GLMs; for deep models or non-smooth/non-separable structures, maintaining tight safe bounds per iteration may be difficult or costly. A discussion on extending beyond GLMs (or failure modes when bounds are loose) would help.
- Overhead vs. dimension: Computing p̂ costs O(n log n) every iteration. For very large n and small d, this may dominate the per-iteration cost. Can p̂ be updated incrementally (e.g., with heaps or lazy updates) to amortize the overhead? Empirical scaling on truly large n (without subsampling) would strengthen the case.
- SGD results are modest: The approach yields clear gains for CD but only small improvements for SGD in the presented setups. It would be helpful to investigate minibatching, alternative bound constructions for gradient norms, or tasks where per-component gradient norms are more skewed.
- Comparisons: While the paper cites relevant adaptive schemes, experimental comparisons to recent adaptive CD methods (e.g., gap-based sampling, AISTATS’17) or to practical stale/upper-bound approximations in SGD would contextualize gains. Also, variance-reduced methods (SVRG/SAGA) with non-uniform sampling could be a useful baseline.
- Theory presentation: Convergence rates for the safe scheme are deferred to the appendix; a concise main-text theorem for strongly convex and convex cases would improve clarity. The min–max interchange relies on Sion/Komiya—stating the exact conditions assumed on C_k and V would help the reader.
- Minor clarity issues: Occasional typos and notational slips; the introduction could better contrast with “approximate” gradient-based schemes that may underperform uniform, to position why “safe” matters.

Quality
The technical development appears sound: the optimality of √L-weighted probabilities, min–max safety, and the O(n log n) solver are well-motivated and (as stated) proved in the appendix. The GLM bound-tracking is standard and correct. Experiments are reasonable and align with theory (clear CD improvements; small SGD gains). Some baselines and scalability tests could be expanded.

Clarity
Generally well-organized, with a clean separation of ideal (full-info) vs. safe (partial-info) settings. Algorithm 4 is intuitive with the thresholding rule. A bit more exposition on the min–max interchange and a concise rate statement in the main text would aid readability. Typos should be cleaned.

Originality
High. The robust min–max formulation for adaptive sampling under uncertainty, together with the safety guarantee and efficient solver, is a fresh and useful perspective that differs from prior heuristics or fixed-importance rules.

Significance
Moderate to high, especially for CD on large sparse problems and for scenarios where safe bounds are available or can be cheaply maintained (including distributed settings). The always-better-than-fixed guarantee and near-optimal behavior when bounds tighten are attractive. Impact on SGD seems more situational.

Pros
- New safe min–max formulation and efficient solver
- Strong guarantees; recovers optimal sampling when possible
- Practical for GLMs; clear CD speedups with small overhead
- Insightful analysis (competitive ratio, geometry)

Cons
- Limited demonstration beyond GLMs; unclear for deep models
- O(n log n) per-iteration overhead may hinder very large-n use
- Modest SGD gains; limited comparative baselines
- Key convergence results relegated to appendix; minor clarity issues

Recommendation
Accept (borderline strong). The core idea is novel and broadly useful, with solid theory and promising empirical gains for CD. Even with some experimental gaps and scope limitations, the “safe adaptive sampling via min–max” concept is a valuable contribution likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Reviewer confidence
High on optimization/sampling; medium on breadth of empirical comparisons.
