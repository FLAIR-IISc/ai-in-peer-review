This paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD), aiming to provide theoretical guarantees while remaining computationally efficient. The core idea is to maintain safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient entries and use these bounds to formulate a convex optimization problem for determining the sampling distribution. The authors demonstrate theoretical improvements over fixed sampling strategies and provide empirical validation on several datasets.

While the paper addresses a relevant and important problem – efficient adaptive sampling – and presents a novel approach, the level of detail and rigor is surprisingly inconsistent, and the paper is riddled with minor (but cumulatively significant) issues. My overall assessment is that the paper, despite its potentially interesting ideas, falls short of NIPS standards due to a lack of polish and numerous technical shortcomings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Adaptive importance sampling is a crucial topic for scaling machine learning algorithms to large datasets.
*   **Novel Approach:** The formulation based on safe gradient bounds is a potentially promising direction.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper attempts to provide theoretical guarantees, which is commendable.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in certain settings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistency:** The notation is inconsistent throughout the paper. For example, `∇if` is defined as the i-th component of the gradient vector, while in other places, it seems to represent the gradient along the i-th coordinate direction. The inconsistent use of brackets ([x]i) is particularly jarring.
*   **Lack of Clarity in Optimization Formulation:** The leap from the upper bound minimization in (3) to the optimization problem (6) is not clearly explained. The connection between the bounds `l` and `u` and the objective function is not well-motivated.
*   **Oversimplification in Algorithm 4:** Algorithm 4 relies on a condition (9) that is presented without sufficient justification. The claim of O(n log n) complexity is also not rigorously demonstrated—it seems dependent on specific implementation details and the properties of L.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. The focus is heavily on coordinate descent, and the SGD results are less compelling. A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art sampling methods (beyond uniform sampling) would be beneficial.
*   **Vague Claim of Efficiency:** The assertion that the overhead is "comparable with the cost to evaluate a single component of the gradient" is not always true, particularly for complex gradient computations. A more detailed cost analysis is needed.
*   **Unnecessary/Unclear Lemma 2.4:** The Lemma 2.4 seems extraneous and doesn’t clearly contribute to the main argument. Its role in the overall development is unclear.
*   **Typographical Errors and Grammatical Mistakes:** The paper contains numerous minor errors (e.g., “Li-Lipschitz continuous gradients1”, “Tr[L]” instead of “trace(L)”), which detract from its professionalism.
*   **Missing Details in Experimental Setup:** Crucial details regarding the experimental setup are missing. For example, how were the initial bounds `l` and `u` chosen? How was the learning rate scheduled?
*   **Weak Connection to Related Work:** The related work section, while extensive, lacks critical discussion. The paper doesn't adequately position itself within the broader landscape of adaptive sampling methods.

**Specific Comments:**

*   Equation (1) is a standard result but isn’t clearly linked to the coordinate-wise smoothness assumption.
*   The claim that αk does not depend on the chosen direction ik in the CD method is misleading. It depends on pk, which depends on the gradient, and therefore indirectly on the direction.
*   The notation in Section 2.3, particularly the definition of vk, is unclear.
*   The discussion of the competitive ratio (ρk) is not fully developed and lacks concrete interpretations.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in machine learning.
*   The proposed approach is novel and potentially beneficial.
*   The empirical results are promising.

**Con:**

*   The paper lacks rigor and polish.
*   The technical details are often unclear or incomplete.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited.
*   Numerous minor errors detract from the paper's credibility.
*   The benefits over existing methods are not convincingly demonstrated in all settings.

**Overall Recommendation:**

**Reject.** 

While the core idea of the paper is intriguing, the numerous technical flaws and inconsistencies prevent it from reaching the quality standards expected for NIPS. The paper requires significant revision to address the points raised above. In its current state, it reads more like a preliminary draft than a finished scientific contribution. I would encourage the authors to address the issues raised in this review and resubmit a more polished and rigorous version of the paper in the future. The potential of the idea deserves further investigation, but a substantial amount of work is required to bring it to fruition.
