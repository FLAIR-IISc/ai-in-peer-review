

# Peer Review: "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in stochastic optimization: how to design adaptive importance sampling strategies for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) that are both theoretically justified and computationally efficient. While adaptive sampling based on gradient information can theoretically outperform fixed sampling strategies (e.g., uniform or Li-based importance sampling), previous approaches either require full gradient computations in each iteration (making them computationally prohibitive) or lack theoretical guarantees. 

The authors propose a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method that uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to compute an adaptive sampling distribution efficiently. The key innovation is formulating the sampling problem as a convex optimization problem over the safe bounds, ensuring the proposed sampling is provably better than fixed importance sampling while being computationally efficient. The paper provides theoretical guarantees, an efficient algorithm for computing the sampling distribution (with O(n log n) complexity), and empirical validation on generalized linear models with L1 and L2 regularization.

This work builds on previous research on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 23, 36, 37]) but addresses the critical computational bottleneck of requiring full gradient computations. It also relates to work on safe screening [14, 15] and adaptive sampling strategies [5, 21, 23, 26], but offers a novel approach that provides both theoretical guarantees and computational efficiency.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-justified. The authors establish that their proposed sampling is provably better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2) and provide a competitive ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5-3.6).
- The computational complexity analysis is strong, showing that the proposed method has O(n log n) complexity per iteration, which is comparable to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component for many problems.
- The paper carefully distinguishes between the ideal full-information case (which requires full gradient computation) and their practical safe adaptive approach, providing a clear theoretical bridge between the two.
- The empirical evaluation is comprehensive for the problem class studied (GLMs), showing significant improvements over fixed sampling strategies in both CD and SGD.

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the GLM case for the safe gradient bounds. The paper claims the method is "completely generic" but provides no evidence for other problem classes beyond GLMs. It would strengthen the paper to include at least one additional problem type where the safe gradient bounds can be efficiently maintained.
- The paper does not provide convergence rate guarantees for the proposed method (only for the idealized full-information case in the appendix). This is a significant gap since the main contribution is a practical algorithm, and convergence rates are crucial for optimization methods.
- The relationship between the proposed method and the ideal gradient-based sampling is not clearly articulated in the main text (the paper defers to the appendix for this explanation).
- The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.6) relies on a relative accuracy condition that may not hold in practice for many problems, limiting the practical utility of the theoretical bound.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement, theoretical contributions, and empirical validation are logically presented.
- The key insights and contributions are clearly articulated in the introduction and summarized in the abstract.
- The algorithmic descriptions (Algorithms 1-4) are precise and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
- The connection between the theoretical guarantees and the practical implementation is sometimes unclear. For example, the paper states that the method "recovers the gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting" but doesn't explain how this happens in practice.
- Some technical details (like the derivation of the competitive ratio bound) are deferred to the appendix without sufficient guidance for the reader.
- The empirical section could better explain why the performance gains are more pronounced for CD than for SGD (the paper notes this but doesn't provide a thorough explanation).

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The approach of using safe upper and lower bounds to compute an adaptive sampling distribution in a theoretically guaranteed way is novel. Previous work either used approximations without guarantees [22, 36, 37] or relied on full gradient computation [23].
- The optimization formulation (7) for the sampling distribution is a significant contribution that provides a principled way to handle limited gradient information.
- The paper's focus on the "safety" aspect (guaranteeing performance never worse than fixed sampling) distinguishes it from other adaptive sampling approaches.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from related work on adaptive sampling with bounds (e.g., [36] and [23]). While the authors cite these works, they don't clearly explain how their method improves upon them.
- The claim that the method is "completely generic" is not adequately supported by the experimental validation, which is limited to GLMs.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in optimization algorithms that is directly relevant to many machine learning applications.
- The method has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of CD and SGD methods for large-scale problems, with minimal computational overhead.
- The empirical results demonstrate clear practical benefits over fixed sampling strategies, which are commonly used in practice.

**Weaknesses:**
- The significance is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of the experimental validation (only GLMs). If the method only works well for GLMs, its broader impact on the machine learning community would be limited.
- The paper doesn't clearly articulate how the proposed method would fit into existing optimization frameworks (e.g., how it would interact with other acceleration techniques).

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written and technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical guarantees are strong, the computational efficiency is well-justified, and the empirical results are convincing for the problem class studied. The main limitations are the narrow scope of the experimental validation and the lack of convergence rate guarantees for the proposed method.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The problem addressed is important and well-motivated
- The theoretical contributions are significant and well-supported
- The method is computationally efficient with a clear practical benefit
- The empirical results are convincing for the problem class studied
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The experimental validation is limited to GLMs, and it's unclear how broadly applicable the method is
- The paper lacks convergence rate guarantees for the proposed practical algorithm
- The relationship to prior work on adaptive sampling with bounds could be clearer
- The claim of "completeness" is not adequately supported by the empirical results

## Recommendations

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, and the main weaknesses can be addressed with reasonable effort. Specifically:

1. The authors should provide at least one additional empirical example beyond GLMs to demonstrate broader applicability of the method.
2. The paper should include convergence rate guarantees for the proposed practical algorithm (not just the idealized full-information case).
3. The authors should clarify the relationship between their method and prior work on adaptive sampling with bounds, explicitly stating how their approach improves upon previous methods.
4. The connection between the theoretical guarantees and practical implementation should be made clearer throughout the paper.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a significant contribution to a fundamental problem in optimization with clear theoretical and practical implications. With the minor revisions suggested above, it would be an excellent addition to the conference proceedings.

## Specific Comments

1. In Section 3.2, Theorem 3.2 claims that the proposed sampling is always better than Li-based sampling, but the paper doesn't clearly explain why this is significant compared to prior work. It would be helpful to explicitly compare with previous approaches that claim similar guarantees but may lack computational efficiency.

2. The paper states that "the proposed sampling is the best possible with respect to the limited information at hand" (Section 3), but doesn't provide a formal proof of this statement. It would strengthen the paper to include this proof.

3. In Figure 2, the paper compares fixed stepsize vs. optimal stepsize, but doesn't clearly explain why the "big" stepsize performs better. This is important because the proposed method relies on the optimal stepsize.

4. The paper claims the method "can easily be added as an improvement to both CD and SGD type methods," but doesn't provide concrete examples of how to integrate it into existing frameworks. This would be valuable for practitioners.

5. The reference to "safe screening" [14, 15] is mentioned but not properly connected to the proposed method. It would be helpful to clarify the relationship between the two concepts.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to optimization for machine learning. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
