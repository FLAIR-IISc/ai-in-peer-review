This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The central problem addressed is that optimal adaptive sampling (e.g., gradient-based) is computationally prohibitive as it requires full gradient information at each iteration, while practical approximations lack theoretical guarantees and can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors' main contribution is to formalize the selection of a sampling distribution in a robust optimization framework. Assuming access to per-coordinate lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes (`l_k` and `u_k`), they propose choosing a distribution `p_k` that minimizes the worst-case value of a key term in the convergence analysis, taken over all possible true gradients consistent with the bounds. This min-max problem yields a "safe" sampling distribution that is provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., based on Lipschitz constants). The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute this distribution. The authors also demonstrate how to obtain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and present empirical results on several real-world datasets showing improvements over fixed sampling schemes.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary strength of this work is its principled approach. Instead of relying on heuristics like using stale gradients or simply plugging in upper bounds, the paper formulates the problem of choosing a sampling distribution under uncertainty as a min-max optimization problem (Eq. 7). This is a well-founded and rigorous way to proceed. The resulting theoretical guarantee (Theorem 3.2) that the proposed sampling is provably superior to Li-based importance sampling is a significant contribution. This "safety net" addresses a critical weakness of many prior adaptive sampling methods, which could degrade performance.

2.  **Efficiency:** The proposed method would be of limited practical value if the optimization problem were expensive to solve. The authors provide an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the safe sampling distribution, making the per-iteration overhead manageable, particularly for problems where the feature dimension `d` is large compared to `n`.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The theoretical development is easy to follow, and the connection between the convergence analysis and the proposed optimization problem is well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Quality of Bounds:** The entire framework is predicated on the availability of tight and efficiently maintainable lower and lower bounds (`l`, `u`) on the gradient information. The paper provides a method for GLMs based on the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality (Section 4). This is a standard and rather loose bounding technique. While the experiments show good performance even with these loose bounds, the paper does not sufficiently explore the sensitivity of the method to the quality of these bounds. For problem classes beyond GLMs, obtaining such bounds might be difficult or computationally expensive, limiting the method's applicability. The claim that "very loose safe gradient bounds are sufficient" feels more like an empirical observation than a rigorously established fact.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Comparison:** The experimental validation, while showing clear benefits over uniform sampling, is incomplete. The primary baselines are uniform sampling (a non-adaptive method) and optimal full-information sampling (an impractical oracle). The paper criticizes prior work that uses approximations (e.g., based on stale values or upper bounds), but it fails to include them as baselines in the experiments. A compelling demonstration of superiority would require comparison against established, practical adaptive methods. For instance, a simple and common heuristic is to form a sampling distribution using only the upper bounds `u_k`. Comparing against such a baseline is crucial for assessing the practical value of solving the more complex min-max problem.

3.  **Underwhelming SGD Performance:** The empirical gains for SGD are marginal compared to those for CD (Figures 7-9). The authors note this discrepancy but offer little analysis as to why. This observation weakens the paper's claim of providing a generic improvement for both CD and SGD-type methods. If the benefit is primarily for CD, the paper should be framed more explicitly in that context, with a deeper investigation into why the benefits do not readily transfer to the tested SGD settings.

4.  **Novelty of Stepsize:** The paper introduces an adaptive stepsize strategy for CD (Section 2.1) that is coupled with the sampling distribution. While effective, this style of update is standard in the analysis of SGD with importance sampling. Its presentation as a novel contribution for CD could be more clearly situated within the existing literature.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper introduces a theoretically sound and elegant method for adaptive importance sampling. The core contribution—a robust min-max formulation that provides a "safety" guarantee—is a notable improvement over prior heuristic approaches. The work is of high technical quality and is well-presented.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by concerns about the practical validation and scope. The method's performance is fundamentally tied to the quality of gradient bounds, and its general applicability beyond the class of problems discussed is unclear. Most importantly, the empirical evaluation lacks comparisons to other practical adaptive sampling baselines, making it difficult to judge the true state-of-the-art advance. The weak results for SGD also raise questions about the generality of the approach.

While the theoretical foundation is strong, the paper would be significantly improved by a more comprehensive empirical study that includes stronger baselines and a deeper analysis of the method's sensitivity to its core assumptions. Given the solid theoretical contribution, I lean towards acceptance, but the authors should be strongly encouraged to address the weaknesses in the final version.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a principled, robust optimization framework for adaptive sampling.
*   Provides a strong theoretical guarantee of being "safe," which is a key advantage over previous work.
*   The proposed algorithm is computationally efficient.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation is weak; it lacks comparisons to relevant practical adaptive sampling methods.
*   The method's utility is highly dependent on the availability of tight gradient bounds, which is not explored in depth.
*   The observed benefit for SGD is minimal, questioning the claimed generality of the method.
