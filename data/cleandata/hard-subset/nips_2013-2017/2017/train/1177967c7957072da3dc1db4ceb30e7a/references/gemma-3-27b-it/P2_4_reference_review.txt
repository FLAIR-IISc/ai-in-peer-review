## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The key idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling in a way that is computationally efficient and comes with theoretical guarantees. The authors demonstrate that their scheme is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling and recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting. They present a practical algorithm that relies on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient and show its effectiveness on real datasets.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established literature on importance sampling for reducing computational cost in optimization algorithms. It acknowledges the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and computational feasibility of existing adaptive strategies like gradient-based sampling.  It correctly positions itself against prior work, such as those by Zhao & Zhang, Csiba et al., and Richtarik et al., highlighting the limitations of existing approaches and offering a solution that addresses them.  The connection to duality gap-based sampling for CD is also appropriately noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating the improvement over fixed sampling and characterizing the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm. The Lemma 2.1 and subsequent examples showcase a clear understanding of the benefits of adaptive sampling in CD.
* **Practicality:** The algorithm presented is computationally efficient, requiring only O(n log n) time per iteration (assuming efficient bound tracking), making it potentially suitable for large-scale datasets. The discussion on tracking gradient bounds in GLMs is particularly insightful.
* **Safety Guarantee:** The key strength is the “safe” nature of the approximation. By relying on safe bounds, the algorithm guarantees performance at least as good as fixed sampling, a crucial property in practical applications.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is standard and the key ideas are explained in a relatively accessible manner.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed scheme, especially for coordinate descent. The comparison with uniform and gradient-based sampling is informative.

**Weaknesses:**

* **SGD Results Less Compelling:** The experimental results for SGD are less dramatic than those for CD. While the method doesn't *hurt* performance, the gains are relatively small. This weakens the overall impact of the paper. More analysis on why the improvement is less pronounced in SGD would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The reliance on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient necessitates an additional tracking step, and the performance of the algorithm is sensitive to the quality of these bounds. While the paper provides a method for GLMs, it doesn’t thoroughly explore the challenges of obtaining these bounds in other settings.
* **Limited Novelty in Bounds Tracking:**  The method for tracking bounds in GLMs builds on existing techniques (using the change in gradients due to coordinate updates). While presented clearly, it doesn’t represent a breakthrough in bound estimation itself.
* **Competitive Ratio Analysis Complex:** The discussion of the competitive ratio (Lemmas 3.5 & 3.6) feels somewhat detached from the core message of the paper and could be simplified or made more intuitive.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. Claims are generally well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The work is complete in the sense that it provides a fully defined algorithm and analysis. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., dependence on gradient bounds).
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written and organized. However, the mathematical notation can be dense in places. Providing more intuitive explanations alongside the equations would improve accessibility.
* **Originality:** The idea of formulating adaptive sampling as a convex optimization problem with safety constraints is novel. While building on existing concepts, the paper presents a unique combination of techniques. The related work section is comprehensive.
* **Significance:** The results are important, particularly for CD-based optimization. The potential for reducing computational cost without sacrificing performance is significant, especially for large-scale problems. While the SGD results are less compelling, the overall contribution is valuable.


**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Strong theoretical foundations and guarantees.
* Computationally efficient and practical algorithm.
* Novel approach to adaptive sampling with a safety guarantee.
* Demonstrably effective on real datasets, especially for coordinate descent.

**Against:**

* Less impressive results for SGD.
* Dependence on accurate gradient bounds.
* Limited novelty in bound tracking methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**.  The theoretical contributions are solid, the algorithm is well-designed and practical, and the experimental results are convincing, particularly for CD. While the SGD results are less striking, they do not invalidate the overall contribution. The safety guarantee is a significant advantage, and the clear presentation makes the paper accessible to researchers in the field.  The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not outweigh the strengths. 

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I would encourage the authors to expand on the discussion of gradient bound tracking techniques for more complex models and to investigate the reasons for the limited improvement in the SGD experiments.
