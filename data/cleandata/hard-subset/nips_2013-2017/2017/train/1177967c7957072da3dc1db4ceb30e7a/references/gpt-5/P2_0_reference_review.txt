Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for first-order methods, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). With full gradient information, it revisits and strengthens the case for gradient-based adaptive sampling: for smooth coordinate-wise problems, sampling i with probability proportional to √Li |∇i f(x)| and using a coupled stepsize yields strictly better expected one-step decrease than fixed (Li-based) or uniform sampling and can achieve up to an n-fold speedup in extreme cases.

The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive scheme when full gradients are unavailable. The authors assume per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds on the per-coordinate gradient magnitudes (CD) or per-component gradient norms (SGD): ` ≤ |c| ≤ u. They pose the selection of the sampling distribution as a min–max problem: choose p to minimize the worst-case upper bound on expected progress over all c in the uncertainty set. They show (i) this distribution is never worse than fixed Li-based sampling in the worst case, (ii) it coincides with the full-information optimal sampling when the bounds are tight, and (iii) there is an O(n log n) algorithm (via sorting and a thresholding structure) to compute the distribution and a matched stepsize α = 1/v, where v is the min–max value. They provide a competitive ratio analysis versus the full-information oracle sampling. The paper also shows how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs) in both CD and SGD using simple curvature arguments and Cauchy–Schwarz. Experiments on standard text datasets demonstrate strong gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD, and highlight the importance of the coupled adaptive stepsize.

This work builds on and distinguishes itself from prior adaptive sampling for SGD (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, Needell et al.) and for CD/gap-based sampling (e.g., Perekrestenko et al., Csiba et al., Osokin et al.) by providing a principled min–max formulation with a worst-case guarantee versus fixed sampling under limited information, and an efficient exact algorithm for the resulting distribution.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical insight: the expected progress bound is optimized jointly over p and α, cleanly recovering known full-information optima and quantifying potential n-fold gains.
- Novel “safe” min–max formulation under gradient uncertainty, with a provable guarantee: never worse than fixed importance sampling and best possible in worst-case sense given the uncertainty set.
- Efficient algorithmic solution (O(n log n)) via a simple thresholding structure; transparent dependence on Li and the bounds.
- Practicality: concrete recipes to maintain safe bounds for GLMs in both CD and SGD with O(n) per-iteration updates, and empirical validation on multiple datasets.
- Honest discussion: the paper acknowledges smaller gains for SGD and emphasizes the role of adaptive stepsizes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Computational overhead: the per-iteration cost to update bounds (O(n)) and compute the sampling (O(n log n)) is substantial. The claim that this is negligible when d = Ω(n) is situational; in many problems d << n, making the overhead dominant. The experiments suggest feasibility on the chosen datasets, but broader scalability is uncertain. Periodic updates or amortization strategies are not explored.
- Guarantees versus fixed sampling: Theorem 3.2(ii) shows “never worse than” Li-based in worst case; the claim of “provably better” depends on non-degenerate bounds and may be loose in early iterations with very wide intervals. The paper would benefit from a clearer statement distinguishing “never worse” (guarantee) from “often better” (empirical).
- Limited baselines: For CD, comparisons omit strong deterministic rules (Gauss–Southwell variants), recent adaptive/gap-based heuristics with stale gradients, and accelerated CD with non-uniform sampling. For SGD, comparisons to variance-reduced methods or modern importance sampling variants are absent. This weakens the empirical case for broad significance.
- SGD theory and practice: While the formulation carries over to SGD, the reported gains are small. The paper could better delimit when the approach is likely to help SGD (e.g., heterogeneity in component gradient norms, quality of bounds).
- Clarity/details: Some derivations (e.g., min–max interchange and optimality conditions) are deferred; Algorithm 4 is terse. A brief intuitive derivation in the main text would aid readability.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: The technical core appears sound. The expected decrease model, optimal p and α in full info, min–max formulation, and the O(n log n) algorithm are well-justified. The competitive ratio bounds are informative albeit somewhat loose and reliant on relative accuracy of bounds. The empirical study supports the claims for CD but is limited in baselines and scale.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Notation is dense but standard. Algorithms and the role of vk/αk are explained. Some steps (min–max swap, threshold rule) could be sketched in the main text for self-containment.
- Originality: The safe min–max sampling under uncertainty and its efficient exact solution are, to my knowledge, new in this context. The coupling with stepsize and worst-case dominance over fixed sampling are also novel.
- Significance: For CD on GLMs and similar problems where gradient bounds can be tracked cheaply and are informative, this has potential impact. The “never worse” guarantee is attractive. For SGD, the benefit appears limited in practice. The O(n log n) overhead may impede adoption unless amortized or approximated.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the sampling distribution be updated less frequently (e.g., every M steps) with negligible loss, reducing overhead to O((n log n)/M) on average?
- How does the method behave with sparse features and incremental bound updates that exploit sparsity?
- Can the authors compare to Gauss–Southwell-Lipschitz and gap-safe adaptive sampling baselines?
- Please clarify in the main text the min–max interchange (assumptions for Sion/Komiya) and provide a brief intuition for the threshold structure in Theorem 3.4.
- For SGD, can mini-batch variants and importance sampling within a batch be combined with your safe scheme?

Arguments for acceptance
- Solid theoretical contribution with a clear, principled formulation and efficient algorithm.
- Strong empirical gains for CD on real datasets.
- General idea (safe adaptive sampling under uncertainty) is broadly applicable and likely to inspire follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overhead may be prohibitive in many regimes; practicality beyond tested datasets is unclear.
- Empirical section lacks comparisons to several strong baselines; SGD gains are modest.
- Some claims about “provably better” could be softened or clarified.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a neat and principled advance in adaptive importance sampling with meaningful guarantees and convincing CD results, though practicality and empirical breadth could be strengthened.
