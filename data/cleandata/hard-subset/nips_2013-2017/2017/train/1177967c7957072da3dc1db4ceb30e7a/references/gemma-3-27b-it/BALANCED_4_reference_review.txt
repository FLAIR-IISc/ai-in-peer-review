This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, aiming to improve computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees compared to existing approaches. The core idea is to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling while maintaining provable advantages over uniform sampling, and recovering the full gradient-based sampling in ideal settings. The authors provide theoretical analysis, algorithm design, and empirical validation on several datasets.

**Summary of Contributions:**

1.  Theoretical justification for gradient-based sampling in CD, showing potential speed-ups.
2.  A generic and efficient adaptive importance sampling strategy applicable to both CD and SGD.
3.  Integration of the proposed scheme into CD and SGD for structured optimization problems.
4.  Empirical demonstration of the scheme's performance on real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis justifying the benefits of adaptive sampling, particularly for coordinate descent. The connection to gradient-based sampling and the demonstration of improvements over uniform sampling are valuable contributions.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed "safe" adaptive importance sampling is efficiently computable and addresses the drawbacks of directly implementing gradient-based sampling (computational cost, lack of guarantees).
*   **Generality:** The method is presented as a generic improvement applicable to both CD and SGD, broadening its potential impact.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed scheme on various datasets and with different regularization techniques, supporting the theoretical findings. The inclusion of timing results alongside accuracy is helpful.
*   **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is well-organized, and the notation is generally clear. The algorithms are presented in a concise and understandable manner.
*   **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge and address the limitations of their approach, such as the overhead introduced by computing the safe bounds and the varying performance between CD and SGD.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions about Safe Bounds:**  While the paper emphasizes the importance of safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient, the practical application relies on efficiently *obtaining* these bounds. The discussion in Section 4, while reasonable, feels somewhat hand-wavy and doesn't fully address the challenges of obtaining tight and efficient bounds for more complex models.  More discussion of the practical limitations and potential difficulties in generating these bounds would be valuable.
*   **SGD Results Less Compelling:** The empirical results for SGD show only marginal improvements over uniform sampling, which contradicts the theoretical benefits presented. A more in-depth investigation into why the scheme is less effective in the SGD setting is needed. Is it due to the choice of parameters, the specific datasets, or inherent limitations of the method in the stochastic setting?
*   **Limited Comparison to Existing Adaptive Methods:**  The paper doesn't extensively compare its approach to other existing adaptive sampling methods (e.g., those based on duality gaps [5, 23] mentioned in the introduction). A more thorough comparison would help position the contribution more clearly within the existing literature.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** The statement regarding the computational complexity (O(n log n) per iteration) could benefit from further clarification.  The dependence on the sorting algorithm and its impact on the overall runtime are not fully explained.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** Lemma 3.6's condition on γ could benefit from further explanation, as it is a relatively specific requirement.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound adaptive importance sampling scheme.
*   The proposed method is practical and addresses a significant challenge in large-scale optimization.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach in the CD setting.
*   The work has the potential to significantly impact the efficiency of coordinate descent and stochastic gradient descent algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The performance gains in the SGD setting are limited and require further investigation.
*   The reliance on safe gradient bounds and the lack of detailed discussion on their efficient computation could limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The comparison to existing adaptive sampling methods is not exhaustive.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the proposed algorithm is both practical and general. While the results for SGD are less compelling, the strong performance in the CD setting and the potential for improvement make this a worthwhile contribution. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and could be addressed with further research and refinement. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion on the practical challenges of obtaining safe gradient bounds for more complex models and provide guidance on appropriate strategies.
2.  Investigate the reasons for the limited performance gains in the SGD setting and consider exploring alternative parameter settings or modifications to the algorithm.
3.  Provide a more comprehensive comparison to other existing adaptive sampling methods.
4.  Clarify the computational complexity analysis, specifically the dependence on the sorting algorithm.
5.  Elaborate on the condition in Lemma 3.6 to make it more readily understandable.
