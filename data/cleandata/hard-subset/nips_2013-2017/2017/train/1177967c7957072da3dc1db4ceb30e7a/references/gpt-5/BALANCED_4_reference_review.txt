Summary
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to choose per-iteration sampling probabilities and stepsizes that maximize an upper bound on expected one-step progress, but under limited information: only safe lower/upper bounds on (absolute) coordinate gradients (for CD) or component gradient norms (for SGD) are available. The authors formulate a minimax problem over distributions and all gradients consistent with these bounds, prove that the resulting distribution is never worse than fixed importance sampling (Li-based) and recovers gradient-based sampling when full information is available, and provide an O(n log n) algorithm to compute the probabilities. They also propose a stepsize tied to the minimax value. Experiments on GLMs with L1/L2 regularization show substantial gains over uniform/fixed sampling for CD and modest gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
- Fixed importance sampling for CD and SGD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Qu, Richtárik, Zhang) is a baseline. This work improves it adaptively.
- Gradient-based adaptive sampling (e.g., Zhao & Zhang; Zhu; Perekrestenko et al.) can be optimal but requires full gradients or stale/approximate estimates; guarantees often do not dominate uniform. This work provides a worst-case optimal distribution given bounds, with a guarantee of never underperforming fixed sampling.
- Gauss–Southwell/steepest CD (e.g., Nutini et al., Approximate Steepest CD) relates to “full-information” sampling; the proposed scheme interpolates between fixed and steepest depending on bound tightness.
- Screening rules (e.g., Gap Safe) are orthogonal but relevant for maintaining bounds; authors cite them.

Strengths
- Clear, principled minimax formulation of adaptive sampling under uncertainty. The guarantee “always better than fixed Li-based sampling” is compelling and, to my knowledge, novel.
- Efficient algorithm (O(n log n)) for computing the safe probabilities/stepsize; the construction is intuitive (clipping to bounds or to a common threshold) and reminiscent of water-filling.
- Sound analysis: one-step progress bounds, properties of the safe sampling, competitive ratio bounds, and recovery of gradient-based sampling in the full-information limit.
- Practical integration for GLMs: simple, safe bound updates via Cauchy–Schwarz with O(n) maintenance; experiments show good improvements for CD in both iterations and wall time.
- The adaptive stepsize coupled to the sampling is well-motivated and empirically important.

Weaknesses
- Overhead vs. benefit trade-off: per-iteration O(n) bound updates plus O(n log n) probability computation can be substantial in sparse high-dimensional settings, especially when coordinate updates are cheap (small d and/or very sparse A). The paper argues this is mild when computing a single coordinate gradient is Θ(d) with d = Ω(n), but many ML problems have d << n and sparse features.
- Bounds can be very loose (Cauchy–Schwarz on bag-of-words), potentially making the minimax stepsize conservative. While experiments still show gains, the method’s sensitivity to bound tightness is not systematically studied.
- SGD results show limited improvements over uniform; analysis for when and why improvements should be expected in finite-sum settings is light, and baselines (e.g., variance-reduced methods with importance sampling, AdaSDCA) are not compared.
- Empirical setup choices (binarizing features, fixed λ=0.1, subsampled datasets) could bias results; justification and sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Limited discussion of interaction with acceleration (e.g., ACDM non-uniform sampling), block-CD, and mini-batch sampling; it would be useful to clarify compatibility and potential gains.

Quality
The theoretical claims appear technically sound, with clear lemmas and a constructive algorithm. Some key proofs are deferred to the appendix but seem standard (minimax via Sion’s theorem, optimality conditions). The empirical evaluation supports the main claims for CD but is less compelling for SGD. Complexity claims are reasonable but could use more nuanced discussion for sparse scenarios.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. The derivation of the minimax problem, geometric interpretation, and algorithm are well explained. Some notation is heavy; a high-level restatement of Algorithm 4’s intuition would help. The experimental section could better justify dataset preprocessing and include more detailed runtime accounting.

Originality
Formulating and solving the worst-case optimal sampling under safe bounds, with guarantees strictly dominating fixed sampling, seems novel and a meaningful advance over prior adaptive sampling based on stale or heuristic estimates.

Significance
For CD on convex GLMs, this is a practical and theoretically justified improvement likely to be adopted, especially when gradient information can be cheaply bounded or tracked. The impact for SGD appears more modest; further work tying the approach to variance reduction or stronger per-iteration gains could raise significance.

Pros
- Novel, principled adaptive sampling under uncertainty with strict domination guarantee.
- Efficient algorithm; recovers gradient-based sampling with full information.
- Strong improvements for CD; simple integration for GLMs.

Cons
- Overhead may be nontrivial in sparse/cheap-update regimes.
- Gains for SGD are small; limited comparison to modern baselines.
- Sensitivity to bound tightness not thoroughly evaluated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations on bound tightness (e.g., tighter bounds via curvature tracking vs. Cauchy–Schwarz) and their effect on vk, accuracy, and time.
- Present detailed runtime breakdowns (bound maintenance, sorting, sampling, gradient eval) and experiments on sparse datasets with varying d/n to map the overhead regime.
- Compare against additional adaptive baselines: Gauss–Southwell (and approximate versions), AdaSDCA/adaptive probabilities, stale-gradient sampling, and ACDM with non-uniform sampling.
- Explore compatibility with acceleration and mini-batching; analyze if the safe scheme can be combined with SVRG/SAGA-style variance reduction.
- Justify preprocessing choices (binarization, λ), include sensitivity analyses, and evaluate on unmodified standard datasets.
- For SGD, provide theory indicating when improvements are expected (e.g., heterogeneous component Lipschitz/variance structure) and design experiments accordingly.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a clean and theoretically strong contribution with practical relevance for CD. While the empirical scope could be broader and the SGD impact is limited, the core idea and guarantee are valuable and likely to influence subsequent work.

Confidence
High.
