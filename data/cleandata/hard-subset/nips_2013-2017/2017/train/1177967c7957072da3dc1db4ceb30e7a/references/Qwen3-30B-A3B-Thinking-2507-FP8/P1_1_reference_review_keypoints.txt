- Proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for CD and SGD that avoids full gradient computation while providing theoretical guarantees.  
- Key contributions: (1) shows adaptive sampling can give up to n-fold speedups for CD, (2) develops an efficient adaptive sampling strategy using limited gradient info, (3) integrates the strategy into CD and SGD for structured problems, (4) validates empirically on real datasets.  
- Key insight: formulate optimal sampling as an optimization problem using safe upper/lower gradient bounds to guarantee improvements over fixed sampling.  
- Algorithmic complexity: the sampling distribution can be computed efficiently in O(n log n) time per iteration.  
- Empirical finding: substantial gains for coordinate descent, more modest benefits for SGD.  

- Strength: Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous proofs (Theorems 3.2 and 3.4) and an elegant optimization formulation.  
- Strength: Practical contribution—easy integration into existing CD/SGD with minimal overhead comparable to a single gradient component.  
- Strength: Clear and comprehensive empirical validation across datasets, losses, and regularizers.  
- Strength: Careful distinction from prior adaptive sampling work, claiming a never-worse-than-fixed guarantee.  
- Strength: Practical method to compute safe gradient bounds for GLMs, making it widely applicable.  

- Weakness: Lacks theoretical explanation for why CD benefits far more than SGD empirically.  
- Weakness: Limited discussion of scalability to extremely large problems (e.g., billions of features) despite O(n log n) cost.  
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to some recent adaptive sampling methods that may address similar limitations.  
- Weakness: Empirical section omits implementation details on how safe gradient bounds and initial bounds were handled in practice.  
- Weakness: Does not provide a detailed convergence rate analysis for the proposed method.  

- Specific comment: Make the intuition clearer for how adaptive stepsizes can be up to n times larger than fixed sampling (Section 2.1).  
- Specific comment: Strengthen the geometric intuition for why the worst-case guarantee approach outperforms using extreme bounds (Section 3.1).  
- Specific comment: Discuss extending the safe gradient bound approach beyond GLMs to more complex models (Section 4).  
- Specific comment: Include comparisons to theoretical worst-case bounds in the "theoretical sampling quality" plots to show closeness to optimal.  
- Specific comment: Provide a deeper discussion explaining the weaker SGD results compared to CD (Section 5).  

- Overall assessment: Strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions; strengths outweigh minor weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  

- Arguments for acceptance: novel gradient-avoiding adaptive sampling with theoretical guarantees, provably better than fixed sampling, practical O(n log n) implementation, comprehensive experiments, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments for rejection: unexplained CD vs. SGD performance gap, insufficient comparisons to some recent methods, and limited scalability discussion.
