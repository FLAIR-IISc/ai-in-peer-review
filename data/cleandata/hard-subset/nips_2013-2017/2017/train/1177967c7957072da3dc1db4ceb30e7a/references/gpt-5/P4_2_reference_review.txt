Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). With full gradient information, the authors show that sampling coordinates proportionally to √Li|∇if(x)| (and using a stepsize jointly optimized with the sampling) maximizes the expected one-step decrease under the standard quadratic upper bound model, improving over fixed (Li-based) importance sampling by up to a factor n in special cases. This aligns with prior gradient-based sampling for SGD (e.g., Zhao & Zhang; later works on non-uniform/stale/upper-bound-based sampling).

The core contribution addresses the impracticality of computing full gradients each iteration. The authors propose a “safe” adaptive scheme that assumes per-iteration coordinate-wise safe lower and upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (or component-gradient norms for SGD). They formulate the choice of sampling distribution p as a min-max problem: choose p to minimize the worst-case normalized quadratic upper bound over all gradients consistent with the bounds. They prove (i) this choice has the best worst-case guarantee given the available information, (ii) it is never worse than fixed Li-based sampling, and (iii) it recovers the full-information optimal sampling when the bounds collapse to the true gradient. They also derive the associated optimal stepsize α = 1/vk from the min-max value.

The min-max problem admits an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (sorting-based thresholding) to compute p and the implicit worst-case gradient within the bounds. They also give worst-case competitive ratio bounds versus the full-information optimal sampling.

For GLMs with separable regularizers, they show how to maintain safe gradient bounds across iterations using simple updates (e.g., Cauchy–Schwarz bounds on inner products), at O(n) per iteration. Experiments on rcv1, real-sim, and news20 indicate substantial gains for CD versus uniform/Li-based sampling, with performance close to the full-information oracle and small overhead; SGD shows smaller gains, consistent with prior observations.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The one-step progress analysis under coordinate-wise smoothness is standard; the full-information optimal sampling and stepsize match prior results for SGD and extend naturally to CD. The safe scheme is formulated as a convex min-max problem over a box of feasible gradients; the use of minimax (Sion/Komiya) and KKT conditions yields a clear characterization and an efficient algorithm. The dominance over fixed sampling follows by feasibility. Competitive ratio bounds are reasonable (general bound via wk and a relative-accuracy-based γ^4 bound). Assumptions (convexity, coordinate-wise Lipschitz continuity) and the safety of gradient bounds are explicit.
- Experimental methodology: Datasets and tasks are standard; comparisons include uniform, fixed Li-based, proposed safe scheme, and full-information oracle. Results substantiate theory for CD; for SGD, improvements are modest as noted. Timing analyses show overhead can be small in sparse/high-dimensional settings.

Clarity
The paper is largely clear and well-organized. The derivation of the safe sampling (Theorems 3.2, 3.4) is concise, with proofs in the appendix. Algorithm 4 could benefit from more intuition in the main text (the thresholding/water-filling structure). Some notational slips and typos appear; the SGD variant could use a dedicated algorithm box analogous to CD. The rationale for the specific stepsize choice and its unbiasedness property is correct but could be explained more directly.

Originality
The robust (min-max) formulation for adaptive sampling under bounded gradient information, together with the guarantee of never being worse than fixed importance sampling and the O(n log n) exact algorithm, appears novel. Prior works used stale or upper-bound-based approximations without such worst-case optimality guarantees and could be worse than uniform. The joint optimization of stepsize and sampling and the reduction to the full-information optimal rule when bounds tighten are also appealing.

Significance
For CD on convex problems, the approach offers a principled and practical way to leverage partial gradient information with provable gains over fixed sampling, and performance close to the (impractical) oracle. The scheme is generic and could be adopted in various coordinate and finite-sum settings, including distributed scenarios where gradient bounds are tracked separately. The impact for SGD seems less pronounced empirically, which aligns with some prior findings; nevertheless, the safe guarantee is valuable.

Strengths
- Clear min-max formulation yielding a sampling that is worst-case optimal given available bounds.
- Provable dominance over fixed Li-based sampling; recovers full-information optimal sampling when possible.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm with a simple structure.
- Practical bounding strategies for GLMs; empirical gains for CD with small overhead.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on safe bounds: the proposed Cauchy–Schwarz-based bounds can be very loose; no quantitative study of how bound tightness affects vk or the competitive ratio, and no empirical tracking of γ or ρk.
- Overhead: O(n log n) per iteration can be significant when n is large and d ≪ n; limited exploration of scalability and amortization strategies.
- SGD: theoretical setup largely mirrors known results; empirical gains are small; conditions under which the safe scheme materially helps SGD are not characterized.
- Convergence rates are deferred; the main text focuses on one-step progress. Presenting full rates (convex/strongly convex) would strengthen the story.
- Comparisons: missing baselines using stale gradients or other adaptive approximations from prior work; datasets are limited to sparse text-like GLMs.

Suggestions
- Include convergence theorems with rates in the main text, with clear assumptions.
- Empirically quantify bound tightness and relate it to vk and observed speedups; report γ estimates during training.
- Add baselines using stale values/upper bounds from prior adaptive sampling works.
- Explore scaling with n and d, and strategies to amortize or partially update the sampling distribution.
- Provide a clear algorithm box for the SGD variant and clarify computational costs in that setting.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean and novel robust formulation with strong guarantees that strictly improve on fixed sampling.
- Practical, efficient algorithm; demonstrable gains for CD on standard benchmarks.
- General framework likely to influence adaptive sampling design in coordinate methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical scope and modest gains for SGD; unclear applicability beyond convex GLMs.
- Computational overhead may hinder adoption in very large-n or low-d settings; scalability analysis is limited.
- Some theoretical results (full convergence rates) are not featured in the main paper.

Overall, the paper offers a solid and principled contribution to adaptive importance sampling, particularly for CD, with a clear theoretical guarantee and practical algorithm.
