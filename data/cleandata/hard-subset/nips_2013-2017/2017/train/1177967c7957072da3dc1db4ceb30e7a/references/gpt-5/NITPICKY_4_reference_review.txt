Summary
The paper addresses adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). With full information, the “optimal” sampling is known to be gradient-based (proportional to |∇i|√Li for CD, or to ‖∇fi‖ for SGD), but is impractical. The authors propose a “safe” adaptive scheme computable with only interval bounds on magnitudes of gradient components (or component gradients in finite-sum SGD). They formulate a minimax problem over sampling distributions and a set C of feasible gradient magnitudes defined by lower/upper bounds, derive properties of the solution p̂ (always at least as good as fixed L-based sampling in the worst case), and give an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute p̂. They also propose an adaptive stepsize α = 1/vk where vk is the optimal value of the minimax problem, and show that with full information their scheme recovers gradient-based sampling. They demonstrate empirical gains for CD on GLMs; SGD benefits are modest.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on gradient-based adaptive sampling for SGD (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, Zhu), duality-gap/violation-based sampling for CD (e.g., Perekrestenko et al., Csiba et al.), and safe screening ideas (e.g., Gap Safe). The novelty is a worst-case optimal sampling under interval uncertainty, with a guarantee relative to fixed importance sampling and a computational scheme that is independent of problem structure except for bound updates.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: a clean minimax formulation to pick p̂ under uncertainty Ck, with a provable worst-case guarantee not worse than fixed Li-based sampling.
- Reduction to the known optimal sampling when full information is available.
- Computationally plausible O(n log n) solver for p̂ via sorting and thresholding, with a geometric interpretation.
- Generic applicability to CD and SGD; simple and elegant dependence of stepsize on vk.
- Empirical improvements for CD on several datasets even with very loose bounds.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and derivations:
- Equation (3) appears to be missing a factor 1/2 in the quadratic term; correspondingly, Lemma 2.1 and inequality (4) seem dimensionally and quantitatively inconsistent. Minimizing −α‖g‖2 + (α2/2)V yields α* = ‖g‖2/V and expected decrease ‖g‖4/(2V) = (α*/2)‖g‖2, not α*2‖g‖2 as stated. This affects subsequent comparisons and the interpretation of αk.
- Section 4 claims “∇2h(Ax) = In” for least-squares h(Ax) = 1/2‖Ax−b‖2. This is incorrect; ∇2f(x) = A⊤A. While equation (11) is consistent with A⊤A via ⟨ai, aik⟩, the stated Hessian identity is wrong (also dimensionally inconsistent).
- The min–max interchange needed for inequality (*) in (8) is nontrivial. The objective V(p, c)/‖c‖22 is convex in p but not concave in c (due to the denominator). The paper cites that details are in the appendix, but with the current text it is unclear whether conditions of Sion’s theorem hold after the transformations used. This casts doubt on Theorem 3.2’s proof sketch.
- Handling of signs: For CD the sampling depends on |∇i|, and Section 3 assumes c ≥ 0. The paper sometimes treats c as the true gradient and sometimes as magnitudes. This needs to be made consistent throughout (especially in (6)–(10) and Theorem 3.4).
- Algorithm 4 pseudocode is unclear and risks O(n2) if norms are recomputed at each inner iteration. It overloads symbols (` and u as both vectors and indices), and the update m := ‖c‖22 · ‖√Lc‖−11 is circular because c on undecided indices itself depends on m. The intended amortized O(n log n) procedure should be spelled out with explicit maintenance of partial sums to ensure linear-time per iteration of the outer loop.

Assumptions and practicality:
- The O(n) update per iteration to maintain bounds [ℓ, u] (by computing or bounding ⟨ai, aik⟩ for all i) is heavy for large n and undermines the appeal of CD’s O(1) per-iteration cost. The argument “when d = Ω(n), overhead reduces to O(log n)” is not compelling in typical sparse high-dimensional settings (often d ≪ n or data is extremely sparse). Using only Cauchy–Schwarz yields very loose bounds that may delay benefits and could degrade the competitive ratio in practice; more discussion/experiments are needed.
- For SGD, Lemma 4.1 relies on ∇2hi at an intermediate point; bounds require control of curvature and correlations for all i each iteration. The reported modest gains for SGD suggest the method may not be worthwhile there without stronger bounding machinery.

Empirical evaluation:
- Missing baselines: There is no comparison to other adaptive CD samplers beyond uniform and the intractable optimal (e.g., Gauss–Southwell variants, approximate GS, stale-gradient importance sampling, gap-based sampling; e.g., Nutini et al., Stich et al. 2017, Csiba–Richtárik).
- Experimental choices (bag-of-words with all nonzero entries set to 1, fixed λ=0.1 across datasets) are nonstandard and may bias correlations and the efficacy of bounds; no sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter tuning reported.
- Timing results are not strictly comparable across figures (different machines), and there is no breakdown of overhead for computing p̂ and maintaining bounds. No variance/error bars or multiple seeds are reported.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous typos and notation issues: “preformance,” “l.- and u.-bounds,” “vector l = (L1, ..., n)>,” “st Conference on…” line, inconsistent use of L, Li, l; footnote markers misused; several equations contain notational inaccuracies as noted above.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues) and inconsistently formatted; some important related work is not adequately contrasted experimentally.

Pros
- Simple, principled minimax formulation yielding a “safe” adaptive sampler with a clear worst-case guarantee over fixed Li-based sampling.
- Reduces to gradient-based optimal sampling with full information.
- Potentially useful idea for settings where tight safe bounds are cheaply maintainable (e.g., some structured problems).

Cons
- Technical inaccuracies (incorrect Hessian statement; likely error in Lemma 2.1 bound; unclear min–max justification).
- Algorithm 4 clarity and complexity guarantees insufficiently substantiated in the main text.
- Practicality of O(n) per-iteration bound updates questionable; benefits for SGD are weak.
- Missing key baselines and rigorous timing on the same hardware; experimental setup not fully convincing.
- Many typos/inconsistencies and incomplete references.

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially impactful for CD, but the current submission has several technical and presentation issues that undermine confidence (notably the errors in Section 2 and 4), limited empirical comparisons, and unclear practicality for large-scale problems. With corrected analysis, clearer algorithms, stronger baselines, and tighter experiments, this work could be competitive in a future submission.

Confidence
High.
