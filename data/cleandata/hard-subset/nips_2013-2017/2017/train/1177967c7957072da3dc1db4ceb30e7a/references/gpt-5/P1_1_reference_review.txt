Summary
This paper addresses adaptive importance sampling for randomized optimization methods, focusing primarily on coordinate descent (CD) and also discussing stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to bridge the gap between theoretically optimal gradient-based sampling—which typically requires full gradient information each iteration—and practical schemes that must operate with limited information.

Main contributions:
- Full-information optimality: For CD with coordinate-wise smoothness (Li), the paper shows that the gradient-based distribution p∗ ∝ √Li |∇i f(x)| with an accompanying stepsize αk = ||∇f||^2 / ||√L∇f||^2 minimizes the standard quadratic upper-bound model and can yield up to an n-fold speed-up over uniform (or Li-based) fixed sampling when Li are equal.
- Safe adaptive sampling under limited information: At each iteration, the method assumes access to safe lower and upper bounds on either the absolute coordinate gradients (CD) or per-component gradient norms (SGD). It formulates the sampling choice as a convex min–max problem: pick p to minimize the worst-case model-based progress over all gradients consistent with the bounds. The resulting distribution p̂ is shown to be never worse than fixed Li-based sampling and reduces to the full-information optimal distribution when bounds become tight.
- Efficient algorithm: The min–max can be solved in O(n log n) time via a sorting-based procedure (Algorithm 4), yielding both p̂ and the recommended stepsize α̂ = 1/vk. A geometric interpretation is given. A competitive-ratio analysis compares p̂ to hindsight-optimal sampling within the uncertainty set.
- Practical instantiations: For generalized linear models (GLMs) with separable regularizers, the paper derives simple safe bounds on gradients using mean value expansions and Cauchy–Schwarz, enabling O(n) updates of bounds per iteration.
- Empirical evaluation: On several classification/regression tasks (square, squared hinge, logistic; L1/L2 regularization) and datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20 and subsets), the proposed scheme improves iteration complexity and wall-clock time versus fixed sampling, and approaches full-information optimal sampling. Gains in SGD are modest.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on a large literature on non-uniform sampling for CD and SGD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.; Nesterov; Zhao & Zhang; Csiba et al.; Allen-Zhu et al.). Prior adaptive methods often either require full gradient information, rely on stale values, or heuristically use upper bounds, and may lack guarantees relative to fixed sampling. The novelty here is a principled min–max “safe” construction using interval bounds that provably dominates fixed importance sampling and recovers the gradient-based rule when full information is available. The work is complementary to steepest/Gauss-Southwell CD and approximate steepest selection (e.g., Nutini et al.; Stich et al.), and to screening rules, which the authors cite.

Quality
- Theory: The one-step progress analysis and the optimality of p∗ under the quadratic model are standard and used carefully. The safe min–max formulation is well-motivated, and theorems establish: (i) p̂ minimizes the worst-case upper bound over the uncertainty set; (ii) V(p̂, c) ≤ Tr[L] ||c||^2 for all c in the set, i.e., not worse than Li-based sampling; (iii) with full information, it reduces to the classical gradient-based sampling. The O(n log n) solver for the min–max and the geometric characterization are valuable contributions. Competitive-ratio bounds are somewhat loose but informative.
- Experiments: Demonstrate iterative and time gains for CD on multiple losses and regularizers; visualize vk relative gains; compare against uniform and full-information optimal. For SGD, improvements are small, consistent with prior observations. The evaluation supports the main claims, though see limitations below.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful algorithms and a clean notation for the model-based analysis. Some suggestions:
- Emphasize earlier the per-iteration computational implications (O(n) bound updates plus O(n log n) sampling) and when this is amortized by per-iteration gradient costs (e.g., dense vs. sparse settings).
- Provide a brief convergence statement in the main text (currently deferred) for the adaptive stepsize scheme, beyond per-iteration decrease.
- Clarify initialization when u = ∞ (how Algorithm 4 behaves) and stability safeguards.

Originality
The “safe” min–max design of an adaptive sampling distribution under interval bounds, with a constructive O(n log n) algorithm and a guarantee to dominate fixed sampling, appears novel. The integration with CD via an adaptive stepsize tied to the min–max value vk is also a nice design. Prior adaptive methods did not provide this worst-case guarantee nor a systematic way to exploit uncertain gradient information.

Significance
If the overhead can be controlled, the method offers a principled way to get most of the benefits of gradient-based sampling without computing full gradients. The guarantee “never worse than fixed importance sampling” is attractive and could influence practice and follow-up work on adaptive sampling, screening, and distributed implementations.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Computational overhead: The approach assumes O(n) updates of gradient bounds per iteration and O(n log n) work to compute p̂. This can be prohibitive in standard CD settings where per-iteration work is O(nnz(aik)). The paper argues the overhead is negligible when d = Ω(n); however, in many sparse high-dimensional problems nnz(aik) ≪ n, so O(n) work per iteration could dominate. The experiments often use reduced-size subsets and bag-of-words features; stronger evidence on large, sparse problems without subsampling is needed.
- Comparisons: Baselines are mostly uniform vs. full-information optimal. It would strengthen the case to compare against (a) Gauss-Southwell/approximate steepest CD (e.g., Nutini et al.; Stich et al. 2017), (b) adaptive probability methods such as AdaSDCA or dual methods with adaptive sampling, and (c) heuristic bound-based adaptive sampling used in practice. For SGD, comparisons with importance-sampled variance-reduced methods could be informative.
- Global rates: The paper mainly provides one-step progress guarantees and a worst-case dominance result. End-to-end complexity improvements (rates under strong convexity or convexity) that quantify the effect of bound looseness over iterations would increase the theoretical impact.
- Practical bound maintenance: The bound update for general GLMs via Cauchy–Schwarz can be very loose; while experiments show gains, tighter but still efficient alternatives (e.g., block-wise, lazy, or screening-based maintenance) would help. Memory and sparsity considerations are not discussed in depth.

Pros
- Principled, novel min–max formulation with a constructive O(n log n) algorithm.
- Provable worst-case dominance over fixed importance sampling; recovers full-information optimal sampling.
- General framework applicable to CD and SGD; clear stepsize design tied to vk.
- Empirical gains on several CD tasks; careful visualization of vk evolution.

Cons
- Potentially substantial per-iteration overhead (O(n) + O(n log n)), especially problematic for sparse CD where per-iteration cost is typically tiny.
- Limited comparisons to strong adaptive/greedy baselines; SGD gains are small.
- Lack of full convergence-rate results reflecting bound accuracy.
- Some experiments on subsampled datasets; timing results across different machines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide experiments on full-scale sparse datasets without subsampling, reporting both iteration and wall-clock time, and include comparisons to approximate steepest/Gauss-Southwell and adaptive SDCA variants.
- Discuss and, if possible, implement amortized or lazy updates of bounds and p̂ (e.g., update sampling distribution every T steps, reusing sort order as bounds evolve).
- Present convergence rates under standard assumptions, making explicit how vk affects rates and how bound tightness translates into practical speedups.
- Clarify initialization/stability when bounds are very loose (e.g., u = ∞), and provide guidance on setting/maintaining bounds efficiently in sparse settings.

Recommendation: Weak accept
This is a solid and original theoretical contribution with a practical algorithm and supportive experiments for CD. Addressing the computational overhead and adding stronger baselines would significantly strengthen the paper, but the core idea and guarantees are noteworthy and of interest to the NIPS community.
