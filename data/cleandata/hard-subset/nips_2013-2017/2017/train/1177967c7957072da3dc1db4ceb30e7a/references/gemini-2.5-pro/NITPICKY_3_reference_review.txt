This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key challenge in adaptive sampling is that computing the optimal, gradient-based sampling distribution is often as expensive as a full gradient step, negating the benefits of stochastic methods. Prior work often relies on heuristics or approximations (e.g., using stale gradients or upper bounds) that lack guarantees of being better than simple uniform or static importance sampling.

The main idea of this paper is to maintain safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient components (or their norms). Instead of heuristically using these bounds, the authors formulate a minimax optimization problem to find a sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case one-step progress objective, taken over all possible gradients consistent with the bounds. The authors show this problem has a solution that is provably better than or equal to static L-based importance sampling. They provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute this "safe" sampling distribution and demonstrate how to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models. Empirical results on several datasets show the method outperforms static sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time, and is competitive with the impractical optimal sampling scheme.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The core contribution, formulating the selection of a sampling distribution as a minimax problem (Equation 7) over a "confidence set" of possible gradients, is novel and elegant. It provides a principled way to handle uncertainty in gradient information, moving beyond the heuristics commonly used in the literature.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantee:** Theorem 3.2 provides a crucial guarantee that the proposed sampling is never worse than standard (fixed) importance sampling based on Lipschitz constants. This "safety net" is a significant advantage over many existing adaptive methods and directly addresses one of their main drawbacks.
3.  **Efficient Algorithm:** The authors provide an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the minimax problem, making the proposed scheme computationally practical. The overhead is often acceptable, especially when the feature dimension `d` is large relative to `n`.
4.  **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method against relevant baselines (uniform, optimal) on several real-world datasets and for different models. The inclusion of plots for the stepsize parameter `v_k` provides excellent insight into the algorithm's behavior and confirms its adaptive nature.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practical method for adaptive importance sampling. The core minimax idea is a significant conceptual contribution, and the "safety" guarantee addresses a critical flaw in prior work. The empirical results are strong and validate the approach.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The paper's execution is sloppy. It is riddled with minor errors in notation, presentation, and clarity. More importantly, a key theoretical result (Lemma 3.6) appears to be incorrect as stated. While the main contribution (Theorem 3.2 and Algorithm 4) seems sound, the accumulation of these flaws detracts significantly from the quality of the work and suggests a rushed preparation.

**Recommendation:** Reject.

While the core ideas presented in this paper are valuable and potentially of high impact, the manuscript in its current form does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The combination of a potentially significant technical error in the theoretical analysis and a large number of minor issues related to clarity, polish, and formatting prevents a confident recommendation for acceptance. I would encourage the authors to perform a thorough revision to correct the errors, improve the clarity of their presentation, and resubmit to a future conference. A revised version that addresses these numerous issues would likely constitute a very strong paper.
