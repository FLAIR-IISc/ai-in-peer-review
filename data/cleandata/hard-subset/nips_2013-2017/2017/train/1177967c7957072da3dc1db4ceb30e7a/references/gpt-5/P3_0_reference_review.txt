Summary
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for both coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It starts from the standard observation that, under a quadratic upper bound model (coordinate-wise Li-smoothness), the expected one-step decrease with randomized coordinate selection depends on a trade-off between a stepsize α and a sampling distribution p. With full gradient information, the optimal p for CD has probabilities proportional to √Li|∇if(x)| and yields a provable speed-up over fixed (Li-based) sampling, potentially up to a factor n in special cases. Since computing this adaptive p exactly is often infeasible, the core contribution is a min-max formulation that uses safe lower and upper bounds on the unknown coordinate magnitudes (or component gradients for SGD) to choose a distribution p̂ and a stepsize α̂ = 1/v that minimize the worst-case ratio V(p, c)/||c||² over all c in the uncertainty box C = {c: ℓ ≤ c ≤ u}. The resulting sampling is guaranteed to be never worse (in expectation under the quadratic model) than fixed Li-based sampling, and coincides with the full-information optimum when the bounds are tight. The paper gives an O(n log n) algorithm to compute p̂ via a thresholding/clipping rule reminiscent of water-filling, shows how to maintain safe gradient bounds efficiently for GLMs, and reports experiments demonstrating substantial gains for CD and modest gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
- Fixed and adaptive non-uniform sampling in CD/SGD are well studied (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Zhao & Zhang; Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.). Prior “adaptive” schemes often rely on stale or upper-bound gradients without a guarantee they outperform uniform/fixed sampling.
- This work’s novelty is a robust, information-theoretic formulation: given only safe intervals for gradient magnitudes, choose the distribution that optimizes worst-case progress under the standard quadratic model. This “safe dominance” guarantee over fixed sampling is, to my knowledge, not present in earlier adaptive strategies based on bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: reducing adaptive sampling under uncertainty to a convex min-max problem is elegant and natural. The derived sampling p̂ has strong properties: (i) optimal among all distributions given the uncertainty set; (ii) never worse than fixed Li-based sampling; (iii) recovers the full-information optimum when ℓ = u.
- Methodological contribution: explicit O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) with a clear structural solution (clipping c to the box or to a threshold √Li m), and connections to a geometric characterization (Rayleigh-type quotient).
- Practicality: discussion of how to maintain safe bounds in GLMs for both CD and SGD, with cheap updates via Cauchy–Schwarz. The algorithm requires only the sampled coordinate’s gradient, like standard CD/SGD.
- Empirical evidence: clear and relevant experiments on sparse text datasets. For CD, substantial speed-ups in iterations and wall-clock, with adaptive stepsize being crucial. For SGD, improvements are smaller, which is honestly reported.

Weaknesses and questions
- Computational overhead in large-n regimes: Computing p̂ afresh each iteration in O(n log n) can be nontrivial when n is very large and per-iteration gradient cost is O(1)–O(nnz of one column). The paper argues that when d = Ω(n) the overhead is amortized to O(log n), but many ML settings have d << n (e.g., very high-dimensional sparse problems where a single coordinate update is extremely cheap). It would strengthen the case to:
  - Report wall-clock breakdowns isolating sampling time vs. gradient computation time across varying n,d regimes.
  - Discuss incremental/approximate updates of p̂ (e.g., lazy recomputation every T steps, maintaining heaps of active constraints) and the incurred effect on guarantees.
- Minimax interchange and convexity: The key inequality (∗) relies on interchanging min over p and max over c. While Sion-type arguments can apply, the ratio V(p, c)/||c||² is not jointly convex-concave in (p, c) in an obvious way. The main text defers this to the appendix. Given the centrality of the guarantee, the assumptions ensuring the minimax swap (compactness, convexity in p, appropriate quasiconvexity/concavity in c over a box) should be stated clearly in the main paper, along with any caveats.
- Quality of safe bounds: The GLM bounds based on Cauchy–Schwarz are very loose (especially with ℓ0 = 0, u0 = ∞ initially). While the experiments show that even loose bounds eventually help, the method’s practical benefits hinge on bound tightness. It would be helpful to:
  - Quantify how vk evolves with the tightness of bounds (e.g., controlled synthetic ablations varying u/ℓ ratios) and relate to the competitive ratio bounds (Lemma 3.5–3.6).
  - Explore stronger, yet cheap, bound maintenance (e.g., data-dependent preconditioning, curvature tracking, block-diagonal Hessian surrogates) to improve early-epoch performance.
- Scope and claims: The title and introduction suggest broad applicability (including deep learning), but theory and experiments focus on convex GLMs. Please temper claims accordingly or include a short empirical study on a simple nonconvex setting, clarifying what can/cannot be guaranteed without convexity/Li-smoothness.
- Experimental coverage:
  - For CD, compare also to Gauss–Southwell variants (Nutini et al.) and to adaptive rules based on stale gradients/duality gaps (e.g., Perekrestenko et al., Csiba et al.), to isolate the value of “safety.”
  - Report the frequency of p̂ recomputation and its time; consider a variant recomputing every T iterations with analysis/empirics.
  - For SGD, results show small gains; discuss when the method is expected to help (e.g., highly heterogeneous per-example gradient norms) and include such a case.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with a clean narrative from full-information optimality to limited-information robust sampling. A few presentation issues:
- Some equations appear to miss division by p_i in V(p,·) (e.g., Eq. (3) formatting). Please standardize notation; define V(p, x) early and consistently.
- Algorithm 4 reuses symbols u, ℓ as both vectors and indices; rename loop indices to avoid ambiguity.
- State the precise assumptions for the minimax arguments in the main text.
- Explicitly state the smoothness assumptions and how to compute Li for the GLM instances used.

Originality
The robust min-max formulation for adaptive sampling under interval uncertainty and the “never-worse-than Li-based” guarantee together with an efficient constructive algorithm appear novel and nontrivial relative to prior adaptive sampling methods.

Significance
For CD on convex problems, this is a solid step forward: a practical, principled adaptive sampler that dominates fixed sampling and approaches gradient-based sampling without full-gradient cost. The idea is simple to implement and likely to be adopted. The impact for SGD seems more modest, but the safety guarantee and methodology could be useful in settings with high gradient heterogeneity.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, principled formulation with strong guarantees and efficient algorithm
- Clear improvement over fixed sampling and competitive with full-information sampling in CD
- Practical bound maintenance for GLMs; reproducible evaluation
- Novelty relative to existing adaptive/bound-based samplers

Cons for acceptance
- Per-iteration O(n log n) overhead may be prohibitive in very large-n, cheap-coordinate settings; limited discussion of amortization/approximations
- Key minimax interchange conditions are deferred; assumptions should be clearer
- Limited empirical comparisons to the strongest adaptive CD baselines; modest gains for SGD
- Presentation/notation issues to fix

Recommendation
Lean accept for NeurIPS, contingent on clarifying the minimax assumptions, expanding empirical comparisons (especially to Gauss–Southwell/duality-gap-based and stale-gradient methods), and discussing practical strategies to reduce the O(n log n) overhead in large-n regimes. The core idea is sound, well-motivated, and likely to influence practice for CD.
