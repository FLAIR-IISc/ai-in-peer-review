

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors argue that while adaptive importance sampling theoretically outperforms fixed sampling, existing approaches either require full gradient computation (making them computationally expensive) or lack theoretical guarantees. Their solution computes an adaptive sampling distribution using only safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, which they claim can be maintained efficiently for generalized linear models. The key theoretical claim is that their method is provably better than fixed importance sampling while being computationally efficient (O(n log n) per iteration).

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a well-structured theoretical analysis with clear mathematical formulation. The core contribution (Theorem 3.2) establishes that their sampling method is provably better than fixed sampling, which is a significant theoretical advance over previous work. The computational complexity analysis is sound, and the authors demonstrate that their method can be implemented at minimal additional cost (O(n log n) vs O(d) for gradient evaluation). The empirical results show consistent improvement over fixed sampling on multiple datasets and loss functions, which supports their theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weakness is the lack of thorough comparison with existing adaptive sampling methods. The authors cite [36, 23] as previous work but fail to systematically compare their "safe" approach with these methods. For instance, [36] also proposes gradient-based sampling with bounds, and it's unclear how the proposed "safe" optimization formulation (Equation 7) differs from their approach. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the claimed theoretical advantage is truly novel.

The empirical evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive. The authors report that "the effect of the proposed sampling is less pronounced in the three SGD experiments" compared to CD, but they don't provide a detailed explanation for this discrepancy or compare with other adaptive sampling methods in the SGD setting. The paper lacks a clear ablation study showing how much of the improvement comes from the sampling strategy versus the adaptive stepsize (which is a separate contribution).

The theoretical guarantees rely on a key assumption: that safe gradient bounds can be efficiently maintained. While the authors demonstrate this for generalized linear models (Section 4), they don't provide evidence that this assumption holds for a broader class of problems. The claim that "the machine learning community could profit from further research in that direction" suggests the method may be limited to specific problem classes, which weakens the general applicability claim.

The paper also lacks a clear discussion of the practical limitations. For instance, the paper claims the overhead is "small enough to outperform fixed importance sampling in terms of total computation time," but the timing results (Figures 6 and 9) don't provide clear evidence of this when compared to existing adaptive methods.

**Originality and Significance:**
While the theoretical guarantee that the method is provably better than fixed sampling is valuable, the novelty is limited. The paper builds on a well-established line of work on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 23]), and the "safe" approach appears to be a refinement rather than a fundamentally new concept. The significance is somewhat diminished by the fact that the method only provides a modest improvement over fixed sampling, particularly in SGD settings where the paper admits the gains are "slight."

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents a technically sound method with a clear theoretical contribution to adaptive importance sampling. However, the lack of thorough comparison with existing methods, insufficient empirical validation, and limited scope (only demonstrated for GLMs) weaken the significance of the contribution. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison with prior adaptive sampling methods, a clearer explanation of the novelty relative to existing work, and more comprehensive experiments across diverse optimization problems.

The paper does not demonstrate a substantial improvement over the state of the art, and the claimed "safe" approach does not sufficiently distinguish itself from previous work on adaptive sampling with bounds. While the method may be useful in specific contexts, the paper fails to convincingly establish its broader relevance to the machine learning community.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject

*Pro:*
- Clear theoretical contribution showing provable improvement over fixed sampling
- Sound computational complexity analysis
- Consistent empirical improvements over fixed sampling on multiple datasets

*Con:*
- Insufficient comparison with prior adaptive sampling methods
- Limited scope of empirical validation (only GLMs)
- Unclear distinction from existing work on adaptive sampling with bounds
- Modest practical improvements in some settings (e.g., SGD)
