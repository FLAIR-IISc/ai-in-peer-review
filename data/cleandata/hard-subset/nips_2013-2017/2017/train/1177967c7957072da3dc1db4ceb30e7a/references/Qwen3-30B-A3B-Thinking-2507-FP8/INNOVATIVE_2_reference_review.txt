

# Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

This paper introduces "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," a novel approach to adaptive sampling for optimization algorithms like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The authors address a fundamental challenge in adaptive importance sampling: while theoretically superior to fixed sampling, gradient-based adaptive methods often require expensive full gradient computations. Their key insight is to use safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information to create a computationally efficient sampling strategy that maintains theoretical guarantees. The proposed method provably outperforms fixed importance sampling while requiring only O(n log n) computation per iteration, making it practical for large-scale optimization.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes theoretically that gradient-based adaptive sampling in CD can achieve speed-ups of up to a factor of the dimension n compared to fixed sampling. Second, it proposes a novel "safe" adaptive sampling strategy that computes an optimal sampling distribution using only safe gradient bounds, avoiding the need for full gradient computation. Third, it provides a detailed analysis of the method's theoretical properties, including worst-case guarantees and competitive ratios. Finally, it demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the approach on real-world datasets using generalized linear models.

This work builds on previous literature on adaptive importance sampling [22, 36, 37], but makes a bold conceptual leap by framing the problem as an optimization over the set of possible gradient vectors consistent with the available bounds. This is a significant departure from previous approaches that simply used extreme bounds (e.g., [36, 23]), which might be suboptimal. The authors prove that their method is the "best possible" with respect to the limited information available, providing a strong theoretical foundation for their approach.

The paper's strengths are numerous. The theoretical contribution is substantial, with clear proofs of the method's superiority over fixed sampling and detailed analysis of its computational complexity. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating consistent improvements over fixed sampling across multiple datasets and loss functions. The paper also provides an elegant algorithm (Algorithm 4) for computing the safe sampling distribution efficiently in O(n log n) time, which is a significant practical contribution. The authors clearly articulate the novelty of their approach and how it differs from previous work, addressing a key gap in the literature.

I have two main concerns. First, while the paper focuses on GLMs as the primary application, the generality of the approach for more complex models (e.g., deep learning) is not thoroughly explored. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could better justify why their method would extend to more complex settings. Second, the empirical results show that the proposed method yields less dramatic improvements in SGD compared to CD, but the paper doesn't adequately explain this discrepancy. A more in-depth analysis of this difference would strengthen the paper.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured sections, clear notation, and thorough theoretical development. The algorithms are presented concisely, and the empirical results are well-organized and informative. The writing is professional and accessible to the NIPS community.

In terms of significance, this work addresses a fundamental problem in optimization that affects a wide range of machine learning applications. The proposed method provides a theoretically sound and computationally efficient approach to adaptive sampling, which could be widely adopted in practice. The paper makes a clear contribution to the state of the art, demonstrating both theoretical novelty and practical utility.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper highly compelling. The core idea of "safe" adaptive sampling is both novel and conceptually elegant, addressing a real problem with significant theoretical and practical implications. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded to more complex models, the current results are sufficiently convincing to demonstrate the method's value.

The paper's theoretical guarantees and computational efficiency make it stand out from previous work on adaptive importance sampling, which often sacrificed theoretical guarantees for computational efficiency. The authors have successfully bridged this gap, creating a method that is both theoretically sound and practically viable.

In summary, this paper makes a significant contribution to optimization for machine learning by introducing a novel adaptive sampling strategy that is both theoretically justified and computationally efficient. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and addresses a problem of broad interest. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Strengths**:
- Novel conceptual approach to adaptive importance sampling
- Strong theoretical guarantees (provably better than fixed sampling)
- Efficient algorithm with O(n log n) computational complexity
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation on real datasets
- Clear distinction from previous work with rigorous analysis

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited exploration of applicability to non-GLM models
- Insufficient explanation for the observed difference in performance between CD and SGD
- Could provide more detailed analysis of the competitive ratio in practice
