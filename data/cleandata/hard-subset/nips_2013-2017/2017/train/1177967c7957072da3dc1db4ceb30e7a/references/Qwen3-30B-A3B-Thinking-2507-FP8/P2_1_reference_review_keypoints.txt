- Proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for CD and SGD that uses provable bounds on gradient information.
- Replaces expensive full-gradient sampling with a convex optimization formulation that computes a sampling distribution from safe upper/lower gradient bounds.
- Presents an O(n log n) per-iteration algorithm to compute the adaptive sampling distribution.
- Proves the method is always at least as good as fixed importance sampling and recovers optimal gradient-based sampling in the full-information case.
- Shows how to compute safe gradient bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs).
- Empirical results on rcv1, real-sim, and news20 across multiple losses and regularizers show consistent speedups over fixed sampling in iterations and runtime.
- Notes adaptive sampling yields larger benefits for coordinate descent than for SGD, supported by experiments.
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous with clear lemmas, theorems, and full proofs in the appendix.
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and reproducible with clear notation and algorithm descriptions.
- Contribution is original: first safe adaptive sampling approach that is provably never worse than static sampling.
- Method is practically significant: modest overhead relative to gradient cost and broadly applicable to large-scale problems.
- Empirical scope limited to GLMs; no demonstrations on more complex models beyond GLMs.
- Lacks direct empirical comparisons to other adaptive importance sampling methods (e.g., [22,36,37]).
- Provides a worst-case competitive ratio bound but does not empirically analyze how tight this bound is.
- Overall assessment: strong, novel, practically relevant work that meets NIPS criteria; recommendation to accept.
