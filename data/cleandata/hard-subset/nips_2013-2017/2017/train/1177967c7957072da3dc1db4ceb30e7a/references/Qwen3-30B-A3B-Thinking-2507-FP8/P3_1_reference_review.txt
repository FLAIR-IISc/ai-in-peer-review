

# Peer Review: "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors argue that while optimal gradient-based adaptive sampling can theoretically lead to significant speedups (up to a factor of n in special cases), it's computationally infeasible due to the need for full gradient information. The paper's key contribution is a practical alternative that uses only safe upper and lower bounds on gradient entries, while still providing theoretical guarantees that the method is always better than fixed importance sampling (like uniform sampling).

The authors present a theoretical framework showing that the optimal adaptive sampling distribution can be computed efficiently (O(n log n) per iteration) by solving a convex optimization problem. They demonstrate how to maintain safe gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs), and provide empirical evidence that the method outperforms fixed sampling strategies in both CD and SGD settings, with minimal computational overhead.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical foundation is solid. The authors successfully derive the optimal adaptive sampling distribution (Example 2.3) and prove that it offers significant theoretical improvements over fixed sampling (Theorem 2.1).
- The "safe" adaptive sampling approach (Section 3) is theoretically rigorous. Theorem 3.2 provides a strong guarantee that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling, which is a significant improvement over previous work that often lacked such guarantees.
- The computational complexity analysis (Theorem 3.4) is thorough, showing that the proposed method requires O(n log n) time per iteration, which is comparable to the cost of computing a single gradient component for many problems.
- The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple loss functions (square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss), regularization types (L1, L2), and real-world datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20).

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper's claim that safe gradient bounds can be efficiently computed is somewhat superficial. While Section 4 provides a method for GLMs, it doesn't adequately address how this would work for more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks) or non-GLM objectives. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide a clear path forward.
- The theoretical analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5) is somewhat weak. The bound ρk ≤ vk/Lmin could be as large as Tr[L]/Lmin, which might be large for many practical problems. The paper doesn't provide insight into when this ratio is small in practice.
- The empirical results show that the proposed method outperforms fixed sampling, but the comparison to the optimal (but infeasible) adaptive sampling is not as compelling as the theory suggests. The vk values in Figure 3-6 are often much smaller than the worst-case bound, but the paper doesn't explain why this is the case.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address how the method scales with extremely high-dimensional problems (e.g., when d >> n), which is where adaptive importance sampling would be most valuable.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized with clear section headings that logically progress from theory to practice.
- The notation is consistent and well-defined throughout the paper.
- The theoretical results are presented in a logical sequence, building from the optimal sampling case to the safe adaptive approach.
- The figures (Figures 2-9) effectively illustrate the empirical results and provide clear visual comparisons between methods.

**Weaknesses:**
- The proof of Lemma 2.4 (and related results) is deferred to the appendix, which makes it difficult to assess the completeness of the theoretical foundation without consulting the supplementary material.
- Algorithm 4 (computing the safe sampling) is described somewhat cryptically. The indexing of the arrays (`sort`, `usort`, etc.) is not intuitive, and the algorithm would benefit from a more detailed explanation or a small example.
- The paper claims that the computational overhead is "small enough to outperform fixed importance sampling in terms of total computation time" (Section 5), but doesn't provide a clear comparison of the actual wall-clock times for the different methods.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The core contribution of "safe adaptive importance sampling" is novel. Previous work on adaptive importance sampling either required full gradient information (which is computationally prohibitive) or used approximations without theoretical guarantees.
- The paper's approach of formulating the sampling problem as a convex optimization problem with safe bounds is a fresh perspective that distinguishes it from previous work.
- The authors clearly establish the relationship between their method and prior work (e.g., they cite and contrast with Perekrestenko et al. "Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling" and Csiba et al. "Stochastic Dual Coordinate Ascent with Adaptive Probabilities").

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from the work of Perekrestenko et al. (2016), which also proposes an adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent. While the current paper's method is more computationally efficient, the distinction isn't clearly articulated.
- The theoretical guarantee that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed sampling has been established in prior work (e.g., by Richtárik & Takáč in "On optimal probabilities in stochastic coordinate descent methods"), though the computational efficiency is a new contribution.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The problem of efficient adaptive importance sampling is important for large-scale machine learning, where optimization speed is critical.
- The method has the potential to significantly improve the convergence of CD and SGD algorithms with minimal computational overhead, making it attractive for practical applications.
- The approach is general and can be applied to a wide range of optimization problems, not just the specific examples shown in the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
- The empirical results show only modest improvements in some cases (e.g., in SGD, the proposed method only slightly outperforms uniform sampling). This limits the practical significance of the contribution.
- The paper doesn't provide a clear comparison to other adaptive sampling methods (e.g., gradient-based sampling with stale gradients, duality gap-based sampling) in terms of both performance and computational cost.
- The paper doesn't adequately address how the method would perform on extremely large-scale problems (e.g., with billions of features), which is where adaptive importance sampling would be most valuable.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The "safe adaptive importance sampling" approach is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrated to be effective in practice. The key innovation is providing a practical method that achieves the theoretical benefits of adaptive sampling without requiring full gradient information.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. More detailed discussion of how to obtain safe gradient bounds for non-GLM problems
2. A more thorough comparison to other adaptive sampling methods
3. Better explanation of why the empirical results show better performance than the worst-case theoretical bounds
4. More analysis of the method's scalability to extremely high-dimensional problems

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical results are sound, the proposed method is practical and efficient, and the empirical evaluation is thorough. While there are some limitations in the scope and presentation, the paper is of high quality and would be of interest to the NIPS community.

The paper's main contributions—providing a theoretically guaranteed, computationally efficient adaptive importance sampling method—address a significant gap in the literature and have the potential to improve the performance of many optimization algorithms used in machine learning.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 4, provide more concrete examples of how to obtain safe gradient bounds for non-GLM problems (e.g., deep neural networks, kernel methods).

2. Add a comparison to the method proposed by Perekrestenko et al. (2016) in terms of both theoretical guarantees and computational cost.

3. In Figure 3-6, include a dashed line showing the performance of the optimal adaptive sampling (which requires full gradient information) to better illustrate the gap between the proposed method and the theoretical optimum.

4. Provide a more detailed analysis of the method's scalability to extremely high-dimensional problems (e.g., with d > 10^6 features).

5. Clarify the relationship between the proposed method and other adaptive sampling approaches (e.g., those based on duality gaps) in the related work section.

6. In Algorithm 4, add a small example demonstrating how the algorithm works with concrete numbers.

7. In Section 5, provide a more detailed comparison of the actual wall-clock times for the different methods, not just the number of iterations.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With the suggested improvements, it would be even more impactful.
