Summary
This paper studies adaptive importance sampling for randomized optimization, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors first show that the “full-information” gradient-based sampling (probabilities proportional to sqrt(Li)|∇if| for CD, and to ‖∇fi‖2 for SGD) maximizes the one-step expected decrease under the standard smooth quadratic model, and can yield speedups up to a factor n over fixed (Li-based or uniform) sampling. The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive sampling when full gradient information is unavailable: given per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds `k, uk on either coordinate gradients (CD) or component gradient norms (SGD), the sampling is chosen by solving a min–max problem that minimizes a worst-case surrogate of the expected decrease over all gradients consistent with those bounds. The resulting p̂k is shown to be never worse than fixed importance sampling and to coincide with the full-information optimal sampling when the bounds are tight. The min–max problem admits an efficient O(n log n) solution via a thresholding/sorting routine (Algorithm 4). The paper also shows how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for GLMs, and provides experiments on classification/regression tasks showing substantial gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on non-uniform/importance sampling for CD/SGD (e.g., Nesterov ’12; Richtárik & Takáč; Zhao & Zhang ’15; Needell et al. ’14) and adaptive strategies based on gradients/gaps (e.g., Nutini et al. ’15; Csiba et al. ’15; Perekrestenko et al. ’17).
- The novelty is the safe min–max formulation using interval bounds on gradients, yielding guarantees that dominate fixed sampling, and an efficient algorithm to compute the distribution each iteration. This is distinct from heuristics using stale/full gradients or loose upper bounds, for which “never worse than fixed” guarantees are typically missing.

Quality
- Theory: The one-step expected improvement analysis is standard, and the optimized αk(pk) and p? are correct under the smooth quadratic model. The min–max problem is well-motivated; the “safety” guarantee (Theorem 3.2) is clear and useful. The geometric view and the O(n log n) algorithm (Theorem 3.4) are elegant. The comparison to the hindsight-optimal distribution via competitive ratio is informative, though bounds can be loose and rely on a relative accuracy condition (Lemma 3.6).
- Some technical details would benefit from being in the main text: (i) the justification of swapping min and max in (7) (Sion/Komiya conditions: convexity–concavity and compactness of the sets), (ii) explicit convergence rates for Algorithm 2 under convex/strongly convex settings (beyond one-step decrease and appendix pointers), to help readers connect to known CD complexity.
- Empirics: For CD, the method consistently improves over fixed sampling and tracks the full-information optimal closely. For SGD, improvements are small, aligned with the theory that benefits are problem-dependent.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, with helpful intuition and algorithm boxes. A few presentation suggestions:
  - Move a self-contained convergence rate statement for Algorithm 2 into the main text.
  - Explicitly state the assumptions required to apply the minimax theorem to (7) (convexity, compactness).
  - Clarify the initialization with u0 = ∞: how is Algorithm 4 applied when upper bounds are infinite in the first iteration; is p̂k then uniform by construction?
  - Be explicit about computational models: in CD on sparse GLMs, the per-iteration cost is often O(nnz(ai)), much less than O(d). The claimed O(n) bound updates and O(n log n) sampling can dominate; discuss amortization (e.g., recomputing p̂k every T steps), lazy updates, or maintaining sampling on an active set.

Originality
- The safe min–max formulation and its exact, efficient solution for interval-bounded gradients are, to my knowledge, novel. Prior adaptive importance sampling either requires full/stale gradients or uses upper bounds without a “never worse than fixed” guarantee. Showing recovery of the optimal full-information distribution when bounds are tight strengthens the conceptual contribution.

Significance
- For CD, the contribution is practically relevant and theoretically solid: it offers a principled way to adapt probabilities without computing full gradients, with guarantees strictly dominating fixed sampling. This is likely to influence subsequent work on adaptive sampling and screening-like strategies.
- For SGD, the significance appears more limited empirically; nonetheless, the framework is general and could benefit problems with more heterogeneous component gradients, or with tighter safe bounds.

Strengths
- Clean and principled min–max formulation with strong guarantees (dominates fixed sampling, recovers optimal with full info).
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm with a clear thresholding structure.
- Concrete procedures to maintain safe bounds for GLMs.
- Empirical results validate substantial CD gains with small wall-clock overhead on tested datasets.
- Good positioning within related work.

Weaknesses and questions
- Computational overhead: Maintaining bounds in O(n) and recomputing p̂k in O(n log n) every iteration can be prohibitive in typical CD regimes (very large n, sparse columns with small nnz). Please discuss:
  - Practical strategies: recomputing p̂k every T iterations; maintaining/refreshing bounds only for a subset; exploiting sparsity to update only coordinates affected by aik; incremental updates to p̂k without full resorting.
  - Wall-clock on truly large-n settings (e.g., full news20 features), and sensitivity to recomputation frequency.
- Comparisons: Experiments mostly compare to uniform and full-information “optimal.” Please add baselines:
  - Gauss–Southwell/approximate steepest CD (Nutini et al. 2015; Stich et al. 2017).
  - Gap-based/adaptive-probability CD (e.g., Csiba–Qu–Richtárik 2015; Perekrestenko et al. 2017).
- SGD results show minor gains; temper claims or add tasks where adaptive sampling should help (e.g., highly skewed per-example Lipschitz/gradient norms), possibly with mini-batching comparisons (Csiba–Richtárik).
- Assumptions and details:
  - Explicitly state that Ck is a compact box and V(p,c)/‖c‖2 is convex in p, concave in c (or provide the exact form used) for valid min–max swap.
  - Clarify how Algorithm 4 handles ties and infinite bounds; what numerical stability issues arise in computing m and norms?
  - Provide the main convergence rate theorem for Algorithm 2 (e.g., expected linear rate for strongly convex f with constants depending on vk).

Minor
- Several typos/formatting issues (e.g., missing footnote markers, spacing, sub/superscripts). A careful proofreading is advised.

Pro/Con summary
Pros:
- Strong, novel theory with practical algorithm.
- Strictly better than fixed sampling by design; recovers optimal when bounds tighten.
- Clear integration into CD/SGD; solid CD empirical gains.

Cons:
- Per-iteration O(n)–O(n log n) overhead may limit scalability; needs more discussion and ablations.
- Limited SGD impact; missing comparisons to other adaptive CD methods.
- Some theoretical details relegated to appendix that would aid readability.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The conceptual contribution and CD improvements justify acceptance; addressing scalability and comparative baselines would further strengthen the paper.

Confidence: High.
