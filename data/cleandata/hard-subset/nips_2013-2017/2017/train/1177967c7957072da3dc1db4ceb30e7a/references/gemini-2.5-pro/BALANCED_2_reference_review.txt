This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," designed for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The key motivation is to overcome the main drawbacks of existing adaptive sampling schemes, which are either computationally expensive (requiring full gradient information) or lack theoretical guarantees, sometimes performing worse than simple static sampling.

The core idea is to operate with efficiently computable safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient information (coordinate-wise magnitudes for CD, component-wise norms for SGD). Instead of using these bounds heuristically, the authors formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a principled minimax optimization problem. The goal is to find the distribution that maximizes the worst-case expected progress over all possible true gradients that lie within the given bounds. This "safe" formulation guarantees that the resulting sampling distribution is provably at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., based on Lipschitz constants), while adapting to the optimization trajectory. The paper provides a non-trivial and efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this minimax problem. The authors demonstrate how to derive the necessary gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide a thorough empirical evaluation on several real-world datasets, showing significant performance gains, particularly for coordinate descent.

This work builds upon a rich literature of adaptive sampling for stochastic optimization [e.g., 22, 23, 36]. However, it makes a distinct and important contribution by formally addressing the uncertainty inherent in using approximate gradient information. While previous methods often rely on stale values or upper bounds which can lead to suboptimal performance, this paper's minimax approach provides a robust and theoretically sound solution to this challenge.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization (Eq. 7) is the main contribution and is highly novel. It provides a principled and elegant solution to the problem of selecting a sampling distribution under uncertainty. This "safe" approach, which guarantees performance at least as good as static sampling, is a significant advance for making adaptive methods more reliable and practical.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and provides strong theoretical support for the proposed method. Theorem 3.2, which establishes the "safety" guarantee (i.e., the method is provably better than Li-based sampling), is a key result. Furthermore, Theorem 3.4 and Algorithm 4 provide a non-obvious and efficient O(n log n) solution to the minimax problem, which is crucial for the method's practicality.

3.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. It effectively builds the case from the ideal full-information setting to the practical limited-information setting. The work is a complete research contribution, containing a novel formulation, theoretical analysis, an efficient algorithm, a concrete application to an important model class (GLMs), and a comprehensive empirical study.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. For coordinate descent, the safe adaptive sampling scheme performs nearly as well as the impractical optimal sampling (using the full gradient) and significantly outperforms static uniform sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time. This confirms that the O(n log n) overhead is justified in practice. The visualization of the stepsize parameter `vk` effectively illustrates that the method is successfully adapting and achieving better progress than the worst-case bound would suggest.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Algorithm 4:** While the algorithm for computing the safe sampling is a key contribution, its presentation in Algorithm 4 is quite dense and may be difficult for readers to understand. The logic involving the pointers `u` and `l` and the implicit construction of the solution vector `c` could be explicated more clearly. Including a small, worked-out example in the appendix would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

2.  **Applicability Beyond GLMs:** The method for obtaining and updating the safe bounds (`l`, `u`) is demonstrated for GLMs, where gradient updates have a specific structure. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion on the challenges and potential strategies for applying this framework to other major model classes, particularly deep neural networks. For SGD on neural networks, efficiently maintaining non-trivial bounds on `||∇fi(x)||` for all `i` is a significant challenge, as updates are dense and second-order information is generally intractable. Acknowledging this limitation and suggesting potential research directions would be valuable.

3.  **Discussion of SGD Performance:** The authors correctly note that the empirical gains for SGD are much less pronounced than for CD. The review would benefit from a deeper discussion into the potential reasons for this. Is this phenomenon related to the specific datasets/models, or does it point to a more fundamental difference between the variance of coordinate gradients versus component gradients in these optimization problems? Providing some insight here could help practitioners understand in which scenarios the proposed method is likely to provide the largest benefit.

4.  **Nuance on Computational Overhead:** The paper argues the O(n log n) overhead is acceptable when the cost of a single gradient computation (`d`) is large. This is a fair point. However, the analysis could be more nuanced. For instance, in problems with a very large number of examples/coordinates `n` but a low feature dimension `d` (e.g., `d << log n`), this overhead could become the bottleneck. A brief discussion of the regimes (in terms of `n` vs. `d`) where the method is most likely to yield wall-clock time improvements would be a helpful addition.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   The novel and principled minimax formulation for robust adaptive sampling is a significant theoretical contribution.
    *   The method comes with strong "safety" guarantees, addressing a key weakness of prior work.
    *   The paper is a complete and high-quality contribution with solid theory, an efficient algorithm, and compelling empirical results for CD.
    *   The proposed technique has the potential for broad impact as a generic, reliable improvement for CD and some SGD methods.

*   **Against Acceptance (minor):**
    *   The practical applicability to models beyond GLMs, especially deep networks, is not fully established.
    *   The empirical gains for SGD are marginal in the experiments shown.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The novel formulation for safe adaptive sampling is a substantial and important contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The paper is well-executed and presents a complete and convincing story. It is a clear candidate for acceptance.
