This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy for both Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The authors motivate their work by pointing out the drawbacks of existing adaptive strategies—specifically, weaker theoretical guarantees and computational infeasibility—and aim to provide a scheme that is both efficient and theoretically sound. The core contribution lies in formulating an optimization problem to determine the optimal sampling distribution based on available gradient bounds, resulting in a method guaranteed to outperform uniform sampling. The paper includes theoretical analysis demonstrating speedups for CD, a practical algorithm, and empirical evaluation on real datasets.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the limitations of existing approaches. The theoretical analysis in Section 2 provides a solid foundation for understanding the potential benefits of adaptive sampling, particularly for CD. The proposed safe adaptive sampling strategy in Section 3 is well-defined, and the algorithm (Algorithm 2) appears computationally feasible. The experimental results in Section 5 demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over fixed sampling strategies.  The inclusion of detailed timings and additional plots analyzing the stepsize parameter `vk` is valuable.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides theoretical justification for the proposed method, demonstrating its superiority over fixed sampling strategies.  The analysis of speedup factors (up to a factor of *n* for CD) is particularly compelling.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient, avoiding the need to compute full gradients in each iteration, a common bottleneck in other adaptive sampling methods.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed safe adaptive sampling strategy (Algorithm 2) is well-defined and readily implementable.  The method for efficiently computing the sampling distribution (Algorithm 4) is a significant contribution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results on real datasets consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, validating the theoretical findings.
* **Clear Writing and Structure:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Gradient Bound Assumption:** The reliance on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient (`, u`) is a significant assumption. While the authors provide examples for GLMs, the applicability of this approach to more complex models or non-differentiable functions is not fully explored.  The sensitivity of performance to the tightness of these bounds is not thoroughly investigated.
* **SGD Results:** The performance gains observed in the SGD experiments are less pronounced than those for CD. This discrepancy is not fully explained, and the authors could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the reasons behind this difference.
* **Competitive Ratio Analysis:**  While Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6 provide theoretical bounds on the competitive ratio, they rely on conditions (e.g., γ ≥ 1) that might not always hold in practice. A more nuanced analysis of the competitive ratio under realistic scenarios would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison is mostly against uniform sampling and the “optimal” sampling, which is not practically computable. Comparing against other existing adaptive sampling schemes (e.g., those mentioned in the introduction) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed method’s advantages.
* **Notation:** The notation can be dense. While generally consistent, some definitions (e.g., the use of both `l` as a vector and `L` as a matrix) could potentially cause confusion.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in optimization—efficiently leveraging data for faster convergence.
* The proposed method offers strong theoretical guarantees and a practical algorithm with demonstrable performance improvements.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.
* The empirical evaluation provides compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on safe gradient bounds limits the applicability of the method to certain classes of problems.
* The less convincing results for SGD suggest that the method may not be universally effective.
* The analysis of the competitive ratio could be more thorough and realistic.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution that addresses an important problem in optimization with a novel and theoretically sound approach. While the reliance on safe gradient bounds and the weaker results for SGD are limitations, the overall strengths of the paper—particularly the theoretical guarantees, computational efficiency, and empirical validation—outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Expand Discussion of Gradient Bounds:** Provide a more detailed discussion of how to obtain safe gradient bounds for a wider range of models and functions. Explore the sensitivity of the method's performance to the tightness of these bounds.
* **Investigate SGD Performance:** Conduct a more in-depth analysis of the SGD results to understand why the performance gains are less pronounced than those for CD. Consider alternative strategies for applying the adaptive sampling scheme to SGD.
* **Refine Competitive Ratio Analysis:**  Provide a more nuanced analysis of the competitive ratio under realistic scenarios, relaxing some of the restrictive conditions.
* **Add Comparison to Existing Methods:** Include a comparison of the proposed method against other existing adaptive sampling schemes.
* **Clarify Notation:** Review the notation to ensure consistency and clarity.
