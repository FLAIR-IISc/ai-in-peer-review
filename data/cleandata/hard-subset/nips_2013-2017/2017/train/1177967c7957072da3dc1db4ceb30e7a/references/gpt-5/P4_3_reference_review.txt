Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for randomized optimization methods, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It first shows that, under a standard quadratic upper-bound model, the gradient-based sampling for CD—where coordinates are sampled proportionally to √Li |∇i f(x)| and the stepsize is chosen accordingly—dominates fixed (static) importance sampling based on Li, with potential speedups up to a factor n in special cases. The analogous result for SGD (sampling components proportional to gradient norms) is revisited and simplified.

The main contribution addresses the practical challenge that gradient-based sampling requires full gradient information each iteration. The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive sampling scheme computed from cheap per-iteration lower/upper bounds on the (coordinate) gradient magnitudes (for CD) or component gradient norms (for SGD). The sampling is computed by solving a minimax problem over the uncertainty set Ck = {c: `k ≤ c ≤ uk}, namely minp∈Δ maxc∈Ck V(p,c)/||c||^2 with V(p,c) = ∑i Li c_i^2 / p_i, and using stepsize αk = 1/vk where vk is the optimal value. The resulting distribution is shown to have the best worst-case guarantee over the uncertainty set and to be never worse than fixed Li-based sampling. An O(n log n) algorithm (sorting-based, akin to water-filling) computes the solution and recovers full-information gradient-based sampling when `k = uk.

For GLMs with separable regularizers, the paper shows how to maintain safe bounds on gradients cheaply via incremental updates using inner products; similar bounds are derived for finite-sum SGD cases, with computational overhead O(n) to update bounds and O(n log n) to compute the distribution per iteration. Experiments on several text datasets show substantial improvements for CD and smaller gains for SGD, while time overhead is reported to be small in practice. The approach connects to and extends prior adaptive and non-uniform sampling work for CD/SGD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč, Nesterov, Zhao & Zhang, Csiba et al., Allen-Zhu et al., Stich et al.), but differs by giving a principled safe min-max formulation with guarantees under partial information.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The one-step expected progress model and derivation of optimal full-information sampling for CD are correct and consistent with known results (p ∝ √Li|gi|, α ∝ ||g||^2/||√L g||1). The unbiased update with αk/pik is standard.
- The limited-information formulation minp maxc∈C V(p,c)/||c||^2 is well-motivated. The existence of a saddle point and exchange of min/max are plausible under convexity/compactness (Sion-type arguments are cited).
- Theorems claim that the safe sampling is minimax optimal over Ck and never worse than Li-based sampling. The logic is sound given the V functional and the feasible inclusion of pL.
- The O(n log n) algorithm based on thresholding (capped at bounds or at √Lim) is consistent with water-filling solutions; deriving p from c via p ∝ √L c matches the Lagrangian conditions.
- The bounds construction for GLMs via gradient tracking and Cauchy–Schwarz yields valid safe intervals; however, tightness may be weak in practice.
- Convergence beyond one-step progress is said to follow from standard techniques; summarizing the resulting rates in the main text would strengthen the presentation.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized, with precise notation and explicit algorithms. Minor typos and grammatical issues exist but do not impede understanding.
- The safe-bounds maintenance is explained concretely for GLMs; stating explicit formulas for the resulting `k, uk would improve clarity.
- Complexity discussion could be clearer on regimes where O(n log n) per iteration is negligible, and on practical strategies (e.g., updating the sampling distribution less frequently).

Originality
- The minimax “safe” adaptive sampling under partial information with guarantees strictly better than any fixed distribution (in the worst case over Ck) appears novel. The constructive O(n log n) algorithm and its recovery of full-information sampling are also new.
- Prior adaptive strategies often rely on stale gradients or heuristic upper bounds; this work formalizes the limited-information setting and provides worst-case guarantees.

Significance
- For CD, the approach is potentially impactful: it bridges the gap between theoretically optimal gradient-based sampling and practical per-iteration costs, with guarantees and strong empirical gains.
- For SGD, experiments indicate modest improvement; the significance may be limited unless tighter bounds or cheaper updates are available.
- The overhead O(n log n) per iteration can be substantial when n is very large; applicability may hinge on problem structure (e.g., when computing a single coordinate/component gradient is already Ω(n) or when updates are amortized).

Strengths
- Clear theoretical justification for adaptive sampling in CD and a principled, safe limited-information alternative.
- Minimax guarantee: never worse than fixed importance sampling; recovers optimal sampling when bounds tighten.
- Efficient algorithm to compute the distribution.
- Practical bound-maintenance recipes for GLMs; empirical validation on multiple datasets.
- Careful discussion of stepsize choice and its importance.

Weaknesses and questions
- Computational overhead: O(n log n) per iteration may be prohibitive for very high-dimensional problems (e.g., millions of coordinates). Can p̂ be updated less frequently or incrementally?
- Dependence on bound tightness: with very loose bounds (e.g., `0 = 0, u0 = ∞), early-iteration benefits are limited; more systematic strategies for tighter bounds would help.
- Limited baselines: missing comparisons to approximate Gauss–Southwell rules, stale-gradient adaptive sampling, or other recent adaptive CD methods beyond uniform/Li-based.
- SGD gains are small; analysis or guidance on when meaningful benefits can be expected (e.g., variance structure, condition numbers) would be useful.
- Global convergence rates are relegated to the appendix; a brief statement in the main text would strengthen the contribution.

Suggestions
- Evaluate scalability on larger n (without subsampling features), report wall-clock for computing p̂ separately, and explore lazy/periodic recomputation.
- Compare against approximate GS and other adaptive CD baselines; include ablations on bound tightness strategies (e.g., using data-dependent tighter inner-product bounds).
- Consider mini-batch variants and integration with variance reduction methods for SGD.
- Clarify assumptions and computation of coordinate-wise Lipschitz constants in general settings.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled and novel safe adaptive sampling under limited information with solid theory.
- Strong empirical improvements for CD, with minimal assumptions and easy integration.
- Clear potential to influence practice for coordinate methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Scalability concerns due to O(n log n) per-iteration overhead and O(n) bound updates.
- Limited improvement for SGD and limited baseline comparisons.
- Some theoretical results (full convergence rates) not prominently presented.

Overall, the paper provides a meaningful and well-justified contribution to adaptive sampling for CD, with a novel and practical formulation under partial information. The impact for SGD is less clear, and additional scalability experiments and baselines would strengthen the case.
