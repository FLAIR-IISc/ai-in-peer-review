

# Peer Review: "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary of Main Contributions

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in large-scale optimization: efficient adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors argue that while adaptive importance sampling (based on gradient information) can theoretically outperform fixed sampling strategies by orders of magnitude (e.g., up to a factor of n in favorable cases), previous approaches either require expensive full-gradient computations or lack theoretical guarantees that they outperform fixed sampling.

The paper's key contribution is a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme that:
1. Can be efficiently computed in every iteration with O(n log n) complexity
2. Is provably better than any fixed importance sampling strategy
3. Recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting
4. Works with safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information (avoiding full gradient computation)

The authors provide a minimax formulation to find the optimal sampling distribution under gradient bounds, prove theoretical guarantees, and demonstrate practical implementation for generalized linear models (GLMs) with L1/L2 regularization. The empirical results show significant improvements over fixed sampling for CD methods, though the gains are more modest for SGD.

This work builds on previous research on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) but addresses a critical gap: previous approaches either required full gradient computation (inadmissible for large-scale problems) or used approximations without theoretical guarantees of improvement over fixed sampling.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides clear theoretical guarantees that the proposed method is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), which is a significant contribution. The minimax formulation in Section 3.1 is novel and well-motivated.

2. **Practical Computational Complexity**: The authors demonstrate that the proposed method has O(n log n) computational complexity per iteration, which is often comparable to computing a single gradient component (Θ(d) for d-dimensional data). This makes the method truly practical for large-scale problems.

3. **Clear Connection Between Theory and Practice**: The paper successfully bridges theoretical guarantees with practical implementation, showing how to compute safe gradient bounds for GLMs (Section 4) and how to efficiently implement the sampling algorithm (Algorithm 4).

4. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation**: The experiments cover multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20), multiple loss functions (square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss), and both L1 and L2 regularization. The results consistently show that adaptive sampling outperforms fixed sampling in terms of convergence speed.

5. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear section headings, logical flow, and consistent notation. The key concepts are explained thoroughly with illustrative examples.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation**: While the paper demonstrates effectiveness for GLMs, it doesn't show how the method generalizes to more complex models (e.g., neural networks, non-GLM problems). The authors claim the approach is "completely generic," but this isn't convincingly demonstrated. It would be valuable to test on at least one non-GLM problem to show broader applicability.

2. **Inconsistent Performance Between CD and SGD**: The empirical results in Section 5 show a dramatic difference in performance between CD and SGD (significant gains for CD, only modest gains for SGD). The paper doesn't adequately explain this discrepancy. Why does the method work much better for CD than for SGD? The theoretical analysis in Section 2.2 for SGD doesn't seem to account for this empirical observation.

3. **Comparison with Other Adaptive Methods**: The paper compares only to fixed sampling (uniform and Li-based) but doesn't compare to other adaptive sampling methods that might be computationally cheaper (e.g., [22, 36, 37]). It would strengthen the argument to show how the proposed method compares to these alternatives, especially those that claim to be computationally efficient.

4. **Sensitivity to Gradient Bounds Quality**: The paper assumes safe gradient bounds can be computed efficiently for GLMs, but doesn't explore how the method performs when these bounds are loose or inaccurate. In practice, how sensitive is the performance to the quality of the bounds? A sensitivity analysis would be valuable.

5. **Limited Discussion of Non-Convex Settings**: The theoretical guarantees are for convex functions with coordinate-wise Lipschitz gradients. It's unclear how the method would perform on non-convex problems, which are increasingly common in machine learning (e.g., deep learning). The authors mention this limitation only briefly in the conclusion.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand Empirical Evaluation**: Include results on at least one non-GLM problem (e.g., a simple neural network training task) to demonstrate broader applicability. This would strengthen the claim that the method is "completely generic."

2. **Address CD vs. SGD Performance Discrepancy**: Provide a more detailed analysis of why adaptive sampling performs better for CD than for SGD. Is it related to the nature of the bounds or the optimization dynamics? This is a critical observation that deserves more attention.

3. **Add Comparison to Other Adaptive Methods**: Include a comparison with other computationally efficient adaptive sampling methods (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) to better contextualize the contribution.

4. **Include Sensitivity Analysis**: Add a section showing how performance degrades as the gradient bounds become looser. This would provide practical insights for implementation.

5. **Clarify Theoretical Scope**: Explicitly state the limitations of the theoretical guarantees (e.g., convexity requirement) and discuss potential extensions to non-convex settings.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization by providing a theoretically sound and computationally efficient method for adaptive importance sampling. The key innovation of using safe gradient bounds in a minimax formulation to guarantee improvement over fixed sampling is novel and well-executed.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides strong theoretical guarantees supported by empirical results. The main limitation is the narrow scope of the empirical validation (limited to GLMs), which prevents a full assessment of the method's generality.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper is a strong contribution to the field of optimization for large-scale machine learning. The theoretical guarantees are compelling, the computational complexity is well-analyzed, and the empirical results are convincing for the problems studied. The suggested revisions would make the paper even stronger, but the current version is already of high quality and makes a clear contribution to the field.

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in large-scale optimization with a practical solution that advances the state of the art. It would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Provides the first theoretically guaranteed method for adaptive importance sampling that is computationally efficient (O(n log n) per iteration)
- Solves a critical gap in the literature: previous adaptive methods either required full gradient computation or lacked theoretical guarantees of improvement over fixed sampling
- The method is generic and can be applied to both CD and SGD
- Empirical results consistently show significant improvements over fixed sampling for CD methods
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured

**Con Acceptance:**
- Empirical validation limited to GLMs (not tested on more complex models)
- Doesn't adequately explain why performance gains are much larger for CD than for SGD
- Lacks comparison to other adaptive sampling methods that might be computationally efficient
- Limited discussion of non-convex applications, which are increasingly important in machine learning

The strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and the suggested revisions are relatively minor. The contribution is substantial and directly relevant to the NIPS community's interest in efficient large-scale optimization methods.
