## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the theoretical benefits of adaptive sampling but acknowledging the practical challenges of computing optimal adaptive distributions. They propose a method that efficiently computes a safe approximation to gradient-based sampling, guaranteeing improvement over fixed sampling strategies while maintaining theoretical soundness. The key idea is to formulate the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem, leveraging available upper and lower bounds on gradient information. They provide theoretical analysis demonstrating performance guarantees and showcase empirical results on GLMs with various regularization techniques, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon existing work in adaptive importance sampling, specifically referencing gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37] and duality gap-based sampling [5, 23].  It clearly positions itself within the context of balancing theoretical guarantees with computational feasibility, addressing a known limitation of earlier adaptive methods. The connection to stochastic coordinate descent and its complexities is well-established [18, 19, 2].  The paper also correctly cites relevant works on coordinate descent convergence rates and optimization techniques [2, 9, 11, 35, 27]. The work addresses recent advancements in safe screening techniques [14, 15], and combines these with sampling techniques. 

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving that the proposed sampling strategy is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling. The connection to optimal gradient-based sampling is also clearly established. The convergence analysis, although summarized, points towards strong theoretical foundations.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm provides a practical and efficient solution.  The authors demonstrate that the computational overhead compared to fixed sampling is manageable, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The O(n log n) complexity for computing the sampling distribution is a significant achievement.
* **Generality:**  The scheme is generic and applicable to both CD and SGD, increasing its potential impact.
* **Safe Approximation:** The use of upper and lower bounds on gradients ensures a "safe" approximation, guaranteeing improvement over static sampling even with limited information. This addresses a critical practical concern.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results on various datasets and with different loss functions and regularization techniques provide strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivations, methodology, and results. The notation is consistent and accessible.
* **Practical Insights**: The analysis of how to track safe gradient bounds for GLMs offers practical guidance for implementing the proposed scheme.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The method relies on the availability of safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients. While the paper demonstrates how to obtain these bounds for GLMs, it doesn't deeply explore the challenges of obtaining such bounds for more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks). A more thorough discussion of these limitations would be beneficial.
* **Limited SGD Results:** While the authors demonstrate that the proposed scheme works for SGD, the improvements are less pronounced compared to CD. A deeper investigation into the reasons for this difference could strengthen the paper.
* **Complexity of Algorithm 4:** Although the authors claim O(n log n) complexity for Algorithm 4, the steps involved in sorting and checking inequalities might require more detailed justification. The constant factors might be significant for large datasets.
* **Limited discussion of practical initialization**: The authors mention the importance of initialization, but don't perform a deeper ablation of the sensitivity of the method to this choice.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in optimization, balancing theoretical guarantees with computational feasibility in adaptive sampling.
* The proposed method is novel, efficient, and theoretically sound.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the scheme on relevant datasets.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented.
* The contribution is likely to be of interest to a broad audience of researchers working on optimization and machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on gradient bounds could be a limiting factor for some applications.
* The improvements in SGD performance are less significant than in CD.
* The complexity analysis of Algorithm 4 could be more detailed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of adaptive optimization. The authors successfully address a challenging problem with a novel and well-motivated solution. The theoretical analysis and empirical results are convincing, and the paper is clearly written.  The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion on the challenges of obtaining safe gradient bounds for more complex models.
2.  Investigate the reasons for the smaller performance improvements in SGD and provide a more detailed analysis.
3.  Provide a more detailed justification for the O(n log n) complexity of Algorithm 4, potentially including a discussion of constant factors.
4.  Explore the sensitivity of the method to the initialization of gradient bounds.
