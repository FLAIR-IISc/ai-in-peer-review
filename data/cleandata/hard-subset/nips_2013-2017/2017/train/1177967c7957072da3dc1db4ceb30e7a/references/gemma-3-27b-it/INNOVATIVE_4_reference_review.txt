This paper introduces a novel approach to adaptive importance sampling, aiming to bridge the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical computation. The core idea – formulating the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem solvable with efficient bounds – is a strong and potentially impactful contribution. It directly addresses a known weakness of existing adaptive sampling methods, which often suffer from high computational cost or weak theoretical underpinnings. The connection to coordinate descent and stochastic gradient descent is well-motivated, and the empirical results, while not overwhelmingly groundbreaking, provide initial support for the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formulation of adaptive sampling as an optimization problem with safe bounds is genuinely novel. It provides a principled way to balance adaptivity with computational feasibility. The associated theorems establishing theoretical guarantees are significant.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides clear theoretical analysis, demonstrating improvements over static sampling and establishing connections to existing gradient-based approaches. The derivation of the bounds and the convergence analysis appear solid.
* **Generality:** The framework is presented as applicable to both coordinate descent and SGD, increasing its potential impact.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper acknowledges the computational cost and provides an efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) for computing the approximate sampling distribution. The discussion of how to obtain safe gradient bounds in practical scenarios (Section 4) is valuable.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear notation and a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Empirical Results:** While the empirical results are positive, the improvements over baseline methods are not dramatic. The comparisons with existing adaptive sampling methods (beyond uniform sampling) could be more comprehensive.  The experiments seem somewhat limited in scope and dataset variety.
* **Complexity of Implementation:**  While the algorithm is efficient in theory, the practical implementation and tuning of the safe bounds ` and `u may require significant effort. This is not fully addressed in the paper.  The overhead of computing the bounds in each iteration needs to be explicitly discussed in relation to the gains.
* **Conservative Approach:** The emphasis on “safe” bounds, while laudable from a theoretical perspective, may lead to overly conservative sampling distributions in practice, limiting the potential benefits. The paper could benefit from a discussion of how to dynamically adjust the tightness of the bounds.
* **SGD Results:** The observation that the method performs less impressively with SGD is somewhat concerning and warrants further investigation. The reasons for this discrepancy aren’t fully explored.
* **Lack of Comparison to Recent Advances:** The related work section, while extensive, doesn’t fully engage with the latest advances in adaptive sampling and variance reduction techniques.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a truly novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling.
* The problem addressed is important, and the proposed solution has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of optimization algorithms.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas and results.
* The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature.
* Even a modest improvement in practical performance, coupled with the theoretical guarantees, justifies acceptance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The empirical results are not sufficiently compelling to demonstrate a significant practical advantage over existing methods.
* The implementation complexity and the potential for conservative sampling could limit the real-world applicability of the approach.
* The lack of a thorough comparison to recent advances in the field weakens the claim of novelty.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of optimization. While the empirical results are not spectacular, the novelty and theoretical strength of the proposed approach are compelling. The paper introduces a new perspective on adaptive sampling and provides a solid foundation for future research. I lean towards **acceptance**, prioritizing the novel theoretical contribution and the potential for future impact over the limitations in the empirical evaluation. The authors should be encouraged to address the concerns regarding implementation complexity and the performance of SGD in future work. I suggest a minor revision, focusing on strengthening the empirical evaluation and providing a more detailed discussion of the practical considerations.
