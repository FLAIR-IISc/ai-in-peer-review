**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors first review the theoretical benefits of optimal, full-gradient-based adaptive sampling, which can offer significant speed-ups (up to a factor of `n`) over fixed or uniform sampling but is computationally infeasible. The core contribution is a method to approximate this optimal sampling distribution when only safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes are known. The proposed approach formulates the selection of the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem, seeking a distribution that performs best in the worst-case scenario allowed by the bounds. The authors provide an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm to solve this problem and prove that the resulting sampling strategy is never worse than standard fixed importance sampling. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on generalized linear models (GLMs) for which the required gradient bounds can be maintained efficiently.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The theoretical motivation in Section 2, which establishes the potential gains of adaptive sampling and introduces an adaptive step-size, is clear and well-argued. The main theoretical contribution—the min-max formulation (Eq. 7) for finding a robust sampling distribution and the associated proofs (Theorem 3.2)—appears correct and provides a principled way to handle uncertainty in gradient information. This "safety" guarantee, ensuring performance is no worse than a standard baseline (Li-based sampling), is a strong and valuable property that distinguishes it from heuristic approaches. The derivation of an efficient `O(n log n)` solver (Algorithm 4) for the proposed optimization problem is also a solid contribution.

However, the work's overall quality is undermined by the limited scope of its practical validation. The claims of efficiency and superiority rest on two key assumptions that are not rigorously tested: (1) that useful gradient bounds can be cheaply obtained for a wide class of problems, and (2) that the method's performance gains justify its computational overhead.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation is established clearly, the proposed method is derived step-by-step, and the key theoretical results are stated plainly. The inclusion of pseudo-code for the main algorithmic components aids reproducibility.

**Originality:**
The idea of using bounds on gradient information to inform adaptive sampling is not entirely new (e.g., [36, 23]). The primary novelty lies in the specific min-max formulation to find a robust distribution that is provably safe. This is a clever and theoretically elegant approach to dealing with the uncertainty inherent in using bounds, moving beyond simpler heuristics like using the upper bounds directly.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is high, as improved sampling for CD and SGD could impact a vast range of machine learning applications. However, the paper's current demonstration of this significance is not fully convincing.

My primary concerns are as follows:

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Baselines:** The experiments compare the proposed method ("safe adaptive") against two extremes: fixed uniform/importance sampling (a weak baseline) and optimal full-gradient sampling (an infeasible upper bound). This comparison is incomplete and potentially misleading. A rigorous evaluation must include comparisons against other *practical* adaptive sampling schemes from the literature, such as those using stale gradients [22] or other approximations based on bounds [36, 23]. Without such a comparison, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed "safe" formulation offers a meaningful advantage over existing, established adaptive heuristics.

2.  **Practicality of Gradient Bounds:** The entire method hinges on the ability to maintain tight and cheap bounds `l` and `u`. Section 4 demonstrates this for GLMs, using a fairly standard application of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality. These bounds can be quite loose, and it is unclear how the method would perform in such cases. The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the method to the quality of these bounds. More importantly, its applicability to more complex models prevalent at NeurIPS, such as neural networks, is not addressed. The claim that this is a "generic" improvement to SGD feels overstated when its practicality is only shown for a specific class of convex problems.

3.  **Computational Overhead:** The `O(n log n)` complexity to compute the sampling distribution at each iteration is a non-trivial cost. The authors argue this is negligible for "tall" data matrices where `d = Ω(n)`, but many real-world datasets are "fat" (`n >> d`), where this overhead could easily dominate the `O(d)` cost of a single coordinate update. While the timing plots in Figures 6 and 9 show an overall benefit, they are limited to a few specific datasets and do not provide a clear picture of the trade-off. A more thorough analysis of the regimes where the computational overhead is justified is needed.

4.  **Limited Gains for SGD:** The authors honestly report that for SGD, the performance gain of optimal sampling itself over uniform sampling is slight, and consequently, their proposed method also shows a less pronounced effect. Given that SGD is the dominant optimizer for large-scale deep learning, this observation significantly curtails the potential impact of the proposed method. The major benefits appear confined to the CD setting.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically principled min-max framework for adaptive sampling.
*   It provides a strong "safety" guarantee, ensuring the method is provably no worse than standard fixed importance sampling.
*   The proposed algorithm is supported by a sound theoretical analysis and an efficient implementation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental evaluation is critically flawed due to the absence of comparisons with relevant, state-of-the-art practical adaptive sampling methods.
*   The practical applicability of the method beyond the specific case of GLMs is not established.
*   The computational overhead of `O(n log n)` per iteration may be prohibitive in many common settings, and this trade-off is not adequately explored.
*   The observed benefits for SGD, a major target optimization framework, are marginal.

**Conclusion:**
This paper presents a theoretically elegant idea for robust adaptive importance sampling. The methodological contribution is sound and the provable "safety" is an appealing feature. However, the work in its current form feels incomplete. The claims of practical superiority are not substantiated by a rigorous experimental comparison against established, practical adaptive methods. The reliance on problem-specific bounds and the non-trivial computational overhead raise further questions about the method's general applicability and significance. While the core idea is promising, it requires more thorough validation to be endorsed as a practical advancement over the state of the art.
