- Proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling method for CD and SGD using safe lower/upper gradient bounds that avoids full gradient computation and is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling, with up to n-fold speedup for CD.

- Builds on existing adaptive sampling literature but distinguishes itself by ensuring "safety" (provable improvement) without expensive gradient computations, supported by key lemmas and GLM experiments.

- Theoretical soundness: provides strong guarantees (Theorem 3.2) and precise complexity bounds, closing the gap between optimal and practical sampling.

- Practical implementation: low overhead (O(n log n) per iteration, often O(log n)), making it suitable for large-scale problems where d = Ω(n).

- Clear exposition: well-organized presentation, clear mathematical formulations, and structured algorithms.

- Comprehensive empirical validation: experiments across multiple datasets, losses, and regularizations, showing clear benefits for CD.

- Limited SGD gains: empirical improvements for SGD are minimal and the paper does not sufficiently explain when the method benefits SGD.

- Narrow application scope: safe bounds are derived for GLMs via Cauchy-Schwarz and extensions to complex models (e.g., neural nets) are not demonstrated.

- Missing comparisons: lacks direct experimental comparison to other adaptive methods (e.g., gradient-stale approaches) that might be more relevant benchmarks.

- Overlooked practical behavior of bounds: assumes efficient computation of bounds but does not analyze how quickly they tighten during optimization.

- Overall assessment: high-quality, novel, and practically implementable work with strong CD results, clear theoretical contributions, and minor limitations.

- Recommendation: accept — significant contribution with minor limitations that do not outweigh its strengths.
