Summary
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It first revisits the “full-information” setting, showing that gradient-based, iteration-dependent sampling (for CD, sampling proportional to √Li|∇if(xk)|) improves the expected one-step progress over fixed importance sampling (proportional to Li), with potential n-fold speedup in favorable cases. The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive scheme when full gradients are unavailable. The authors assume per-iteration safe lower and upper bounds on the per-coordinate gradient magnitudes (for CD) or per-component gradient norms (for SGD). They then pose a min–max problem to choose the sampling distribution p that minimizes the worst-case bound on expected progress over the uncertainty set Ck defined by these bounds. They prove (i) a best worst-case guarantee within this model and (ii) that their p̂ is always at least as good as fixed importance sampling (again, within the quadratic smoothness model). They derive an O(n log n) algorithm to compute p̂ given `k, uk and show how to maintain simple safe bounds for GLMs using Cauchy–Schwarz. Experiments on several text datasets show substantial gains for CD compared to uniform/fixed sampling, and modest gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
There is a substantial literature on non-uniform/adaptive sampling for CD and SGD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.; Csiba et al.; Zhao & Zhang; Perekrestenko et al.; approximate/steepest CD; gap-safe rules for screening). The paper’s full-information optimal distribution is aligned with gradient-based sampling discussed previously. The novelty here appears to be the specific min–max “safe” formulation that, given bounds on gradients, yields a distribution guaranteed (under the smoothness model) to dominate fixed sampling and that recovers the full-information scheme when bounds collapse.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: adaptive sampling can be superior but is often impractical; using bounds to get a tractable surrogate is reasonable.
- The min–max formulation and solution characterization (Theorems 3.2, 3.4) are neat and, to my knowledge, not standard in this form.
- The guarantee “never worse than fixed importance sampling” within the model is appealing and improves over heuristic uses of stale gradients or upper bounds.
- The O(n log n) construction from bounds is explicit; the geometric interpretation is informative.
- Empirically, for CD, the scheme often matches much of the benefit of full gradient-based sampling.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practicality and overhead. The method requires per-iteration O(n) bound maintenance plus O(n log n) sampling computation, which is substantial relative to standard CD/SGD that aim for O(1) overhead per iteration. The paper argues this is acceptable when d = Ω(n), but many large-scale problems have d << n, and CD’s appeal often lies in very cheap iterations. It is unclear how this scales when n is 10^6+. Please provide experiments on larger n and detailed profiling comparing total overhead to baselines optimized for low per-iteration cost.
- Evolving bounds and sorting. Theorem 3.4 invokes sorting √L^{-1}`k and √L^{-1}uk. Since `k, uk change every iteration, do you re-sort at each iteration? If not, how is sorting amortized? If yes, O(n log n) per iteration may dominate. A more explicit complexity accounting is needed.
- Initialization with infinite bounds. Experiments start with `0=0 and u0=∞. How is Algorithm 4 defined with ∞? The min–max problem is ill-posed with unbounded Ck. In practice, do you cap u0 to a finite value? If so, is “safety” preserved?
- Model-based “safety.” The “never worse” guarantee is with respect to the quadratic upper bound model (L-smoothness) and the uncertainty set. This does not directly imply that the method is never worse in terms of actual objective progress or iteration complexity, let alone wall-clock time. The paper should be clearer about this limitation and, ideally, provide end-to-end convergence rates that dominate fixed sampling (not just one-step model-based bounds).
- Novelty vs. prior adaptive sampling/screening. There is related work on adaptive probabilities based on optimality violations/duality gaps (e.g., Csiba–Qu–Richtárik; Perekrestenko et al.), and gap-safe screening rules (Ndiaye et al.) that also use safe bounds. The paper should better position how the proposed min–max design differs in theory and practice from these (e.g., do they also admit “never worse” guarantees in a comparable sense?). A head-to-head empirical comparison against these established adaptive strategies is missing and important.
- Limited SGD benefits. The method yields small gains for SGD, consistent with prior observations that importance sampling can be less decisive for SGD unless carefully matched to variance properties. This weakens the generality claims; the paper should temper statements for SGD and/or provide cases where the method clearly helps for SGD beyond marginal improvements.
- Experimental design details. Datasets are subsetted and preprocessed (all non-zeros set to 1); λ fixed to 0.1 across tasks; timing results come from different machines. These choices make it harder to generalize conclusions. Also missing are comparisons to steepest CD (Gauss–Southwell) or approximate steepest rules, which are strong adaptive baselines.
- Clarity/rigor. Several derivations and proofs are deferred; convergence rates for the practical algorithm are only sketched. Algorithm 4 reuses symbols (u as index and vector) and is hard to follow. The paper would benefit from cleaning typos and improving exposition.

Quality
Technically interesting and mostly sound within the smoothness model; however, key practical issues (handling ∞ bounds, per-iteration cost with changing bounds, end-to-end rates) need clarification. Empirical validation is limited with respect to established adaptive baselines and scalability.

Clarity
Overall readable but with many typos and some confusing algorithmic descriptions. A more thorough and careful presentation of Algorithm 4 and complexity is needed.

Originality
Moderate. The specific min–max “safe” construction appears novel; the broader theme of adaptive sampling from partial gradient information is well studied.

Significance
Potentially useful for CD on certain convex problems, but practical impact is uncertain given overhead and limited gains for SGD. Stronger evidence and comparisons are needed to influence practice.

Pros
- Conceptually clean min–max design with a constructive algorithm.
- Model-based guarantee dominating fixed sampling.
- Empirical gains for CD on standard datasets.

Cons
- Questionable practicality due to O(n)–O(n log n) per-iteration overhead and evolving bounds.
- Ambiguity around initialization with infinite bounds.
- Limited SGD benefits; missing comparisons to strong adaptive baselines (Gauss–Southwell, gap-based).
- Convergence guarantees for the practical algorithm not convincingly established in the main text.

Questions for authors
1) How do you handle u0=∞ in Algorithm 4? Do you cap or warm-start in a way that preserves safety?
2) Do you re-sort at every iteration? If not, how is the sorting reused as `k, uk change?
3) Can you provide end-to-end convergence rates showing global improvement over fixed sampling for the practical scheme?
4) Please include comparisons to Gauss–Southwell/approximate steepest CD and gap-based adaptive probabilities.
5) Can you provide scalability experiments with n up to 10^6 and detailed runtime breakdowns?
