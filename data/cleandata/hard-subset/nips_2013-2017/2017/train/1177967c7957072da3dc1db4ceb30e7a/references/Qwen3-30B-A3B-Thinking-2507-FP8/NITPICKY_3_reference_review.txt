

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors argue that while gradient-based adaptive sampling offers theoretical advantages over fixed sampling, it's computationally expensive to implement. Their solution computes approximate adaptive sampling using safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, with theoretical guarantees that the proposed sampling never performs worse than fixed importance sampling.

The paper has a clear structure and presents a practical approach to a well-motivated problem. The theoretical foundation in Section 2 is generally sound, though I identified several technical inconsistencies that need addressing. The main contribution is the safe sampling framework in Section 3, which appears to be a reasonable extension of previous work on adaptive importance sampling.

**Major Concerns:**

1. **Notational Inconsistencies and Typos:** The paper contains multiple notational errors that undermine technical precision. In equation (1), "Lu = L" should be "L_u = L" to indicate the Lipschitz constant depends on direction. The expression "η 2Lu 2 ‖u‖ 2" in equation (1) should be "η²L‖u‖²". In Example 2.2, Tr[L] is used without definition (it should be the trace of L, i.e., sum of Li). The definition of L_min (minimum Li) is also missing from the main text.

2. **Algorithm 4 Initialization Error:** The initialization "c = 0n, u = 1, ` = n" is problematic. Variables u and ` should be initialized as vectors of appropriate size, not scalars. This could cause implementation errors.

3. **Gradient Bound Construction:** The paper claims to maintain both upper and lower bounds on gradient entries using Cauchy-Schwarz, but |〈ai,aik〉| ≤ ‖ai‖·‖aik‖ only provides upper bounds. The paper lacks a method to obtain meaningful lower bounds, which are essential for the proposed method.

4. **Computational Complexity Claim:** The claim that "when d = Ω(n), the introduced overhead reduces to O(log n) per iteration" is misleading. The O(n log n) sampling computation would be dominated by the O(d) gradient computation per coordinate, making the overhead O(1), not O(log n).

5. **Theoretical Comparison:** The paper doesn't clearly distinguish its approach from related work, particularly the "Gradient-based sampling" paper by Rong Zhu (cited as [26] but missing from references). The paper should explicitly explain how their "safe" approach differs from previous adaptive sampling methods.

**Minor Concerns:**

1. In equation (3), the term "(∇ikf(xk)) 2" should be written as "(∇ikf(xk))²" for consistency with mathematical notation.

2. The paper states "the computational cost of the proposed scheme is of the order O(n log n) per iteration" but fails to clarify that this refers to the sampling computation only, not the full gradient evaluation.

3. In Section 5, the paper claims "the measured value vk was always significantly below the worst case estimate," but provides no concrete evidence or comparison to the theoretical worst case.

**Strengths:**

- The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for adaptive importance sampling.
- The safe sampling approach is well-motivated and has provable guarantees.
- The empirical evaluation on GLMs demonstrates practical benefits over fixed sampling.
- The paper is well-organized and generally clear, with appropriate references to related work.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a practical improvement to adaptive importance sampling with theoretical guarantees, the technical inconsistencies and unclear claims significantly undermine its contribution. The notational errors and missing definitions make it difficult to fully assess the theoretical claims. The computational complexity claim is misleading, and the paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from prior work. The empirical results support the main claims but lack sufficient detail for full reproducibility.

The paper's contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking, and the minor technical flaws prevent it from being a strong candidate for NIPS. While the approach is sound and practical, the paper would benefit from significant technical refinement before publication. The theoretical results are not sufficiently novel to warrant acceptance at NIPS in their current form.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper has significant technical flaws that need to be addressed before it can be considered a strong contribution to the field. The minor methodological details identified here are substantial enough to outweigh the paper's practical benefits.
