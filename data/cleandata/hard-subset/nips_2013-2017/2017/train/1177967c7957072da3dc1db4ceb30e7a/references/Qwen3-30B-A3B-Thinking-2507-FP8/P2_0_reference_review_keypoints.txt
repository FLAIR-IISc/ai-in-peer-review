- Proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for CD and SGD that uses gradient bounds instead of full gradients.  
- Proves the sampling is never worse than fixed importance sampling while capturing much of the benefit of optimal gradient-based sampling.  
- Shows safe gradient bounds can be efficiently maintained for GLMs and sampling can be computed in O(n log n) per iteration.  

- Theorem 3.2 and supporting lemmas provide strong theoretical guarantees that the safe sampling outperforms fixed sampling.  
- Method is computationally practical and yields significant improvements even with loose gradient bounds.  
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes helpful examples and pseudocode (Algorithms 1–4).  
- Empirical results across multiple datasets and loss functions show consistent improvements in iteration count and runtime, especially for CD.  
- Approach is broadly applicable to CD and SGD with concrete implementation details for GLMs.  

- Limited development of gradient bound construction beyond GLMs; no concrete non-GLM examples provided.  
- Adaptive sampling yields only marginal empirical benefits for SGD, unlike strong gains for CD, with limited explanation for the gap.  
- The claimed O(n log n) overhead is not fully contextualized relative to the cost of computing a single gradient component (Θ(d)).  
- Insufficient direct comparison with other recent adaptive/non-uniform sampling methods to highlight distinctions.  

- Theoretical analysis is rigorous with well-constructed proofs and appropriate experimental design.  
- Paper is clearly written and accessible, though some technical proofs are deferred to the appendix.  
- The safe sampling formulation and competitive ratio analysis are original contributions.  
- Work is significant: practical, theoretically grounded improvement to adaptive importance sampling with potential impact on large-scale ML.  
- Recommendation: accept (NIPS) — strong theoretical and empirical contribution despite limited SGD benefits.  
- Final thought: a valuable, well-executed paper that advances practical adaptive sampling with provable guarantees.
