This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The central problem it addresses is that existing adaptive sampling methods, while often empirically powerful, can be computationally expensive (e.g., requiring a full gradient calculation) or lack theoretical guarantees, sometimes performing worse than simple uniform sampling.

The core idea is to operate with incomplete information, specifically by maintaining safe lower and upper bounds (`l`, `u`) on the gradient magnitudes for each coordinate or data point. Instead of using a heuristic based on these bounds (e.g., sampling based on the upper bounds `u`), the authors formulate the problem of choosing the sampling distribution `p` as a minimax optimization problem. The goal is to find the distribution `p` that minimizes the worst-case value of a key term in the convergence analysis, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradient vectors `c` consistent with the bounds `[l, u]`. This principled approach leads to a "safe" sampling distribution `p̂` that is provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform or `L_i`-based sampling). The authors provide an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm to compute this distribution and demonstrate how the required bounds can be maintained efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs).

This work is well-situated within the extensive literature on stochastic and coordinate-wise optimization. It builds directly upon the line of research in adaptive importance sampling [22, 5, 23, 36], which aims to improve upon fixed importance sampling schemes [18, 25]. The key distinction and primary contribution of this paper is the introduction of the "safe" minimax framework. Prior work often relied on stale gradient information [22] or approximations based on upper bounds [36, 23], which lack the robust worst-case guarantee provided here. The proposed method provides a theoretical bridge between the computationally expensive "optimal" sampling and the less effective but cheap fixed sampling schemes.

### Strengths:

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The minimax formulation for deriving the sampling distribution (Equation 7) is a highly novel and elegant contribution. It provides a principled way to handle the uncertainty inherent in not knowing the full gradient. This "safe" property, guaranteeing performance at least as good as fixed sampling (Theorem 3.2), is a significant theoretical advance that addresses a key drawback of previous adaptive methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development is clear, culminating in an efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) for computing the proposed sampling distribution. The proof sketch for Theorem 3.4, based on analyzing optimality conditions, is convincing. The connection drawn between the optimization problem and a geometric interpretation (minimizing the angle with the vector of Lipschitz constants `l`) provides useful intuition.

3.  **Practical Relevance:** The authors ground their theoretical framework in a practical setting by showing how to efficiently compute and update the required gradient bounds (`l`, `u`) for an important class of problems (GLMs) in Section 4. The `O(n log n)` complexity of computing the sampling distribution is often acceptable, especially when the cost of a single coordinate gradient evaluation is high (i.e., when `d` is large).

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. It logically progresses from the ideal full-information setting (Section 2) to the practical limited-information setting (Section 3), motivating the core contribution very effectively. The experimental section is thorough, evaluating performance in terms of both iterations and wall-clock time, and honestly reports both successes (for CD) and limitations (less pronounced gains for SGD).

### Weaknesses:

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The practical performance of the method is critically dependent on the tightness of the bounds `l` and `u`. The bounds derived in Section 4 via the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality can be quite loose, particularly for high-dimensional data where data vectors `a_i` and `a_ik` may be nearly orthogonal, leading to a large gap between the true inner product and the bound. If the bounds are very loose (e.g., `l_i -> 0`, `u_i -> infinity`), the safe sampling distribution would presumably revert to a non-adaptive one. A discussion on the sensitivity of the method to bound quality would strengthen the paper. For instance, an experiment on synthetic data where bound tightness could be controlled would be illustrative.

2.  **Scalability of `O(n log n)` Overhead:** While the complexity analysis is correct, the `O(n log n)` per-iteration overhead may still be a bottleneck for problems with extremely large `n` (e.g., millions of data points or features), even if `d` is large. The experiments are conducted on datasets with `n` up to ~72k, where this cost is manageable. The paper would be more complete with a discussion of this limitation and potential avenues for mitigation, such as applying the safe sampling computation to a randomly selected subset of coordinates or updating the distribution less frequently.

3.  **Limited Discussion on SGD vs. CD Performance:** The authors empirically observe that the gains from adaptive sampling are significantly smaller for SGD than for CD. This is an important finding, but the paper offers little intuition or analysis as to *why* this might be the case. Is it because the variance inherent in sampling data points in SGD is the dominant factor, making the choice of which point to sample less critical? Or is the distribution of `||∇f_i(x)||` norms typically more uniform than that of `|∇_i f(x)|` values? Expanding on this observation would add considerable depth.

### Summary and Recommendation:

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The "safe" adaptive importance sampling framework is theoretically sound, practically relevant, and elegantly addresses a known weakness of prior adaptive methods. The paper is clearly written and supported by solid theoretical analysis and empirical evidence. The identified weaknesses are more in the vein of limitations and avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   A novel and principled minimax formulation for adaptive sampling.
*   Strong theoretical guarantees of being provably better than fixed importance sampling.
*   An efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm for implementation.
*   Demonstrated practical applicability to GLMs.
*   Thorough and honest experimental validation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The method's performance relies heavily on the quality of gradient bounds, which can be loose.
*   The per-iteration overhead could be prohibitive for web-scale problems.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a complete and important piece of work that is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers. I strongly recommend acceptance.
