## NIPS 2017 Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

**Paper Summary:** This paper proposes a novel and efficient adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling while guaranteeing theoretical improvements over uniform or fixed importance sampling. The approach centers around formulating an optimization problem using safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients and efficiently solving it to determine the sampling distribution. The authors provide theoretical analysis showing speedups in CD and demonstrate the scheme's applicability and effectiveness through experiments on real-world datasets.

**Relation to Previous Work:** The paper builds on a rich body of work in adaptive importance sampling. It correctly positions itself within the landscape of existing methods like uniform sampling, fixed importance sampling, and gradient-based sampling, highlighting their limitations. The authors acknowledge previous work on gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37] and duality-gap-based sampling [5, 23], and emphasize the challenges of computational cost and theoretical guarantees associated with these methods. The connection to stochastic coordinate descent and variance reduction techniques is clearly articulated. The comparison with existing work and the clear articulation of limitations are strong points.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper offers solid theoretical foundations for the proposed method. The analysis demonstrating the speedup in CD (up to a factor of n) is compelling. The guarantee of being provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling is a significant advantage.  The derivation of optimal stepsize strategies further strengthens the theoretical contribution.
* **Practical Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is designed to be computationally tractable. The algorithm for approximating the optimal sampling distribution (Algorithm 4) has a time complexity of O(n log n), which is manageable, especially when compared to the cost of computing full gradients.  The discussion of efficient gradient bound tracking in GLMs further enhances the practical appeal.
* **Generality:** The method is presented as being applicable to both CD and SGD, increasing its potential impact. The fact that it can be easily integrated into existing algorithms is a major strength.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are explained clearly. The inclusion of Algorithm 1, 2, 3 and 4 significantly aid understanding.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed sampling scheme, particularly in the CD setting. The detailed analysis comparing the proposed method with other sampling strategies (uniform, optimal) is valuable.
* **Safe Approximation:** The focus on *safe* approximations, guaranteeing at least performance comparable to fixed sampling, is a crucial contribution, addressing a common weakness in adaptive sampling schemes.

**Weaknesses:**

* **SGD Performance:** While the theory extends to SGD, the empirical results show only modest improvements over uniform sampling in that context. The authors acknowledge this, but a deeper investigation into why the performance gap is smaller for SGD would be beneficial. Perhaps the bounds used in SGD are significantly less informative than in CD.
* **Gradient Bound Assumptions:** The effectiveness of the method heavily relies on the quality of the safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients.  While the paper demonstrates how to compute these bounds for GLMs, it doesn’t address the challenges of obtaining accurate bounds for more complex models. The robustness of the method to imprecise bounds could be explored.
* **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The analysis of the competitive ratio (ρk) while present, feels somewhat underdeveloped.  While Lemma 3.6 provides a bound, it relies on a specific condition on the bounds ` and `u`. More discussion on the practical implications of this condition and how to assess it would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  The experiments are primarily focused on GLMs.  Extending the evaluation to more diverse optimization problems would increase the confidence in the general applicability of the method.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-founded approach to adaptive importance sampling.
* It addresses a practical challenge in adaptive sampling – computational cost – by proposing an efficient approximation algorithm.
* The guarantee of being provably better than fixed sampling is a significant strength.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in the CD setting.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The performance gains in the SGD setting are modest.
* The method's reliance on accurate gradient bounds could be a limitation for certain applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed algorithm is efficient, and the experimental results are encouraging. While the performance in the SGD setting could be improved and further investigation into the robustness to inaccurate gradient bounds would be beneficial, these weaknesses are not severe enough to outweigh the strengths of the work. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10

I believe this paper is a worthy addition to NIPS 2017. It presents a novel and practical approach to adaptive importance sampling with strong theoretical guarantees and promising empirical results.  It is a significant step forward in making adaptive sampling more accessible and effective for a wider range of optimization problems.
