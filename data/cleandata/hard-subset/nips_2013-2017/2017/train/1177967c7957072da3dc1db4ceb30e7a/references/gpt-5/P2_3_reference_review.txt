Summary
The paper addresses adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors start by showing that gradient-based adaptive sampling can substantially improve one-step progress over fixed distributions (uniform or Li-based), with up to an n-fold gain in favorable cases. The key contribution is a “safe” adaptive sampling scheme that, given per-coordinate lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (CD) or component gradient norms (SGD), selects a distribution p̂ by solving a min–max problem: choose p to minimize the worst-case quadratic upper-bound V(p,c)/‖c‖^2 over all feasible c consistent with the bounds. They show that this choice is (i) optimal in the worst case given the limited information, (ii) never worse than fixed importance sampling, and (iii) recovers the full-information gradient-based distribution when exact gradients are known. They provide an O(n log n) algorithm to compute p̂ and a corresponding stepsize α̂ = 1/vk. They also show how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs) and provide experiments on several datasets showing strong gains for CD and modest gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on literature on CD and SGD sampling: uniform and fixed importance sampling [Nesterov; Richtárik & Takáč], gradient-based sampling for SGD [Zhao & Zhang; others], and adaptive strategies based on duality gaps/violations [Perekrestenko et al.; Csiba et al.].
- Distinguishes itself by formulating a robust min–max selection under interval uncertainty on gradients and by proving that the proposed p̂ is always at least as good as fixed importance sampling according to the standard quadratic model, with an efficient exact algorithm for the distribution.
- Connects to Gauss–Southwell-style selection by showing the potential n-fold speedup in the equal-L case (bridging to steepest coordinate descent behavior).

Quality
- Theory: The expected progress bound for CD with directionally unbiased step scaling is standard and sound. The derivation of the full-information optimal distribution (p* ∝ √L |∇f|) follows from minimizing V(p,∇f), and the speedup argument is clear. The min–max formulation under interval bounds is well-motivated; the guarantees that p̂ achieves the best worst-case ratio and is never worse than fixed Li-based sampling are correctly stated. The existence of α̂ = 1/vk as the optimal stepsize under the model is consistent. The O(n log n) construction via thresholding/clipping (defining ĉ with components in {ℓi, √Lim, ui}) is plausible and supported by KKT-type reasoning.
- Practicality: The approach requires maintaining safe bounds (ℓ,u) and recomputing p̂ each iteration. The paper proposes simple updates via Cauchy–Schwarz and shows O(n) bound maintenance and O(n log n) sampling computation. This is a double-edged sword: it is straightforward but can be costly when n is large relative to feature dimension d or when standard CD implementations exploit sparsity to keep per-iteration costs near nnz(ai_k). The authors argue the overhead is small when d = Ω(n); however, many ML problems have n ≫ d, so this assumption needs careful discussion.
- Experiments: For CD on GLMs with L1/L2 and a few text datasets, the proposed method substantially improves iteration and time to accuracy relative to uniform/fixed sampling, approaching the oracle full-gradient sampling. For SGD, improvements are mild, matching the theory’s suggestion that benefits can be smaller. Timings are provided but not always on the same hardware; still, trends are reasonable. A notable omission is comparison to other adaptive CD sampling baselines (e.g., gap-based/violation-based or stale-gradient schemes), which would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The derivations and algorithms are clear at a high level. The statement of the min–max problem, its geometric interpretation, and the thresholding rule are helpful.
- Some points could be clearer:
  - The computational trade-offs: when does the O(n) bound maintenance dominate? Guidance on update frequency (e.g., recompute p̂ every few steps) would help.
  - Details of bound maintenance for general h in GLMs: the reliance on ∇^2h and its efficient bounding should be elaborated with concrete examples beyond least squares.
  - Pseudocode for Algorithm 4 is terse; a short proof sketch for the optimality of ĉ’s form in the main text would aid comprehension.

Originality
- The safe min–max formulation that uses interval bounds to derive a sampling distribution with worst-case optimality and a provable dominance over fixed sampling appears novel. The O(n log n) exact solver exploiting a threshold m and clipping to {ℓi, √Lim, ui} is also a neat technical contribution. While adaptive sampling itself is not new, the robust-with-limited-information angle with guarantees seems new to the NeurIPS literature.

Significance
- The contribution is practically relevant for CD and potentially for SGD, offering a principled way to exploit partial gradient information without risking worse performance than standard fixed schemes. The theoretical guarantee “never worse than fixed” is compelling.
- The practical impact may be tempered by per-iteration overhead in regimes where n is large and updates are sparse; further engineering (lazy updates, periodic recomputation) may be needed. The observed strong CD gains suggest the idea could influence practice for classes of problems where bound maintenance is cheap or can be parallelized. For SGD, the significance is moderate given smaller gains.

Pros
- Clear theoretical motivation and guarantees; recovers full-information optimum when available.
- Elegant O(n log n) algorithm with explicit solution structure.
- Empirical gains for CD are substantial and consistent with theory.
- Uses only safe bounds; robust to looseness of bounds.

Cons
- Bound maintenance and p̂ computation are O(n)–O(n log n) per iteration; may be heavy in common regimes (n ≫ d, sparse data).
- Limited empirical comparisons to other adaptive sampling baselines beyond uniform/fixed and oracle; no Gauss–Southwell/violation-based comparisons.
- SGD benefits are modest; discussion of when to expect large gains could be deeper.
- Timing results across different machines reduce comparability.

Questions and suggestions
- Can p̂ and vk be recomputed less frequently (e.g., every T iterations) without losing most benefits? Any empirical sensitivity study?
- For general GLMs, how to bound 〈ai, ∇^2h(Ax̃) aik〉 tightly and cheaply? Examples beyond least squares would help.
- Can you exploit sparsity to update bounds only for coordinates with large potential change (e.g., via screening-like ideas) to reduce O(n) maintenance?
- Please include comparisons to adaptive gap/violation-based CD sampling and stale gradient-based SGD as stronger baselines.
- Provide per-iteration profiling to quantify the overhead and its scaling with n,d,nnz.

Overall evaluation
- Quality: good theoretical development, reasonable experiments; practical cost trade-offs need more nuance.
- Clarity: good, with room for more detail on computational aspects.
- Originality: strong within adaptive sampling literature.
- Significance: moderate to strong for CD; moderate for SGD.

I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing practical overhead and adding comparisons to other adaptive baselines, as the safe min–max sampling idea is novel, well-justified, and empirically promising for CD.
