Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for randomized optimization methods, with a focus on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core idea is to approximate the gradient-based optimal sampling distribution without computing the full gradient each iteration, by maintaining safe lower/upper bounds on per-coordinate gradients (CD) or per-component gradient norms (SGD). The authors formulate a min-max problem that, given these intervals, selects a sampling distribution p̂k and a step-size α̂k = 1/vk that (i) is never worse than fixed importance sampling (Li-based), (ii) recovers the true gradient-based distribution when bounds collapse to the exact values, and (iii) is optimal w.r.t. the available information. They provide an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute p̂k and show how to maintain safe bounds for GLMs efficiently. Experiments on several datasets demonstrate substantial improvements for CD, with smaller gains for SGD.

The work connects to and extends a sizable literature on non-uniform sampling for CD/SGD and adaptive probabilities: fixed importance sampling [Nesterov’12; Richtárik & Takáč], gradient-based sampling for SGD [Zhao & Zhang’15], adaptive duality-gap sampling for CD/SDCA [Qu et al.; Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.], Gauss-Southwell and approximate steepest CD [Nutini et al.; Stich et al.]. The novelty is the “safe” min-max formulation using interval information and the resulting provable guarantee of never underperforming fixed sampling, together with an efficient algorithm to solve it each iteration.

Quality:
- Theory: The one-step expected progress analysis for CD with γk = αk / p_i is standard, and the choice of αk and pk to minimize the quadratic upper bound is well-motivated. The optimal full-information sampling (Example 2.3) and its comparison to Li-based sampling are correct and clarify the potential n-fold speedup. The min-max formulation (Eq. 7) and Theorems 3.2/3.4 are the main technical contributions: p̂k is the optimal choice under interval uncertainty, guarantees V(p̂,c) ≤ Tr[L]‖c‖^2 for all c in the uncertainty set, and can be computed in O(n log n). The competitive ratio bounds (Lemmas 3.5/3.6) are informative.
- Assumptions and scope: The CD analysis assumes coordinate-wise L-smoothness. For GLMs with separable regularizers, the presentation uses “gradient” notation even when ψ is non-smooth (L1); in practice, proximal CD/coordinate updates are used. The paper should make explicit which parts of the analysis extend to composite settings (e.g., replace gradients with composite gradient mappings) and under what assumptions; otherwise, there is a mismatch between theory and the L1 experiments.
- Practicality: The bound-maintenance strategy for GLMs (via tracking gradient changes and using Cauchy–Schwarz to bound inner products) is simple and safe, albeit quite loose. The empirical results suggest it suffices in practice for CD.
- Experiments: The CD results show clear improvements in iterations and time; the SGD gains are minor, which the authors acknowledge. Some implementation details are missing (see below).

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear separation between full-information optimal sampling, the safe surrogate under interval uncertainty, and applications to GLMs. However:
- The treatment of non-smooth separable regularizers needs clarification (gradient vs proximal updates).
- The computational overhead discussion could be more nuanced: O(n log n) per iteration can be prohibitive when n is large and per-iteration gradient computations are cheap (e.g., n ≫ d or very sparse features). It would help to discuss amortization strategies and incremental updates to Algorithm 4.
- Some notational overloading (L as both scalar and diagonal matrix), minor typos, and incomplete reference formatting detract from readability.

Originality:
The safe min-max formulation given interval bounds and the O(n log n) algorithm to recover a distribution that is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling is, to my knowledge, novel. Prior works used stale/heuristic estimates or upper bounds to define adaptive probabilities but lacked this robust guarantee. The step-size coupling with the sampling distribution to preserve unbiasedness is standard but well integrated here.

Significance:
For CD, the contribution appears practically and theoretically impactful: it bridges a gap between ideal gradient-based sampling and feasible implementations with strong worst-case guarantees, and empirically delivers substantial speedups. For SGD, the significance is more limited as the experimental gains are small; the method is still principled, but its practical advantage over uniform/importance sampling for typical finite-sum problems is less clear from the current evidence.

Pros:
- Clear theoretical motivation for gradient-based sampling and quantified potential speedups.
- Novel safe adaptive sampling under interval uncertainty with strong guarantees: never worse than fixed importance sampling; reduces to optimal when bounds are tight.
- Efficient algorithm (O(n log n)) with a neat thresholding characterization.
- Practical recipe to maintain safe bounds in GLMs; empirical CD results on real datasets are convincing.
- Competitive ratio analysis provides additional insight.

Cons:
- Computational overhead: O(n log n) per iteration can be large for high-dimensional problems (e.g., text with n ≈ 10^6). The paper lacks a discussion of amortizing or incrementally updating p̂k, and how often p̂k is recomputed in practice. The timing results are on different machines and do not isolate overhead clearly.
- Composite objectives: Theory is presented for smooth f, yet experiments include L1; a proper composite/proximal analysis should be provided or claims adjusted.
- SGD section: Limited empirical benefit; claims about broad utility for SGD should be toned down or supported with stronger experiments/analysis (e.g., settings where gradient norms are highly heterogeneous).
- Details: Missing ablations on bound tightness, sparsity, frequency of updating p̂k; incomplete reference formatting.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Clarify how the analysis extends to composite objectives (proximal CD), and what Lipschitz constants and model inequality (1) become in that case.
- Describe the implementation details for Algorithm 4 in large-scale settings: Do you recompute p̂k every iteration? Can you maintain sorted structures and update in o(n log n) using the rank-one nature of bound shifts? Any amortized complexity results?
- Provide ablations on how the tightness of `, u affects performance (e.g., Cauchy–Schwarz vs. tighter data-dependent bounds).
- Report wall-clock overhead attributable to computing p̂k (on the same machine), and evaluate regimes n ≫ d and d ≫ n, with sparse data.
- For SGD, consider problems with known high variance in component gradients, mini-batching, or variance-reduced settings where adaptive sampling is known to help, to better showcase the method’s utility.

Overall evaluation:
This is a solid and well-motivated theoretical contribution with a practically relevant algorithm for CD, and a principled but less compelling story for SGD. Provided the authors clarify the composite setting and better address/update strategy and overhead, I would lean toward acceptance.
