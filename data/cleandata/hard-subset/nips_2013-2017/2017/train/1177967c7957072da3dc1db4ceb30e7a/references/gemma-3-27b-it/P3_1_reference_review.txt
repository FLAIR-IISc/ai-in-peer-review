## NIPS 2017 Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for both Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the theoretical benefits of adaptive sampling but acknowledging the computational and theoretical difficulties associated with existing approaches like gradient-based sampling.  Their core contribution is a “safe” adaptive sampling strategy that provably outperforms uniform or fixed importance sampling, while being computationally efficient and requiring only readily available gradient bounds (rather than full gradient information). They provide theoretical analysis demonstrating speedups in CD, an optimization formulation for the sampling distribution, and empirical validation on benchmark datasets using generalized linear models.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on a well-established body of work in stochastic optimization and importance sampling. It correctly situates itself within the context of uniform and fixed importance sampling (e.g., [18, 19, 2, 33]) and adaptive strategies like gradient-based sampling [22, 5, 26]. It acknowledges the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and computational cost, specifically referencing limitations discussed in [23, 3]. The work connects to research on accelerated coordinate descent [17, 19] and robust stochastic approximation [27].  The authors also appropriately position their work within the context of safe screening techniques [14, 15] by utilizing bounds on gradient information.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for their proposed method. The proof of improvement over fixed sampling is convincing, and the analysis of the optimization problem for the sampling distribution is well-developed. Lemma 2.1 and the discussion around the optimal sampling distribution (Example 2.3) are particularly insightful.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  A key strength is the focus on practical computability.  The proposed “safe” sampling approach only requires bounds on the gradients, making it significantly more efficient than methods requiring full gradient computation.  The O(n log n) complexity for computing the sampling distribution is also a significant advantage.
* **Generality:** The method is applicable to both CD and SGD, increasing its potential impact. The demonstration of its applicability to different objective functions (squared loss, hinge loss, logistic loss) and regularization techniques (L1, L2) further highlights its versatility.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the core ideas are explained in a reasonably accessible manner. The inclusion of an algorithm breakdown (Algorithms 1-4) is very helpful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, consistently showing improvements over fixed sampling and competitive performance compared to optimal sampling. The visualizations illustrating the evolution of *vk* are particularly valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The method’s performance relies on the quality of the gradient bounds ` and `u`. While the paper provides an example of how to compute these bounds for GLMs, the practicality and robustness of this approach in more complex settings isn’t fully explored.  A more detailed discussion of how to obtain or approximate these bounds in other scenarios would be beneficial.  The sensitivity of the method to the tightness of these bounds deserves further investigation.
* **Limited Empirical Comparison:** While the empirical results are positive, the comparison with existing adaptive sampling methods (e.g., [22, 5, 26]) is limited. A direct comparison to these techniques would strengthen the claims about the superiority of the proposed method, especially in SGD.
* **Competitive Ratio Discussion:** The discussion of the competitive ratio (Section 3.2) feels somewhat disconnected from the practical evaluation. While theoretically interesting, the conditions for achieving the bounds presented in Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6 aren’t explicitly assessed in the experiments.
* **Complexity Analysis:** The complexity claim that the computational overhead is "of the order O(n log n) per iteration" needs qualification. It's implicitly assuming 'd' (data dimension) is relatively small compared to 'n' (number of data points). In high-dimensional settings, gradient computation (Θ(d)) might dominate, making the O(n log n) overhead less significant.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in stochastic optimization – the trade-off between adaptive sampling benefits and computational cost.
* The proposed “safe” adaptive sampling scheme is a novel and theoretically sound solution.
* The method is generally applicable and computationally efficient.
* The empirical results provide strong evidence of its effectiveness.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on gradient bounds and the lack of a thorough discussion of their practicality in diverse settings is a concern.
* The limited empirical comparison with other adaptive sampling techniques weakens the claims of superiority.
* The discussion of the competitive ratio feels somewhat theoretical and not fully grounded in the experimental results.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The proposed “safe” adaptive sampling scheme is a valuable contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. While the reliance on gradient bounds and the limited empirical comparison are weaknesses, the strengths of the paper – its theoretical soundness, computational efficiency, and generality – outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the following:

1. **Expand the discussion of gradient bounds:** Provide a more detailed discussion of how to obtain or approximate these bounds in a wider range of scenarios, and analyze the sensitivity of the method to the tightness of these bounds.
2. **Include a comparison to existing adaptive sampling methods:**  Add a direct comparison to at least one relevant adaptive sampling technique (e.g., from [22, 5, 26]) in the empirical evaluation.
3. **Clarify complexity analysis:** Explain the assumption that 'd' is relatively small compared to 'n' in the discussion of computational complexity.



**Confidence:** 8/10 (High confidence in the technical soundness and potential impact of the work, but some reservations regarding the practical implications of the gradient bound assumption and the limited empirical comparisons.)
