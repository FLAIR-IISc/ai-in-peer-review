- Proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for CD and SGD that approximates gradient-based sampling efficiently with theoretical guarantees.  
- Algorithm is computationally practical (O(n log n) per iteration with efficient bound tracking) and recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting.  
- Leverages safe upper and lower gradient bounds to ensure the method is at least as good as fixed sampling.  
- Properly situates the work relative to prior importance sampling literature and notes connections to duality gap-based sampling for CD.  
- Theoretical analysis is rigorous, demonstrating improvement over fixed sampling and characterizing convergence (e.g., Lemma 2.1).  
- Practicality highlighted via discussions on tracking gradient bounds in GLMs and efficient implementation.  
- Safety guarantee is a key strength, providing worst-case performance at least as good as fixed sampling.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and uses standard, accessible notation.  
- Empirical results show clear benefits for coordinate descent on real datasets and informative comparisons to uniform and gradient-based sampling.  
- SGD experiments show smaller, less compelling gains, weakening the impact for that setting.  
- Method depends on quality of safe gradient bounds and requires additional tracking, which may be challenging outside GLMs.  
- Bound-tracking technique for GLMs builds on existing methods and lacks major novelty.  
- Competitive ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5 & 3.6) feels detached and could be simplified or made more intuitive.  
- Overall quality: technically sound with a complete algorithm and supported claims.  
- Clarity: generally clear but some dense mathematical notation; more intuition would help accessibility.  
- Originality: novel formulation of adaptive sampling as a convex problem with safety constraints, despite building on existing ideas.  
- Significance: important contribution for CD, offering cost reductions without sacrificing performance; SGD impact is limited.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, practical and efficient algorithm, safety guarantee, convincing CD experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: modest SGD improvements, reliance on accurate gradient bounds, limited novelty in bound estimation.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept; encourage expanded discussion on bound-tracking in complex models and analysis of limited SGD gains.
