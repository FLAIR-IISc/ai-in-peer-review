## NIPS 2017 Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of adaptive importance sampling for accelerating coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the drawbacks of existing adaptive strategies – often poor theoretical guarantees or high computational cost. They propose a novel, efficient adaptive sampling scheme that aims to overcome these limitations. The core idea is to formulate the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem, leveraging safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to avoid full gradient computations. They provide theoretical analysis demonstrating improvements over uniform and fixed importance sampling, and empirical validation on real-world datasets.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon a rich history of work in stochastic optimization and coordinate descent. It correctly positions itself within the literature on importance sampling (both static and adaptive), referencing key papers like those by Nesterov, Richtárik, Zhao & Zhang, and Csiba.  The connection to duality gap-based sampling (Csiba et al.) and gradient-based sampling (Zhao & Zhang) is clearly articulated. The authors also acknowledge the challenges with theoretical guarantees in adaptive sampling, referencing works that demonstrate potential performance degradation compared to uniform sampling.  A strength is the clear linking to prior work on safe screening techniques (Liu et al.) which informs the approach to bounding gradient information.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is a significant strength. The paper rigorously establishes the advantages of the proposed scheme over fixed sampling, and provides bounds on the competitive ratio. The derivation of the optimal sampling strategy in the full-information setting (Section 2) provides a valuable baseline.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  A key contribution is the focus on practical efficiency. By leveraging safe gradient bounds, the authors avoid the computational bottlenecks of directly calculating the optimal sampling distribution. The proposed algorithm offers a good balance between performance and computational cost. The O(n log n) complexity of the sampling computation is reasonable for many applications.
* **Generality:** The approach is explicitly presented as generic, applicable to both CD and SGD methods, and potentially extensible to other optimization algorithms.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real-world datasets provide strong evidence supporting the theoretical findings.  The comparison with uniform sampling and the demonstration of improvements in both iteration complexity and timing are compelling. The visualization of `vk` helps understand the adaptive step size.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are presented in a logical flow.  The inclusion of an appendix with deferred proofs is appreciated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Gradient Bound Acquisition:** While the paper highlights the importance of safe gradient bounds, the discussion of *how* to obtain these bounds in practice feels somewhat limited.  The examples in Section 4 (GLMs) are helpful, but a more general discussion of strategies for estimating or bounding gradients, especially in more complex models, would strengthen the paper. The reliance on Cauchy-Schwarz bounding, while simple, might be overly conservative in some cases.
* **SGD Performance:** The empirical results for SGD show less dramatic improvements compared to CD. The authors acknowledge this and speculate about potential reasons, but a more thorough investigation of the performance differences between CD and SGD would be beneficial.  Why is the adaptive sampling less effective in the SGD setting?
* **Competitive Ratio:** While the paper provides bounds on the competitive ratio, the conditions for achieving tighter bounds (Lemma 3.6) seem restrictive.  A discussion of the practical implications of these conditions and strategies for mitigating them would be valuable.
* **Limited Discussion of Memory Requirements:** While the computational complexity of the sampling step is discussed, the memory requirements for storing the gradient bounds (`l` and `u`) are not explicitly addressed. This could be a concern for very high-dimensional datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The proposed adaptive importance sampling scheme is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically validated. The work addresses a real challenge in applying adaptive sampling techniques in practice and offers a practical solution. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the paper.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and guarantees.
* Novel and efficient algorithm with practical applicability.
* Compelling empirical results demonstrating improved performance.
* Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
* Addresses a relevant and important problem in stochastic optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited discussion on obtaining safe gradient bounds in general settings.
* Less pronounced performance gains in the SGD setting.
* Restrictive conditions for tighter competitive ratio bounds.




**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions – requesting a more detailed discussion of gradient bound acquisition and further investigation of the SGD performance). I believe this paper has the potential to be highly influential and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
