This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors motivate the work by highlighting the theoretical benefits and practical challenges of adaptive sampling, namely the computational cost and theoretical guarantees. They present a novel scheme that aims to address these limitations by approximating gradient-based sampling using safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient. The key contribution lies in formulating the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem and providing an efficient algorithm for its solution. The theoretical analysis demonstrates favorable properties, including provable improvements over fixed sampling and recovery of gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting. Empirical evaluation on GLMs confirms the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed adaptive sampling scheme, including convergence analysis and comparisons to existing methods. The proofs, while deferred to the appendix, appear rigorous.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed algorithm offers a computationally efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling, making it applicable to large-scale problems. The O(n log n) complexity is a significant advantage.
*   **Safety Guarantee:** The use of safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient ensures that the adaptive sampling is never worse than fixed sampling, providing a valuable guarantee.
*   **Generality:** The scheme is presented as being applicable to both CD and SGD methods, increasing its potential impact.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on GLMs demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method, showcasing its performance on real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notation and Clarity:** The notation is dense and occasionally ambiguous. For instance, the definition of `∇if` as `<x, ei>` is non-standard and could be confusing. The use of multiple definitions for ‘p’ (probability vector, distribution) necessitates careful tracking.
*   **Presentation of Contributions:** The contributions listed in the introduction are somewhat repetitive and lack specificity. For example, stating that the scheme "enjoys favorable properties" is vague.
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is primarily focused on GLMs. While these are important models, the results may not generalize to other types of optimization problems. The comparison to state-of-the-art methods beyond uniform sampling is limited. The SGD results seem less convincing than the CD results.
*   **Discussion of Gradient Bounds:** The paper briefly mentions the importance of efficient gradient bound tracking (Section 4) but lacks a detailed discussion of the practical challenges and potential limitations of this step.  How sensitive is the performance to the tightness of the bounds? A more in-depth analysis of this aspect would be valuable.
*   **Minor Technical Details:**  Several minor inconsistencies and potentially misleading statements exist. For example, the claim that the computational cost is “comparable” to evaluating a single gradient component is not universally true, especially when ‘d’ (dimensionality) is small compared to ‘n’ (number of data points). The proof of Lemma 3.5 and the discussion around Lemma 3.6 in the appendix are slightly hand-wavy and require further clarification.
*   **Formatting:** Throughout the paper, there are instances of poor formatting, such as inconsistent spacing, missing punctuation, and minor grammatical errors.  While not critical, these detract from the overall presentation.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in optimization with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The safety guarantee is a significant advantage, making the proposed scheme more robust and reliable.
*   The experimental results provide evidence of the method's effectiveness.

**Con:**

*   The clarity of the presentation is hampered by dense notation and occasional ambiguity.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and lacks extensive comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Several minor technical inconsistencies and formatting issues detract from the overall quality.
*   The discussion of gradient bound tracking is insufficient.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. However, the presentation suffers from a lack of clarity and some minor technical issues. While the theoretical results are strong and the empirical validation is promising, the limited scope of the experiments and the insufficient discussion of practical considerations raise concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, focusing on improving the clarity of the presentation, expanding the empirical evaluation, and providing a more detailed discussion of the gradient bound tracking process. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact. A more thorough proofreading to correct formatting errors is also essential. The underlying idea is strong, but the paper currently falls short of the high standards for acceptance at NIPS.
