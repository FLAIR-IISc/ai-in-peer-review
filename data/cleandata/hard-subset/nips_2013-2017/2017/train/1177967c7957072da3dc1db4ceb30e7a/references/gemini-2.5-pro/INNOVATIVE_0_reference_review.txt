This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The central problem addressed is that optimal gradient-based sampling is computationally prohibitive (requiring full gradient information at each step), while common approximations lack theoretical guarantees and can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors' core idea is to work with efficiently maintainable safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. Instead of using these bounds heuristically, they frame the selection of the sampling distribution as a principled minimax optimization problem. The goal is to find a probability distribution that minimizes the worst-case value of the optimization progress upper bound, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradient vectors consistent with the given bounds. This "safe" formulation is the paper's main conceptual contribution. The authors show that this problem can be solved efficiently (in O(n log n) time) and, crucially, prove that the resulting sampling distribution is guaranteed to perform better than standard fixed importance sampling (like L-based or uniform sampling). They demonstrate how to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide empirical evidence that their method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in wall-clock time and approaches the performance of the impractical optimal sampling scheme.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Novelty:** The standout contribution of this paper is the framing of the adaptive sampling problem as a minimax game (Equation 7). This is a bold and elegant conceptual shift from prior work, which has largely relied on heuristics or using upper bounds as direct proxies for gradient magnitudes. This principled approach to handling uncertainty in the gradient information is a significant and novel idea.

2.  **Important Theoretical Guarantee:** The "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2), proving that the proposed sampling is provably superior to fixed Li-based sampling, is a powerful result. This addresses a major weakness of previous adaptive methods, which could degrade performance. This guarantee makes adaptive sampling a much more trustworthy and appealing tool for practitioners.

3.  **Practicality and Significance:** The paper successfully bridges theory and practice. It not only presents a strong theoretical concept but also provides an efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) to implement it. The O(n log n) complexity is practical for many problems, as demonstrated in the experiments where the method achieves wall-clock time improvements. The proposed scheme is generic and can be applied to both CD and SGD, increasing its potential impact. This work has the potential to become a standard technique for accelerating first-order methods.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly, the technical development is easy to follow, and the connection between the theory and the final algorithm is explicit.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Simplicity of Gradient Bounds:** The method's overall performance is contingent on the quality of the safe gradient bounds (`l`, `u`). The technique presented in Section 4 for GLMs, based on the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, is a simple and effective proof-of-concept but may yield quite loose bounds in some cases. While the experiments show this is sufficient to achieve strong performance, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion of how tighter, more sophisticated bounding techniques might further improve the method. This is less a flaw of the core proposal and more a direction for future work.

2.  **Less Pronounced Gains for SGD:** The experimental results for SGD, while still showing an improvement, are notably less dramatic than for CD. The authors correctly observe this, but a deeper discussion on why this might be the case (e.g., differences in how information propagates in CD vs. SGD, or the nature of the variance) would strengthen the paper's analysis.

3.  **Incomplete Competitive Analysis:** The competitive ratio analysis in Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6 feels somewhat preliminary. The conditions for the bound in Lemma 3.6 are quite strong, and the problem in Lemma 3.5 is non-convex. While interesting, this analysis doesn't feel as complete as the main "safety" result. However, this is a minor point that does not detract from the paper's primary contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The novel minimax formulation for robust adaptive sampling is a significant conceptual advance.
*   The paper provides the first practical adaptive sampling method with a formal guarantee of outperforming standard fixed sampling schemes, a major milestone for this line of research.
*   The method is supported by a sound theoretical analysis and an efficient algorithm.
*   Empirical results confirm that the theoretical advantages translate into real-world performance gains in terms of both iteration count and wall-clock time.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The method for maintaining the gradient bounds is relatively simple, and the overall performance depends on it.
*   The experimental validation is confined to GLMs, and its effectiveness on more complex models (e.g., in deep learning) remains an open question.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that introduces a genuinely novel and important idea. The minimax approach to "safe" adaptive sampling is a bold step forward that provides the theoretical rigor that was missing in many previous heuristic methods. While the experimental scope could be broader and the specific bounding techniques could be more sophisticated, these are not critical flaws. The core contribution is the new framework itself, which is both elegant and impactful. This work prioritizes a significant new concept over exhaustive validation, and in doing so, it opens up a promising new direction for research in stochastic optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
