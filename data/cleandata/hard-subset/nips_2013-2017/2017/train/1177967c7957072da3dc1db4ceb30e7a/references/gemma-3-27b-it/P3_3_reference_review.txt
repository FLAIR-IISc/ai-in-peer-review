## NIPS 2017 Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

**Summary:**

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy for both Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling by utilizing safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient entries (or norms) in each iteration. The authors theoretically demonstrate the benefits over fixed sampling, particularly for CD (potentially a speedup factor of *n*), and provide a practical algorithm for computing the sampling distribution. Empirical evaluation on real datasets confirms the effectiveness of the proposed approach, especially in the CD setting.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established field of adaptive importance sampling, referencing key works by Csiba et al., Richtárik et al., and Zhao & Zhang. It clearly positions itself within the existing literature, acknowledging the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and computational feasibility of adaptive strategies. The authors address a key limitation of prior gradient-based approaches—their computational cost—by proposing a safe and efficient approximation.  They correctly identify that a crucial bottleneck is the need for full gradient information, and their approach circumvents this issue by leveraging bounds rather than precise values. The connections to coordinate descent methods, and the improvements over existing strategies like steepest descent and uniform sampling, are well-established within the optimization literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis rigorously establishes the advantages of the proposed method over fixed importance sampling. The derivation of the optimal sampling distribution (Example 2.3) and the subsequent approximation (Section 3) are clearly presented and justified. The bounds established in Lemma 2.1 and Theorem 3.2 are strong and well-motivated.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is designed to be computationally feasible, with a runtime of O(n log n) for computing the sampling distribution—a significant improvement over methods requiring full gradient computation. The ability to update the gradient bounds incrementally during optimization (Section 4) further enhances its practicality.
* **Generality:** The method is applicable to a broad class of optimization problems, particularly those amenable to CD and SGD, and can be combined with various regularization techniques. The authors demonstrate this generality through their experiments on GLMs.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The empirical results validate the theoretical findings, demonstrating the improved performance of the proposed sampling scheme on real datasets. The focus on easily computable gradient bounds makes the approach readily applicable in many machine learning scenarios.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas. The notation is clear and consistent, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a readable manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **SGD Performance:**  While the theoretical benefits are presented for SGD, the empirical results show only a modest improvement over uniform sampling. This discrepancy warrants further investigation and discussion.  The authors acknowledge this, but a deeper analysis of why the theoretical benefits don't translate as strongly to SGD would be valuable. Is it the tightness of the bounds?  Is the theoretical model inaccurate for SGD?
* **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The reliance on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient introduces an assumption that might not always be easily satisfied in practice. While the authors provide a practical method for estimating these bounds in the context of GLMs, it's unclear how well this approach would generalize to more complex models.  Further discussion of scenarios where obtaining such bounds is challenging would strengthen the paper.
* **Competitive Ratio Interpretation:** The competitive ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) is somewhat abstract and lacks a clear interpretation in terms of practical performance gains.  Relating the competitive ratio to the expected reduction in iteration complexity would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation, while comprehensive for CD, is somewhat limited for SGD. Expanding the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of datasets and models would strengthen the conclusions.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound adaptive importance sampling strategy that addresses a significant limitation of existing methods.
* The proposed algorithm is computationally efficient and applicable to a wide range of optimization problems.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, particularly in the context of coordinate descent.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The performance gains for SGD are less pronounced than those observed for CD, raising questions about the generalizability of the approach.
* The reliance on safe gradient bounds introduces an assumption that may not always be easily satisfied in practice.
* The competitive ratio analysis lacks a clear practical interpretation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field of optimization, particularly in the context of adaptive importance sampling. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the proposed algorithm is computationally efficient, and the empirical results are compelling, especially for coordinate descent. While the SGD results are less striking, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. 

**Rating:** **Accept (Strongly Consider)**

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*  Investigate the discrepancy between the theoretical and empirical performance of the method in the SGD setting.
*  Discuss the challenges of obtaining safe gradient bounds for more complex models beyond GLMs.
*  Provide a more intuitive interpretation of the competitive ratio analysis.
*  Expand the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of datasets and models for SGD.
* Consider adding a discussion on potential extensions of the method to other optimization algorithms beyond CD and SGD.
