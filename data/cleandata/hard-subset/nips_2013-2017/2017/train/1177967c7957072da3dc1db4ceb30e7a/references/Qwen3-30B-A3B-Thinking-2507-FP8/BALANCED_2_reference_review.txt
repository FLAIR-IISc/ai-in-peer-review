

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The authors argue that while gradient-based adaptive sampling can theoretically outperform fixed sampling (by up to a factor of n in CD), it often requires full gradient computation per iteration, making it computationally inadmissible. The key contribution is a method to compute an adaptive sampling distribution efficiently using safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information, with the guarantee that it is always better than fixed importance sampling. The authors demonstrate the approach for generalized linear models, showing that safe gradient bounds can be maintained in O(n) time per iteration, with the sampling computation adding O(n log n) overhead.

This work builds on the substantial body of literature on adaptive sampling for optimization (e.g., Csiba et al. 2015; Perekrestenko et al. 2017; Zhao & Zhang 2015), but makes a novel contribution by providing a theoretically grounded "safe" method that avoids the pitfalls of previous adaptive approaches that rely on approximations.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees that the proposed method is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), which is a significant contribution. The proofs are well-structured and the claims are well-supported.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors demonstrate that the proposed method can be implemented with only O(n log n) overhead per iteration, which is acceptable when d = Ω(n) (as in many machine learning problems). This bridges the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical implementation.

3. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation**: The paper evaluates the method on multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with different loss functions (squared hinge, logistic, square loss) and regularization (L1, L2). The results consistently show that adaptive sampling significantly outperforms fixed sampling in terms of iterations and total computation time.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear notation and a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical validation. The algorithm descriptions are particularly clear.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scope of Application**: While the authors demonstrate the method for generalized linear models, it's not clear how broadly applicable this approach is to other optimization problems. The paper doesn't discuss how the safe gradient bounds could be maintained for non-GLM problems (e.g., deep learning), which limits the method's potential impact.

2. **SGD Performance Discrepancy**: The empirical results show that the adaptive sampling provides significant improvements for CD but only marginal gains for SGD (Figures 7-9). The paper doesn't provide a thorough theoretical explanation for this discrepancy, which weakens the significance claim for SGD applications.

3. **Comparison with Recent Work**: While the paper cites relevant work, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from recent NIPS papers on adaptive sampling (e.g., Perekrestenko et al., "Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling," ICML 2017). The "safe" aspect is the key novelty, but this needs to be more clearly articulated in relation to prior work.

4. **Theoretical Bounds**: The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5-3.6) provides useful bounds but the worst-case bounds (ρk ≤ vk/Lmin) may be too loose to be practically meaningful, as the empirical results show much better performance than the theoretical worst-case suggests.

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical results and thorough empirical validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the weaker performance for SGD). The work represents a complete piece of research.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the main ideas are clearly presented. The algorithms are described precisely.

**Originality**: The key novelty lies in the "safe" approach to adaptive sampling that guarantees theoretical superiority over fixed sampling while being computationally efficient. While related to prior work on adaptive sampling, this specific formulation with theoretical guarantees appears to be novel.

**Significance**: The method has clear practical significance for CD-based optimization, which is widely used in machine learning. The O(n log n) overhead is acceptable for many problems, and the method can be easily integrated into existing implementations. However, the limited impact on SGD applications reduces the overall significance.

## Recommendation
The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, with strong theoretical guarantees and practical implementation details. The main weakness is the limited scope of applicability (primarily GLMs) and the relatively modest gains for SGD, which somewhat limits the broader impact.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:
1. More clearly distinguish their contribution from recent NIPS/ICML work on adaptive sampling.
2. Provide a more thorough discussion of the SGD performance discrepancy, including potential theoretical explanations.
3. Elaborate on the generality of the approach beyond GLMs, or better articulate the scope of the method's applicability.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of optimization methods for machine learning. With the suggested revisions, it would be a strong NIPS contribution.
