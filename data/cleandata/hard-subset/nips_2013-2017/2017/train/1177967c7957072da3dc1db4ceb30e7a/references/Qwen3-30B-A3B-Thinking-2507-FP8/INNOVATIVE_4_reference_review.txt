

# Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

This paper introduces a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" framework for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address a critical gap in adaptive importance sampling literature: previous methods either lacked theoretical guarantees or required computationally expensive full gradient computations. Their key innovation is a method to compute adaptive sampling distributions efficiently using safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, while maintaining provable guarantees that the sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it theoretically demonstrates that gradient-based sampling in CD can yield speedups up to a factor of the dimension n compared to fixed sampling. Second, it proposes a novel "safe" adaptive sampling approach that can be efficiently computed in each iteration, with theoretical guarantees that it outperforms fixed sampling. Third, it provides a practical implementation for structured optimization problems like generalized linear models, showing how to maintain safe gradient bounds efficiently. Finally, it presents empirical evidence demonstrating substantial improvements over fixed sampling on real-world datasets.

The theoretical contributions are particularly strong. The authors rigorously prove that their proposed sampling distribution is the best possible given the limited gradient information available, and that it provably outperforms fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2). This is a significant advancement over previous adaptive methods that often lacked such guarantees. The paper's key insight is recognizing that we can formulate the sampling problem as an optimization problem that minimizes the worst-case performance over the range of possible gradient values, rather than approximating the optimal sampling directly.

The empirical results are compelling. For CD methods, the proposed approach consistently outperforms fixed sampling by significant margins in both iterations and total computation time, with the computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration) being negligible compared to the gradient evaluation cost (O(d)) when d = Ω(n). The paper's demonstration that even very loose gradient bounds (from simple Cauchy-Schwarz estimates) yield substantial improvements over fixed sampling is particularly impressive.

The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and makes a strong case for the significance of its contribution. The authors carefully distinguish their work from prior art, particularly addressing the limitations of previous adaptive sampling methods (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) that often failed to provide theoretical guarantees of superiority over fixed sampling.

From a novelty perspective, this paper represents a bold conceptual advance in optimization for machine learning. It's not merely an incremental improvement to existing adaptive sampling methods, but rather a fundamentally new approach that solves a key theoretical and practical limitation of previous work. The "safe" aspect of their sampling—ensuring it's never worse than fixed sampling—addresses a critical concern in adaptive methods that previous approaches often overlooked.

The main limitation is that the empirical gains for SGD are less pronounced than for CD, which the authors acknowledge. However, this is a minor point given the paper's primary focus on CD and the fact that the proposed method still outperforms fixed sampling for SGD. The paper could have included more discussion on how the safe gradient bounds might be obtained for more complex model classes beyond generalized linear models, but this is a natural direction for future work rather than a weakness in the current contribution.

The paper excels in all four NIPS evaluation criteria:
- Quality: Strong theoretical foundation with complete proofs, well-supported empirical results.
- Clarity: Well-organized, clear notation, and effective use of figures.
- Originality: Introduces a genuinely novel approach to adaptive importance sampling with provable guarantees.
- Significance: Addresses a fundamental problem in scalable machine learning optimization with practical implications.

The reviewer is highly impressed by the novelty and theoretical rigor of this work. While the empirical results for SGD are less dramatic than for CD, this does not diminish the significance of the contribution, which is primarily a theoretical advance with broad applicability to optimization methods. The paper represents a clear step forward in adaptive importance sampling, offering both theoretical guarantees and practical implementation details.

This paper would be a strong addition to NIPS, introducing a significant new concept that could influence future work in optimization for machine learning. The authors have successfully balanced theoretical rigor with practical implementation considerations, and their approach represents a meaningful advance over previous work in this area.

**Recommendation: Accept**
