### Summary

The paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The core problem with existing adaptive sampling methods is that the optimal sampling distribution (e.g., based on gradient norms) is often too expensive to compute at every iteration. Prior attempts to approximate this distribution using stale information or simple upper bounds lack theoretical guarantees and can perform worse than non-adaptive uniform sampling.

The key idea of this paper is to reframe the problem. Instead of using a point estimate for the unknown gradient information, the authors assume access to "safe" lower and upper bounds (`l` and `u`) for the gradient components or their norms. They then formulate the selection of the sampling distribution `p` as a minimax optimization problem: find the distribution `p` that minimizes the worst-case one-step progress, where the worst-case is taken over all possible true gradient vectors consistent with the bounds `l` and `u`.

The main contributions are:
1.  A novel and principled minimax framework for choosing a sampling distribution under uncertainty.
2.  A proof that the resulting "safe" sampling distribution is guaranteed to be no worse than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., Li-based or uniform sampling), a critical guarantee missing from prior work.
3.  An efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute this safe sampling distribution at each iteration.
4.  A demonstration of how to derive and efficiently maintain the required safe bounds for an important class of problems, Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).
5.  Empirical results on several real-world datasets that show the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in wall-clock time and nearly matches the performance of the impractical, full-information optimal sampling.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its **originality and the boldness of its core idea**. The problem of computationally expensive adaptive sampling is well-known. However, instead of offering another heuristic approximation, the authors provide a fundamentally new and elegant formulation. The shift from using a point estimate (like a stale gradient or an upper bound) to reasoning about an entire uncertainty set (`Ck`) and optimizing for the worst-case scenario within that set is a significant conceptual contribution. This minimax approach is principled, robust, and represents a novel direction for research in stochastic optimization.

The paper's **significance** is high. It provides a practical solution to a widespread problem. The theoretical guarantee that the method is "safe"—provably better than fixed sampling—is a major selling point. This "safety net" removes a significant practical barrier to the adoption of adaptive methods, which were previously seen as powerful but potentially risky. The proposed framework is generic and could inspire similar "safe" approaches in other areas where decisions must be made with limited or uncertain information.

The **technical quality** is strong. The theoretical development is sound, leading from the one-step progress bound to the minimax problem and its solution. The proposed O(n log n) algorithm for computing the sampling distribution is non-trivial and makes the entire approach computationally feasible. The experiments are well-designed, convincingly demonstrating that the method works as advertised. The inclusion of plots showing the evolution of the step-size parameter `vk` provides excellent insight and connects the empirical performance back to the theory.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper's main weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the core contribution.

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The effectiveness of the entire scheme hinges on the quality of the safe bounds `l` and `u`. The paper demonstrates a simple method for GLMs using the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which may be quite loose in practice. While the results are impressive even with these simple bounds, the paper would benefit from a deeper discussion on the impact of bound tightness or pointers to more sophisticated methods for obtaining them. However, I view this as a fruitful area for future work building on this paper's framework, rather than a fundamental flaw of the paper itself.

2.  **Computational Overhead:** The authors argue that the O(n log n) overhead for computing the sampling is negligible when the cost of a gradient update is high (e.g., when data dimension `d` is large). This is a fair point, but in settings where `n` is massive and `d` is small, this overhead could become a bottleneck. While the timing experiments are convincing for the tested regimes, this limitation could be acknowledged more explicitly.

### Recommendation

**Decision:** Accept

**Justification:**

This paper is a standout submission that embodies the kind of innovative and conceptually bold research that NIPS should showcase. It addresses a well-established problem with a genuinely novel approach. The minimax formulation for finding a robust sampling distribution is both theoretically elegant and practically effective.

While one could point to the simplicity of the proposed gradient bounding technique or the computational overhead in certain regimes as minor flaws, these are far outweighed by the strength and novelty of the core idea. The paper doesn't just offer an incremental improvement; it provides a new lens through which to view the problem of optimization under uncertainty. The guarantee of being "provably better" than fixed sampling is a powerful result that elevates this work far above previous heuristic methods. The paper is well-written, the theory is sound, and the experiments are convincing. This work has high potential to influence future research and practice in large-scale machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
