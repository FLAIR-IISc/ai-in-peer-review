

# Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

## Summary
This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address a key limitation of gradient-based adaptive sampling, which theoretically offers significant speedups (up to a factor of n in dimension) but requires expensive full gradient computations. Their approach uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient entries to compute an approximate sampling distribution that is provably better than fixed importance sampling. The paper provides theoretical analysis showing that the proposed sampling is never worse than Li-based (fixed) sampling, develops an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling distribution, and validates the approach on real datasets.

## Strengths
**Technical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical foundations. Theorems 3.2 and 3.4 establish that the proposed sampling distribution is never worse than fixed importance sampling (Li-based sampling) and provides a worst-case competitive ratio. The optimization formulation in Section 3.1 is well-motivated and the proof of Algorithm 4's correctness is sound. The paper clearly distinguishes between the ideal adaptive sampling (requiring full gradient) and the practical safe sampling approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The notation is consistent, the theoretical development flows logically, and the main contributions are clearly stated. The empirical results align with theoretical claims, with the proposed method achieving near-optimal performance with minimal computational overhead.

**Novelty:** The key novelty lies in using safe gradient bounds to guarantee the adaptive sampling never performs worse than fixed sampling—a guarantee not established for previous approaches that used similar bounds. The paper clearly differentiates from prior work (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) by providing this theoretical guarantee.

**Empirical Validation:** The experiments on real-world datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) demonstrate clear advantages over fixed importance sampling in convergence speed and computational efficiency. The results show that the method achieves near-optimal performance (comparable to the ideal gradient-based sampling) with only a small computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration).

## Weaknesses
**Limited Scope for SGD:** While the paper claims applicability to both CD and SGD, the theoretical analysis is primarily focused on CD. The SGD experiments show less significant improvements than for CD, and the paper does not adequately explain this discrepancy. The theoretical analysis for SGD is minimal compared to CD.

**Computational Complexity Claim:** The paper states the overhead is "comparable with the cost to evaluate a single component of the gradient" for d = Ω(n), but does not provide sufficient detail to substantiate this claim for very large-scale problems where O(n log n) might become significant.

**Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant work, it could provide a more thorough comparison with recent advances in adaptive importance sampling, particularly in distributed optimization settings.

## Pro and Con Acceptance
**Pro:**
- Theoretical guarantees are rigorous and meaningful (provable improvement over fixed sampling)
- Practical implementation with minimal computational overhead
- Clear experimental validation showing significant performance gains
- Addresses a key limitation in adaptive importance sampling literature

**Con:**
- Limited theoretical analysis for SGD (primary focus on CD)
- Computational complexity claim needs stronger justification
- Less impressive results for SGD compared to CD

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The theoretical results are sound, the proposed method is practical, and the empirical validation is convincing. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a practical implementation of adaptive importance sampling with provable theoretical guarantees.

The main limitation is the limited scope for SGD, but this does not detract from the paper's core contribution, which is well-motivated and addresses a significant limitation in current literature. The approach is sufficiently general to be applicable to a wide range of optimization problems, and the paper provides a clear pathway for extending it to other problem classes.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written, novel in its approach to adaptive sampling, and significant for practitioners who want to use adaptive importance sampling in their optimization algorithms. The work represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches to adaptive importance sampling.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
