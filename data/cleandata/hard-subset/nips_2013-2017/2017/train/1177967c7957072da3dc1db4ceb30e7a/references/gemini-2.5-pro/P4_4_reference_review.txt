### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The work is motivated by the fact that while adaptive sampling (e.g., gradient-based) can significantly outperform static sampling schemes, computing the optimal sampling distribution is often as expensive as a full gradient computation, and approximations can lack theoretical guarantees, sometimes performing worse than uniform sampling.

The core idea is to operate with "safe" lower and upper bounds on the gradient information (coordinate-wise absolute values for CD, component-wise gradient norms for SGD) that are cheap to maintain. Given these bounds, the authors formulate the problem of choosing the best sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. Specifically, they seek the distribution that minimizes the worst-case value of a key term in the convergence analysis, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradient vectors consistent with the known bounds.

The main contributions are:
1.  A theoretical analysis for CD showing that optimal gradient-based sampling can improve convergence by a factor of up to `n` (the dimension) compared to fixed sampling.
2.  The proposal of the "safe" sampling scheme based on the minimax formulation. The authors prove that this scheme is guaranteed to perform at least as well as standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., `L_i`-based sampling), thus addressing a major drawback of prior adaptive methods.
3.  An efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve the minimax problem and compute the safe sampling distribution in each iteration.
4.  A demonstration of how to maintain the required safe gradient bounds efficiently (in O(n) time per iteration) for an important class of problems: Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).
5.  Empirical validation on several real-world datasets, showing significant improvements in convergence speed (both in iterations and wall-clock time) over fixed sampling schemes, particularly for CD.

This work builds upon a line of research in adaptive importance sampling for stochastic optimization [5, 22, 23, 36]. It distinguishes itself by introducing a robust, worst-case optimization approach to handle uncertainty in gradient information, which leads to the "safety" guarantee that was absent in previous methods that relied on approximations like stale gradients or upper bounds alone.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs provided in the appendix. The central result (Theorem 3.2), which establishes that the proposed sampling is provably better than fixed `L_i`-based sampling, is a strong and valuable guarantee. The derivation of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the non-trivial minimax problem is a key contribution that ensures the practical viability of the approach. The experimental methodology is sound, comparing the proposed method against relevant baselines (uniform, optimal) and evaluating performance in terms of both iteration complexity and wall-clock time.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The motivation for a "safe" adaptive scheme is well-argued in the introduction. The progression from the ideal full-information setting (Section 2) to the proposed limited-information approach (Section 3) is logical and easy to follow. The problem formulation in (7) is precise. The inclusion of Figure 1, which contrasts the different sampling algorithms, is helpful for understanding the computational trade-offs. The experimental results are presented clearly, and the plots effectively illustrate the performance gains. One minor point for improvement is the presentation of Algorithm 4, which is quite dense; a more intuitive explanation of its mechanics in the main text could enhance readability for a broader audience.

**Originality:**
The paper's primary contribution is highly original. While adaptive importance sampling is an active area of research, the formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization over a "safe set" of possible gradients defined by lower and upper bounds is a novel and principled approach. This contrasts with prior works that often rely on heuristics, stale information, or only upper bounds, without providing worst-case performance guarantees relative to a standard baseline. The idea of seeking robustness against the uncertainty of gradient information is a significant conceptual advance. The proposed directionally-unbiased stepsize for CD is also a neat contribution that simplifies the analysis.

**Significance:**
The results presented are significant and likely to be impactful. The proposed method addresses a critical practical issue with existing adaptive sampling schemes: the risk of performing worse than simple, non-adaptive methods. By providing a "safety" guarantee, the authors have developed a method that practitioners can adopt with more confidence. The approach is generic and applicable to both CD and SGD. The demonstration that even simple, loose bounds (derived via Cauchy-Schwarz for GLMs) are sufficient to achieve substantial speedups is very promising. This work could inspire further research into maintaining tighter and more efficient bounds for a wider variety of machine learning models, thereby broadening the applicability of safe adaptive sampling.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Scope of Bound Maintenance:** The paper demonstrates how to maintain safe bounds for GLMs. While this is an important problem class, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion on the feasibility and challenges of deriving and maintaining such bounds for other models, such as those used in deep learning. The practical utility of the framework is contingent on the availability of these bounds.
2.  **Modest Gains for SGD:** The empirical results for SGD show less dramatic improvements compared to CD. In the experiments shown, even the optimal (full-information) sampling provides only a marginal benefit over uniform sampling. The paper notes this but does not explore the potential reasons. A deeper discussion on why adaptive sampling appears more impactful for CD than SGD in these settings would be valuable.
3.  **Impact of Bound Looseness:** The experiments successfully use loose bounds. However, an analysis or discussion of the relationship between the tightness of the bounds `l` and `u` and the quality of the resulting sampling distribution (e.g., its proximity to the optimal `p*`) would add further insight.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   The core idea of formulating adaptive sampling as a minimax problem over a safe set is novel, elegant, and theoretically sound.
*   The paper provides a strong "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2), proving the proposed method is never worse than standard fixed importance sampling, which is a major practical advantage over prior art.
*   The method is made practical by an efficient O(n log n) algorithm for computing the sampling distribution.
*   The empirical results for coordinate descent are compelling, demonstrating significant speedups in both iteration count and wall-clock time on real datasets.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The demonstration of how to efficiently maintain the required gradient bounds is limited to GLMs, leaving its applicability to other important model classes an open question.
*   The empirical performance gains for SGD are marginal in the presented experiments, which may limit the perceived impact of the method for that family of optimizers.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novel formulation and strong theoretical guarantees, significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The work represents a clear and important contribution to the field of stochastic optimization.
