

# Peer Review: "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address a key limitation of existing adaptive importance sampling methods: while theoretically superior to fixed sampling, they often require expensive computations (e.g., full gradient computation) that negate their benefits. The proposed method computes a sampling distribution using safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information, which can be efficiently maintained during optimization. The key theoretical contribution is an optimization formulation that guarantees the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform or Li-based), while being computationally tractable (O(n log n) per iteration). The authors demonstrate their approach on generalized linear models with L1/L2 regularization, showing significant improvements in convergence rates.

The work builds upon the extensive literature on adaptive importance sampling for optimization [22, 23, 36, 37] but addresses a critical gap: previous methods either lacked theoretical guarantees or required inadmissible computations. The paper properly contextualizes its contribution against relevant prior work, including the seminal work on adaptive sampling by Zhao and Zhang [36] and recent advances in non-uniform sampling [16, 25, 24].

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees. Theorem 3.2 establishes that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling, a significant improvement over prior adaptive methods that lacked such guarantees. The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5-3.6) provides valuable insights into the worst-case performance.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors develop an efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the safe sampling distribution in O(n log n) time, which is comparable to the cost of computing a single gradient component. This makes the method truly practical for large-scale problems.

3. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation**: The experiments on real-world datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with various loss functions (square loss, hinge loss, logistic loss) and regularizers (L1, L2) convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over fixed sampling. The authors also provide insightful analysis of the adaptive stepsize (vk) behavior.

4. **Clear Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a clean distinction between the ideal (full-gradient) adaptive sampling and the safe approximation, clearly demonstrating how the latter can be computed efficiently without sacrificing theoretical guarantees.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Empirical Validation**: While the experiments on GLMs are thorough, the method's applicability to more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks) is not explored. Given that the paper claims to be "completely generic," this limitation should be acknowledged, and future work should address this gap.

2. **SGD Performance Gap**: The paper notes that "the effect of the proposed sampling is less pronounced in the three SGD experiments" compared to CD. The authors don't adequately explain why this is the case, which is a significant omission given that SGD is a more widely used optimization method.

3. **Comparison with Recent Methods**: The paper references recent adaptive sampling methods [22, 5, 26, 10, 21, 23] but doesn't provide a direct comparison with some of the most recent approaches (e.g., [3, 21, 23]). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's claim of superiority.

4. **Practical Implementation Details**: While the paper discusses the computational complexity, it doesn't provide specific timing results comparing the proposed method with baseline methods on large-scale datasets. The claim that "the introduced overhead reduces to O(log n) per iteration" when d = Ω(n) would be more convincing with concrete timing data.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The "safe" adaptive importance sampling approach addresses a real gap in the literature by providing a method that is both theoretically guaranteed to outperform fixed sampling and computationally efficient. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the method's benefits.

The main limitations are the limited scope of the empirical validation and the unexplained performance gap between CD and SGD. However, these are not fundamental flaws but rather opportunities for future work. The paper's contributions are substantial and will likely be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in optimization.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field with strong theoretical guarantees and practical implementation. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrated to be effective on real-world problems. While there are some limitations in scope, they do not undermine the core contribution, which is well-supported by both theory and experiments. The paper meets all four review criteria at a high level:

- **Quality**: Exceptional theoretical analysis with well-supported claims
- **Clarity**: Well-organized, clearly written with precise mathematical formulation
- **Originality**: Novel optimization formulation for safe adaptive sampling that distinguishes it from prior work
- **Significance**: Provides a practically useful method that can be easily integrated into existing optimization frameworks with demonstrable performance gains

The paper advances the state of the art in optimization methods for machine learning and should be accepted for publication at NeurIPS.
