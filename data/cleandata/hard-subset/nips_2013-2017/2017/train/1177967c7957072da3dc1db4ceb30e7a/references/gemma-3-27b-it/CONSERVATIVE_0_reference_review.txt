This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD), aiming to improve computational efficiency over fixed sampling strategies. The core idea revolves around computing sampling probabilities based on available, bounded gradient information, rather than requiring full gradient computations. The authors present theoretical justifications for their approach, demonstrating advantages over uniform sampling and establishing guarantees related to worst-case performance. They also provide an algorithm for efficient computation of these probabilities and validate the method empirically on several datasets.

The paper is generally well-written and structured, presenting a clear progression of ideas from motivation to theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the existing literature on importance sampling and adaptive optimization techniques. However, while the theoretical results appear solid, the practical significance of the proposed method, particularly for SGD, feels somewhat limited given the computational overhead.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis provides convincing arguments for the theoretical benefits of the proposed adaptive sampling, particularly in the context of CD. The bounds established offer valuable insights into the performance guarantees.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm for computing the sampling distribution is efficient (O(n log n)) and avoids the need for full gradient computations, making it potentially applicable to large-scale problems.
*   **Safety Guarantees:** The emphasis on "safe" sampling – ensuring that the proposed distribution is never worse than fixed sampling – is a valuable contribution, addressing a common concern with adaptive methods.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper effectively positions its contribution within the existing landscape of importance sampling and coordinate descent methods.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on standard datasets, providing empirical support for the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited SGD Gains:** The empirical results show only modest improvements for SGD compared to uniform sampling. This raises questions about the practical benefit of the proposed scheme in this setting, given the added computational complexity.  The authors acknowledge this but don't deeply investigate *why* the gains are smaller for SGD.
*   **Gradient Bound Acquisition:**  The practical utility heavily relies on accurately maintaining safe gradient bounds `l` and `u`. While the authors discuss one method for GLMs, the applicability and ease of maintaining these bounds in more complex models aren't fully explored.  The sensitivity of the performance to the tightness of these bounds is also not thoroughly investigated.  Loose bounds will diminish any benefit.
*   **Overhead vs. Benefit:** While the O(n log n) overhead is reasonable, it’s not insignificant.  For very high-dimensional problems (e.g., news20), where d is much larger than n, the potential gains may be outweighed by the computational cost of computing the sampling distribution in each iteration.
*   **Comparison to Existing Adaptive Methods:** A more thorough comparison to existing adaptive sampling techniques (e.g., those based on gradient norms as in [22, 36, 37]) would strengthen the paper.  It’s unclear how this approach compares in terms of computational cost and performance to these alternatives.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** While generally well-written, some sections (e.g., the discussion around Lemma 2.4) are dense and could benefit from clearer explanations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically implementable adaptive sampling scheme.
*   The safety guarantees are a valuable contribution, addressing a key concern with adaptive methods.
*   The empirical results, while not overwhelmingly strong for SGD, demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in the CD setting.
*   The work addresses an important problem in large-scale optimization.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The practical gains for SGD are modest, and the computational overhead may outweigh the benefits in certain settings.
*   The reliance on accurate gradient bounds introduces a potential practical limitation.
*   The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison to existing adaptive sampling methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the theoretical contributions are solid and the proposed method is well-motivated, the limited gains observed for SGD and the potential challenges in maintaining accurate gradient bounds raise concerns about its overall impact. The paper represents a reasonable incremental improvement, but it doesn’t fundamentally shift the state of the art.  I would recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors address the limitations regarding SGD and discuss the practical considerations of maintaining safe gradient bounds in more detail. Further, a clearer comparison with competing adaptive sampling schemes would strengthen the paper's contribution.
