Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies adaptive importance sampling for randomized coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The ideal “full-information” sampler chooses coordinates (or data points) with probabilities proportional to gradient magnitudes (or component gradient norms), which maximizes the one-step improvement under a quadratic smoothness model. However, computing these probabilities requires full gradients each iteration, which is prohibitive.

The core contribution is a “safe” adaptive sampler that, at each iteration, uses efficiently maintainable lower/upper bounds on the (absolute) coordinate gradients for CD (or norms of component gradients for SGD) and chooses a distribution p̂k by solving a robust min–max problem:
minp∈Δ maxc∈Ck V(p,c)/||c||^2, where Ck is the box defined by the bounds and V(p,c)=∑i Li c_i^2/p_i. This yields two guarantees: (i) p̂k is optimal in a worst-case sense over the uncertainty set Ck, and (ii) it is never worse (in the same model) than classical fixed importance sampling pL∝Li (hence never worse than uniform when Li=L). The resulting step-size is α̂k=1/vk, where vk is the optimal value of the min–max problem, and the per-iteration update uses the standard importance-sampling scaling γk=α̂k/p̂k[i].

A key technical result shows the min–max reduces to a thresholding problem: the worst-case ĉ has entries ĉi=clip(√Li m, ℓi, ui) for a scalar m satisfying m=||ĉ||^2/||√L ĉ||1. An O(n log n) algorithm computes ĉ and p̂k∝√L ĉ. The scheme recovers the full-information sampler as the bounds tighten.

For tractability of bounds, the paper details how to maintain safe lower/upper bounds in GLMs for CD and SGD using smoothness and simple inequalities (e.g., Cauchy–Schwarz), updating in O(n) time per iteration. Experiments on several text datasets show substantial iteration/time gains over fixed sampling for CD (less so for SGD), with performance close to the full-information oracle.

This builds on and differs from: importance sampling for CD/SGD with fixed probabilities [Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.; Zhao & Zhang], adaptive sampling via duality gaps or stale gradients [Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.; Stich et al.], Gauss-Southwell rules, and screening rules. The novelty is a principled robust optimization of the sampling distribution under uncertainty that guarantees no degradation vs fixed sampling and converges to the gradient-based optimum as bounds tighten.

Quality (soundness)
- The derivations aligning one-step progress with V(p,∇f)/||∇f||^2 and the choice γk=αk/pk[i] are standard and correct; with pL and α=1/Tr[L] they recover the classical per-coordinate step 1/Li.
- The min–max formulation is well-motivated. The reduction to the thresholding structure and the O(n log n) algorithm are elegant and technically solid. The stated guarantees (never worse than pL; best worst-case over Ck; recovery of full-information limit) follow from the formulation.
- The exchange of min and max (and inequality (∗)) is delicate; the paper cites Sion/Komiya-style results. Please ensure all conditions (compactness, convexity/concavity in the relevant variables) are explicitly checked, since the ratio V(p,c)/||c||^2 is not convex in c. The appendix likely addresses this, but it is critical.
- There are some notational inconsistencies/typos that should be fixed for correctness and readability: V(p,x) is defined with multiplication by p in the main text, but the subsequent lemma and results require V(p,x)=∑ Li x_i^2/p_i. Algorithm 4’s variable names (ℓ,u reused as indices vs vectors) are confusing.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, with a clear narrative from ideal sampling to robustified, efficiently computable sampling, and then to practical bound maintenance and experiments.
- However, several equations and definitions suffer from typos (noted above), and some proofs critical to the main guarantees are deferred. A cleaner, self-contained statement of assumptions and a short proof sketch for min–max interchange would help.
- The complexity discussion is concise but omits an important caveat: maintaining bounds in O(n) per iteration can dominate the per-iteration cost in sparse high-dimensional problems where a coordinate update costs O(nnz of a column). See weaknesses below.

Originality
- The robust min–max formulation of adaptive sampling under safe gradient bounds, its closed-form threshold structure, and the worst-case improvement guarantee over fixed sampling appear novel. Prior adaptive schemes either rely on stale/full gradients, heuristic proxies, or do not provide a worst-case “never worse” guarantee.
- The unified treatment for CD and SGD with the importance-sampling-consistent stepsize is clean and connects well to prior lines of work.

Significance
- The approach offers a principled way to leverage partial gradient information without risking degradation versus fixed sampling, and it nearly matches the ideal sampler as bounds tighten. For CD, empirical gains are sizable and of practical interest.
- The impact for SGD seems more limited in practice (as the authors also note). Still, the framework is general and could inspire robust adaptive sampling in other settings.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual advance: principled robust adaptive sampling with a guaranteed baseline and convergence to the ideal distribution as information improves.
- Solid theory with interpretable guarantees; elegant O(n log n) algorithm via thresholding.
- Thoughtful connection to unbiased importance-sampled updates and stepsize choice.
- Empirical results on multiple losses/regularizers for CD substantiate the theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practicality for sparse large-scale settings: The bound maintenance and sampling computation cost O(n) and O(n log n) per iteration, respectively. In many CD applications with extremely sparse features, a single coordinate update is O(nnz in the chosen column), often far less than O(n). Updating bounds for all coordinates each iteration can be the dominant cost. Suggestions:
  - Consider amortization: recompute p̂k every T iterations, or maintain lazy updates with event-triggered refresh.
  - Exploit sparsity structure: update bounds only for coordinates with nonzero overlap with the active coordinate (using an inverted index), or maintain per-block bounds.
  - Provide experiments quantifying overhead vs coordinate computation cost on truly sparse data, and compare wall-clock with and without bound updates on the same machine.
- Empirical comparisons: Include stronger baselines such as Gauss–Southwell(-Lipschitz), duality gap–based adaptive CD [Perekrestenko et al.], and cached/stale gradient-based samplers to contextualize gains.
- SGD section: Theoretical development is less complete (e.g., explicit stepsize schedules, assumptions on smoothness/strong convexity), and empirical gains are modest. Either strengthen theory/experiments (e.g., variance-reduced methods, minibatching with importance weights, tighter bounds than Cauchy–Schwarz) or de-emphasize.
- Min–max proof details and conditions (compactness of Ck with infinite upper bounds; behavior when ℓ=0, u=∞ at initialization): The paper uses ℓ0=0, u0=∞; explain how vk and p̂k are well-defined initially (e.g., scale invariance of the ratio, choice of pL).
- Notational/typo issues: Fix V(p,x) definition, clean Algorithm 4 pseudo-code, and ensure consistent use of absolute values vs signed gradients.

Pros/cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and principled approach with clear theoretical guarantees.
- Elegant algorithmic solution.
- Strong improvements for CD empirically; practical relevance.

Cons:
- Overhead may limit practicality in sparse regimes; needs deeper empirical validation and engineering discussion.
- Comparisons could be stronger; SGD impact limited.
- Some technical details deferred/opaque and a few notational errors.

Recommendation
Borderline accept leaning accept. The paper presents a meaningful theoretical and algorithmic contribution to adaptive sampling with a compelling story and promising CD results. Addressing the practicality concerns and tightening presentation would make it a clear accept.
