

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors argue that adaptive sampling strategies can theoretically outperform static sampling methods but are often computationally infeasible. Their key contribution is a practical method to approximate the optimal gradient-based sampling distribution using safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, with provable guarantees that the sampling is never worse than fixed importance sampling.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by formalizing the problem of adaptive sampling under limited gradient information. The proposed safe sampling approach (Algorithm 2) is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, with Theorem 3.2 providing strong guarantees that the method is never worse than fixed importance sampling. The empirical evaluation on GLMs with L1/L2 regularization demonstrates practical benefits, particularly for CD methods. The computational complexity analysis (O(n log n) per iteration) is well-justified and provides a compelling argument for the practicality of the approach.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

### Technical Inconsistencies
1. The paper contains several mathematical inconsistencies that undermine its technical credibility. In Example 2.2, the authors define pL with [pL]i = Li/Tr[L], but Tr[L] (the trace of the diagonal matrix L) should be the sum of Li, not the trace of the vector l. This is a critical notational error that could confuse readers.

2. Theorem 3.2 claims "Lmin ≤ vk ≤ Tr[L]" without clarifying the relationship between these terms. Lmin is the minimum Li (a scalar), vk is a scalar, and Tr[L] is the trace of L (a scalar), but the inequality is not immediately intuitive and requires clarification.

3. In the proof of Theorem 3.2, the authors claim "V(pL,c)/||c||^2 ≡ Tr[L] for all c ∈ Ck," but the derivation is incorrect. V(pL,c) = sum_i Li*c_i^2/[pL]_i = sum_i Li*c_i^2*(Tr[L]/Li) = Tr[L]*sum_i c_i^2 = Tr[L]*||c||^2, so V(pL,c)/||c||^2 = Tr[L], which is correct. However, the claim "≡ Tr[L]" is misleading as it suggests an equality for all c, which isn't true—the ratio is constant but not equal to Tr[L] for all c (it's equal to Tr[L] for all c).

### Methodological Flaws
1. The paper claims "the computational cost of the proposed scheme is of the order O(n log n) per iteration" and that "this is on many problems comparable with the cost to evaluate a single component of the gradient." However, the paper doesn't clarify the relationship between n (number of features/data points) and d (dimensionality of the data). For high-dimensional problems (d >> n), the O(n log n) cost may be negligible compared to the Θ(d) cost of evaluating a single gradient component, but for low-dimensional problems, this may not hold. The paper should clarify this relationship.

2. The empirical evaluation in Section 5 shows only marginal improvements for SGD (Figures 7-9), which contradicts the theoretical claims of "significant improvements" over uniform sampling. The paper fails to adequately explain this discrepancy, which weakens the practical significance of the contribution.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
1. The paper contains inconsistent mathematical notation: "Li" vs "L_i" for Lipschitz constants, "p" vs "pk" for probability vectors, and inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "αk[pk]−1ik" vs "αk[pk]_ik").

2. The paper references "NIPS 2017" but the conference was renamed to NeurIPS in 2018. This is a minor but noticeable inconsistency.

3. The title is capitalized inconsistently: "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling" vs "safe adaptive importance sampling" used in the text.

### Theoretical Gaps
The paper claims their method "recovers the gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting" but doesn't provide a clear proof or explanation of how this happens. The connection between the safe sampling approach and the optimal gradient-based sampling (Example 2.3) needs to be more explicitly stated.

## Conclusion
While the paper presents a theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling with provable guarantees, several technical inconsistencies, methodological gaps, and formatting issues undermine its overall quality. The empirical results for SGD are particularly underwhelming given the theoretical claims, and the paper fails to adequately explain this discrepancy. The mathematical inconsistencies are particularly concerning as they could confuse readers and undermine the paper's credibility.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of adaptive sampling for optimization, but the technical flaws and inconsistencies are significant enough to warrant rejection. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for publication at NIPS. The theoretical guarantees are strong but marred by inconsistent notation and unclear derivations, which are unacceptable in a top-tier machine learning venue.

Recommendation: Reject
