This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The central idea is to bridge the gap between fixed, computationally cheap but suboptimal sampling strategies (e.g., uniform sampling) and optimal, gradient-based adaptive strategies that are computationally prohibitive as they require full gradient information at each iteration.

The authors' approach relies on maintaining safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient components (for CD) or the norms of component gradients (for SGD). Instead of using these bounds heuristically, they formulate the problem of choosing a sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem: find the distribution `p` that minimizes the worst-case value of the one-step progress bound, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradients consistent with the current bounds. The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem. Key theoretical contributions include showing that the resulting sampling distribution is provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling (hence "safe") and demonstrating that optimal sampling can theoretically achieve a speedup of up to factor `n` in CD. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated empirically on several real-world datasets for generalized linear models.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Novelty:** The core contribution—formulating the selection of a sampling distribution as a minimax problem given bounds on the gradient—is highly original and theoretically principled. This provides a robust way to handle uncertainty about the gradient information, moving beyond prior heuristic approaches (e.g., using upper bounds as estimates) which lack guarantees. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of adaptive sampling.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and leads to strong guarantees. Theorem 3.2, which proves that the proposed "safe" sampling is always better than or equal to fixed Li-based importance sampling, is the cornerstone of the paper and provides a compelling reason to use this method. The development of an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the sampling distribution is crucial and makes the approach practical. The analysis in Section 2, which includes a novel adaptive step-size for CD and clarifies the potential speedup, is also a valuable contribution.

*   **Significance and Impact:** This work addresses a very relevant and active area of research. Improving the efficiency of fundamental optimizers like CD and SGD has a broad impact. The proposed method is generic and can be seen as a "plug-and-play" enhancement for many existing solvers, provided that gradient bounds can be maintained. The paper convincingly demonstrates how to do this for the important class of generalized linear models (GLMs). The promise of achieving near-optimal adaptive performance without the prohibitive cost of full gradient computation is highly significant.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-structured. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, methodically building the case for a "safe" adaptive approach. The theoretical results are stated precisely, and the experimental section clearly presents the empirical validation of the claims.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are several areas where it could be improved.

1.  **Clarity on Bound Computation and Quality (Section 4):** The section on obtaining the safe gradient bounds is crucial for the practical application of the method, but it is rather brief.
    *   For GLMs, the update rule relies on bounding terms like `⟨a_i, a_{i_k}⟩`. The paper mentions using Cauchy-Schwarz, which can be very loose. A brief discussion on the quality of these bounds and how their tightness affects the performance (i.e., the value of `v_k`) would be insightful. The experimental plots show `v_k` is much better than the worst-case `Tr[L]`, which suggests the bounds are effective in practice, but a more direct analysis or discussion would strengthen the paper.
    *   For the SGD case (Lemma 4.1), the derivation of the bounds is even less detailed. The update depends on `∇^2 h_i`, which implies a need to bound the second derivatives of the loss functions. It would be helpful to explicitly state what assumptions are made here (e.g., Lipschitz Hessian) and how the final bounds `l_k` and `u_k` are constructed. This is important for reproducibility.

2.  **Analysis of SGD Performance:** The experiments show that the proposed method offers significant speedups for CD but only marginal gains for SGD (Figures 7-9). The paper notes this discrepancy but does not offer an explanation. This is a key finding that warrants more discussion. Why is adaptive sampling less effective for SGD in this setting? Is it because the gradient norms `||∇f_i(x_k)||` are less informative or vary less than the coordinate gradients `|∇_i f(x_k)|`? Or are the bounds derived for the SGD case significantly looser than for the CD case? Providing some intuition or analysis on this point would make the experimental section more complete.

3.  **Computational Complexity Overhead:** The paper correctly states the overhead of computing the sampling is `O(n log n)`. It then claims this reduces to `O(log n)` per iteration when `d = O(n)`. This is a bit imprecise. The cost of one gradient update is `O(d)`, so the *relative* overhead is `O(n log n / d)`. If `d=n`, the relative cost is `O(log n)`, which is not negligible. It would be clearer to state that the overhead becomes insignificant when `d >> n log n`. While the timing experiments show the method is indeed faster overall, a more nuanced discussion of the computational trade-offs would be beneficial.

4.  **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The competitive ratio `ρ_k` (Lemma 3.5, 3.6) is an interesting theoretical tool for analyzing the quality of the sampling. However, it feels disconnected from the rest of the paper as it is not used in the experiments. It would be a nice addition to empirically estimate or plot an upper bound on `ρ_k` (e.g., `v_k / w_k`, if `w_k` can be efficiently lower-bounded) during training. This would provide a quantitative measure of how close the "safe" sampling is to the "in-hindsight" optimal sampling at each step.

### Decision

This is an excellent paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the literature on stochastic optimization. The "safe" sampling framework is elegant, well-supported by theory, and shown to be effective in practice. The identified weaknesses are relatively minor and mostly pertain to areas where the paper could be clarified or expanded. They do not detract from the core strength of the contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
