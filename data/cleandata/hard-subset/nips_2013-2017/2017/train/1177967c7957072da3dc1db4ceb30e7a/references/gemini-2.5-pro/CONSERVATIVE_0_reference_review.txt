### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD), termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling". The authors acknowledge that optimal gradient-based sampling is computationally infeasible as it requires full gradient information at each iteration. Conversely, existing approximations can lack theoretical guarantees and sometimes perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The core contribution is a method that uses safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient information (magnitudes for CD, norms for SGD) to define a feasible set for the unknown true gradient. Instead of heuristically using these bounds, the authors formulate a minimax problem: find the sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case objective progress, where the worst-case is over all possible gradients within the defined bounds. This approach yields a sampling distribution that is provably at least as good as fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform sampling). The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this minimax problem. They demonstrate how to derive the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and present empirical results on several real-world datasets, showing their method outperforms fixed sampling and approaches the performance of the impractical optimal sampler.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled Theoretical Formulation:** The central idea of formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem (Equation 7) is elegant and methodologically sound. It provides a rigorous way to handle the uncertainty inherent in having only partial gradient information, which is a clear improvement over more heuristic approaches that might, for instance, simply sample based on the upper bounds.

2.  **"Safety" Guarantee:** The proof that the proposed sampling is provably never worse than fixed Li-based importance sampling (Theorem 3.2) is a significant strength. This "safety net" addresses a critical weakness of many adaptive schemes, where the overhead of adaptation can lead to worse performance than a simple, non-adaptive baseline. This guarantee provides a strong theoretical justification for the method's adoption.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the proposed method is well-defined, and the theoretical results are stated precisely.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope and Dependence on Bounds:** The entire framework is critically dependent on the ability to compute and maintain tight, safe bounds on the gradient information (`l` and `u`). The paper demonstrates this for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs), a well-understood class of convex problems. However, the proposed bounding strategy based on Cauchy-Schwarz is quite loose. The authors themselves note in the experiments that they use "very loose safe gradient bounds". While the method performs well in this context, it is unclear how its performance would scale to problems where such bounds are either much looser or computationally expensive to obtain. The applicability to more complex, non-convex models prevalent at NIPS, such as deep neural networks, is not addressed and appears highly non-trivial. The paper’s contribution is therefore confined to a specific, albeit important, class of optimization problems.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Baselines:** The experimental evaluation compares the proposed method ("safe sampling") primarily against uniform sampling and the computationally prohibitive "optimal sampling". While this demonstrates the method works better than a simple baseline and approaches an ideal one, it fails to compare against other relevant, *practical* adaptive sampling methods. For example, methods based on stale gradients [22] or simpler upper-bound approximations [36, 23] are key competitors that are mentioned in the introduction but absent from the empirical evaluation. A thorough validation would require demonstrating superiority not just over non-adaptive methods, but also over existing state-of-the-art adaptive heuristics. Without these comparisons, the practical significance of the proposed method is not fully established.

3.  **Computational Overhead:** The proposed O(n log n) procedure to compute the sampling distribution is incurred at *every iteration*. The authors argue this is acceptable when the cost of a gradient computation `d` is large (d = Ω(n log n)). However, for many large-scale machine learning problems, `n` (number of features for CD, or data points for SGD) can be much larger than `d`, making this overhead significant. The experiments are run on datasets where this overhead does not appear to be prohibitive, but the analysis of the trade-off is not sufficiently deep. For instance, one could perform `log(n)` uniform sampling steps in the time it takes to compute one "safe" sample; it is not obvious that the latter is always preferable.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses for Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound minimax framework for adaptive sampling under uncertainty.
*   It provides a crucial "safety" guarantee, ensuring the method is provably better than standard fixed importance sampling, a property lacking in many competing approaches.
*   The method is clearly presented and supported by an efficient algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical utility of the method is only demonstrated for a limited class of problems (GLMs), and its applicability to more complex models is questionable and unproven.
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete. It lacks crucial comparisons to other established, practical adaptive sampling methods, making it difficult to assess its place in the state of the art.
*   The per-iteration overhead of O(n log n) may be a significant limitation in settings where `n` is very large relative to the cost of a single coordinate/stochastic gradient evaluation.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a methodologically sound and theoretically appealing idea, its evaluation is not comprehensive enough. The lack of comparison to relevant practical baselines and the unaddressed questions of its applicability beyond convex GLMs limit its demonstrated significance. The work is a solid contribution to the theory of stochastic optimization but requires more extensive evidence to be endorsed as a new state-of-the-art practical method.
