

# Peer Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address a key challenge in adaptive importance sampling: while gradient-based sampling theoretically offers significant speedups over fixed sampling (up to a factor of the dimension n), it typically requires expensive full-gradient computations in each iteration. The paper's main contribution is a practical method that efficiently computes adaptive sampling distributions using only safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, while still providing theoretical guarantees that the method is never worse than fixed importance sampling.

The authors first establish theoretical foundations by showing that gradient-based sampling for CD (Example 2.3) can outperform fixed sampling (Example 2.2) by a factor of n. They then formulate the problem of finding the best adaptive sampling distribution under safe gradient bounds as a convex optimization problem (Equation 7), which they solve efficiently in O(n log n) time (Theorem 3.4). The paper demonstrates that these bounds can be efficiently computed for generalized linear models (GLMs), which are common in machine learning applications. Extensive empirical evaluations on multiple datasets and loss functions show that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in CD, with only a small computational overhead.

This work builds on and extends prior work on adaptive importance sampling [22, 36, 37], but makes a key theoretical and practical advance by providing a method that is both computationally efficient and theoretically guaranteed to outperform fixed sampling.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with clear proofs for all key claims (Lemma 2.4, Theorem 3.2, Theorem 3.4, etc.). The authors carefully distinguish between the optimal (full-information) case and their practical "safe" approach, and they provide clear theoretical guarantees about the performance of their method relative to fixed sampling.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors test their method on multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with various loss functions (square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss) and regularization types (L1, L2), providing both iteration complexity and time-based comparisons. They also include detailed analysis of the adaptive stepsize (vk) to demonstrate how the method leverages gradient information. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., the less pronounced effect in SGD compared to CD) and provides a thoughtful discussion of the observed results.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the key concepts (optimal sampling, safe sampling, gradient bounds) are clearly explained. The algorithms (Algorithms 1-4) are presented clearly, and the connection between theory and implementation is well-maintained.

However, some sections could be improved for clarity. For instance, the explanation of why the proposed sampling is better than uniform sampling in the context of Example 3.1 could be more intuitive. The relationship between the theoretical guarantees and the practical implementation could also be made more explicit in some places. The appendix contains valuable technical details, but some of the key proofs (e.g., the proof of inequality (*) in Equation 8) might be better placed in the main text to improve flow.

## Originality

The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. While adaptive importance sampling has been studied before [22, 36, 37], the key novelty here is the "safe" approach that provides theoretical guarantees while being computationally efficient. Previous methods either required full gradient computations (which defeats the purpose of stochastic methods) or used approximate bounds without theoretical guarantees about performance relative to fixed sampling.

The authors clearly distinguish their work from prior contributions and provide a novel optimization formulation (Equation 7) that leads to a practical method with provable advantages. The paper also makes a strong case for the practical applicability of their method by demonstrating how to efficiently compute the safe gradient bounds for GLMs, which are a common class of problems in machine learning.

## Significance

The problem addressed in this paper is highly significant for large-scale machine learning applications. The proposed method provides a theoretically grounded approach to adaptive importance sampling that can be efficiently implemented, with empirical results showing substantial improvements over fixed sampling. The computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration) is small compared to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component (which is typically O(d)), making the method practically viable.

The paper advances the state of the art in adaptive sampling by providing a method that combines theoretical guarantees with practical efficiency. This is particularly valuable because previous adaptive sampling methods either lacked theoretical guarantees or were computationally prohibitive. The results are likely to be of interest to both practitioners (who can directly apply the method) and researchers (who can build on the theoretical framework).

## Strengths

- Strong theoretical foundation with clear guarantees that the proposed method outperforms fixed sampling
- Practical and efficient implementation (O(n log n) per iteration)
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets, loss functions, and regularization types
- Clear demonstration of how to compute safe gradient bounds for GLMs, making the method applicable to real-world problems
- Well-organized presentation that effectively connects theory to practice

## Weaknesses

- The paper could provide more insight into why adaptive sampling is less effective in SGD compared to CD (the empirical results show this, but the theoretical explanation is limited)
- The analysis of the competitive ratio (ρk) could be more detailed, particularly regarding how it varies with different gradient bounds
- The paper focuses primarily on GLMs for demonstrating the gradient bound computation, but it would be valuable to mention potential extensions to more complex models (though the authors do acknowledge this as future work)
- Some of the key mathematical details (e.g., the proof of inequality (*) in Equation 8) are deferred to the appendix, which may make the paper less accessible to some readers

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling with strong theoretical foundations, practical implementation, and comprehensive empirical validation. The paper addresses a real problem in large-scale machine learning with a well-motivated solution that advances the state of the art. The method is both theoretically sound and practically useful, with the potential to be widely adopted in the machine learning community.

The paper is of high quality, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The authors have carefully considered the strengths and limitations of their approach, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the advantages of their method.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief discussion of why adaptive sampling shows less improvement in SGD compared to CD, perhaps based on the theoretical analysis in Section 2.2.

2. In the experimental results, include a comparison of the time per iteration for the proposed method versus fixed sampling to better illustrate the computational overhead.

3. For the competitive ratio analysis (ρk), consider adding a small example showing how ρk varies with different gradient bounds to provide more intuition.

4. The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the geometric interpretation of the optimization problem (Section 3.2) to help readers better understand why the proposed solution is optimal.

5. The paper mentions that "no initialization of the gradient estimates is needed" - it would be helpful to explain briefly why this is the case (i.e., why the method converges quickly even from `0 = 0n, u0 = ∞n).

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. I believe it will be of interest to the NIPS community and will likely have a positive impact on both theoretical and practical aspects of large-scale optimization.
