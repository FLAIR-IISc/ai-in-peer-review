Summary
This paper tackles adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to bridge the gap between “optimal” gradient-based sampling—which typically requires full gradient information each iteration—and practical feasibility. The authors propose a safe adaptive sampling scheme that only uses per-iteration lower/upper bounds on coordinate (or component) gradients. They cast the choice of sampling distribution as a min–max optimization over a quadratic upper bound surrogate, derive a distribution p̂ and stepsize α̂ that (i) recover the full-information optimal sampling when bounds are tight and (ii) are provably never worse than fixed importance sampling (Li-based). They further provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute p̂ using a constructive characterization (Theorem 3.4) together with a geometric interpretation, and show that for CD the expected one-step progress can improve by up to a factor n in special cases. They give simple, cheap procedures to maintain safe bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs), and show empirical gains—especially for CD—on standard datasets; gains for SGD are smaller.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on non-uniform/importance sampling for CD/SGD (Richtárik & Takáč; Nesterov; Zhao & Zhang), adaptive probabilities (e.g., Csiba et al., Perekrestenko et al.), and steepest/Gauss–Southwell CD (Nutini et al., Stich et al.). Prior “gradient-based” sampling typically optimizes expected progress under a quadratic model but requires full gradients or resorts to stale/upper-bound heuristics with no safety guarantees. Safe screening/VI ideas (Liu et al., Ndiaye et al.) inform the notion of bounding quantities. The paper’s novelty is to pose and solve a min–max problem over the uncertainty set defined by bounds, yielding a distribution with a worst-case guarantee that strictly improves on classical fixed sampling.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A principled “safe” adaptive importance sampling under limited information, formulated as a convex min–max and solved efficiently. The guarantee “never worse than fixed Li-based sampling” is crisp and appealing.
- Theory: Clear one-step progress analysis; characterization of the optimal p̂ and stepsize α̂=1/vk; geometric interpretation; constructive O(n log n) algorithm; competitive-ratio bounds. The connection showing recovery of full-information sampling is elegant.
- Practicality: Simple ways to maintain safe bounds for GLMs via cheap inner-product bounds; the per-iteration overhead is modest and often amortized.
- Empirics: Consistent and sometimes large gains for CD across losses and regularizers; stepsize adaptation is shown to matter; plots of vk demonstrate meaningful advantage despite loose bounds.

Weaknesses
- Scope of experiments: Limited to GLMs with synthetic-safe bounds; no comparisons to the strongest adaptive CD baselines (e.g., Gauss–Southwell variants, duality-gap sampling [Perekrestenko et al.], AdaSDCA) on the same tasks, nor to approximate steepest CD with screening/cache mechanisms.
- SGD results are modest: The improvement is small relative to uniform, suggesting limited practical impact for SGD as presented. A deeper analysis of when/why the method helps SGD would strengthen significance.
- Computational overhead and scaling: Maintaining bounds costs O(n) per iteration plus O(n log n) for sampling; for very large n and small d this may dominate. The paper argues d=Ω(n) in many problems, but an empirical breakdown of overhead vs. baseline would be helpful; also consider sparse updates or partial refresh of bounds.
- Min–max justification: The interchange of min and max (used in inequality (*)) is deferred to the appendix. The objective is a ratio V(p,c)/||c||^2; please clarify convex–concave properties and the precise conditions under which Sion’s theorem applies. This is central to Theorem 3.2.
- Assumptions and generality: Convex, coordinate-wise Lipschitz setting with known Li. Discussion of robustness to misspecified Li, and extension to composite/non-smooth beyond separable regularizers would help. Non-convex objectives are out of scope.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The safe min–max formulation and its solution appear correct and useful, with a constructive algorithm and supporting lemmas. Some key technical steps are deferred; bringing the min–max interchange conditions and the complexity analysis into the main text would improve confidence. Experiments are solid for CD but thin on baselines and analysis for SGD.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The motivation, algorithms, and theoretical properties are presented cleanly. Minor typos and formatting issues (e.g., references, footnotes) should be corrected. It would help to:
- Explicitly state the convex–concave structure enabling the min–max swap.
- Summarize full convergence rates (not only one-step progress) for Algorithm 2 in the main text.
- Provide an algorithmic complexity table contrasting per-iteration costs vs. baselines.

Originality
High. The “safe under uncertainty” min–max construction for adaptive sampling with provable improvement over fixed schemes is, to my knowledge, new. The efficient solver and geometric view are also novel contributions, beyond earlier heuristic/upper-bound-based adaptive sampling strategies.

Significance
Moderate to high. For CD on convex problems, the method is likely to be used and built upon, given its safety guarantee and practicality. Impact on SGD seems less clear from current experiments, but the conceptual framework could influence adaptive sampling in broader settings, including variance-reduced methods, block/multi-coordinate sampling, and distributed tracking.

Questions and suggestions
- Please compare against Gauss–Southwell (exact/approximate) and duality-gap/adaptive-probability baselines on the same datasets.
- Provide ablations on bound tightness: how do better bounds affect vk and wall-clock?
- Discuss extensions to minibatch/block sampling, variance-reduced methods, and distributed implementations (beyond high-level remarks).
- Clarify how often one needs to recompute/sort to maintain p̂ efficiently across iterations (can we reuse order statistics?).
- Analyze robustness to inexact Li and to stale or one-sided bounds.

Pros
- Novel safe adaptive sampling with strong worst-case guarantee.
- Efficient constructive algorithm; recovers full-info optimal sampling.
- Solid theory and clear connection to stepsize choice.
- Strong empirical gains for CD.

Cons
- Limited experimental baselines; small gains for SGD.
- Overhead may be nontrivial for very large n; scalability discussion could be deeper.
- Some key theoretical justifications are deferred/implicit.

Recommendation: Accept (weak accept). The safety-guaranteed, efficiently computable adaptive sampling under limited information is a fresh and useful idea with promising empirical evidence for CD. Even with some experimental gaps, the conceptual advance merits inclusion.

Confidence: High on overall assessment; medium on all technical details pending appendix verification.
