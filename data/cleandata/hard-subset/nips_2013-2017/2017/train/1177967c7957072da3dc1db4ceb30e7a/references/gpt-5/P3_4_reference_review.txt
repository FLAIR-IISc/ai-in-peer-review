Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles adaptive importance sampling for stochastic first-order methods, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and SGD. It first re-derives that gradient-based sampling is optimal under the quadratic upper bound model and can yield up to an n-fold gain over fixed or uniform sampling in CD. The core contribution is a “safe” adaptive scheme when full gradients are unavailable: given per-coordinate (CD) or per-component (SGD) lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes, the authors formulate a min–max problem over probabilities p and unknown gradients c in a box Ck that selects p to minimize the worst-case expected decrease bound. They prove this sampling never underperforms Li-based fixed importance sampling (and hence uniform when Li are equal) and provide an O(n log n) algorithm to compute it. They also propose to use a corresponding “safe” stepsize α̂k = 1/vk, where vk is the min–max value. They show how to maintain safe gradient bounds efficiently for GLMs, and support the approach with experiments on several datasets. The approach relates to prior works on fixed importance sampling [Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.], gradient-based adaptive sampling [Zhao & Zhang; Zhu], and duality-gap-based adaptive CD [Perekrestenko et al.; Csiba et al.]. The novelty is a robust optimization formulation producing a distribution that is provably never worse than fixed sampling given uncertainty sets.

Strengths:
- Clear optimization-based formulation of adaptive sampling with uncertainty, with a clean guarantee: p̂ is optimal in a worst-case sense over the given bounds and is never worse than Li-based sampling.
- Efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) with an intuitive “water-filling” structure; the geometric interpretation aids understanding.
- The expected one-step progress analysis for CD is standard but used effectively to motivate the scheme, including a new stepsize rule that recovers gradient-based sampling when full information is available.
- Practical guidance on maintaining safe bounds in GLMs for CD and SGD, including simple Cauchy–Schwarz updates.
- Experiments demonstrate notable gains for CD and modest gains for SGD, consistent with theory.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Per-iteration complexity. Updating bounds for all coordinates in O(n) and recomputing p̂ in O(n log n) per iteration is heavy. Many large-scale CD applications rely on O(1) or sublinear overhead per step, exploiting sparsity. The paper argues the overhead is acceptable when d = Ω(n), but this is not the common regime for large sparse problems with very large n (number of coordinates). The experiments use moderate-sized or subsampled datasets; scalability to n ≫ 10^5 is not convincingly demonstrated.
- Practical maintenance of safe bounds. For general h, the argument relies on second-derivative terms evaluated at an unknown x̃; while the paper suggests safe bounding (e.g., logistic hi'' ≤ 1/4), concrete procedures and tightness for common losses are under-specified in the main text. For SGD, the update in Lemma 4.1 is correct but the proposed bounds may be overly loose, which can degrade adaptivity while still incurring O(n) update cost.
- The “always better than fixed sampling” guarantee is given at the level of the per-step quadratic upper bound (V/‖c‖^2), not necessarily translating into uniformly better convergence in f for all problems. Empirically the results are positive, but a clearer statement of how the min–max guarantee translates to global rates (e.g., for strongly convex f) would strengthen the theoretical story.
- Min–max interchange. The main theorem relies on exchanging min and max; the paper cites Sion/Komiya but defers details. Since V(p, c)/‖c‖^2 is not jointly convex-concave, the exact conditions under which the interchange is valid should be carefully spelled out in the main paper.
- Clarity and presentation. There are several typos and minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., definitions of V(p, x), missing constants and footnote markers, inconsistent referencing). Some proofs central to the claims (e.g., optimal α, min–max interchange) are deferred; brief sketches in the main text would help.
- Comparisons. It would be valuable to include comparisons against gap-based adaptive CD methods (e.g., Perekrestenko et al., Csiba et al.) and Gauss–Southwell variants beyond a theoretical remark, particularly on wall-clock time. For SGD, comparisons to practical gradient-importance heuristics with stale updates or mini-batch importance would be informative.

Quality:
The theoretical development appears sound given the stated assumptions; the water-filling characterization is elegant and the safety guarantee is clear. However, the computational overhead and practical maintenance of tight bounds are potential bottlenecks. Experimental evidence supports benefits for CD but is less compelling for SGD.

Clarity:
Generally well organized with helpful intuition (geometric view), but the manuscript would benefit from cleaning up notation, moving key assumptions and conditions (e.g., for min–max) into the main text, and adding more implementation details for bound maintenance.

Originality:
The robust optimization view of adaptive sampling under uncertainty and the O(n log n) algorithm to compute the worst-case-optimal distribution appear novel relative to prior adaptive schemes based on stale gradients, upper bounds, or duality gaps.

Significance:
If the overhead can be controlled (e.g., via incremental updates or amortization), the approach could influence practical adaptive sampling in CD. The rigorous “safe” guarantee is conceptually appealing. Impact for SGD seems limited based on the current experiments.

Questions and suggestions for authors:
- Can you derive amortized O(log n) or better updates for p̂ when only one coordinate is updated and bounds change marginally? Any data structures to avoid full resorting every iteration?
- In high-dimensional sparse settings, can you maintain bounds only for an active subset with lazy updates, and still retain safety?
- Please state explicitly the conditions under which min–max interchange holds for (7), and provide a short proof sketch in the main text.
- Provide global convergence rates for strongly convex f using α̂k, and clarify how the safety guarantee propagates to overall complexity bounds relative to pL.
- Expand details for safe hessian bounds for logistic/squared hinge (tight constants), and discuss how bound tightness affects the competitive ratio and empirical performance.
- Add wall-clock comparisons on larger n (e.g., full news20 features), and include comparisons to gap-based and Gauss–Southwell CD.

Pros:
- Novel robust formulation; provable safety over fixed sampling.
- Efficient algorithm with clear structure.
- Solid theoretical motivation; strong CD empirical gains.

Cons:
- Per-iteration overhead may be prohibitive for very large n.
- Bound maintenance and min–max conditions under-specified in main text.
- Limited SGD benefit and limited comparisons to strongest adaptive baselines.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The conceptual contribution and guarantees are interesting and relevant; addressing the practicality and clarity concerns would strengthen the paper. Confidence: high in technical area, medium regarding empirical scalability.
