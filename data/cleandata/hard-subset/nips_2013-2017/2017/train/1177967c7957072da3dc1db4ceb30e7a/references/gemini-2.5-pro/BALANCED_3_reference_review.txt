### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) methods. The authors first motivate the use of adaptive, gradient-based sampling by showing its potential for significant theoretical speed-ups over static sampling schemes (e.g., uniform or Lipschitz-based). They acknowledge the primary drawback of such optimal sampling: its computational infeasibility, as it requires knowledge of the full gradient at each iteration.

To bridge this gap, the authors propose a practical approximation. Their key idea is to operate with safe lower and upper bounds (`l`, `u`) on the gradient magnitudes, which can be maintained efficiently for certain problem classes. Instead of heuristically using these bounds (e.g., sampling based on the upper bounds `u`, which may perform worse than uniform sampling), the authors introduce a principled, "safe" approach. They formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem: find the distribution `p` that minimizes the worst-case expected progress, where the worst case is taken over all possible gradient vectors consistent with the bounds `l` and `u`.

The main contributions are:
1.  A theoretical analysis of optimal gradient-based sampling for a variant of CD with adaptive stepsizes, showing a potential speed-up factor of `n`.
2.  The formulation of the "safe" sampling problem as a well-defined optimization problem (7) and a proof that its solution is guaranteed to be no worse than standard fixed importance sampling.
3.  An efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute this safe sampling distribution at each iteration.
4.  A demonstration of how to efficiently maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).
5.  Comprehensive experiments on several real-world datasets, showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms static sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time, and performs close to the infeasible optimal sampling.

This work builds on a line of research on adaptive sampling for stochastic optimization [22, 5, 23, 36], but distinguishes itself through its robust minimax formulation for handling partial gradient information, which provides a theoretical safety guarantee that was absent in prior approximation schemes.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—formulating the choice of an approximate sampling distribution as a minimax problem to guarantee robustness—is highly novel and elegant. It provides a principled solution to a critical problem in adaptive sampling: how to leverage partial information without risking performance degradation. The resulting guarantee that the method is provably better than or equal to fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2 (ii)) is a very strong and significant result. This "safety net" makes the adaptive strategy much more attractive for practical use.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The derivation of the optimal sampling for CD, the formulation of the safe sampling problem, and the characterization of its solution (Theorem 3.4) are clear and well-supported by proofs in the appendix. The development of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the inner problem is crucial for the method's practicality and is a solid algorithmic contribution in its own right.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of first presenting the ideal, full-information setting (Section 2) to motivate the problem, and then introducing their practical, limited-information solution (Section 3). The connection between the theory and the empirical results is reinforced by plotting the evolution of the step-size parameter `vk`, which provides a clear visualization of the method's advantage over static sampling.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough, covering both CD and SGD across multiple standard datasets and popular models (Lasso, Ridge, Logistic Regression, SVM). The results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed safe sampling method is effective, outperforming uniform sampling in both iteration complexity and wall-clock time, indicating that the computational overhead is well-justified by the accelerated convergence.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1.  **Scope of Applicability and Generality:** The paper claims the proposed scheme is "completely generic," but the practical method for obtaining and maintaining the safe gradient bounds in Section 4 is specific to GLMs. For the method to have a broader impact, particularly in the context of SGD for deep learning, a discussion of how such bounds could be derived for more complex, non-linear models is needed. While this is a challenging research question in itself, acknowledging this limitation and suggesting potential avenues (e.g., using local smoothness constants, bounds from activation functions, or leveraging stale gradients to form confidence intervals) would make the paper more complete and forward-looking.
2.  **Experimental Comparisons to Other Heuristics:** The primary baselines in the experiments are uniform sampling (a static method) and optimal sampling (an infeasible oracle). A more direct validation of the proposed minimax formulation would involve comparing it against other practical *approximations* of adaptive sampling. For instance, what is the performance of a simpler heuristic that samples proportionally to the upper bounds `u_k`, as is done in some prior work [36, 23]? Example 3.1 provides a toy counterexample, but an empirical comparison on the main experimental tasks would more powerfully demonstrate the practical value of the "safe" approach over simpler, potentially unsafe heuristics.
3.  **Discussion of Computational Overhead:** The O(n log n) overhead per iteration is analyzed, and the authors rightly argue it can be negligible when the cost of a coordinate update, O(d), is high (i.e., `d = Ω(log n)`). However, in many large-scale machine learning scenarios, one might have `n` (number of samples/coordinates) being much larger than `d` (features). In such cases, the O(n log n) cost of sorting could dominate the O(d) cost of the SGD/CD update. A more nuanced discussion of the parameter regimes (`n` vs. `d`) where the method is most beneficial would be helpful for practitioners. The timing plots are convincing for the tested datasets, but this broader discussion would be valuable.
4.  **Less Pronounced Gains for SGD:** The authors honestly report that the performance gains for SGD are less significant than for CD. This is an interesting finding. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion speculating on the potential reasons. Could it be that the inherent variance from sampling data points `f_i` in SGD is the primary bottleneck, making the choice of sampling distribution a second-order effect compared to CD, where there is no such sampling variance?

### Decision

This paper presents a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practically effective method for adaptive importance sampling. The core idea of using a minimax formulation to ensure safety is a significant contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The paper is technically strong, clearly written, and supported by convincing experiments. The identified weaknesses are minor and relate more to the scope of the discussion and experimental comparisons rather than fundamental flaws in the method itself. This is a high-quality contribution that will likely be of great interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and principled minimax formulation for robust adaptive sampling.
*   Strong theoretical guarantee: provably no worse than fixed importance sampling.
*   Efficient algorithm for implementation.
*   Comprehensive and convincing empirical results on relevant problems.
*   High clarity and quality of writing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Limited discussion on applicability beyond GLMs.
*   Lack of empirical comparison to other adaptive sampling heuristics.

The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. I recommend accepting this paper.
