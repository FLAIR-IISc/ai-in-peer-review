### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the challenge of designing efficient adaptive importance sampling strategies for large-scale optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). While adaptive sampling based on full gradient information is known to be theoretically superior to static (e.g., uniform or Lipschitz-based) sampling, its practical application is often infeasible due to the prohibitive cost of computing the full gradient at each iteration. Existing approximations often lack theoretical guarantees and can perform worse than static sampling.

The central contribution of this work is a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme. The key idea is to operate with efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient component magnitudes (for CD) or norms (for SGD). Instead of using a heuristic approximation based on these bounds, the authors propose a principled approach: they formulate the problem of choosing the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. Specifically, they seek a distribution that minimizes the worst-case expected progress, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradient vectors consistent with the known bounds. The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem, yielding both a "safe" sampling distribution and an adaptive step size. A key theoretical result is that this proposed sampling strategy is provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling, thus providing a "safety" guarantee that was missing in prior work. The effectiveness of the method is demonstrated on generalized linear models, where the required gradient bounds can be updated efficiently, and confirmed through extensive experiments on real-world datasets.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The core idea of framing the selection of a sampling distribution as a minimax problem (Equation 7) is highly original and theoretically sound. It provides a robust way to handle the uncertainty inherent in having only partial gradient information (i.e., the bounds `l` and `u`). This is a significant conceptual advance over previous ad-hoc approaches that rely on stale information or only use upper bounds, which can lead to poor performance.

2.  **Strong "Safety" Guarantee:** A major strength of this work is the proof that the proposed sampling scheme is always at least as good as the standard fixed (Li-based) importance sampling in terms of the one-step expected progress (Theorem 3.2). This "safety net" is a crucial property for any practical adaptive method, as it gives practitioners confidence that the method will not underperform relative to a well-understood baseline. This addresses a key drawback of many existing adaptive heuristics.

3.  **Efficient and Practical Algorithm:** The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the solution to their minimax problem. They correctly argue that for many problems where the data dimension `d` is large, the overhead of this computation is negligible compared to the cost of a single coordinate update. The experimental results on wall-clock time confirm this, showing that the method is not just better in terms of iteration complexity but also faster in practice.

4.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is very well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the theoretical development is rigorous, and the connections between the theory, the proposed algorithm, and the experiments are strong. The work is self-contained, covering the theoretical justification for ideal adaptive sampling, the development of the safe approximation, an efficient implementation, and a thorough empirical validation.

### **Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions**

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The performance of the proposed method hinges on the quality of the safe lower and upper bounds `l` and `u`. The method presented in Section 4 for Generalized Linear Models relies on a simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequality to bound inner products. While empirically effective, this can produce very loose bounds, particularly if feature vectors are high-dimensional and sparse, or nearly orthogonal. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the tightness of these bounds. For instance, does the sampling distribution gracefully degenerate to Li-based sampling as the bounds become uninformative (e.g., `l` -> 0, `u` -> infinity)? This seems likely from the theory but would be worth stating explicitly.

2.  **Limited Experimental Baselines:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over fixed sampling and its proximity to the performance of the computationally expensive "optimal" sampling. However, to fully appreciate the contribution of the "safe" minimax formulation, it would be beneficial to compare against other practical *approximate* adaptive methods. For example, a comparison against a baseline that samples naively based on the upper bounds `u`, or one that uses stale gradient information, would directly highlight the practical advantage of the more sophisticated and robust approach proposed here.

3.  **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The theoretical analysis of the competitive ratio in Section 3.2 is an interesting addition, but its practical implications are not fully clear. The bound in Lemma 3.5 depends on `wk`, which appears difficult to compute, and the condition in Lemma 3.6 seems quite strong. It would be helpful if the authors could provide more intuition on when this condition might be met in practice. Alternatively, an empirical evaluation of the competitive ratio `ρk` during the experiments would be a valuable addition, connecting this piece of theory more directly to the observed performance.

### **Decision**

I recommend **acceptance** for this paper.

The paper presents a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practical solution to a significant problem in large-scale machine learning optimization. The minimax formulation for deriving a safe adaptive sampling distribution is a genuinely new and elegant idea that provides a robust alternative to existing heuristics. The combination of strong theoretical guarantees (particularly the "safety" property), an efficient algorithm, and compelling empirical results makes this a high-quality contribution. While there are minor areas for improvement, as suggested above, the strengths of the paper are substantial and it is likely to have a significant impact on the field.
