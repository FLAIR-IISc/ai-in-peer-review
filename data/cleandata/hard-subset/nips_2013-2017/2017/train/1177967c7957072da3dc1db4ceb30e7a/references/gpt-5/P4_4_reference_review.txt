Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It first shows that, under a standard quadratic upper-bound model with coordinate-wise Lipschitz constants Li, choosing per-iteration sampling probabilities proportional to √Li|∇if(xk)| (with an appropriate directionally-unbiased stepsize γk = αk/pik) optimizes the expected one-step decrease. This can yield up to an n-fold speedup over uniform or Li-based fixed sampling, recovering steepest coordinate descent when Li are equal.

The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive sampling scheme when full gradient information is not available. The method assumes per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds `k, uk on either |∇if(xk)| (CD) or ‖∇fi(xk)‖ (SGD). It then solves a convex min–max problem to choose probabilities p̂k (and stepsize α̂k) that minimize the worst-case upper bound on expected suboptimality decrease over all c ∈ Ck = {c : `k ≤ c ≤ uk}. The resulting distribution: (i) is provably optimal for the given uncertainty set (best worst-case guarantee), (ii) is never worse (in the model’s expected progress) than fixed Li-based sampling, and (iii) converges to the full-information gradient-based sampling when bounds become exact. An O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) computes p̂k by a thresholding/clamping procedure characterized by a closed-form KKT structure.

For GLMs, the paper shows how to maintain safe bounds on gradient coordinates/norms with O(n) per-iteration updates using simple inner-product bounds (e.g., Cauchy–Schwarz), providing concrete applicability. Experiments on rcv1, real-sim, and news20 indicate strong improvements for CD in both iterations and wall-clock time; for SGD, gains are smaller, consistent with prior observations that gradient-based sampling often helps less in SGD.

The work connects to and extends prior adaptive sampling strategies: gradient-based sampling for SGD [e.g., Zhao & Zhang], gap-based/adaptive CD [Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.], and steepest-coordinate/GS rules. Unlike prior approaches relying on stale or approximate full gradients, this paper designs a principled min–max formulation with a safety guarantee relative to fixed sampling. The min–max interchange relies on standard minimax results (Sion/Komiya).

Strengths
- Clear theoretical formulation: derives the optimal full-information sampler for CD (proportional to √Li|∇i|) within the quadratic model and introduces a convex min–max formulation to handle uncertainty in gradient magnitudes.
- Safety guarantee: p̂k is never worse (in the model) than Li-based fixed sampling; best worst-case among distributions consistent with the bounds.
- Efficient algorithm: O(n log n) method to compute p̂k with a simple thresholding structure; competitive-ratio analysis provides additional insight.
- Practicality: concrete recipes to maintain safe bounds for GLMs with O(n) updates per iteration.
- Empirical validation: demonstrates substantial CD speedups and shows vk trajectories corroborating theoretical improvements; reports both iteration and time metrics.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Cost model and scalability: The per-iteration overhead O(n) (to update bounds) + O(n log n) (to compute p̂k) can be significant when n is large and the cost to compute one coordinate/component gradient is small (e.g., d ≪ n). The paper argues this overhead is amortized when d = Ω(n), but experiments and discussion on regimes where d ≪ n would clarify practicality. Suggest including experiments with very large n and small d, and/or strategies to amortize updates (e.g., partial/batched refresh of p̂k).
- Tightness of bounds: The safe bounds via Cauchy–Schwarz can be loose, potentially limiting gains. Empirical results are still positive, but a sensitivity study quantifying performance vs. bound tightness would strengthen the case. Including variants with tighter screening/curvature-based bounds would be informative.
- SGD impact: Improvements for SGD are small in the reported settings. A discussion clarifying when the method is expected to help SGD (e.g., high gradient-norm heterogeneity, ill-conditioned settings, or with curvature information) would help set expectations; adding comparisons to variance-reduction or mini-batch importance sampling baselines would be useful.
- Assumptions and clarity: The “directionally unbiased” stepsize scheme γk = αk/pik is central; a brief stability discussion and explicit convergence statements in the main text (beyond appendices) would improve clarity. Notational overload of L (scalar vs. matrix) could be streamlined. In Section 4, clarify that for f(x)=h(Ax)+∑ψi(xi) with h(z)=1/2||z−b||^2, ∇^2h(z)=I in R^d and the implied change in ∇f follows via chain rule (A^T A factor), as done in Eq. (11).
- Related work positioning: A more explicit comparison to gap-based/adaptive CD (e.g., [5, 23]) and approximate steepest CD ([32])—both theoretically and empirically—would better situate the novelty. Also, cite and contrast to importance sampling for mini-batches and adaptive sampling with stale gradients more thoroughly.

Quality
- Theoretical developments are technically sound within the quadratic upper-bound model and coordinate-wise smoothness. The min–max optimality and safety bounds are well-motivated; Algorithm 4 appears correct with a clear complexity bound. Convergence arguments are standard and deferred to the appendix; providing a concise theorem statement in the main text would help. The empirical methodology is reasonable and supports claims for CD.

Clarity
- Generally well-written with clear notation and algorithms. Some notational choices (reuse of L) and minor leaps (Section 4 derivations) could be clarified. Providing a brief pseudocode for the SGD variant would help completeness.

Originality
- The safe min–max formulation for choosing adaptive sampling under partial information, with an efficient algorithm and a guarantee that it is never worse than fixed sampling, appears novel relative to prior adaptive/stale/upper-bound approaches.

Significance
- For CD, the approach is practically relevant and can materially reduce runtime, with theoretical guarantees. For SGD, benefits seem situational; nevertheless, the framework is general and could inspire further advances in safe adaptive sampling under uncertainty.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled min–max formulation with safety guarantees and efficient algorithm.
- Strong theoretical justification for gradient-based sampling in CD and clear improvement guarantees over fixed sampling.
- Practical gradient-bound maintenance for GLMs and convincing CD experiments with time and iteration gains.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overhead may be non-negligible in regimes with large n and small per-coordinate cost; limited empirical evaluation of such regimes.
- Modest gains for SGD and limited comparison to alternative adaptive baselines in SGD.
- Some clarity/positioning issues and reliance on potentially loose bounds.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
