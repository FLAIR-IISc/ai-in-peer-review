Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles adaptive importance sampling for randomized coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). With full information, the authors recall/derive that gradient-based probabilities proportional to √Li|∇if(x)| (CD) or to ‖∇fi(x)‖2 (SGD) maximize a standard expected progress model and can be up to n times faster than uniform/fixed importance sampling. The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive sampling rule that does not require full gradient information: at each iteration, given coordinate-wise safe lower/upper bounds on the relevant gradient magnitudes (|∇if(x)| for CD; ‖∇fi(x)‖2 for SGD), the sampling distribution pk is chosen as the solution of a minimax problem minp∈Δ maxc∈Ck V(p,c)/‖c‖2, where V(p,c)=∑i Li c2i/pi and Ck is the interval box defined by the bounds. The method also prescribes an adaptive stepsize αk=1/vk, where vk is the optimal value of that minimax. The solution has attractive properties: it is provably never worse (under the model) than any fixed importance sampling based on Li; it coincides with the full-information optimal distribution when bounds collapse to the true gradients; and it admits an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) per iteration via a sorting-based procedure. The paper shows how to maintain safe gradient bounds cheaply for generalized linear models (GLMs), and presents experiments on several text datasets for CD and SGD.

The work relates to non-uniform/importance sampling for randomized CD and SGD (e.g., Nesterov; Richtárik/Takáč; Qu et al.; Zhao & Zhang), adaptive sampling based on gradients or duality gaps (e.g., Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.; gap-safe rules), and Gauss-Southwell-type heuristics. The novelty here is a principled, worst-case optimal sampling under partial information with a guarantee that it is never worse than fixed Li-based sampling with respect to the modeling inequality.

Quality
- Theory: The derivations are sound under the standard smooth convex model (coordinate-wise Lipschitz). The minimax problem is well-posed, and the paper outlines the use of minimax theorems to swap min/max. The constructive solution (Theorem 3.4) and O(n log n) algorithm are convincing. The “always better than fixed Li-based sampling” property is clearly stated (Theorem 3.2).
- Scope/assumptions: Guarantees are model-based (via quadratic upper bounds) and for convex problems. The competitive ratio analysis provides some insight but the bound in Lemma 3.6 relies on a somewhat artificial relative accuracy condition.
- Empirics: On CD for GLMs, the proposed method handily outperforms fixed sampling in iterations and time, and tracks the full-information optimal sampling closely. For SGD, improvements are mild; the authors acknowledge both the smaller benefit and the overhead.
- Completeness: Convergence rates for the ideal adaptive CD algorithm are only sketched and deferred; adding a concise theorem in the main text would strengthen the narrative. Comparisons do not include other adaptive-probability methods under partial information (e.g., gap-based CD, approximate Gauss-Southwell, Csiba et al. for SDCA/SAG), which would better position the contribution.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The high-level idea, the optimization formulation, and the properties are clear. A few suggestions:
- Clarify notation early: L denotes both a scalar smoothness and the diagonal matrix diag(Li); consistently use bold/roman to avoid confusion.
- Provide a more explicit pseudo-code for Algorithm 4 (mapping between sorted arrays and original indices, updates of m), and a brief intuitive derivation of condition (9) in the main text.
- In Section 4, be explicit about assumptions needed to bound 〈ai,∇2h(Ax̃)aik〉 and how to obtain practical bounds without expensive Hessian access.
- Fix minor typos (e.g., “preformance”, “inadmissible”; “l.- and u.-bounds”).

Originality
Framing adaptive sampling under partial information as a minimax problem and deriving an efficient closed-form-like solution appears novel. Prior works either use full gradients, stale values, or heuristic/upper-bound-based approximations without a guarantee of being never worse than fixed sampling. The safe guarantee and the O(n log n) procedure are the key original elements. The step-size coupling αk=1/vk is also a useful addition.

Significance
For CD in convex GLMs and similar problems where gradient bounds can be cheaply maintained, the method is practical and yields real gains, with a guarantee that is attractive to practitioners. The SGD case is less compelling due to O(n) bound-updates per iteration, which undermines the usual appeal of SGD; nonetheless, the framework could be useful when bounds are maintained asynchronously or in distributed settings, as the authors note. The ideas may also inspire extensions to block CD, minibatching, or variance-reduced methods.

Pros
- Principled minimax formulation yielding a “safe” adaptive sampling rule strictly dominating fixed sampling under the model.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm with a simple geometric interpretation and explicit construction.
- Practical integration into CD for GLMs via simple bound maintenance; strong empirical improvements.
- Recovers full-information optimal sampling in the limit of tight bounds; clear step-size rule.

Cons
- Overhead: O(n log n) sampling plus O(n) bound updates per iteration can be non-negligible; for SGD it largely negates the benefit.
- Empirical comparisons are limited: no head-to-head with other adaptive strategies under limited information (gap-based, approximate Gauss-Southwell).
- Competitive ratio bounds are somewhat weak or rely on restrictive assumptions; no empirical study of bound tightness vs. performance.
- Main-text lacks a crisp convergence rate theorem for the proposed CD algorithm.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a concise convergence theorem (rate) for CD with the proposed sampling/step-size in the main text.
- Provide wall-clock breakdowns showing the overhead of bound updates and sampling vs. gradient computation; discuss strategies to amortize costs (e.g., refreshing bounds every T iterations).
- Compare against additional adaptive baselines (e.g., gap-based sampling, Gauss-Southwell approximations, Csiba et al.’s adaptive SDCA) and include steepest coordinate descent where feasible.
- Discuss extensions to block CD/minibatch selection and to variance-reduced methods; clarify applicability beyond GLMs.
- Release code and document implementation details for reproducibility.

Recommendation: Accept (borderline-leaning positive), primarily for the solid theoretical contribution and strong CD performance; the SGD part is less compelling but does not detract from the main value.

Confidence: High on the technical assessment of the convex/CD setting; medium on breadth of empirical comparisons.
