Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses adaptive importance sampling for stochastic optimization, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The ideal “full-information” strategy samples coordinates (or data points) proportionally to gradient magnitudes, which maximizes the expected one-step decrease under a quadratic upper model. However, computing full gradients each iteration is often intractable. The authors propose a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme that uses per-coordinate lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes to define a min–max optimization (over sampling distributions and all gradients consistent with the bounds). They show: (i) a closed-form characterization of the saddle-point solution via a thresholding condition, (ii) an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting-based) to compute the distribution p̂ and its associated step size α̂ = 1/v, (iii) a guarantee that p̂ is never worse, in a precise sense, than fixed importance sampling (Li-based), and (iv) when full information is available, the method recovers the optimal gradient-based sampling. They also discuss how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs), and provide empirical results on several datasets, demonstrating strong gains for CD and modest gains for SGD.

The work builds on and connects to: importance sampling for CD and SGD with fixed distributions [Nesterov; Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.], adaptive gradient-based sampling for SGD [Zhao & Zhang; Zhu], duality-gap based/adaptive CD [Perekrestenko et al.; Csiba et al.], and safe screening [Liu et al.; Ndiaye et al.]. The novelty is a principled min–max formulation that yields a sampling rule with a worst-case guarantee relative to any fixed distribution, computable from bounds rather than full gradients.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and formulation of the gap between optimal adaptive sampling and computational feasibility; the proposed min–max formulation is elegant and principled.
- Solid one-step progress analysis for CD with directionally unbiased step γk = αk/pik, leading to an interpretable criterion V(p, c) and optimal p ∝ |√L c|.
- The safe distribution p̂ is provably best in the worst case over the uncertainty set, and is guaranteed to outperform Li-based (fixed) sampling; it recovers the full-information sampling when bounds are tight.
- Algorithmic contribution: an O(n log n) procedure to compute p̂ via sorting and a thresholding condition; geometric interpretation is insightful.
- Practicality: concrete, easy-to-compute safe bounds for GLMs via Cauchy–Schwarz; discussion for both CD and SGD.
- Empirical evidence on several datasets shows substantial speedups for CD, bringing performance close to the ideal full-information method, and demonstrates the importance of adaptive step sizes.

Weaknesses and questions
- Computational overhead: Computing p̂ every iteration in O(n log n) and updating bounds in O(n) may be prohibitive when n is large and d ≪ n (common in high-dimensional sparse problems). The paper argues costs are negligible when d = Ω(n), but this assumption is not always met (e.g., news20). It would be valuable to report how frequently p̂ and the bounds are recomputed in practice, and provide ablations (e.g., update every T steps) with timing on large n.
- Global convergence complexity: While one-step decrease guarantees and a sketch of convergence are given (appendix), the main text does not present end-to-end iteration complexity theorems for convex/strongly convex cases explicitly comparing rates to fixed sampling. Stating a concrete complexity bound for Algorithm 2 would strengthen the theoretical claims of “provably better.”
- Min–max interchange and optimality: The guarantee relies on interchanging min and max in (7) (referenced to the appendix using Sion/Komiya). Given g(p, c) = V(p, c)/||c||^2 is convex in p and not concave in c, the minimax justification is delicate. More intuition and a self-contained proof sketch in the main text would improve confidence.
- Empirics for SGD: Gains are modest, and the paper acknowledges this. Including comparisons to state-of-the-art adaptive/stale gradient-based samplings for SGD [Zhao & Zhang; Needell et al.] and to variance-reduction baselines would clarify when the proposed method is beneficial.
- Comparisons for CD: It would be informative to compare against gap-based sampling [Perekrestenko et al.] and Gauss–Southwell rules (or their efficient approximations), since these are strong adaptive CD baselines.
- Practical details: Timings in different figures are on different machines, limiting comparability. More standardized wall-clock evaluations and a breakdown of time spent on computing bounds vs sampling vs updates would help. Sensitivity to λ and to bound tightness (e.g., alternatives to Cauchy–Schwarz) would be useful.
- Clarity/notation: Some typos and equation references (e.g., “2.4”) and minor inconsistencies (sign/absolute values) hinder readability. The role of the “directionally unbiased” step γk = αk/pik and its relation to standard CD stepsizes could be explained more clearly.

Quality
The theoretical development is thoughtful and mostly sound, with an appealing min–max perspective, closed-form characterization, and correctness guarantees. The most delicate points are the minimax interchange and the computational feasibility in very high-dimensional settings. Experiments are supportive for CD but limited in breadth and baseline coverage.

Clarity
Overall well organized, with a helpful figure contrasting ideal, safe, and fixed sampling. Some proofs relegated to the appendix should be summarized in the main text. Clarify computational cost assumptions, frequency of updates, and provide more implementation detail for bound maintenance.

Originality
The safe min–max formulation over gradient bounds and its efficient solver appear novel, as is the guarantee of never being worse than fixed importance sampling under uncertainty. The connection to adaptive stepsizes α̂ = 1/v is also neat.

Significance
For practitioners using CD on GLMs and related problems, the method offers a principled way to exploit partial gradient information with guarantees and practical gains. Its impact on SGD seems more limited based on current experiments. If computational overhead concerns are addressed (e.g., amortized updates), the approach could see adoption.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide explicit convergence-rate theorems for Algorithm 2 in convex/strongly convex settings, comparing to fixed sampling rates.
- Include stronger baselines (gap-based, Gauss–Southwell, stale-gradient sampling) and standardized wall-clock comparisons.
- Discuss and evaluate amortized or periodic updates of p̂; consider data structures to maintain the thresholding solution incrementally to reduce per-iteration cost below O(n log n).
- Expand the discussion and main-text sketch of the minimax interchange and KKT-based derivation of the thresholding solution.
- Add sensitivity analyses to bound tightness and to λ; explore tighter bounds beyond Cauchy–Schwarz.

Pros
- Principled, novel min–max formulation with safe, data-adaptive sampling.
- Closed-form characterization and efficient algorithm.
- Strong CD results; guarantees strictly dominate fixed importance sampling.

Cons
- Potentially high per-iteration overhead for large n; limited discussion of amortization.
- Limited SGD gains and baseline comparisons.
- Convergence-rate statements for the full algorithm not prominent in the main text.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The safe adaptive sampling idea is novel and well motivated, with solid theory and promising CD experiments. Strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying computational practicality would further improve the paper.
