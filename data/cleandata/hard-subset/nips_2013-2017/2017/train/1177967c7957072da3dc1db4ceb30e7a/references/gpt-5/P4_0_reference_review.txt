Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). Starting from the standard expected one-step decrease bound under (coordinate-wise) smoothness, the authors show that a gradient-based sampling distribution p*(x) ∝ √L ∘ |∇f(x)| (for CD) maximizes this bound and can in special cases yield up to an n-fold speed-up over fixed distributions such as Li-based/Uniform sampling. Since computing p*(x) requires full gradient information, the core contribution is a “safe” adaptive scheme that uses easily-maintained per-coordinate lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (or component gradient norms in SGD) to choose a sampling distribution p̂ and stepsize α̂ by solving a min-max problem: min_p max_c∈C V(p,c)/||c||^2 with C defined by the bounds and V(p,c)=∑ Li c_i^2/p_i. The authors prove:
- p̂ achieves the best worst-case guarantee among all p given the uncertainty set C (Theorem 3.2(i));
- p̂ is never worse than fixed Li-based sampling (Theorem 3.2(ii));
- a geometric characterization and an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute p̂ exactly via sorting and thresholding (Theorem 3.4);
- competitive ratio bounds vs. the hindsight-optimal sampling that knows the true c (Lemmas 3.5–3.6).

For GLMs with separable regularizers, they derive simple safe bounds using the evolution of gradients across iterations and Cauchy–Schwarz bounds on feature inner products, updateable in O(n) per iteration. Experiments on text datasets show substantial gains for CD (close to full-information gradient sampling), and smaller gains for SGD, consistent with prior observations. The work relates to non-uniform sampling for CD/SGD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.; Nesterov; Needell et al.), adaptive sampling via duality gaps or violations (Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.), and gradient-based sampling for SGD (Zhao & Zhang; Zhu). The novelty here is a safe, optimization-based sampling rule with a provable “never worse than fixed sampling” guarantee under limited information and an efficient exact solver.

Quality:
- Technical correctness: The one-step progress analysis for CD with αk depending on pk is standard. The identification of p*(x) ∝ √L ∘ |∇f(x)| minimizing V(p,∇f)/||∇f||^2 is correct. The safe scheme is formulated as a convex-concave min-max and the “never worse than Li-based” guarantee follows by feasibility. The ability to interchange min and max is nontrivial; the paper states a proof via minimax theorems in the appendix—this is a key technical point and should be made explicit in the main text with precise conditions (compactness, convexity/concavity). The O(n log n) algorithm based on KKT conditions and sorting appears sound and is an attractive contribution.
- Assumptions and practicality: The safe bounds for GLMs are valid but can be loose (Cauchy–Schwarz), which the authors acknowledge; the competitive ratio bounds reflect this via a relative-accuracy condition (γ). The per-iteration overhead is O(n) to update bounds plus O(n log n) to compute p̂, which can dominate when n is large and coordinate updates are cheap (e.g., sparse problems with small d per coordinate). The paper argues this overhead is acceptable when computing one coordinate gradient is Θ(d) with d=Ω(n), but many ML settings have n≫d (e.g., high-dimensional sparse text), making the stated per-iteration overhead a potential bottleneck. This trade-off deserves deeper analysis and/or amortization strategies.
- Empirical evaluation: CD results are convincing and include both iteration and time metrics; SGD improvements are small, which is fairly reported. Comparisons are primarily against uniform and the infeasible full-information oracle. It would strengthen the case to compare against other adaptive non-uniform baselines that use stale/upper-bound gradients or dual-gap based probabilities (e.g., Csiba–Qu–Richtárik; Perekrestenko et al.) to situate gains under similar overhead constraints.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, with clear motivation, algorithms, and theoretical statements. Some presentation issues should be addressed:
- Notational typos (e.g., “Lei = Li”, “vector l = (L1, …, n)>”) and minor inconsistencies in equations (e.g., factors in Lemma 2.1/eq. (4)) should be corrected.
- The precise conditions for the minimax swap should be stated in the main text, not only deferred to the appendix.
- Algorithm 4 pseudocode overloads symbols (` and u as indices vs. vectors) and would benefit from clearer variable names and a brief proof sketch.
- The computational complexity discussion should delineate regimes where O(n log n) is negligible vs. dominant, and discuss options for periodic updates or maintaining p̂ incrementally.

Originality:
The idea of using a min-max optimization over a bounded gradient uncertainty set to derive a sampling distribution that is provably safe relative to fixed sampling appears novel, as is the efficient exact algorithm to compute it. Prior works use stale or upper-bound-based approximations without such a worst-case guarantee. The step-size coupling α̂=1/vk and the geometric characterization add insight. For SGD, gradient-based sampling is known; the safe variant under uncertainty is new to my knowledge.

Significance:
For CD, adaptive sampling can yield substantial gains; providing a principled, efficient, and safe approximation that nearly matches the oracle is valuable. The worst-case guarantee “never worse than Li-based” is appealing in practice. The main caveat is computational overhead scaling with n: in many high-dimensional problems, O(n) or O(n log n) per iteration is substantial. If the approach can be amortized (e.g., update p̂ every T steps, incremental maintenance), the practical impact would be higher. The theoretical results are of interest to the optimization community; broader adoption will depend on addressing overhead and comparisons to alternative adaptive baselines.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation of safe adaptive importance sampling under limited information with strong worst-case guarantees.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm with clean geometric interpretation.
- Demonstrated empirical gains for CD close to oracle sampling; careful reporting for SGD.
- Bridges adaptive sampling literature with robust optimization ideas; likely to inspire follow-up work on safe adaptive strategies and better bound maintenance.

Cons for acceptance:
- Per-iteration overhead O(n)+O(n log n) can be prohibitive when n is large and coordinate computations are cheap; more extensive discussion/experiments on scalability and amortization are needed.
- Limited empirical comparisons to other adaptive non-uniform methods beyond uniform and oracle.
- Some theoretical details (minimax interchange conditions, global convergence rates under strong convexity with the adaptive rule) are deferred; explicit end-to-end complexity improvements over fixed sampling are not fully quantified.
- Presentation issues and incomplete references should be addressed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide explicit conditions and a concise proof sketch for the minimax interchange; state convexity/compactness assumptions.
- Discuss and experiment with periodic/incremental updates of p̂ (e.g., recompute every T iterations), measuring the trade-off between overhead and performance on truly large n datasets without subsampling.
- Add comparisons to gap/violation-based adaptive sampling and stale-gradient heuristics with matched computational budgets.
- Quantify global convergence rate improvements (e.g., under strong convexity) using vk trajectories, and clarify when the method recovers steepest CD behavior.
- Improve pseudocode clarity and fix typographical issues; complete and standardize references.
