

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper introduces a novel approach to adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The key insight is that existing adaptive sampling methods often fail to guarantee improvements over fixed sampling, and the authors propose a "safe" adaptive scheme that provably outperforms fixed importance sampling while maintaining computational efficiency.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes theoretical guarantees showing that gradient-based adaptive sampling can achieve speedups of up to factor n over fixed sampling. The core innovation is the "safe" adaptive importance sampling method, which uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to formulate an optimization problem for selecting the optimal sampling distribution. The authors prove this approach is always better than fixed importance sampling, and provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling distribution. The method is shown to be applicable to both CD and SGD, and the authors demonstrate its integration with generalized linear models.

The paper stands out for its bold theoretical approach to a fundamental problem in optimization. Previous work on adaptive importance sampling often relied on approximations that could perform worse than uniform sampling. The authors' key insight is to frame the sampling problem as a convex optimization problem with respect to safe gradient bounds, enabling provable guarantees. This approach represents a significant conceptual advance over prior work, which typically used heuristics or approximations without theoretical guarantees.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The theoretical contributions are rigorously proven, and the empirical evaluation on real datasets demonstrates practical improvements in optimization speed. The authors provide a thorough comparison of their method with fixed importance sampling and optimal (but computationally infeasible) adaptive sampling. The computational complexity analysis is sound, showing the overhead is manageable (O(n log n) per iteration, comparable to computing a single coordinate of the gradient).

Strengths:
- Novel theoretical framework for adaptive importance sampling with provable guarantees
- Clear conceptual advance over previous work that lacked safety guarantees
- Practical implementation with manageable computational overhead
- Strong empirical results demonstrating significant improvements in CD optimization
- Well-structured and clearly written paper with proper contextualization in the literature

Weaknesses:
- The empirical results for SGD are less impressive than for CD, with adaptive sampling providing only marginal improvements over uniform sampling
- The paper focuses primarily on generalized linear models, limiting the scope of empirical validation
- The method's performance on more complex models (beyond GLMs) is not explored
- Some details about the implementation of gradient bounds could be expanded

The paper's main contribution is its theoretical guarantee that the proposed sampling method will never be worse than fixed importance sampling, which previous approaches couldn't establish. This is a significant theoretical advance that addresses a fundamental limitation in adaptive sampling. The authors' approach is both elegant and practical, making it a valuable contribution to optimization theory and its application in machine learning.

The paper demonstrates significant significance for the field. Optimization is a fundamental component of machine learning, and the proposed method provides a theoretically grounded way to improve the efficiency of CD and SGD algorithms. The computational overhead is small enough to be practically useful, making this more than just a theoretical contribution. The method could be applied to a wide range of machine learning problems, particularly those involving large-scale optimization.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical contributions, I find this paper to be highly compelling. The authors have identified a key limitation in existing adaptive sampling approaches and provided a theoretically sound solution that addresses this limitation. The paper represents a significant conceptual advance rather than an incremental improvement on existing methods.

The minor weaknesses in the empirical results for SGD and limited scope of the experimental validation do not detract from the paper's overall contribution. The theoretical advances are substantial, and the practical implementation is sound. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations in their empirical evaluation.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to adaptive importance sampling that solves a fundamental problem in optimization. The method is both theoretically sound and practically implementable, with clear advantages over previous approaches. The contribution represents a significant advance in the field of optimization for machine learning, with potential to impact a wide range of applications.

I recommend acceptance. This paper's bold theoretical contribution, combined with its practical implementation and empirical validation, makes it a strong candidate for NIPS. The novelty and significance of the work outweigh the minor limitations noted above.
