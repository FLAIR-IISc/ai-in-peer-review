### Summary

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors first revisit the theory of optimal gradient-based adaptive sampling, highlighting its potential for significant acceleration (up to a factor of `n` for CD) over static sampling strategies like uniform or Lipschitz-based sampling. They acknowledge the primary drawback of such optimal sampling: its computational cost, which requires full gradient information in every iteration.

The core contribution of the paper is a novel method to approximate this optimal sampling distribution when only safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes (for CD) or norms (for SGD) are available. Instead of heuristically using these bounds, the authors formulate the problem of choosing a sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. The goal is to find a distribution that minimizes the worst-case value of a term in the convergence bound, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradient vectors consistent with the known bounds. This formulation leads to a "safe" sampling distribution that is provably at least as good as fixed importance sampling, a guarantee that previous approximation schemes lack. The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this minimax problem. Finally, they demonstrate how to derive and maintain the required gradient bounds efficiently for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide empirical results on several real-world datasets showing that their method outperforms static sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time.

This work builds upon a line of research on adaptive importance sampling for stochastic optimization methods [22, 5, 23, 36]. While prior work has also used gradient approximations or bounds to make adaptive sampling practical, this paper's primary novelty lies in the principled minimax formulation, which provides a theoretical guarantee of robustness and safety against the uncertainty in the gradient information.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound.
*   The theoretical analysis in Section 2, particularly for CD with a novel adaptive step size, is clear and well-motivated.
*   The minimax formulation for the safe sampling (Equation 7) is a principled and elegant approach to handling the uncertainty inherent in using gradient bounds.
*   The main theoretical result, Theorem 3.2, which establishes that the proposed sampling is provably better than fixed Li-based sampling, is a strong and important guarantee.
*   The derivation of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling distribution (Algorithm 4) demonstrates that the proposed approach is not just of theoretical interest but is also computationally practical.
*   The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and a comprehensive set of experiments on multiple datasets and models.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The motivation is established effectively in the introduction, clearly positioning the work in the context of existing literature and highlighting the gap it aims to fill.
*   The logical flow from the ideal, full-information setting (Section 2) to the practical, limited-information setting (Section 3) is easy to follow.
*   Section 4 provides a concrete and crucial example of how the required gradient bounds can be computed and maintained for an important class of problems (GLMs), which greatly enhances the paper's accessibility and demonstrates its practical relevance.
*   The experimental setup and results are presented clearly. The inclusion of plots for the step size parameter `v_k` provides valuable insight into the behavior of the algorithm.

**Originality:** The paper presents significant original contributions.
*   The central novel idea is the minimax formulation for deriving a sampling distribution from gradient bounds. This "safe" or "robust" approach to adaptive sampling is, to my knowledge, new and provides a stronger theoretical foundation than previous heuristics that use, for example, only the upper bounds [36, 23].
*   The development of an efficient O(n log n) solver for this specific minimax problem is a non-trivial algorithmic contribution.
*   The formal analysis of gradient-based sampling for CD with the proposed adaptive step size (Section 2.1) is also a valuable contribution that complements existing results for SGD.

**Significance:** The results presented are likely to be of interest and use to both researchers and practitioners in machine learning and optimization.
*   The proposed method offers a "drop-in" improvement for widely used CD and SGD optimizers, providing acceleration with a strong theoretical guarantee of not being worse than standard fixed sampling schemes.
*   The computational overhead of the method is modest (O(n log n)), making it practical for many problems where the cost of a single coordinate or component gradient evaluation is of a similar or higher order.
*   The framework is generic, meaning it can be applied to any problem where one can efficiently maintain safe bounds on gradient components, potentially inspiring further research into developing such bounding techniques for other model classes.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**
1.  **Clarity of Algorithm 4:** While the algorithm is presented, its mechanics are not very intuitive from the main text. A brief intuitive explanation of how the sorting and iterative fixing of coordinate values in `c` relate to the KKT conditions of the optimization problem would improve the clarity for readers who do not dive into the appendix.
2.  **Scope and Dependence on Bound Quality:** The practical performance of the method depends on the ability to maintain reasonably tight gradient bounds efficiently. The paper provides a good example for GLMs using a simple Cauchy-Schwarz based update. However, for problems with dense data or more complex structures, these bounds might be loose, potentially diminishing the gains over static sampling. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the tightness of the bounds `l` and `u` would be beneficial. Furthermore, the applicability to other important models, such as deep neural networks, is not obvious and could be mentioned as a limitation and direction for future work.
3.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The experiments convincingly show the advantage over static sampling and proximity to the impractical optimal sampling. However, a comparison with other practical adaptive sampling heuristics, such as those based on stale gradients [22] or simpler upper-bound-based approximations [36], would provide a more complete empirical picture and better situate the proposed method among its direct competitors.
4.  **SGD Performance:** The paper notes that the performance gains for SGD are less pronounced than for CD. This is an interesting observation that warrants more discussion. Is this phenomenon related to the specific bounds used, the nature of the SGD update, or the datasets? Exploring this point further could provide deeper insights.

### Arguments for and against acceptance
**Arguments for:**
*   Strong, novel, and theoretically sound contribution to the important area of stochastic optimization.
*   The "safety" guarantee (provably better than static sampling) is a significant advantage over prior adaptive sampling approximations.
*   The method is practical, with an efficient O(n log n) implementation and demonstrated effectiveness on real-world problems.
*   The paper is well-written and the claims are rigorously supported.

**Arguments against:**
*   The practical applicability is currently demonstrated mainly for GLMs; its utility for other major model classes is an open question.
*   The empirical evaluation lacks a direct comparison to other state-of-the-art *practical* adaptive sampling methods.

Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper are substantial. The proposed minimax framework for safe adaptive sampling is an elegant and powerful idea with solid theoretical backing and clear practical potential. It represents a definite advance in the field.
