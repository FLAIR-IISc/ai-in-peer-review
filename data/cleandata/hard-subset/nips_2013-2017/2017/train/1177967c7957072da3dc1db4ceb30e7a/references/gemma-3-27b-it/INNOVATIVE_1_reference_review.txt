This paper presents a novel approach to adaptive importance sampling for both coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea – deriving a sampling distribution based on safe bounds on gradient information – is a strong one, addressing a key weakness of existing adaptive methods: computational cost and theoretical guarantees. The theoretical analysis demonstrating potential speedups (up to a factor of *n* for CD) is compelling, and the proposed algorithm aims to bridge the gap between fully informed optimal sampling and computationally feasible approximations. The connections to existing work on importance sampling and coordinate descent are well-established.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of formulating adaptive sampling as a convex optimization problem over safe gradient bounds is genuinely novel. It provides a principled way to balance adaptation with computational efficiency. This shifts the paradigm from directly estimating gradients to strategically sampling based on available information.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper includes a solid theoretical foundation, proving guarantees on the performance relative to fixed sampling strategies. The analysis in Section 2 regarding the potential speedup in CD is particularly insightful.
*   **Generality:** The proposed scheme is presented as applicable to both CD and SGD, increasing its potential impact.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The authors acknowledge the computational challenges of fully adaptive sampling and propose a practical algorithm (Algorithm 2) with clear complexity analysis. The discussion of tracking gradient bounds in GLMs demonstrates a path towards real-world applicability.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing improvements over fixed sampling in terms of iterations and, importantly, time.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The performance hinges on the quality of the safe gradient bounds ` and `u`. While the authors provide a GLM example, the general applicability and ease of obtaining such bounds in more complex models aren’t thoroughly discussed. The sensitivity of the algorithm to the tightness of these bounds warrants further investigation.
*   **SGD Results are Mild:** The empirical results for SGD are less dramatic than those for CD. The authors acknowledge this, but a deeper investigation into why the benefits are less pronounced in the SGD setting would be valuable.
*   **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art SGD:** The SGD experiments could benefit from a comparison to more advanced SGD variants (e.g., Adam, RMSprop) with adaptive learning rates, as these are commonly used in practice. It’s not immediately clear how this method stacks up against these baselines.
*   **Clarity of Competitive Ratio:** The discussion surrounding the competitive ratio (ρk) in Section 3.2, while mathematically rigorous, could benefit from more intuitive explanation. The implications of Lemma 3.6, specifically the constraint on γ, might not be immediately clear to all readers.
*   **Detailed Complexity Analysis:** A more detailed breakdown of the computational complexity, particularly considering the overhead of maintaining the gradient bounds ` and `u, would be useful.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a truly novel and theoretically grounded approach to adaptive importance sampling.
*   The proposed method offers a compelling trade-off between adaptivity and computational cost.
*   The empirical results, particularly for CD, demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas and contributions.
*   Even if the SGD improvements are modest, the theoretical framework opens avenues for future research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on safe gradient bounds is a potential limitation, and the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of their practical acquisition.
*   The weaker results for SGD raise questions about the generalizability of the approach.
*   The complexity analysis, while present, could be more detailed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a genuinely novel contribution. While there are some weaknesses, the originality and theoretical soundness of the approach, coupled with the promising empirical results, outweigh these concerns. The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in stochastic optimization – balancing adaptation with computational feasibility – and offers a compelling solution. I believe this work has the potential to influence future research in this area.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I am willing to overlook the minor flaws and incomplete validation in favor of the significant novelty and potential impact of this work. I encourage the authors to address the points raised above in a revision, particularly regarding the discussion of gradient bounds and the analysis of the SGD results.
