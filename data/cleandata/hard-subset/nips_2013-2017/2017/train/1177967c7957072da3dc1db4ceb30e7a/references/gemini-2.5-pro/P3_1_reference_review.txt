This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The central idea is to address a key drawback of existing adaptive sampling strategies: while potentially faster, they can perform worse than simple static sampling (like uniform or Lipschitz-based) and often require computationally expensive full gradient information.

The authors' approach is to work with efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on the gradient components (for CD) or component gradient norms (for SGD). Instead of using these bounds to form a point estimate, they formulate a minimax optimization problem: find the sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case expected progress over all possible true gradients consistent with the bounds. The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem. A key theoretical contribution is the proof that the resulting sampling distribution is guaranteed to be better than or equal to fixed Lipschitz-based importance sampling, justifying the "safe" moniker. The authors demonstrate how to derive and maintain the required bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs) and provide extensive empirical evidence showing their method significantly outperforms static sampling and approaches the performance of the computationally infeasible optimal gradient-based sampling.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is sound and well-presented.

*   The formulation of the safe sampling problem as a minimax optimization problem (Eq. 7) is elegant and directly targets the core weakness of prior heuristic approaches.
*   Theorem 3.2, which establishes the "safety" of the proposed sampling (i.e., it is provably better than fixed Li-based sampling), is a strong and important result. This guarantee is the paper's main theoretical pillar.
*   The characterization of the solution to the minimax problem (Theorem 3.4) and the accompanying efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) are non-trivial and represent a solid algorithmic contribution.
*   The experimental evaluation is thorough. The authors test their method on multiple real-world datasets, for both CD and SGD, with different loss functions and regularizers. The inclusion of wall-clock time comparisons (Figures 6 and 9) is crucial and effectively demonstrates that the method's theoretical advantages translate into practical speedups, despite the O(n log n) overhead. The plots visualizing the value of `v_k` provide excellent insight into the algorithm's dynamics.

A minor weakness is that the method for deriving the safe gradient bounds in Section 4 for GLMs relies on a simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which may be loose. The performance of the overall scheme is contingent on the quality of these bounds. While the paper shows even these simple bounds are effective, a discussion on the impact of bound tightness would be beneficial.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction. The structure logically progresses from the ideal "full information" setting in Section 2 to the practical "limited information" approach in Section 3. The notation is consistent and well-defined. The algorithms are clearly presented, and the figures are informative and support the paper's claims effectively. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the intuition behind their method, for instance, through the geometric interpretation of the optimization problem.

### Originality

The paper makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on stochastic optimization. While adaptive importance sampling is a well-studied area, existing methods often rely on heuristics (e.g., using stale gradients or upper bounds as point estimates) that lack theoretical guarantees of improvement over static sampling.

The primary novelty lies in the "safe" formulation. Framing the problem of choosing a sampling distribution from bounds as a minimax game is, to my knowledge, new in this context. This principled approach, which leads to a provably robust strategy, clearly distinguishes this work from prior art such as [22, 23, 36]. The paper correctly positions itself relative to related work and clearly articulates its unique contributions.

### Significance

The results are highly significant for both researchers and practitioners in large-scale machine learning.

*   The proposed method provides a practical and theoretically-grounded way to accelerate fundamental optimization algorithms like CD and SGD. The "safety" guarantee removes a major barrier to the adoption of adaptive sampling, as practitioners no longer need to risk performance degradation compared to simpler, well-understood methods.
*   The framework is generic. While demonstrated on GLMs, the core idea of using safe bounds `l, u` can be applied to any problem where such bounds can be efficiently maintained. This opens up avenues for future research on designing effective bounding strategies for other model classes (e.g., neural networks).
*   The work provides new theoretical insights into the design of adaptive sampling schemes and highlights the benefits of a worst-case, robust optimization perspective.

One point for consideration is the empirical results for SGD, where the gains were less pronounced than for CD. The authors acknowledge this, but further discussion on why this might be the case (e.g., problem structure, dataset characteristics) would add value. Additionally, a direct empirical comparison to other practical adaptive sampling schemes (e.g., duality gap-based methods [5, 23]) would have provided a more complete picture of the state-of-the-art, though the comparison to the theoretical "optimal" sampling is already very compelling.

### Summary

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Method:** The minimax formulation for safe adaptive sampling is a highly novel and theoretically sound approach to a well-known problem.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantee:** The proof that the method is always better than fixed importance sampling is a significant contribution that addresses a major weakness of prior adaptive schemes.
3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The paper provides an efficient algorithm and demonstrates its applicability to an important class of models (GLMs), showing clear wall-clock time improvements in extensive experiments.
4.  **High Clarity and Quality:** The paper is extremely well-written, technically solid, and the results are presented convincingly.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The practicality beyond GLMs is not fully established, as deriving and maintaining tight bounds for more complex models could be challenging.
2.  Empirical comparison against other state-of-the-art *practical* adaptive samplers is missing.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It addresses a relevant problem with a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practical solution. This is an excellent contribution that is likely to be influential and built upon by others in the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
