This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for both coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, aiming to improve computational efficiency while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The core idea is to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling by leveraging safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient information. The authors demonstrate theoretically that their scheme outperforms uniform or fixed importance sampling and recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting. They also provide empirical evidence on real-world datasets to support their claims.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The theoretical analysis, particularly the derivations in Section 2 regarding the speed-up achievable with adaptive sampling in CD, is solid and convincing. The proposed safe adaptive sampling strategy in Section 3 is well-defined and its properties are clearly stated. The practical considerations discussed in Section 4, specifically how to track gradient bounds for GLMs, are valuable. The empirical evaluation in Section 5 is comprehensive, covering various loss functions and regularization techniques on several datasets. The inclusion of figures demonstrating the evolution of `vk` provides valuable insight into the behavior of the proposed algorithm.

One significant strength of this work is the focus on *safe* adaptive sampling. Unlike many adaptive sampling techniques, this approach provides provable guarantees that the performance will not be worse than uniform sampling, even with limited information. This is a crucial aspect for practical applicability. The authors successfully bridge the gap between theoretical optimality (gradient-based sampling) and computational feasibility by introducing a practical approximation that maintains strong performance guarantees.  The efficient computation of the sampling distribution using Algorithm 4 is also a substantial contribution.  The connection to existing work on importance sampling and coordinate descent is well-established, and the paper appropriately cites relevant literature. The analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) is particularly insightful.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the theoretical analysis is strong, it relies on assumptions about the smoothness and coordinate-wise Lipschitz continuity of the objective function. It would be beneficial to discuss the robustness of the proposed method when these assumptions are violated, or to explore alternative conditions that guarantee performance.  The empirical evaluation, while thorough, could be expanded to include more challenging datasets or problems with higher dimensionality.  The performance gain in the SGD experiments seems rather modest compared to the CD experiments. A more detailed analysis of the reasons behind this difference would be valuable.  The connection between the proposed method and existing algorithms like SAGA or SVRG could be further clarified. While the authors mention related work, a more direct comparison to these methods, particularly in terms of convergence rates and computational cost, would strengthen the paper. Finally, the notation, while generally clear, can be dense and may benefit from occasional clarification. For example, defining `∆n` inline when first used would improve readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and guarantees.
*   Novel and practical approach to adaptive importance sampling.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating significant performance gains for CD.
*   Addresses a crucial practical challenge – efficient and safe adaptive sampling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Assumptions about the objective function's smoothness may limit applicability.
*   Modest performance gains observed in SGD experiments.
*   Limited comparison to closely related state-of-the-art algorithms (SAGA, SVRG).
*   Dense notation can occasionally hinder readability.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme is a valuable addition to the toolkit of methods for large-scale machine learning. While some minor weaknesses exist, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above – specifically, a more detailed discussion of the assumptions made, an analysis of the SGD performance, and a more direct comparison to related algorithms.  The potential impact of this work is considerable, and it is likely to be of interest to a wide audience of researchers and practitioners.
