## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the theoretical benefits of adaptive sampling, but also the computational challenges and potential instability of existing approaches like gradient-based sampling. Their key contribution is a "safe" adaptive sampling strategy that provides provable improvements over fixed sampling while remaining computationally efficient. This is achieved by formulating the sampling problem as a convex optimization problem, incorporating safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, and developing an efficient algorithm to compute the resulting sampling distribution. The paper includes both theoretical analysis (convergence rates for CD, complexity estimates) and empirical validation on real-world datasets demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical justification for adaptive importance sampling, demonstrating its potential speedups over fixed sampling, particularly for CD. The analysis clearly articulates the trade-offs between optimal sampling (requiring full gradient information) and practical approximations.
* **Practicality & Efficiency:** The proposed "safe" sampling approach addresses the key limitations of existing adaptive methods. By relying on safe bounds instead of exact gradient information, the authors achieve computational efficiency without sacrificing theoretical guarantees. The O(n log n) complexity for computing the sampling distribution is a significant advantage.
* **Generality:** The method is presented as being applicable to both CD and SGD, making it broadly relevant.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on real-world datasets (RCV1, real-sim, news20) provide strong evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison with fixed sampling and the analysis of the stepsize parameter (vk) are particularly insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a reasonably accessible manner.
* **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Adaptive sampling is a crucial technique for handling large-scale datasets, and the authors’ work provides a valuable contribution to this area.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Gradient Bound Tracking:** While the authors demonstrate how to track safe gradient bounds in the context of GLMs, the discussion remains somewhat limited.  A more detailed exploration of strategies for obtaining and maintaining these bounds for other models (e.g., deep neural networks) would be beneficial. The practical challenges of doing so in complex scenarios are not fully addressed.
* **SGD Performance:** The experimental results show a modest improvement with SGD. The authors acknowledge this, but further investigation into the reasons behind this limited gain would be helpful. Is it due to the specific datasets used, the choice of SGD parameters, or inherent limitations of the approach in the SGD setting?
* **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The analysis of the competitive ratio (ρk) provides insights into the performance relative to optimal sampling, but the conditions required for stronger bounds (Lemma 3.6) might be restrictive in practice. A more nuanced discussion of the expected competitive ratio in realistic scenarios would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison primarily focuses on uniform sampling and the optimal sampling scheme. A comparison to other recent adaptive sampling techniques (beyond those cited) would strengthen the paper's positioning within the field.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in machine learning. The proposed "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme is both theoretically sound and practically relevant. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field by developing a method that bridges the gap between optimal adaptive sampling and computationally feasible approximations. The empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and organized. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and guarantees.
* Practical and efficient algorithm with O(n log n) complexity.
* Demonstrated empirical performance on real-world datasets.
* Generality to both CD and SGD methods.
* Addresses a relevant and important problem in large-scale optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited discussion of gradient bound tracking for complex models.
* Modest performance improvement with SGD.
* Competitive ratio analysis could be more nuanced.
* Limited comparison to the most recent state-of-the-art methods.

**Rating:**

**Strong Accept (8/10)**

I believe this paper is a valuable contribution to the NIPS community and deserves to be accepted. The strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and the work is likely to have a positive impact on the field. I recommend the authors address the minor weaknesses raised above in a revised version of the manuscript. I specifically recommend expanding the discussion on tracking safe gradient bounds and comparing against more recent, competitive approaches.
