

# Peer Review: "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in large-scale optimization: how to efficiently implement adaptive importance sampling in coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors show that gradient-based adaptive sampling can theoretically provide significant speedups (up to a factor of the dimension n) over fixed sampling strategies, but the optimal approach requires computing the full gradient in each iteration, which is computationally prohibitive.

The key contribution is a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme that computes an efficient approximation of the optimal gradient-based sampling. The authors formulate the problem as finding the best sampling distribution given safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient information, which can be maintained efficiently during optimization (e.g., for generalized linear models). They prove that their method is always better than fixed importance sampling, and provide an O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling distribution.

The paper makes four key contributions: (1) theoretical justification for adaptive sampling in CD, (2) a generic safe adaptive sampling strategy, (3) practical integration into CD and SGD for structured problems, and (4) empirical validation on real datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2) showing that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling, which is a significant contribution that previous adaptive sampling approaches couldn't establish. The proof techniques are sound, and the connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation is well-maintained.

2. **Novel Approach to Safe Approximation**: The authors' key insight—formulating the adaptive sampling problem as a convex optimization problem with safe gradient bounds—is genuinely novel. Previous work (e.g., [36, 37]) used heuristic approximations, but this paper provides a theoretically grounded approach that guarantees performance relative to fixed sampling.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper provides concrete examples of how to compute safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models (Section 4), which is crucial for establishing the practical viability of the approach. The computational complexity analysis (O(n log n) per iteration) is well-justified and realistic for large-scale problems.

4. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation**: The experimental results (Section 5) effectively demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method across multiple datasets and loss functions. The comparison against multiple baselines (uniform sampling, fixed importance sampling, optimal sampling) provides a thorough evaluation.

5. **Clear Distinction from Related Work**: The paper carefully distinguishes itself from prior work on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) by explicitly addressing the computational limitations of previous approaches and providing a theoretically justified alternative.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited SGD Results**: While the paper claims to be applicable to both CD and SGD, the empirical results for SGD (Figures 7-9) show only marginal improvements over uniform sampling. This is surprising given the theoretical motivation for adaptive sampling in SGD. The paper should more thoroughly address why the improvements are less pronounced in SGD compared to CD, as this undermines the claim of "complete genericity" for both methods.

2. **Assumption on Gradient Bounds**: The paper assumes that safe gradient bounds can be efficiently computed, but the examples provided are limited to generalized linear models (Section 4). While this is a good starting point, it's unclear how general this approach is for more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks, non-linear models). The authors should discuss potential extensions to more complex settings or acknowledge the current limitations.

3. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper states that the overhead is O(n log n), but for very large n (e.g., news20 dataset with 1.3M features), this could become significant. The comparison to the cost of computing a single gradient component (O(d)) is somewhat misleading since d (dimensionality) is often much smaller than n (number of features). For the news20 dataset, d=1,355,191 and n=19,996, so O(n log n) would be comparable to O(d), not dominated by it as claimed.

4. **Lack of Ablation Study**: The paper doesn't provide an ablation study showing the impact of different components of the proposed method (e.g., the effect of using tighter gradient bounds vs. the naive Cauchy-Schwarz bounds). This would help readers understand how sensitive the performance is to the quality of the gradient bounds.

5. **Theoretical vs. Empirical Gap**: The theoretical results guarantee that the method is better than fixed sampling, but the empirical results show that it's often close to the optimal (full-gradient) adaptive sampling. The paper doesn't fully explain why the theoretical guarantee (which depends on the worst-case competitive ratio) doesn't translate to an even larger gap in practice.

## Critical Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical results. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., the limited benefits for SGD), which demonstrates honesty in their evaluation. The paper is a complete work rather than a position paper.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the key contributions are clearly stated. Some sections (particularly the theoretical derivations in Section 3) could benefit from additional intuition to make them more accessible to readers less familiar with the technical details. The empirical results are clearly presented with appropriate visualizations.

**Originality**: This is a significant contribution to the field. The safe adaptive sampling approach is genuinely novel and addresses a key limitation of previous adaptive sampling methods. While there is related work on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37]), none provide the theoretical guarantees of being always better than fixed sampling. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is important for large-scale machine learning, where computational efficiency is crucial. The proposed method has practical significance as it can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations with minimal overhead. The demonstrated speedups (up to a factor of n in CD) are substantial for large-scale problems. However, the limited improvements in SGD somewhat reduce the overall significance compared to what the paper initially promises.

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper with solid theoretical contributions and good empirical validation. The safe adaptive importance sampling approach is a meaningful advancement in the field of large-scale optimization. While the limited improvements in SGD are a notable weakness, the paper's contributions to coordinate descent are substantial and well-justified.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the following points:

1. Provide a more thorough explanation for the limited improvements observed in SGD compared to CD.
2. Expand the discussion on the generalizability of the safe gradient bounds approach beyond generalized linear models.
3. Clarify the computational complexity analysis for the case where n (number of features) is much larger than d (dimensionality).
4. Add an ablation study showing the impact of different gradient bound quality on the performance.

## Final Comments

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale optimization by providing a theoretically sound and practically efficient method for adaptive importance sampling. The "safe" approach to approximating the optimal gradient-based sampling is a clever solution to a long-standing practical challenge. The empirical results are compelling for coordinate descent, and the theoretical guarantees are robust. While the SGD results are less impressive, the paper's core contribution to CD optimization is significant enough to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS.

The paper is well-positioned to influence both the theory and practice of large-scale machine learning optimization, particularly for problems where coordinate descent is the method of choice. The authors have provided a clear roadmap for implementing their method in practice, which should facilitate adoption by the machine learning community.
