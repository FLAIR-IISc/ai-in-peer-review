

# Review: "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address a fundamental challenge in adaptive importance sampling: while theoretically optimal gradient-based sampling can significantly outperform fixed sampling (with speed-ups potentially reaching a factor of the problem dimension n), it typically requires computing the full gradient at each iteration, which is computationally prohibitive.

The key insight is to use safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information (rather than the exact gradient) to compute an approximate sampling distribution. Crucially, the authors prove that their proposed sampling is *provably never worse* than fixed importance sampling, while still capturing much of the benefit of the optimal (but infeasible) gradient-based sampling. The paper demonstrates that for generalized linear models (GLMs), these safe bounds can be efficiently maintained during optimization, making the approach computationally practical.

This work builds on previous research in adaptive importance sampling (citing [22, 5, 26, 36, 37]) but differs by providing theoretical guarantees about the performance of the approximate sampling method. The authors also show how to efficiently compute their proposed sampling distribution in O(n log n) time per iteration, which is comparable to the cost of computing a single gradient component.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees. Theorem 3.2 proves that the proposed safe sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling, and Lemma 3.5-3.6 provide bounds on the competitive ratio. This is a significant contribution because previous adaptive sampling methods lacked such guarantees.

2. **Practical Impact**: The method is designed to be computationally feasible while providing theoretical guarantees. The paper demonstrates that even very loose gradient bounds (obtained via simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequalities) yield significant improvements over fixed sampling, making the approach practical for real-world applications.

3. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The key concepts (like the "safe" sampling formulation) are introduced with helpful examples (Examples 2.2-2.3). The algorithms are clearly presented with pseudocode (Algorithms 1-4).

4. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The experimental section provides comprehensive evidence across multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) and different loss functions (squared hinge loss, logistic loss, square loss). The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms fixed sampling in terms of both iteration count and total computation time.

5. **Applicability**: The method is presented as a generic improvement that can be added to both CD and SGD methods, with specific implementation details for GLMs. The paper also discusses how the approach could be extended to more complex models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Gradient Bound Construction**: While Section 4 demonstrates how to construct safe gradient bounds for GLMs, the paper doesn't provide a general framework for other problem classes. The claim that "there are many other ways to track safe gradient bounds" (Section 4) feels underdeveloped, as no specific examples are given for non-GLM problems.

2. **Limited SGD Results**: The empirical results show that the adaptive sampling provides only marginal benefits for SGD (Figure 7-9), in contrast to the significant improvements seen for CD (Figures 2-6). The paper doesn't adequately explain this discrepancy, which might limit the method's appeal for practitioners primarily using SGD.

3. **Theoretical Complexity Analysis**: The paper claims the overhead of the proposed method is O(n log n) per iteration, but doesn't sufficiently explain why this is negligible compared to gradient computation. For datasets where d = O(n), the overhead is O(log n), but the paper doesn't clarify how this compares to the cost of computing a single gradient component (which is typically Θ(d)).

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper cites recent work on adaptive importance sampling [36, 23], but doesn't explicitly contrast their "safe" approach with other recent methods like the non-uniform sampling approaches in [22, 36, 37] that also aim to make adaptive sampling computationally feasible.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by well-constructed proofs (referenced to the appendix). The experimental design is appropriate and provides convincing evidence for the claimed advantages. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the marginal benefits for SGD) and provide theoretical guarantees about the method's performance. The paper is complete, presenting both theoretical contributions and practical validation.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistently used, and the key concepts are explained with helpful examples. The algorithms are presented in a way that makes them easy to implement. The only minor clarity issue is that some of the more technical proofs are deferred to the appendix, which might make it slightly harder for readers to follow the main arguments without consulting the appendix.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution by providing a theoretically grounded approach to adaptive importance sampling that maintains the key advantage of being "safe" (never worse than fixed sampling). While previous work has proposed adaptive sampling methods, none have provided the same guarantees about the performance of the approximate sampling. The safe sampling formulation in Section 3.1 and the competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5-3.6) are novel contributions that distinguish this work from previous approaches.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high. Adaptive importance sampling has the potential to significantly improve the efficiency of large-scale optimization algorithms, which are fundamental to modern machine learning. The paper demonstrates that the proposed method can be efficiently implemented with minimal computational overhead while providing clear theoretical guarantees and empirical benefits. The fact that even very loose gradient bounds yield significant improvements makes the approach particularly practical for real-world applications.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, with strong theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The paper addresses a well-known challenge in adaptive importance sampling with a novel approach that provides practical benefits without sacrificing theoretical guarantees.

The main limitation is the marginal benefit observed for SGD, but this doesn't detract from the overall contribution, as the method is still theoretically sound and provides significant benefits for CD, which is widely used in many machine learning applications. The paper's contribution is clearly differentiated from previous work, and the theoretical and empirical results are compelling.

## Final Thoughts

This paper represents a solid contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The "safe" adaptive importance sampling approach provides a principled way to make adaptive sampling computationally feasible while maintaining theoretical guarantees, which is a significant step forward from previous methods. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides both theoretical and empirical evidence for its claims. The method is general enough to be applicable to a wide range of optimization problems and could have a meaningful impact on large-scale machine learning applications.

The authors have successfully addressed a practical challenge in optimization with a theoretically grounded solution, and I believe this work will be valuable to both practitioners and researchers in the field.
