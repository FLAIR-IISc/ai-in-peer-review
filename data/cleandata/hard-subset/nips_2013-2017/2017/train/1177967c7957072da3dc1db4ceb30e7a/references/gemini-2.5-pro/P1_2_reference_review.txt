### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core problem it addresses is that while optimal adaptive sampling strategies (e.g., gradient-based) can offer significant speedups, they are often computationally infeasible as they require full gradient information at each iteration. Existing approximations can perform worse than simple uniform sampling and often lack theoretical guarantees.

The authors' main contribution is a novel framework for computing an adaptive sampling distribution based on limited information. The key idea is to maintain safe lower and upper bounds on the unknown gradient components (or their norms). Instead of using a heuristic based on these bounds (e.g., using the upper bounds as estimates), the authors formulate a minimax optimization problem: find the sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case slowdown, considering all possible true gradients that lie within the given bounds.

The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It provides a clear theoretical analysis motivating adaptive sampling for CD, showing a potential speedup factor of up to `n` over uniform sampling.
2.  It introduces the "safe" sampling method via the minimax problem and proves that the resulting distribution is guaranteed to be better than or equal to fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform or Li-based sampling). This "safety net" is a crucial property.
3.  It presents an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm to solve this minimax problem, making the approach practical.
4.  It demonstrates a simple `O(n)` method to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).
5.  It provides extensive empirical evidence on real-world datasets, showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time, and performs nearly as well as the impractical optimal sampling scheme.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled and Novel Approach:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization (Equation 7) is a highly original and principled way to handle uncertainty about the gradient. It moves beyond heuristics and provides a robust solution with strong theoretical backing. This is the paper's main conceptual contribution and is very elegant.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2), proving that the proposed sampling is never worse than standard fixed importance sampling, is a significant result. This addresses a major drawback of many existing adaptive schemes, which can sometimes underperform and require careful tuning.

3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The authors provide not only a strong theoretical framework but also a practical algorithm to implement it. The `O(n log n)` algorithm for computing the sampling distribution is efficient enough for many applications, and the paper demonstrates a simple `O(n)` method for updating the necessary gradient bounds for a large class of problems (GLMs).

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. They compare against the correct baselines (fixed sampling and the computationally expensive optimal sampling). The inclusion of both iteration complexity and wall-clock time is important, as it shows the computational overhead of the method is justified by the faster convergence. The plots of the `vk` value effectively connect the empirical performance to the theory.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the tightness of the lower and upper bounds (`l`, `u`). The paper proposes a simple method based on Cauchy-Schwarz for GLMs, which can be quite loose, especially if features are highly correlated. While the experiments show this is sufficient to achieve good performance, the paper could benefit from a discussion on the sensitivity of the method to bound quality. For instance, how much does the competitive ratio `ρk` degrade as the bounds `[uk]i / [lk]i` grow?

2.  **Computational Overhead in High Dimensions:** While `O(n log n)` is efficient, it could become a bottleneck for problems where `n` (the number of coordinates or data points) is extremely large and the cost of a single gradient update (`d` in the GLM case) is very small. The paper mentions this is acceptable when `d = Ω(n)`, but in sparse settings or for simpler models, the sampling cost might dominate the update cost. This limitation could be stated more explicitly.

3.  **Clarity of Algorithm 4:** The presentation of Algorithm 4 is quite dense and could be improved for clarity and reproducibility. The variables `u` and `l` are used as indices, which clashes with their use as bound vectors throughout the rest of the paper. A more detailed explanation or pseudocode with clearer variable names would be helpful.

4.  **Limited Discussion on SGD Results:** The paper notes that the performance gain for SGD is less pronounced than for CD. This is an interesting finding, but the paper does not offer much intuition or analysis as to why this might be the case. Is it a feature of the datasets, or a more fundamental difference between coordinate-wise gradients and component function gradients? A short discussion would strengthen the paper.

### Detailed Comments on Review Criteria

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs (some deferred to the appendix), and the experimental methodology is sound. The work is complete and presents a self-contained contribution.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the progression from the full-information case to the limited-information case is logical. As noted above, the description of Algorithm 4 could be improved. The introduction of the non-standard stepsize `γk = αk/[pk]ik` for CD is clever and central to the analysis, and it is explained well.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While adaptive importance sampling is an active area of research, the proposed minimax framework for achieving a "safe" sampling distribution is novel. It provides a new perspective on how to utilize partial information (bounds) in a robust way, which distinguishes it clearly from prior work that relies on stale values or heuristic estimates.

*   **Significance:** The results are significant and likely to be impactful. The proposed method offers a "best-of-both-worlds" solution: the speed of adaptive sampling with the safety of fixed sampling. Given its practicality and strong guarantees, it has the potential to become a standard technique for accelerating CD and SGD methods. Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale optimization will likely use and build upon these ideas.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The "safe" adaptive sampling framework is elegant, theoretically sound, and practically effective. It addresses a clear and important problem with a robust solution. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work.
