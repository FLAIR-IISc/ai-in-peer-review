Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It first shows, under coordinate-wise Lipschitz smoothness, that gradient-based sampling for CD (probabilities proportional to √Li|∇if(x)| with a matched stepsize) maximizes a standard quadratic upper-bound model’s expected progress and can yield up to an n-fold improvement over fixed Li-based (or uniform) sampling. For SGD on finite sums, it recalls analogous results where sampling proportional to per-component gradient norms is “optimal” for the same model.

The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme when full gradient information is unavailable. The authors assume access in each iteration to safe lower/upper bounds on the relevant gradient magnitudes (|∇if(x)| for CD; ‖∇fi(x)‖ for SGD), and pose a robust min-max optimization to choose probabilities p and stepsize α that are optimal in the worst case over the uncertainty set defined by these bounds. They show the resulting distribution p̂ is never worse than the fixed Li-based distribution and is the best possible given only the bounds. They provide an O(n log n) algorithm (via sorting and KKT checks) to compute p̂ and α, and show that in the full-information limit the method recovers gradient-based sampling.

For applicability, the paper shows how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs), leveraging exact or bounded updates to gradient entries using inner product estimates (e.g., via Cauchy–Schwarz) and known curvature bounds (e.g., logistic). Experiments on several text datasets suggest substantial gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD compared to fixed importance sampling, and competitive performance relative to the full-information oracle.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: casting adaptive sampling under limited information as a robust optimization over uncertainty sets of gradients, and deriving a closed-form characterization with an efficient O(n log n) algorithm.
- Theoretical guarantees: (i) per-step expected decrease is at least as good as fixed Li-based sampling; (ii) optimality within the information model (best worst-case distribution over the bounds); (iii) recovery of full-information gradient-based sampling when bounds are tight.
- Practicality for a class of problems: concrete procedures to maintain safe gradient bounds for GLMs; clear statement of computational overhead and when it is negligible; competitive empirical results, especially for CD.
- Clarity of the high-level idea and connection to prior adaptive sampling (gradient-based, duality-gap-based) and fixed importance sampling literature.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Computational overhead and scalability: The proposed scheme requires O(n) updates to bounds plus O(n log n) to compute p̂ per iteration. This can dominate per-iteration cost in many CD settings where updating a single coordinate is O(1)–O(nnz(ai)), and in SGD where computing one component gradient is O(d). The paper argues overhead is acceptable when d = Ω(n), which is not typical for many large-scale problems; more discussion on amortization (e.g., recomputing p̂ every T steps), incremental maintenance, or blockwise variants would strengthen practical significance.
- Composite/nonsmooth objectives: The CD update xk+1 = xk − γk∇ik f(xk) presumes smoothness; yet experiments include L1-regularized problems. Many CD methods in that setting use proximal updates. It is unclear whether the theoretical guarantees (stepsize selection, expected decrease bound) carry to proximal CD; a precise statement of assumptions and an extension (or limitation) to composite objectives are needed.
- Tightness and construction of safe bounds: The use of Cauchy–Schwarz may yield very loose bounds; while the method is robust, loose bounds can erode gains. The paper would benefit from quantifying how bound looseness affects ρk (beyond the provided γ-based bound) in typical GLMs (e.g., logistic with 1/4 curvature bound) and providing empirical ablations with tighter/looser bounds.
- Global convergence rates: The main theorems give one-step improvement guarantees; appendices indicate that standard techniques yield convergence rates. Presenting at least one explicit global rate (e.g., for convex/strongly convex CD) comparing constants vs. fixed sampling would improve completeness and significance.
- Experimental scope: For SGD, gains are modest and the method adds overhead; a comparison to existing adaptive sampling heuristics (e.g., stale gradients, upper-bound-based methods) would contextualize benefits. Similarly for CD, comparisons to other adaptive rules (Gauss–Southwell or approximate variants, duality-gap-based sampling) would be informative. Reporting wall-clock including the overhead breakdown, and studying recomputation frequency, would clarify practicality.
- Clarity and minor issues: Algorithm 4 overloads symbols (e.g., u as vector and index), and could be streamlined. Some notation (Tr[L], Lmin) and variable naming could be standardized. A brief proof sketch of the min-max interchange would aid readability.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling and derivations appear consistent. The min-max formulation, its equivalence to maximizing the ratio ‖√Lc‖1/‖c‖2 over the uncertainty set, and the O(n log n) algorithm are plausible and well-motivated. Details are deferred to the appendix; a more self-contained proof outline would help. Assumptions for composite objectives need clearer articulation.
- Empirical support: Experiments support the claims for CD, with improvements over fixed sampling and proximity to the oracle. For SGD, improvements are smaller; this is acknowledged.

Clarity
- Generally clearly written, with good structure, motivation, and related work. Some sections (Algorithm 4, assumptions for nonsmooth cases) would benefit from additional exposition and consistent notation.

Originality
- The robust “safe” sampling under gradient uncertainty, with worst-case optimality and an efficient solver, is a novel angle relative to prior adaptive importance sampling that relies on stale/full gradients or upper bounds without safety guarantees.

Significance
- The approach is potentially impactful for CD on problems where maintaining bounds is cheap and recomputation can be amortized. For SGD, the benefits may be limited unless overhead is reduced. The safety guarantee differentiates the method and may influence future adaptive sampling designs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the treatment of composite objectives and whether a proximal variant of the analysis/algorithm is available.
- Provide at least one explicit global convergence rate bound with improved constants vs. fixed sampling.
- Evaluate amortized strategies (recompute p̂ every T steps), and include overhead breakdowns.
- Compare against additional adaptive baselines (e.g., stale-gradient sampling, duality-gap sampling, Gauss–Southwell approximations).
- Tighten the presentation of Algorithm 4 and unify notation.

Pro/Con summary
Pros:
- Novel robust adaptive sampling formulation with safety guarantee.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm; recovers full-information sampling.
- Practical bound-maintenance strategies for GLMs; strong CD experiments.

Cons:
- Per-iteration O(n)–O(n log n) overhead may limit applicability; need amortization.
- Composite/nonsmooth case not fully formalized.
- Limited SGD gains and baseline comparisons.
- Global rates and practical overhead analysis could be expanded.

Overall, the paper presents a solid theoretical and algorithmic contribution with promising empirical results for CD. Addressing the noted clarity and practicality points would strengthen the work.
