### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling". The core motivation is to bridge the gap between static sampling methods (like uniform or fixed importance sampling), which are computationally cheap but can be suboptimal, and adaptive methods (e.g., gradient-based sampling), which are theoretically superior but often computationally prohibitive as they require full gradient information at each iteration.

The authors first revisit the theory of adaptive importance sampling, formalizing the potential speed-up of gradient-based sampling, which can be up to a factor of `n` (the dimension) for CD. The main contribution is a new method for handling the practical setting where the full gradient is unknown. Instead of relying on potentially inaccurate point estimates (like stale gradients or upper bounds), the proposed approach assumes access to safe lower and upper bounds (`l_k`, `u_k`) on the gradient magnitudes. The key idea is to formulate the problem of choosing a sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem: find the distribution `p` that minimizes the worst-case value of a term in the expected progress bound, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradients `c` consistent with the bounds `l_k` and `u_k`.

The authors prove that the resulting "safe" sampling distribution is guaranteed to perform at least as well as standard fixed importance sampling, overcoming a major drawback of prior adaptive approximation schemes. They provide a non-trivial and efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm to solve this minimax problem. To demonstrate practicality, they show how to maintain the required gradient bounds efficiently (in `O(n)` time) for the important class of Generalized Linear Models (GLMs). The paper is supported by extensive experiments on several real-world datasets, showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs in the appendix. The central contribution, the minimax formulation for robust sampling (Equation 7), is rigorously analyzed. Theorem 3.2, which establishes the "safety" guarantee (i.e., the method is provably no worse than fixed `L_i`-based sampling), is a strong and important result that provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approach. The development of an efficient `O(n log n)` solver (Algorithm 4) for this non-trivial optimization problem is a significant technical achievement. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering multiple datasets, loss functions, and regularizers for both CD and SGD, and it compellingly validates the theoretical claims and practical utility of the method.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is laid out effectively, starting with the ideal full-information setting in Section 2 before transitioning to the practical limited-information setting in Section 3. This structure makes the paper easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the problem formulation is precise. The inclusion of Figure 1, which contrasts the different sampling algorithms, is helpful for understanding the workflow. Section 4 provides a clear and concrete example of how the required gradient bounds can be maintained in practice for GLMs, connecting the theory to a practical application. A minor point for improvement could be to add more intuition to the description of Algorithm 4 in the main text, as its mechanics are somewhat complex and rely on the optimality conditions detailed in the appendix.

**Originality:** The paper presents a highly original approach to adaptive importance sampling. While the concepts of adaptive sampling and using gradient approximations are not new, the formulation of the problem as finding a sampling distribution that is robust to uncertainty in the gradient is novel. Previous works typically rely on point estimates from bounds (e.g., using the upper bound) or stale information, without guarantees against worst-case scenarios. The minimax framework is a fundamentally different and more principled way to handle this uncertainty. The proposed directionally-unbiased stepsize for CD is also a novel element that helps unify the analysis with SGD-type methods.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant and addresses a key practical challenge in large-scale optimization. The "safety" guarantee makes adaptive sampling a much more attractive option for practitioners, removing the risk of performing worse than a standard, reliable baseline. The proposed method is generic enough to be applied to both CD and SGD and can be integrated as a drop-in improvement. The computational overhead of `O(n log n)` is often negligible compared to the gradient computation costs in high-dimensional problems (`d >> n`), as demonstrated by the wall-clock time improvements in the experiments. This work provides a practical, efficient, and theoretically sound method that is likely to be used by researchers and practitioners. It also opens up new research directions, such as developing methods for maintaining tighter gradient bounds for more complex models.

### Arguments for Acceptance (Pro)
*   **Novel Formulation:** The minimax optimization framework for choosing a sampling distribution under uncertainty is a novel and principled contribution.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a crucial "safety" guarantee, proving that the proposed sampling is never worse than standard fixed importance sampling, which was a key limitation of prior heuristic approaches.
*   **Efficient Algorithm:** A non-trivial and efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm is provided to compute the proposed sampling distribution, making the method practical.
*   **Demonstrated Practicality:** The paper shows how the required gradient bounds can be efficiently maintained for GLMs, a wide and important class of models.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Extensive experiments on real-world datasets show significant improvements over fixed sampling in both iteration complexity and wall-clock time.

### Arguments against Acceptance (Con)
*   **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The performance of the method is inherently tied to the quality (tightness) of the lower and upper bounds on the gradient. The simple Cauchy-Schwarz based bounds used in the paper might be quite loose in practice, potentially limiting the gains in some scenarios.
*   **Clarity of Algorithm 4:** The main text could provide more intuition for the `O(n log n)` solver (Algorithm 4), which is a key component of the contribution. Its current description is very condensed.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically effective solution to an important problem in machine learning optimization.
