**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors correctly identify a key challenge with adaptive sampling: while theoretically optimal strategies like gradient-based sampling can offer significant speedups, their computational cost (requiring full gradient information) is prohibitive. Existing approximations that rely on stale or bounded gradient information lack guarantees and can underperform even simple uniform sampling.

The main contribution is a novel framework to address this. The authors assume access to safe lower and upper bounds (`l`, `u`) on the gradient information (magnitudes for CD, norms for SGD). Instead of using these bounds heuristically, they formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem (Equation 7). This problem seeks a distribution that minimizes the worst-case upper bound on the objective's progress over all possible true gradients consistent with the bounds. The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem and proves that the resulting "safe" sampling is guaranteed to be at least as good as fixed, L-based importance sampling. The authors demonstrate how to maintain the required bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide extensive experiments supporting the method's practical effectiveness.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Formulation:** The core idea of defining the sampling distribution via a min-max game against an adversary choosing the worst-case gradient within given bounds is novel and compelling. It provides a principled way to handle uncertainty in the gradient information.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantee:** The "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2), proving the proposed sampling is never worse than the standard fixed importance sampling, is a significant result. This directly addresses a major drawback of prior approximate adaptive sampling schemes and makes the proposed method a reliable replacement for fixed sampling.
3.  **Efficient Algorithm:** The authors provide a practical O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the proposed optimization problem, ensuring the per-iteration overhead of the adaptive scheme is manageable, especially when the feature dimension `d` is large.
4.  **Comprehensive Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering multiple datasets, loss functions (square, squared hinge, logistic), and regularizers (L1, L2) for both CD and SGD. The results consistently demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach over fixed sampling baselines.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the strong core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of technical inaccuracies, notational ambiguities, and presentational flaws which severely undermine its quality.

1.  **Critical Technical Error in Bound Maintenance:** The entire practical justification of the method rests on the ability to efficiently maintain the safe bounds `l` and `u`. However, the derivation for the CD case on GLMs appears to be incorrect. In Section 4, the gradient update is given as `∇if(xk+1)−∇if(xk) = γk〈ai,aik〉`. The correct update for a least-squares objective `(1/2)||Ax-b||^2` should be `∇if(xk+1)−∇if(xk) = -γk * ∇_{ik}f(xk) * 〈ai,aik〉`. The authors have omitted the crucial `∇_{ik}f(xk)` term. This error invalidates the proposed method for tracking bounds, as it would require knowing the exact value of a gradient component to bound the change in others, creating a circular dependency. This is a fundamental flaw in a core part of the paper's contribution.

2.  **Pervasive Notational Ambiguity and Imprecision:** The paper suffers from consistently unclear notation, which makes it difficult to follow and verify the technical claims.
    *   In Example 2.3, the expression for the optimal stepsize `αk(p?k)` uses the non-standard notation `‖√L∇f(xk)‖2`. The text does not clarify if this is an L2-norm, a squared L1-norm, or something else. This ambiguity persists throughout the paper (e.g., `‖c‖22`). Based on first principles, the denominator should be `‖√L∇f(xk)‖_1^2`, but this is not what is written.
    *   In Section 2.1, the proposed stepsize is `γk = αk[pk]−1ik`, a confusing way to write `αk / [pk]_ik`.
    *   The core upper bound in Equation (3) is presented with the term `Likα k [pk]2ik (∇ikf(xk))`. The notation `[pk]2ik` is impenetrable, and the expression appears to be dimensionally incorrect as `[pk]_ik` should be in the denominator.
    *   Algorithm 4 uses overloaded variables (`l` for the lower bound vector and `l` for an index counter), making the procedure extremely difficult to parse.

3.  **Lack of Professional Polish:** The manuscript is unacceptably sloppy for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   There are numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "deep leaning" in the Introduction, "data chose" in Section 5).
    *   A placeholder footnote ("st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...") is present on the first page.
    *   The reference list is formatted inconsistently.
    *   Figure 1 is not a figure but three blocks of text presented as one.

4.  **Overstated Generality:** The paper claims the scheme is "completely generic," but only provides a (flawed) derivation for maintaining bounds on GLMs. The challenges of applying this method to other important model classes, such as neural networks, where maintaining meaningful yet cheap bounds is non-trivial, are not discussed.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the central idea of "safe" adaptive sampling via a min-max formulation is elegant and promising, the current manuscript is not ready for publication. The presence of a critical technical error in the derivation of the gradient bounds for CD (Section 4) calls into question the practical applicability of the method as presented. This major flaw, combined with a pervasive lack of clarity in notation, numerous typographical errors, and an overall lack of polish, prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper would require a major revision to correct the technical errors, clarify the notation, and improve the overall presentation before it could be reconsidered.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core theoretical idea of a "safe" adaptive sampling framework is novel, interesting, and addresses a clear need in the literature.
*   The guarantee that the method will not perform worse than fixed sampling is a strong and valuable contribution.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   A critical technical error in the derivation of the gradient bound updates for CD undermines the paper's practical methodology.
*   The paper is filled with ambiguous, non-standard notation that makes its technical content difficult to verify and reproduce.
*   The manuscript's presentation is unpolished, with numerous typos and formatting issues that fall well below the standards expected for NIPS.
*   Claims of generality are not sufficiently supported.
