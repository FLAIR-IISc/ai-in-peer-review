### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a new adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core problem addressed is that existing adaptive sampling methods are often either computationally prohibitive (e.g., requiring full gradient computation at each step) or lack theoretical guarantees, sometimes performing worse than simple uniform sampling.

The main idea is to use efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes (for CD) or gradient norms (for SGD) to define a "safe" set of possible gradients. Instead of picking an arbitrary gradient from this set (e.g., the upper bound, as in some prior work), the authors formulate a min-max optimization problem. They seek a sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case convergence bound over all possible true gradients within the safe set.

The key contributions are:
1.  A novel min-max formulation for choosing the sampling distribution under uncertainty, which is robust by design.
2.  A strong theoretical guarantee that the resulting sampling strategy is provably at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform sampling in the L-smooth case), a property lacking in many previous adaptive schemes.
3.  An efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this min-max problem, making the method practical for large-scale settings.
4.  A demonstration of how to derive and maintain the required gradient bounds for an important class of problems (Generalized Linear Models) with only O(n) overhead per iteration.
5.  Empirical validation on several real-world datasets showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time, and performs nearly as well as the computationally expensive "optimal" sampling that uses full gradient information.

This work builds upon the literature of adaptive sampling for stochastic optimization (e.g., [5, 22, 23, 36]) but distinguishes itself by providing a principled, robust optimization framework that yields strong "safety" guarantees.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The core contribution—formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a min-max problem (Eq. 7)—is elegant and theoretically sound. It provides a robust solution to the problem of acting on incomplete information, which is a significant conceptual advance over heuristic or overly optimistic approaches (e.g., sampling based only on upper bounds).
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2) is the paper's most important result. Proving that the method is never worse than standard Li-based importance sampling directly addresses a major practical concern with adaptive methods and makes the proposed scheme highly appealing. This is a clear improvement over prior work where adaptive schemes could degrade performance.
3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The theory is supported by an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) for computing the safe sampling distribution. The authors also show how to maintain the necessary gradient bounds for GLMs with a practical O(n) update cost per iteration. This combination of strong theory and practical implementation is a major strength.
4.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. They clearly compare the proposed method against key baselines (uniform/fixed, and optimal full-information sampling). The results show a clear win for the proposed method, not just in iteration complexity but also in wall-clock time, demonstrating that the computational overhead is well worth it. The inclusion of plots for the step-size parameter `vk` provides excellent insight into why the method works well.
5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clearly articulated, the theoretical development is easy to follow, and the contributions are stated upfront and well-supported by the content.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The method's performance hinges on the quality of the lower and upper bounds (`l`, `u`). The paper uses a simple Cauchy-Schwarz based approach for GLMs, which can be quite loose. A discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the tightness of these bounds would strengthen the paper. For instance, if the bounds are very wide (`l_i` close to 0 and `u_i` large), the safe sampling might default to being very close to uniform sampling.
2.  **Limited Scope of Bound Derivation:** While the GLM example is important and well-executed, the paper's general framework is applicable to any problem where such bounds can be derived. The paper would have broader impact if it discussed or referenced how to obtain such bounds for other significant model classes, such as those used in deep learning. For SGD, obtaining per-sample gradient norm bounds `||∇fi(x)||` without significant overhead is a major challenge for complex models.
3.  **Marginal Gains for SGD:** As the authors honestly note, the empirical gains for SGD are much less pronounced than for CD. While this is a valuable scientific finding, the paper could benefit from a deeper discussion on why this might be the case. Is it because the intrinsic variance of SGD sampling dominates the variance from the coordinate/component selection? Or are the gradient norm bounds derived in this setting less effective?

### **Detailed Comments**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs (though many are deferred to the appendix, which is standard). The experimental setup is sound, uses relevant real-world datasets, and supports the main claims of the paper. The authors are careful to point out the limitations, such as the modest performance gains in their SGD experiments.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The notation is standard and consistent. The flow from motivating the problem to presenting the full-information optimal case, then the limited-information "safe" case, and finally the practical implementation and experiments, is logical and compelling. Algorithm 4 is a bit dense, but its purpose and complexity are clearly explained.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While adaptive importance sampling is an active area, the specific min-max formulation to guarantee safety against a baseline is a novel and powerful idea. It represents a new way of thinking about how to use approximate information in stochastic optimization. The work is well-situated within the existing literature, and its unique contributions are clearly articulated.

**Significance:** The results are significant and likely to be influential. The proposed method provides a "best-of-both-worlds" solution: it captures much of the benefit of adaptive sampling while providing the safety of a fixed sampling scheme. Given its modest computational overhead, it could easily be integrated into existing CD and SGD solvers for a wide range of machine learning problems, particularly for convex optimization on sparse, high-dimensional data where CD methods excel. It advances the state of the art by making adaptive sampling more robust and trustworthy.

### **Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The combination of a novel and principled theoretical framework, strong "safety" guarantees, and convincing empirical results makes it a standout submission. The weaknesses identified are minor and represent opportunities for future work rather than fundamental flaws. I strongly recommend acceptance.
