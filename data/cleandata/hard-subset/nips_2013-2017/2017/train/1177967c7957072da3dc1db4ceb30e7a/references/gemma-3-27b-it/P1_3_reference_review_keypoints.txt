- Introduces a practical, provably safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent and SGD that recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting.  
- Selects sampling distributions via convex optimization using efficiently computable safe upper/lower gradient bounds.  
- Sampling computation is efficient (sorting-based O(n log n)) and avoids full gradient evaluations.  
- Builds on prior adaptive sampling and CD/SGD literature (e.g., Zhao & Zhang 2017, Csiba et al. 2015, Nesterov 2012).  
- Theoretical guarantees show improved expected progress over fixed/uniform sampling and link to optimal gradient-based sampling.  
- Method is general and applicable to both CD and SGD; demonstrated on generalized linear models with concrete bound derivations.  
- Empirical results on RCV1, real-sim, and news20 show significant iteration and runtime gains versus uniform and gradient sampling.  
- Presentation is clear, with concise algorithms and good notation.  
- Major weakness: heavy reliance on the quality of safe gradient bounds; unclear how to obtain them for complex models and sensitivity to inaccuracies.  
- SGD gains are less pronounced than for CD; reasons for this discrepancy are not fully explored.  
- Competitive ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5/3.6) is mathematically dense and would benefit from a more intuitive explanation.  
- Comparisons are limited to uniform and gradient-based sampling; lacking evaluation against more recent adaptive methods or advanced optimizers.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important scalability/efficiency trade-off, offers rigorous theory, practical algorithm, and compelling experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: dependence on gradient bounds, weaker SGD performance, and potentially inaccessible complex analysis.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution—theoretically sound, efficient, empirically effective—recommended for acceptance (rating 8.5/10).  
- Recommendations: broaden discussion on obtaining safe bounds beyond GLMs, investigate SGD performance causes, give intuitive competitive ratio explanations, and compare to newer state-of-the-art methods.
