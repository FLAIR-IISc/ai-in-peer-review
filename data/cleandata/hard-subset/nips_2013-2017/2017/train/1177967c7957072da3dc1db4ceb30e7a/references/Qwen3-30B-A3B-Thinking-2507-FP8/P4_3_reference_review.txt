

This paper proposes "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling" for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address a key limitation of existing adaptive importance sampling approaches: while theoretically promising (gradient-based sampling can offer speedups up to a factor of dimension n), these methods often require full gradient computations in each iteration, which is computationally prohibitive. The paper introduces a "safe" approximation that uses only safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information to compute an adaptive sampling distribution. Crucially, the authors prove this sampling is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling, and the scheme can be efficiently computed in O(n log n) time per iteration.

The paper relates to prior work on importance sampling in optimization (e.g., [2, 18, 19, 36, 37]) but distinguishes itself by providing a theoretically sound approximation that maintains the key advantage of adaptive sampling without the computational burden. Unlike previous approaches that use approximate gradient information (e.g., [36]), the proposed method guarantees improvement over fixed sampling. The authors demonstrate their approach on generalized linear models, showing how safe gradient bounds can be efficiently maintained during optimization.

**Strengths:**
- **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is strong and well-supported. The paper establishes clear bounds showing the proposed method is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2) and provides a detailed analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5, 3.6). The empirical evaluation on multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with various loss functions and regularizers demonstrates practical effectiveness, showing significant improvements in convergence speed over fixed sampling strategies.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The notation is consistently defined, and the key contributions are articulated clearly. The mathematical derivations are presented logically, though some proofs are deferred to the appendix. The figures (Figures 2-9) effectively illustrate the performance gains and theoretical properties of the proposed method.
- **Originality:** The paper introduces a novel optimization formulation for adaptive sampling that uses safe gradient bounds in a theoretically sound way (equation 7). This differs significantly from previous work that either required full gradient computations or used approximations without guarantees. The specific solution method (Algorithm 4) and its O(n log n) complexity represent a clear technical contribution.
- **Significance:** The proposed method has low computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration, comparable to gradient evaluation costs for many problems) and provides a practical solution to a well-known problem in large-scale optimization. The results demonstrate meaningful improvements in convergence speed across multiple datasets and problem types, making it likely to be adopted by practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical guarantees for the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5, 3.6) rely on assumptions about the gradient bounds (e.g., γ-accuracy in Lemma 3.6), which might not always hold in practice. The paper could better clarify how these assumptions relate to real-world optimization scenarios.
- While the paper demonstrates effectiveness on GLMs, the extension to more complex models (beyond the simple GLM examples in Section 4) is not fully explored. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion but do not provide concrete examples or analysis of more complex architectures.
- The empirical evaluation focuses primarily on CD methods; the results for SGD (Figures 7-9) show less pronounced improvements compared to CD, but the paper does not sufficiently explain this discrepancy or explore whether the method could be adapted for better SGD performance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically efficient solution to a well-known problem in optimization.
- The method provides provable guarantees that previous adaptive sampling approaches lacked.
- The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over fixed sampling strategies across multiple datasets and problem types.
- The computational overhead is minimal (O(n log n) per iteration), making it suitable for large-scale applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The practical benefits for SGD are less pronounced than for CD, and the paper does not sufficiently address why this is the case or how to improve it.
- The extension to more complex models beyond GLMs is limited, which could restrict the method's applicability.
- Some theoretical guarantees depend on specific assumptions about gradient bounds that may not be easily satisfied in all optimization scenarios.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical foundation is solid, the proposed method is both practical and provably better than existing approaches, and the empirical results validate its effectiveness. The paper addresses a genuine limitation in existing adaptive importance sampling techniques and provides a solution that is both theoretically sound and computationally efficient. While the empirical results for SGD are less impressive than for CD, the method's clear advantages for CD (which is widely used in machine learning) and the general applicability to structured optimization problems make this a strong contribution to NIPS. The paper is well-written, clearly articulates its contributions, and provides sufficient evidence to support its claims.
