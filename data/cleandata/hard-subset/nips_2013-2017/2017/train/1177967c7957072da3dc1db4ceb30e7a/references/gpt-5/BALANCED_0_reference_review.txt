Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles adaptive importance sampling for stochastic optimization, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key observation is that “gradient-based sampling” (probabilities proportional to magnitude of partial derivatives, or per-example gradient norms for SGD) maximizes a quadratic upper-bound model of expected progress, and can yield up to an n-fold improvement over fixed (Li-based or uniform) sampling. However, exact gradient-based probabilities are often infeasible to compute each iteration.

The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive sampling scheme that, given per-coordinate (or per-example) safe lower/upper bounds on the (unknown) gradient magnitudes, computes a probability vector p̂k and a stepsize α̂k via a minimax program: minimize over p the worst-case ratio V(p,c)/||c||^2 over all c consistent with the bounds, with V(p,c)=∑ Li c_i^2 / p_i. This yields guarantees: p̂k is optimal in this information-limited sense, never worse than fixed Li-based sampling, and recovers the ideal gradient-based sampling when full information is available. An O(n log n) algorithm computes p̂k (via sorting and a thresholding condition), and the authors show how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for a broad GLM class (using Cauchy–Schwarz updates for CD and second-order scalar curvature for SGD). Experiments on several text datasets show clear improvements for CD (especially with adaptive stepsizes) and smaller gains for SGD. The work relates to and extends prior adaptive and importance sampling strategies in CD and SGD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Zhao & Zhang; Needell et al.; Csiba/Qu/Richtárik), safe screening (Ndiaye et al., Liu et al.), and Gauss-Southwell/steepest-coordinate ideas.

Quality
- Theory: The one-step expected progress analysis is standard and sound. The minimax formulation is elegant; properties (best worst-case within bounds, dominance over Li-based sampling, recovery of optimal sampling with full information) are convincing. The O(n log n) algorithm is well motivated by KKT conditions and a geometric view. The competitive-ratio bounds (via vk and wk and a relative-accuracy assumption) are helpful, though somewhat loose.
- Assumptions/practicality: The approach hinges on maintaining safe bounds `,u. For GLMs, the paper proposes simple, conservative bounds updated in O(n) per iteration, making the total overhead O(n log n) for sampling plus O(n) for bound maintenance. This is practical when d is large (feature dimension or cost of a gradient component) relative to n, but may be heavier in regimes with small d and large n.
- Empirics: CD results are strong and consistent with theory; the impact of adaptive stepsizes is clearly demonstrated. For SGD, improvements are modest (also acknowledged). Baselines cover uniform, fixed importance (Li-based), and the unattainable “optimal” full-gradient sampling. A comparison to other adaptive/proxy schemes (e.g., dual residual/violation-based sampling, stale/approximate gradient-based sampling, Gauss-Southwell variants) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with clear notation and a helpful decomposition of the problem. The optimization view (Eq. 7) and geometric interpretation are insightful. Some details are deferred to the appendix (minimax interchange, algorithm proof), which is acceptable, but a brief intuitive justification in the main text would aid readability. The description of how the method handles nonsmooth separable regularizers in CD could be clarified, especially regarding proximal updates and stepsize choice.

Originality
Formulating adaptive sampling under limited information as a principled minimax problem and solving it efficiently with guarantees “never worse than fixed sampling” is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial. Prior works either use exact or stale gradients, heuristic upper bounds, or duality gaps; this paper’s safe, information-theoretic optimality within bounds is new. The O(n log n) constructive algorithm is also a useful contribution.

Significance
For CD, the contribution is significant: a practical, plug-in sampling rule that can yield substantial speedups with rigorous guarantees. For SGD, the empirical gains are weaker, but the framework is general and could be combined with stronger gradient-norm bounding or variance-reduction methods. The safe-bounds perspective could influence future adaptive schemes under partial information, including distributed settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean minimax formulation with strong, interpretable guarantees.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm; easy to integrate into CD and SGD.
- Solid theory showing strict dominance over fixed importance sampling and recovery of optimal sampling with full information.
- Empirical improvements for CD across multiple losses/regularizers; clear analysis of stepsize effects.
- Practical recipes for maintaining safe bounds in common GLMs.

Cons for acceptance
- Overhead O(n) for bound updates plus O(n log n) for sampling each iteration can be nontrivial when n is large and per-iteration gradients are cheap (small d). More discussion on amortization or partial updates would help.
- SGD benefits are limited; additional baselines and tasks (variance-reduced methods, modern deep models where gradient norm bounds may be tractable) are missing.
- Limited comparison to alternative adaptive CD samplers (e.g., dual residuals, gap-based rules) and approximate Gauss-Southwell variants.
- Clarity on handling nonsmooth separable regularizers via proximal CD updates and stepsize adaptation could be improved.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include baselines with practical adaptive samplers: dual residual/violation-based sampling (e.g., Qu et al.; Csiba et al.), gap-based sampling, and stale-gradient approximations.
- Analyze and/or implement amortized updates: maintain sorted structures, lazy updates of `,u, sampling every T steps, or only for a candidate subset, to reduce per-iteration overhead.
- Provide more guidance on proximal coordinate updates with L1 regularization: how α̂k couples with soft-thresholding/prox steps; ensure monotonicity.
- Extend experiments to variance-reduced SGD (SVRG/SAGA) with importance sampling; quantify the trade-offs in epochs vs wall-clock under different n,d sparsity regimes.
- Ablate bound tightness: compare Cauchy–Schwarz bounds vs tighter precomputed Gram-based bounds to show sensitivity and the benefit of tighter bounds.
- State the precise conditions for the minimax interchange in the main text (convexity/compactness), and give a brief proof sketch.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a principled and efficiently implementable adaptive sampling method with strong guarantees and clear gains for CD. While the SGD gains and empirical breadth could be stronger, the core idea is novel and valuable, and the work should be of interest to the NeurIPS community.

Confidence
High on the theoretical assessment; moderate on empirical generality.
