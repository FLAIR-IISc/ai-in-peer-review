Summary
The paper proposes a “safe adaptive importance sampling” scheme for randomized Coordinate Descent (CD) and SGD. The key idea is to approximate the gradient-based (per-iteration) optimal sampling distribution without computing all gradient components, using efficiently maintainable lower/upper bounds on coordinate (or component) gradients. The authors formalize this as a min–max problem: choose p to minimize the worst-case value of V(p,c)/||c||^2 over a box C of admissible gradient magnitudes c defined by the bounds. They show: (i) with full info the method recovers the gradient-based sampling p∗ ∝ √L|∇f| for CD (and the analogous norm-based sampling for SGD); (ii) with bounded info, the proposed p̂ is “never worse than” fixed Li-based sampling (and strictly better in many cases); (iii) they give an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute p̂. They also argue how to maintain safe bounds cheaply for GLMs, and present experiments on several text datasets showing notable improvements for CD and modest ones for SGD.

Relation to prior work
The work is well positioned with respect to: non-uniform/importance sampling for CD and SGD [Nesterov; Richtárik & Takáč; Csiba et al.; Allen-Zhu et al.], gradient-based sampling for least squares / ERM [Zhao & Zhang; Zhu], Gauss-Southwell / steepest coordinate descent [Nutini et al.; Stich et al.], safe screening [Liu et al.; Ndiaye et al.]. The specific novelty is a min–max formulation to be optimal under interval uncertainty on gradient magnitudes, with a constructive algorithm and a worst-case dominance guarantee over static Li-based sampling.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean “best under given information” formulation; reduces to known gradient-based sampling in the full-information limit.
- General enough to cover CD and SGD (though the theory and algorithms are developed primarily for CD).
- Theorem 3.2 provides a clear, easy-to-interpret guarantee (never worse than Li-based sampling; bounded vk ∈ [Lmin, Tr L]).
- Algorithm 4 is simple and runs in O(n log n) time; the structure of the solution (thresholding of √Li m within [ℓ,u]) is intuitive.
- Empirical results on GLMs show consistent CD gains (both iteration-wise and in wall-clock), and the authors candidly report smaller gains for SGD.

Weaknesses and issues
Technical correctness and rigor
- Inconsistency in the definition of V(p,x). Earlier derivations (and the optimality p∗ ∝ √Li|xi|) imply V(p,x) = ∑i Li x_i^2 / p_i. Lemma 2.4 defines V(p,x) as ∑i Li x_i^2 [p]_i (missing denominator). This propagates to several formulas. Please fix and ensure all subsequent statements use the same V.
- Equation (3): the last term should be ∑i Li α_k^2 (∇if)^2/(2 [p_k]_i). As written, the placement of brackets is ambiguous.
- The interchange of min and max in (7) (inequality (∗)) is asserted; the proof is deferred, but the ratio V(p,c)/||c||^2 is not jointly convex–concave in (p,c). Please provide precise conditions (e.g., transformation to an equivalent convex–concave form) or a correct application of a minimax theorem (Sion/Komiya) to justify the swap.
- Dimensional inconsistency: for least squares h(z) = 0.5||z − b||^2 with z ∈ R^d, ∇^2 h(z) = I_d, not I_n (Section 4).
- Safe bounds for general GLMs (beyond least squares) rely on Lemma 4.1 and a point x̃ on the segment [x_k, x_{k+1}]. The bounds are only as tight as available bounds on ∇^2 h and inner products 〈ai, a_j〉. The paper does not quantify how the looseness of these bounds affects vk or overall convergence rates; the “competitive ratio” bounds (Lemma 3.6) are very loose (γ^4) and lack evidence of tightness.
- Initialization with ℓ0 = 0, u0 = ∞ leads to a degenerate C0; Algorithm 4 must handle m, ||√Lc||1, ||c||2 when c = 0 (division by zero). This is not addressed.
- The proposed step size γk = αk/[p_k]_{i_k} is “directionally unbiased,” but stability constraints (e.g., γk ≤ 1/L_{i_k}) are not discussed beyond the bound-based derivation. Please clarify the conditions under which the steps are guaranteed safe.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and formatting issues: “preformance,” repeated/stray text (“st Conference...”), inconsistent equation numbering, missing footnotes (“1” after Li Lipschitz; “2” in Example 2.3), ambiguous brackets, and overloaded symbols in Algorithm 4 (u used as both vector of upper bounds and a scalar index). Algorithm 2/3 descriptions are cramped; Algorithm 1–3 figure labels are hard to parse.
- Notation drift between d and n: in CD with A ∈ R^{d×n}, the cost of computing a coordinate gradient depends on nnz(a_i), not Θ(d) in general. Complexity discussion conflates d and n and ignores sparsity; it should be corrected and made precise.
- Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues), misnumbered relative to in-text citations, or placeholders; some important related baselines are not compared experimentally (e.g., stale gradient-based samplers, upper-bound approximations, adaptive SDCA with probabilities).

Experimental concerns
- Data preprocessing: “all non-zero entries set to 1” is unusual and may distort Lipschitz constants and gradient magnitudes; justification is missing. λ is fixed to 0.1 across all tasks without tuning.
- Overhead: The method requires O(n) bound updates + O(n log n) sampling per iteration. For large n and sparse data, this can dominate the cost of a single coordinate update. Empirical timing should isolate overhead vs. compute, and all timing should be on the same machine (Figures 6 vs. 9 are not comparable).
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to strong adaptive baselines that work with partial info (e.g., stale gradients, inexpensive upper bounds, adaptive SDCA probabilities), and to deterministic Gauss-Southwell variants when feasible.

Originality and significance
- The min–max “safe best” formulation and its thresholding solution are, to my knowledge, novel and interesting. However, the impact is tempered by the heavy cost per iteration and by relatively modest SGD gains. Stronger theoretical rates (e.g., global rates under strong convexity with explicit dependence on vk trajectories) and broader, carefully controlled experiments would improve significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and appealing conceptual contribution; recovers full-information optimal sampling and is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm with interpretable structure.
- Demonstrated gains for CD on standard datasets.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical inconsistencies (definition of V, minimax justification, dimensional errors) and many presentation/notation issues.
- Complexity and practicality concerns for large, sparse problems; lack of rigorous sparsity-aware analysis.
- Experimental setup lacks critical baselines and ablations; some choices are questionable (binarized features, fixed λ).
- Incomplete and messy references.

Recommendation
Reject. The core idea is promising, but the paper in its current form has too many technical and presentation flaws, and the empirical evaluation is not yet convincing enough. I encourage the authors to (i) fix the mathematical inconsistencies and provide a rigorous minimax justification; (ii) clean up notation and algorithms; (iii) provide sparsity-aware complexity and a careful experimental study with stronger baselines and ablations. I would be enthusiastic to see a polished revision.
