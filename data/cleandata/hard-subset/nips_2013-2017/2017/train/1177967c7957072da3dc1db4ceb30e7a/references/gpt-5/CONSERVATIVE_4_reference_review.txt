Summary
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It first revisits the “full-information” optimal sampling that uses current gradient magnitudes (for CD: probabilities proportional to √Li|∇i f|; for SGD: proportional to ‖∇fi‖2), and shows it can substantially improve one-step progress relative to fixed (Li-based or uniform) sampling. The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive scheme for the limited-information setting: maintain per-coordinate lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (or component gradient norms), and choose sampling probabilities by solving a min–max problem that optimizes worst-case progress over all gradients consistent with the bounds. The resulting probabilities p̂ minimize maxc∈[ℓ,u] V(p,c)/‖c‖^2, with V(p,c) = ∑i Li c_i^2 / p_i. The paper proves: (i) p̂ is worst-case optimal given the bounds, (ii) p̂ never worse than fixed Li-based sampling under the quadratic model, and (iii) an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting plus thresholding) to compute p̂. They also propose a stepsize α = 1/vk where vk is the optimal value of the min–max. They describe how to maintain “safe” bounds in GLMs using simple curvature arguments and Cauchy–Schwarz, and present experiments on several datasets showing strong gains for CD and minor gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on established results on non-uniform sampling for CD (Richtárik & Takáč; Nesterov; Qu et al.), adaptive or gap-based sampling (Perekrestenko et al.; Csiba et al.), gradient-based sampling for SGD (Zhao & Zhang; Needell et al.), and Gauss–Southwell-type rules (Nutini et al.). The full-information solutions align with known optimality under quadratic models. The novelty is the robust min–max formulation using interval bounds and the efficient solver, with a guarantee “not worse than fixed sampling” under the same surrogate model.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: the min–max formulation cleanly captures “best robust” probabilities given uncertainty sets and yields interpretable structure (thresholding solution).
- The O(n log n) algorithm based on sorting and a self-consistent threshold appears correct and practical; the characterization c_i = clamp(√Li m, ℓ_i, u_i) is elegant.
- Solid one-step progress analysis under the standard quadratic upper bound model; p̂ dominates fixed Li-based sampling in that model.
- Empirical results for CD indicate clear iteration and (some) wall-clock improvements, even with very loose bounds, suggesting robustness.
- The method is generic and can be plugged into CD and SGD; step-size coupling with vk is principled.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical guarantees are limited to the surrogate quadratic model and expected one-step progress; there is no end-to-end convergence rate showing improved constants over fixed sampling for general convex problems under the proposed bounded-information scheme. Appendix claims aside, the paper does not present a clear theorem stating overall complexity improvements versus uniform/fixed under realistic assumptions about bound quality.
- The “safe bounds” maintenance is only sketched. For general smooth losses (e.g., logistic), the proposed bounds via Cauchy–Schwarz and unknown Hessian terms can be extremely loose; the practical tightness, cost to maintain, and conditions ensuring validity are not quantified. For nonsmooth composite objectives (e.g., L1), assumptions and handling (subgradients vs gradients) are under-specified.
- Computational overhead is nontrivial: O(n) to update bounds and O(n log n) to compute p̂ per iteration. The claim that this is negligible because computing one coordinate gradient costs Θ(d) is not convincing in common sparse high-dimensional settings where coordinate updates are O(nnz of a column) and can be very cheap. The experiments do not systematically quantify overhead across sparsity regimes. Some timing plots are on different machines, making comparisons hard to interpret.
- Experimental scope is limited: comparisons omit key adaptive baselines (e.g., Gauss–Southwell, gap-based CD sampling, adaptive SDCA probabilities, approximate steepest CD). Using an “optimal” full-information oracle is informative but not a competing method. For SGD, improvements are minor; the paper does not analyze why or provide settings where SGD benefits materially.
- The competitive ratio bounds (e.g., ρ ≤ γ^4 under relative accuracy) are loose and not actionable; no empirical study of ρ or wk is provided.
- Clarity and rigor: several derivations are deferred; the critical min–max interchange used in inequality (*) requires conditions (convex–concave structure) that are not clearly stated for V(p,c)/‖c‖^2 over a box; please justify via Sion/Komiya with precise convexity/compactness arguments. Some notation and typos impede readability.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide a formal convergence theorem for CD with the proposed p̂ and α = 1/vk, giving iteration complexity with explicit constants, and compare to fixed Li-based sampling under reasonable assumptions on bound quality (e.g., relative error).
2. What are sufficient conditions for the min–max interchange in (7)? Please state convexity/compactness properties and cite the exact theorem used with complete assumptions.
3. Quantify and ablate overhead: per-iteration wall-clock breakdown for bound updates, probability computation, and coordinate updates on sparse datasets. When does the overhead negate benefits? How does performance vary with sparsity and n/d?
4. Compare against stronger baselines: Gauss–Southwell, gap-based adaptive CD (Perekrestenko), adaptive SDCA (Csiba et al.), and approximate steepest coordinate descent (Stich et al.). Also include stale-gradient-based adaptive sampling heuristics.
5. Elaborate on handling composite/nonsmooth terms: are gradients replaced by subgradients? Are Lipschitz constants for composite handled via standard proximal CD models?
6. Provide code and hyperparameter details (step sizes for SGD, regularization sweeps) and unify timing experiments on the same hardware.

Pros
- Elegant robust formulation with an efficient algorithm.
- Clear improvement over fixed sampling for CD in experiments.
- Generic and easy to integrate; principled step-size coupling.

Cons
- Guarantees restricted to surrogate one-step model; no clear overall complexity improvement versus fixed sampling.
- Overhead and bound maintenance practicality unclear, especially for sparse problems.
- Limited experimental baselines; modest gains for SGD.
- Some theoretical steps deferred/under-justified; clarity issues.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The robust min–max design and O(n log n) solver are interesting and potentially useful for CD, but the paper lacks comprehensive empirical validation against strong adaptive baselines and does not convincingly demonstrate end-to-end gains with realistic overhead across settings. Strengthening the theoretical guarantees beyond the surrogate model and expanding experiments would make this contribution more compelling.

Confidence
High (familiar with adaptive sampling, coordinate methods, and SGD literature).
