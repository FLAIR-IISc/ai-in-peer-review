

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a novel approach to adaptive importance sampling for optimization algorithms like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The key insight is that while gradient-based adaptive sampling theoretically outperforms fixed sampling, it's computationally inadmissible to compute the full gradient in every iteration. The authors introduce a "safe" method that works with only safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information (which can be efficiently maintained for many machine learning problems), and solves for the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem. The method provably outperforms fixed importance sampling while requiring only O(n log n) computation per iteration.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes theoretically that adaptive sampling can provide speedups of up to O(n) compared to fixed sampling for CD methods. Second, it introduces a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy that efficiently approximates optimal gradient-based sampling without full gradient computation. Third, it demonstrates how this method can be integrated into CD and SGD on structured optimization problems, and provides empirical evidence of its effectiveness on real datasets.

The theoretical foundation is strong, with clear lemmas establishing the properties of the proposed sampling distribution. Theorem 3.2 shows that the proposed sampling is always better than Li-based (fixed) importance sampling, which is a significant theoretical guarantee that previous adaptive sampling methods lacked. The authors also provide a geometric interpretation of their approach and prove that the solution can be computed efficiently in O(n log n) time.

Empirically, the method shows clear advantages over fixed sampling strategies on real-world datasets for CD optimization (with L1 and L2 regularization), with the adaptive sampling converging significantly faster than fixed sampling while incurring only a modest computational overhead. The paper demonstrates that even very loose gradient bounds (obtained via simple Cauchy-Schwarz estimates) are sufficient to yield substantial improvements over fixed sampling.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While adaptive importance sampling has been explored before, previous approaches either required full gradient computation (making them inadmissible) or used approximations without theoretical guarantees. The authors' "safe" approach of formulating the sampling as a convex optimization problem with respect to the bounds provides a theoretically grounded method that is both computationally feasible and provably better than fixed sampling. This represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The algorithms are presented with pseudocode, the notation is consistent, and the experimental setup is appropriate for the claims being made. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the method is less effective for SGD than CD), which demonstrates careful evaluation.

Strengths:
- Novel approach to adaptive sampling with strong theoretical guarantees
- Provably better than fixed importance sampling (a guarantee previous methods lacked)
- Efficient implementation (O(n log n) per iteration)
- Clear empirical validation showing practical benefits
- General applicability to both CD and SGD

Weaknesses:
- The method's effectiveness for SGD is less pronounced than for CD, which could be better explained
- The empirical evaluation is limited to generalized linear models, though the authors claim broader applicability
- Some of the theoretical derivations could be simplified for greater clarity

The significance of this work is high. Large-scale optimization is a fundamental challenge in machine learning, and adaptive sampling is a key technique for improving computational efficiency. The authors' method provides a theoretically grounded approach to adaptive sampling that can be easily integrated into existing optimization frameworks with minimal computational overhead. The results suggest this could become a standard technique in optimization for machine learning.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work in adaptive importance sampling. It's not just a minor improvement to existing methods but a fundamentally new approach to the problem that provides theoretical guarantees where previous methods did not. The authors have successfully bridged the gap between theoretically optimal adaptive sampling and practical implementation by developing a method that is both computationally feasible and provably better than fixed sampling.

Given the high novelty of the approach, strong theoretical guarantees, and clear empirical benefits, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field that will likely be widely adopted in optimization for machine learning. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded to more problem domains, the current results are sufficient to demonstrate the value of the approach. The computational overhead is shown to be small compared to the benefits, making it a practical solution for real-world applications.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental challenge in machine learning optimization with a novel theoretical approach that has immediate practical implications. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a method that is both theoretically sound and practically efficient.
