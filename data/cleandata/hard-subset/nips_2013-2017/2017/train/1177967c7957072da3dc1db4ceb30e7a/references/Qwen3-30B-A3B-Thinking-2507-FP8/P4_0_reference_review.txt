

This paper introduces "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," a method for improving the efficiency of coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms through theoretically sound adaptive sampling. The authors address a key limitation of existing adaptive importance sampling approaches: while theoretically superior to fixed sampling, previous methods often require expensive full gradient computations or use approximations without guarantees of performance improvement over fixed sampling. The paper's core contribution is a novel approach that uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to compute an adaptive sampling distribution that is provably better than fixed importance sampling.

The paper begins by establishing the theoretical benefits of adaptive sampling over fixed strategies (Section 2), demonstrating that optimal gradient-based sampling can yield significant speedups (up to a factor of n in dimension). The authors then formulate the problem of finding the optimal sampling distribution under safe gradient bounds as a convex optimization problem (Section 3), proving that their proposed solution (Algorithm 2) is never worse than fixed importance sampling and can be computed efficiently in O(n log n) time per iteration. The paper further demonstrates how to compute these safe bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs) in Section 4, showing that gradient bounds can be maintained in O(n) time per iteration. Empirical validation on multiple datasets with various loss functions and regularizers (Section 5) demonstrates that the proposed method consistently outperforms fixed sampling in terms of convergence speed and total computation time.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear proofs of key properties (Theorem 3.2, Lemma 3.5, Lemma 3.6) that establish the method's superiority over fixed sampling. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, demonstrating that the overhead of the proposed method is minimal (O(n log n) per iteration, comparable to gradient evaluation costs for many problems). The empirical results are comprehensive and well-presented, covering multiple datasets, loss functions, and regularization types, with clear evidence of the method's practical benefits. The paper also effectively contextualizes its contribution within the literature, distinguishing it from prior work on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., gradient-based sampling methods that require full gradients or use unguaranteed approximations).

The paper's main weaknesses involve scope limitations and empirical comparisons. While the authors demonstrate how safe gradient bounds can be computed for GLMs, the approach's applicability to more complex models (beyond GLMs) is not thoroughly explored, and the paper does not address whether similar bound computation techniques exist for other important machine learning models. Additionally, the empirical results show significantly greater improvements for CD than for SGD, but the paper does not adequately explain this discrepancy or provide a systematic comparison with the most recent related work (e.g., Allen-Zhu et al. 2017 on non-uniform sampling). The safe gradient bounds used in experiments are relatively loose (derived from Cauchy-Schwarz), and it's unclear whether tighter bounds would yield substantially better performance.

The paper's novelty is well-established: it presents a new approach to adaptive importance sampling that provides theoretical guarantees not present in previous methods. The "safe" aspect—using bounds to ensure performance never worse than fixed sampling while still capturing adaptive benefits—represents a significant conceptual advance over prior work. This is a substantial contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning, particularly for large-scale problems where computational efficiency is critical.

The significance of the work is clear: the proposed method provides a practical way to incorporate adaptive sampling into CD and SGD without the computational overhead of full gradient computation. The empirical results demonstrate that this approach leads to faster convergence and lower total computation time, making it immediately applicable to real-world machine learning problems. The method is general enough to be integrated into existing optimization frameworks with minimal modification.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- Strong theoretical guarantees for the proposed method
- Efficient computational implementation with minimal overhead
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and problem types
- Clear distinction from prior work with theoretical advantages
- Practical relevance to large-scale machine learning optimization

Con arguments against acceptance:
- Limited scope of the gradient bound computation method (primarily demonstrated for GLMs)
- Inconsistent performance gains between CD and SGD
- Lack of thorough comparison with the most recent related work

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a substantial contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. It meets NIPS scope by addressing a fundamental challenge in scalable machine learning optimization with theoretical rigor and practical relevance. The method is technically sound, the analysis is thorough, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The paper advances the state of the art in adaptive sampling for optimization algorithms, providing a theoretically grounded approach that is both practical and efficient.
