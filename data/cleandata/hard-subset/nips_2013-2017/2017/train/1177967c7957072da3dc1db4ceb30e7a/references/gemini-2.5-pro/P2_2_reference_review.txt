### Summary

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core problem addressed is the trade-off between static sampling (e.g., uniform or fixed importance sampling), which is computationally cheap but suboptimal, and fully adaptive sampling (e.g., based on the full gradient), which is theoretically superior but computationally prohibitive.

The authors propose a principled way to bridge this gap. Their method assumes access to safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient information (coordinate-wise absolute values for CD, component-wise norms for SGD) at each iteration. Instead of using these bounds heuristically, they formulate the problem of choosing the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem: find the distribution that minimizes the worst-case one-step progress, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradient vectors consistent with the given bounds. This "safe" formulation leads to a key theoretical guarantee: the resulting sampling distribution is provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling. The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this optimization problem and demonstrate how to derive the necessary bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs). Empirical results on several real-world datasets show that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling and approaches the performance of the computationally expensive optimal sampling.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The central contribution—formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax problem (Equation 7)—is elegant and theoretically sound. It provides a robust way to handle uncertainty in the gradient information, moving beyond previous heuristic approaches that rely on stale values or simple upper bounds. This is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2) is a major strength. The proof that the proposed sampling `p̂` is always better than or equal to Li-based importance sampling provides strong justification for its use. This "do-no-harm" property is highly desirable and often missing in other adaptive schemes, which can sometimes underperform simpler baselines.

3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The theoretical contribution is complemented by a practical algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the safe sampling distribution. The O(n log n) complexity is efficient, making the overhead acceptable for many problems, particularly when the cost of a coordinate update is non-trivial (e.g., when the feature dimension `d` is large). The paper also provides a concrete and simple method for maintaining the gradient bounds for the important class of GLMs.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established by first analyzing the full-information setting (Section 2) and then moving to the more realistic limited-information setting (Section 3). The contributions are clearly stated and the experimental section is thorough, validating the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The performance of the method is fundamentally tied to the quality of the lower and upper bounds `l` and `u`. The paper demonstrates a simple approach using the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which can yield very loose bounds, especially when data vectors `a_i` are nearly orthogonal. While the experiments show this is sufficient for significant gains, a deeper analysis of the method's sensitivity to bound tightness would be beneficial. For instance, how does the competitive ratio `ρ_k` behave as the ratio `u_i / l_i` grows?

2.  **Computational Overhead in Certain Regimes:** While O(n log n) is efficient, it is still an overhead incurred at *every* iteration. For problems where `n` is very large but the cost of a single update (proportional to `d`) is very small, this per-iteration cost could dominate and negate the gains in convergence rate. The paper briefly acknowledges this by noting the overhead is low when `d = Ω(n)`, but a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs across different `n/d` regimes would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Limited Gains for SGD:** The authors honestly report that the performance gains for SGD are much less pronounced than for CD (Figures 7-9). While this is a valuable empirical finding, the paper lacks a discussion or hypothesis for *why* this might be the case. Is it because the norms of component gradients `||∇f_i(x)||` exhibit less variance than the magnitudes of coordinate gradients `|∇_i f(x)|` for the problems tested? Providing some intuition here would add significant value.

### Detailed Evaluation Against NIPS Criteria

*   **Quality: High.** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs provided in the appendix. The minimax formulation is solid, and the proposed algorithm correctly solves it. The experimental evaluation is carefully conducted and supports the main claims, honestly reporting both strengths (for CD) and relative weaknesses (for SGD). This is a complete and well-executed piece of work.

*   **Clarity: Excellent.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The logical flow from motivation to theory, algorithm, application, and experiments is clear and compelling. The notation is standard and defined properly. Key concepts like the "safe" optimization problem are introduced with clear justification. An expert reader would have sufficient information to reproduce the results.

*   **Originality: High.** The core idea of using a minimax framework to derive a safe sampling distribution from gradient bounds is highly original. It represents a significant step forward from previous adaptive sampling methods that used stale information or upper bounds in a more heuristic fashion [22, 36, 23]. The work is well-situated within the existing literature and its novel contributions are clearly articulated.

*   **Significance: High.** This work addresses a fundamental problem in large-scale optimization. The proposed method is generic, practical, and comes with strong theoretical guarantees, making it highly likely to be used by both researchers and practitioners. The "safety" aspect makes it particularly appealing, as it removes the risk of performance degradation that can deter the adoption of more complex adaptive methods. The framework of operating on safe bounds could also inspire further research into maintaining and utilizing such bounds for other aspects of optimization. The paper clearly advances the state-of-the-art in adaptive sampling for first-order methods.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically sound contribution that is also practical and effective. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and represent opportunities for future work rather than fundamental flaws. The paper meets all the criteria for a NIPS publication and is a clear candidate for acceptance.
