This paper tackles the important problem of adaptive importance sampling, aiming to bridge the gap between the theoretical advantages of adaptive schemes and their practical computational cost. The core idea—to formulate the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem solvable with available information—is genuinely novel and represents a significant step forward. The theoretical analysis demonstrating guarantees relative to uniform and fixed importance sampling is solid, and the connection to gradient-based sampling is well-articulated. The authors successfully identify a practical algorithm (Alg. 2) with a manageable computational overhead (O(n log n)). The empirical evaluation on GLMs is thorough, demonstrating performance gains in both iterations and, importantly, wall-clock time.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of adaptive sampling as a convex optimization problem with bounds is a clever and original contribution. This moves beyond direct approximations of gradient-based sampling and provides a principled way to find a “safe” adaptive distribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis establishing theoretical guarantees—provably better than uniform/fixed sampling—is strong and well-presented.  The connection to existing work on gradient-based sampling is clearly demonstrated.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed algorithm is efficient (O(n log n) per iteration), making it viable for large-scale problems.  The identification of practical bounds in the context of GLMs is a valuable addition.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The empirical evaluation is thorough, including comparisons with both uniform sampling and optimal gradient-based sampling (where feasible).  The results consistently demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Bounding Assumptions:** The performance heavily relies on the quality of the lower and upper bounds (`l` and `u`) on the gradient. While the authors show how to obtain these bounds in the GLM setting, the general applicability to other models depends on the availability of similar bounding techniques. The sensitivity analysis to potentially loose bounds would be valuable.
*   **SGD Performance Gap:** The empirical results for SGD show less dramatic improvements compared to coordinate descent. This discrepancy warrants further investigation. The authors briefly acknowledge this, but a deeper discussion of why the gains are smaller in the SGD setting would be beneficial.
*   **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The analysis of the competitive ratio (rho) provides insight into the worst-case performance, but could be made more concrete with examples.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and significant idea for adaptive importance sampling.
*   The theoretical analysis is strong and provides guarantees on the performance of the proposed method.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach, particularly for coordinate descent.
*   The O(n log n) computational overhead is reasonable and makes the method applicable to large-scale problems.
*   Even with the reliance on bounds, the method provides a principled way to leverage available information for adaptive sampling.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The performance of the method relies heavily on the quality of the gradient bounds.
*   The improvement over uniform sampling is less pronounced in the SGD setting.
*   The competitive ratio analysis could be more detailed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling. The practical algorithm is efficient, and the empirical results are promising. While the reliance on gradient bounds and the smaller gains in SGD are minor concerns, they do not detract significantly from the overall value of the contribution.  I believe this paper represents a significant advance in the field and is worthy of acceptance, prioritizing its novel idea over minor flaws. The paper pushes a new direction and could stimulate further research on safe adaptive sampling strategies beyond the specific bounds considered here.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
