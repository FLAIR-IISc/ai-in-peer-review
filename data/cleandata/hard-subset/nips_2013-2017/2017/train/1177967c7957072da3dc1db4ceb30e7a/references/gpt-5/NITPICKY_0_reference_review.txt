Summary
The paper studies adaptive importance sampling for both coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). It first revisits “full-information” gradient-based sampling, showing that, for CD with coordinate-wise Lipschitz constants Li, choosing p∗ ∝ √Li |∇i f(x)| and a direction-unbiased stepsize γk = αk/pik optimizes a standard quadratic surrogate and can yield up to n-fold one-step improvement over uniform sampling in the isotropic case. The main contribution is a “safe” adaptive scheme usable without full gradient information: maintain per-coordinate lower/upper bounds `k ≤ |ci| ≤ uk on gradient magnitudes (or component gradient norms for SGD), and pick pk by solving a convex min–max problem minp∈Δ maxc∈Ck V(p,c)/||c||2 with V(p,c) = ∑i Li c2i /pi and Ck = {c : `k ≤ c ≤ uk}. This yields stepsize αk = 1/vk, where vk is the optimal value. The authors prove p̂ achieves the best worst-case guarantee w.r.t. the current bounds and is never worse than fixed L-based sampling. They derive an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) for computing p̂ via a thresholding/water-filling construction and show geometric and competitive-ratio properties. They then describe how to maintain safe bounds in GLMs (linear models with separable regularizers), and present experiments on several datasets indicating marked gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of the “safe” sampling problem under interval uncertainty and a principled min–max solution; nice geometric characterization and competitive-ratio discussion.
- Solid derivations in the full-information setting; the p∗ ∝ √L |∇ f| optimality is standard but the presentation connecting to direction-unbiased stepsizes is helpful.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute p̂ from bounds, with an intuitive water-filling structure.
- Practical guidance for GLMs to maintain conservative bounds, and empirical evaluation showing large improvements for CD.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical correctness
- Inconsistency with non-smooth regularizers in CD. Section 2 assumes f has coordinate-wise Lipschitz continuous gradients and uses the update xk+1 = xk − γk ∇ik f(xk) eik. Later, Section 4 and experiments include L1 regularization. For composite (non-smooth) objectives, the CD update is not the gradient step (requires solving a 1D proximal/coordinate subproblem). The proposed stepsize and progress bound (3) no longer apply as stated. This is a major gap: the theory for CD should either be extended to composite objectives (analogous to results in SGD cited for Zhao & Zhang) or the experiments restricted to smooth cases.
- Equation (11) for least-squares GLMs appears incorrect/missing factors. For f(x) = 1/2||Ax − b||2 + ∑ ψi(xi), a coordinate change δ at ik yields ∇i f(xk+1) − ∇i f(xk) = ⟨ai, aik⟩ δ, with δ = −γk ∇ik f(xk). Equation (11) omits the dependence on ∇ik f(xk) and sign. This is critical for computing safe bounds; the update magnitude scales with |∇ik f(xk)|. Please correct and propagate to the bounding logic.
- Misstatement of Hessians: “Using that this h is twice differentiable with ∇2 h(Ax) = In” for least-squares. The Hessian of h(z) = 1/2||z − b||2 is Id in z-space; for f(x)=h(Ax) it is A⊤A in x-space. This confusion between spaces recurs and may lead to incorrect constants.
- Squared hinge loss is not twice differentiable everywhere; the safe-bound derivation (Lemma 4.1 and surrounding text) assumes twice differentiability. The paper should clarify how bounds are maintained through non-differentiable points (e.g., use global Lipschitz of gradients, upper bounds on h'', or subgradient bounds).
- Stepsize ambiguity in Lemma 2.1. The lemma states E[f(xk+1)|xk] ≤ f(xk) − αk(pk) 2 ||∇f(xk)||2 (typesetting unclear). If αk∗ = ||∇||2 / V, then the optimal expected decrease is (1/2) αk∗ ||∇||2. Please fix notation and ensure consistency.
- Complexity claims are optimistic. Computing p̂ each iteration costs O(n log n) plus O(n) to update bounds. In typical sparse high-dimensional GLMs, n (coordinates/features) can be much larger than d (data dimension), and the cost per CD step is O(nnz(ai)) or small. The condition “d = Ω(n)” to make overhead negligible is rarely met in text datasets (e.g., news20 with n ≈ 1.3M features, d ≈ 20k samples). The paper should either amortize p̂ updates (e.g., update every T steps), use partial updates, or provide timing breakdowns. As written, the per-iteration overhead can dominate.
- Limited coverage for SGD. The safe formulation is sketched, but no full algorithm or theory analogous to CD is provided. Empirically, the improvements for SGD are small; significance there is limited.

Clarity
- Notation overload and inconsistencies: n is used as both number of coordinates (CD) and number of samples (SGD); l vs. L vs. √L; reuse of symbols in Algorithm 4 (u and ` as both vectors and indices). Numerous typos (“deep leaning”, “preformance”, “st Conference…”), missing punctuation, and incomplete references degrade readability.
- Several central proofs are deferred to the appendix, yet key correctness issues (composite objectives, bound updates) are left unclear in the main text.

Originality
- The safe min–max formulation and efficient solver are novel and interesting. However, adaptive/non-uniform sampling for CD and SGD, including gap/GS rules and adaptive probabilities (e.g., Nutini et al. 2015; Csiba et al. 2015; Perekrestenko et al. 2017), are established. A more thorough empirical comparison with these stronger adaptive baselines is needed.

Significance
- If the composite-case gap is addressed and the complexity issue mitigated, the approach could be widely useful for CD. As is, the theoretical guarantees apply to smooth f, while experiments emphasize L1/L2-regularized problems and show mainly CD gains. The limited SGD benefits reduce the overall impact.

Detailed questions and suggestions
- Extend the CD analysis to composite objectives with separable ψi, deriving the correct coordinate update and associated progress bound under non-uniform sampling, or restrict experiments to smooth cases.
- Correct Eq. (11) and the Hessian statements; show explicitly how |∇ik f(xk)| enters the bound updates and how you compute safe bounds when only its value for ik is revealed each step.
- Discuss and/or implement amortized updates of p̂ (e.g., recompute every T iterations; maintain heaps for thresholds) to reduce O(n log n) overhead; provide wall-clock breakdowns per iteration across datasets.
- Compare against Gauss–Southwell and duality-gap adaptive CD, and against heuristic gradient-based sampling that reuses stale gradients, to ground the gains.
- Clarify the role of absolute values in p∗ and in the bounds; specify treatment of signs.
- Improve notation and fix numerous typos; complete the references with years and venues.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean safe adaptive sampling formulation with provable dominance over fixed sampling and an efficient solver.
- Strong empirical improvements for CD on several datasets.
- Potentially broadly applicable idea if extended to composite settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Significant methodological gaps for the non-smooth/composite cases used in experiments.
- Technical errors/omissions (Eq. (11), Hessian statement, stepsize notation).
- Overhead analysis not convincing for large n; limited SGD gains; missing comparisons to stronger adaptive baselines.
- Numerous clarity/formatting issues.

Recommendation
Leaning to reject in current form due to the composite-objective mismatch, technical inaccuracies in the gradient update/bounds, and insufficient empirical baselines and complexity justification. The core idea is promising; with corrected theory for composite CD, stronger baselines, and refined implementation addressing overhead, this work could be impactful.
