- Paper studies adaptive importance sampling for CD and SGD, showing √Li |∇i f| sampling with matched stepsize improves one-step decrease and can give up to n-fold speedup with full gradients.
- Main contribution: a “safe” min–max adaptive sampling scheme using per-iteration lower/upper bounds on coordinate gradient magnitudes, optimizing worst-case expected progress.
- The safe distribution is never worse than Li-based sampling in the worst case and equals the full-information optimum when bounds are tight.
- Authors give an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting + thresholding) to compute the sampling distribution and a matched stepsize α = 1/v for the min–max value v.
- Competitive ratio analysis compares the safe scheme to an oracle full-information sampler.
- Practical recipes to maintain safe bounds for GLMs in CD and SGD with O(n) per-iteration updates are provided.
- Experiments show strong gains for CD, smaller gains for SGD, and highlight the importance of the coupled adaptive stepsize.
- Strength: clear theoretical insight optimizing expected progress jointly over p and α, recovering known full-information optima.
- Strength: novel min–max formulation with a provable “never worse than fixed sampling” guarantee under uncertainty.
- Strength: efficient, transparent algorithmic solution with explicit dependence on Li and bounds.
- Strength: practical implementation guidance for GLMs and empirical validation on multiple datasets.
- Strength: honest discussion acknowledging limited SGD gains and emphasizing adaptive stepsize role.
- Weakness: per-iteration computational overhead (O(n) bound updates, O(n log n) sampling) can be substantial when d << n; scalability unclear.
- Weakness: “provably better” claims hinge on non-degenerate/tight bounds; worst-case guarantee is only “never worse,” and early-iteration performance may be loose.
- Weakness: limited empirical baselines—omits Gauss–Southwell variants, gap-based heuristics with stale gradients, accelerated CD with non-uniform sampling, and variance-reduced/modern importance-sampling SGD methods.
- Weakness: SGD benefits are small in experiments; paper should better characterize when the approach helps SGD (e.g., heterogeneity, bound quality).
- Weakness: some derivations and Algorithm 4 are terse; min–max interchange and threshold intuition should be sketched in the main text.
- Suggestion: evaluate less-frequent sampling updates (every M steps) to amortize overhead.
- Suggestion: exploit sparsity for incremental bound updates and report behavior in sparse settings.
- Suggestion: compare to Gauss–Southwell-Lipschitz and gap-safe adaptive sampling baselines.
- Suggestion: clarify min–max interchange assumptions (Sion/Komiya) and give intuition for the threshold structure.
- Suggestion: explore mini-batch variants and importance sampling within batches for SGD.
- For acceptance: solid theoretical contribution, efficient algorithm, strong CD empirical gains, and broadly applicable safe-adaptive idea.
- Against acceptance: overhead may be prohibitive in many regimes, empirical section lacks strong baselines, and some “provably better” claims should be softened.
- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept.
