

# Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

## Summary
This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors argue that gradient-based adaptive sampling can theoretically outperform fixed importance sampling by a factor of the dimension n, but computing it exactly is computationally expensive. Their key contribution is a method to efficiently approximate the optimal sampling distribution using safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information, while guaranteeing the approximation is always better than fixed sampling. The method requires O(n log n) time per iteration, which is comparable to evaluating a single coordinate of the gradient, making it practical for large-scale problems. The paper demonstrates this approach on generalized linear models with L1/L2 regularization, showing significant improvements over fixed sampling.

## Strengths
The paper makes several strong contributions. First, the theoretical foundation is solid, with clear proofs showing that the proposed sampling is provably better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2). The paper also provides a clear geometric interpretation of the optimization problem (Section 3.2), which enhances understanding of the approach. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, with a convincing argument that the O(n log n) overhead is negligible compared to gradient evaluation for typical machine learning problems (Section 4). The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) and loss functions (square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss) to demonstrate the method's effectiveness. The results consistently show that adaptive sampling significantly outperforms fixed sampling in terms of iterations and total computation time.

## Weaknesses
The primary weakness concerns the novelty claim. The paper references Perekrestenko et al. (2017, "Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling"), but does not sufficiently distinguish their approach from this prior work. Both papers address adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent, and Perekrestenko et al. also propose a method for approximating the optimal sampling distribution. The paper should more explicitly compare to this work, explaining why their minimax optimization formulation (Equation 7) represents a meaningful advance over prior approximations. Without this, the novelty is difficult to assess.

The empirical evaluation also has limitations. While the paper demonstrates the method's superiority over fixed importance sampling, it does not compare to other adaptive sampling methods in practice. For instance, it would be valuable to see how their approach performs against the "gradient-based sampling" (Example 2.3) that requires full gradient computation, as well as other practical adaptive methods like those in Csiba et al. (2015) or Zhao and Zhang (2015). The paper states that the optimal sampling "performs only slightly better than uniform sampling" in SGD experiments, but doesn't clarify how this compares to other adaptive methods.

The paper's theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2) are strong but conservative. The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5, 3.6) provides worst-case bounds that are likely much larger than what is observed in practice (as noted in the empirical results). The paper could provide more insight into why the theoretical worst-case bounds are so loose compared to empirical performance.

## Constructive Suggestions
1. Strengthen the novelty argument by explicitly comparing to Perekrestenko et al. (2017) and other related work. Explain precisely how the minimax optimization formulation (Equation 7) differs from previous approaches and why it leads to better practical performance.

2. Add comparative experiments against other adaptive sampling methods. This would better demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach beyond just being better than fixed sampling.

3. Include a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the theoretical worst-case bounds and empirical performance. The paper notes that "the measured value vk was always significantly below the worst case estimate" - exploring why this is the case could provide valuable insights.

4. Extend the discussion of gradient bound computation beyond GLMs. While the paper demonstrates how to compute bounds for GLMs, it would be valuable to discuss how the method could be applied to more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks) or to provide more concrete guidance for practitioners.

## Overall Assessment
This is a well-written paper with a strong theoretical foundation and promising empirical results. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling method addresses a practical problem in large-scale optimization with clear theoretical guarantees and reasonable computational overhead. The work makes a solid contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning.

The primary concern is the novelty claim, which needs to be strengthened through clearer comparison to recent related work. With this addressed, the paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS. The empirical results are compelling, and the method appears to have broad applicability to machine learning problems involving large-scale optimization.

## Recommendation
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of adaptive sampling for optimization, with strong theoretical guarantees and practical results. The minor concerns regarding novelty and experimental comparison can be addressed in the camera-ready version. The method is both theoretically sound and practically relevant, with clear potential for adoption in large-scale machine learning applications.
