This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, aiming to improve computational efficiency and maintain theoretical guarantees. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient entries, formulated as a convex optimization problem solvable in O(n log n) time. The authors demonstrate theoretical improvements over fixed sampling strategies, particularly for CD, and provide empirical validation on several datasets.

While the motivation – reducing computational cost in large-scale machine learning – is sound and the problem well-defined, I find the paper’s contributions incremental and the empirical results not compelling enough to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The theoretical justification, while present, feels largely a re-statement of known benefits of adaptive sampling, and the practical advantages of the proposed method over existing approaches are not convincingly demonstrated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Well-defined Problem:** The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in optimization, especially for large datasets.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The analysis provides theoretical guarantees for the proposed method, showing improvements over fixed sampling. The lemma regarding the speedup factor 'n' in CD is interesting.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm is presented clearly and appears implementable, with computational complexity analysis.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature on importance sampling and coordinate descent.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** The core idea – approximating gradient-based sampling with bounds – isn’t entirely novel. Similar approaches relying on gradient bounds have been explored, although perhaps not with the same convex formulation. The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight how this approach differs significantly from, and improves upon, previous work.
*   **Limited Empirical Benefit:** The empirical results are mixed. While improvements are observed in some cases, the magnitude of the improvement is often modest, and the reported gains are not always consistent across datasets and different loss functions. The SGD results, in particular, are underwhelming, showing only slight improvements over uniform sampling.
*   **Overly Optimistic Assumptions:** The assumption of readily available and easily maintainable "safe" gradient bounds (`, u`) is a significant simplification. While the authors provide a mechanism for tracking these bounds in the GLM setting, it's unclear how well this will generalize to more complex models. The efficiency of maintaining these bounds could easily negate the benefits of the adaptive sampling.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison is mostly against uniform sampling and the ideal gradient-based sampling (which isn't computable). More thorough comparisons against existing adaptive sampling methods (e.g., those mentioned in the introduction, like [22, 5, 23]) are needed to demonstrate the advantage of the proposed scheme.
*   **Clarity of Notation:** While generally clear, some notation (e.g., use of 'l' for both a vector and a scalar) can be confusing.

**Specific Concerns:**

*   The discussion of the "optimal" sampling distribution (Example 2.3) feels somewhat disconnected from the practical algorithm (Algorithm 2). The practical algorithm relies on approximations due to the bound constraints, and the connection between the theoretical optimal distribution and the approximate one is not fully explored.
*   The impact of the accuracy of the bounds (`, u`) on the performance of the algorithm is not adequately investigated. How sensitive is the algorithm to inaccurate bounds?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
*   The algorithm is well-defined and implementable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental and does not represent a substantial advance over existing work.
*   The empirical results are not sufficiently compelling to justify acceptance to NIPS.
*   The reliance on safe gradient bounds may limit the applicability of the method.
*   The lack of comprehensive comparison against strong baselines.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While technically sound and well-written, the limited novelty, modest empirical gains, and reliance on potentially strong assumptions make it a less compelling contribution for NIPS. The paper would be more suitable for a workshop or a conference with a lower acceptance bar. The authors should consider significantly expanding the empirical evaluation, comparing against a wider range of baselines, and more thoroughly analyzing the sensitivity of the algorithm to the accuracy of the gradient bounds. Furthermore, they should better articulate the unique contributions of their approach compared to existing methods for approximating gradient-based sampling.
