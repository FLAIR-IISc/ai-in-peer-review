

# Peer Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

## Summary

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address a key limitation of existing adaptive importance sampling approaches: while theoretically superior to fixed sampling, they require computationally expensive full gradient evaluations. The authors propose a novel method that computes an adaptive sampling distribution using safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, which can be efficiently maintained during optimization. The key theoretical contribution is proving that their proposed sampling distribution is always at least as good as fixed importance sampling, with the potential for significant speedups (up to dimension n) in CD. The paper demonstrates the approach on generalized linear models with L1 and L2 regularization.

The work builds on prior research in adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) and fixed importance sampling (e.g., [18, 19, 2]), but makes a novel contribution by formulating the sampling problem as a convex optimization with safe bounds rather than relying on approximations. This differs from previous approaches that used stale values or upper bounds without theoretical guarantees.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides rigorous proofs that the proposed sampling distribution is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), a guarantee not established in previous adaptive sampling methods. The derivation of the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5, 3.6) is particularly valuable.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The authors demonstrate that the computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration) is small compared to the cost of evaluating a single coordinate gradient (O(d)), making the approach practically feasible. The efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) is well-designed and clearly explained.

3. **Clear Empirical Validation**: The experiments on real-world datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with various loss functions (squared hinge, logistic, square loss) convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits. The results show significant improvements in CD (with L1 and L2 regularization) and more modest but still positive results in SGD.

4. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear explanations of the theoretical contributions, algorithms, and empirical results. The notation is consistent, and the appendix provides complete proofs.

5. **Practical Insight**: The authors provide a concrete method for maintaining safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models (Section 4), showing that these bounds can be updated in linear time O(n) per iteration.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation**: The paper focuses exclusively on generalized linear models with L1/L2 regularization. While these are important applications, the method could be more compelling with experiments on other problem types (e.g., deep learning, non-GLM problems) to demonstrate broader applicability.

2. **Inconsistent Performance in SGD**: The paper notes that adaptive sampling provides less significant improvements in SGD compared to CD (Section 5), but doesn't provide a clear theoretical explanation for this discrepancy. A deeper analysis would strengthen the paper.

3. **Comparison with Alternative Adaptive Methods**: The paper doesn't compare with other recent adaptive sampling methods (e.g., [21, 23, 26]) that might offer better computational efficiency while maintaining theoretical guarantees. This weakens the claim of "safe" superiority.

4. **Computational Complexity Claim**: While the paper claims O(n log n) complexity for the sampling computation, the actual implementation (Algorithm 4) involves sorting operations that might be more expensive in practice than the paper suggests, particularly for very large n.

5. **Theoretical Limitation in SGD**: The paper doesn't establish a theoretical guarantee for the SGD case that matches the strong CD guarantees, which weakens the claim of generality.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the optimization literature. The theoretical guarantees are novel and rigorous, the practical implementation is well-executed, and the empirical results are convincing. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on adaptive importance sampling by providing a method that is both theoretically sound and computationally efficient.

The main weaknesses are the limited scope of the empirical evaluation and the lack of comparison with alternative adaptive sampling methods. However, these do not significantly undermine the core contribution, which is the novel "safe" approach to adaptive importance sampling with provable guarantees.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, important contribution to the optimization literature that will be of interest to the NIPS community. The method is both theoretically sound and practically valuable, and the authors have clearly demonstrated its effectiveness on real-world problems.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:**
- Provides the first theoretically guaranteed adaptive importance sampling method that is computationally efficient
- Demonstrates significant practical improvements in coordinate descent (up to 2x faster convergence)
- The computational overhead is minimal (O(n log n) vs. O(d) for gradient evaluation)
- The method is generic and can be applied to both CD and SGD

**Con:**
- Limited empirical validation (only GLMs tested)
- Less pronounced improvements in SGD compared to CD
- Doesn't compare with all relevant adaptive sampling methods
- Theoretical guarantee for SGD is weaker than for CD

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The theoretical contribution is novel and important, and the practical implementation is well-executed. The paper advances the state of the art in adaptive importance sampling by providing a method that is both theoretically sound and computationally efficient.
