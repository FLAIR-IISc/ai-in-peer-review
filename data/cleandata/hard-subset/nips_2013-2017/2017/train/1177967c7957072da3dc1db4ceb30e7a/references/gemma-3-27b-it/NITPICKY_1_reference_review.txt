This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The central idea is to formulate the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem, leveraging available safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to avoid the computational burden of full gradient computations. The authors provide theoretical guarantees on the performance of their scheme, demonstrating improvements over fixed importance sampling and connections to gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting. Empirical evaluations on GLMs corroborate the theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a clear theoretical framework for adaptive importance sampling, with provable guarantees on its performance relative to uniform and fixed importance sampling. The analysis of CD methods, demonstrating a potential speed-up of a factor of the dimension `n`, is particularly noteworthy.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed scheme addresses a key challenge in adaptive sampling – computational cost. By relying on safe gradient bounds, the algorithm avoids the need for full gradient computations, making it applicable to large-scale problems.
*   **Well-Defined Algorithm:** Algorithm 2 provides a concrete and efficient method for computing the adaptive sampling distribution, with a clear analysis of its computational complexity (O(n log n)).
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on GLMs demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing improvements in both iteration count and runtime compared to fixed importance sampling.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of existing work on importance sampling, coordinate descent, and stochastic gradient methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notation and Presentation:** The notation is occasionally cumbersome and inconsistent. For example, the use of both `Li` and `L` to represent Lipschitz constants (coordinate-wise and global respectively) is confusing. The equation numbering seems off, with (1) appearing before the introduction of the notation used within it. Also, the paper could benefit from better visual aids (e.g. diagrams, illustrations of algorithms) to clarify complex concepts.
*   **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** While the authors acknowledge the importance of obtaining safe gradient bounds, the discussion of how to do so in practice feels somewhat cursory. The statement "We postpone the discussion… to Section 4" feels weak, as Section 4 simply describes how to *derive* them for GLMs, rather than a broader discussion of applicability. The effectiveness of the proposed scheme hinges on the quality of these bounds, and a more in-depth analysis of their impact would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical evaluation focuses exclusively on GLMs. While this provides a good starting point, it would be valuable to demonstrate the performance of the proposed scheme on a wider range of optimization problems and datasets, including non-convex and more complex machine learning models.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** There are some minor technical inconsistencies and typos throughout the paper. For instance, the phrase "directionally-unbiased updates" feels like jargon and could be more explicitly defined. The statement in the proof of Lemma 2.1 ("for every direction u ∈ Rn and Lu = L") is awkwardly worded.
*   **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** While the analysis of the competitive ratio (ρk) is interesting, its practical interpretation is not fully clear. The conditions under which ρk is bounded by γ4 seem restrictive, and a more detailed discussion of the implications of this result would be helpful.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling.
*   Addresses a practical challenge in adaptive sampling – computational cost.
*   Provides concrete algorithm with clear complexity analysis.
*   Empirically validated on relevant machine learning tasks.

**Con:**

*   Cumbersome notation and presentation.
*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Reliance on safe gradient bounds, with limited discussion of their acquisition.
*   Minor technical inconsistencies and typos.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The theoretical results are strong, and the proposed algorithm is well-defined and efficient. However, the paper suffers from some shortcomings in terms of presentation, clarity, and experimental scope. While the core ideas are sound, the numerous minor flaws detract from the overall impact of the work. I lean towards **reject** due to the pervasive, albeit small, imperfections. A revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly focusing on clarifying the notation, expanding the experimental evaluation, and providing a more thorough discussion of gradient bound acquisition, would significantly improve the quality of the paper. The volume of minor issues suggests insufficient polishing before submission.
