This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling efficiently by computing safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients and solving a convex optimization problem to determine the sampling distribution. The authors provide theoretical guarantees demonstrating improvements over uniform sampling and empirical validation on GLMs.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, proposed method, theoretical analysis, and experimental results. It builds on existing work in adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 5, 23]) and addresses limitations of previous approaches, such as computational cost and theoretical guarantees. The connection to existing literature is well established through citations. The notation is well-defined, and the mathematical derivations appear sound, though a detailed verification would require significant effort. The inclusion of both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation strengthens the contribution. The paper effectively highlights the practical benefits of the proposed approach through experiments on standard datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides theoretical guarantees showing that the proposed adaptive sampling scheme is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed scheme aims to address the computational cost of gradient-based sampling by using safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients.
*   **Generality:** The method is applicable to both CD and SGD methods, broadening its potential impact.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed scheme on real-world datasets.
*   **Safe Sampling Guarantee**: The 'safe' property provides a strong practical advantage, ensuring performance doesn't degrade below fixed sampling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors claim the overhead is small, implementing and maintaining the safe gradient bounds (`, u`) could be non-trivial for complex models or data distributions. The efficiency argument depends on `d` being sufficiently large relative to `n`.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on generalized linear models. It would be beneficial to evaluate the performance of the proposed scheme on more complex machine learning models and datasets, particularly deep learning models.
*   **SGD Performance:** The experiments show only a modest improvement in SGD performance compared to uniform sampling. Further investigation is needed to understand why the performance gains are less pronounced for SGD.
*   **Dependence on Safe Bounds:** The performance is directly tied to the quality of the safe bounds. A discussion on the sensitivity of the method to inaccurate bounds would be helpful.
*   **Algorithm 4 complexity:** While O(n log n) is stated, the practical impact of this overhead is not fully explored.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in machine learning—efficiently reducing the computational cost of optimization algorithms.
*   The proposed method is theoretically sound and provides provable guarantees on performance.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed scheme on standard datasets.
*   The "safe" sampling aspect is a crucial practical contribution.
*   The paper contributes to the growing body of work on adaptive importance sampling.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   The implementation complexity of maintaining safe bounds may limit its practical applicability in some scenarios.
*   The modest improvement in SGD performance raises concerns about its generalizability.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed adaptive importance sampling scheme is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically validated. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is sufficiently novel and significant to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The theoretical guarantees and the 'safe' sampling property are particularly compelling.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. A minor revision request to add a discussion on the sensitivity to bounds quality and the practical implications of the O(n log n) complexity of Algorithm 4 would be beneficial. Further evaluation on more complex datasets could also strengthen the paper.
