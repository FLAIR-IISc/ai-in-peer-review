- Paper introduces a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for Coordinate Descent and SGD that uses gradient bounds instead of full gradients.  
- Core claim: the scheme provably outperforms uniform or fixed importance sampling while remaining computationally efficient.  
- The authors provide theoretical analysis showing speedups in CD and formulate the optimal sampling distribution.  
- Empirical validation on benchmark datasets (GLMs) shows consistent improvements over fixed sampling and competitive performance versus optimal sampling.  
- Method requires only readily available gradient bounds (lower/upper), avoiding expensive full-gradient computations.  
- Computational complexity for computing the sampling distribution is stated as O(n log n) per iteration.  
- The approach is applicable to multiple losses (squared, hinge, logistic) and regularizers (L1, L2), increasing generality.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly notated, and includes helpful algorithmic breakdowns (Algorithms 1–4).  
- Lemma 2.1 and the discussion of the optimal sampling distribution are particularly insightful and theoretically solid.  
- Weakness: performance depends on the quality/tightness of the gradient bounds, and practicality in complex settings is not fully explored.  
- Weakness: limited empirical comparisons to other adaptive sampling methods (e.g., prior gradient-based techniques).  
- Weakness: competitive ratio discussion (Section 3.2) feels disconnected from experiments and its conditions aren’t empirically assessed.  
- Weakness: complexity claim needs qualification—when d (feature dimension) is large, gradient computation (Θ(d)) may dominate and invalidate the O(n log n) overhead claim.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a significant trade-off in stochastic optimization, offers a novel theoretically sound solution, is generally applicable and computationally efficient, and shows strong empirical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on gradient bounds without broad practicality analysis, limited direct empirical comparison to adaptive baselines, and some theory not grounded in experiments.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with significant potential impact; strengths outweigh weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Required revisions: expand discussion on obtaining/approximating gradient bounds and sensitivity to their tightness; add direct empirical comparison to at least one adaptive sampling method; clarify complexity assumptions regarding d vs. n.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
