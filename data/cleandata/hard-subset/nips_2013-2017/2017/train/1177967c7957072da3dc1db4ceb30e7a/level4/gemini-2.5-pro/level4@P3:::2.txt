### **Summary**

This paper addresses the challenge of making adaptive importance sampling practical for large-scale optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). While adaptive strategies based on full gradient information are known to be highly effective, they are computationally prohibitive. The authors propose a "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling" scheme that approximates the optimal gradient-based sampling by using efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes.

The core idea is to formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax problem: find the sampling distribution that minimizes the expected one-step progress objective in the worst case, over all possible gradients consistent with the known bounds. The paper presents an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem. A key theoretical contribution is proving that the resulting sampling distribution is guaranteed to be better than or equal to standard fixed importance sampling strategies (e.g., sampling proportional to Lipschitz constants), thus making the method "safe." The authors demonstrate how to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide extensive empirical evidence showing that their method significantly accelerates convergence in both iterations and wall-clock time compared to non-adaptive methods.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Principled Approach:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization (Equation 7) is a novel and elegant way to handle the uncertainty inherent in using gradient bounds. It provides a principled foundation for deriving an adaptive sampling scheme, moving beyond heuristics or approximations that lack guarantees.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The "safety" guarantee in Theorem 3.2 is a significant strength. Proving that the proposed adaptive scheme is never worse than the widely-used Li-based importance sampling addresses a critical weakness of many adaptive methods, which can sometimes underperform simpler baselines. This makes the proposed method a reliable drop-in improvement.

3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the safe sampling distribution. Crucially, the authors also show in Section 4 how to maintain the necessary gradient bounds for an important class of problems (GLMs) with only O(n) overhead per iteration. This demonstrates that the overall approach is computationally feasible and practical for real-world applications.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experiments are extensive, covering multiple datasets, loss functions (square, squared hinge, logistic), and regularizers (L1, L2) for both CD and SGD. The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over fixed sampling, not only in iteration count but also in wall-clock time, confirming that the computational overhead is justified by the faster convergence.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Presentation of Figures:** A major issue with the current manuscript is the quality of the figures. All figures (2-9) are rendered at a very low resolution, making them completely unreadable. The axes, labels, legends, and data plots are indistinguishable. To properly evaluate the empirical claims, it is essential that these figures are corrected. I suggest increasing their size and ensuring all text is legible, possibly moving some results to the appendix if space is a concern.

2.  **Overall Complexity Analysis:** The paper provides a per-iteration complexity analysis (O(n log n) for sampling + O(n) for bound updates). However, it does not provide an analysis of the *total* computational complexity required to reach an ε-optimal solution. It would significantly strengthen the paper to include a discussion on how the per-iteration overhead impacts the overall convergence rate in terms of total computation. For example, if the proposed sampling reduces the required number of iterations by a factor greater than `log(n)`, the method provides a net win. A formal statement or even a detailed discussion of this trade-off would be very valuable.

3.  **Clarity of Comparison to Uniform Sampling:** Theorem 3.2(ii) establishes superiority over Li-based sampling. While Remark 3.3 correctly notes that this implies superiority over uniform sampling when all `Li` are equal, this important special case could be highlighted more prominently. A simple corollary or a more explicit statement would make the theoretical contribution clearer, as uniform sampling is arguably the most common baseline.

### **Minor Comments**

*   **Typo:** On Line 85, in the definition of `[p*k]_i`, the denominator contains `∇f(x)`. For consistency, this should likely be `∇f(xk)`.
*   **Notation:** On Line 135, the solution to the optimization problem is denoted `(p̂, ĉ)`. For consistency with the context of the `k`-th iteration, it might be clearer to use `(p̂k, ĉk)`, though the current notation is also understandable.

### **Final Assessment**

This paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and practically useful contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The "safe" formulation of adaptive importance sampling is novel and the associated guarantees are a significant improvement over prior work. Despite the major issue with the figures' legibility, the technical content of the paper is strong. Assuming the presentation issues can be easily fixed, the paper is a clear contribution to the NIPS community.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and principled minimax formulation for adaptive sampling.
*   Strong "safety" guarantee (provably better than fixed sampling), which is a key advantage.
*   Efficient algorithm and clear demonstration of applicability to important problem classes (GLMs).
*   Convincing empirical results (assuming they can be made visible).

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The figures are currently unreadable, which severely hinders the evaluation of the experimental section. This must be fixed.
*   The lack of an overall complexity analysis (combining per-iteration cost with convergence rate) leaves an important theoretical question unanswered.
