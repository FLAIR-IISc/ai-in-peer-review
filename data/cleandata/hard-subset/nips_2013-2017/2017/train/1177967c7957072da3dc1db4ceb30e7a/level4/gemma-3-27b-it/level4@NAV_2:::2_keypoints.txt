- Core contribution: proposes adaptive importance sampling for CD and SGD using safe bounds to approximate gradient-based sampling, addressing computational infeasibility.
- Theoretical guarantee: Theorem 3.2 (Conclusion 2) shows provable improvement over L_i-based sampling and approximation to gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting.
- Practical relevance: method reduces the computational cost of adaptive sampling, making it applicable to larger problems.
- Generality: the sampling scheme is generic and easily integrated into both coordinate descent and stochastic gradient descent.
- Empirical validation: extensive experiments across datasets and regularization settings support the theoretical claims.
- Presentation: paper is well-structured and clearly connects to prior work.
- Strength: strong theoretical analysis (proofs and lemmas) underpinning the method.
- Strength: practical impact due to making adaptive sampling computationally feasible.
- Strength: broad applicability increases potential impact.
- Weakness: figures are small and poorly labeled, impairing result interpretation; suggest moving some to the appendix for larger display.
- Weakness: lacks a direct comparison to uniform sampling (p = 1/n), which would aid intuition and completeness.
- Weakness: complexity analysis is incomplete—need total-cost quantification including O(n) updates of l_k and u_k to reach ε-optimality.
- Minor issues: typo in Line 85 (denominator should be ‖x_k‖) and inconsistent notation in Line 135 (remove subscript k from p̂ and ĉ).
- Pro-accept: addresses a significant optimization problem, is theoretically sound, general, empirically supported, and well-written.
- Con-accept: small unclear figures, missing uniform-sampling comparison, and insufficient complexity discussion limit assessment of practical implications.
- Overall: strong paper with significant impact; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address figures and complexity analysis.
