This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of optimization, specifically concerning adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core idea – approximating gradient-based sampling with a safe, efficiently computable scheme – is well-motivated, and the theoretical results, particularly Conclusion 2 in Theorem 3.2 demonstrating improvement over L<sub>i</sub>-based sampling, are promising. The paper is generally well-structured and the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature. However, several points could be addressed to improve clarity and impact.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The authors address the challenge of efficiently implementing adaptive importance sampling, which, while theoretically attractive, often suffers from computational infeasibility. They propose a novel sampling strategy based on safe bounds on the gradient, proving that it is always superior to uniform or fixed importance sampling and can be efficiently computed. The method is shown to be applicable to both CD and SGD, with extensive numerical testing demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme offers a practical approach to a problem that has previously lacked efficient solutions.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees, demonstrating the superiority of the proposed method over existing approaches. The connection to optimal gradient-based sampling is particularly compelling.
* **Generality:** The method is presented as a generic improvement applicable to both CD and SGD, broadening its potential impact.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive numerical testing provides convincing evidence of the scheme's performance on real datasets. The inclusion of comparisons to different stepsize strategies is insightful.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing methods and motivates the need for a more efficient and theoretically sound approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity and Notation:** While generally well-written, some sections could benefit from clearer explanations and more consistent notation. (See specific comments below.)
* **Figure Presentation:** The figures are currently too small and lack essential elements (ticks, labels, numbers), hindering their interpretability. This significantly impacts the assessment of empirical results.
* **Comparison to Uniform Sampling:**  The paper focuses primarily on comparing the proposed method to L<sub>i</sub>-based sampling. A direct comparison to the simpler and widely used uniform sampling (p=1/n) would provide a more complete picture of the benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Complexity Analysis:** The paper lacks a clear discussion of the overall complexity impact of the proposed method. Estimating the total complexity of RCD or SGD with the new sampling scheme (including the cost of updating the bounds l<sub>k</sub> and u<sub>k</sub>) would be valuable.

**Specific Comments:**

* **Line 85:** In the denominator of p<sup>*</sup><sub>i</sub>, it should be x<sub>k</sub>, not x.
* **Line 135:**  Consider removing the subscript k for  p̂ and ĉ for consistency with other notation.
* **Figures:** All figures lack essential elements (ticks, labels, numbers) and are too small to effectively convey information.  Moving some figures to the appendix to allow for larger, clearer presentations is strongly recommended.
* **Theorem 3.2 Comparison:** Providing a result similar to Conclusion 2 in Theorem 3.2, but comparing performance to uniform sampling (p=1/n) instead of L<sub>i</sub>-based sampling, would strengthen the paper's impact.
* **Complexity Analysis:** Please provide an analysis of the total complexity of RCD or SGD incorporating the proposed sampling scheme, including the cost of updating l<sub>k</sub> and u<sub>k</sub> to achieve an epsilon-optimal solution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in optimization with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The proposed method offers a practical solution that is both efficient and provably better than existing alternatives.
* The extensive numerical results provide strong evidence of the scheme's effectiveness.
* The paper is well-written and demonstrates a good understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The figures are currently inadequate and hinder the assessment of empirical results.
* The lack of a direct comparison to uniform sampling and a detailed complexity analysis weakens the paper’s impact.
* Minor clarity issues and inconsistencies in notation need to be addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference. While some revisions are necessary, particularly addressing the issues with the figures and providing a more thorough complexity analysis, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and empirical validation of the proposed method are compelling. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above to improve the clarity and impact of the paper. Specifically, they *must* improve the quality of the figures and provide a discussion of the overall computational complexity.
