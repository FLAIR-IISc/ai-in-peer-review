Summary
This paper proposes a safe and efficient adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate full gradient-based sampling—which is known to be theoretically optimal but computationally infeasible—using per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes. The authors formulate a minimax problem over sampling distributions and gradient vectors consistent with these bounds, and show that the resulting sampling p̂ is optimal in this worst-case sense, strictly better than fixed L-based importance sampling, and never worse than uniform sampling. The method admits an O(n log n) computation via sorting and can be integrated into CD and SGD without computing full gradients. The paper also shows that in the full-information case, adaptive gradient-based sampling yields substantial improvements over fixed sampling (up to a factor n in CD), and provides empirical results on GLMs demonstrating strong gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD. This work relates to prior adaptive sampling for SGD (e.g., gradient-norm-based sampling) and CD (e.g., duality-gap-based sampling), but differs by providing a safe approximation with provable dominance over fixed distributions.

Evaluation
- Quality: The theoretical development is sound. The minimax formulation, its solution (via Theorem 3.4 and Algorithm 4), and the guarantees in Theorem 3.2 are clear and compelling. The empirical evaluation supports the claims, especially for CD. The discussion of how to maintain safe bounds in GLMs is practical.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well organized and easy to follow. However, figures are unreadable at current size, and there are a couple of minor notation inconsistencies (see specific comments). Adding an explicit complexity statement would further improve clarity.
- Originality: The “safe” minimax formulation for adaptive sampling and its efficient solution appear novel relative to prior gradient- or gap-based adaptive schemes.
- Significance: The guarantees that p̂ is provably better than fixed importance sampling and never worse than uniform are significant. The method is generic and should be useful in practice for large-scale CD and SGD.

Pros
- Clear theoretical justification that full-information gradient-based sampling can outperform fixed sampling in CD (up to factor n).
- Novel safe adaptive sampling p̂ with strong guarantees: optimal under the given bounds, better than L-based sampling, and efficiently computable in O(n log n).
- Generic scheme applicable to both CD and SGD; easy to integrate.
- Practical guidance on maintaining safe gradient bounds in GLMs; empirical results confirm benefits for CD.

Cons
- Figures are too small to read; this hampers evaluation of experimental results.
- Minor notation inconsistencies.
- While the paper discusses per-iteration overhead, it lacks an explicit end-to-end complexity bound to reach an ε-optimal solution under the new sampling.
- An explicit comparison to uniform sampling (p = 1/n) paralleling Conclusion 2 of Theorem 3.2 would be helpful.

Specific comments and suggestions
1) Line 85: In the denominator of p∗i, it should be xk rather than x.

2) Line 135: Consider removing the subscript k for p̂ and ĉ at that point for consistency with nearby notation.

3) Figures: I cannot read ticks, labels, numbers, or captions—everything is too small. Please move some plots to the appendix to free space, and enlarge the remaining figures (including axis labels and legends) in the main text.

4) Theorem 3.2, Conclusion 2: This shows p̂ is always better than L-based sampling. Please add a parallel result explicitly comparing against uniform sampling (p = 1/n). While Remark 3.3 covers the special case Li = L (where L-based reduces to uniform), an explicit statement covering uniform sampling in general would be valuable for readers who adopt uniform sampling by default.

5) Overall complexity: Please discuss how the proposed sampling affects the total complexity of CD/SGD to reach an ε-optimal solution. For CD, a bound that incorporates:
- O(n) per-iteration updates to ℓk and uk,
- O(n log n) to compute p̂k (Algorithm 4),
- plus the standard cost to compute the selected coordinate gradient,
would be useful. For example, provide a convergence rate in terms of vk (through α̂k = 1/vk), and translate it into total time to ε-suboptimality including the overhead for maintaining bounds and computing p̂k. Similarly, for SGD, please comment on the end-to-end complexity under your scheme.

Overall recommendation
This is an interesting and well-executed paper with valuable theoretical and practical contributions. Conclusion 2 in Theorem 3.2 is particularly useful, as it guarantees improvement over L-based sampling—a widely used baseline. Addressing the readability of figures, fixing minor notation issues, adding an explicit comparison to uniform sampling, and providing a clear total complexity discussion would further strengthen the paper.
