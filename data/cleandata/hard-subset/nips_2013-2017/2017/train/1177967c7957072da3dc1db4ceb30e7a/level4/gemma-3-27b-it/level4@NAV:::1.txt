The authors introduce a practical adaptive importance sampling method tailored for coordinate descent and stochastic gradient algorithms. Their approach leverages established lower and upper bounds on gradient magnitudes to create an effective approximation of gradient-based sampling. The proposed technique is theoretically demonstrated to be optimal within the defined bounds, consistently outperforming uniform or fixed importance sampling, and can be implemented with minimal computational overhead. While adaptive importance sampling isn’t new, this work uniquely formulates the problem of optimal distribution selection as a convex optimization, accompanied by an efficient solution algorithm. 

This is a well-presented paper and a valuable addition to the literature on importance sampling.

Comments:

The proof of Lemma 2.1 appears to be missing a factor of 2 in the definition of α*. 
Example 3.1’s demonstration feels slightly unclear. Given the maximization objective in (7), setting *c* to either the upper or lower bound seems preferable to uniform sampling. The example’s intention to illustrate this point could be more clearly articulated, as it currently contrasts with the preceding statement suggesting that directly using the bounds can be suboptimal.
Line 4 of Algorithm 4’s condition – given that *m* is defined as the maximum value in *l<sup>sort</sup>*, it’s unclear if this condition would ever be met.
Line 7 of Algorithm 4 – should this reference *u<sup>sort</sup>* instead of *c<sup>sort</sup>*?
The numerical evaluation would be strengthened by comparisons to existing adaptive sampling strategies (e.g., [2], [5],[21]) and to fixed importance sampling utilizing a non-uniform distribution.
Including timing results for the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) experiments would provide a more complete assessment of the method's performance.
The paper lists an incorrect title for reference [14]. 
Please include the following relevant work: Csiba and Richtarik, "Importance Sampling for Minibatches" (2016) – available on arXiv.
