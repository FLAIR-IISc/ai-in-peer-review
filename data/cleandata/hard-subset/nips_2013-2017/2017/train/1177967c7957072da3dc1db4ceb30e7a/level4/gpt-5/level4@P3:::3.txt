Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for stochastic first-order methods, with a focus on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate the ideal gradient-based sampling—which is often computationally infeasible—using per-coordinate (or per-component) safe lower and upper bounds on gradients or gradient norms. Given these bounds, the authors solve a min-max problem to obtain the sampling distribution that is provably optimal in the worst case over the uncertainty set. They further couple the sampling with a compatible stepsize choice α = 1/v, where v is the value of the min-max problem, yielding a directionally unbiased update and improved one-step progress bounds. They show that:
- with full information, their choice recovers the known gradient-based sampling (and in CD can yield up to an n-fold speed-up over uniform sampling);
- under bounds, their “safe” distribution is guaranteed to be at least as good as fixed importance sampling (Li-based) and uniform sampling; and
- the sampling distribution can be computed efficiently in O(n log n) time via a sorting-based routine.

They also show how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs), and present experiments on several text datasets demonstrating substantial gains for CD and more modest gains for SGD.

This work builds on and unifies several lines of research: non-uniform/importance sampling for CD and SGD [e.g., Nesterov; Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.], gradient-based/adaptive sampling [Zhao & Zhang; Zhu], duality-gap and Gauss-Southwell-type strategies [Nutini et al.; Perekrestenko et al.], and safe screening ideas [Ndiaye et al.; Liu et al.]. The novelty here is the explicit min-max formulation that yields the best sampling distribution consistent with safe bounds, along with an efficient algorithm and guarantees that it dominates fixed importance sampling.

Quality
- Theory: The analysis is careful and largely sound. The one-step progress bound, the optimal distribution under full information, and the safe min-max formulation are well motivated. The use of Sion’s minimax theorem (and convexity/compactness assumptions) to justify swapping min and max is appropriate; it would help to make the precise conditions explicit in the main text. The competitive ratio analysis is informative, although the lower-bounding subproblem can be non-convex and is used mainly for interpretability.
- Algorithms and complexity: The O(n log n) computation of the safe distribution via sorting, combined with O(n) updates of gradient bounds, is compelling when per-iteration gradient cost is Θ(d) with d = Ω(n). The paper appropriately notes cases where overhead might matter; an empirical breakdown of time per iteration would strengthen the case.
- Experiments: CD results show clear improvements in both iteration complexity and wall-clock time; SGD gains are smaller, which the authors acknowledge. The experimental suite is relevant (GLMs with L1/L2, common datasets). Additional ablations (e.g., sensitivity to bound tightness, overhead vs. d/n, effect of better inner-product bounds than Cauchy–Schwarz) would further bolster the empirical claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with helpful intuition (e.g., geometric view of the min-max solution). However:
- Figures in Section 5 are very hard to read. Please enlarge the plots and font sizes and ensure labels and legends are legible.
- Fix minor typos and wording (e.g., “preformance”→“performance”; “compute cost”→“computational cost”; clarify “l.- and u.-bounds” as lower/upper bounds).
- Summarize the assumptions for the minimax interchange in the main text.
- Briefly sketch Algorithm 4’s correctness and clarify the role of m and the decision set D in the main body.

Originality
The safe min-max formulation that yields the best sampling over an uncertainty set of gradients, with a sorting-based exact solver and guarantees relative to Li-based/uniform sampling, is novel to my knowledge. The coupling with a distribution-dependent stepsize for CD also adds a useful, principled step-size policy. The work clearly distinguishes itself from prior adaptive strategies that either require full gradients or use heuristic bounds without worst-case optimality guarantees.

Significance
The approach is broadly applicable and easy to integrate into CD and SGD methods. For CD, the empirical and theoretical gains are substantial and practically meaningful. For SGD, the improvements are less pronounced in the reported settings, but the framework remains attractive, and could benefit more when gradient norms are highly skewed or when better bounds are available. The “safety” guarantee—never worse than fixed importance sampling given the same bounds—is appealing for practitioners.

Pros
- Clear, principled min-max formulation; exact, efficient solver (O(n log n)).
- Provable dominance over Li-based/uniform sampling under the same information.
- Recovers gradient-based sampling with full information; compelling CD speed-ups.
- Generic and easy to integrate; practical bound maintenance for GLMs.
- Solid mix of theory and experiments; useful geometric intuition.

Cons
- Experimental figures are difficult to read; limited ablations on overhead and bound tightness.
- SGD gains are modest; comparisons to other adaptive/importance-weighted SGD baselines could be expanded.
- Assumptions enabling the minimax swap and step-size coupling could be stated more explicitly.
- The O(n log n) overhead may be non-negligible when d << n or for extremely sparse updates; more profiling would help.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enlarge all plots and labels; provide clearer legends and axes.
- Add time-per-iteration breakdowns showing the overhead of computing p̂ vs. gradient computation for different d/n regimes and sparsity levels.
- Include an ablation on the tightness of bounds (e.g., exact inner products vs. Cauchy–Schwarz) and its effect on vk and convergence.
- For SGD, compare against additional adaptive baselines (e.g., importance-weighted minibatching, SVRG/SAGA with non-uniform sampling) or settings with greater gradient norm heterogeneity.
- Summarize the assumptions of Sion’s theorem used, and provide a short proof sketch in the main text for the interchange of min and max.
- Clarify memory requirements for storing bounds and any precomputed quantities.

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated and technically solid paper that offers a practical and principled way to reap the benefits of adaptive importance sampling without full gradient information, with strong results for coordinate descent and reasonable generality. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarity issues (especially figures) and, if possible, adding small ablations to quantify overhead and bound tightness.
