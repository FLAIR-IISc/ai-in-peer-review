## Review of “Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling”

This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling with a method based on safe bounds on the gradient, ensuring theoretical guarantees and computational efficiency. The paper demonstrates that the proposed sampling is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling and can be integrated into existing algorithms with minimal overhead. Overall, this is a technically sound and potentially impactful contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The authors address the challenge of efficiently implementing adaptive importance sampling, which is theoretically appealing but often computationally intractable due to the need for full gradient information. They propose a method that leverages safe bounds on gradients to approximate the optimal sampling distribution. This approximation is provably better than static sampling strategies and can be computed efficiently, particularly when integrated with CD and SGD. The paper provides theoretical analysis demonstrating improvements over fixed sampling (up to a factor of the dimension *n* for CD) and extensive numerical validation on real datasets. The key contributions include theoretical justification of adaptive sampling, a practical and efficient approximation algorithm, and empirical demonstration of its effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis, with clear proofs and lemmas supporting the claims. The connection between gradient-based sampling and the proposed approximation is well-established.
* **Novelty:** The approach of using safe bounds to approximate gradient-based sampling is a novel and practical contribution. It addresses a key limitation of existing adaptive methods.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees, demonstrating that the proposed sampling scheme is always better than uniform or fixed importance sampling.
* **Practicality:** The algorithm is designed to be efficiently computable and readily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations. The discussion of computational complexity and practical considerations is valuable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on various datasets and with different loss functions support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are clear and concise.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to Uniform Sampling:** While a comparison to L<sub>i</sub>-based sampling is provided (Conclusion 2 in Theorem 3.2), a direct comparison with uniform sampling (p=1/n) would further strengthen the results and provide a clearer baseline for evaluating the gains achieved.
* **Figure Clarity:** The figures are quite small and lack clear labels, ticks, and numbering, hindering easy interpretation of the results. Moving some details to the appendix would allow for larger, more readable figures.
* **Complexity Analysis:** A more detailed analysis of the total computational complexity of CD and SGD with the proposed sampling method, including the overhead of updating the bounds `l<sub>k</sub>` and `u<sub>k</sub>`, would be beneficial. Providing a concrete estimate of the total complexity for achieving an ε-optimal solution would be particularly valuable.
* **Minor Typographical Errors:** There are minor typos, such as in Line 85 (denominator should be x<sub>k</sub>, not x). These should be addressed during the revision process.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in optimization for machine learning: the efficient implementation of adaptive importance sampling.
* The proposed method is theoretically sound, novel, and demonstrably improves upon existing techniques.
* The empirical results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the algorithm.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the ideas and contributions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of a direct comparison to uniform sampling weakens the evaluation, although the comparison to L<sub>i</sub>-based sampling is informative.
* The small and unclear figures hinder the clear communication of the experimental results.
* The absence of a detailed complexity analysis limits the understanding of the practical implications of the proposed method.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution that warrants acceptance to NIPS. The proposed method is a valuable addition to the toolkit for optimization in machine learning, and the theoretical and empirical results are compelling. Addressing the weaknesses mentioned above – particularly improving the figures and providing a more detailed complexity analysis – would further strengthen the paper.

**Specific Feedback:**

* **Line 85:** Correct the denominator in the expression for p^*<sub>i</sub> to x<sub>k</sub>.
* **Line 135:** Consider removing the subscript k from  p̂ and ĉ for consistency.
* **Figures:**  Enlarge the figures, add clear labels to axes, include tick marks, and provide informative captions. Consider moving less critical details to the appendix.
* **Experimentation:** Include results comparing the performance to uniform sampling (p=1/n).
* **Complexity Analysis:** Provide a detailed analysis of the total computational complexity of CD and SGD with the proposed sampling method, including the overhead of updating the bounds.



**Confidence:** 8/10 (Strongly Accept with Minor Revisions)
