

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address a key challenge in adaptive sampling: while gradient-based adaptive sampling theoretically outperforms fixed sampling, it is computationally infeasible due to the need for full gradient computation in each iteration. The authors propose an efficient approximation that uses safe bounds on gradient information to compute a sampling distribution that is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling.

The paper builds upon and extends several important lines of work at NIPS and elsewhere. It relates to the growing literature on adaptive sampling for optimization algorithms (e.g., [22, 23, 36, 37]), which has shown that adaptive strategies can significantly outperform static sampling. The paper also connects to the work on non-uniform sampling for coordinate descent [2, 5, 18, 19] and stochastic gradient methods [6, 7, 16, 25, 27, 33]. However, unlike previous approaches that rely on approximations that might be worse than uniform sampling [36, 37], the authors' method provides theoretical guarantees that their approximation is always better than fixed importance sampling.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) It establishes theoretical advantages of adaptive sampling over fixed sampling in CD methods, showing potential speed-ups proportional to the problem dimension; (2) It proposes a safe, efficient adaptive sampling scheme that can be integrated into both CD and SGD; (3) It demonstrates practical implementation on structured optimization problems; and (4) It provides extensive numerical validation.

The paper's strengths are numerous. The theoretical contributions are well-motivated and rigorously proven, particularly the key result in Theorem 3.2 that the proposed sampling is provably better than fixed importance sampling (which is commonly used in practice). The authors carefully address the computational feasibility of their approach by showing that the sampling distribution can be computed in O(n log n) time per iteration, which is often negligible compared to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component. The experimental validation on multiple datasets and loss functions demonstrates consistent improvements over fixed sampling, with the proposed method achieving convergence rates comparable to the optimal (but computationally infeasible) gradient-based sampling.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from theoretical development to practical implementation and validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the key insights are presented with appropriate technical depth.

I have a few suggestions for improvement that would strengthen the paper. First, the authors should explicitly compare their method with uniform sampling in a theorem analogous to Theorem 3.2, as uniform sampling is the most commonly used baseline in the literature. This would provide a more complete theoretical picture of the method's advantages.

Second, the authors should provide a more detailed complexity analysis. Specifically, they should analyze the total computational complexity of their method for finding an ε-optimal solution, including the O(n) cost for updating the gradient bounds. This would help practitioners understand the trade-offs between computational cost and convergence rate.

Third, the figures in the paper are difficult to interpret due to small text and labels. The authors should either improve the figure resolution and labeling or move some of the figures to the appendix to maintain clarity in the main text.

The paper demonstrates significant technical quality with rigorous theoretical guarantees and extensive empirical validation. The method is clearly original, as it provides a novel way to compute adaptive sampling distributions that are provably better than existing approximations. The significance is high, as the proposed method can be easily integrated into existing optimization algorithms with minimal computational overhead, leading to substantial speedups in practice.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in optimization with clear theoretical and practical contributions. The work advances the state of the art in adaptive sampling methods for large-scale optimization, which is a key challenge in modern machine learning applications.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with clear theoretical results, practical implementation, and extensive validation. The minor suggestions for improvement would further strengthen an already excellent paper. The method is likely to be widely adopted by practitioners due to its simplicity and effectiveness, making it a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
