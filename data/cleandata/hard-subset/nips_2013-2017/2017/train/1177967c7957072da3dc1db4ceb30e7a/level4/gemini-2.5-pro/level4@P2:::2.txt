This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The key motivation is that while theoretically optimal adaptive sampling (e.g., gradient-based) offers significant speedups, it is often computationally infeasible as it requires full gradient information at each iteration. Existing approximations can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The main contribution is a novel scheme that works with safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. Instead of using a point estimate from these bounds, the authors formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. This problem seeks the distribution that minimizes the worst-case objective function upper bound, over all possible true gradients consistent with the given bounds. The paper proves that the resulting sampling distribution is (i) provably better than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., Li-based or uniform sampling), (ii) the optimal solution with respect to the given bounds, and (iii) can be computed efficiently in O(n log n) time. The authors demonstrate how to maintain the required gradient bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs) and provide extensive experiments showing significant performance improvements over fixed sampling schemes.

### Strengths

*   **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The core idea of formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax problem (Equation 7) is elegant and novel. It provides a principled way to handle the uncertainty in gradient information, which is a fundamental challenge in creating practical adaptive sampling methods.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantee:** Theorem 3.2(ii) is a significant result. It proves that the proposed sampling scheme is guaranteed to be better than or equal to Li-based importance sampling. This "safety net" is a crucial advantage over prior adaptive methods based on approximations or stale values, which could potentially degrade performance below that of even uniform sampling. This makes the proposed method much more reliable for practical use.
*   **Computational Feasibility:** The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the non-trivial optimization problem for the sampling distribution. This, combined with the O(n) update for the gradient bounds in GLMs, ensures that the per-iteration overhead is manageable and often negligible compared to the gradient computation itself, especially when d is large.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough, covering multiple datasets, loss functions, and regularizers. The results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method (Algorithm 2) significantly outperforms fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time, and often approaches the performance of the computationally expensive optimal sampling (Algorithm 1).

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Lack of Overall Convergence Analysis:** While the paper analyzes the expected one-step progress and the per-iteration complexity of computing the sampling distribution, it does not provide a full convergence analysis for the overall algorithm. A key question remains unanswered: what is the total complexity for the proposed CD or SGD variants to find an ε-optimal solution? Deriving such a rate would substantially strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution. This analysis should ideally connect the quality of the bounds (i.e., the tightness of `u-l`) to the convergence rate, clarifying how much is gained over the Tr[L] rate of Li-based sampling.

2.  **Clarity of Figures:** The clarity of the paper is significantly hampered by the experimental figures. In the submitted PDF, all figures (Figures 2-9) are rendered at a very low resolution, making the axes, labels, legends, and curves completely illegible. This prevents a proper assessment of the empirical results. I strongly urge the authors to remake these figures at a much higher resolution and ensure they are readable. To create more space, some results could be moved to the appendix, allowing the main paper's figures to be larger.

3.  **Comparison with Uniform Sampling:** Theorem 3.2(ii) provides a comparison with Li-based sampling. While Remark 3.3 notes that this implies an improvement over uniform sampling in the special case where all Li are equal, it would be beneficial to state a more direct and general comparison to uniform sampling. A formal corollary or a more detailed remark comparing `vk` with `n * L_avg` (the equivalent value for uniform sampling) would make the guarantees clearer.

### Minor Points

*   In Equation (3), the third term in the final expression appears to be missing a factor of 1/2. Please double-check the derivation.
*   Line 85: In the denominator of the expression for `[p*k]_i`, the gradient `∇f(x)` should likely be `∇f(xk)`.
*   Line 135: In Theorem 3.2, for notational consistency with the problem statement in (7), you might consider removing the subscript `k` from `(p̂, ĉ)`.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of designing computationally feasible adaptive importance sampling methods.
*   The proposed minimax formulation is novel and provides a strong theoretical guarantee of improvement over standard fixed sampling schemes, a property lacking in many prior approaches.
*   The method is supported by an efficient algorithm and comprehensive experiments that demonstrate its practical effectiveness.

**Arguments for Rejection/Major Revision:**
*   The lack of a full convergence rate analysis (i.e., total complexity to reach ε-accuracy) is a significant theoretical omission.
*   The figures are currently illegible, which severely undermines the empirical contributions of the paper and its overall clarity.

Overall, I find this to be a strong paper with a novel idea and important practical implications. The theoretical guarantee of safety is a key selling point. However, the weaknesses, particularly the missing convergence analysis and the poor quality of the figures, are significant. I would be inclined to accept this paper if the authors can address these points, especially by providing a clear plan for including a convergence analysis and by fixing the figures in the final version.
