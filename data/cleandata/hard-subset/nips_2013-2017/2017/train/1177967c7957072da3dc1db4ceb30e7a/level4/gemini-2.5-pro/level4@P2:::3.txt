### Review Summary

This paper introduces a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The central problem addressed is that optimal adaptive sampling strategies, such as those based on full gradient information, are computationally infeasible in large-scale settings. Meanwhile, common approximations can perform worse than simple uniform sampling. The authors' proposed solution is to work with efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes (for CD) or norms (for SGD).

The core contribution is a novel min-max formulation (Equation 7) that finds a sampling distribution with the best worst-case performance, given the uncertainty expressed by these bounds. The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this optimization problem. Key theoretical results establish that the proposed sampling is provably always better than or equal to fixed importance sampling (including uniform sampling) and recovers the optimal gradient-based sampling when full information is available. The authors demonstrate how to maintain the required bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs) and provide extensive experiments showing significant speed-ups over non-adaptive methods. This work is well-positioned within the literature on adaptive sampling (e.g., [5, 22, 23, 36]), but distinguishes itself by providing a practical scheme with strong safety guarantees that were absent in prior approximations.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper is technically sound. The theoretical contributions are solid and well-supported.

*   The derivation of the expected one-step progress (Equation 3) and the formulation of the ideal adaptive sampling problem are clear and standard.
*   The min-max formulation for finding the best sampling distribution given uncertain gradient information (Equation 7) is the paper's central and most novel technical contribution. This is a well-principled approach to handling the uncertainty inherent in using gradient bounds.
*   Theorem 3.2, which proves that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed Li-based sampling, provides the critical "safety" guarantee that is the main selling point of the method.
*   The characterization of the solution to the min-max problem and the efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Theorem 3.4 and Algorithm 4) to compute it are non-trivial and demonstrate the practical feasibility of the approach.
*   The application to GLMs in Section 4, showing how to maintain the bounds efficiently, provides a concrete and important use case.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and effectively supports the paper's claims, comparing the proposed method against uniform sampling and the computationally expensive "optimal" sampling.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and generally easy to follow.

*   The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature.
*   The progression from the ideal "full information" case (Section 2) to the practical "limited information" case (Section 3) is logical and helps the reader understand the core ideas.
*   The contributions are clearly enumerated.
*   The algorithms are presented clearly.

One major point of concern regarding clarity is the presentation of the figures in Section 5. They are too small, and the text, labels, and legends are nearly illegible. For the final version, these figures must be significantly enlarged to be useful to the reader.

### Originality and Significance

The paper makes several original and significant contributions.

*   **Originality:** While the idea of using bounds to approximate gradient-based sampling exists (e.g., [36, 23]), the min-max formulation to find a robust sampling distribution *within* those bounds is novel. Previous approaches often resorted to heuristics, such as sampling based on the upper bounds, without guarantees of improvement over uniform sampling. The proposed "safe" approach is a clear and original conceptual advance. The efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) for solving this problem is also a novel contribution.

*   **Significance:** The work is highly significant for practitioners and researchers working on large-scale optimization.
    *   It provides a practical "drop-in" method to accelerate standard CD and SGD algorithms.
    *   The safety guarantee is crucial; it removes the risk of performance degradation that has been a barrier to the adoption of approximate adaptive methods.
    *   The empirical results show that the method can achieve performance close to that of the infeasible optimal sampling, leading to substantial real-world speed-ups in both wall-clock time and iteration count.
    *   The ideas presented could inspire further research into maintaining and utilizing bounds for other aspects of optimization.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **Novel and Well-Founded Method:** The min-max formulation for robust adaptive sampling is a novel, elegant, and principled solution to a well-known problem in large-scale optimization.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The "safety" guarantee (provably outperforming fixed sampling) is a significant theoretical result that distinguishes this work from prior heuristics and makes the method highly reliable in practice.
3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to implement the method and demonstrate its low overhead in practice, making it a genuinely useful tool.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are comprehensive and clearly demonstrate that the proposed method delivers significant performance improvements on real-world datasets and important machine learning models.

### Arguments against Acceptance / Areas for Improvement

1.  **Presentation of Figures:** As mentioned, the figures in the experimental section are illegible and must be fixed. This is a minor but important issue of presentation.
2.  **Bound Tightness:** The method for maintaining bounds in Section 4 uses a simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which can be quite loose. While the experiments show this is sufficient, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion on the sensitivity of the method's performance to the tightness of the bounds `l` and `u`.
3.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The experiments compare against uniform and optimal (infeasible) sampling. A comparison against other *practical* adaptive schemes (e.g., using stale gradients [22] or sampling from upper bounds [36]) would provide a more complete picture of its empirical standing.

Despite these minor points for improvement, the strengths of the paper are compelling. It presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization.
