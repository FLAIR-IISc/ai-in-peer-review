Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using per-iteration lower and upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (for CD) or component gradient norms (for SGD). The sampling distribution is chosen by solving a convex min–max problem that is provably optimal with respect to these bounds, strictly improves over uniform and fixed importance sampling, and reduces to true gradient-based sampling when full gradient information is available. The method can be computed in O(n log n) time per iteration and integrates easily with existing CD and SGD algorithms.

The work is positioned against prior adaptive schemes such as gradient-based sampling and gap-based sampling, which either require full gradients each iteration or rely on approximations that can underperform uniform sampling. Compared to earlier approaches that plug upper bounds into heuristic formulas, this paper formulates the choice of probabilities as an explicit optimization problem and provides worst-case optimality guarantees under the available bounds.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution: the sampling distribution minimizes a tight upper bound on expected suboptimality decrease over all gradients consistent with the bounds, and is always at least as good as uniform/fixed importance sampling.
- Elegant optimization formulation and efficient algorithm (O(n log n)) to compute the sampling from bounds, with a constructive characterization of the solution.
- Broad applicability: works for CD and SGD and is compatible with general GLMs; bound-updating strategies are simple and safe.
- Empirical results show meaningful speedups in CD, with overhead small enough to improve wall-clock time; the method approaches the performance of ideal gradient-based sampling without full gradient cost.
- Well written and easy to follow overall.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Theory/exposition issues needing clarification:
  - Lemma 2.1: the expression for the optimal α appears to be missing a factor of 2 in α*. Please check the derivation around the quadratic upper bound minimization.
  - Example 3.1 vs. Eq. (7): Eq. (7) defines a min over p and max over c in the box Ck. The example seems to suggest that picking c at either the lower or upper bound can outperform uniform sampling, which appears at odds with the preceding claim that such naive choices can be poor. Please clarify the intent: is the example showing that using either extreme can also be bad (relative to the truly optimal min–max solution), or that extremes can sometimes beat uniform but still be suboptimal?
- Algorithmic details:
  - Algorithm 4, line 4: given m = max(ℓsort), will the condition [ℓsort]ℓ > m ever be true? Please check whether m is updated before this test or if an off-by-one/initialization issue exists.
  - Algorithm 4, line 7: it likely should reference usort rather than csort.
- Empirical evaluation:
  - Comparisons to other adaptive schemes (e.g., [2], [5], [21]) are missing. Including these would better position the proposed method relative to recent alternatives.
  - Fixed importance sampling baselines should include non-uniform static distributions, not only uniform, to isolate the gain from adaptivity.
  - No timing results for SGD are shown. Given the small iteration gap between uniform and adaptive SGD in the reported plots, timing comparisons would help evaluate practical value there.
- References:
  - The title of reference [14] appears incorrect.
  - Please add: Csiba and Richtárik, “Importance Sampling for Minibatches,” arXiv, 2016.

Assessment by criteria:
- Quality: The main claims are well supported by analysis. The safe optimality under bounds and the guarantee over fixed sampling are clearly stated and proved. A few minor derivation/details should be corrected (α* in Lemma 2.1; Algorithm 4 lines) and the Example 3.1 narrative clarified.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Sections 3.1–3.4 are clear, with a helpful geometric view. A short pseudocode summary for the SGD variant would improve completeness.
- Originality: The min–max formulation for sampling under safe bounds and the associated optimality guarantees are, to my knowledge, novel, improving upon heuristic bound-based approximations in prior work.
- Significance: For CD, the method yields substantial speedups with practical computational cost and clear guarantees; for SGD, benefits appear smaller empirically, but the approach is principled and broadly applicable.

Pros:
- Strong theoretical guarantees; optimal under available information.
- Efficient and generic algorithm; easy to integrate with CD/SGD.
- Solid empirical gains for CD with minimal overhead.

Cons:
- Limited baselines in experiments; no SGD timing results.
- Minor issues in proofs/algorithms and a potentially confusing example.
- A few reference and presentation glitches.

Suggestions:
- Fix Lemma 2.1 factor and Algorithm 4 line issues; clarify Example 3.1.
- Add comparisons to recent adaptive sampling baselines and a non-uniform fixed sampling baseline.
- Include SGD timing plots or discuss overhead explicitly.
- Correct reference [14] and add the minibatch importance sampling citation.
