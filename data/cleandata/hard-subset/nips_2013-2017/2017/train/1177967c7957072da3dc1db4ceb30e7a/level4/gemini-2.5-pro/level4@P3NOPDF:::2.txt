This paper proposes an interesting new sampling strategy for Randomized Coordinate Descent (RCD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithms, based on iteratively updated upper and lower bounds of the gradients. The work provides valuable theoretical insights and presents a compelling alternative to existing sampling methods.

A significant strength of the paper is the theoretical analysis presented in Theorem 3.2. In particular, Conclusion 2, which demonstrates that the proposed sampling strategy can outperform the commonly used L_i-based importance sampling, is a strong and useful result. This provides a clear theoretical justification for the new method and highlights its potential for practical impact.

While the core ideas are promising, I have several comments and suggestions for improvement that I believe would strengthen the manuscript.

1.  **Clarity of Figures:** All figures in their current form are unreadable. They are too small, and essential details such as axis ticks, labels, numbers, and legends are illegible. To properly convey the empirical results, the authors must significantly increase the size and resolution of these plots. If space is a constraint, I suggest moving some of the less critical figures or experimental details to an appendix to allow for larger, clearer figures in the main body of the paper.

2.  **Comparison with Uniform Sampling:** The comparison against L_i-based sampling in Theorem 3.2 is very insightful. To further contextualize the paper's contribution, it would be highly beneficial to include a similar theoretical comparison against standard uniform sampling (i.e., p_i = 1/n). Since uniform sampling is a fundamental and widely used baseline, establishing the theoretical superiority of the proposed method over this simpler strategy would make the results more comprehensive and impactful.

3.  **Overall Complexity Analysis:** The paper should include a more detailed discussion of the total computational complexity. The proposed sampling method requires updating the bounds l^k and u^k, which appears to incur an O(n) cost at each iteration. It is crucial to analyze how this overhead affects the total complexity required to reach an epsilon-optimal solution. For RCD, what is the final complexity bound when accounting for both the cost of this sampling procedure and the number of iterations required? A clear analysis of this trade-off is essential for readers to assess the practical benefits of the proposed approach compared to methods with cheaper per-iteration sampling costs (like uniform or static L_i-based sampling).

4.  **Minor Notational Suggestions:**
    *   On Line 85, in the denominator of the expression for p_i^*, the variable should likely be x^k instead of x to maintain consistency with the notation for the k-th iteration.
    *   On Line 135, for notational consistency, the authors might consider removing the subscript 'k' from \hat{p} and \hat{c}, as these quantities appear to be defined independently of the iteration index.

In summary, this is a solid paper with a valuable contribution to the literature on stochastic and coordinate descent methods. Addressing the points above, particularly concerning the figure quality, comparison to uniform sampling, and the total complexity analysis, would significantly improve the clarity and impact of the work.
