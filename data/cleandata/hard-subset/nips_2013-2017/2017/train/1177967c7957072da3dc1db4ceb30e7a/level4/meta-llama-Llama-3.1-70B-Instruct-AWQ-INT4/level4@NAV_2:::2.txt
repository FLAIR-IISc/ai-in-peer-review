The paper "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling" proposes an efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors provide a novel way to compute the adaptive sampling distribution using safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient, which is computationally tractable.

The main idea of the paper is to formulate the problem of picking the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem and present an efficient algorithm to compute the solution. The proposed sampling scheme is generic and can be easily integrated into existing algorithms. The authors demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed sampling scheme through extensive numerical testing.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the experimental results are convincing. The authors also provide a discussion on how to track the gradient information in generalized linear models (GLMs) during optimization, which is useful for practitioners.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the proposed sampling scheme is provably better than any fixed importance sampling, which is a significant improvement over previous methods. Secondly, the computational cost of the proposed scheme is of the order O(n log n) per iteration, which is comparable with the cost to evaluate a single component of the gradient. Finally, the authors provide a thorough analysis of the proposed method, including a discussion on the competitive ratio and the total complexity of the algorithm.

However, there are a few minor issues that need to be addressed. Firstly, there is a typo in Line 85, where the denominator of p^*i should be xk instead of x. Secondly, the subscript k for \hat p and \hat c in Line 135 should be removed for consistency. Thirdly, the figures are too small and lack labels, numbers, and words, which makes them difficult to read. It would be better to move some of the figures to the appendix to gain space and show larger figures.

Additionally, it would be useful to provide a result similar to Conclusion 2 in Theorem 3.2 but comparing with uniform sampling (p=1/n) instead of L_i based method. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method.

Finally, it would be helpful to remark on how the new sampling method affects the total complexity of RCD or SGD. For example, what is the total complexity (including the O(n) time for updating lk and uk) for RCD to find an epsilon-optimal solution?

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is a significant improvement over previous methods. With some minor revisions, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field of machine learning.
