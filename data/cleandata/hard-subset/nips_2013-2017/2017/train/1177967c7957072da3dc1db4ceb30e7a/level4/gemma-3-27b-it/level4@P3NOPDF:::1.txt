## Review of “Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling for Coordinate Descent and Stochastic Gradient Methods”

This paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded “safe” adaptive importance sampling strategy for both coordinate descent and stochastic gradient methods. The authors propose an efficient approximation to gradient-based sampling, leveraging lower and upper bounds on gradient values. A key strength of the work is the theoretical guarantee that the proposed method is optimal with respect to the given bounds, consistently outperforming uniform or fixed importance sampling, while incurring negligible computational overhead. While adaptive importance sampling is not new, this work provides a novel formulation – framing the selection of the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem – and an efficient algorithm for its solution. Overall, this is a well-written paper and a valuable contribution to the field of importance sampling techniques.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous analysis and proves the optimality of the proposed method given the available bounds. This is a significant accomplishment.
*   **Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is computationally efficient, making it practical for deployment in large-scale optimization problems.
*   **Novel Formulation:** The convex optimization formulation of the sampling distribution selection is a novel and insightful contribution.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and theoretical results.

**Weaknesses/Questions:**

*   **Lemma 2.1:** The proof of Lemma 2.1 appears to be missing a factor of 2 in the definition of α*. Please verify.
*   **Example 3.1:** The discussion surrounding Example 3.1 is somewhat confusing. Equation (7) indicates a maximization problem, suggesting that setting *c* to either the upper or lower bound would be preferable to uniform sampling. However, the preceding statement claims that naively setting *c* to the bounds can be suboptimal. Clarification on the intended takeaway of this example would be beneficial.
*   **Algorithm 4 - Line 4:** Given that *m* is defined as max(*l*<sup>sort</sup>), the condition in Line 4 will likely never be satisfied. Please double-check this condition.
*   **Algorithm 4 - Line 7:** It appears there is a typo in Line 7. *u*<sup>sort</sup> should likely be used instead of *c*<sup>sort</sup>.
*   **Comparison to Existing Adaptive Methods:** The numerical results would be significantly strengthened by comparisons to other established adaptive sampling schemes, such as those presented in [2], [5], and [21]. It would also be valuable to compare against fixed importance sampling using a non-uniform distribution chosen *a priori*.
*   **SGD Timing Results:** The paper lacks timing results for Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). Including these results would provide a more complete picture of the method’s performance and computational cost in a commonly used optimization setting.
*   **References:** The title of reference [14] is incorrect. Additionally, please consider adding the following relevant work: Csiba and Richtarik, “Importance Sampling for Minibatches,” arXiv, 2016.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant and well-motivated contribution to the area of importance sampling. The theoretical results are strong, the method is efficient, and the presentation is clear. Addressing the minor concerns outlined above, particularly adding comparisons to existing adaptive methods and providing timing results for SGD, would further enhance the impact of this work.
