

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address a key limitation in existing adaptive importance sampling approaches: while gradient-based sampling offers theoretical advantages over static strategies, it is computationally infeasible in practice due to the need for full gradient computation at each iteration. The proposed method efficiently approximates gradient-based sampling using safe lower and upper bounds on gradient values, providing three key guarantees: (i) it is provably optimal with respect to the given bounds, (ii) it always outperforms uniform and fixed importance sampling, and (iii) it can be computed efficiently with negligible extra cost. The approach is generic and applicable to various optimization problems, with the authors demonstrating its integration into CD and SGD on generalized linear models.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on adaptive sampling strategies, which has been explored in previous NIPS works such as [2, 5, 21, 22, 23, 26, 36, 37]. While earlier approaches relied on approximations using stale gradient values or upper bounds (e.g., [36, 37]), this work presents a novel formulation where the optimal sampling distribution is determined through a convex optimization problem that incorporates the safe bounds. This represents a substantial theoretical advance over prior methods, as it provides provable guarantees that the proposed sampling is never worse than fixed importance sampling.

**Strengths:**
- **Quality:** The theoretical contributions are strong and well-supported. The paper establishes rigorous proofs showing the proposed sampling is optimal with respect to the given bounds and always superior to fixed sampling strategies. The computational complexity analysis is thorough, showing the overhead is O(n log n) per iteration, which is comparable to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component in many applications.
- **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear organization. The mathematical presentation is precise, and the algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-4) are easy to follow. The authors effectively use examples to illustrate key concepts (e.g., Examples 2.2 and 2.3).
- **Originality:** The formulation of adaptive sampling as a convex optimization problem with safe bounds represents a novel approach to the problem. While adaptive importance sampling has been studied before, the specific use of safe bounds in this manner with provable guarantees is new to the literature.
- **Significance:** The results demonstrate significant practical improvements over fixed sampling strategies, with the proposed method achieving performance nearly matching the optimal (but infeasible) gradient-based sampling. The numerical experiments on real datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) confirm the theoretical claims with meaningful speedups.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Technical detail:** The proof of Lemma 2.1 appears to be missing a factor of 2 in the optimal α*. This should be corrected in the final version.
- **Algorithm clarity:** In Algorithm 4, line 4 states "m = max(`sort)" but this condition (if [`sort]` > m) would never be satisfied since m is initialized to the maximum value. Similarly, line 7 should reference "usort" rather than "csort."
- **Experimental comparisons:** The numerical results would benefit from comparisons to other adaptive sampling schemes (e.g., [2, 5, 21]) and fixed importance sampling with non-uniform distributions. The paper currently only compares to uniform sampling and fixed importance sampling.
- **SGD results:** The paper lacks timing results for SGD experiments (Figures 7-9 show iteration complexity but not computational time), which is a notable omission given the paper's emphasis on computational efficiency.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically efficient solution to an important problem in optimization.
- The novel formulation of adaptive sampling as a convex optimization problem with provable guarantees represents a significant advance over prior work.
- The implementation is straightforward and can be integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations with minimal overhead.
- The numerical results confirm the theoretical claims on real-world datasets with meaningful speedups.

*Con:*
- Some technical details in the proofs and algorithms require correction.
- The experimental section would be strengthened by additional comparisons to alternative adaptive sampling methods.
- The lack of timing results for SGD experiments limits the assessment of computational efficiency.

The authors have addressed all reviewer comments in their rebuttal, including correcting the missing factor in Lemma 2.1, clarifying Algorithm 4, and acknowledging the need for additional comparisons. The paper is well-written, makes a clear theoretical contribution, and demonstrates significant practical value. The proposed method represents a substantial improvement over existing adaptive importance sampling approaches and has the potential to impact a wide range of optimization problems in machine learning.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of optimization algorithms with clear theoretical and practical significance. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and should be accepted.
