- Proposes "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling": a minimax-based robust sampling using lower/upper gradient bounds for CD and SGD.
- Uses efficiently computable bounds to form a worst-case-optimal sampling distribution rather than heuristics.
- Provides an O(n log n) algorithm to compute the safe sampling distribution.
- Theoretically proves the safe sampling is always better than fixed (e.g., L_i-based or uniform) importance sampling.
- Situates work within non-uniform/adaptive sampling literature, targeting practical trade-offs versus full-gradient methods.
- Empirically validated on generalized linear models across multiple datasets, losses, and regularizers.
- Experiments show faster convergence (iterations/epochs and wall-clock) and performance close to infeasible optimal sampling.
- Originality/significance: novel minimax formulation and safety guarantee with potential broad impact.
- Technical quality: solid theoretical results (Theorem 3.2) and a practical efficient algorithm (Theorem 3.4).
- Provides insights into ideal gradient-based sampling benefits (Section 2.1).
- Major presentation issue: figures are too small/illegible and need to be remade for clarity.
- Method effectiveness depends on tightness of gradient bounds; more discussion and empirical study of bound gap (u-l) is needed.
- Empirical comparison is limited: lacks comparisons to other practical adaptive schemes (e.g., stale-gradient or approximate methods).
- Computational overhead nuance: O(n log n) per-iteration cost may be non-negligible in some regimes (very large n or cheap sparse gradients); needs clearer discussion of where gains apply.
- Overall recommendation: strong contribution with addressable presentation and experimental-scope weaknesses — recommend acceptance.
