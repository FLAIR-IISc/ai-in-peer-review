

This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address a key limitation of adaptive importance sampling methods, which often require computationally expensive full gradient computations. Their approach uses safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information to construct an efficient sampling distribution that is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes that gradient-based sampling in CD methods can theoretically outperform classical fixed sampling, with potential speedups of up to the dimension n. Second, the authors propose a generic and efficient adaptive sampling strategy that can be integrated into both CD and SGD. Third, they demonstrate how to efficiently implement this scheme on structured optimization problems. Finally, they provide extensive numerical evidence showing the practical benefits of their approach.

Regarding quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The key theorems (particularly Theorem 3.2) provide rigorous guarantees about the sampling method's performance. The experimental results are thorough and well-designed, using multiple datasets and loss functions to validate the approach. The paper acknowledges the computational cost of the proposed method (O(n log n) per iteration) and carefully analyzes how this compares to standard implementations.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some aspects could be improved. The mathematical notation is consistent, but the figures lack readable labels, tick marks, and numbers, making it difficult to interpret results. The paper would benefit from moving some of these details to an appendix to improve the main figures' readability. The writing is clear and well-organized, with logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical validation.

In terms of originality, the paper builds on previous work on adaptive importance sampling but offers a novel approach that addresses key computational limitations. The authors properly contextualize their work against relevant NIPS papers (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) and demonstrate how their method differs from existing approaches. The safe adaptive sampling technique is genuinely novel, with the key insight being the use of convex optimization to find the best sampling distribution given safe gradient bounds.

The significance of this work is substantial. For practitioners using CD and SGD methods, the proposed sampling scheme offers a practical way to improve convergence without significant computational overhead. The paper demonstrates that even with very loose gradient bounds (obtained through simple methods like Cauchy-Schwarz), the adaptive sampling significantly outperforms fixed sampling strategies. This has clear implications for large-scale machine learning applications where computational efficiency is critical.

Key strengths include:
- Rigorous theoretical guarantees that are well-motivated
- Generality of the method across various optimization problems
- Careful analysis of computational costs (O(n log n) per iteration)
- Comprehensive empirical validation with multiple datasets and loss functions

Key weaknesses include:
- Figures lack readable labels, tick marks, and numbers
- Theoretical comparison to uniform sampling (p_i = 1/n) is missing
- Total complexity analysis for finding an ε-optimal solution is incomplete

I recommend the authors address the following specific points:

1. In Line 85, the denominator of p^*_i should be x_k, not x.

2. In Line 135, the authors should remove the subscript k from \hat{p} and \hat{c} for consistency.

3. The figures should have readable labels, tick marks, and numbers. Consider moving some details to an appendix to improve readability.

4. The authors should provide a theoretical comparison between their method and uniform sampling (p_i = 1/n) to complement the comparison with L-based sampling in Theorem 3.2.

5. The authors should analyze how the proposed method affects the total complexity of RCD or SGD, including the O(n) cost for updating the gradient bounds. Specifically, they should provide the total complexity (including these overheads) for finding an ε-optimal solution.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to optimization for machine learning. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme addresses a practical limitation in existing methods and offers a theoretically sound approach with clear empirical benefits. With the suggested revisions, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.
