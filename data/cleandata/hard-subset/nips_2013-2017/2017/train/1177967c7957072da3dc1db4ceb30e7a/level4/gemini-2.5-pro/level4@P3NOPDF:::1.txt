This paper introduces a novel and "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent and stochastic gradient methods. The core idea is to leverage lower and upper bounds on gradient norms to formulate an efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling, thereby avoiding the high cost of computing the full gradient at each iteration. A key contribution is the formulation of the problem of selecting the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem, for which the authors provide an efficient solving algorithm. The proposed method is proven to be the optimal strategy with respect to the given bounds, provably outperforming standard uniform sampling, all while incurring negligible additional computational cost.

This is a well-written paper that makes a nice contribution to the study of importance sampling techniques for large-scale optimization.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novel Formulation:** The framing of the optimal sampling problem as a convex optimization problem based on gradient bounds is novel and elegant.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The method is supported by strong theoretical results, proving its optimality relative to the bounds and its superiority over uniform sampling.
*   **Practicality:** The proposed algorithm is efficient, ensuring that the overhead of adapting the sampling distribution is minimal. This is a crucial feature for any practical implementation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

**Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**
While this is a strong paper, the following points could be addressed to further improve its quality and impact:

1.  **Proof of Lemma 2.1:** In the proof of Lemma 2.1, it appears there may be a missing factor of 2 in the expression for α*. Please double-check this derivation.

2.  **Clarification of Example 3.1:** The presentation of Example 3.1 is slightly confusing. The text directly preceding it states that a naive approach of setting the sampling probabilities based on just the upper or lower bounds can be suboptimal. However, the example in (7) seems to suggest that doing so is preferable to uniform sampling. Clarifying the precise takeaway message of this example in relation to the preceding statement would be helpful for the reader.

3.  **Questions on Algorithm 4:** There are a couple of points in Algorithm 4 that would benefit from clarification:
    *   In line 4, the condition `u_i^{sort} < m` seems unlikely to be satisfied, given that `m` is defined as `max(l^{sort})` and we generally expect `u >= l`. Could you please clarify the logic or scenario where this condition holds?
    *   In line 7, it seems there may be a typo, where `c^{sort}` should perhaps be `u^{sort}`.

4.  **Expanded Experimental Comparisons:** The empirical evaluation is sound but could be strengthened with additional baselines. While the comparison to uniform sampling is valuable, including comparisons against other adaptive sampling schemes (e.g., those in references [2], [5], [21]) would provide a more comprehensive picture of the method's performance in the landscape of existing techniques. Additionally, comparing against a well-chosen *fixed* non-uniform importance sampling distribution would be an informative baseline.

5.  **Timing Results for SGD:** The paper provides timing results for the coordinate descent experiments, which effectively demonstrates the low overhead of the proposed method. It would be beneficial to include similar timing results for the SGD experiments to confirm that these practical benefits extend to that setting as well.

6.  **Minor Corrections to References:**
    *   The title of the paper in reference [14] appears to be incorrect.
    *   Please consider adding the following relevant work: Csiba and Richtarik, "Importance Sampling for Minibatches," arXiv preprint, 2016.
