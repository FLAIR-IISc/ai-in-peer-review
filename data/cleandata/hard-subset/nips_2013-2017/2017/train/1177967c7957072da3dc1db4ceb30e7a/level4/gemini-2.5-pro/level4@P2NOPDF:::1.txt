### **Summary**

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for stochastic optimization methods, specifically coordinate descent and stochastic gradient descent. The core idea is to leverage lower and upper bounds on gradient norms to efficiently approximate an ideal, but computationally expensive, gradient-based sampling distribution. The authors formulate the problem of finding the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem and provide an efficient algorithm for its solution.

A key contribution is the proof that the proposed strategy is optimal with respect to the given bounds and that it provably outperforms standard uniform sampling or any fixed importance sampling scheme. The authors also demonstrate that this adaptive distribution can be computed with negligible computational overhead. The paper is well-written and presents a solid theoretical and practical contribution to the study of importance sampling techniques.

### **Strengths**

*   **Novel Formulation:** The framing of the optimal sampling problem as a convex optimization problem is novel and elegant.
*   **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The method is supported by rigorous proofs demonstrating its optimality relative to the bounds and its superiority over standard baselines.
*   **Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm for computing the sampling distribution is highly efficient, making the method practical for real-world applications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

### **Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

While the paper is strong overall, there are several points that could be clarified or improved:

1.  **Proof of Lemma 2.1:** In the proof of Lemma 2.1, it appears there might be a missing factor of 2 in the final expression for α*.
2.  **Clarification on Example 3.1:** Example 3.1 seems to suggest that setting the constant `c` in Equation (7) to either the upper or lower bound is superior to uniform sampling. This is slightly confusing given the preceding statement that such a "naive approach" can be suboptimal. A clarification on the main takeaway from this example would be helpful.
3.  **Algorithm 4 Details:** There are a couple of points in Algorithm 4 that would benefit from clarification:
    *   **Line 4:** Given that `m` is defined as `max(l^{sort})`, it is unclear if the condition `m >= u_k^{sort}` will ever be satisfied. Please clarify the circumstances under which this condition holds.
    *   **Line 7:** Is the use of `c^{sort}` a typo? It seems it should be `u^{sort}` in this context.
4.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The empirical evaluation could be significantly strengthened. I would suggest including comparisons to other adaptive importance sampling schemes (e.g., those in references [2], [5], [21]). Additionally, comparing against a strong baseline of a *fixed, non-uniform* importance sampling distribution would provide a more complete picture of the proposed method's practical advantages over simpler non-uniform strategies.
5.  **SGD Timing Results:** The paper claims the method adds negligible extra cost, but there are no timing results provided for the SGD experiments. Including these would help substantiate this important practical claim.
6.  **Minor Corrections:**
    *   The title of the paper in reference [14] appears to be incorrect.
    *   The authors may also consider citing the relevant work: Csiba and Richtarik, "Importance Sampling for Minibatches," arXiv, 2016.
