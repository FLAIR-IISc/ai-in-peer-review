This paper proposes a novel method for adaptive importance sampling, termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for use in large-scale optimization algorithms like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The central idea is to use efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on gradient information to define a robust sampling distribution. Unlike prior work that might use heuristics on such bounds, this paper formulates the selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. This approach finds a distribution that is optimal under the worst-case realization of the true gradients within the given bounds. The authors provide a fast O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem. Theoretically, they prove that the resulting "safe" sampling scheme is always better than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform sampling). This work is situated within a growing body of literature on non-uniform and adaptive sampling for optimization, aiming to address the high computational cost of ideal adaptive methods (which require full gradient information) while providing stronger guarantees than existing approximations. The method's efficacy is demonstrated on generalized linear models, with extensive experiments supporting the theoretical claims.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its novel and principled approach to handling uncertainty in gradient information. Formulating the problem of choosing a sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem (Equation 7) is a clever method for deriving a robust strategy. This leads to the key "safety" guarantee: the sampling is provably superior to fixed sampling schemes. This is a significant advantage over many heuristic-based adaptive methods, whose performance can sometimes be worse than uniform sampling. The proposed framework is generic and could have a broad impact on the design of stochastic optimization algorithms.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and provides strong theoretical support for its claims.
    1.  The guarantee that the proposed sampling `p̂` is always better than `L_i`-based fixed sampling (Theorem 3.2) is a powerful result.
    2.  The development of an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm (Algorithm 4, Theorem 3.4) to compute this sampling distribution is crucial, as it ensures the method is practical and computationally viable.
    3.  The analysis also provides valuable insights into the performance of ideal gradient-based sampling for CD (Section 2.1), highlighting its potential benefits.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors test their method on both CD and SGD across three real-world datasets, using multiple loss functions and regularizers. The results consistently show that the proposed adaptive sampling significantly outperforms fixed sampling in terms of both convergence speed (iterations/epochs) and wall-clock time. Crucially, the performance often closely tracks that of the computationally infeasible "optimal" sampling, demonstrating the effectiveness of the safe approximation.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Clarity of Figures:** A major weakness in the current presentation is the poor quality of the figures in Section 5. The plots (Figures 2-9) are rendered too small, making the text, legends, and data series nearly illegible. This significantly hinders the reader's ability to assess the empirical evidence. The authors should remake all figures to be larger and clearer for the final version of the paper.
*   **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The method's effectiveness hinges on the ability to maintain useful lower and upper bounds on the gradient information. The paper demonstrates a simple approach for GLMs using the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which can be quite loose. While the method performs well even with these loose bounds, the paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion on the relationship between the tightness of the bounds (i.e., the gap `u-l`) and the resulting performance gain. Lemma 3.6 touches upon this theoretically, but an empirical investigation would be very insightful.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Other Practical Adaptive Methods:** The experiments compare primarily against uniform sampling (a weak baseline) and the infeasible optimal sampling (an upper bound on performance). To better contextualize the contribution, a comparison against other *practical* adaptive importance sampling schemes would be highly valuable. For example, methods based on stale gradients [22, 1] or other approximations of the optimal distribution [23, 36] would serve as more competitive baselines and provide a clearer picture of the state-of-the-art.
*   **Nuance on Computational Overhead:** The paper claims the `O(n log n)` overhead per iteration is often negligible. While true for some problem settings (e.g., `d >> n`), this may not hold universally. In cases where `n` is very large and the cost of a single coordinate gradient is low (e.g., due to sparsity), this overhead could become a bottleneck. A more detailed discussion of the problem regimes (in terms of `n`, `d`, and data sparsity) where the proposed method provides the most significant wall-clock time improvements would be beneficial.

### Summary of Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The novel formulation of safe adaptive sampling is both theoretically sound and practically effective. The main weaknesses relate to the presentation (figures) and the scope of the empirical comparison, which are addressable. The strengths, particularly the novel methodology and its strong theoretical guarantees, far outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
