- Introduces "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling" that computes adaptive sampling distributions using safe lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes.  
- Provably produces the optimal sampling distribution under those bounds and never worsens performance relative to uniform or fixed importance sampling.  
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees and competitive ratio analysis (Theorems 3.2–3.4, Lemma 3.5–3.6).  
- Extends adaptive sampling theory to coordinate descent (CD) and discusses SGD, filling a gap where CD was less explored.  
- Generic method with O(n log n) per-iteration overhead, often negligible compared to gradient computation.  
- Practical implementation for GLMs with efficient bound maintenance and clear algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1–4).  
- Empirical validation on rcv1, real-sim, news20 shows large speedups for CD and moderate gains for SGD (Figures 3–9).  
- Builds on prior adaptive-sampling work but crucially provides a provably safe approximation where earlier methods could be worse than uniform.  
- Figure quality is poor (small fonts, overlapping lines, low contrast), making empirical results hard to interpret.  
- The paper does not adequately explain why adaptive sampling yields weaker improvements for SGD than for CD.  
- Scope is limited by focus on GLMs; lacks discussion on maintaining safe gradient bounds for non-GLM problems (e.g., deep learning).  
- Strengths: rigorous theory, practical relevance, clear exposition, low overhead, and ease of integration.  
- Weaknesses: unreadable figures, unexplained SGD performance gap, and limited applicability discussion beyond GLMs.  
- Overall recommendation: accept — significant, well-motivated, and technically strong contribution with minor issues to address.
