The authors present a "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent and stochastic gradient methods. Based on lower and upper bounds on the gradient values, an efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling is proposed. The method is proven to be the best strategy with respect to the given bounds, always better than uniform or fixed importance sampling, and can be computed efficiently with negligible extra cost. Although adaptive importance sampling strategies have been previously proposed, the authors present a novel formulation of selecting the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem and present an efficient algorithm to solve it.

This paper is well-written and a valuable contribution to the study of importance sampling techniques. The motivation for developing a computationally feasible adaptive sampling method is clear, and the theoretical guarantees are strong. The connection to existing work is well-established, and the proposed approach builds upon prior research in a meaningful way. The empirical evaluation provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis supporting the proposed method, including proofs of its optimality and efficiency compared to existing approaches.
*   **Computational Efficiency:**  The algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient, making it practical for large-scale optimization problems. The authors clearly articulate the computational overhead and demonstrate its feasibility.
*   **Novel Formulation:** Framing the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem is a novel and insightful approach.
*   **Practical Applicability:** The method is generic and can be easily integrated into existing coordinate descent and stochastic gradient algorithms.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Extensive numerical experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed sampling scheme on real-world datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison to Other Adaptive Methods:** While the authors compare against uniform and fixed importance sampling, a comparison to other adaptive schemes (e.g., [2], [5],[21] as noted by the author) would strengthen the evaluation and provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
*   **Missing SGD Timing Results:** The absence of timing results for SGD hinders a complete assessment of the method’s performance in that context.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** A few minor typos and potential logical inconsistencies were identified in the original draft (specifically regarding Lemma 2.1, Example 3.1, and Algorithm 4 lines 4 and 7 – all addressed in the rebuttal). 

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper addresses an important problem in large-scale optimization and presents a compelling solution with strong theoretical and empirical support. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and computationally efficient. Despite a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh them. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-justified approach to adaptive importance sampling.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides strong guarantees on the performance of the proposed method.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on real-world datasets.
*   The method is computationally efficient and can be readily integrated into existing optimization algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of comparison to other adaptive sampling schemes is a minor drawback.
*   The missing SGD timing results limit the completeness of the empirical evaluation.
*   The initially identified minor technical issues, now addressed in the rebuttal.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept.** I have read the author rebuttal and found the responses to my questions to be satisfactory. The authors clarified the technical points and acknowledged the limitations of the comparison to other adaptive methods, indicating an understanding of the broader research landscape.  This paper represents a significant advance in the field of importance sampling and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS 2017.
