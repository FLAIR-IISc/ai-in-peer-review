This paper addresses the timely and relevant problem of safe adaptive importance sampling (AIS). The authors propose a novel safeguarding strategy designed to improve the robustness and stability of existing AIS algorithms. A key contribution of this work is its generality; the proposed method is presented as a modular component that can be readily integrated with a wide range of popular importance sampling techniques. The paper provides both theoretical analysis to ground the proposed strategy and a set of experiments to demonstrate its practical efficacy.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Generality and Practicality:** A significant strength of the proposed approach is its broad applicability. By framing the method as a general-purpose "wrapper," the authors make their contribution immediately useful to practitioners and researchers working with various existing AIS methods. This modularity is a commendable feature.

2.  **Theoretical and Empirical Support:** The paper is well-balanced, presenting a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed safeguarding mechanism alongside empirical validation. The theoretical results provide confidence in the method's soundness, while the experiments effectively showcase its practical benefits over standard baselines. This combination of theory and practice strengthens the paper's overall message.

**Areas for Improvement:**

The primary weakness of the paper in its current form lies in its presentation, specifically regarding the figures in Section 5. A major revision of these figures is necessary for the paper to be accessible and its claims to be fully verifiable by the reader.

*   **Figure and Label Legibility:** The figures in the experimental section are rendered at a size that makes them extremely difficult to read. The data points, trend lines, and confidence intervals are often indistinguishable. Furthermore, the axis labels, legends, and titles use a font size that is far too small, rendering them illegible both on screen and in print. This critical issue prevents a proper assessment of the empirical results, which are a cornerstone of the paper's contribution.

*   **Recommendation for Figures:** It is strongly recommended that the authors remake all figures in Section 5 to ensure they are clear, legible, and effectively communicate the experimental outcomes. This includes significantly increasing the overall size of the plots and using a much larger font for all text elements. If space is a constraint, the authors might consider restructuring the layout or moving some secondary results to an appendix to allow the primary figures to be displayed more prominently.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a valuable and well-motivated contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The proposed method is general, principled, and supported by both theory and experiments. However, the significant presentation issues with the experimental figures currently detract from the paper's quality and clarity. If the authors can thoroughly address the legibility of the figures, this paper would represent a solid contribution to the conference.
