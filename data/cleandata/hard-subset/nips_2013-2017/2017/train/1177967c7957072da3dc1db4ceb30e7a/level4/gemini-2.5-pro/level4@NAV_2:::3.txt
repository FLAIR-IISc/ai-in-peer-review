This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling strategy, named Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling, designed to accelerate large-scale optimization algorithms like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The central idea is to address the computational infeasibility of optimal gradient-based sampling, which requires full gradient information at each iteration. Instead, the proposed method relies on efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. The authors formulate the problem of choosing a sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem, seeking the distribution that offers the best worst-case performance guarantee given the uncertainty described by the bounds. This approach yields a sampling scheme that is (i) provably superior to any fixed importance sampling strategy (including uniform sampling), (ii) efficiently computable in O(n log n) time, and (iii) recovers the optimal gradient-based sampling if full information is available. The paper demonstrates how to maintain the necessary gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provides extensive empirical validation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality & Significance:** The main contribution is the "safety" guarantee. Many prior adaptive sampling methods based on approximations or stale information can, in principle, perform worse than simple uniform sampling. This work provides a rigorous theoretical guarantee (Theorem 3.2) that the proposed sampling is always an improvement over fixed sampling strategies. This is a significant and valuable property for a practical algorithm.
*   **Originality:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a min-max optimization over the set of possible gradients defined by the bounds (Equation 7) is novel and principled. It directly tackles the uncertainty of having limited information, rather than simply plugging in an estimate (e.g., the upper bound) into the optimal sampling formula. The efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem is also a key contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation is well-established in the introduction, the theoretical development is easy to follow, and the connection to practical implementations (Section 4) is well-explained. The separation of the ideal "full information" case (Section 2) from the practical "limited information" case (Section 3) effectively builds the argument.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly for CD. The results show that the safe adaptive sampling scheme approaches the performance of the oracle-like optimal sampling in terms of iterations, while significantly outperforming fixed sampling in wall-clock time, confirming that the computational overhead is manageable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Figures:** The figures in Section 5 are very small and the text within them is difficult to read. The results appear compelling, but their presentation hinders a full appreciation of the empirical evidence. I would strongly recommend enlarging the plots and increasing the font size for labels, legends, and titles in the final version.
*   **Performance Gap between CD and SGD:** The empirical results for SGD (Figures 7-9) show a much less pronounced improvement over uniform sampling compared to the CD experiments. The paper notes this observation but does not offer a detailed analysis. A discussion on why this might be the case—for instance, are the gradient norm bounds inherently looser or less informative for SGD, or is adaptive sampling fundamentally less impactful in that setting?—would strengthen the paper's conclusions.
*   **Overhead Analysis:** The O(n log n) overhead per iteration for computing the sampling distribution is non-trivial. The authors correctly state that this is negligible when the feature dimension `d` is large compared to `n`. However, for "short and fat" data matrices (d << n), this cost could dominate the O(d) cost of a gradient computation. A more explicit discussion of this trade-off and the regimes where the method is most beneficial would be valuable.
