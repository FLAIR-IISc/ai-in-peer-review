- Topical and relevant: Addresses an active practical need in safe adaptive importance sampling.  
- Generality: Method is modular and compatible with many existing estimators, increasing impact.  
- Theoretical support: Provides formal guarantees that clarify when the approach is sound and trade-offs involved.  
- Empirical validation: Experiments substantiate theory and show utility beyond synthetic settings.  
- Clarity of motivation: The need for “safe” mechanisms in adaptive importance sampling is well motivated.  

- Figure readability: Plots are too small; fonts, line widths, and color choices need improvement and clearer captions.  
- Definition of “safe”: Paper needs a precise, early statement of the safety criterion and comparison to alternatives.  
- Assumptions: Underlying assumptions for theoretical results should be listed in one place and discussed for realism.  
- Trade-offs: Clarify the cost of safety (variance, bias, sample complexity) versus standard adaptive IS methods.  
- Comparisons: Ensure empirical baselines include strong alternatives (non-adaptive IS, adaptive w/o safeguards, self-normalized, control variates) or justify omissions.  
- Sensitivity and ablations: Include component ablations, hyperparameter sensitivity analyses, and multiple seeds with error bars.  
- Scalability and overhead: Discuss computational/memory overhead and scaling with dimension and sample size; report wall-clock times.  
- Practical guidance: Provide concise hyperparameter choices, diagnostics, and a practitioner’s checklist for adoption.  
- Reproducibility: Release code or provide detailed pseudocode, preprocessing, and full hyperparameter settings.  

- Safety guarantees: Specify whether guarantees are high-probability, uniform-in-time, and their robustness to model misspecification.  
- Sensitivity to mismatch: Clarify sensitivity in high-dimensional proposal-target mismatch scenarios.  
- Interaction with variance reduction: Explain compatibility with control variates, stratification, resampling, and whether guarantees still hold.  
- Conservativeness: Identify regimes where the safety mechanism may be overly conservative and how to relax adaptivity.  

- Notation consistency: Ensure consistent symbols and introduce all notation on first use.  
- Cross-references and proofs: Cross-reference theorems/assumptions clearly and include brief proof sketches for key results.  
- Figure captions: Make captions self-contained and interpretative, highlighting take-home messages.  

- Overall assessment: Timely and potentially impactful work with solid theory and experiments; main shortcomings are presentation and clarity of safety, assumptions, and trade-offs—recommend revisions and view favorably pending fixes.
