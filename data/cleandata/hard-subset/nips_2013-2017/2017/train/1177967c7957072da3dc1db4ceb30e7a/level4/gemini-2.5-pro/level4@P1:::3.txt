This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for first-order optimization algorithms like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The central idea is to improve upon static sampling strategies (e.g., uniform or L-based sampling) by adapting the sampling distribution during optimization, while avoiding the prohibitive computational cost of fully adaptive methods that require calculating the entire gradient at each iteration.

The authors' approach is to work with efficiently computable and maintainable safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. The core contribution is to formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem: finding the distribution that maximizes the expected progress under the worst-case realization of the gradient within the given bounds. The paper presents a clear motivation for this "safe" or robust approach, provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute this distribution, and proves that the resulting sampling scheme is guaranteed to be better than any fixed importance sampling strategy. This theoretical guarantee is a key differentiator from prior work on approximate adaptive sampling, where performance could degrade to be worse than uniform sampling. The method is demonstrated on generalized linear models (GLMs), and its practical performance is validated through extensive experiments on several real-world datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a min-max optimization (Equation 7) is an elegant and principled way to handle the uncertainty inherent in using partial gradient information. This provides a robust framework for adaptive sampling that is theoretically well-founded. The guarantee that the proposed sampling is provably better than fixed sampling (Theorem 3.2) is a significant result that addresses a major weakness of previous adaptive schemes.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The paper provides strong theoretical support for the proposed method. The analysis clearly establishes the advantages over static sampling and shows that the scheme gracefully recovers the optimal gradient-based sampling when full gradient information is available. The provision of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) for computing the sampling distribution is crucial, making the method practical.

3.  **Clarity and Practicality:** The paper is well-written and the proposed framework is generic. The authors provide a concrete and important example—training GLMs—to demonstrate how the required safe gradient bounds can be derived and updated efficiently (in O(n) time). This makes the method readily applicable and easy for practitioners to adopt.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both CD and SGD, multiple loss functions and regularizers (Lasso, Ridge, logistic, hinge loss), and several standard real-world datasets. The inclusion of both iteration-wise and time-wise performance plots is commendable, as it demonstrates that the theoretical speed-ups translate into tangible wall-clock time improvements, even with the computational overhead of the sampling algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Presentation Quality of Figures:** A significant issue with the current manuscript is the poor quality of the figures in the experimental section (Figures 2-9). They are rendered too small, and the fonts used for axis labels, legends, and titles are unreadable. This makes it extremely difficult for a reader to inspect the plots, verify the paper's empirical claims, and fully appreciate the performance gains. For the final version of the paper, it is essential that these figures are made significantly larger and clearer.

2.  **Scope of Applicability:** While the demonstration on GLMs is effective, the practical impact of the proposed method hinges on the ability to efficiently compute and maintain safe gradient bounds. The paper would be stronger if it included a broader discussion on other classes of models or problem structures for which this is feasible. This would help the reader better understand the scope and potential limitations of the approach.

3.  **Clarity of Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The analysis of the competitive ratio in Section 3 (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) is theoretically interesting but its practical implications are not fully clarified. The bound from Lemma 3.5 requires solving a non-convex problem, making it difficult to evaluate, while the condition in Lemma 3.6 seems rather strong and its applicability is not demonstrated. A brief discussion on the tightness of these bounds or their utility in practice would be beneficial.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, theoretically sound, and practical method for adaptive importance sampling. The strengths—a principled robust formulation, strong theoretical guarantees, and thorough empirical validation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to presentation and could be addressed in a revision. The work makes a solid contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization.
