This paper introduces a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for accelerating large-scale optimization algorithms like coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The authors first revisit the concept of optimal gradient-based adaptive sampling, which, while theoretically providing significant speedups over static sampling (e.g., uniform or Li-based), is computationally impractical as it requires calculating the full gradient in every iteration. The core contribution of this work is an efficient and "safe" approximation to this optimal sampling. The proposed method relies on maintaining safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient components. Instead of using a heuristic to pick a value from these bounds, the authors formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem. This problem seeks the distribution that minimizes the worst-case convergence bound over all possible gradients consistent with the bounds. The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem and proves that the resulting sampling distribution is guaranteed to be better than or equal to fixed Li-based importance sampling. The utility of the method is demonstrated on generalized linear models, where such gradient bounds can be updated efficiently, and validated through extensive experiments.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the sampling choice as a min-max optimization problem over a set of possible gradients defined by bounds is a novel and principled approach. It directly addresses the key challenge of adaptive sampling: how to make decisions with limited, uncertain information.
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The "safety" guarantee presented in Theorem 3.2(ii) is a significant strength. Proving that the proposed adaptive scheme is never worse than a standard fixed importance sampling strategy provides a strong theoretical justification that has been lacking in some prior heuristic-based approaches. This makes the method robust and reliable.
*   **Practicality:** The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling distribution. For many problems where the dimension `n` is not significantly larger than the feature dimension `d`, the overhead of this computation is acceptable, as demonstrated by the timing experiments. The authors also show a practical method for maintaining the required gradient bounds for the important class of generalized linear models.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating that the proposed sampling scheme significantly outperforms uniform and fixed importance sampling in both epochs and wall-clock time. The performance often gets close to the impractical, full-information optimal sampling, validating the effectiveness of the approach.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Figures:** The figures in their current state are unreadable. They are too small, and axis labels, ticks, numbers, and legends are illegible. This severely hinders the assessment of the empirical results. The authors should significantly enlarge the figures, ensuring all text is clearly readable. It may be necessary to move some plots to the appendix to create adequate space in the main paper.

2.  **Overall Complexity Analysis:** While the paper analyzes the complexity of computing the sampling distribution per iteration (O(n log n)) and updating the bounds (O(n)), it stops short of providing an overall convergence rate for an algorithm like CD or SGD equipped with this new sampling. A full theoretical analysis showing the total complexity to reach an ε-optimal solution would be a valuable addition. This would allow for a more complete comparison against the known complexity of methods using uniform or Li-based sampling.

3.  **Comparison to Uniform Sampling:** Theorem 3.2 provides a clear comparison to Li-based sampling. While Remark 3.3 notes that this implies superiority over uniform sampling when all Li are equal, a more general statement comparing the proposed scheme to uniform sampling for the case of non-uniform Lipschitz constants would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

4.  **Minor Points:**
    *   In Example 2.3 (Optimal sampling), the denominator in the definition of p*k appears to use `x` instead of `xk`. Please check for consistency.
    *   In the statement of Theorem 3.2, the use of `p̂` and `ĉ` without the subscript `k` is appropriate for the time-independent optimization problem (7). The authors may wish to ensure this notational choice is used consistently throughout the section.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   The paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and principled min-max framework for adaptive importance sampling.
    *   It provides a strong "safety" guarantee, ensuring the method is provably better than standard fixed sampling schemes.
    *   An efficient algorithm for the proposed sampling is provided, and its practical effectiveness is demonstrated through strong empirical results on real-world datasets.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The presentation quality is hampered by unreadable figures, which must be fixed.
    *   The theoretical analysis is incomplete, as it lacks an overall complexity result for the full optimization algorithm.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The weaknesses are addressable, with the illegible figures being the most critical point for revision. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the presentation issues, particularly the figures.
