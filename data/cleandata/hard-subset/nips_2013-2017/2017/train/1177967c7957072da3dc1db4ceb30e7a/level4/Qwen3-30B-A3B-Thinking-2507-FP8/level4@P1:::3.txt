

This paper introduces a novel approach to adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors propose a "safe" adaptive sampling scheme that efficiently approximates gradient-based sampling while providing theoretical guarantees that the sampling is always better than uniform or fixed importance sampling. The core contribution is a convex optimization formulation that computes the optimal sampling distribution under safe gradient bounds, which can be efficiently implemented in O(n log n) time per iteration. The method is generic and can be integrated into existing algorithms with minimal computational overhead, as demonstrated through extensive empirical evaluation on generalized linear models.

The paper builds on a well-established line of work in adaptive sampling for optimization methods. Previous approaches like gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37] have shown theoretical advantages but are computationally infeasible for large-scale problems. The authors correctly identify that approximations based on upper bounds [36, 37] or stale values [22, 1] often fail to maintain theoretical guarantees. Their key insight is formulating the sampling problem as a robust optimization that minimizes the worst-case performance under safe gradient bounds, rather than approximating the true gradient. This differs significantly from prior work that simply uses extreme bounds (e.g., [36, 23]).

The theoretical contributions are strong and well-justified. The authors prove that their proposed sampling distribution is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2) and provide bounds on the competitive ratio (Lemmas 3.5-3.6). The connection between the sampling problem and the geometry of gradient bounds is particularly elegant. The analysis for both CD (Section 2.1) and SGD (Section 2.2) is thorough, with clear examples showing how the proposed method outperforms static sampling strategies. The extension to practical implementation via safe gradient bounds (Section 4) is well-motivated, with concrete examples for generalized linear models that demonstrate how bounds can be maintained in O(n) time.

The empirical evaluation (Section 5) is comprehensive and supports the theoretical claims. The authors compare their method against fixed importance sampling, uniform sampling, and optimal (but computationally infeasible) gradient-based sampling. The results consistently show that their method achieves performance close to the optimal sampling while maintaining minimal computational overhead. The observation that even very loose gradient bounds (derived from Cauchy-Schwarz) yield significant improvements over fixed sampling is particularly compelling. The experiments cover multiple loss functions (squared hinge, logistic, square loss) and regularization types (L1, L2) on three standard datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20), with careful attention to computational complexity.

The paper excels in quality, with rigorous theoretical analysis that is well-supported by empirical results. The clarity of presentation is generally good, though some technical details in Sections 3.2 and 4 could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon, making the key contributions accessible to the NIPS audience.

The main weakness is the poor quality of the figures in Section 5, which are difficult to read due to small fonts and low contrast. This significantly hinders the ability to assess the performance comparisons. The authors should increase the figure sizes and improve label visibility in the final version.

The originality of the approach is strong. While adaptive sampling has been studied before, the authors' formulation as a robust optimization problem with safe bounds provides a fundamentally different perspective that addresses the key limitation of previous approaches—lack of theoretical guarantees for the approximations. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly from [36, 23], by providing provable guarantees for their sampling method.

The significance of this work is high. The proposed method provides a practical way to achieve the theoretical benefits of adaptive sampling without incurring prohibitive computational costs. For large-scale machine learning applications, the O(n log n) overhead is often negligible compared to the cost of gradient evaluation (O(d) for d features), making this approach immediately applicable to real-world problems. The authors demonstrate significant speedups in both CD and SGD, with the most dramatic improvements observed in CD.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical insights are valuable, the approach is practical, and the empirical validation is thorough. The minor issue with figure quality does not detract from the paper's substantial contributions.

Pro: 
- Strong theoretical guarantees with novel robust optimization formulation
- Practical implementation with minimal computational overhead
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple problem types
- Clear differentiation from prior work with provable advantages

Con:
- Figure quality in Section 5 needs significant improvement
- The less pronounced improvement in SGD experiments (Figures 7-9) could benefit from more detailed explanation

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. I recommend acceptance.
