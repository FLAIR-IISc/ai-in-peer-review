This paper provides interesting theoretical and practical insights into randomized coordinate descent (RCD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with a new sampling strategy. In particular, Conclusion 2 of Theorem 3.2 is compelling: it indicates that the proposed sampling outperforms the commonly used L-based sampling, which is both widely adopted and well-studied. The paper is well-motivated and, if the claims hold broadly, the method could have meaningful impact on practice.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for improving sampling in RCD/SGD and a theory-driven proposal.
- Conclusion 2 in Theorem 3.2 is practically useful and directly relevant to how practitioners choose sampling distributions.
- The analysis appears to be carefully developed and the method seems straightforward to implement.

Main comments and suggestions
1) Correctness and notation
- Line 85: In the denominator of p_i^*, it should be x_k rather than x (use the current iterate).
- Line 135: Consider removing the subscript k from hat p and hat c for consistency with earlier notation, unless the dependence on k is essential and should be emphasized. In either case, please clarify the intended convention.

2) Comparison to uniform sampling
- The paper convincingly shows an advantage over L-based sampling. To contextualize the gain, please provide a result analogous to Conclusion 2 of Theorem 3.2 that compares the proposed sampling against uniform sampling (p_i = 1/n). This could be either a direct theoretical dominance result, a bound that isolates the regimes where your method strictly improves over uniform, or a clean corollary under standard assumptions (e.g., smooth convex objectives with coordinate-wise Lipschitz constants).
- If a full theoretical dominance result is not possible in general, a discussion of conditions under which your scheme improves over uniform (e.g., variance reduction or sharper constants tied to problem heterogeneity) would strengthen the contribution. Complementary empirical comparisons against uniform would also be valuable.

3) Complexity and implementation overhead
- Please discuss how the new sampling scheme affects total computational complexity for RCD/SGD, not just iteration complexity. In particular, if probabilities or related quantities (e.g., l_k and u_k) must be updated each iteration, what is the amortized per-iteration cost? If updates require O(n) work, does this negate the iteration savings unless updated lazily or with data structures (e.g., Fenwick trees/alias tables) enabling O(log n) sampling and incremental updates?
- For RCD, provide an end-to-end complexity bound (or at least a back-of-the-envelope estimate) for reaching an epsilon-optimal solution that includes the cost of maintaining and updating l_k and u_k. Clarify whether the O(n) update is needed every iteration or can be done periodically; if periodic, discuss the effect on convergence.
- For SGD, discuss whether probability updates depend on gradients or other quantities that are costly to maintain and how this impacts wall-clock performance.
- If possible, include wall-clock plots (not just iteration counts) and an ablation isolating the overhead of probability maintenance versus the resulting convergence speedup.

4) Figures and presentation
- Figures are currently unreadable (ticks, labels, numbers, and text are too small). Please enlarge them and move some to the appendix to free space. Ensure all axes, units, legends, and problem settings are clearly visible. Consider standardizing font sizes and line widths across figures.

5) Additional clarifications
- Provide a short algorithm box or pseudocode summarizing the sampling update and the main loop, including any data structures used for efficient sampling and updates.
- Briefly discuss robustness and sensitivity: how performance varies with problem conditioning, coordinate-wise Lipschitz heterogeneity, and any tunable parameters introduced by the method.
- If your theory hinges on particular assumptions (e.g., strong convexity, smoothness, bounded variance), make them explicit near the main theorems and comment on whether the results extend to nonconvex settings or only to convex cases.

Minor
- Proofread for small typographical issues and ensure consistent notation throughout (e.g., whether iterates carry subscript k, and whether quantities are defined per-iteration or globally).

Overall assessment
This is a promising and well-motivated paper with a practically relevant theoretical result. The main strengths are the clear improvement over L-based sampling and the potential for immediate impact on how RCD/SGD are implemented. To further strengthen the contribution, I encourage the authors to: (i) include a comparison to uniform sampling in both theory and experiments; (ii) analyze total computational complexity, including the overhead of probability updates; and (iii) improve figure readability and notation consistency. With these revisions, the paper would be significantly clearer and more compelling.
