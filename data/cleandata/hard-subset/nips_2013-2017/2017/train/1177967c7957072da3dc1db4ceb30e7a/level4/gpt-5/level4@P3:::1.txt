Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate the optimal gradient-based sampling distribution using computable lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (for CD) or component gradient norms (for SGD). At each iteration, the sampling distribution is obtained by solving a convex minimax problem that is provably optimal under the available bounds, with an O(n log n) algorithm. The authors show: (i) with full gradient information, the ideal adaptive sampling for CD chooses probabilities proportional to sqrt(Li)|∇i f| and yields up to a factor-n speed-up over fixed distributions; (ii) given only safe bounds, the proposed sampling p̂ achieves the best worst-case guarantee over all distributions consistent with the bounds and is never worse than Li-based fixed sampling (hence also never worse than uniform when Li are equal); (iii) the scheme integrates seamlessly with CD and SGD and uses an adaptive stepsize αk matched to the sampling.

This work builds directly on prior importance sampling for CD/SGD (e.g., Richtárik and Takáč 2016; Qu, Richtárik and Zhang; Csiba, Qu and Richtárik; Zhao and Zhang; Zhu; Perekrestenko et al.; Osokin et al.), as well as Gauss–Southwell-type rules (Nutini et al.) and screening rules for sparsity (Liu et al.; Ndiaye et al.). The authors’ main conceptual novelty is to formulate sampling selection under partial gradient information as a tractable minimax optimization with guarantees, contrasting with prior heuristics that use stale or upper bounds without worst-case dominance guarantees.

Quality
- Theory: The full-information analysis for CD is clear and shows that adaptive sampling and a coordinated stepsize αk yield guaranteed improvements over fixed distributions, matching the literature for SGD. The safe-bounds formulation is well-motivated; the minimax objective V(p,c)/‖c‖² and its characterization (including the geometric view) are elegant. The O(n log n) construction via sorting appears correct and practical.
- Assumptions: The paper provides concrete mechanisms for maintaining safe bounds in GLMs and shows O(n) update costs. The competitive ratio analysis (ρk) and the role of wk and vk clarify how bound tightness influences performance.
- Empirics: Experiments on GLMs show strong gains for CD (both in iteration counts and time), while SGD gains are smaller. The plots of vk/Tr[L] help interpret adaptive stepsize dynamics.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The role of αk and pk, and the transition from full information to safe bounds, are explained cleanly. A few notational and algorithmic details need correction (see Specific comments).

Originality
Formulating adaptive sampling under partial information as a convex minimax problem and deriving a distribution that is provably optimal with respect to the bounds—and guaranteed to dominate fixed sampling—appears novel. The resulting algorithm is simple and efficient.

Significance
Adaptive sampling is widely used in large-scale optimization. A method that is easy to plug into CD/SGD, has negligible overhead in many regimes, and comes with worst-case improvements over fixed sampling is valuable. The demonstrated speed-ups for CD suggest practical impact. The approach could also influence work on distributed/asynchronous settings and minibatching.

Specific comments and questions
- Lemma 2.1: The optimal αk for the quadratic upper bound seems to be missing a factor of 2. Please check the algebra in Eq. (3) and the minimizer.
- Example 3.1 vs. Eq. (7): Right before Example 3.1, you argue that choosing c at the lower or upper bounds can be poor. In the example, since (7) is a minimax in p (min over p, max over c), it would help to explicitly explain how the example demonstrates suboptimality of picking c = ℓ or c = u in place of solving the minimax. As written, it can be read as suggesting that extremal c looks better than uniform, which appears at odds with the intended message.
- Algorithm 4, line 4: Given m is initialized as max(ℓsort), will the condition [ℓsort]ℓ > m ever trigger? Please clarify the initialization and loop invariants so this branch is reachable when appropriate.
- Algorithm 4, line 7: It appears the variable should be usort rather than csort.
- Empirical comparisons: To better position the contribution, please include comparisons to other adaptive schemes beyond uniform and fixed Li-based distributions, e.g., gap-based/violation-based or gradient-based approximations [Allen-Zhu et al. 2017; Csiba–Qu–Richtárik 2015; Perekrestenko et al. 2017; Osokin et al. 2016; Zhao–Zhang 2015; Zhu 2016]. Even if exact apples-to-apples are hard, a few representative baselines would strengthen the case.
- SGD timing: You provide iteration-based plots for SGD but no wall-clock timings. Given the O(n log n) overhead, including timing would help assess practical trade-offs, especially in regimes with d ≪ n.
- Scope of “always better”: Theorems show p̂ is never worse than Li-based fixed sampling in the worst case over Ck, and often better. It might be clearer to state “never worse and often better” rather than “always better.”
- Overhead discussion: Please discuss regimes where O(n log n) may be non-negligible (e.g., tiny d, very sparse updates), and whether amortization, partial updates, or minibatch variants of your minimax could help.
- Competitive ratio: Provide empirical estimates of ρk or proxies (e.g., vk and wk) over time to relate theory to practice.
- Minor typos: “deep leaning” → “deep learning.”

- Please add: Csiba and Richtárik, “Importance Sampling for Minibatches,” arXiv:1602.05801, 2016.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation with strong guarantees (dominates fixed sampling under any safe bounds).
- Efficient algorithm with modest overhead and easy integration into CD/SGD.
- Solid empirical gains for CD; informative diagnostic plots (vk evolution).
- Unifies and clarifies the relationship between full-information optimal sampling and practical bounded-information approximations.

Cons
- Limited empirical comparisons to other adaptive/non-uniform baselines.
- No timing results for SGD; gains there are modest.
- A few algorithmic and presentation details need correction/clarification.
- Practicality depends on availability and tightness of safe bounds; only simple GLM settings are explored.

Overall assessment and recommendation
This is a strong and well-executed paper with a clear theoretical contribution and practical relevance. Addressing the minor issues above and adding broader empirical comparisons (and SGD timing) would further strengthen it.
