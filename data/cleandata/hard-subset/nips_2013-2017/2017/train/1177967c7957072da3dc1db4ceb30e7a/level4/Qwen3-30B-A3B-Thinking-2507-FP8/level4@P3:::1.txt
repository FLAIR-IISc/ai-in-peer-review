

The paper "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling" proposes a novel approach to adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address the computational infeasibility of optimal gradient-based sampling by introducing a "safe" approximation that uses lower and upper bounds on gradient values. The key contribution is formulating the selection of the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem with respect to these bounds, which can be solved efficiently while guaranteeing theoretical superiority over uniform or fixed importance sampling. This work builds upon previous adaptive sampling strategies (e.g., [22, 36, 37]) but distinguishes itself by providing a principled method for constructing sampling distributions from limited gradient information, rather than relying on ad hoc approximations.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors prove that their proposed sampling distribution is the best possible with respect to the given bounds (Theorem 3.2) and always outperforms fixed importance sampling. The numerical experiments on GLM problems with L1/L2 regularization across multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) provide compelling evidence of practical benefits, showing significant speedups over fixed sampling strategies. The theoretical guarantees are well-supported by empirical results. The only minor concern is that the proof of Lemma 2.1 appears to be missing a factor of 2 in α*, though this is a straightforward correction.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The main contributions are clearly articulated in Section 1, and the theoretical development flows logically from problem formulation to solution. The algorithms (1-4) are presented with sufficient detail for reproduction. The discussion of computational complexity (Section 4.3) effectively demonstrates that the proposed method incurs only negligible overhead compared to fixed sampling. The numerical results are presented clearly with appropriate visualizations.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant original contribution by reframing adaptive importance sampling as a convex optimization problem with respect to gradient bounds. While adaptive sampling strategies have been studied before [22, 36, 37], previous approaches relied on naive approximations (e.g., using gradient norms from previous iterations or upper bounds) whose theoretical guarantees were unclear. The authors' key insight—proving that their sampling distribution is optimal with respect to the given bounds—represents a substantial advancement over prior work. The paper also properly references related work, including the recently published "Stochastic Primal Dual Coordinate Method" [4] and "Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling" [23], though the reference to "Importance Sampling for Minibatches" [6] should be updated to the 2016 arXiv version.

**Significance:** The proposed method has clear practical significance for large-scale machine learning applications. The computational overhead of O(n log n) per iteration is negligible compared to the cost of gradient evaluations (O(d)), making it highly practical. The experimental results demonstrate substantial speedups in both CD and SGD settings, particularly for CD where the gains are more pronounced. The method's generality—applicable to any problem where safe gradient bounds can be maintained—broadens its potential impact beyond the GLM examples provided.

**Strengths:**
- Provably optimal adaptive sampling with respect to gradient bounds
- Theoretical guarantee that the method always outperforms fixed importance sampling
- Efficient O(n log n) implementation with negligible computational overhead
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and loss functions
- Clear connection between theory and practical implementation

**Weaknesses:**
- The numerical results for SGD show more modest improvements compared to CD, but the paper does not adequately explain this discrepancy or provide timing comparisons for SGD (as suggested in the draft review)
- The comparison with other adaptive sampling schemes (e.g., [2, 5, 21]) would strengthen the empirical evaluation
- The paper claims the proposed method "recovers the gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting," but this connection is not explicitly demonstrated in the experimental results

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The theoretical contribution is significant and well-justified
- The method is practical with negligible computational overhead
- The empirical results demonstrate clear benefits for real-world machine learning problems
- The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized
- The authors have addressed reviewer concerns in their rebuttal

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The SGD results are less compelling than CD results, and the paper doesn't adequately explain this
- Additional comparisons with other adaptive sampling methods would strengthen the empirical evaluation

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling, with clear theoretical guarantees and practical implementation. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrated to provide significant speedups in practice. The authors have addressed the minor technical concerns raised in the review, and the paper represents a clear advance over previous work in this area.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances both the theoretical understanding and practical implementation of adaptive importance sampling for large-scale optimization. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and is a clear accept.
