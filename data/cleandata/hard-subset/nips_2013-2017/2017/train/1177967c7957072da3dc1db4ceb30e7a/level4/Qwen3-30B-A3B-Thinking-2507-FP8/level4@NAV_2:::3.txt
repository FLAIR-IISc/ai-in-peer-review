

This paper addresses a timely challenge in adaptive importance sampling for optimization algorithms, extending prior work on gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37] while resolving computational infeasibility issues that plague existing methods. The authors propose a safe adaptive sampling scheme that uses provably tight gradient bounds to guarantee theoretical superiority over fixed sampling (Theorem 3.2) while maintaining computational efficiency (O(n log n) per iteration). The approach is rigorously validated through both theoretical analysis (Sections 2-3) and empirical results on generalized linear models (Section 5), demonstrating significant speedups in coordinate descent with minimal overhead.  

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2) are technically sound, proving the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling and recovering gradient-based sampling in the full-information limit. The experimental validation (Figures 3-6) provides robust evidence of practical benefits across multiple datasets and loss functions.  
*Originality:* The optimization formulation for sampling under safe bounds (Section 3.1) represents a novel contribution distinct from prior approximations [36, 23], which lack worst-case guarantees. The method’s generality (applicable to CD and SGD) and seamless integration into existing frameworks are well-motivated.  
*Significance:* The work addresses a critical bottleneck in scalable optimization, with demonstrated speedups on real-world datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20). The computational overhead (O(n log n)) is negligible for large-scale problems, making it immediately applicable to practitioners.  

**Weaknesses:**  
*Clarity:* Section 5 figures (particularly Figures 2-9) suffer from poor visibility due to small font sizes and low contrast, making convergence trends and error bars difficult to interpret. This undermines the empirical evaluation’s accessibility.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Strong theoretical foundation, practical implementation with minimal overhead, significant empirical gains, and clear relevance to NIPS themes.  
*Con:* Figure presentation issues that obscure key experimental findings.  

The paper makes a compelling contribution to adaptive optimization with high technical merit. Addressing the figure clarity concerns would significantly strengthen the submission.
