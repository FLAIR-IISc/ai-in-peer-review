- Proposes Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling (SAIS) that uses maintainable lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes to enable adaptive sampling without full gradient knowledge.  
- Formulates sampling as a min-max optimization over gradient uncertainty, yielding a distribution with worst-case performance guarantees.  
- Theoretical guarantee: SAIS is provably no worse than any fixed importance sampling (including uniform) (Theorem 3.2).  
- Efficient algorithm: computes the safe sampling distribution in O(n log n) time and recovers optimal gradient-based sampling when full information is available.  
- Demonstrates how to maintain gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).  
- Empirical results: strong validation for Coordinate Descent (CD), approaching oracle performance in iterations and outperforming fixed sampling in wall-clock time.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a clear separation of full-information vs limited-information cases.  
- Figures are small and hard to read; plots should be enlarged and labels/fonts increased for clarity.  
- SGD experiments show much smaller gains over uniform sampling than CD, but lack detailed analysis explaining this difference.  
- O(n log n) overhead per iteration can be significant for "short and fat" datasets (d << n); more discussion on trade-offs and regimes of benefit is needed.
