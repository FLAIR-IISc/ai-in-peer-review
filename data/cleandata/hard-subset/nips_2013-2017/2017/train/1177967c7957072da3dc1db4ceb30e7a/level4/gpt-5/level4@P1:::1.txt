Summary
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds on the (absolute) gradient coordinates for CD, or on component gradient norms for SGD. The sampling distribution is obtained as the solution to a convex min–max problem that is provably (i) optimal with respect to the available bounds, (ii) uniformly better than uniform or fixed (Li-based) importance sampling, and (iii) computable in O(n log n) time per iteration. With full information, the method recovers the classic gradient-based optimal sampling. The authors also present an efficient routine (Algorithm 4) to compute the safe distribution and show how to maintain conservative gradient bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs). Empirically, the approach achieves substantial speedups for CD on several datasets, with smaller but still reasonable gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to fixed non-uniform sampling for CD/SGD [18, 19, 24, 25], adaptive schemes such as gradient-based sampling for SGD [22, 36, 37], and gap-based sampling for CD [5, 23]. Unlike previous approaches that either rely on full gradients or ad hoc approximations (e.g., stale values or loose bounds), this paper formulates the selection of the sampling distribution under limited information as a principled convex optimization problem and provides theoretical dominance guarantees over fixed schemes.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of adaptive sampling under uncertainty via a min–max problem; the resulting distribution is optimal for the given bounds and strictly dominates fixed Li-based sampling.
- Strong theoretical support: in full-information settings, the scheme recovers gradient-based sampling; with limited information, it provides best-possible worst-case guarantees and competitive ratio bounds.
- Efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) with O(n log n) overhead and simple bound maintenance for common models (GLMs). In many regimes this overhead is negligible compared to per-iteration gradient work.
- Solid empirical results for CD, including useful diagnostics (vk trajectories) that corroborate the theoretical picture.

Weaknesses and questions
- Proof of Lemma 2.1: α* seems to be missing a factor of 2.
- Example 3.1: Immediately after stating that naively fixing c to the lower or upper bound can be suboptimal, the example suggests that, because (7) involves a maximization over c, picking c at the bounds might outperform uniform. Is the example intended to show the opposite? The message is unclear; please clarify.
- Algorithm 4, line 4: Since m = max(ℓsort), will that condition ever be satisfied? Please double-check the logic and indexing.
- Algorithm 4, line 7: Should this refer to usort rather than csort?
- Empirical comparisons: The experimental section would be strengthened by comparisons with additional adaptive baselines (e.g., [2], [5], [21]) and with fixed importance sampling using non-uniform, data-driven distributions (beyond Li-based).
- Why are there no timing results for SGD? Since the claim is “negligible extra cost,” wall-clock comparisons for SGD would be informative, especially as SGD’s iteration cost can be lower than CD’s.
- References: The title of reference [14] appears incorrect. Please fix. Also, consider adding Csiba and Richtárik (2016), “Importance Sampling for Minibatches” (arXiv), which is closely related.
- Practicality of O(n log n): While often negligible, the extra O(n log n) work per iteration may not be small when d ≪ n or when sampling frequency is very high. Some discussion on amortization, partial updates, or subsampling to reduce this overhead would be useful.

Quality
- The technical development is sound, with clean reductions to convex min–max problems and clear dominance guarantees. The full-information recovery is reassuring.
- The competitive ratio analysis is helpful; however, the bound in Lemma 3.6 relies on a relative accuracy condition. A brief discussion of when this condition holds in practice would add value.

Clarity
- Overall well-written and organized. The treatment of Algorithms 1–4 and the GLM bound-tracking is clear and practically oriented.
- Please address the notational issues and possible typos highlighted above (Lemma 2.1, Example 3.1, Algorithm 4).

Originality
- The safe adaptive sampling formulation via min–max optimization is novel and distinguishes the work from prior gradient-based or heuristically adaptive schemes.

Significance
- The approach is general, theoretically justified, and practically relevant. The empirical improvements for CD are substantial; the benefit for SGD is smaller but still meaningful. Given the ubiquity of CD/SGD, this contribution is likely to be adopted and extended.

Pros
- Principled and provably superior adaptive sampling under limited information.
- Efficient algorithm with practical bound-maintenance strategies for GLMs.
- Strong empirical performance for CD and informative diagnostics.

Cons
- Minor correctness/clarity issues (Lemma 2.1, Example 3.1, Algorithm 4).
- Missing comparisons to several adaptive baselines and no SGD timing.
- Overhead discussion could better delineate regimes where cost is not negligible.

Minor recommendations
- Correct the title of reference [14].
- Add Csiba and Richtárik (2016), “Importance Sampling for Minibatches,” arXiv.
- Consider reporting results with additional non-uniform fixed baselines.
- Provide SGD wall-clock timings and discuss overhead mitigation strategies.
