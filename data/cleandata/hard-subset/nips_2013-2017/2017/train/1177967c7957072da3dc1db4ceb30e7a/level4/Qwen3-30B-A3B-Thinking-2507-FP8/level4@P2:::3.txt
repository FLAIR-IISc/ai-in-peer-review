

This paper introduces a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" framework for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, addressing a key limitation in existing adaptive sampling approaches. The authors propose an efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling that leverages safe bounds on gradient information, providing theoretical guarantees while maintaining computational feasibility. The work builds on prior research in adaptive importance sampling [22, 36, 37] but addresses the critical gap that previous methods either lacked theoretical guarantees or incurred prohibitive computational costs.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) proving that gradient-based sampling in CD can provide up to n-fold speedups over fixed sampling; (2) developing a generic, efficient adaptive sampling strategy that can be integrated into both CD and SGD; (3) demonstrating practical implementation on structured optimization problems; and (4) providing comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and loss functions.

Strengths: The paper excels in theoretical quality, establishing rigorous proofs (Theorems 3.2, 3.4) showing that the proposed method is always better than uniform or fixed importance sampling, with a bounded competitive ratio. The computational complexity analysis (O(n log n) per iteration) is particularly compelling, as this overhead is often negligible compared to gradient computation costs (O(d) for d-dimensional data). The paper demonstrates strong empirical significance, showing substantial speedups across multiple datasets and loss functions while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The clarity of the presentation is excellent, with well-structured sections, clear notation, and detailed proofs in the appendix. The authors effectively contrast their approach with prior work, addressing the key limitation of previous adaptive sampling methods that either lacked theoretical guarantees or were computationally infeasible.

The paper's significance is substantial, as it provides a practical solution to a fundamental challenge in large-scale optimization. The method can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness on real-world machine learning problems including generalized linear models with L1/L2 regularization. The experimental section is thorough, covering multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) and loss functions (square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss).

Weaknesses: The figures in Section 5 are problematic, as they are difficult to interpret due to small font sizes and insufficient resolution. The authors should increase the figure dimensions and label sizes to ensure readability. While the paper demonstrates strong theoretical and empirical results for CD, the SGD experiments show more modest improvements compared to CD. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation for this discrepancy, as it affects the perceived significance of the approach for SGD applications. The paper could strengthen its originality claim by more explicitly contrasting with recent adaptive sampling methods [36, 37] and clarifying the specific theoretical advances over these approaches.

The paper addresses all four key criteria effectively:
- Quality: Strong theoretical foundations with comprehensive empirical validation
- Clarity: Well-organized, with clear notation and detailed proofs
- Originality: Novel approach to using safe gradient bounds for adaptive sampling
- Significance: Practical, widely applicable method with substantial speedups

The authors have made a significant contribution to optimization for large-scale machine learning, providing a theoretically sound and practically efficient solution to adaptive importance sampling. The method's computational efficiency (O(n log n) per iteration) and guaranteed improvement over fixed sampling make it particularly compelling for real-world applications.

Pro arguments for acceptance:
1. Strong theoretical guarantees with clear proofs
2. Practical implementation with minimal computational overhead
3. Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and loss functions
4. Clear, well-structured presentation with detailed proofs
5. Significant potential impact for large-scale machine learning applications

Con arguments against acceptance:
1. Figures in Section 5 require improvement for readability
2. SGD results show more modest improvements compared to CD
3. Could better clarify how this work differs from recent adaptive sampling approaches

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed method is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically validated across multiple scenarios. The minor issues with figure presentation can be easily addressed in the camera-ready version. Given the paper's strong technical quality, clear presentation, and practical significance, I recommend acceptance.
