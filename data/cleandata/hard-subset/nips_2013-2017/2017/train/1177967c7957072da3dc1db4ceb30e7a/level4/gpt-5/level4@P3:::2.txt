Summary
This paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate the ideal gradient-based sampling—known to be optimal in expectation but computationally infeasible—using per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds on (component-wise) gradient magnitudes. The authors formulate the choice of sampling distribution as a convex min–max problem over these bounds, derive a closed-form structure of the optimal solution, and provide an O(n log n) algorithm based on sorting. They prove that the resulting sampling is (i) optimal w.r.t. the information available (best worst-case guarantee over the admissible gradient set), (ii) always at least as good as Li-based (fixed) importance sampling, and (iii) recovers full gradient-based sampling when bounds are tight. They show how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs), and present experiments for both CD and SGD, observing substantial speed-ups for CD and smaller gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on and improves adaptive sampling methods for SGD based on gradient magnitudes (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, 2015) and adaptive CD strategies using duality gaps or approximate importance sampling (e.g., Csiba et al., Richtárik & Takáč, Perekrestenko et al.). Unlike previous approaches relying on heuristic or stale estimates, the proposed min–max “safe” formulation yields guarantees that the adaptive distribution is never worse than fixed (Li-based) sampling, with an efficient algorithm and clean characterization of optimality.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The min–max formulation over safe gradient bounds and its solution (including the “water-filling”-like construction and O(n log n) algorithm) appear new and elegant.
- Theoretical guarantees: Best worst-case guarantee relative to the information set; dominance over Li-based sampling; recovery of full-information optimal sampling; competitive-ratio bounds.
- Practicality: Simple integration into CD/SGD and concrete recipes to maintain bounds for GLMs with negligible extra overhead in common regimes.
- Empirical evidence: Consistent speed-ups in CD across losses/regularizers and real datasets; analysis of the role of adaptive stepsizes.

Weaknesses
- Limited SGD gains: Improvements for SGD are modest; discussion on when to expect benefits could be strengthened.
- Complexity accounting: The paper lacks a clear end-to-end complexity bound to an ε-optimal solution that includes the O(n) bound updates and O(n log n) sampling computation.
- Clarity/notation: A few minor inconsistencies/typos; figures are hard to read; some proofs (e.g., min–max interchange) deserve more explicit conditions.
- Scope of safe bounds: Beyond GLMs, guidance for other models and tighter bounds would increase applicability.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound and well-motivated, with clear optimization-based derivations, matching algorithms, and meaningful guarantees. The empirical evaluation supports the claims for CD. The paper would benefit from an explicit convergence complexity bound incorporating the extra per-iteration costs and from deeper analysis of when SGD benefits materially.

Clarity
Overall well organized, but figure readability is poor, and a few notational glitches are distracting (see detailed comments). Adding a short “complexity to accuracy” subsection would improve clarity.

Originality
High. The min–max safe sampling formulation and the efficient algorithm with provable dominance over Li-based sampling are, to my knowledge, new.

Significance
High for CD: the approach is generic, easy to adopt, and provides practical speed-ups with guarantees. Moderate for SGD: benefits seem problem-dependent; guidance on when to apply would help.

Detailed comments and suggestions
1) Line 85: In the denominator of p∗i, use xk instead of x for consistency with the iteration.
2) Line 135: Consider dropping the subscript k in p̂ and ĉ within Theorem 3.2 to keep notation uncluttered (or state explicitly that p̂ and ĉ refer to iteration k).
3) Figures: Axes ticks, labels, and legends are too small to read. Please enlarge figures; move some panels to the appendix to free space.
4) Uniform sampling comparison: Theorem 3.2(ii) shows dominance over Li-based sampling. Remark 3.3 covers the uniform case when Li = L. It would be valuable to add an explicit corollary that compares V(p̂, c) to uniform sampling p = 1/n for general heterogeneous Li, or at least to state a bound of the form V(p̂, c) ≤ Buniform(L, c) · ∥c∥² with a transparent constant, to make the uniform comparison explicit beyond the equal-Li setting.
5) End-to-end complexity: Please state overall complexity (iterations × per-iteration cost) to reach an ε-optimal solution for CD and SGD under standard assumptions (e.g., L-smooth and µ-strongly convex). For CD, expressing rates in terms of vk (e.g., expected decrease ∝ 1/vk) and then bounding vk (Lmin ≤ vk ≤ Tr[L]) would yield a clean complexity bound. Include the O(n) cost for maintaining bounds and O(n log n) for computing p̂k, and discuss regimes (e.g., d = Ω(n)) where amortized overhead is O(log n) relative to a coordinate gradient evaluation. A similar treatment for SGD (with standard step-size choices) would clarify practical trade-offs.
6) Proof details: When invoking min–max interchange (Sion/Komiya), please state continuity/convexity/compactness conditions verifying applicability. This aids reproducibility.
7) Bounds beyond GLMs: The paper hints at broader applicability. A short discussion on other models (e.g., generalized additive models, deep linear layers, or proximal composite problems) and how to maintain safe bounds (even if looser) would broaden impact.
8) Practicalities: Comment on initialization (e.g., l0 = 0, u0 = ∞) and how quickly performance departs from fixed sampling; memory/storage for bounds; and whether p̂k can be updated incrementally instead of recomputed from scratch.
9) Baselines: Consider including recent adaptive CD/SGD baselines (e.g., gap-safe or optimality-violation sampling) in the plots, or at least a brief discussion of relative pros/cons.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled formulation with strong guarantees and efficient algorithm.
- Demonstrated practical benefits for CD on real datasets.
- Broad applicability and easy integration into existing methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical gains for SGD and lack of guidance on when gains are expected.
- Missing explicit ε-complexity accounting that includes the adaptive overhead.
- Presentation issues (figures, minor notation errors).

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a solid and practically useful contribution to adaptive sampling for CD, with clean theory and promising experiments. Addressing the clarity and complexity-accounting suggestions would further strengthen the work.
