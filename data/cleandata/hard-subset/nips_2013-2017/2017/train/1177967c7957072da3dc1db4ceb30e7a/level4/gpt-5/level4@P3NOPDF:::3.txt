This paper studies safe adaptive importance sampling and proposes a generic method that can be integrated with a range of existing algorithms. The work combines theoretical analysis with empirical validation to support the proposed strategy.

Strengths
- Topical and relevant: Safe adaptation in importance sampling is an active area, and the paper addresses a concrete practical need.
- Generality: The method appears modular and compatible with many existing estimators, which increases its potential impact.
- Theoretical support: The inclusion of formal guarantees is a major plus. The results help clarify when the approach is provably sound and what trade-offs are involved.
- Empirical validation: The practical experiments substantiate the theory and demonstrate utility beyond synthetic settings.
- Clarity of motivation: The problem is well motivated, and the need for “safe” mechanisms in adaptive importance sampling is clearly argued.

Weaknesses and suggestions for improvement
- Figure readability: The plots in Section 5 are too small and the font sizes on axes and legends are difficult to read. Please enlarge the figures, increase font sizes and line widths, and ensure that the color palette is accessible (e.g., to color-blind readers). Adding clear captions with take-home messages and consistent panel labeling would help.
- Definition of “safe”: While the high-level goal is clear, the paper would benefit from a precise, early statement of the safety criterion used (e.g., finite-variance guarantees, bounded bias under adaptation, conservative risk bounds) and a short discussion of how this compares to alternative notions in the literature.
- Assumptions: Make the assumptions underlying the theoretical results explicit and easy to find. It would help to list them in one place, explain their necessity, and comment on their realism in common applications.
- Trade-offs: Clarify the cost of safety (variance, bias, or sample complexity) relative to standard adaptive importance sampling methods. Summarizing the key trade-offs in a short paragraph or a table would guide practitioners.
- Comparisons: Ensure that empirical comparisons include strong baselines (e.g., non-adaptive IS, adaptive IS without safeguards, self-normalized variants, control variate approaches). If some baselines are omitted, briefly justify why.
- Sensitivity and ablations: It would be useful to include ablations that isolate the contribution of each component of the proposed method and sensitivity analyses to key hyperparameters (e.g., adaptation frequency, step sizes, clipping levels, if relevant). Reporting multiple random seeds with error bars would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Scalability and overhead: Please discuss the computational and memory overhead introduced by the safety mechanism and how it scales with dimension and sample size. If applicable, report wall-clock times alongside statistical efficiency.
- Practical guidance: Provide concise guidelines for choosing hyperparameters and diagnosing failure modes. A small “practitioner’s checklist” (e.g., recommended defaults and when to use them) would increase adoption.
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code, scripts, and configurations. If code cannot be shared, include detailed pseudocode, data preprocessing steps, and all hyperparameter settings.

Questions for the authors
- What precise safety guarantees are provided (e.g., high-probability bounds, uniform-in-time guarantees), and do they hold under model misspecification?
- How sensitive is the method to mismatch between proposal and target in high-dimensional settings?
- Does the approach interact with common variance-reduction techniques (control variates, stratification, resampling), and can it be stacked with them without violating the guarantees?
- Are there regimes where the safety mechanism is overly conservative and slows convergence? If so, can adaptivity be relaxed in a controlled way?

Minor presentation comments
- Ensure notation is consistent across sections; introduce all symbols upon first use.
- Cross-reference theorems, assumptions, and lemmas clearly, and provide brief proof sketches in the main text for key results to aid intuition.
- Improve figure captions to be self-contained and interpretative (what to look at and why it matters).

Overall assessment
This is a timely and potentially impactful contribution. The proposed method’s generality, together with theoretical guarantees and supporting experiments, makes it a compelling submission. The primary issues are presentation-related, especially the readability of figures and the need for clearer statements of the safety criterion, assumptions, and trade-offs. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and its value to practitioners. Pending these improvements, I view the work favorably.
