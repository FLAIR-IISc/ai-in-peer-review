This paper addresses the important problem of adaptive importance sampling, a technique widely used to accelerate optimization algorithms for large-scale applications. The authors propose an efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, offering provable guarantees and demonstrating its effectiveness in both theoretical analysis and extensive numerical experiments with coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The work builds upon existing adaptive sampling strategies [22, 5, 26, 10, 21, 23], acknowledging their computational limitations and potential for worse performance than uniform sampling [23, 3]. This paper distinguishes itself by providing a computationally feasible approximation with theoretical guarantees of improvement over static sampling schemes.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The proposed sampling scheme is theoretically sound, demonstrably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling (Section 3, Theorem 3.2), and efficiently computable. The authors clearly articulate the connection between gradient-based sampling and the practical limitations that motivate their approach.  The analysis of CD (Section 2) provides a strong theoretical foundation, showing potential speedups of up to a factor of the dimension ‘n’.  The algorithm is generic and seamlessly integrates with existing optimization methods (CD and SGD). The empirical evaluation (Section 5) is comprehensive, covering various datasets and regularization techniques, and validates the theoretical findings. The inclusion of analysis regarding the quality of the safe gradient bounds (Figures 3-9) adds further insight.

The paper does have some weaknesses, though these are relatively minor. While the theoretical contributions are substantial, the presentation can be dense and assumes a strong background in optimization.  The notation is quite heavy, which can make following the derivations challenging. As noted in the original submission, the figures in Section 5 are difficult to read, hindering a quick assessment of the numerical results. The discussion of related work could be slightly expanded, perhaps mentioning recent advances in variance reduction techniques within SGD, such as SAGA or SVRG [Schmidt et al., 2015; Defazio et al., 2014], to better contextualize the proposed method. It would also be useful to briefly discuss the limitations of the safe bound approach - under what conditions might the bounds be overly conservative and thus diminish the benefits of adaptive sampling?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous analysis and provable guarantees for the proposed sampling scheme.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The method addresses a practical challenge in large-scale optimization and offers a computationally efficient solution.
*   **Novelty:** The approach of formulating the sampling problem as a convex optimization problem to ensure safety and optimality is novel.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Generality:** The scheme is applicable to a wide range of optimization algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Dense Presentation:** The theoretical analysis may be challenging for readers without a strong background in optimization.
*   **Figure Readability:** The figures in Section 5 require improvement for clear interpretation.
*   **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** A more thorough discussion of potential limitations of the safe bound approach would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contributions are significant, the technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are compelling. Addressing the minor weaknesses regarding figure readability and expanding on the limitations of the safe bound approach would further strengthen the manuscript. This work represents a valuable addition to the literature on adaptive importance sampling and has the potential to impact a broad range of machine learning applications.
