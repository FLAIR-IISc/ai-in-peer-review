### **Summary**

This paper introduces a "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for stochastic optimization methods, specifically coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core problem addressed is that optimal adaptive sampling schemes (e.g., based on full gradient information) are computationally prohibitive, while common approximations can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors' key idea is to work with efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient components (or their norms). They frame the problem of choosing the best sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem: find the distribution that maximizes the expected progress in the worst case, over all possible true gradients that lie within the given bounds. This principled approach leads to a sampling strategy that is (i) provably the best possible given the limited information, (ii) guaranteed to be at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling (including uniform sampling), and (iii) can be computed efficiently via a novel O(n log n) algorithm. The authors demonstrate how to derive and update the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide extensive experiments showing significant speedups over fixed sampling strategies.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization problem (Equation 7) is novel and provides a theoretically sound way to handle the uncertainty inherent in using gradient bounds. This is a significant conceptual contribution that moves beyond heuristic approximations.

2.  **Quality of Theoretical Results:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees. The "safety" guarantee—that the proposed sampling is provably never worse than fixed Li-based importance sampling—is a crucial and highly desirable property. It addresses a major drawback of previous adaptive methods, where approximations could inadvertently slow down convergence. The derivation of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve the optimization problem makes the entire approach practical.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of first presenting the ideal (but infeasible) adaptive sampling scheme (Section 2) and then developing their practical, safe approximation as a principled solution to the information gap (Section 3). The connection between the theory and the practical implementation for GLMs (Section 4) is also clearly explained.

4.  **Practical Relevance:** The proposed method is generic and can be integrated into existing CD and SGD frameworks. The empirical results, particularly for coordinate descent, demonstrate substantial performance gains over uniform sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time, confirming that the computational overhead of the method is manageable and worthwhile.

### **Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement)**

1.  **Experimental Comparisons:** While the experiments clearly show the benefit over uniform sampling and the ideal (infeasible) sampling, the evaluation would be significantly stronger with comparisons to other state-of-the-art adaptive sampling schemes. For instance, comparing against the duality gap-based sampling for CD (e.g., Csiba et al. [5], Perekrestenko et al. [23]) or other adaptive methods would provide a more complete picture of where this method stands. Additionally, the fixed sampling baseline is uniform sampling (as Li=L), but a comparison against non-uniform fixed sampling (Li-based sampling, when Li are not all equal) would be more comprehensive.

2.  **Missing Timing Results for SGD:** The paper claims the method is efficient for both CD and SGD. However, timing results are only provided for the CD experiments (Figure 6). The absence of wall-clock time plots for SGD (Section 5, Figures 7-9) makes it difficult to assess the practical overhead and real-world benefit in that setting.

### **Overall Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, theoretically sound, and practical method for adaptive importance sampling. The core idea of using a minimax formulation to guarantee safety is a strong contribution. Despite the room for improvement in the experimental comparison section, the strengths of the paper in terms of originality, theoretical quality, and clarity are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
