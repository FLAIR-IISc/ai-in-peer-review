- Summary: Proposes "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling" that uses gradient magnitude bounds to compute a minimax-optimal sampling distribution efficiently (O(n log n)), improving adaptive sampling practicality for CD and SGD.

- Strength: Novel minimax formulation provides a principled way to derive adaptive sampling under gradient uncertainty.

- Strength: Theoretical "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2) shows the method is never worse than Li-based importance sampling.

- Strength: Practical and efficient — O(n log n) sampling algorithm and O(n) per-iteration bound updates for GLMs.

- Strength: Extensive experiments across datasets, losses, and regularizers show faster convergence in iterations and wall-clock time.

- Weakness: Figures 2–9 are low resolution and unreadable, preventing proper evaluation of empirical claims.

- Weakness: No analysis of total computational complexity to reach ε-optimality; impact of per-iteration overhead on overall runtime is not discussed.

- Weakness: Comparison to uniform sampling could be clarified and a corollary highlighting superiority in the equal-Li case should be stated more explicitly.

- Minor: Typo on Line 85 — denominator `∇f(x)` likely should be `∇f(xk)`.

- Minor: Notation consistency — solution `(p̂, ĉ)` might be clearer as `(p̂k, ĉk)` for the k-th iteration.

- Final assessment: Strong, well-motivated, and practical contribution with provable guarantees; presentation issues (figures) and missing total-cost analysis need addressing.

- Arguments for acceptance: novel minimax approach, strong safety guarantee, efficient algorithm with GLM applicability, and convincing empirical improvements.

- Arguments for rejection: unreadable figures hinder empirical validation and lack of overall complexity analysis leaves an important question unanswered.
