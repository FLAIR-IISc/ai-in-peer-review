The authors present a "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent and stochastic gradient methods. The paper is well-written and provides a nice contribution to the study of importance sampling techniques.

The main idea of the paper is to develop an efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling, which is based on safe bounds on the gradient. The proposed sampling distribution is provably the best sampling with respect to the given bounds, always better than uniform sampling and fixed importance sampling, and can be computed efficiently for negligible extra cost.

The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their approach, including the optimization formulation for sampling and the proposed safe adaptive importance sampling strategy.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a generic and efficient adaptive importance sampling strategy that can be applied to both CD and SGD methods. The authors also demonstrate how the novel scheme can efficiently be integrated into existing algorithms, such as CD and SGD, on an important class of structured optimization problems.

The experimental evaluation of the proposed adaptive sampling scheme on relevant machine learning tasks is also a strong point of the paper. The results show that the proposed adaptive sampling scheme significantly outperforms fixed importance sampling in iterations and time, and that the introduced computational overhead is small enough to outperform fixed importance sampling in terms of total computation time.

The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results, and the paper is a good scientific contribution to the field.

However, there are a few minor issues that need to be addressed. For example, the proof of Lemma 2.1 seems to be missing a factor of 2 in alpha^*. Additionally, Example 3.1 is confusing, and it is not clear what the example is intending to show.

In terms of originality, the paper provides a novel formulation of selecting the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem and presents an efficient algorithm to solve it. The authors also provide a new perspective on the problem of adaptive importance sampling, which is different from previous approaches.

The significance of the paper is high, as it provides a new and efficient adaptive importance sampling strategy that can be applied to a wide range of machine learning problems. The results of the paper can be used to improve the performance of CD and SGD methods, which are widely used in machine learning.

Overall, I think this paper is a clear accept. The authors have addressed my questions and comments, and the paper provides a significant contribution to the field of machine learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results.
* The paper provides a generic and efficient adaptive importance sampling strategy that can be applied to both CD and SGD methods.
* The experimental evaluation of the proposed adaptive sampling scheme on relevant machine learning tasks is strong.
* The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

Arguments against acceptance:

* Minor issues with the proof of Lemma 2.1 and Example 3.1.
* The paper could benefit from comparisons to other adaptive sampling schemes and against fixed importance sampling with a non-uniform distribution.
* There are no timing results for SGD.

However, these issues are minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the paper. Therefore, I think this paper is a clear accept.
