- Paper proposes a safe, adaptive importance sampling scheme for CD and SGD using per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds on coordinate gradients or component gradient norms.  
- Sampling distribution is computed by solving a convex min–max problem that gives the best worst-case guarantee given the bounds.  
- The method provably outperforms fixed Li-based importance sampling and recovers full-information gradient sampling when exact gradients are available.  
- An O(n log n) algorithm is provided to compute the optimal sampling distribution, with efficient maintenance of bounds for GLMs.  
- Empirical results show substantial speedups for CD and modest gains for SGD; ablations on stepsizes clarify sources of improvement.  
- Strength: clear conceptual contribution and simple, efficient algorithmic solution.  
- Strength: sound theoretical guarantees including “best worst-case” property and never worse than Li-based sampling.  
- Strength: practical applicability with negligible overhead when d = Ω(n) and straightforward integration into CD/SGD.  
- Weakness: main theoretical comparison is to Li-based sampling; lacks a general direct comparison to uniform sampling when Li are unequal.  
- Weakness: total end-to-end complexity (including bound maintenance) to reach ε-optimality is not explicitly stated.  
- Weakness: figures are too small/illegible, reducing clarity of empirical section.  
- Weakness: limited empirical benefits demonstrated for SGD; more analysis/guidance on when SGD gains occur is needed.  
- Weakness: reliance on safe bounds — more discussion on bound tightness effects and alternatives to Cauchy–Schwarz bounds is needed.  
- Specific comment: fix notation in optimal CD sampling expression—denominator should use ∇f(xk), not ∇f(x).  
- Specific comment: in Theorem 3.2, clarify or remove subscripts on p̂ and ĉ to indicate per-iteration nature.  
- Specific comment: enlarge figures, reduce panels, and move some plots to appendix for readability.  
- Specific suggestion: add a theorem or statement directly comparing p̂ to uniform sampling p = 1/n in the general Li≠L case.  
- Specific suggestion: provide end-to-end convergence rates and total runtime bounds including per-iteration overheads and when overheads are negligible.  
- Minor clarifications: add pseudocode for the SGD variant, discuss initialization of bounds, robustness to loose bounds, and heuristics to tighten them.  
- Minor suggestion: if possible, include empirical comparison to heuristics that use only upper bounds to highlight min–max benefit.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel principled formulation, clean guarantees, efficient algorithm, strong CD empirical gains, and practical ease of integration.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited SGD improvements, missing uniform-sampling theory comparison, figure readability, and some notational issues.  
- Overall: solid and timely contribution with a leaning toward acceptance if uniform comparison, figure readability, and end-to-end complexity are addressed.
