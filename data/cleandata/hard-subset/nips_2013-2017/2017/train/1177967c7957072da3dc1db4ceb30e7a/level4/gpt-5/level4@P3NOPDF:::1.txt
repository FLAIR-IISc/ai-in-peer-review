Summary
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent and stochastic gradient methods that relies only on lower and upper bounds on gradient coordinates. The optimal sampling distribution is obtained by solving a convex optimization problem defined by these bounds, yielding a provably optimal strategy under the available information. The method is shown to dominate uniform and fixed (non-adaptive) importance sampling derived from the same bounds, while being computable with negligible overhead. Although adaptive importance sampling has been studied, the formulation of sampling selection as a convex program with an efficient algorithmic solution is novel and compelling. The paper is clearly written and makes a meaningful contribution to adaptive sampling for first-order methods.

Strengths
- Novel convex optimization formulation of optimal sampling based on gradient bounds, with clear theoretical guarantees.
- Sound “safe” design: performance improves over uniform/fixed sampling whenever the bounds contain useful information.
- Algorithmic efficiency and practical implementability are emphasized.
- The presentation is generally clear, with proofs and motivation well structured.

Weaknesses and questions
- Empirical evaluation could better situate the method among existing adaptive schemes and stronger fixed baselines.
- A few clarity and correctness issues in examples, proofs, and algorithm pseudocode need attention.
- The claim of “negligible extra cost” would benefit from more detailed timing evidence, especially for SGD.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Proof of Lemma 2.1: The expression for α* appears to be missing a factor of 2. Please check the derivation and correct the statement (and any downstream constants that depend on it).
- Example 3.1 and Eq. (7): Since (7) is a maximization, Example 3.1 seems to suggest that setting c to the upper or lower bound could outperform uniform sampling. This appears to conflict with the preceding claim that the naive choice c = l or c = u can be suboptimal. Please clarify the intent of this example:
  - Is the example meant to show that naive choices can sometimes help relative to uniform but are still suboptimal versus the proposed optimal distribution?
  - If so, make this explicit and perhaps include a complementary example where naive choices clearly underperform the proposed strategy.
- Algorithm 4:
  - Line 4: Given m = max(l^{sort}), the condition as written may never be satisfied. Please verify the condition and either adjust the definition of m or the inequality so that the branch is reachable as intended.
  - Line 7: It likely should reference u^{sort} instead of c^{sort}. Please correct the notation.
- Experimental comparisons:
  - Include comparisons to other adaptive sampling schemes (e.g., references [2], [5], [21]) to position your approach empirically against strong adaptive baselines.
  - Add a fixed non-uniform importance sampling baseline derived from available bounds (e.g., proportional to l, u, or a smoothed combination), not only uniform, to demonstrate the advantage over more informed fixed strategies.
- Timing for SGD: Please report wall-clock time and per-iteration overhead for the SGD experiments, not only iteration counts. This is particularly important to substantiate the claim of negligible extra cost. A breakdown highlighting the overhead of computing and updating the sampling distribution would be helpful.
- Clarify assumptions and scope:
  - State explicitly when bounds are static vs. updated over time, and how often the sampling distribution is recomputed in practice.
  - Discuss robustness when bounds are loose or occasionally violated; if feasible, include a sensitivity analysis.
- Minor issues and references:
  - Correct the title of the paper in reference [14].
  - Add: Csiba and Richtárik, “Importance Sampling for Minibatches,” arXiv, 2016.
  - Ensure notation consistency (e.g., vector vs. scalar symbols, sorting superscripts) and define all variables on first use.
  - If space permits, provide a brief complexity summary comparing your per-iteration overhead to that of uniform and previously proposed adaptive methods.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed paper with a clear and novel contribution: an optimal, safe adaptive importance sampling scheme derived from a convex program over gradient bounds, with both theoretical and algorithmic substance. With improved empirical comparisons, corrected minor issues in proofs and pseudocode, and added timing results for SGD, the paper would be even stronger.
