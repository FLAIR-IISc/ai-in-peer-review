

This paper addresses safe adaptive importance sampling (SAIS), an increasingly important problem in probabilistic inference and reinforcement learning where maintaining safety constraints during sampling is critical. The authors propose a novel framework that integrates seamlessly with existing importance sampling (IS) algorithms, offering both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The work is timely and addresses a genuine gap in the literature, as most prior methods focus on variance reduction without explicit safety considerations.  

The paper’s primary strengths lie in its theoretical rigor and practical applicability. The authors establish convergence guarantees under mild assumptions, demonstrating that SAIS maintains safety constraints (e.g., bounded importance weights or risk measures) while asymptotically minimizing variance. This is a significant advancement over heuristic approaches that often sacrifice safety for efficiency. Crucially, the method is designed to be *generic*: it can be incorporated into diverse IS pipelines (e.g., sequential Monte Carlo, adaptive MCMC) without requiring algorithm-specific modifications. The empirical section validates this generality across multiple domains, including Bayesian inference on synthetic data and policy evaluation in reinforcement learning benchmarks. Results show consistent variance reduction (up to 40% lower than baselines) while strictly adhering to safety thresholds, with ablation studies confirming the necessity of each component. The experiments are well-designed, using standard metrics (e.g., effective sample size, risk violation rates), and the code is publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.  

Despite these strengths, the paper suffers from a critical flaw that undermines its contribution: the figures in Section 5 (particularly Figures 3–5) are rendered at insufficient resolution and with illegibly small labels. At standard print or screen sizes, key elements—such as error bars, axis labels, and curve annotations—are nearly indistinguishable. For instance, in Figure 4, the "SAIS" and "Baseline" legend entries are truncated, and the y-axis label "Variance Reduction (%)" is too small to read without zooming. This severely impedes the reader’s ability to assess the empirical results, which are central to the paper’s claims. The figures should be regenerated at a minimum of 1.5× the current resolution, with all labels increased to at least 12pt font size (14pt recommended for axes). Additionally, the line styles and markers should be made more distinct (e.g., using solid/dashed lines and varied symbols) to ensure clarity in black-and-white print. Without these adjustments, the empirical evidence loses credibility, as readers cannot verify the reported improvements.  

A few minor points warrant attention. The motivation in Section 1 could better distinguish SAIS from related work on *constrained* IS (e.g., [Cite], [Cite]), which often assumes known constraint functions. Clarifying how SAIS handles *unknown* or *adaptive* constraints would strengthen the novelty claim. The theoretical section (Section 3) briefly references "stability" but lacks a formal definition—adding a short paragraph on how stability relates to safety constraints would improve precision. Finally, while the method’s generality is emphasized, the experiments could more explicitly demonstrate integration with a non-trivial IS algorithm (e.g., adaptive IS in [Cite]), rather than relying on a custom implementation.  

The paper’s core contribution—providing a theoretically grounded, plug-and-play SAIS framework—is compelling and addresses a real need in the community. The empirical results, once figures are fixed, would robustly support the claims. However, the current figure quality is unacceptable for a venue like NeurIPS, where visual clarity is paramount for evaluating methodological advances. This issue is not merely aesthetic; it directly challenges the paper’s validity, as readers cannot trust results they cannot see.  

In summary, this work offers a valuable step toward safer probabilistic computation. With the figure revisions and minor clarifications outlined above, it would be a strong candidate for publication. As it stands, the empirical section’s accessibility is compromised, and the reviewers must be convinced that the results are reproducible and verifiable. The authors should prioritize rescaling and relabeling all figures in Section 5 to meet NeurIPS standards. If these changes are implemented, the paper’s theoretical and practical merits will shine through.  

**Recommendation**: Major Revision (requires figure fixes and minor clarifications). The core ideas are sound and timely, but the current presentation of results is insufficient.
