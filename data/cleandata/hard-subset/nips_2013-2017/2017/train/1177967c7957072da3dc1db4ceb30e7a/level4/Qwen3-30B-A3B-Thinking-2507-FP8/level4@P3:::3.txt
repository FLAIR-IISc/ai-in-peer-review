

This paper introduces "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," a method to efficiently compute adaptive importance sampling distributions for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that provably outperforms fixed sampling strategies while avoiding the computational infeasibility of exact gradient-based sampling. The authors demonstrate that their approach—relying on safe lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes—yields the best possible sampling distribution under these bounds, with theoretical guarantees that it never degrades performance relative to uniform or fixed importance sampling. The method is generic, integrates seamlessly into existing algorithms, and incurs only O(n log n) overhead per iteration, which is often negligible compared to gradient computation costs.

The work builds directly on prior NIPS contributions addressing adaptive sampling, such as Zhao & Zhang (NIPS 2015) on gradient-based sampling for SGD and Allen-Zhu et al. (ICML 2017) on non-uniform sampling for CD. However, it distinguishes itself by resolving a critical gap: prior adaptive schemes (e.g., [36, 37]) often rely on approximations that *can* be worse than uniform sampling, while this paper provides a *provably safe* approximation. The authors also extend theoretical results to CD methods (Section 2.1), where adaptive sampling was less explored than in SGD, and demonstrate practical applicability to generalized linear models (GLMs) via efficient bound maintenance (Section 4).

**Strengths:**  
1. **Theoretical rigor:** The paper delivers strong guarantees (Theorems 3.2–3.4), proving the sampling distribution is optimal under the given bounds and always superior to fixed importance sampling. The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5–3.6) is particularly compelling.  
2. **Practical relevance:** The method is designed for real-world implementation, with clear examples for GLMs (Section 4) and empirical validation on standard datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20). The O(n log n) overhead is convincingly argued to be negligible (Section 4.3).  
3. **Clarity and completeness:** The exposition is well-structured, with precise notation and clear algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1–4). The experiments (Section 5) comprehensively compare adaptive sampling against uniform and fixed importance sampling, demonstrating significant speedups in CD (Figures 3–6) and moderate gains in SGD (Figures 7–9).

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Figure readability:** The figures in Section 5 (e.g., Figures 2–9) are difficult to interpret due to small fonts, overlapping lines, and insufficient contrast. For example, distinguishing between "optimal," "proposed," and "uniform" sampling curves in Figure 3 requires magnification. This undermines the empirical contribution.  
2. **SGD performance gap:** The paper notes that adaptive sampling yields "less pronounced" improvements in SGD (Section 5) compared to CD, but does not adequately explain why. Given that gradient-based sampling is theoretically optimal for SGD (Section 2.2), the authors should discuss whether this is due to the specific bound maintenance strategy (Section 4.2) or inherent limitations of the approach in the SGD context.  
3. **Scope limitation:** While the GLM case is well-motivated, the paper does not address whether safe gradient bounds can be maintained for non-GLM problems (e.g., deep learning), potentially limiting broader applicability. A brief discussion of this would strengthen the significance.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:*  
- Solves a key practical bottleneck in adaptive sampling with provable guarantees.  
- Theoretical results are rigorous, novel, and directly address a gap in prior work.  
- Empirical results convincingly demonstrate real-world efficiency gains.  
- Method is generic, easy to integrate, and computationally efficient.  

*Con:*  
- Figure quality significantly impedes understanding of empirical results.  
- Limited explanation for the weaker SGD performance relative to CD.  
- Scope could be broadened to address non-GLM optimization problems.  

**Overall Recommendation:**  
This paper makes a significant contribution to the theory and practice of adaptive sampling in large-scale optimization. The core idea—using safe bounds to compute a *provably safe* adaptive distribution—is novel, well-motivated, and rigorously supported. The empirical validation is strong for CD methods, and the method’s low overhead makes it highly practical. While the figure quality and SGD performance gap require addressing, these are minor issues for a technically robust paper. The work aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on high-impact, theory-grounded contributions to machine learning. **I recommend acceptance.**
