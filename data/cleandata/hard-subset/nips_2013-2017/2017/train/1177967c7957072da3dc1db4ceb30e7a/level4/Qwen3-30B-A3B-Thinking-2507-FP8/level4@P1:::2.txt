

This paper introduces a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address a key limitation of adaptive importance sampling methods: while theoretically promising, existing approaches often require expensive full gradient computations at each iteration. The proposed method uses safe bounds on gradient information to efficiently compute a sampling distribution that is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling.

The work builds on prior literature in adaptive importance sampling [22, 36, 37] and coordinate descent methods [18, 19, 2]. The authors make a clear theoretical contribution by demonstrating that their safe sampling method provides a worst-case guarantee superior to static sampling strategies while maintaining computational efficiency. This is particularly relevant to NIPS as it addresses a practical challenge in large-scale optimization critical for modern machine learning applications.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical contribution is strong: The authors prove that their sampling method is provably better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), a significant improvement over prior work that often lacked such guarantees. The implementation is practical, with the method computable in O(n log n) time per iteration—comparable to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component in many applications. The empirical validation is thorough, demonstrating significant speed-ups across multiple real-world datasets and optimization tasks (L1/L2 regularization, various loss functions). The paper is well-organized and presents technical results logically, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The main weaknesses are minor but worth noting. The connection to computational complexity is not fully developed: While the paper shows the sampling can be computed efficiently, it lacks a detailed analysis of how this affects the total complexity of finding an ε-optimal solution. It's unclear how the O(n log n) overhead compares to the cost of gradient evaluations (typically O(d) for d-dimensional data). The comparison with uniform sampling in Theorem 3.2 is limited to L-based sampling; a direct comparison with uniform sampling (p=1/n) would strengthen the argument, as uniform sampling is a very common baseline in practice. Additionally, several figures lack clear labels, ticks, and legends, making them difficult to interpret—moving some elements to an appendix would improve readability.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to optimization for machine learning. The theoretical guarantees are rigorous, the method is practical, and the empirical results convincingly validate the claims. The authors have addressed an important practical limitation in adaptive importance sampling that has been noted in prior work [36, 37].

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: It's technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel approach to a well-known problem (Originality), and addresses a significant challenge in large-scale optimization (Significance).

Pro:
- Strong theoretical contribution with provable guarantees
- Practical and efficient implementation
- Clear presentation and well-executed experiments
- Addresses a meaningful problem in large-scale optimization

Con:
- Limited analysis of total computational complexity
- Comparison with uniform sampling could be strengthened
- Figure readability needs improvement

Overall, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization algorithms for machine learning, with clear theoretical insights and practical benefits. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the paper's significant contribution to the literature.
