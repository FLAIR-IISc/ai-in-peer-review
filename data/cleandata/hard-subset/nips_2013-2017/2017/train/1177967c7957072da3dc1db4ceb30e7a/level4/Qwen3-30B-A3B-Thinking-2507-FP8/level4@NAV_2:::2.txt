

This paper introduces a novel safe adaptive importance sampling technique for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms, addressing a significant gap in the literature where adaptive sampling methods often suffer from computational infeasibility or lack of theoretical guarantees. The authors propose a method that efficiently computes sampling distributions using safe gradient bounds, proving that their approach is provably better than both uniform sampling and fixed importance sampling (L-based sampling), while maintaining computational efficiency (O(n log n) per iteration). The work builds on prior research in adaptive sampling (e.g., Zhao and Zhang 2015, Csiba et al. 2015) but significantly advances the field by providing a theoretically sound approximation that avoids the computational pitfalls of previous methods.

The paper makes several strong contributions: (1) it establishes theoretical guarantees for adaptive sampling in CD methods that demonstrate potential speed-ups of up to n-fold compared to fixed sampling; (2) it proposes a generic, efficient adaptive sampling strategy applicable to both CD and SGD; (3) it demonstrates practical integration into structured optimization problems; and (4) it provides comprehensive empirical validation on real-world datasets. The theoretical results (particularly Theorem 3.2) are well-supported, with the key insight that the proposed sampling is always better than L-based sampling, which is a commonly used strategy in practice.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with appropriate mathematical notation and sufficient technical detail for reproducibility. The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering multiple loss functions and regularization types across several standard datasets. The authors' approach to gradient bound tracking for generalized linear models (Section 4) is particularly practical and demonstrates the method's applicability to a broad class of machine learning problems.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical guarantees with clear proofs
- Practical implementation with minimal computational overhead
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple settings
- Clear exposition of the relationship between adaptive sampling and convergence rates
- Demonstrated superiority over common baselines (L-based and uniform sampling)

Weaknesses:
1. In Line 85, the denominator of p^*i should be xk rather than x.
2. The notation for \hat p and \hat c in Line 135 should be consistent (removing the subscript k would improve clarity).
3. All figures lack readable ticks, labels, and numbers due to insufficient size; moving some to the appendix would allow for larger, more legible figures.
4. The comparison in Theorem 3.2 (Conclusion 2) should be extended to include uniform sampling (p=1/n) for completeness, as this is a standard baseline.
5. The paper does not analyze how the proposed sampling method affects the total complexity of CD or SGD, including the O(n) time for updating gradient bounds. A detailed complexity analysis (e.g., total iterations to achieve ε-optimality) would strengthen the practical contribution.

The paper is of high quality, original, and significant. It advances the state of the art in adaptive sampling for optimization algorithms with both theoretical and practical contributions. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's overall quality.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical foundation, practical implementation with minimal overhead, comprehensive empirical validation, and clear demonstration of advantages over standard baselines.

Arguments against acceptance: None beyond the minor technical issues noted, which are easily resolved.
