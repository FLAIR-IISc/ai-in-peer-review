This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors argue that optimal gradient-based sampling is theoretically well-justified, but its computation is often infeasible. To address this issue, they rely on safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient, which are used to formulate the problem of picking the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem. The proposed method is generic and can be easily integrated into existing algorithms.

The paper provides both theoretical results and practical experiments to support the proposed strategy. The authors show that the novel sampling distribution provably performs better than any fixed importance sampling, which is a guarantee that could not be established for previous samplings derived from safe lower and upper bounds. The computational cost of the proposed scheme is of the order O(n log n) per iteration, which is comparable with the cost of evaluating a single component of the gradient.

The empirical evaluation of the proposed adaptive sampling scheme on relevant machine learning tasks shows promising results. The authors demonstrate that adaptive importance sampling significantly outperforms fixed importance sampling in iterations and time. They also show that adaptive sampling requires adaptive stepsizes, and that very loose safe gradient bounds are sufficient for the proposed method to work well.

One issue with the paper is the quality of the figures in Section 5, which are very difficult to see properly. These figures should be made larger, and the labels also made larger, so that the reader can see them properly.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper meets the criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The authors provide a clear and well-written paper that is easy to follow. The proposed method is original and significant, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of its theoretical properties and empirical performance.

Here is a list of arguments pro and con acceptance:

Pro:

* The paper proposes a novel and significant contribution to the field of machine learning.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the theoretical properties of the proposed method.
* The empirical evaluation of the proposed method shows promising results.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Con:

* The figures in Section 5 are difficult to see properly and should be improved.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with existing methods.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, but with the condition that the authors improve the quality of the figures in Section 5.
