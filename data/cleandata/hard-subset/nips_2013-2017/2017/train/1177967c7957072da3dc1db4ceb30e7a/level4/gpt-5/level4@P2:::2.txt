Summary
This paper proposes a safe, adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate ideal gradient-based sampling by using per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds on per-coordinate gradients (for CD) or per-component gradient norms (for SGD). The sampling distribution is obtained as the solution of a convex min–max problem that yields (i) the best worst-case guarantee given the bounds, (ii) performance provably better than fixed Li-based importance sampling (and uniform sampling in the coordinate-wise L-smooth case), and (iii) recovery of the full-information gradient-based sampling when exact gradients are available. The paper provides an O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling distribution, shows how to maintain bounds efficiently for GLMs, and demonstrates substantial empirical speedups for CD (with more modest gains for SGD). This contributes to a line of work on adaptive sampling for SGD and CD (e.g., gradient-based and gap-based schemes), but provides a new “safe” formulation with provable guarantees and practical complexity.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: formulate adaptive sampling under gradient bounds as a min–max problem and derive the optimal distribution with a simple O(n log n) algorithm.
- Sound theory: the “best worst-case” property and the guarantee that the proposed sampling is never worse than Li-based importance sampling; recovery of the full-information optimum.
- Practicality: efficient maintenance of gradient bounds for GLMs; negligible overhead in regimes where d = Ω(n); straightforward integration into CD/SGD.
- Convincing experiments for CD across several datasets and losses; ablations on stepsizes help isolate where the gains come from.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- The main theoretical comparison is with Li-based sampling. A direct, general comparison to uniform sampling (without assuming Li = L) would strengthen the message, as uniform remains the default in many applications.
- The total complexity to reach an ε-optimal solution (including the cost of maintaining bounds) is not stated explicitly. Providing end-to-end iteration/time complexity under convex/strongly convex assumptions would help practitioners.
- Figures are too small to read (ticks/labels illegible), which limits the clarity of the empirical section.
- The benefits for SGD appear limited in the reported experiments; additional analysis or guidance on when to expect gains would be useful (e.g., dependence on the tightness of bounds, variance structure).
- The approach relies on safe bounds; more discussion on how bound tightness affects vk, and on alternatives to the Cauchy–Schwarz bounds, would be valuable.

Specific comments and suggestions
1) Notation correction: In the expression for the optimal CD sampling p∗ in Section 2 (your Line 85), the denominator should use ∇f(xk) (i.e., evaluated at xk), not at x.

2) Consistency: In the statement of Theorem 3.2 (your Line 135), consider dropping the subscript k for p̂ and ĉ to match the theorem’s general form, or clarify the per-iteration nature of the objects.

3) Figures: Axis ticks, labels, and legends are not readable. Please enlarge all figures, reduce the number of panels per figure, and move some to the appendix to restore readability.

4) Theory vs. uniform sampling: Please add a statement analogous to Theorem 3.2(ii) that compares directly against uniform sampling p = 1/n in the general case Li not all equal. For example, a bound of the form V(p̂, c) ≤ C · ‖c‖2 with C ≤ V(punif, c)/‖c‖2 for all c ∈ Ck, or conditions under which p̂ uniformly dominates punif, would make the practical advantage clearer.

5) End-to-end complexity: Provide convergence rates and total complexity for reaching an ε-optimal solution when using the proposed sampling, including:
- The per-iteration overhead O(n log n) for computing p̂ and O(n) for updating `k, uk.
- How these costs compare to the gradient evaluation (e.g., Θ(d) in GLMs) and when they are negligible (e.g., d = Ω(n)).
- Explicit iteration complexity under convex and strongly convex settings (e.g., rates derived from the one-step progress with α̂k = 1/vk), and the resulting total runtime bounds.

Additional minor clarifications
- Provide a short pseudo-code snippet for the SGD variant akin to Algorithm 2 for CD.
- Discuss initialization of `k, uk (you use `0 = 0, u0 = ∞ in experiments) and whether mild initialization can improve early-epoch performance.
- Comment on robustness when bounds are loose, and on practical heuristics to tighten them (e.g., cached inner products, mini-batch probing, or model-specific curvature bounds).
- If space permits, add an empirical comparison of p̂ versus a heuristic that uses only upper bounds (common in prior work), to highlight the benefit of the proposed min–max construction.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled formulation with clean guarantees and an efficient algorithm.
- Strong empirical gains for CD on real datasets.
- Generic and easy to integrate; likely to influence practice in CD-based training.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited improvement for SGD in current experiments and limited guidance on when it helps most.
- Missing explicit, general comparison to uniform sampling in theory.
- Figures’ readability issues and some notational inconsistencies.

Overall evaluation
A solid and timely contribution to adaptive sampling, with clear theory, practical algorithms, and strong results for CD. Addressing the uniform-sampling comparison, improving figure readability, and adding end-to-end complexity statements would further strengthen the paper. I lean toward acceptance, particularly for its impact on CD and its principled “safe” approach.
