This paper introduces a practical adaptive importance sampling method tailored for coordinate descent and stochastic gradient algorithms. The core idea is to leverage lower and upper bounds on gradient magnitudes to construct an effective approximation of gradient-based sampling. The authors demonstrate that their proposed strategy is optimal within the defined bounds, consistently outperforming uniform or fixed importance sampling, and achieves this with minimal computational overhead. While adaptive importance sampling is not new, this work uniquely frames the problem of optimal distribution selection as a convex optimization task and provides an efficient solution algorithm. 

Overall, the paper is clearly written and represents a valuable addition to the literature on importance sampling.

Specific Comments:
The proof of Lemma 2.1 appears to be missing a scaling factor of 2 in the definition of α*.
In Example 3.1, equation (7) involves maximization; therefore, setting the parameter *c* to either the upper or lower bound should yield better performance than uniform sampling. The example’s intent regarding this point is somewhat unclear, particularly given the preceding statement suggesting that directly using the bounds can be suboptimal.
Line 4 of Algorithm 4 includes a condition that, given *m* is defined as the maximum of *l<sup>sort</sup>*, may never be met.
Line 7 of Algorithm 4 likely contains a typographical error and should reference *u<sup>sort</sup>* instead of *c<sup>sort</sup>*.
The numerical evaluation would be strengthened by comparisons to existing adaptive sampling techniques (e.g., [2], [5],[21]) and against fixed importance sampling employing a non-uniform distribution. 
The absence of timing results for the Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithm is a notable omission.
The title of the cited paper [14] is inaccurate.
Please include the following reference: Csiba and Richtarik, "Importance Sampling for Minibatches," arXiv, 2016.
