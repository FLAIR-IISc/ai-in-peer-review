This paper introduces a "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and coordinate descent (CD) methods. The key idea is to leverage efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes to approximate the optimal, but computationally expensive, gradient-based sampling distribution. The authors formulate the problem of choosing the best sampling distribution given these bounds as a min-max optimization problem. They present an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem. The resulting sampling scheme is termed "safe" because it is theoretically guaranteed to perform better than standard uniform or fixed importance sampling, a property that previous approximations often lack. The method is evaluated empirically on several large-scale datasets, demonstrating significant speedups for CD in particular.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Theoretical Soundness:** The primary contribution is the novel and principled formulation of selecting an adaptive sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem (Equation 7). This is an elegant way to handle the uncertainty arising from using bounds instead of the true gradient values. The theoretical results are strong, especially the guarantee that the proposed sampling is provably superior to any fixed importance sampling scheme (Theorem 3.2). This "safety" guarantee is a significant advantage over prior heuristics for approximating adaptive distributions, which can sometimes underperform even uniform sampling.

2.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the safe sampling distribution in each iteration. For many problems where the data dimension `d` is large relative to the number of examples/coordinates `n`, this overhead is negligible compared to the cost of a single gradient computation, as convincingly argued by the authors and demonstrated in the timing experiments for CD.

3.  **Generality:** The proposed framework is generic and can be readily integrated into a wide range of existing CD and SGD solvers. The paper clearly illustrates how to derive and update the necessary gradient bounds for an important class of problems (Generalized Linear Models), demonstrating the method's applicability.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The theoretical contributions are presented logically, and the connections between the ideal (but infeasible) sampling and the proposed practical approximation are well-explained.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Limited Experimental Baselines:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including more baselines. The current comparisons are primarily against uniform sampling and the ideal (full-gradient) sampling. It would be more informative to also compare against:
    *   Other adaptive importance sampling schemes from the literature (e.g., duality gap-based sampling from [5] or other approximations).
    *   A non-uniform *fixed* importance sampling baseline (e.g., the Li-based sampling from Example 2.2), which is often a strong and simple baseline.

2.  **Missing Timing Results for SGD:** While the paper provides timing results for CD, similar results for SGD are absent. Wall-clock time is a critical metric for evaluating the practical utility of the method. Including timing plots for the SGD experiments would help verify that the O(n log n) overhead of computing the sampling distribution does not negate the gains in iteration complexity.

3.  **Points Requiring Clarification:** A few specific parts of the paper were unclear and could benefit from revision:
    *   **Proof of Lemma 2.1:** The expression for the optimal stepsize `αk` in the appendix seems to be missing a factor of 2.
    *   **Example 3.1:** This example is confusing. It is used to illustrate that naively using the lower or upper bounds (`l` or `u`) to define the sampling distribution can be suboptimal. However, the numbers provided seem to show that using either `l` or `u` results in a larger value of `V(p, c)/||c||^2` than uniform sampling, which, according to the optimization problem in (7), would be better. Please clarify the intent of this example.
    *   **Algorithm 4:** There are two potential issues in the algorithm's description.
        *   In Line 4, the condition `[`sort]` > m` seems unlikely to ever be true on the first iteration, given that `m` is initialized as `max(`sort)`. Could you clarify the logic here?
        *   In Line 7, the variable `c^{sort}` is used, but it does not appear to be defined. Should this be `u^{sort}`?

**Minor Comments:**

*   The title for reference [14] in the bibliography appears to be incorrect.
*   The authors may consider adding a relevant reference: Csiba and Richtarik, "Importance Sampling for Minibatches", arXiv, 2016.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practical contribution to the important area of large-scale optimization. The "safety" guarantee is a compelling feature that distinguishes this work from prior art. Despite the suggestions for improving the experimental evaluation and clarifying minor technical points, the core ideas are strong and significant.
