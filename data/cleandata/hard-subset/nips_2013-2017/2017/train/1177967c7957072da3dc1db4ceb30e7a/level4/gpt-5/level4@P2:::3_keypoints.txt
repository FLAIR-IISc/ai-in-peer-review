- Proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling method that uses provable upper/lower bounds on per-coordinate gradients to approximate gradient-based sampling without full-gradient costs.  
- Formulates an optimization that returns a sampling distribution and stepsize which is optimal under the bounds and never worse than uniform or fixed importance sampling.  
- Presents an O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling distribution and shows efficient bound maintenance for GLMs, integrating the approach into CD and SGD.  
- Theoretical guarantees: distribution is provably optimal given bounds, dominates uniform/fixed sampling, and yields interpretable competitive-ratio and expected-progress bounds.  
- CD analysis and adaptive stepsize design are strong theoretical contributions.  
- Method is generic and applicable across many convex objectives (e.g., GLMs with separable regularizers).  
- Practicality: computation (O(n log n)) and O(n) bound updates can be negligible when per-iteration gradient costs dominate (e.g., d = Ω(n)).  
- Empirical results show improvements close to full-information optimal sampling for CD, and even loose Cauchy–Schwarz bounds can give gains.  
- Figures in Section 5 are hard to read and need larger, higher-contrast labels and legends.  
- Empirical scope is narrow (text datasets and GLMs); broader domains (dense/structured data, different regularizers, non-GLM convex models) are needed.  
- Gains for SGD are modest on reported datasets; discussion needed on when SGD benefits most (variance, curvature, bound tightness).  
- Overhead may be non-negligible for very small d or extremely sparse updates; a detailed timing breakdown and ablations on bound tightness are requested.  
- Bound maintenance currently uses simple Cauchy–Schwarz estimates; discussing tighter yet efficient alternatives (data-dependent preconditioning, block/screening bounds) would be helpful.  
- Reproducibility: clarify implementation details (initialization of bounds, handling active coordinates, baseline step-size tuning) and ensure timing comparability.  
- Quality assessment: analysis is sound with clear expected-progress comparisons and an efficient algorithm for the minimax solution.  
- Clarity assessment: generally well written, but figure readability and expanded discussion on where gains are largest would improve the paper.  
- Originality assessment: the safe minimax formulation and efficient solution are novel and fill a gap between theory and practice.  
- Significance assessment: likely to be adopted in CD solvers; impact on SGD is more problem-dependent but still relevant.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled generic method, strong theoretical guarantees, efficient and practical algorithm, and demonstrated CD improvements.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited experimental breadth, modest SGD gains, heavy reliance on bound quality, and current figure readability issues.  
- Actionable recommendations: enlarge/relabel figures; add diverse experimental settings and timing breakdowns; expand discussion on constructing/maintaining tighter bounds and conditions favoring SGD improvements.
