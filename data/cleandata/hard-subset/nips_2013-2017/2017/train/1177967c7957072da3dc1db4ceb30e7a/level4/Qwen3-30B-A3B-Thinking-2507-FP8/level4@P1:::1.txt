

The paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD), leveraging computable lower and upper bounds on gradient magnitudes to approximate gradient-based sampling efficiently. The authors formalize the optimal sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem over these bounds, proving their method is (i) the best possible under the given constraints, (ii) strictly superior to uniform or fixed importance sampling, and (iii) computable in O(n log n) time per iteration—often negligible compared to gradient evaluation costs. This work builds on prior adaptive sampling approaches (e.g., gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37]) but addresses their key limitations: computational infeasibility and lack of theoretical guarantees against fixed sampling. Notably, it extends to CD and SGD and provides concrete strategies for maintaining safe gradient bounds in generalized linear models (GLMs), a common machine learning setting.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by rigorously establishing provable guarantees for adaptive sampling under limited gradient information—a gap in prior work [36, 37]. The convex optimization formulation (Equation 7) and efficient solution via Algorithm 4 are novel and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate the method’s practicality through extensive experiments on GLMs (Lasso, Ridge, logistic regression) across multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20), showing consistent speedups over fixed sampling (uniform and Li-based) in CD while maintaining minimal overhead (O(n log n) per iteration). The analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5, 3.6) and the demonstration that even loose Cauchy-Schwarz bounds suffice (Section 4) are compelling. The paper is exceptionally clear, with well-structured proofs, intuitive examples (e.g., Example 2.3), and thorough experimental validation. It directly addresses NIPS criteria: high *quality* (rigorous theory + experiments), *clarity* (well-organized, self-contained), *originality* (novel optimization framework for adaptive sampling), and *significance* (enables faster optimization in large-scale ML).

**Weaknesses:**  
While the theoretical guarantees are strong, the numerical evaluation could be strengthened. The comparison against other adaptive schemes (e.g., [2, 5, 21]) is limited to CD; SGD results (Figures 7–9) show minimal gains over uniform sampling, but no timing comparisons against alternative adaptive methods (e.g., [5, 21]) are provided. This omission weakens the significance claim for SGD, where adaptive sampling is less established. Additionally, the paper references "Csiba and Richtarik. 'Importance Sampling for Minibatches' 2016" but does not cite it—this should be added to contextualize related work. The Algorithm 4 pseudocode contains minor indexing errors: Line 4 should check `if [l_sort][l] > m` (not `c_sort`), and Line 7 should reference `u_sort` (not `c_sort`). While these are implementation details, they affect reproducibility. The proof of Lemma 2.1 requires a factor of 2 correction in the expression for α* (the minimizer of Equation 3), which should be clarified.

**Recommendation:**  
This is a high-quality, original contribution with clear theoretical and practical merits. The method’s provable superiority over fixed sampling, minimal overhead, and applicability to CD/SGD make it a valuable addition to optimization toolkits for large-scale ML. The minor weaknesses (missing comparisons, indexing errors) are easily addressable and do not undermine the core contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in adaptive sampling by resolving the key trade-off between theoretical guarantees and computational feasibility—a critical need in modern optimization. I recommend **accept**.
