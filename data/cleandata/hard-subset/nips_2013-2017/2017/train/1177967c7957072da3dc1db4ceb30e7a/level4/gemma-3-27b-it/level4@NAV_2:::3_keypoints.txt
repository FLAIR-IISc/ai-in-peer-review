- Addresses adaptive importance sampling for large-scale optimization by approximating gradient-based sampling with safe gradient bounds and provable guarantees.
- Proposes a computationally feasible approximation that improves over static sampling schemes with theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2).
- Builds on prior adaptive sampling work while mitigating their computational limitations and risks of worse-than-uniform performance.
- Formulates the sampling problem as a convex optimization to ensure safety and near-optimality — a novel approach.
- Provides strong theoretical analysis for coordinate descent, showing potential speedups up to a factor of the dimension n.
- Algorithm is generic and integrates seamlessly with existing methods like CD and SGD.
- Empirical evaluation is extensive across datasets and regularizers, validating theoretical claims.
- Includes analysis of the quality of safe gradient bounds (Figures 3–9), adding practical insight.
- Presentation is dense and assumes strong optimization background, which may limit accessibility.
- Heavy notation makes derivations challenging to follow.
- Figures in Section 5 are hard to read, hindering quick assessment of numerical results.
- Related-work discussion could be expanded to mention variance-reduction methods (e.g., SAGA, SVRG) for better context.
- Lacks a thorough discussion of limitations of the safe-bound approach, such as conditions leading to overly conservative bounds.
- Arguments for acceptance: rigorous theory, practical relevance, novelty, strong empirical validation, and broad applicability.
- Arguments against acceptance: dense presentation, figure readability issues, and insufficient discussion of limitations.
- Overall recommendation: accept, subject to improving figure clarity and expanding discussion of safe-bound limitations.
