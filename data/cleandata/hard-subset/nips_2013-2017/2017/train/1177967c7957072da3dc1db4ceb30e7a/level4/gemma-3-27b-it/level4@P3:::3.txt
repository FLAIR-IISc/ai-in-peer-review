This paper addresses the important problem of adaptive importance sampling, a technique used to accelerate optimization algorithms for large-scale applications. The authors propose an efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, offering provable guarantees and demonstrating practical speed-ups with coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). This work builds upon a substantial body of research in adaptive sampling, notably [22, 5, 23] which explore gradient-based and duality-gap-based sampling, and acknowledges the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and computational feasibility, as discussed in [23, 3]. The proposed approach cleverly avoids full gradient computation by leveraging safe bounds, a strategy related to approximations discussed in [36, 37], but with a novel guarantee of performing no worse than uniform sampling.

The paper's strengths lie in its theoretical rigor and its practical relevance. The authors clearly articulate the benefits of adaptive sampling, particularly in the context of CD (showing a potential speed-up factor of *n*). The proposed method is generic and can be readily integrated into existing algorithms. The inclusion of both theoretical analysis *and* extensive numerical validation is commendable, lending strong support to the claims. The connection to existing work is well-established, appropriately citing relevant publications. The clear presentation of the optimization formulation in Section 3 and the accompanying theorems (3.2, 3.4) are particularly strong points. The discussion of maintaining safe gradient bounds for GLMs (Section 4) provides a concrete example of how the method can be applied in practice.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses. While the theoretical contributions are solid, the presentation is occasionally dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. For example, the connection between Lemma 2.4 and the subsequent discussion could be made more explicit. The paper would also benefit from a clearer discussion of the assumptions underlying the safe gradient bounds (Section 4). Specifically, how sensitive is the performance to the tightness of these bounds? Are there scenarios where these bounds are difficult to obtain or maintain efficiently? Although the authors acknowledge the limitations of their experimental setup, a more in-depth discussion of the choice of datasets and regularization parameters would be valuable. Furthermore, the competitive ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) feels somewhat disconnected from the main flow of the paper and could be streamlined or explained with more context.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper provides provable guarantees for the proposed sampling scheme, demonstrating its superiority to uniform sampling.
*   **Practical relevance:** The method is generic, efficient, and demonstrates significant speed-ups in experiments with CD and SGD.
*   **Clear connection to existing work:** The paper appropriately positions itself within the broader literature on adaptive importance sampling.
*   **Novel contribution:** The approach of formulating the sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem and providing an efficient algorithm to solve it is novel and effective.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Dense presentation:** Some sections are theoretically dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
*   **Limited discussion of safe bounds:** The sensitivity of the method to the tightness of the safe gradient bounds is not fully explored.
*   **Minor presentation issues:** The figures in Section 5 are difficult to read and require improvement. The connection between certain lemmas/theorems and the overall narrative could be made clearer.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The theoretical results are solid, the proposed method is practical and efficient, and the experimental validation is convincing. While the presentation could be improved in certain areas, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focusing on improving the clarity of the presentation and addressing the concerns regarding the discussion of safe gradient bounds and figure readability. Specifically, the authors should enlarge the figures in Section 5 and ensure the labels are clearly visible.
