This paper addresses the important problem of adaptive importance sampling, a technique widely used to accelerate optimization algorithms for large-scale applications. The authors propose an efficient approximation to gradient-based sampling, leveraging safe bounds on the gradient to achieve a distribution that is provably better than uniform sampling and, in ideal conditions, recovers gradient-based sampling. A key strength is the method’s generality, allowing easy integration into existing algorithms like coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The paper presents both theoretical guarantees and extensive numerical validation, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing adaptive strategies – computational cost and potentially weaker theoretical guarantees compared to uniform sampling – and positioning their work as a solution to these challenges. The theoretical contributions, particularly the analysis of CD with adaptive sampling (showing potential speed-ups of up to the dimension *n*), are significant. The proposed scheme’s provable advantages over uniform and fixed importance sampling are clearly stated and supported by the analysis. Furthermore, the extensive numerical results on real datasets provide strong evidence of the method’s practical effectiveness, demonstrating performance gains in both iteration complexity and execution time. The connection to existing work on gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37] and duality gap-based sampling [5, 23] is well established. The paper also correctly cites relevant optimization literature [18, 19, 2, 35, 27].

A notable weakness is the presentation of the experimental results. The figures in Section 5 are difficult to interpret due to their small size and font. Increasing the figure size and label font would significantly improve readability and allow for easier assessment of the results. While the authors acknowledge the need for safe gradient bounds and demonstrate a method for obtaining them in the context of GLMs, a broader discussion of the challenges and limitations of obtaining these bounds in different application domains would be beneficial. The paper also assumes knowledge of the objective function's structure for efficient bound computation; elaborating on the applicability of this approach to more general, less structured problems would strengthen the work. The discussion around the competitive ratio (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) feels somewhat isolated and could be integrated more seamlessly into the main narrative.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides provable guarantees for the proposed sampling scheme, demonstrating its superiority over existing methods.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The method is generic and easily integrated into widely used algorithms (CD and SGD), with demonstrated improvements on real-world datasets.
*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** Adaptive importance sampling is crucial for scaling optimization algorithms to large-scale problems.
*   **Clear Presentation of Ideas:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, methodology, and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Poor Figure Quality:** The small size and font of the figures in Section 5 hinder the interpretability of the experimental results.
*   **Limited Discussion of Bound Computation:** The discussion around obtaining safe gradient bounds could be more comprehensive and address challenges in different application domains.



**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of optimization. While the figure quality is a concern, it is a relatively minor issue that can be easily addressed. The theoretical and practical contributions are substantial, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon improving the figure quality in the final version. The clarity of the theoretical results and the potential impact of the proposed method justify its inclusion in NIPS.
