## Review of NIPS 2017 Submission

This paper presents interesting insights into the Random Coordinate Descent (RCD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) algorithms, particularly regarding the impact of sampling strategies. The theoretical results are promising, and Conclusion 2 of Theorem 3.2 is particularly valuable, demonstrating the potential superiority of the proposed sampling method over the commonly used L<sub>i</sub>-based sampling. However, several aspects require clarification and improvement before acceptance.

**Summary of Strengths:**

The paper tackles an important problem: improving the efficiency of RCD and SGD through judicious sampling. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the demonstrated improvement over L<sub>i</sub>-based sampling is a significant contribution. The work is well-motivated and clearly articulates the limitations of existing sampling methods.

**Areas for Improvement:**

I have identified several points that, if addressed, would significantly strengthen the paper. These relate to technical correctness, clarity of presentation, and experimental/analytical completeness.

1. **Technical Correction:** In Line 85, the denominator of p<sup>*</sup><sub>i</sub> appears to incorrectly use ‘x’ instead of ‘x<sub>k</sub>’. This should be corrected to ensure the mathematical validity of the expression.

2. **Notation Consistency:**  In Line 135, the subscript ‘k’ on  p̂ and ĉ appears unnecessary and inconsistent with the surrounding notation. Removing it would improve readability and maintain a more coherent mathematical presentation.

3. **Figure Readability:** All figures are currently too small and lack essential elements for interpretation. Specifically, tick marks, labels, numbers, and appropriate font sizes are missing. This makes it extremely difficult to extract meaningful information from the visualizations. I strongly recommend either increasing the figure size within the main body of the paper, or moving some figures to the appendix to allow for larger, clearer presentations of the remaining figures. Prioritizing clarity in visual representation is crucial.

4. **Comparison to Uniform Sampling:** The paper effectively compares the proposed sampling method against L<sub>i</sub>-based sampling. However, a comparison against uniform sampling (p = 1/n) would provide a more complete benchmark.  A result analogous to Conclusion 2 in Theorem 3.2, demonstrating performance relative to uniform sampling, would significantly enhance the understanding of the proposed method’s advantages. This is a standard baseline in the sampling literature and its inclusion is essential.

5. **Complexity Analysis:** A crucial aspect missing from the analysis is a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method. While the paper focuses on sampling efficiency, the overhead associated with implementing the new sampling strategy must be considered. Specifically, the authors should remark on how the proposed method impacts the total complexity of RCD or SGD. For example, quantifying the overall complexity – including the O(n) time required for updating l<sub>k</sub> and u<sub>k</sub> – for RCD to achieve an ε-optimal solution would be highly valuable.  This analysis should demonstrate that the gains from improved sampling outweigh any added computational cost.  A concrete statement of the overall complexity, perhaps in terms of iterations required to reach a certain accuracy, would be particularly helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical results are promising, and the identified improvement over existing sampling methods is significant. However, addressing the points raised above – particularly regarding figure readability, comparison to uniform sampling, and complexity analysis – is crucial for strengthening the paper and ensuring its clarity and impact. I recommend a rejection with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit, contingent on addressing the concerns outlined in this review.
