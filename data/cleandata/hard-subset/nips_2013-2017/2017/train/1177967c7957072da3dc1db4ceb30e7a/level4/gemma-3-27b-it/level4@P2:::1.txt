This paper presents a novel and efficient “safe” adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using lower and upper bounds on gradient values, formulating the optimal sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem with an efficient solution algorithm. While adaptive importance sampling is not new, this work offers a unique approach with provable guarantees: the proposed method is optimal given the bounds, always outperforms uniform or fixed importance sampling, and incurs negligible computational overhead. The paper is well-written and constitutes a valuable contribution to the field.

**Summary of Key Ideas and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses a key challenge in applying adaptive importance sampling: computational feasibility. Existing methods, like gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37] and duality gap-based sampling [5, 23], often require computations that negate their theoretical benefits. The authors bridge this gap by proposing a method based on readily available gradient bounds. This builds upon earlier work on importance sampling [18, 19, 2, 33, 16, 6, 25, 24] and adaptive strategies [22, 5, 26, 10, 21, 23], but provides a novel framework for efficiently approximating the ideal gradient-based approach. The connection to optimal sampling strategies (Example 2.3) provides a strong theoretical foundation and justification for the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is provably optimal *given the bounds*, and demonstrably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling. The theoretical analysis (e.g., Lemma 2.1, Theorem 3.2) provides strong support for the proposed approach.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The authors demonstrate the feasibility of their approach by formulating the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem with an efficient (O(n log n)) solution (Algorithm 4).  The claim of negligible extra cost is compelling.
*   **Generality:**  The proposed scheme is generic and can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD algorithms.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making the complex ideas accessible.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Extensive numerical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on real datasets.  The inclusion of vk plots provides valuable insight into the tightness of the bounds and the resulting stepsize adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Dependency on Bounds:** The performance hinges on the quality of the lower and upper bounds on the gradients. While the paper provides a practical method for estimating these bounds (Section 4), the sensitivity of the method to inaccurate bounds could be further explored.
*   **Limited Comparison to Other Adaptive Methods:** While the authors compare to uniform and fixed importance sampling, comparisons to other adaptive sampling schemes (e.g., [2], [5], [21]) would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Lack of Timing Results for SGD:** The absence of timing results for SGD limits the assessment of its practical performance in that setting.
*   **Minor Errors/Typos:** A few minor errors were identified (e.g., incorrect reference title [14], potential issues in Algorithm 4 – lines 4 & 7) .

**Specific Comments and Questions (addressed in rebuttal):**

*   The author’s clarification regarding the factor of 2 in Lemma 2.1 is helpful.
*   The explanation of Example 3.1 clarifies its intent to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach over naive bound selection.
*   The authors’ response regarding the condition in Algorithm 4 (line 4) is clear.
*   The explanation regarding the lack of timing results for SGD is reasonable, and the acknowledgment of future work is appreciated.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling.
*   The method addresses a practical limitation of existing adaptive strategies.
*   The empirical results are compelling.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Con Acceptance:**

*   The performance is dependent on the quality of the gradient bounds.
*   The comparison to other adaptive methods is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field. The proposed method offers a practical and efficient way to leverage adaptive importance sampling, with strong theoretical guarantees and promising empirical results.  The authors have addressed my initial concerns in the rebuttal, reinforcing my positive assessment. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (addressing the typos and potentially adding a comparison to additional adaptive sampling schemes if space permits).
