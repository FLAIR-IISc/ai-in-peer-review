- Introduces a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent and SGD using efficiently computable gradient/norm bounds.  
- Addresses infeasibility of full-gradient optimal sampling and risks of naive approximations performing worse than uniform.  
- Proposes a novel min-max formulation (Eq. 7) to find the sampling distribution that is worst-case optimal given bound uncertainty.  
- Provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Theorem 3.4 / Algorithm 4) to solve the min-max problem.  
- Theoretical guarantees: the proposed sampling is always at least as good as fixed importance sampling (including uniform) and recovers optimal sampling when full information is available (Theorem 3.2).  
- Derives expected one-step progress (Eq. 3) and frames the ideal adaptive sampling problem clearly and rigorously.  
- Shows how to maintain the required lower/upper gradient bounds for generalized linear models efficiently (Section 4).  
- Extensive experiments demonstrate significant speed-ups over non-adaptive methods and performance close to infeasible optimal sampling.  
- Paper is technically sound; derivations and theoretical contributions are well-supported.  
- The min-max approach is a novel, principled alternative to prior heuristics that used bounds without safety guarantees.  
- Safety guarantee is a key practical advantage, reducing risk of performance degradation from adaptive methods.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough but compares mainly to uniform and infeasible optimal sampling.  
- Presentation is generally clear, with good organization and readable algorithms.  
- Major clarity issue: figures in Section 5 are too small and illegible; must be enlarged for the final version.  
- Bound maintenance currently uses a simple Cauchy-Schwarz bound that can be loose; sensitivity to bound tightness should be discussed.  
- Experimental comparisons should include other practical adaptive schemes (e.g., stale-gradient sampling or upper-bound sampling) for a fuller empirical picture.
