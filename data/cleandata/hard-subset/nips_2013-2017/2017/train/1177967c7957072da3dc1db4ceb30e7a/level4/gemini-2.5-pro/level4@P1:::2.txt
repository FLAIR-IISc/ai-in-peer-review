This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for stochastic optimization algorithms like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The core idea is to approximate the optimal, but computationally infeasible, gradient-based sampling distribution. The authors propose using efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes for each coordinate or data point. Based on these bounds, they formulate a minimax optimization problem to find a sampling distribution that is robust in a worst-case sense to the uncertainty in the true gradient. The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm for computing this distribution and proves that the resulting sampling scheme is guaranteed to perform better than standard fixed importance sampling strategies. The method's practicality is demonstrated on generalized linear models, showing how the required gradient bounds can be maintained at a low computational cost.

**Strengths**

1.  The primary strength of the paper is the "safety" guarantee. Theorem 3.2(ii) provides a clear and valuable theoretical result: the proposed adaptive sampling is provably never worse than the widely used L_i-based importance sampling. This is a significant advantage over previous adaptive methods, which often lack such guarantees and could potentially underperform fixed sampling in certain scenarios.

2.  The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization problem (Equation 7) is elegant and well-motivated. It provides a principled way to handle the uncertainty arising from using gradient bounds instead of the exact (and expensive to compute) gradients.

3.  The proposed algorithm for solving this optimization problem (Algorithm 4) is efficient, ensuring that the sampling scheme is computationally practical. The empirical results support this, showing that the method outperforms fixed sampling in total computation time, not just in the number of iterations.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Overall Complexity Analysis:** The paper analyzes the per-iteration complexity of the proposed method (O(n log n) for sampling, plus the cost of updating the bounds). However, to fully assess the significance of the proposed method, a complete convergence analysis is needed. It would be crucial to have a theorem stating the total computational complexity (i.e., the total runtime) required for the algorithm to find an epsilon-optimal solution. This would allow for a direct theoretical comparison with the overall complexity of methods using uniform or L_i-based sampling and would more rigorously quantify the benefits of the adaptive scheme.

2.  **Comparison with Uniform Sampling:** Remark 3.3 provides a comparison to uniform sampling for the special case where all coordinate-wise Lipschitz constants L_i are equal. Given that uniform sampling is arguably the most common and simplest baseline, it would strengthen the paper to present this comparison more formally and prominently. Could a general result, analogous to Theorem 3.2(ii), be established for the uniform case? This would make the benefits over this key baseline more explicit.

3.  **Clarity of Figures:** A major issue with the current manuscript is the legibility of the figures. All plots in Figures 2-9 are too small, rendering the axes, labels, legends, and data curves unreadable. I strongly recommend that the authors remake these figures at a much higher resolution and a larger size. To create sufficient space, it may be helpful to move some of the less critical experimental results to an appendix.

**Minor Comments**

*   In Example 2.3 (around line 85), please double-check that the gradient `∇f(x)` is consistently written as `∇f(xk)` to refer to the current iterate.
*   In the definition of the solution in Equation 7 (around line 135), you define `(p̂k, α̂k)`. However, the variables in the optimization problem itself, `(p̂, ĉ)`, do not have the subscript k. For notational consistency, you may want to remove the subscript k from `p̂` and `ĉ` in Theorem 3.2 and the surrounding text, as they are solutions to a general optimization problem that is not specific to iteration `k`.
