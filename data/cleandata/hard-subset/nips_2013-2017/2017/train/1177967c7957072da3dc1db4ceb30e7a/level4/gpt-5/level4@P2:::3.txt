This paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling strategy that approximates gradient-based sampling using provable upper and lower bounds on gradient magnitudes. The method aims to capture most of the benefit of fully adaptive sampling—known to accelerate CD and SGD—without the prohibitive cost of computing full gradients at each iteration. The authors formulate an optimization problem that, given per-coordinate (or per-component) gradient bounds, returns a sampling distribution and stepsize with two guarantees: it is optimal under the available bounds and never worse than uniform or fixed importance sampling. They show how to compute this distribution in O(n log n), and how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for generalized linear models. The approach is integrated into CD and SGD, with theory showing improved expected progress over fixed sampling and experiments reporting practical speedups.

Relation to prior work: Adaptive importance sampling for SGD and CD has been explored via gradient-based sampling, duality-gap-based sampling, and heuristics using stale or approximate information. A key limitation in past work is the need for full gradient information or approximations that may offer no worst-case improvement over uniform sampling. This paper contributes a principled “safe” alternative that (i) reduces to optimal gradient-based sampling when full gradient information is available, and (ii) guarantees improvement over fixed sampling using only bounds.

Strengths
- Clear and general formulation: The method is generic and can be plugged into CD and SGD across a broad class of convex objectives, including GLMs with separable regularizers.
- Theoretical guarantees: The sampling distribution is provably optimal given the bounds, dominates uniform/fixed importance sampling, and provides interpretable competitive-ratio bounds. The analysis for CD, including the adaptive stepsize strategy, is particularly compelling.
- Practicality: The O(n log n) computation for the sampling distribution and O(n) updates for bounds can be negligible in regimes where per-iteration gradient computations dominate (e.g., d = Ω(n)).
- Empirical support: Experiments show improvements close to those of full-information optimal sampling for CD, and demonstrate that even loose Cauchy–Schwarz-based bounds suffice to obtain gains.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Figure readability: The plots in Section 5 are hard to read. Please enlarge the figures and axis labels, and ensure legends are high-contrast and legible.
- Scope of empirical evaluation: Results focus on text datasets and GLMs. Including additional domains (e.g., dense or structured data, different regularizers, or non-GLM convex models) would strengthen the empirical case.
- SGD gains are modest: As noted, the benefits for SGD are smaller than for CD on the reported datasets. A deeper discussion of when the method helps SGD the most (e.g., variance structure, curvature, bound tightness) would be useful.
- Overhead vs. benefit: While O(n log n) is reasonable, it could be non-negligible for very small d or extremely sparse updates. A more detailed timing breakdown (sampling vs. gradient computation) and ablations comparing different bound-tightness levels would clarify trade-offs.
- Bound maintenance: The approach relies on safe bounds that, in the GLM case, use simple Cauchy–Schwarz estimates. Discussing tighter yet still efficient alternatives (e.g., data-dependent preconditioning, block structures, or screening-inspired bounds) could point to further gains.
- Reproducibility details: Clarify implementation choices (e.g., initialization of bounds beyond u0 = ∞, handling of active coordinates, step-size tuning for baselines), and ensure timing experiments are comparable across setups.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The analysis is sound, with clear expected progress comparisons, minimax formulation, and efficient algorithm for computing the distribution. The CD theory and stepsize design are a strong contribution.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. Improving figure readability and expanding discussion on when/why gains are largest would help.
- Originality: The safe minimax formulation and its efficient solution are novel and address a real gap between theory and practice for adaptive sampling.
- Significance: The approach advances practical adaptive sampling with guarantees and is likely to be adopted in CD-based solvers. Its impact for SGD appears more problem-dependent but still relevant.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, generic method with strong theoretical guarantees.
- Efficient algorithm that can be practical at scale.
- Demonstrated improvements over fixed sampling; close to optimal full-information sampling for CD.
- Clear connection to and extension of prior adaptive sampling literature.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited breadth of experiments; modest gains for SGD.
- Heavy reliance on bound quality; more guidance on constructing tighter bounds would be valuable.
- Figures currently hinder interpretability.

Actionable recommendation
- Enlarge and relabel figures in Section 5 for readability.
- Add more diverse experimental settings and provide detailed timing breakdowns.
- Expand discussion on constructing and maintaining tight bounds and on conditions under which SGD benefits most.
