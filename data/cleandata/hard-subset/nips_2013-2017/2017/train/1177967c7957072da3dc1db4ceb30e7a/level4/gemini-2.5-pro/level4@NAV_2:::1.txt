This paper presents a novel and practical adaptive importance sampling strategy, termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for accelerating coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to leverage efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient information to compute an approximation of the optimal, but computationally expensive, gradient-based sampling distribution. In contrast to prior work that often relies on heuristic approximations which can underperform fixed sampling, this paper's key contribution is to formally cast the selection of the optimal sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem with respect to the given bounds. The authors present an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem, resulting in a sampling strategy that is (i) provably optimal given the limited information, and (ii) guaranteed to be superior to uniform and standard fixed importance sampling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization problem (Equation 7) is a significant and elegant contribution. It provides a principled way to handle the uncertainty in gradient information, moving beyond the heuristic approximations used in prior work. The resulting "safety" guarantee—that the method is provably better than standard fixed sampling—addresses a major drawback of previous adaptive schemes and is a crucial result.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis, particularly Theorem 3.2 which establishes the safety guarantee, is strong and well-supported. The paper provides a complete piece of work, from theoretical motivation and analysis to an efficient practical algorithm and empirical validation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem by contextualizing it within the existing literature on importance sampling and highlighting the need for a method that is both adaptive and provably safe.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

The paper is strong, but its clarity and impact could be further improved by addressing the following points:

1.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The empirical evaluation demonstrates a clear benefit over uniform sampling and the infeasible optimal sampling. However, to better situate the work and demonstrate its practical significance, the experiments would be significantly strengthened by including comparisons against other state-of-the-art practical adaptive sampling schemes (e.g., [5, 23]). Furthermore, while the theory compares against Li-based fixed sampling (a very strong baseline), this method should also be included in the experimental plots.
2.  **Completeness of Results:** The experiments show timing results for coordinate descent, which effectively demonstrate the low overhead of the proposed method. For completeness, it would be valuable to include timing results for the SGD experiments as well, to empirically verify that the overhead remains negligible in that setting.
3.  **Minor Clarifications and Corrections:**
    *   In the proof of Lemma 2.1, the expression for the optimal stepsize α* appears to be missing a factor of 2. Could the authors please double-check this derivation?
    *   The purpose of Example 3.1 is slightly confusing. It is introduced after a statement claiming that naive choices for `c` (like the lower or upper bounds) can be poor, yet the example's calculations seem to show these choices are better than uniform sampling. Clarifying the takeaway message of this example would be helpful.
    *   In Algorithm 4, could the authors confirm the logic in line 4? Given the initialization `m = max(lsort)`, it is not immediately clear if the condition `[lsort]l > m` can be satisfied for the largest element. A quick check of the variable names and logic in the pseudocode would be beneficial.
    *   The title of the paper in reference [14] is incorrect.
    *   The authors might consider adding a reference to the related work by Csiba and Richtarik, "Importance Sampling for Minibatches" (2016).

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a novel and important contribution to the literature on importance sampling for large-scale optimization. The core idea is theoretically well-founded, and the resulting algorithm is practical and effective. The main weaknesses relate to the breadth of the experimental comparisons, which are addressable. I believe this work will be of high interest to the NIPS community and I recommend acceptance.
