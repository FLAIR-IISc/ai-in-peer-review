- Proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme: use maintained lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes and solve a minimax problem to pick a sampling distribution, with an O(n log n) solver and provable improvement over fixed sampling.

- Novel minimax formulation yields a principled, robust way to handle uncertain gradient information and a safety guarantee (always better than standard fixed sampling).

- Strong technical development: rigorous theory (Theorem 3.2, 3.4), clear formulation of (7), and an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4).

- Clear presentation and organization, with a logical progression from full-information to limited-information settings and well-explained algorithms.

- Practical relevance: shows how to maintain bounds for GLMs with O(n) overhead and provides extensive experiments (iteration and wall-clock) demonstrating significant speedups close to the infeasible optimal sampler.

- Method performance depends on the tightness of the gradient bounds; loose bounds could limit speedups and the paper should discuss effects and possible re-computation strategies.

- Computational overhead (O(n log n)) may dominate in "short-and-fat" regimes (d << n), potentially making the method slower than uniform sampling in wall-clock time.

- Empirical discrepancy: large gains for coordinate descent but only modest gains for SGD; the paper lacks an explanation for this difference.

- Missing empirical baselines: no comparison to simpler heuristics (e.g., using only upper bounds), which would strengthen claims about the minimax approach's advantage.

- Overall conclusion: a strong, well-written, and impactful contribution with minor limitations; strongly recommended for acceptance.
