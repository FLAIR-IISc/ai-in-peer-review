### Summary

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," designed to accelerate coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The work is motivated by the fact that while optimal adaptive sampling strategies (e.g., based on the full gradient) offer significant theoretical speed-ups, their computational cost is often prohibitive. Conversely, existing approximations can perform worse than simple uniform or fixed importance sampling.

The core idea is to leverage efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on the gradient components (for CD) or their norms (for SGD). Instead of using these bounds heuristically, the authors formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a principled minimax optimization problem. In each iteration, the proposed method finds a sampling distribution that maximizes the guaranteed one-step progress in the worst case, over all possible gradient vectors consistent with the given bounds.

The key contributions are:
1.  A theoretical analysis demonstrating that optimal gradient-based sampling in CD can achieve a speed-up of up to a factor of the dimension `n` compared to fixed sampling, reinforcing the motivation for adaptive methods.
2.  The development of the "safe" sampling scheme, which is shown to be (i) provably superior to fixed `L_i`-based sampling (which includes uniform sampling as a special case), and (ii) the optimal sampling distribution with respect to the worst-case analysis given the available bounds.
3.  An efficient algorithm that computes this sampling distribution in O(n log n) time.
4.  A demonstration of how to derive and maintain the required gradient bounds for an important class of problems, Generalized Linear Models (GLMs), at a cost of O(n) per iteration.
5.  Empirical results on several real-world datasets that validate the method's effectiveness, showing improvements in both iteration count and wall-clock time over fixed sampling schemes.

This work builds upon a rich literature on importance sampling in optimization (e.g., Nesterov, 2012; Richtárik and Takáč, 2016) and adaptive sampling (e.g., Zhao and Zhang, 2015; Csiba et al., 2015). Its main distinction from prior adaptive methods is the "safety" guarantee, which formally addresses the risk of underperforming relative to non-adaptive strategies—a known pitfall of approximate adaptive schemes.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization problem (Equation 7) is well-motivated and provides a solid foundation for the proposed method. The main theoretical results (Theorems 3.2 and 3.4) appear correct and rigorously establish the desirable properties of the proposed sampling scheme. The derived algorithm for solving the optimization problem (Algorithm 4) is non-trivial and its O(n log n) complexity is formally established. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against relevant baselines (uniform, optimal) and measuring both iteration complexity and wall-clock time to properly assess the practical impact of the computational overhead.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation for adaptive sampling in both CD and SGD (Section 2), logically proceeds to the main theoretical contribution of safe sampling with limited information (Section 3), provides a concrete example of its application (Section 4), and concludes with comprehensive experiments (Section 5). The notation is consistent, and the progression of ideas is easy to follow. The inclusion of Figure 1, which contrasts the different sampling algorithms, is particularly helpful for understanding the workflow.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax problem over an uncertainty set of gradients defined by bounds—is novel and elegant. While the idea of using gradient information for sampling is not new, previous works have typically relied on heuristics, stale values, or simple upper bounds, without providing guarantees that the resulting sampling is an improvement over fixed distributions. The "safety" guarantee is a key point of novelty and a significant conceptual advance. The efficient O(n log n) algorithm for solving this specific minimax problem is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem in large-scale machine learning: how to make stochastic optimization methods more efficient. The proposed method is generic and can be integrated into a wide range of existing CD and SGD solvers. The "safety" guarantee makes it a more reliable choice for practitioners compared to previous adaptive heuristics. The demonstration on GLMs is relevant to a large class of machine learning problems. The work provides both a practical algorithm and a new theoretical lens through which to view the problem of adaptive sampling, which is likely to inspire follow-up research.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The practical performance of the method is critically dependent on the tightness of the gradient bounds `l` and `u`. The paper demonstrates one way to obtain these bounds for GLMs using a simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which can be quite loose. A deeper discussion on how the quality of the bounds affects performance would strengthen the paper. For instance, an analysis of how the method's performance interpolates between fixed sampling (for trivial bounds) and optimal sampling (for exact bounds) would be insightful.
2.  **Limited Scope of Bound Derivation:** While the application to GLMs in Section 4 is a valuable proof of concept, the paper does not extensively discuss how to obtain such bounds for other important model classes, such as deep neural networks. This leaves the broader applicability of the method as an open question.
3.  **Analysis of the Competitive Ratio:** The theoretical analysis of the competitive ratio `ρ_k` (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) is somewhat preliminary. Lemma 3.5 relies on solving a non-convex problem to find `w_k`, making the bound difficult to interpret. Lemma 3.6 requires a strong assumption on the relative width of the uncertainty interval that may not hold in practice. A more refined analysis here would provide a better theoretical understanding of the method's sub-optimality compared to the true (but unknown) optimal sampling.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a novel, theoretically principled, and practical solution to the important problem of adaptive importance sampling.
*   **Pro:** The "safety" guarantee (provably better than fixed sampling) is a significant contribution that distinguishes this work from prior adaptive heuristics.
*   **Pro:** The methodology is supported by sound theory, an efficient algorithm, and convincing empirical results on real-world data.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very clearly written and well-structured, making its contributions easy to understand and appreciate.

### Arguments against Acceptance
*   **Con:** The performance relies heavily on the quality of gradient bounds, and the method for deriving these bounds is only detailed for GLMs.
*   **Con:** The theoretical analysis of the competitive ratio could be more comprehensive.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization.
