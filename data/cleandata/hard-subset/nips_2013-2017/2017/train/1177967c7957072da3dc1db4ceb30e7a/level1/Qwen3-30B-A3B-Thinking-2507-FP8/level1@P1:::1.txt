

# Peer Review: Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

This paper proposes a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The key insight is to use safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information to approximate the theoretically optimal gradient-based sampling, which is computationally infeasible to compute directly. The authors prove that their proposed sampling distribution is (i) provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling, (ii) can be efficiently computed in O(n log n) time per iteration (often negligible compared to gradient computation), and (iii) recovers the optimal gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting. The method is generic and applicable to a wide range of optimization problems, with extensive empirical validation on GLMs.

The work builds on prior NIPS contributions on adaptive sampling for CD and SGD (e.g., [22, 36, 37]), but makes a significant theoretical and practical advance. Previous adaptive sampling methods either lacked theoretical guarantees (e.g., [36, 37]) or required computations that were as expensive as full gradient methods. The authors' key innovation is formulating the problem of finding the best sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem over the safe bounds, rather than using ad hoc approximations. This approach is distinct from related work on adaptive sampling for coordinate descent [18, 19, 2, 33, 16, 6, 25, 24] and stochastic gradient methods [22, 36, 37].

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide clear proofs of the key properties (Theorem 3.2), including the guarantee that their sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling. The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5, 3.6) is particularly strong, establishing bounds on the worst-case performance relative to the optimal sampling. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple real-world datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) and various loss functions (square loss, squared hinge, logistic) with both L1 and L2 regularization. The results consistently demonstrate significant speed-ups over fixed sampling and uniform sampling.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent and clear, with careful definitions throughout. The key ideas (the safe bounds, the optimization formulation, the algorithm) are well-explained, and the experimental results are presented with sufficient detail to allow replication. The appendices provide complete proofs for the theoretical claims, which is valuable for readers.

**Originality**: This paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on adaptive sampling. The key innovation is the "safe" approach to approximating gradient-based sampling, which provides theoretical guarantees not present in previous work. Previous approximations (e.g., using upper bounds [36, 37] or stale values [22, 1]) could be worse than uniform sampling, but the authors prove their method is always better. The paper is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on adaptive sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37, 23]) and represents a clear advance in the field.

**Significance**: The results are highly significant for large-scale machine learning, where CD and SGD are workhorses. The method provides substantial speed-ups (often 2-5x) with minimal computational overhead, making it immediately applicable to real-world problems. The theoretical guarantees are particularly valuable, as they provide practitioners with confidence in the method's performance. The paper advances the state of the art in adaptive sampling by solving a fundamental computational bottleneck while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

**Strengths**: 
- Strong theoretical guarantees with clear proofs
- Practical algorithm with minimal computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration)
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and loss functions
- Clear exposition and well-organized presentation
- The method works with very loose safe gradient bounds, making it widely applicable

**Weaknesses**:
- The focus on GLMs (Section 4) limits the generality of the empirical validation; it would be valuable to see results on more complex models beyond GLMs.
- The paper doesn't fully explain why the method shows less dramatic improvements for SGD compared to CD (Figures 7-9), which is a notable observation.
- The "safe bounds" assumption (Section 4) is reasonable for GLMs but might not hold for all problem classes; a more detailed discussion of the limitations of this assumption would strengthen the paper.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments**:

*Pro Acceptance*:
- Provides the first theoretically grounded adaptive sampling method that is provably better than uniform sampling
- Solves a fundamental computational bottleneck in adaptive sampling
- Minimal computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration)
- Comprehensive empirical validation showing significant speed-ups on real datasets
- Clear, well-written paper with strong theoretical foundations

*Con Acceptance*:
- Limited empirical validation beyond GLMs
- Incomplete explanation of the SGD vs. CD performance discrepancy
- The "safe bounds" assumption might not be broadly applicable

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The theoretical guarantees are rigorous, the method is practical, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper advances the state of the art in adaptive importance sampling and provides a valuable tool for practitioners. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
