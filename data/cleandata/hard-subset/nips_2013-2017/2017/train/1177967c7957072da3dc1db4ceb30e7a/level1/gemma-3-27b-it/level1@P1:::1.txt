## Review of Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms, aiming to address the computational infeasibility of fully gradient-based adaptive sampling. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, resulting in a sampling distribution that is provably better than uniform sampling and, in the full information setting, recovers the optimal gradient-based sampling. The authors provide theoretical guarantees and demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive numerical experiments.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper addresses a well-known challenge in large-scale optimization: balancing the benefits of adaptive importance sampling (AIS) with its computational cost. Previous work, such as [22, 5, 26, 10, 21, 23], has shown that AIS can outperform static sampling strategies. However, methods like gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37] and duality-gap based sampling [5, 23] often require per-iteration computation of full gradients, limiting their scalability.  Approximations to these methods [36, 37, 22, 1] often lack theoretical guarantees or perform poorly. This paper builds upon this line of work by proposing a sampling scheme with provable guarantees and a computationally efficient implementation. The work is closely related to [36, 23], as it also aims to make adaptive sampling practical, but differs in its formulation as a convex optimization problem for defining the sampling distribution based on safe bounds, and the associated theoretical guarantees. The connection to stochastic coordinate descent and related variance reduction techniques [3, 18, 19] is also well established.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides strong theoretical results demonstrating the advantages of the proposed sampling scheme over uniform and fixed importance sampling.  The proof that the proposed method is “the best” given the bounds is a significant contribution.
* **Computational Efficiency:** The authors convincingly argue and demonstrate that the proposed sampling scheme can be computed efficiently, potentially at negligible extra cost in many applications. This is a critical aspect for practical adoption.
* **Generality:** The approach is generic and can be readily integrated into existing CD and SGD algorithms. This flexibility enhances its potential impact.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive numerical experiments on real datasets with various GLMs confirm the effectiveness of the proposed sampling scheme, showcasing improvements in both iteration count and runtime.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the key ideas and contributions. The inclusion of Algorithm 1, 2, 3 and 4 is helpful for understanding the practical implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The approach relies on the availability of safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient. While the authors demonstrate how to efficiently estimate these bounds in the context of GLMs (Section 4), the applicability to more complex models where these bounds are difficult to obtain is unclear. A more detailed discussion on the limitations of this assumption and potential remedies would be valuable.
* **Complexity of Optimization Problem (7):** While the authors provide an efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) for solving the optimization problem (7), the O(n log n) complexity per iteration might still be significant for very high-dimensional datasets. A discussion on potential approximations or further optimizations of Algorithm 4 could strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art SGD Methods:** The empirical comparison with SGD methods is somewhat limited.  While the results show improvement over uniform sampling, a comparison to more recent and advanced SGD variants (e.g., Adam, RMSprop) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method’s performance.
* **Competitive Ratio Discussion:** While the discussion of the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5, 3.6) is interesting, it feels somewhat disconnected from the main narrative. A clearer explanation of its practical implications would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in large-scale optimization.
* The theoretical guarantees are strong and the proposed approach is provably beneficial.
* The computational efficiency is a key strength, enabling practical adoption.
* The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on safe gradient bounds might limit the applicability to certain models.
* The O(n log n) complexity of Algorithm 4 could be a bottleneck for very high-dimensional data.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art SGD methods could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme offers a compelling balance between theoretical guarantees, computational efficiency, and practical performance. The limitations are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept**. I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017. The contributions are significant, the paper is well-written, and the results are compelling.  Minor revisions addressing the weaknesses mentioned above would further strengthen the paper.
