This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using bounds on the gradient, ensuring theoretical guarantees and computational efficiency. The authors demonstrate that the proposed scheme is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling and can be integrated into existing algorithms with negligible extra cost. Extensive numerical testing validates the theoretical findings.

The paper builds upon existing work in importance sampling for optimization, particularly adaptive variants. Prior research (e.g., [22, 5, 26]) has explored gradient-based sampling but often faces computational challenges. This work addresses these challenges by proposing a safe approximation based on gradient bounds. It relates to the broader area of accelerated coordinate descent and stochastic optimization methods (e.g., [18, 19, 2, 33]). The work acknowledges limitations of existing approximations ([36, 37]) and aims to provide a more robust and theoretically sound alternative. The connections to existing work are well established and cited appropriately.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating the advantages of the proposed sampling scheme compared to uniform and fixed importance sampling. The provable guarantees are a significant strength.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed method is designed to be computationally efficient, with the overhead claimed to be minimal.
*   **Generality:** The scheme is generic and can be applied to both CD and SGD algorithms, increasing its applicability.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive numerical results on real datasets support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the scheme.
*   **Safe Approximation:** The use of safe bounds is a crucial contribution, ensuring that the approximation doesn't perform worse than fixed sampling.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumption on Gradient Bounds:**  The method relies on the availability of safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient. While the authors provide examples for GLMs, the applicability to other problem classes might depend on the ease of obtaining such bounds.  A more thorough discussion of scenarios where obtaining these bounds is challenging would be beneficial.
*   **Complexity of Algorithm 4:** While claimed to be O(n log n), the explanation could benefit from more details about the implementation nuances.
*   **SGD Performance:**  The results show only modest improvements for SGD. A deeper analysis of why the performance gain is smaller compared to CD would be valuable.
*   **Comparison to Recent Work:** While related work is cited, a more explicit comparison with very recent work on adaptive sampling (e.g., [3, 26, 36]) concerning convergence rates and practical performance could strengthen the paper.
*   **Notation:** While generally clear, the notation could be more consistent. For example, the use of both ` and u to represent bounds, and the subtle differences between the subscript 'k' and superscripts like '?' can sometimes be confusing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in large-scale optimization—efficient adaptive importance sampling.
*   The theoretical results are strong and provide valuable insights into the benefits of the proposed method.
*   The practical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the scheme on real-world datasets.
*   The proposed method is well-motivated, clearly presented, and makes a demonstrable contribution to the field.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on safe gradient bounds might limit the applicability of the method to certain problem classes.
*   The modest performance gains for SGD raise questions about the overall impact of the method in that setting.
*   Minor notational inconsistencies could hinder readability.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The theoretical results are solid, the method is computationally efficient, and the empirical evaluation supports the claims. While the reliance on safe gradient bounds and the limited performance gains for SGD are potential concerns, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above (clarify assumptions around gradient bounds, provide a more detailed analysis of SGD performance, and refine the notation). The clarity and theoretical rigor of the paper outweigh the minor drawbacks.
