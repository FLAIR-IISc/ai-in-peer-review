This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for speeding up coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, providing theoretical guarantees and efficient computation.

The paper is well-motivated. Importance sampling is a crucial technique for large-scale optimization, and adaptive variants offer advantages but often suffer from computational intractability. The authors correctly identify this gap and propose a practical approximation. The connection to existing work is appropriately established, referencing relevant papers on importance sampling, coordinate descent, and stochastic gradient methods. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and contributions.

**Quality:** The paper appears technically sound. The theoretical analysis, particularly the Lemma 2.1 and the derivation of the optimal sampling distribution, seems rigorous. The arguments for why the proposed scheme is provably better than uniform sampling and fixed importance sampling are persuasive. The proof sketches provided (with full proofs deferred to the appendix) suggest a strong understanding of the underlying theory. The presentation of Algorithm 2 and Theorem 3.2, establishing the properties of the proposed sampling, is clear and concise. The discussion of computational complexity and its relation to gradient evaluation is also well-justified.  However, the detailed proofs in the appendix are crucial for a full assessment of the technical soundness, and weren’t fully evaluated within the time constraint.  The experimental section provides evidence supporting the theoretical claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is standard and consistent. The flow of ideas is logical, building from the problem statement to the proposed solution and finally to the experimental validation. The organization into sections (Introduction, Adaptive Importance Sampling with Full Information, Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling with Limited Information, etc.) is effective. However, the mathematical notation can be dense, and some readers might struggle with the more complex derivations without careful study.  More intuitive explanations of the optimization problem in Section 3, before diving into the formal derivation, would have been beneficial.

**Originality:** The idea of approximating gradient-based sampling with safe bounds is novel and contributes to the existing body of work on importance sampling. While adaptive importance sampling is not entirely new, the proposed approach's focus on safety and computational efficiency differentiates it from prior methods. The specific optimization formulation in Section 3, aiming for the “best possible” sampling distribution given limited information, and the resulting efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) appear to be original contributions.  The connection to duality gaps and existing gradient-based sampling methods is appropriately acknowledged.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant. If the proposed scheme can consistently deliver speedups in CD and SGD without sacrificing accuracy, it would be a valuable tool for practitioners dealing with large-scale machine learning problems. The fact that the method can be integrated into existing algorithms with minimal overhead increases its potential for adoption. The experimental results demonstrate promising performance gains on several datasets.  The ability to work with only safe bounds, rather than full gradient information, is particularly important for practical applications. The work advances the state-of-the-art in adaptive importance sampling by providing a theoretically grounded and computationally efficient alternative to existing methods.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis, establishing the advantages of the proposed sampling scheme.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient, making it practical for large-scale problems.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The method addresses a real-world challenge in large-scale optimization and has the potential to improve the performance of widely used algorithms.
*   **Novelty:** The approach of approximating gradient-based sampling with safe bounds and formulating it as a convex optimization problem is original.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed scheme.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Mathematical Density:** The paper contains a significant amount of mathematical notation, which might be challenging for some readers.
*   **Appendix Reliance:** A thorough assessment of the technical soundness requires a careful review of the appendix, which wasn’t feasible within the review timeframe.
*   **SGD Performance:** The experimental results for SGD show relatively modest improvements compared to CD, raising questions about the applicability of the method to all types of optimization problems.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The theoretical contributions are sound, the proposed method is computationally efficient and practically relevant, and the experimental results are promising. While the mathematical density and reliance on the appendix are minor drawbacks, they do not outweigh the paper’s strengths. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, a slightly more intuitive explanation of the optimization formulation in Section 3 would be helpful. A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the method, particularly its performance on SGD compared to CD, would also be valuable.
