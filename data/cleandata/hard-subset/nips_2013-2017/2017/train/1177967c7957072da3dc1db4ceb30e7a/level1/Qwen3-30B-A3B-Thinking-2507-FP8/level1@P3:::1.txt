

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for optimization algorithms like coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core contribution is an efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling that uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to construct a sampling distribution provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling, while avoiding the computational cost of full gradient computation.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds on existing work on adaptive importance sampling [22, 36, 37], which has shown that gradient-based sampling can significantly improve convergence rates over static sampling. However, these methods typically require full gradient computation at each iteration, which is often prohibitive. Previous work [36, 37] has attempted to address this by using upper bounds on gradient norms, but these approaches do not guarantee improvement over fixed sampling.

The authors' key innovation is formulating the optimal sampling problem as a convex optimization problem with respect to safe bounds, and providing an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve it. This differs from previous approaches by guaranteeing that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), which was not established for prior methods using safe bounds.

**Strengths**

1. The theoretical analysis is strong. The paper provides clear proofs that the proposed sampling distribution is provably better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2) and derives the computational complexity (O(n log n) per iteration).

2. The method is practical and easy to implement. The authors demonstrate how to efficiently compute safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs), and show that the overhead is often negligible compared to the cost of evaluating a single coordinate gradient. The paper's computational complexity analysis is thorough, showing the method can be implemented with minimal overhead.

3. The experimental results are convincing. The proposed method shows significant speed-ups over fixed importance sampling in CD (up to a factor of n in theory, and significant improvements in practice), with minimal overhead. The method works well even with very loose safe bounds, requiring no initialization of gradient estimates.

**Weaknesses**

1. The practical benefits are primarily demonstrated for CD, while the improvement for SGD is more modest. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the method works better for CD than SGD, which limits the broader applicability of the approach given the prominence of SGD in modern machine learning.

2. The theoretical analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5 and 3.6) is somewhat abstract and doesn't directly translate to the observed practical improvements. The paper would benefit from a more concrete analysis of how the quality of safe bounds affects performance gains.

3. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from "safe screening" techniques [Liu et al., ICML 2014; Ndiaye et al., 2014], which also use safe bounds for optimization. Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
- Theoretical contribution is significant: provides a provably better adaptive sampling scheme that avoids computational pitfalls of previous approaches.
- Practical benefits are demonstrated on real-world datasets with minimal implementation overhead.
- The method is generic and can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations.
- The paper makes a clear contribution to the optimization literature with sound theoretical analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
- The practical benefits are limited to CD, with less significant improvements for SGD, which is more widely used in modern machine learning.
- The explanation for the CD vs. SGD performance difference is lacking.
- The paper doesn't address how the method would work with non-convex objectives, which are increasingly common in deep learning.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling method is theoretically sound, practically implementable, and provides significant speed-ups for coordinate descent algorithms. While the benefits for SGD are more modest, the method still offers a provable improvement over fixed sampling with minimal overhead.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear theoretical contributions and convincing empirical results. The main limitation is the focus on CD rather than SGD, but the authors provide a clear explanation for why the method works better for CD (due to the nature of coordinate updates). The paper makes a clear, well-supported contribution that advances the state of the art in adaptive importance sampling for optimization algorithms.

I recommend acceptance, as this work represents a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning optimization with a novel solution that provides both theoretical guarantees and practical benefits.
