### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the challenge of adaptive importance sampling for large-scale optimization methods like coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). While it is known that adapting the sampling distribution based on gradient information can significantly accelerate convergence, the "optimal" distributions (e.g., proportional to gradient magnitudes) require computing the full gradient at each iteration, which negates the efficiency of stochastic methods.

The authors propose a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme that circumvents this issue. Instead of exact gradient information, the method relies on efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds (`l_k`, `u_k`) on the gradient magnitudes (for CD) or norms (for SGD). The core contribution is to formulate the selection of the sampling distribution `p_k` as a minimax problem: find the distribution `p_k` that minimizes the worst-case value of a key term in the convergence bound, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradients consistent with the bounds (`l_k` and `u_k`).

The authors show that this minimax problem has an analytical solution that can be computed efficiently in O(n log n) time. Crucially, they prove that the resulting sampling distribution is always better than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform or L_i-based sampling), a "safety" guarantee that previous heuristic approximations lacked. They demonstrate how to maintain the required bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) with O(n) overhead per iteration. Extensive experiments on several real-world datasets confirm the theoretical claims, showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time, and performs close to the computationally infeasible optimal sampling.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The central idea of framing the sampling problem as a minimax optimization over a "confidence set" of gradients defined by the bounds is highly novel and elegant. This provides a principled and robust way to handle the uncertainty in gradient information. The resulting "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2), which proves the method is always an improvement over standard fixed sampling, is a significant contribution that addresses a major shortcoming of prior adaptive sampling approximations.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development in Section 3 is rigorous, leading to the clear optimization problem in (7) and its solution in Theorem 3.4. The provision of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the safe sampling distribution is a strong practical and theoretical result that makes the entire framework viable. The connections drawn between the minimax problem and geometric interpretations (minimizing the angle with the vector of Lipschitz constants) provide valuable intuition.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, distinguishing the proposed work from various existing sampling strategies. The progression from the ideal "full information" setting (Section 2) to the practical "limited information" setting (Section 3) is logical and helps the reader appreciate the core challenges and the proposed solution. The algorithms are presented clearly.

4.  **Practical Relevance and Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate the practicality of their method by showing how to maintain the required gradient bounds for the important class of GLMs. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering multiple datasets, loss functions, and regularizers for both CD and SGD. The inclusion of both iteration-count and wall-clock time plots is crucial and convincingly demonstrates that the computational overhead is justified by the accelerated convergence. The plots showing the evolution of the stepsize parameter `v_k` provide excellent insight and nicely complement the theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The performance of the method is fundamentally tied to the tightness of the gradient bounds `l` and `u`. The paper demonstrates a simple and effective way to maintain these bounds using Cauchy-Schwarz, but these bounds can be quite loose, potentially limiting the achievable speedup. A more detailed discussion on the impact of bound quality would be beneficial. For instance, do the bounds degrade over many iterations, and would it be beneficial to periodically re-compute the full gradient to reset them? Exploring tighter, albeit potentially more expensive, bounding strategies could be an important direction for future work.

2.  **Computational Overhead in Certain Regimes:** The paper argues that the O(n log n) overhead for computing the sampling distribution is often negligible. This holds true when the cost of a single coordinate gradient evaluation, O(d), is large (i.e., `d = Omega(n)`). However, in the setting where the data is "short and fat" (`d << n`), this overhead could dominate the O(d) update cost and make the method slower than uniform sampling in terms of wall-clock time. While the experiments are on datasets where this is not an issue, acknowledging and discussing this limitation would make the paper more complete.

3.  **Discrepancy between CD and SGD Performance:** The experiments show a very significant performance gain for CD, often approaching the ideal full-information sampler. In contrast, the gains for SGD are much more modest, with the optimal sampler itself providing only a small boost over uniform sampling. The paper notes this observation but does not offer an explanation. A discussion of why this might be the case (e.g., perhaps the SGD update changes the geometry so quickly that the gradient norm information becomes stale almost immediately for all components) would add valuable insight.

4.  **Lack of Comparison to Other Heuristics:** The paper provides a strong theoretical argument against prior approximation schemes (e.g., using stale values or only upper bounds). However, an empirical comparison against such a baseline, for instance, a method that naively defines the sampling distribution using only the upper bounds `u_k`, would have strengthened the experimental section. This would have provided a direct empirical validation of the benefits of the proposed minimax "safe" formulation over simpler, non-safe heuristics.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme is novel, theoretically well-grounded, and practically effective. The minimax formulation for choosing the sampling distribution is a powerful idea that provides a much-needed guarantee of improvement over non-adaptive methods. The strengths in novelty, technical quality, and empirical validation far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which mostly represent opportunities for further investigation. The paper is well-written and will likely be influential, inspiring further research into robust and efficient adaptive sampling methods. I strongly recommend acceptance.
