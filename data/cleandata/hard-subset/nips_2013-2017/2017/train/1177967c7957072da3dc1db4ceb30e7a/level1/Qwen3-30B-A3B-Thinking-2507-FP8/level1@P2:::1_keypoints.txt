- Proposes a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for CD and SGD using upper/lower gradient bounds to compute approximate sampling efficiently.
- Formulates sampling as an optimization minimizing worst-case performance over gradient bounds, yielding a distribution always better than fixed sampling.
- Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2, Lemma 3.5, Lemma 3.6) and a bounded competitive ratio relative to the infeasible full-gradient optimum.
- Distinguishes clearly from prior adaptive methods by offering provable guarantees rather than unguaranteed approximations.
- Shows the method has minimal computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration), small compared to typical gradient computation (O(d)).
- Empirical evaluation on multiple real-world datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) and losses (square, squared hinge, logistic) demonstrates consistent significant speedups over fixed importance sampling.
- Demonstrates practical robustness: method works well even with very loose gradient bounds.
- Paper is very clear and well-organized, with consistent notation, illustrative examples, and well-explained pseudocode (Algorithms 1–4).
- Discussion of computational complexity and visualized experimental results are effective and convincing.
- Novel contribution fills a key gap by enabling efficient, provably better adaptive sampling without full gradient computations.
- Method is broadly applicable to large-scale optimization and easily integrable into existing CD/SGD implementations.
- Strengths include strong theory, clear distinction from prior work, comprehensive experiments, practical feasibility, and low overhead.
- Weaknesses: competitive-ratio guarantees require conditions on gradient bounds (though authors argue they're practical).
- Weaknesses: focus mainly on GLMs, with extensions to more complex models noted but left for future work.
- Weaknesses: comparison to optimal full-gradient sampling is limited for SGD (more pronounced gains shown for CD).
- Overall recommendation: accept — strong theory, clarity, novelty, empirical impact, and practical applicability.
