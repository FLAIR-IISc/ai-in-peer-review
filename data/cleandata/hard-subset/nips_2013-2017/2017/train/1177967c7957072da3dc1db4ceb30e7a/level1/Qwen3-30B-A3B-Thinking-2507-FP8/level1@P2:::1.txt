

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms, addressing a key limitation in existing adaptive methods: the computational infeasibility of computing optimal gradient-based sampling distributions. The authors introduce a "safe" approach that uses upper and lower bounds on gradient information to compute an approximate sampling distribution that is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling, with minimal computational overhead.

The paper builds on previous work on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37]), which demonstrated theoretical advantages but required full gradient computations at each iteration. The authors make a clear distinction between their approach and prior work that used approximations without guarantees (e.g., [36, 37]), showing how their safe bounds approach provides provable performance guarantees. The key innovation is formulating the sampling problem as an optimization that minimizes the worst-case performance over the gradient bounds, leading to a distribution that is always better than fixed sampling.

**Quality**: The paper excels in technical quality. The authors provide rigorous theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2, Lemma 3.5, Lemma 3.6) demonstrating that their proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling and has a bounded competitive ratio compared to the optimal (but computationally infeasible) gradient-based sampling. The theoretical results are well-supported by mathematical derivations, with proofs deferred to the appendix. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, using multiple real-world datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) and objective functions (L1/L2 regularization with square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss). The results consistently show significant speedups over fixed importance sampling, with minimal computational overhead. The authors also clearly demonstrate that the approach works even with very loose gradient bounds, which is a strong practical advantage.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The main contributions are clearly stated in the introduction, and the mathematical notation is consistent throughout. The key algorithms (Algorithms 1-4) are presented clearly with well-explained pseudocode. The authors effectively use examples (Examples 2.2, 2.3) to illustrate theoretical concepts before introducing their novel contribution. The discussion of computational complexity (Section 4.3) is particularly clear, showing that the O(n log n) overhead per iteration is minimal compared to gradient computation (O(d)) for typical machine learning problems. The experimental results are presented with appropriate visualizations (Figures 2-9) that clearly show the advantages of the proposed method.

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a key gap in the literature: how to compute adaptive importance sampling distributions efficiently while providing theoretical guarantees. While adaptive importance sampling has been studied before (e.g., [22, 36, 37]), these works either lacked theoretical guarantees or required full gradient computations. The "safe" approach proposed here is novel in how it formulates the sampling problem as an optimization over the gradient bounds, providing the first method that guarantees better performance than fixed sampling while being computationally efficient. The authors cite relevant prior work thoroughly and clearly distinguish their contribution from previous approaches.

**Significance**: The significance of this work is substantial. Large-scale optimization is fundamental to modern machine learning, and the proposed method can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations with minimal overhead. The paper demonstrates real-world impact through extensive experiments showing speedups on multiple datasets and problem types. The theoretical guarantees provide practitioners with confidence in the method's performance. The approach is general enough to be applicable to a wide range of optimization problems, not just the GLM examples shown. The authors also correctly note that the method could be extended to more complex models with further research on gradient bound tracking.

**Strengths**:
1. Strong theoretical guarantees that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling
2. Clear distinction from prior work and demonstration of why previous approaches fail to provide guarantees
3. Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing significant speedups in practice
4. Practical implementation details showing the method is feasible for real-world applications
5. Minimal computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration, comparable to gradient computation)

**Weaknesses**:
1. The theoretical guarantee about the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5, Lemma 3.6) requires some conditions on the gradient bounds, though the authors show these conditions are easily satisfied in practice.
2. The paper focuses primarily on GLMs, though the authors correctly note the approach is general and could be extended to other models.
3. The experimental comparison with the optimal (full-gradient) sampling is limited to the CD case; the SGD results show less dramatic improvements, but the authors provide a reasonable explanation for this.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed method addresses a practical problem with both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation, and the computational overhead is minimal enough to make it widely applicable. The writing is clear, the theory is sound, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate the approach's advantages. This work should be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on large-scale machine learning algorithms.

I recommend acceptance. The paper meets all four review criteria exceptionally well, with strong theoretical foundations, clear presentation, significant novelty, and substantial practical impact. The proposed method has the potential to become a standard component in large-scale optimization algorithms, and the theoretical guarantees provide a solid foundation for its use.
