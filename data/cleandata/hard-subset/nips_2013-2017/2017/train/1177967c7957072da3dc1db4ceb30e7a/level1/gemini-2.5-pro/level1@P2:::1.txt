### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for large-scale optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The core idea is to address the key limitation of optimal gradient-based adaptive sampling: its computational infeasibility due to the requirement of calculating the full gradient at every iteration.

The authors' approach is to use efficiently computable and maintainable "safe" bounds—a lower bound vector `l` and an upper bound vector `u`—on the magnitudes of the gradient components (for CD) or the norms of the partial gradients (for SGD). Instead of naively using these bounds (e.g., using `u` as a proxy for the gradient), which can lead to performance worse than uniform sampling, the paper formulates the problem of choosing the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. The goal is to find a probability distribution `p` that minimizes the worst-case value of the expected progress upper bound, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradients consistent with the bounds `l` and `u`.

The key contributions are:
1.  A theoretical analysis showing that ideal gradient-based sampling can offer a speed-up of up to a factor of `n` for CD.
2.  The "safe" sampling scheme, derived from the minimax formulation, which is (i) provably the best possible given the bounds, (ii) guaranteed to be better than or equal to fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform sampling), and (iii) efficiently computable in `O(n log n)` time.
3.  A demonstration of how to maintain the required gradient bounds efficiently for an important class of problems: Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).
4.  Extensive empirical validation on several real-world datasets, showing significant performance gains in both iteration count and wall-clock time over fixed sampling strategies.

This work relates to a rich literature on importance sampling in large-scale optimization. It builds upon prior work on both fixed importance sampling (e.g., based on Lipschitz constants) and adaptive sampling (e.g., based on gradient norms or duality gaps). The crucial distinction from previous adaptive methods that use gradient approximations or bounds is the "safety" guarantee. While prior methods could potentially underperform compared to uniform sampling, this paper provides a principled way to leverage partial information that guarantees improvement, thus bridging the gap between the theoretical power of adaptive sampling and its practical, reliable implementation.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs provided in the appendix. The central idea of formulating the sampling problem as a minimax optimization (Eq. 7) is sound and well-motivated. The derivation of an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve this problem is a strong technical contribution. The authors are careful in their evaluation, comparing against both uniform sampling and the infeasible "optimal" sampling, providing a clear picture of where their method stands. The experiments are comprehensive, covering multiple loss functions, regularizers, and datasets for both CD and SGD, which strongly substantiates the paper's claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation for "safe" sampling is clearly articulated in the introduction. The paper follows a logical flow, first presenting the ideal full-information setting (Section 2) to motivate the approach, and then introducing the practical limited-information solution (Section 3). The distinction between the proposed method and prior work is made very clear. The figures are informative, especially the inclusion of the `v_k` plots, which provide excellent visual insight into how the adaptive step sizes improve over the fixed baseline. One minor suggestion for improvement would be to add a bit more intuition in the main text about the workings of Algorithm 4, as its connection to the optimality conditions in Eq. (9) is not immediately obvious without consulting the appendix.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is highly original. While adaptive importance sampling is a known concept, the formulation of a "safe" sampling strategy via a minimax problem over an uncertainty set (defined by the gradient bounds) is novel. This approach elegantly resolves a critical weakness of previous approximation-based adaptive samplers—the lack of a performance guarantee over simpler methods. The development of a specialized, efficient solver for this specific minimax problem is also a novel algorithmic contribution. This work is significantly different from previous papers at NIPS and elsewhere, as it is the first to provide a practical adaptive sampling scheme with such a strong theoretical safety guarantee.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be impactful. The proposed sampling scheme is generic and can be readily integrated into many existing CD and SGD solvers as a "drop-in" improvement. The "safety" guarantee is a powerful feature, as it removes the risk for practitioners of using a complex adaptive scheme that might slow down convergence. The work convincingly demonstrates that even with very simple and loose bounds (derived from Cauchy-Schwarz), significant practical speed-ups can be achieved. This opens the door for future research into developing tighter and more sophisticated bounding techniques for various other model classes, which could further enhance the performance of this framework. The paper advances the state-of-the-art in stochastic optimization by making adaptive sampling more practical, reliable, and theoretically sound.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novel and principled approach:** The minimax formulation for finding a safe sampling distribution is a novel and elegant idea that directly addresses a key challenge in adaptive sampling.
*   **Strong theoretical guarantees:** The "safety" guarantee (provably never worse than fixed sampling) is a major theoretical contribution that distinguishes this work from prior art.
*   **Practicality and Efficiency:** The method is accompanied by an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm and is demonstrated to yield significant wall-clock time improvements in practice.
*   **High-quality presentation and evaluation:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and supported by a thorough and convincing set of experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Dependence on bound quality:** The practical performance relies on the quality of the gradient bounds. The paper uses simple, potentially loose bounds; the performance in scenarios where tighter bounds are difficult to obtain is not explored.
*   **Modest gains for SGD:** The empirical results show that the performance gain for SGD is less pronounced than for CD. The paper notes this but does not deeply analyze the reason, which might limit the perceived impact for the SGD-dominant deep learning community.
*   **Overhead concerns:** While the `O(n log n)` overhead is often acceptable, it may become a bottleneck for problems where `n` is extremely large and the cost of a single coordinate/gradient update (`d`) is very small.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a complete piece of work with strong theoretical foundations, a novel algorithm, and compelling practical results. It solves an important problem in a principled and original way. I strongly recommend acceptance.
