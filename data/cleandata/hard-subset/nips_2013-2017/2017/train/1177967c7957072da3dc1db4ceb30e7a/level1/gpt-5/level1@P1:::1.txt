Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles adaptive importance sampling for stochastic optimization, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The ideal adaptive scheme uses gradient-based probabilities that change at each iteration (e.g., sampling coordinates proportional to |∇i f|√Li in CD or components proportional to ‖∇fi(x)‖ in SGD), which maximizes a model-based expected progress bound but is typically infeasible due to the need for full gradient information every iteration. The authors propose a “safe” adaptive sampling method that relies on efficiently maintainable lower/upper bounds on gradients (or component gradient norms). They formulate a convex min–max problem that chooses the sampling distribution p to minimize the worst-case expected progress bound over all gradients consistent with the bounds. They show: (i) this distribution is optimal with respect to the information encoded in the bounds (best worst-case), (ii) it is always at least as good as uniform or fixed Li-based importance sampling, and (iii) it can be computed in O(n log n) time per iteration. They also propose an adaptive stepsize αk coupled with the chosen p, yielding unbiased updates. The method is integrated into CD and SGD, with examples of how to maintain safe bounds in generalized linear models (GLMs), and is empirically validated on several text datasets.

This work relates to a line of research on non-uniform and adaptive sampling in CD/SGD: fixed importance sampling based on Li (e.g., Nesterov; Richtárik & Takáč), gradient-based sampling for SGD (Zhao & Zhang; Needell et al.; Alain et al.), gap-based/adaptive sampling in CD and SDCA (Perekrestenko et al.; Csiba et al.; Qu et al.; Osokin et al.), and steepest/GS rules in CD (Nutini et al.; Stich et al.). Prior “adaptive” methods often either require full gradients or resort to heuristics (stale gradients; upper bounds), without guarantees that they outperform uniform/fixed sampling. The novelty here is a principled, worst-case-optimal sampling given only safe bounds, plus an efficient algorithm and guarantees that it improves on fixed sampling.

Strengths:
- Clear optimization-based formulation: The min–max problem (eq. 7) precisely captures the best distribution given uncertainty sets Ck. The geometric characterization and the efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Thm. 3.4) are elegant.
- Theoretical guarantees: Theorem 3.2 establishes that the proposed sampling is (a) optimal w.r.t. the given bounds and (b) never worse than Li-based sampling (uniform when Li=L). The competitive-ratio discussion (Lemmas 3.5–3.6) adds insight.
- CD analysis and stepsize: The αk choice tied to p (Lemma 2.1) provides a clean expected progress bound and shows potentially large speedups over fixed sampling (up to factor n in favorable cases).
- Practicality in GLMs: Section 4 offers concrete, simple ways to maintain safe bounds using Cauchy–Schwarz, with O(n) updates per iteration; the overall overhead is modest when d=Ω(n).
- Empirical validation: On several GLM tasks, the method nearly matches the ideal (infeasible) gradient-based sampling for CD and outperforms uniform/fixed sampling in both iterations and time, despite using very loose bounds.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Experimental breadth and baselines: While comparisons to uniform and the infeasible “optimal” are useful, the paper lacks comparisons to strong adaptive alternatives for CD, such as duality-gap based sampling (e.g., Csiba et al. 2015; Perekrestenko et al. AISTATS 2017) and Gauss–Southwell variants (Nutini et al., approximate steepest CD by Stich et al. 2017). For SGD, comparisons to stale-gradient sampling or variance-reduced methods with importance sampling (e.g., SAGA/SVRG with non-uniform sampling) would strengthen claims.
- SGD impact is modest: The gains are small for SGD in the presented experiments. Discussion on when the method is expected to help SGD (e.g., heavy-tailed per-example gradient norms, non-uniform curvature) and an expanded empirical study would aid significance.
- Dependence on bounds quality: Guarantees are worst-case over Ck. The paper shows favorable vk values empirically, but a more thorough sensitivity analysis (how bound tightness affects vk and wall-clock) would be valuable. Are there settings where bound maintenance is too loose or too costly?
- Overhead and implementation details: O(n log n) per iteration is acceptable when d=Ω(n), but can be non-trivial otherwise. Can p̂k be updated incrementally across iterations, reusing prior orderings or exploiting sparsity to approach O(s log n) where s coordinates change? Memory costs for tracking bounds should be reported.
- Assumptions and constants: The coordinate-wise Li are crucial; guidance on setting/estimating Li beyond simple GLMs would improve clarity. For general h with ∇2h varying, the safety of bounds via Lemma 4.1 depends on bounding ∇2h along segments; more explicit assumptions and examples would help.
- Reproducibility details: Some timing experiments are on different machines; a standardized setup would better support conclusions. More datasets beyond text and inclusion of denser data would broaden the case.

Quality:
The theoretical development appears sound, resting on standard smoothness models, the minimax swap (Sion/Komiya), and convexity of the inner objective. The proofs are sketched clearly; the algorithmic derivation for Thm. 3.4 is convincing. The empirical evaluation supports the main claims for CD; for SGD, conclusions are more cautious.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. Notation is consistent; the role of αk and p is clear. A few suggestions: collect all assumptions (on smoothness, availability of bounds) early; spell out how Li are chosen in GLMs; fix minor typos; unify the presentation of CD and SGD analyses.

Originality:
The safe min–max formulation and the O(n log n) constructive solution for sampling under interval uncertainty, together with guarantees relative to fixed sampling, are novel to my knowledge. The adaptive stepsize coupled with p in CD and the observation on potential n-fold speedups reinforce novelty. The relation to prior “safe bound” approaches is well positioned.

Significance:
For CD, the method is practically relevant and likely to be adopted in solvers where gradient tracking is feasible, offering robust improvements over fixed sampling with minimal risk. For SGD, significance is more limited based on current experiments, but the framework is general and could be impactful in settings with cheap bound maintenance or in distributed scenarios.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled, generic, and efficient adaptive sampling with worst-case guarantees over uncertainty sets.
- Always improvements over fixed Li-based/uniform sampling; near-optimal in practice for CD.
- Solid theory, clear algorithm, and meaningful empirical gains on standard benchmarks.
- Easy to integrate into existing CD/SGD implementations.

Cons against acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to several strong adaptive CD/SGD baselines, limiting empirical conclusiveness.
- Modest gains for SGD; unclear conditions where it shines beyond GLMs/CD.
- Overhead may be non-negligible when d ≪ n; limited discussion of incremental updates and sparsity.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add baselines: duality-gap adaptive CD/SDCA, Gauss–Southwell variants, and non-uniform SVRG/SAGA.
- Provide an ablation on bound tightness vs. performance, and report exact overhead per iteration.
- Explore incremental maintenance of p̂k to reduce O(n log n) amortized cost.
- Extend experiments to denser data and models with varying curvature; standardize timing environment.
- Clarify assumptions and practical estimation of Li and Hessian bounds in non-quadratic GLMs.

Overall, this is a strong and well-motivated contribution with solid theory and practical relevance, especially for coordinate descent.
