This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The key motivation is that while optimal adaptive sampling strategies (e.g., based on the full gradient) offer significant theoretical speed-ups, they are computationally infeasible as they require information that is as expensive to compute as a full gradient step. Existing approximations often rely on upper bounds of gradient norms, but these can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The main idea of this paper is to work with efficiently maintainable safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient information (magnitudes for CD, norms for SGD). Instead of picking an arbitrary point within these bounds (e.g., the upper bound), the authors propose a principled, robust approach. They formulate the problem of choosing the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem: find the distribution `p` that minimizes the worst-case expected progress, where the worst case is taken over all possible true gradient vectors consistent with the current bounds. This "safe" formulation leads to the paper's key theoretical result: the resulting sampling distribution is provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform or Lipschitz-based sampling), while adapting to the optimization trajectory. The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this minimax problem in each iteration.

This work fits well within the extensive literature on accelerating first-order methods. It directly builds upon and improves the line of work on adaptive importance sampling for SGD (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, 2015; Papa et al., 2015) and CD (e.g., Csiba et al., 2015; Perekrestenko et al., 2017). The novelty lies in the minimax formulation, which provides a "safety" guarantee that was missing from previous schemes that used gradient bounds.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported and the proofs provided in the appendix appear sound. The central result (Theorem 3.2), which establishes that the proposed sampling is always better than Li-based sampling, is strong and provides a compelling reason to use this method. The development of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) for the non-trivial optimization problem in (7) is a solid technical contribution. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering multiple loss functions, regularizers, and standard datasets, and convincingly demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors are also careful to evaluate both iteration complexity and wall-clock time, showing that the computational overhead of the method is justified by the faster convergence.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly, starting with the ideal (but impractical) optimal sampling, and logically progressing to the proposed safe and efficient approximation. The problem formulation is clean and the notation is consistent. The inclusion of Algorithm 1-3 in Figure 1 provides a concise comparison of the different approaches. The empirical results are presented clearly in figures that are easy to interpret. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work. A minor suggestion for improvement would be to include a brief, high-level intuition for why Algorithm 4 works and achieves its O(n log n) complexity in the main text, as the details are currently deferred entirely to the appendix.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is highly original. While the idea of using bounds to approximate gradient norms for sampling is not new, the formulation of the problem as finding a sampling distribution that is robust to the uncertainty in these bounds via a minimax game is novel. This principled approach is what distinguishes this work from prior heuristics and allows for the provable safety guarantee. The specific algorithm derived to solve this minimax problem is also a novel contribution. The paper does an excellent job of citing and differentiating itself from related work.

**Significance:** The results are significant and likely to be impactful. CD and SGD are foundational algorithms in machine learning, and improvements to their efficiency are of broad interest. The proposed method offers a "drop-in" replacement for standard sampling schemes that comes with a strong theoretical guarantee and requires minimal assumptions (the existence of safe bounds). The "safety" property is particularly important from a practical standpoint, as it removes the risk of slowing down convergence, making the method much more attractive for adoption by practitioners. The paper demonstrates how such bounds can be maintained efficiently for the important class of Generalized Linear Models, showcasing a clear path to application. The ideas presented here could inspire further research into robust optimization of other algorithmic choices (e.g., step sizes, block sizes) under limited information.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The minimax approach to deriving a sampling distribution under uncertainty is a novel and elegant idea.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantee:** The method is provably "safe," guaranteeing it will not perform worse than standard fixed importance sampling. This is a crucial advantage over prior adaptive heuristics.
3.  **Efficient Implementation:** The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm, making the method practical.
4.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The experiments show significant speed-ups in both iterations and wall-clock time over standard baselines across various models and datasets.
5.  **High Clarity and Polish:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and presents a complete and convincing piece of research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The practical performance hinges on the ability to maintain reasonably tight gradient bounds. The proposed scheme using a simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequality works well in the experiments, but its effectiveness might degrade in other settings or over very long optimization runs.
2.  **Overhead:** The O(n log n) overhead per step, while often negligible as argued by the authors, could become a bottleneck for problems where n is extremely large and the per-coordinate computation cost `d` is very small.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a novel, significant, and technically sound contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
