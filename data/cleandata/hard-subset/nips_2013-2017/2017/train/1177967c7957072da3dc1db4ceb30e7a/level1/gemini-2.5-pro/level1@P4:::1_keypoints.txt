- Introduces "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," a minimax-based adaptive sampling scheme to accelerate CD and SGD using gradient bounds.
- Motivated by the gap between theoretically optimal adaptive sampling (expensive) and heuristics that can underperform fixed sampling.
- Uses efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on gradient components/norms and optimizes sampling to maximize worst-case one-step progress.
- Theoretically shows gradient-based sampling can yield up to an n-fold speed-up over fixed sampling in CD.
- Proposes a "safety" guarantee: the sampling is provably superior to fixed L_i-based (including uniform) sampling under given bounds.
- Derives the optimal worst-case sampling distribution via a minimax formulation.
- Provides an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the sampling distribution with formal complexity analysis.
- Shows how to obtain and maintain required gradient bounds for GLMs at O(n) cost per iteration.
- Empirical results on real datasets demonstrate iteration and wall-clock improvements over fixed sampling baselines.
- Builds on existing importance-sampling literature but is novel in its minimax safety guarantee and efficient solver.
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, clearly organized, and includes helpful illustrations (e.g., Figure 1).
- Main weakness: practical performance depends heavily on tightness of gradient bounds, which can be loose (e.g., Cauchy-Schwarz bounds for GLMs).
- Limited discussion on deriving bounds for other model classes (e.g., deep networks), leaving broader applicability unclear.
- Analysis of the competitive ratio ρ_k is preliminary: Lemma 3.5 involves a non-convex problem and Lemma 3.6 relies on strong assumptions that may not hold.
- Acceptance arguments: principled, practical, theoretically supported, efficient, empirically validated, and clearly presented.
- Rejection arguments: reliance on bound quality and limited theoretical refinement of the competitive-ratio analysis.
