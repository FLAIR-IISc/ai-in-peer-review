- Proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for CD and SGD based on efficiently maintainable safe lower and upper gradient bounds.
- Motivation: optimal adaptive sampling needs full-gradient info (impractical); existing upper-bound approximations can perform worse than uniform sampling.
- Main idea: formulate sampling choice as a minimax problem minimizing worst-case expected progress over all gradients consistent with current bounds.
- Theoretical guarantee: the resulting "safe" sampling distribution is provably never worse than standard fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform or Lipschitz-based).
- Provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve the minimax problem each iteration.
- Builds on and improves prior adaptive importance sampling work by adding a principled safety guarantee missing from previous heuristics.
- High technical quality: proofs appear sound and Theorem 3.2 is a strong central result.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough across losses, regularizers, and datasets, showing iteration and wall-clock speedups that justify the overhead.
- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well-written and reproducible; minor suggestion to give a high-level intuition for Algorithm 4 in the main text.
- Originality: the minimax safety formulation and the derived algorithm are novel contributions beyond prior bound-based heuristics.
- Significance: method is a practical "drop-in" improvement for CD/SGD with minimal assumptions and potential broad impact; demonstrates applicability to GLMs.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel principled formulation, strong theoretical safety, efficient implementation, excellent empirical results, and high clarity.
- Arguments against acceptance: practical performance depends on bound tightness (may degrade in some settings) and O(n log n) overhead could be a bottleneck when n is extremely large and per-coordinate cost is tiny.
- Overall: strong paper with minor weaknesses; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
