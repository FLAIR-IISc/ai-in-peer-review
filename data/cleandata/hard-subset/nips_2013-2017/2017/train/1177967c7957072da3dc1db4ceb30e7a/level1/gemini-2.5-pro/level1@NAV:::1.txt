This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, named Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling, for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The main idea is to address the prohibitive computational cost of optimal gradient-based sampling, which requires full gradient information at each step. The authors propose using efficiently computable safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. Unlike previous works that use heuristics like sampling based on the upper bounds (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, 2015), this paper introduces a principled approach by formulating the choice of the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem. This finds the distribution that is optimal for the worst-case gradient realization within the given bounds. This "safe" approach is the core contribution, differentiating it from prior art by providing theoretical guarantees.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Originality and Quality:** The formulation of the sampling procedure as a min-max problem is novel and technically sound. It provides a robust way to handle uncertainty in gradient information. The key theoretical result (Theorem 3.2), proving the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling (e.g., uniform sampling), is a strong "safety" guarantee that previous approximations lacked.
2.  **Significance:** The method is generic and can be readily integrated into widely-used CD and SGD optimizers. The paper provides a practical `O(n log n)` algorithm to compute the sampling distribution, ensuring its overhead is minimal for many machine learning problems, especially when `d >> n`. This is convincingly demonstrated by strong empirical results on real-world datasets, which show significant speedups in wall-clock time.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem with the "ideal" full-information case before introducing its practical, "limited-information" solution. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and extensive experiments.

The paper has only minor weaknesses.
**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The method for deriving and maintaining the safe gradient bounds is primarily demonstrated for Generalized Linear Models. While effective here, the performance of the overall scheme is highly dependent on the tightness of these bounds. Its applicability and effectiveness on more complex models where deriving cheap and tight bounds is non-trivial could be discussed further.
2.  The experimental gains for SGD are notably less pronounced than for CD. While the authors acknowledge this, a brief discussion on why this might be the case would have strengthened the empirical section.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practically-impactful contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses.
