Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses adaptive importance sampling for randomized optimization (coordinate descent, CD, and stochastic gradient descent, SGD). Full-information gradient-based sampling is known to be optimal for the one-step quadratic model but is typically infeasible since it requires the full gradient (for CD) or all component gradient norms (for SGD) per iteration. The authors propose a “safe” approximation based on per-iteration lower/upper bounds on these quantities. They cast the problem of choosing sampling probabilities as a minimax problem: choose p to minimize the worst-case ratio V(p, c)/||c||^2 over all c in a box C defined by the bounds (` ≤ c ≤ u), where V(p, c) = ∑i Li c_i^2 / p_i. They show that (i) the resulting distribution p̂ is optimal for the given uncertainty set; (ii) it is always at least as good as fixed Li-based sampling (and thus uniform when Li ≡ L); and (iii) as the bounds tighten to the true c, p̂ recovers the full-information gradient-based sampling. They provide an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute p̂ by a thresholding/clamping scheme (akin to “water-filling”), and choose a stepsize α̂ = 1/vk where vk is the optimal worst-case value. They show in CD that the expected one-step decrease improves over fixed sampling (can be up to a factor n in the equal-L case). For GLMs, they demonstrate how to maintain safe gradient bounds cheaply using simple inequalities (e.g., Cauchy–Schwarz) and give analogous constructions for SGD. Experiments on GLMs (square, hinge, logistic losses; L1/L2 penalties) show substantial gains for CD versus uniform/fixed importance sampling, and smaller gains for SGD, consistent with prior reports (e.g., Zhao & Zhang 2015; Qu & Richtárik 2016; Csiba et al. 2015). The work relates to and extends prior adaptive sampling for CD (e.g., duality-gap sampling, Gauss–Southwell) and SGD (gradient-norm sampling), providing a robust alternative with worst-case guarantees given partial information.

Quality
- Theory: The one-step analysis for CD is standard and sound. The minimax formulation is convex in p and concave (or at least quasi-convex in the relevant ratio) in c on a compact box; the paper cites Sion/Komiya to justify min–max interchange, with proofs in the appendix. The “always-better-than-Li-based” guarantee is clear: since pL is feasible in (7) with value Tr[L], the optimal value vk ≤ Tr[L], yielding V(p̂, c) ≤ Tr[L]||c||^2 for all c in C. The geometric interpretation and the structure of the optimizer (c_i equals either bound or a threshold √Li m) are persuasive and lead to an efficient algorithm.
- Stepsizes: The choice γk = αk/pk[i] and αk = 1/vk yields an unbiased (directionally) update and a safe expected decrease bound; this aligns with established importance-sampling analyses for CD/SGD.
- Practicality: A potential weakness is computational overhead. Each iteration requires O(n) to maintain bounds (when using the conservative Cauchy–Schwarz update) and O(n log n) to compute p̂. This is substantial in typical sparse, high-dimensional CD settings where a coordinate update may cost O(nnz(column)) ≪ n. The paper argues the overhead is negligible when d = Ω(n), but many applications have n large and per-coordinate costs small. Experiments indicate manageable overhead on the presented datasets, but broader evidence (varying sparsity, n/d) would strengthen the claim. For SGD, the observed gains are small, which reduces the practical significance of the added complexity in that setting.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The shift from standard CD stepsizes (γ = 1/Li) to the α/p design is explained, though a brief intuition on “directionally-unbiased” updates would help non-experts. Algorithm 4 is compact; variable naming in the pseudocode (overloading u, `, indices) could be clarified. The theoretical properties (Theorems 3.2, 3.4) are clearly stated, but a main-text convergence theorem (beyond one-step progress) for convex/strongly convex cases would be useful, not only in appendix.

Originality
The robust/minimax formulation for sampling under safe bounds and its exact O(n log n) solution are, to my knowledge, novel. Prior adaptive methods either rely on stale full gradients, heuristic bounds, or duality gaps; here the authors provide worst-case guarantees relative to the uncertainty set and show strict dominance over fixed sampling. The connection to gradient-based sampling in the full-information limit is also clean. The CD result that adaptive gradient-based sampling can yield up to n-fold speedups over fixed sampling in the equal-L case is known in spirit (steepest CD), but the unified, safe approach is new.

Significance
For CD on large-scale GLMs, the method can be impactful, offering near-optimal adaptive sampling without full gradients and with provable advantages over fixed sampling. The O(n log n) per-iteration overhead and O(n) bound maintenance may limit applicability in very sparse regimes; showing amortized or occasional recomputation strategies (e.g., refresh p̂ every T iterations; lazy safe-bounds with expansion) would broaden utility. For SGD, since benefits are modest on tested problems, significance is more limited.

Pros
- Novel, principled minimax formulation yielding a “safe” adaptive sampling distribution with strong worst-case guarantees.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm with clear structure; recovers full-information optimal sampling as bounds tighten.
- Theoretical dominance over fixed importance sampling; clear expected one-step improvement bounds.
- Practical construction of safe bounds for GLMs; extensive experiments showing strong gains for CD.
- Clean integration of stepsize with sampling (α = 1/v), avoiding hand-tuned schedules.

Cons
- Computational overhead O(n) (bound updates) + O(n log n) (sampling) per iteration may be prohibitive in sparse settings; the conditions under which overhead is negligible are not comprehensively explored.
- Limited gains for SGD in experiments; unclear when the method is advantageous for finite-sum problems beyond GLMs.
- Global convergence rates and comparisons are largely deferred to the appendix; main text could present a primary rate theorem.
- Experimental setup uses unnormalized 0/1 features; more diverse preprocessing and sparsity levels would strengthen evidence.
- Some clarity issues in Algorithm 4 pseudocode and in presenting the step-size intuition.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a main-text convergence rate (convex/strongly convex) for Algorithm 2 and an explicit comparison to uniform and Li-based sampling rates.
- Analyze amortized/periodic computation of p̂ and lazy bound maintenance to reduce per-iteration complexity; report ablations on recomputation frequency vs. performance.
- Extend experiments to highly sparse regimes and to problems where per-coordinate costs are very small, quantifying overhead.
- For SGD, include tasks where gradient norms vary significantly (e.g., imbalanced data, non-smooth components) to better assess potential benefits; or discuss when theory predicts limited gains.
- Clarify Algorithm 4 variables and add a brief proof sketch in the main text for Theorem 3.4 to aid reproducibility.

Overall evaluation
A solid and original contribution with clear theory and promising empirical results for CD. While computational overhead and limited SGD gains temper the breadth of impact, the safe minimax construction is elegant and valuable. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address clarity and practical overhead considerations.
