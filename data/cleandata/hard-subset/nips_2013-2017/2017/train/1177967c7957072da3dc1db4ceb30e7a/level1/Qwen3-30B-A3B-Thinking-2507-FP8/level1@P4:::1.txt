

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling technique for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address a key limitation of existing adaptive importance sampling methods, which either require full gradient computations (computationally prohibitive) or use approximations that may be worse than uniform sampling. Their key contribution is a method that uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to formulate an optimization problem that yields a sampling distribution provably better than fixed importance sampling, while being computationally efficient.

The work builds upon previous research on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 36, 37] for gradient-based sampling, [18, 19, 2] for fixed importance sampling) and extends the theoretical foundation established in works like [5, 23]. The authors demonstrate that their approach bridges the gap between theoretically optimal but computationally infeasible adaptive sampling and practical fixed importance sampling.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis showing that the proposed sampling distribution is always better than uniform or fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), with provable convergence guarantees. The authors establish the relationship between their method and the optimal gradient-based sampling (Example 2.3) and characterize the worst-case performance ratio (Lemma 3.5, 3.6).

2. **Computational Efficiency:** The authors demonstrate that their method requires only O(n log n) time per iteration (Theorem 3.4), which is comparable to the cost of computing a single gradient component (Θ(d)) when d = Ω(n). This makes the approach practical for large-scale applications.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The paper provides comprehensive empirical results on multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) across different loss functions (square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss) and regularizations (L1, L2). The results consistently show significant improvements over fixed importance sampling in terms of both convergence speed and total computational time.

4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured with clear mathematical formulations, intuitive examples, and helpful algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-4). The experimental results are presented clearly through multiple figures showing iteration complexity, timing, and the evolution of key parameters like vk.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Generality of Gradient Bounds:** While the authors demonstrate how to obtain safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs), they provide limited discussion of how these bounds could be derived for more complex models beyond GLMs. A more thorough exploration of the applicability to other model classes would strengthen the paper's claim of generality.

2. **Computational Complexity Nuance:** The paper's claim that the overhead is "at negligible extra cost" assumes d = Ω(n), which might not hold for all applications (e.g., very high-dimensional data with small n). A more nuanced discussion of the computational trade-offs across different problem scales would be beneficial.

3. **Comparison to Recent Work:** The paper could better contextualize its contributions against very recent work on adaptive sampling, such as [Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling, Dmytro Perekrestenko et al., AISTATS 2017], which also proposes a similar approach for coordinate descent.

**Significance:** The proposed method addresses an important practical challenge in large-scale optimization. By providing a theoretically sound and computationally efficient alternative to existing adaptive sampling methods, the work has clear potential to improve the performance of CD and SGD algorithms across a wide range of machine learning applications. The empirical results demonstrate significant speedups in practice, making this a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a theoretically sound approach with provable guarantees.
- The computational efficiency makes the method practical for real-world applications.
- The empirical results consistently demonstrate significant improvements over fixed sampling methods.
- The paper is well-written, well-structured, and thoroughly validated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The focus on GLMs limits the immediate applicability to other model classes without additional work.
- The computational complexity analysis could be more comprehensive for different problem scales.
- The paper could better distinguish itself from very recent related work.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The theoretical results are sound, the method is practically efficient, and the empirical validation is comprehensive. The paper addresses an important gap in adaptive importance sampling and provides a solution that is both theoretically justified and computationally feasible. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work.
