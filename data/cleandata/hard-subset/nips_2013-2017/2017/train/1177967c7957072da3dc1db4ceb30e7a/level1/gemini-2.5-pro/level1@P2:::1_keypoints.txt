- Proposes "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling" for CD and SGD that uses maintainable lower/upper gradient bounds (l, u) to avoid full-gradient computation.  
- Frames sampling choice as a minimax optimization minimizing worst-case expected progress over gradients consistent with bounds.  
- Theoretical result: ideal gradient-based sampling can yield up to an n-fold speed-up for coordinate descent.  
- Develops a "safe" sampling scheme that is provably optimal given the bounds, guaranteed no worse than fixed importance sampling, and computable in O(n log n).  
- Provides an efficient method to maintain required gradient bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs).  
- Empirical validation across losses, regularizers, and datasets shows significant iteration and wall-clock improvements over fixed sampling.  
- High technical quality with rigorous proofs and a strong derivation of the O(n log n) solver (Algorithm 4).  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized; figures (e.g., v_k plots) offer useful intuition.  
- Minor clarity suggestion: add more intuition in the main text linking Algorithm 4 to the optimality conditions.  
- Highly original: novel minimax "safety" formulation and specialized efficient solver—first practical adaptive sampler with provable safety.  
- Significant impact: generic drop-in improvement for CD/SGD, safety removes practitioner risk, and simple bounds already yield practical speed-ups.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled novelty, strong theoretical guarantees, practical efficiency, and thorough evaluation.  
- Cons against acceptance: performance depends on bound quality; gains for SGD are modest and not deeply analyzed; O(n log n) overhead may be costly when n is extremely large and per-update cost is tiny.  
- Overall verdict: strengths far outweigh weaknesses; strong recommendation for acceptance.
