Proposes safe adaptive importance sampling using safe upper/lower gradient bounds to produce a sampling distribution provably better than fixed importance sampling while remaining computationally efficient.

Bridges the gap between theoretically optimal but infeasible gradient-based adaptive sampling and practical fixed importance sampling.

Theoretically sound: proves improvement over uniform/fixed sampling (Theorem 3.2), relates to optimal gradient-based sampling, and characterizes worst-case performance (Lemmas 3.5, 3.6).

Computationally efficient: O(n log n) time per iteration (Theorem 3.4), comparable to computing a single gradient component when d = Ω(n).

Strong experimental validation: extensive results on multiple datasets, losses, and regularizations showing faster convergence and lower computational time than fixed importance sampling.

Clear presentation: well-structured math, intuitive examples, explicit algorithms (Algorithms 1–4), and informative figures tracking iteration complexity, timing, and parameter evolution.

Generality concern: safe gradient bounds are demonstrated mainly for GLMs with limited discussion of deriving bounds for more complex model classes.

Complexity nuance: the “negligible extra cost” claim relies on d = Ω(n) and lacks a nuanced analysis for very high-dimensional/small-n regimes.

Comparison gap: could better contextualize and distinguish contributions from very recent related work (e.g., Perekrestenko et al., AISTATS 2017).

Significance: offers a practically useful, theoretically justified method that can improve CD and SGD performance in large-scale optimization.

Arguments for acceptance: solid theory with guarantees, practical computational cost, consistent empirical speedups, and clear exposition.

Arguments against acceptance: limited immediate applicability beyond GLMs, incomplete complexity analysis across problem scales, and insufficient differentiation from recent adaptive-sampling papers.
