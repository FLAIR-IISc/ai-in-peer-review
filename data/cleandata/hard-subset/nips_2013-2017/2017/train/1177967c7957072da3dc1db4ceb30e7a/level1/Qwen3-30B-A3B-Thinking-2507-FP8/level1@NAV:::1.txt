

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address a key limitation of gradient-based adaptive sampling—its computational infeasibility due to requiring full gradient computations—by introducing a method that uses safe bounds on gradient values to construct a provably better sampling distribution. The approach is theoretically grounded, showing that their sampling distribution is always superior to uniform or fixed importance sampling, and can be computed efficiently with O(n log n) complexity per iteration. The paper relates to previous NIPS work on adaptive sampling (e.g., Csiba & Richtárik 2015, Schmidt et al. 2015) but makes a significant advance by providing provable guarantees for the approximate sampling, unlike prior approaches that lacked such guarantees.

The paper excels in quality, with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by clear proofs of key properties (Theorems 3.2, 3.4). The empirical evaluation on real datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) demonstrates substantial speedups over fixed sampling methods, with the proposed method achieving near-optimal performance while incurring minimal computational overhead. The clarity is excellent—the algorithms are well-structured, notation is consistent, and the exposition flows logically from theory to practice. The originality is strong: while prior work used gradient bounds for adaptive sampling, this paper uniquely formulates the sampling problem as a convex optimization with worst-case guarantees, providing a novel solution that outperforms previous approximate approaches.

The significance is high, as the method applies broadly to CD and SGD algorithms in machine learning, offering practical benefits for large-scale optimization. The authors convincingly demonstrate that even very loose gradient bounds suffice for effective sampling, making the approach widely applicable.

Arguments for acceptance: (1) Strong theoretical contributions with provable guarantees; (2) Practical implementation with minimal computational overhead; (3) Extensive empirical validation on real datasets; (4) Clear connection to and advancement beyond prior NIPS work on adaptive sampling.

Arguments against acceptance: (1) The computational complexity (O(n log n)) may be non-trivial for extremely large n; (2) The empirical results for SGD show less dramatic improvements than for CD, though the paper adequately explains this.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, with clear theoretical and practical significance. It addresses a meaningful challenge in scalable optimization with a novel, well-supported solution. I recommend acceptance.
