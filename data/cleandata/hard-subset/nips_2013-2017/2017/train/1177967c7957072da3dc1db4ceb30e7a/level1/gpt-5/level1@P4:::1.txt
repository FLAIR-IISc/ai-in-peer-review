Summary
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for randomized optimization methods, primarily coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). Full-information gradient-based sampling is known to be optimal in a one-step model but computationally infeasible because it requires computing all coordinate/component gradients every iteration. The authors introduce a min-max formulation that, given safe lower and upper bounds on per-coordinate (CD) or per-component (SGD) gradient magnitudes, returns a sampling distribution p̂k and stepsize α̂k = 1/vk that are:
- Optimal in the worst case with respect to the provided bounds.
- Guaranteed to be no worse than fixed Li-based (including uniform) sampling in the same worst-case sense.
- Exact (matching full gradient-based sampling) when bounds are tight.

The key technical ingredients are: (i) formalizing the expected one-step progress bound for CD under unbiased updates with stepsize γk = αk/pik; (ii) showing the optimal distribution with full information is p∝√Li|∇if|; (iii) casting the limited-information case as minp∈Δn maxc∈Ck V(p,c)/||c||2² with Ck a box defined by the safe bounds, and reducing it to maxc∈Ck ||√L c||1/||c||2; (iv) deriving a closed-form thresholding solution for the worst-case c (clipping each coordinate to the box boundary or a common √Li m level) and an O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute p̂k. Theoretical properties include p̂k’s dominance over fixed sampling (Theorem 3.2) and competitive ratio bounds (Lemmas 3.5–3.6). For GLMs, the paper shows how to maintain safe gradient bounds cheaply via simple inner-product updates (using Cauchy-Schwarz). Experiments on GLMs with L1/L2 regularization demonstrate substantial gains for CD (close to full-information sampling) and more modest gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
- Fixed nonuniform sampling in CD (e.g., proportional to Li) goes back to Richtárik & Takáč (2014), Qu et al. (2015), Allen-Zhu et al. (2017). Adaptive sampling based on duality gaps/violations appears in Csiba et al. (2015), Perekrestenko et al. (2017). For SGD, gradient-norm-based sampling is known (Zhao & Zhang, 2015; Zhu, 2016). Approximate gradient-based schemes using stale or upper bounds have been proposed, but without worst-case guarantees that they always outperform fixed distributions.
- The paper’s contribution is a principled min-max formulation that uses safe bounds to produce a sampling guaranteed to dominate fixed sampling, plus an explicit efficient algorithm. This appears novel relative to previous heuristic or bound-based approximations.

Strengths
- Clear optimization-based formulation of the sampling problem under limited information, with a clean reduction to a convex subproblem per fixed c and a tractable worst-case maximization over a box.
- Provable properties: best worst-case guarantee given the bounds; strictly dominates fixed Li-based sampling; recovers optimal full-information sampling when bounds are exact.
- Practical algorithm (Algorithm 4) with O(n log n) complexity per computation of p̂k, and implementable bound maintenance strategies for GLMs in both CD and SGD.
- Empirical results on standard datasets show strong gains for CD, with stepsizes adapting via vk; timing comparisons suggest overhead can be amortized in practice.

Weaknesses
- Computational overhead: computing p̂k is O(n log n) and updating bounds is O(n) per iteration, which can be substantial when n (number of coordinates/features) is large and d (data dimension) is not Ω(n). The paper claims negligible overhead “in many applications,” but scenarios with n≫d (e.g., text with millions of features and tens of thousands of examples) are common; more detailed timing and amortization analysis is needed. Clarify whether p̂k is recomputed every iteration or less frequently, and whether sorting/thresholds are warm-started to reduce costs.
- Guarantees are worst-case with respect to the bounds, not necessarily reflecting realized performance when bounds are very loose. While experiments show gains even with naive Cauchy-Schwarz bounds, tighter or adaptive bounding strategies would strengthen claims, and an ablation on bound tightness vs. performance would be informative.
- Limited baselines: comparisons focus on uniform/fixed Li-based and full-information “optimal.” Missing comparisons to (i) Gauss-Southwell/steepest CD and approximate variants (Nutini et al., 2015; Stich et al., 2017), (ii) adaptive sampling by duality gaps/violations (Csiba et al., 2015; Perekrestenko et al., 2017), and (iii) stale or heuristic gradient-based sampling. This would better position the contribution empirically.
- For SGD, improvements are minor in reported experiments; guidance on when the method is expected to help (e.g., highly heterogeneous component gradients, tighter bounds) would improve significance.

Quality
- Theoretical derivations are well-motivated and appear sound. The reduction to max ||√L c||1/||c||2 over a box and the thresholding structure are elegant; reliance on standard minimax interchanges is appropriate. The CD one-step analysis is standard and correctly leverages unbiased updates.
- Experimental evidence supports the claims for CD. For SGD, results are mixed but honest.
- Some complexity claims could be quantified more carefully; include per-epoch costs and amortized overhead, especially when n≫d.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The notation is heavy but consistent. The step-size parameterization γk = αk/pik could be motivated more explicitly to bridge to standard CD step sizes.
- The practical section on bound maintenance is helpful; adding pseudo-code for SGD and clarifying computation frequency of p̂k would improve reproducibility.
- Minor typos exist; a proofreading pass is suggested.

Originality
- The safe min-max formulation with worst-case dominance over fixed sampling, and its efficient solver, are novel to the best of my knowledge. The full-information CD optimal sampling characterization is known in spirit, but the limited-information safe approximation and guarantees are the main contribution.

Significance
- For CD on GLMs and similar problems where safe bounds are inexpensive and heterogeneous gradients occur, the method is likely to be adopted. The worst-case safety guarantee over fixed sampling is appealing.
- For SGD, impact may be more limited unless tighter bounds or settings with large gradient variance are present.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a more detailed complexity analysis, including how often p̂k is recomputed, warm starts for sorting/thresholds, and amortized per-epoch costs for n≫d.
- Add comparisons to Gauss-Southwell (and approximate variants) and duality-gap adaptive CD methods.
- Include ablations on the effect of bound tightness and update frequency on performance.
- Discuss memory and implementation details for maintaining bounds in large-scale sparse settings.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled adaptive sampling under limited information with solid worst-case guarantees.
- Efficient algorithm with clear structure and implementable bound-maintenance strategies.
- Strong empirical gains for CD across datasets; theory and practice aligned.

Arguments against acceptance
- Potentially high per-iteration overhead when n is large; practicality unclear in common n≫d regimes without amortization details.
- Limited empirical comparisons to other adaptive CD baselines.
- Benefits for SGD are modest in reported experiments; scope of impact may be narrower than claimed.
