This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for speeding up coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling with a method that relies on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients, ensuring theoretical guarantees and computational efficiency. The authors demonstrate that their approach is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling and can be integrated into existing algorithms with minimal overhead.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the limitations of existing adaptive importance sampling methods. The theoretical contributions, particularly the analysis of CD with adaptive sampling (Section 2) and the derivation of the safe sampling scheme (Section 3), are solid and presented with sufficient mathematical rigor. The experimental evaluation (Section 5) provides compelling evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed method on several datasets and with different loss functions.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of adaptive importance sampling, establishing clear performance guarantees. The comparison to fixed and uniform sampling is well-justified.
* **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed safe adaptive sampling scheme is computationally efficient and can be easily integrated into existing optimization algorithms. The algorithm's complexity is well-analyzed.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear notation and explanations. The organization is logical, and the figures are informative.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in practice, validating the theoretical findings. The comparison to baseline methods is fair and comprehensive.
* **Novelty:** The approach of formulating the sampling distribution selection as a convex optimization problem given bounds on the gradient is novel and provides a strong theoretical basis for the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumption on Gradient Bounds:** The reliance on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients (`, u`) is a potential limitation. While the authors discuss how these bounds can be estimated in the context of GLMs (Section 4), it's not clear how easily these bounds can be obtained for more complex models or non-convex optimization problems.  The sensitivity of performance to the tightness of these bounds is not thoroughly investigated.
* **SGD Results are Less Impressive:** The experimental results for SGD show a smaller improvement compared to CD. This discrepancy is not fully explained, and further investigation is needed to understand why the proposed method is less effective in the SGD setting.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** While the datasets used are standard, the experimental evaluation could be expanded to include more diverse machine learning tasks and models. The impact of different regularization parameters and data distributions could also be explored.
* **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemmas 3.5 & 3.6) while interesting, relies on somewhat restrictive assumptions regarding the range of gradient bounds. A more relaxed analysis, or practical guidance on ensuring these conditions hold, could strengthen the results.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The discussion of computational cost could be more precise, particularly concerning the O(n log n) complexity of the sampling computation. A breakdown of the costs associated with different steps in the algorithm would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important problem in large-scale optimization – accelerating iterative algorithms using adaptive sampling.
* The proposed method is theoretically sound, with clear performance guarantees.
* The practical algorithm is efficient and easy to implement.
* The experimental results provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on safe gradient bounds could limit the applicability of the method to certain problems.
* The results for SGD are less compelling than those for CD.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme is a valuable addition to the toolkit of optimization algorithms, and the theoretical analysis and experimental results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the paper.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Elaborate on the challenges of obtaining safe gradient bounds for more complex models.
2.  Investigate the reasons for the less pronounced improvement in the SGD setting.
3.  Consider expanding the empirical evaluation to include a wider range of tasks and models.
4.  Provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost.
5. Clarify the applicability of the competitive ratio analysis and potentially relax the assumptions.
