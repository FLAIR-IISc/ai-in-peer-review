Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for stochastic optimization, applicable to coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate the full-information gradient-based sampling—known to be optimal in maximizing a standard quadratic progress bound but typically infeasible—by solving, at each iteration, a convex minimax problem over a set of safe lower/upper bounds on the (component-wise) gradient magnitudes. The resulting distribution p̂k is:
- worst-case optimal with respect to the available bounds Ck (best minimax sampling),
- provably never worse than Li-based fixed importance sampling (and hence uniform when Li=L),
- efficiently computable in O(n log n) time via an algorithm based on sorting and a simple thresholding structure.

The method yields a stepsize α̂k = 1/vk tied to the minimax value, and recovers the full-information gradient-based sampling when bounds are tight. The authors give complementary results for CD and revisit known results for SGD, provide a competitive-ratio analysis, show how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for GLMs, and present empirical results on text datasets, showing pronounced gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD.

This work relates to and extends: fixed importance sampling for CD/SGD (Nesterov; Richtárik & Takáč), adaptive probabilities using dual residues/optimality violations (e.g., Csiba–Qu–Richtárik; Perekrestenko et al.), gradient-norm sampling for SGD (Zhao & Zhang; Zhu), and screening rules (Liu et al.; Ndiaye et al.) which are complementary. The novelty is the safe minimax formulation and efficient solver that guarantee improvement over fixed sampling without requiring full gradients.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical motivation for adaptive gradient-based sampling; the paper shows explicit expected-progress improvements over fixed sampling in the full-information case (Example 2.3) and quantifies potential n-fold speedups.
- Novel minimax formulation using safe bounds leading to p̂k with strong properties: best worst-case guarantee and dominance over fixed Li sampling (Theorem 3.2). The geometric characterization and the efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Theorem 3.4) are elegant.
- Practical guidance for obtaining bounds in GLMs and for both CD and SGD, with simple safe updates (using Cauchy–Schwarz) and an analysis of per-iteration cost.
- Empirical results on multiple datasets demonstrate large speedups for CD (both in iterations and time) and show that even loose bounds help; overhead appears manageable.
- The stepsize design tied to the sampling (α̂k=1/vk) is principled and improves convergence over conservative fixed stepsizes.

Weaknesses:
- Overhead and practicality: the per-iteration O(n log n) computation and O(n) bound maintenance are nontrivial in high dimensions; the claim of “negligible extra cost” hinges on regimes where the cost of a single coordinate update is Ω(n). Many sparse problems have cheaper coordinate updates, and the paper would benefit from a more systematic runtime breakdown across sparsity regimes and larger n.
- Bounds quality and applicability: the safe bounds based on Cauchy–Schwarz can be very loose; while the experiments show improvements, a more thorough study of how tightness affects vk and performance (beyond the γ-based bound) would strengthen the case. Extending beyond GLMs is discussed but not demonstrated.
- SGD results show limited gains versus uniform; more analysis explaining when adaptive sampling helps in SGD (e.g., variance structure, heterogeneity metrics) would clarify significance for that setting.
- Related work discussion could better situate against adaptive-probability CD methods using dual residues/optimality violations and against approximate Gauss–Southwell rules; empirical comparisons to those would be informative.
- Some theoretical pieces (e.g., full convergence rates) are deferred to the appendix; the main text could summarize rates and explicit constants to aid the reader.

Quality:
- Technical soundness is high. The progress bound derivation, optimal distribution under full information, the minimax formulation (with justification via Sion/Komiya to swap min/max), dominance over Li-based sampling, and the efficient solver are convincing. The competitive ratio bounds (wk, vk, γ^4) are somewhat loose but appropriate for worst-case analysis. Assumptions (coordinate-wise L-smoothness; safe bounds) are reasonable and clearly stated.
- The stepsize and sampling coupling are carefully reasoned. Convergence arguments for CD follow standard machinery from (3) and Lemma 2.1; however, including explicit convergence rates in the main text would enhance completeness.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and organized, with intuitive explanations (e.g., geometric view) and clear algorithms. The roles of n (number of coordinates) and d (ambient/sample dimension) could be clarified earlier to avoid confusion in complexity statements. Minor typos exist but do not impede understanding. Pseudocode for Algorithm 4 is helpful.

Originality:
- The safe minimax construction and its efficient solution appear novel and distinct from prior adaptive sampling and screening approaches. The guarantees “always better than fixed Li” under limited information and the generality across CD/SGD are new. The idea of translating partial-information bounds into a principled robust optimization for sampling is original.

Significance:
- For CD, the contribution is significant: it delivers a broadly applicable, theoretically justified, and efficient way to approximate the intractable gradient-based sampling with consistent empirical gains. For SGD, impact seems more modest in the tested setups; identifying domains where it materially helps would improve the case. Overall, the method is generic and likely to be reused or extended, including to distributed/asynchronous settings.

Questions and suggestions:
- Can the O(n log n) step be amortized via incremental maintenance (e.g., using heaps or partial resort) to O(log n) per update when bounds evolve smoothly? Any experiments on very large n (e.g., 1e6) to assess scalability?
- How sensitive are results to sparsity and to the choice of bound update (tight inner-product bounds vs. Cauchy–Schwarz)? Can tighter screening-based bounds be plugged in?
- Provide ablations on the cost of computing p̂k vs. achieved reduction in epochs, and end-to-end time across varying d/n and sparsity.
- Compare with dual-residue-based adaptive CD (e.g., AdaSDCA variants) and approximate Gauss–Southwell sampling.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong, well-justified theory with clear practical algorithm.
- Addresses a recognized gap: efficient, provably safe adaptive sampling without full gradients.
- Solid empirical evidence for CD across datasets; generic applicability.

Cons for acceptance:
- Overhead analysis and scalability across regimes could be more thorough.
- Limited empirical advantage for SGD; missing comparisons to some adaptive CD baselines.
- Some proofs and convergence rates are relegated to the appendix.

Overall recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a clear, novel, and practically relevant contribution to adaptive sampling for stochastic optimization, with solid theory and promising experiments, particularly for coordinate descent.
