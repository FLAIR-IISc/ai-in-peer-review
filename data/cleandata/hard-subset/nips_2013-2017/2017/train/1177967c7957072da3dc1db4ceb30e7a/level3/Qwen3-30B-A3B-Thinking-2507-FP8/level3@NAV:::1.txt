

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that uses lower/upper gradient bounds to achieve provably optimal sampling with negligible computational overhead. The key insight is formulating the optimal sampling problem as a convex optimization task with respect to the provided gradient bounds, rather than relying on approximations that may perform worse than uniform sampling.

The work makes a valuable contribution to importance sampling literature by addressing a critical gap in adaptive methods: while gradient-based sampling theoretically offers significant speedups, its computational cost has been prohibitive. The proposed method avoids this by using safe bounds to compute an optimal sampling distribution that is always better than fixed importance sampling. The theoretical analysis is sound (with minor corrections addressed in the rebuttal), and the numerical results demonstrate significant performance gains across multiple datasets and problem types.

Strengths include the elegant convex optimization formulation (Theorem 3.2), efficient O(n log n) solver (Algorithm 4), and strong empirical validation. The paper is well-written and clearly relates to previous work on adaptive importance sampling [2, 5, 22, 23, 26, 36, 37] while demonstrating clear advantages.

Weaknesses identified in the initial review (now addressed in the rebuttal) included a minor factor of 2 missing in Lemma 2.1, a confusing example (3.1), and implementation details in Algorithm 4. The authors have satisfactorily addressed these issues. The numerical results could have included more comparisons to adaptive methods [2,5,21] and timing results for SGD, but the authors have clarified these points in their response.

The significance of this work is high: it provides a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient method that can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations with substantial performance benefits. The approach is generic and applicable to many machine learning problems, particularly those with structured objectives where gradient bounds can be efficiently maintained.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in adaptive importance sampling. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: technically sound, clearly presented, original, and significant. The minor issues identified have been resolved, making this a strong candidate for acceptance.
