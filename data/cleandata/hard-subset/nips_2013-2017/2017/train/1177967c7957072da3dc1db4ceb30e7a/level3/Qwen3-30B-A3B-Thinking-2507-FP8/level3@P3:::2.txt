

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling" (NIPS 2017)

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors propose a "safe" approximation of gradient-based sampling that can be efficiently computed in each iteration while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The key insight is to use safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information to define an optimization problem that yields a sampling distribution provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling. The approach is generic and applicable to a wide range of optimization problems, with the authors demonstrating its effectiveness on generalized linear models.

The paper builds upon previous work on adaptive importance sampling [22, 36, 37] which showed that gradient-based sampling can significantly outperform fixed sampling, but requires computationally expensive full gradient computations. The authors' contribution is to provide a theoretically sound approximation that avoids this computational burden while maintaining the theoretical advantages. This distinguishes their work from prior approaches that used either stale gradient values [22, 1] or upper bounds [36, 37] without theoretical guarantees.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical analysis. The authors establish strong theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2) showing that their proposed sampling is always better than Li-based sampling (which includes uniform sampling as a special case). The numerical experiments are comprehensive and demonstrate significant practical benefits across multiple datasets and problem types. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the reduced benefit observed in SGD compared to CD.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, there are several issues that need addressing. Line 85 contains a typo: the denominator of p*_i should be x_k, not x. Line 135 requires consistency: the subscripts k should be removed for \hat{p} and \hat{c}. The figures suffer from poor readability due to small ticks, labels, and numbers; moving some figures to the appendix would improve readability and allow for better visualization.

**Originality:** The proposed method represents a novel approach to adaptive importance sampling. The authors clearly differentiate their work from previous contributions by demonstrating that their sampling scheme is provably better than fixed importance sampling, which was not established for prior methods that used safe bounds. The optimization formulation (7) for finding the best sampling distribution with respect to bounded gradient information is particularly novel.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important practical problem in large-scale optimization. The proposed method can significantly speed up CD and SGD algorithms with minimal computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration), which is often comparable to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component. The numerical results demonstrate clear advantages over fixed sampling strategies in terms of iteration count and total computational time. The authors also provide a useful theoretical analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5, 3.6) that helps understand the worst-case performance.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. The authors should provide an analogue of Conclusion 2 comparing their new sampling to uniform sampling (p = 1/n), as this would strengthen the practical relevance of their theoretical results.
2. A discussion of the impact on total complexity (including the O(n) cost to compute l_k and u_k) would help practitioners evaluate the overall computational benefit.
3. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the conditions under which the safe gradient bounds can be efficiently maintained beyond generalized linear models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically useful approach to adaptive importance sampling
- The theoretical guarantees are strong and clearly established
- The numerical results demonstrate significant practical benefits
- The method is generic and can be applied to many optimization problems
- The computational overhead is minimal (O(n log n) per iteration)

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Some figures need improvement for better readability
- The comparison to uniform sampling could be more explicitly addressed
- The analysis of total computational complexity could be more comprehensive

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The theoretical insights are sound, the practical benefits are clearly demonstrated, and the method is sufficiently novel to warrant publication at NIPS. The minor issues identified (typos, figure readability, and suggested additions) are easily addressable in the camera-ready version.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the technical points and improve figure presentation. The paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding and practical implementation of adaptive importance sampling in large-scale optimization algorithms.
