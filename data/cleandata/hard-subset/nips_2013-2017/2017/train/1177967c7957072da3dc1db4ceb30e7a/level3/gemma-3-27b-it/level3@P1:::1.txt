This paper introduces a novel and efficient adaptive importance sampling scheme for both coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling, which is often theoretically desirable but computationally expensive, by leveraging safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient. The authors formulate the problem of finding the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem and present an efficient algorithm for its solution. A key strength of the proposed method is its theoretical guarantee of outperforming uniform or fixed importance sampling while maintaining a negligible computational overhead.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, providing a comprehensive overview of related work in importance sampling, particularly within the context of optimization algorithms. The authors effectively connect their work to existing literature, highlighting both the strengths and weaknesses of previous approaches. The mathematical derivations are generally sound, and the proposed algorithm is presented in a clear and concise manner. The authors also convincingly argue the practical applicability of their method, especially in scenarios where computing full gradients is prohibitively expensive. 

The originality of this work lies in the formulation of adaptive sampling as a convex optimization problem given gradient bounds, and the efficiency of the proposed solution. While adaptive importance sampling is not entirely new (references [2], [5], [21] are relevant comparisons missing from the original submission), the authors provide a provably better alternative *given* limited gradient information, and a computationally efficient algorithm to compute it. This represents a significant contribution to the field.

The significance of this work is substantial. Adaptive importance sampling can accelerate optimization algorithms, which are fundamental to many machine learning applications. By providing a practical and efficient method, this paper has the potential to be widely adopted by researchers and practitioners. The ability to operate with only safe gradient bounds makes the method applicable in distributed settings and situations with limited computational resources. The extensive numerical evaluation, while needing some augmentation (discussed below), supports the claims of improved performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical guarantees:** The proposed method is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling given the gradient bounds.
*   **Computational efficiency:** The algorithm can be implemented with negligible overhead, making it practical for large-scale problems.
*   **Generality:** The method is applicable to both CD and SGD algorithms.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Novel formulation:** Framing adaptive sampling as a convex optimization problem is a key insight.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lemma 2.1 detail:** The original submission appears to have an error in the calculation of the optimal stepsize, lacking a factor of 2 in alpha*.
*   **Example 3.1 confusion:** The example discussing optimality with respect to bounds needs clarification. It initially suggests setting the sample to the bounds is optimal, then contradicts this by stating it can be suboptimal.
*   **Algorithm 4 errors:** The conditions in lines 4 and 7 of Algorithm 4 require correction for logical consistency.
*   **Incomplete empirical evaluation:** Comparisons to other adaptive sampling methods ([2], [5], [21]) and non-uniform fixed importance sampling are missing. Also, timing results are missing for the SGD experiments.
*   **Minor reference issues:** The title for [14] is incorrect, and the paper misses the relevant Csiba & Richtarik paper ([1]).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical and practical contribution to the field of importance sampling.
*   The proposed method is well-motivated, clearly explained, and supported by empirical evidence.
*   The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed through revisions.
*   The potential impact of this work on the performance of optimization algorithms is substantial.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The presence of errors in the mathematical derivations and algorithm presentation could raise concerns about the correctness of the method.
*   The incomplete empirical evaluation leaves some questions unanswered about the performance of the proposed method compared to existing alternatives.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor issues identified, this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field. The authors have addressed concerns raised in the rebuttal effectively and provided convincing arguments for the correctness and efficacy of their approach. The benefits of the proposed method – theoretical guarantees, computational efficiency, and broad applicability – outweigh the identified weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:** I recommend accepting this paper, contingent upon the authors addressing the minor errors in the mathematical derivations and algorithm presentation, and incorporating the suggested additions to the empirical evaluation.
