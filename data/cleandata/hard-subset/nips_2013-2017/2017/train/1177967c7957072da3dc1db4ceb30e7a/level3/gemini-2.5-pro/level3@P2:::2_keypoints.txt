- Proposes Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling for CD and SGD to avoid costly full-gradient-based sampling.
- Addresses that naive adaptive sampling can be as expensive as full gradients or worse than uniform sampling.
- Introduces a "safe" approximation using efficiently computable lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes/norms.
- Formulates sampling choice as a minimax problem to minimize worst-case progress over gradients consistent with bounds.
- Provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the safe sampling distribution.
- Proves the resulting distribution is always at least as good as Lipschitz-based importance sampling (Theorem 3.2).
- Shows how to maintain the required gradient bounds for generalized linear models with O(n) overhead.
- Empirical results demonstrate significant speed-ups over static sampling schemes.
- Strength: strong theoretical guarantees—safety proof missing from prior heuristics.
- Strength: novel, principled minimax formulation that robustly handles gradient uncertainty.
- Strength: practical and efficient implementation bridging theory and experiments.
- Strength: general framework applicable to many CD/SGD algorithms and a new directionally-unbiased stepsize for CD.
- Weakness: lacks a formal total-complexity analysis combining iteration count reduction with O(n log n) per-iteration overhead.
- Weakness: figures are hard to read and need improved presentation (larger labels/legends or moved plots).
- Suggestion: provide explicit theoretical comparison to uniform sampling beyond the special equal-L case.
- Suggestion: analyze how adaptive stepsize α̂_k = 1/v_k translates to faster convergence versus per-iteration overhead.
- Minor fixes: change ∇f(x) to ∇f(x_k) in Line 85; use iteration subscripts p̂_k and ĉ_k in Theorem 3.2.
- Overall: well-written, original, significant, and recommended for acceptance due to robust practical and theoretical contributions.
