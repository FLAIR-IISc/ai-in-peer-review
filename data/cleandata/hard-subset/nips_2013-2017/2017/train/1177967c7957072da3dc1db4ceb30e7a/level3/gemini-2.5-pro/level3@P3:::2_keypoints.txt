- Proposes "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling": a principled min-max scheme using safe lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes to approximate optimal gradient-based sampling.  
- Novel formulation (Eq. 7) optimizes worst-case variance under gradient bounds and guarantees performance never worse than Lipschitz-based sampling (Theorem 3.2).  
- Provides an efficient solver (Algorithm 4) for the min-max problem with O(n log n) complexity (Theorem 3.4), making the approach practical for high-dimensional problems.  
- Clearly connects the limited-information method to the ideal full-information setting and provides solid theoretical foundations.  
- Empirical results on real datasets show significant speedups over uniform and fixed importance sampling in iterations and wall-clock time; v_k plots corroborate effectiveness even with loose bounds.  
- Major weakness: missing a complete total-runtime/convergence-rate analysis that combines per-iteration cost and expected progress to reach an ε-optimal solution.  
- Figures suffer from poor readability (small fonts on axes/legends); recommend larger fonts or moving some plots to the appendix.  
- Requests a more explicit comparison to uniform sampling (analog of Theorem 3.2(ii) comparing V(p̂,c) to V(p_unif,c)) beyond the special-case remark.  
- Minor notational inconsistencies and typos (confusing arg min notation in Lemma 2.4, missing l1 subscript in Example 2.3 denominator, dropped subscripts in Theorem 3.2).  
- Overall assessment: novel, technically sound, and empirically convincing work; recommended for acceptance subject to addressing complexity analysis and presentation issues.
