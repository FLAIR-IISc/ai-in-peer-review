- Addresses adaptive importance sampling for CD and SGD, focusing on safe, practically efficient methods.  
- Proposes a novel "safe" adaptive sampling using lower/upper gradient bounds that guarantees improvement over uniform/fixed sampling.  
- Shows theoretically that adaptive sampling can outperform static sampling, with potential speedups up to factor n.  
- Presents an efficiently computable sampling scheme (O(n log n) per iteration) that recovers gradient sampling in full-information settings.  
- Integrates the scheme into CD and SGD for structured problems and maintains safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models.  
- Provides solid theoretical support (Theorems 3.2, 3.4) with careful proofs for performance guarantees.  
- Empirical evaluation on real datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with multiple losses and regularizers demonstrates significant speedups.  
- Computational overhead is analyzed and found often comparable to or less than computing a single gradient coordinate.  
- Algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1–4) are clear, precise, and easy to follow.  
- Originality: casts sampling as a convex optimization over safe bounds, addressing limitations of prior adaptive sampling work.  
- Significance: method is generic, easily integrated into existing algorithms, and advances adaptive importance sampling with theoretical guarantees.  
- Strengths summarized: provably better than uniform/fixed sampling, efficient, generic, strong theory and experiments, clear presentation.  
- Weaknesses summarized: figures are hard to read (small font, low contrast), limited discussion of non-GLM applicability, theory doesn't fully address very loose gradient bounds.  
- Recommendation: high-quality paper with strong contribution and practical value; minor figure fixes recommended and strong accept.
