Summary
This paper proposes Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling (SAIS), a practical approximation to gradient-based sampling for stochastic optimization with coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core idea is to avoid computing full gradients by maintaining safe lower/upper bounds on per-coordinate gradients (CD) or per-component gradient norms (SGD), and to select a sampling distribution that is provably optimal under these bounds. The method arises from a min–max formulation that chooses p to minimize the worst-case ratio V(p, c)/||c||^2 over all c consistent with the bounds. The resulting distribution (and step size α̂ = 1/vk) can be computed in O(n log n) via a sorting-based routine. The authors show: (i) with full information the approach recovers gradient-based sampling; (ii) the safe distribution is always better than uniform or fixed Li-based sampling; and (iii) the solution is the best possible given the available bounds. They integrate SAIS into CD and SGD and evaluate on GLMs (square, squared hinge, logistic losses) with L1/L2 regularization on standard sparse text datasets. Experiments show strong gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD. Figures also track vk/Tr[L] to visualize theoretical quality in practice.

Relation to prior work
This contributes to a timely line of work on adaptive importance sampling for stochastic methods (e.g., gradient-based sampling for SGD, Zhao & Zhang 2015; Needell et al. 2014; gap/violation-based sampling for CD, Csiba et al. 2015; Richtárik & Takáč). Unlike prior approximations using stale or upper bounds that lack uniform guarantees, the paper formulates and solves a principled min–max problem that guarantees improvement over fixed distributions and recovers the optimal full-information solution. It relates to steepest coordinate descent (Gauss–Southwell) and optimal non-uniform sampling for CD, and uses minimax tools (Sion/Komiya) to justify optimality. Compared with screening/gap-safe methods, this approach targets sampling rather than feature elimination and is generic across CD/SGD.

Quality
The technical development is careful and convincing. The one-step progress bound, optimal pk with full gradients, and the min–max formulation over safe bounds are standard yet well-integrated. Theorems establish: best worst-case sampling under bounds, dominance over fixed Li sampling, recovery of full-information sampling, and a competitive-ratio analysis. Algorithm 4 is efficient and clear; the O(n log n) complexity is justified. The empirical study is solid: strong improvements for CD in both iterations and time; smaller but positive effects for SGD; and plots of vk/Tr[L] corroborate theory. A few aspects could be strengthened (see weaknesses).

Clarity
Overall well written and organized. The motivation for safe bounds is clear, and the GLM examples concretize how to maintain bounds in O(n) per iteration. However, figures in Section 5 are difficult to read; labels and lines are too small. Please enlarge plots and axis labels and ensure high-resolution rendering. Some notation is dense (e.g., V(p, c), √L); adding a brief intuition box earlier would help.

Originality
The safe min–max formulation and efficient solver yielding a distribution that is provably optimal under bounds, plus the guarantee of always beating fixed sampling, are novel contributions. The integration into both CD and SGD is generic and useful.

Significance
Importance sampling is widely used; ensuring safety and generic applicability is valuable. The paper’s guarantees and practical performance on large sparse datasets suggest impact for practitioners. Gains are especially significant for CD, a major workhorse for GLMs and L1 problems.

Strengths
- Timely topic: safe adaptive importance sampling with strong theoretical and empirical support.
- Generic method applicable to CD and SGD; minimal assumptions (coordinate-wise smoothness).
- Provable properties: best under bounds, strictly better than fixed Li/uniform, recovers optimal sampling with full gradients.
- Efficient O(n log n) solver with a simple sorting-based algorithm.
- Clear demonstration on GLMs; experiments show substantial CD speedups and modest SGD gains.
- Sensible discussion of complexity and how to maintain safe bounds cheaply.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on safe bounds: for some problems the bounds may be loose, which can reduce gains; the paper would benefit from an ablation quantifying performance vs. bound tightness and from alternative bound-maintenance strategies (beyond Cauchy–Schwarz).
- Overhead: O(n log n) per iteration could be significant when d ≪ n or when updates are extremely cheap; consider amortizing or refreshing p less frequently and report results.
- Limited baselines: comparisons to other adaptive CD rules (e.g., Gauss–Southwell variants, approximate steepest CD, gap-based sampling) and to stale gradient sampling for SGD would strengthen the empirical case.
- SGD benefits are small on studied datasets; discuss when SAIS helps SGD more (e.g., high gradient-norm heterogeneity, non-uniform Lipschitz constants).
- Scope: focus on convex GLMs; discussion of extending to variance-reduction methods, mini-batches, and non-convex settings would be useful.
- Presentation: Figures in Section 5 need larger fonts and thicker lines; label vk/Tr[L] axes clearly.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enlarge all figures and axis labels; provide higher-resolution plots.
- Include an ablation varying bound tightness, refresh frequency of p̂k, and cost breakdown (time per iteration).
- Add baselines: Gauss–Southwell, gap-safe sampling, stale-gradient sampling, and mini-batch importance sampling.
- Report results on strongly convex vs. merely convex settings and on a dense dataset; assess sparsity effects.
- Discuss and possibly experiment with partial/periodic recomputation of p̂k to reduce overhead.
- Clarify extensions to variance-reduced SGD (SVRG/SAGA) and block or mini-batch settings.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong theoretical guarantees with a practical, efficient algorithm.
- Generic, easily pluggable method with clear benefits for CD on important ML tasks.
- Empirical results substantiate theory; method is timely and likely to be adopted.

Arguments against acceptance
- Modest empirical gains for SGD and limited breadth of baselines.
- Potential overhead issues in some regimes; lack of amortization experiments.
- Reliance on safe bounds whose tightness varies; limited analysis of this sensitivity.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper advances the state of the art on adaptive importance sampling with a principled, safe, and efficient method, well supported by theory and experiments. Addressing the presentation and empirical breadth would further strengthen it.
