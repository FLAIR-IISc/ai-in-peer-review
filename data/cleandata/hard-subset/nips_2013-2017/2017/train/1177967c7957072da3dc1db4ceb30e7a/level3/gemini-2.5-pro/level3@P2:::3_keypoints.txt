- Proposes "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling" to bridge cheap fixed importance sampling and costly optimal gradient-based sampling.
- Approximates optimal gradient-based distribution using safe lower and upper bounds on gradient magnitudes.
- Formulates sampling selection as a min-max optimization to be optimal for the worst-case gradient within bounds.
- Provides theoretical guarantees: never worse than fixed importance sampling and best possible under the worst-case criterion.
- Offers an efficient algorithm to compute the distribution in O(n log n) time.
- Shows how to maintain required gradient bounds for GLMs with only O(n) overhead per iteration.
- Validates effectiveness with extensive experiments on real-world datasets, showing iteration and wall-clock improvements.
- High technical quality with detailed proofs supporting theoretical claims.
- Well-written and clearly organized, progressing logically from full-information to limited-information settings.
- Major presentation issue: figures in Section 5 are too small and illegible; should be enlarged for the final version.
- Original contribution: novel min-max safety formulation distinguishing it from heuristic upper-bound methods.
- Significant impact: addresses practical adaptive sampling challenges and is likely useful for large-scale optimization.
- Method is safe, practical (manageable overhead), and generic (applicable to CD and SGD).
- Strong arguments for acceptance: novel framework, provable guarantees, efficient implementation, and strong empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear figures and limited derivation of bounds beyond GLMs; needs discussion of applicability to other model classes.
- Overall recommendation: accept — strengths substantially outweigh minor, correctable weaknesses.
