### Summary

This paper introduces a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The key challenge with optimal adaptive sampling schemes, such as those based on full gradient information, is that they are computationally prohibitive. The authors propose an efficient approximation that relies on maintaining safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes (for CD) or gradient norms (for SGD).

The main contribution is a novel and principled approach to derive a sampling distribution from these bounds. Instead of using a heuristic, the authors formulate the problem as a minimax optimization problem: find the sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case variance of the update, considering all possible true gradient vectors that lie within the given bounds. This "safe" sampling distribution is shown to be (i) provably the best possible given the limited information, (ii) guaranteed to be better than uniform or standard fixed importance sampling, and (iii) efficiently computable in O(n log n) time. The authors demonstrate how to maintain the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provide extensive experiments showing significant speedups over fixed sampling strategies.

### Strengths

*   **Originality & Significance:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization problem (Equation 7) is a highly novel and elegant contribution. It provides a principled way to handle the uncertainty inherent in using gradient bounds, a clear advantage over prior work that often relies on heuristics (e.g., using the upper bounds directly). The guarantee that the resulting sampling is always superior to fixed importance sampling is a strong and significant result, addressing a key weakness of many adaptive schemes which can sometimes underperform.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis is solid, and the claims are well-supported. The derivation of the optimal safe sampling distribution and the efficient algorithm to compute it (Algorithm 4) are valuable contributions. The paper presents a complete piece of work, connecting theory, algorithm design, and empirical validation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out, starting from the ideal (but impractical) full-information sampler and logically progressing to the proposed safe, limited-information approach. The exposition is clear and easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

While the paper is strong overall, there are several points that require clarification or improvement.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The empirical evaluation is a bit limited. While the comparison to uniform sampling and the "optimal" (full-information) sampling is insightful, the paper lacks a comparison against other state-of-the-art adaptive importance sampling methods. For instance, comparing against the duality-gap-based methods in [5, 23] or other gradient-based approximations would provide a much clearer picture of where this method stands in practice. The current experiments demonstrate the validity of the approach but not necessarily its competitiveness against other adaptive schemes.
2.  **Algorithm 4 Correctness:** There appears to be a logical flaw in Algorithm 4. In line 3, `m` is initialized as `m = max(l_sort)`. However, the first condition inside the loop (line 4) is `if [l_sort]_l > m`. Since `[l_sort]_l` is the largest remaining element of `l_sort`, this condition seems impossible to satisfy on the first iteration, and it is unclear how the logic proceeds correctly. This is a critical point that needs to be clarified or corrected, as the O(n log n) solver is a central part of the contribution.

**Minor Issues and Questions:**

1.  **Explanation of Example 3.1:** The motivation provided by Example 3.1 is slightly confusing. The text argues that using distributions based on the bounds `l` or `u` can be poor, yet the solution to the minimax problem (7) itself involves finding a worst-case gradient vector `c_hat` which, according to Theorem 3.4, will have components at the bounds or determined by them. The key distinction—that the proposed distribution `p_hat` is robust for *any* `c` in the uncertainty set, unlike distributions formed naively from `l` or `u`—could be explained more clearly to avoid this confusion.
2.  **SGD Timing Results:** The experiments for SGD in Figures 7-8 are missing timing results (unlike the CD experiments in Figure 6). These are crucial for assessing the practical benefit of the method, as they would demonstrate whether the speed-up in convergence (per epoch) outweighs the computational overhead of the `O(n log n)` sampling step.
3.  **Algorithm 4 Typo:** There seems to be a minor typo in line 7 of Algorithm 4. The expression `[c_sort]_u` should likely be `[usort]_u` to maintain symmetry with the logic in line 5.
4.  **Missing References/Citations:** The paper would benefit from including a reference to Csiba & Richtarik, "Importance Sampling for Minibatches" (2016, arXiv), which is relevant to this area. Additionally, the title for reference [14] appears to be incorrect in the bibliography.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound, and novel contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The minimax formulation for handling gradient uncertainty is a significant and valuable idea. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance. However, my recommendation is contingent on the authors addressing the major concerns in their rebuttal, particularly by clarifying the logic of Algorithm 4 and justifying the lack of experimental comparisons to other adaptive sampling methods. Addressing the minor points would further improve the quality of the manuscript.
