This paper proposes a novel safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for first-order optimization methods like coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The work addresses a well-known challenge: while adaptive, gradient-based sampling can theoretically offer significant speed-ups, its practical application is often computationally prohibitive, and common approximations can perform worse than simple uniform sampling. This work builds upon an established line of research in non-uniform sampling (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, 2015; Csiba et al., 2015) but introduces a key innovation. The authors formulate the problem of choosing a sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem, using efficiently computable safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient information. This "safe" approach guarantees that the resulting sampling distribution is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a timely and highly relevant problem in large-scale optimization. The core idea of formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a minimax problem to ensure a "worst-case" performance guarantee against gradient uncertainty is both novel and elegant. This provides a principled way to bridge the gap between expensive optimal sampling and heuristic approximations.
*   **Quality:** The submission is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis—proving that the proposed sampling is optimal with respect to the given bounds and superior to fixed sampling—and comprehensive experiments on several real-world datasets. The proposed method is generic, and the paper clearly demonstrates how it can be integrated with existing CD and SGD frameworks for a broad class of problems, such as generalized linear models.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly motivates the problem, details the proposed method, and presents its theoretical properties before moving to empirical validation.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity:** The primary weakness is the poor readability of the figures in Section 5. The plots, axis labels, and legends are too small to be interpreted without significant zooming, which detracts from the otherwise strong empirical section. This is a minor but important issue that should be addressed.
*   **Quality:** While the method for maintaining the gradient bounds is demonstrated effectively for GLMs, the broader applicability to more complex models (e.g., deep networks) is only touched upon. A brief discussion on the challenges and potential strategies for maintaining such bounds in other settings would strengthen the paper.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that presents a practical, theoretically-grounded, and effective method for adaptive importance sampling. The weaknesses are minor and easily correctable. I recommend acceptance.
