## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for accelerating coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, ensuring theoretical guarantees while maintaining computational efficiency. The authors demonstrate that their proposed sampling strategy is provably better than uniform sampling and fixed importance sampling, and can be integrated into existing algorithms with minimal overhead.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a key challenge in large-scale optimization: the computational cost of adaptive importance sampling methods. The theoretical analysis is strong, with clear proofs for the proposed sampling scheme's advantages. The connection to existing work on gradient-based sampling and duality gap-based sampling is well-established, and the paper effectively positions its contribution within the broader landscape of optimization techniques. The inclusion of extensive numerical testing further strengthens the claims, demonstrating the practical benefits of the proposed approach on real-world datasets.

A significant strength lies in the derivation of a sampling strategy that is *provably* better than uniform sampling, even with limited gradient information. The authors cleverly formulate the problem as a convex optimization, providing a practical algorithm for computing the sampling distribution. The discussion of computational complexity and the demonstration of efficient implementation are also commendable. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more direct comparison to uniform sampling. While the theoretical advantages are established, a dedicated experiment comparing the new sampling to uniform sampling (p = 1/n) would provide a clearer picture of the practical gains. Additionally, the discussion of the total complexity of RCD/SGD, including the cost of updating the `l_k` and `u_k` bounds to reach an ε-optimal solution, would be valuable for a complete assessment of the method's efficiency. 

The figures currently suffer from readability issues. Tick labels and numbers are too small, and some figures could be moved to the appendix to allow for larger, more detailed visualizations. There are also minor technical issues: a typo on line 85 (denominator of p^*_i should be x_k, not x) and a consistency issue on line 135 (remove subscript k for \hat p and \hat c).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling.
*   Provable guarantees of improvement over existing methods.
*   Practical implementation with low computational overhead.
*   Strong empirical validation on real-world datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of a direct comparison to uniform sampling in experiments.
*   Insufficient discussion of the total complexity of the algorithm.
*   Minor readability issues with figures and technical errors in the text.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. Addressing the minor weaknesses would further enhance its contribution and make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.
