

# Review for "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, addressing the computational infeasibility of optimal gradient-based sampling while providing theoretical guarantees. The authors propose using safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information to compute an adaptive sampling distribution that is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling. The key theoretical contribution is Theorem 3.2, which establishes that the proposed sampling distribution always outperforms Li-based importance sampling (which reduces to uniform sampling when all Li are equal), while being efficiently computable in O(n log n) time per iteration.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work on adaptive sampling, particularly the gradient-based sampling approaches by Zhu (NIPS 2013) and Zhao and Zhang (ICML 2015), but makes a clear distinction by providing provable guarantees that their method never performs worse than fixed sampling. The work also connects to recent advances in non-uniform sampling for coordinate descent (e.g., Allen-Zhu et al., ICML 2017; Qu et al., Randomized Dual Coordinate Ascent, ICML 2015), though the authors' key innovation is the safe bound approach with worst-case guarantees.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2) that the proposed sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling, which is a significant improvement over previous bound-based approaches that could sometimes be worse than uniform sampling. The competitive ratio analysis (Lemma 3.5, 3.6) further strengthens the theoretical foundation.

2. **Practical Implementation:** The authors demonstrate that the proposed method can be efficiently integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations with minimal computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration, often negligible compared to gradient evaluation costs). The experimental results on real datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) confirm the practical benefits.

3. **Generality:** The approach is generic and applicable to a wide range of machine learning problems, with the authors demonstrating its application to generalized linear models with various loss functions and regularizers.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Clarity Issues:** Some notation inconsistencies exist (e.g., Line 85 typo - denominator of p^*_i should be ‖√L∇f(xk)‖ instead of x_k). The figures (Figures 2-9) suffer from poor readability (small ticks, labels, numbers), which would benefit from being moved to the appendix or improved in the main text.

2. **Missing Comparison:** The paper does not explicitly compare the proposed sampling to uniform sampling in a way analogous to Conclusion 2 (which compares to Li-based sampling). This would strengthen the argument for the method's superiority.

3. **Complexity Analysis:** The paper does not fully address the impact on total complexity of RCD/SGD, particularly the cost of maintaining the gradient bounds (`k` and `u_k`). While the paper states the overhead is O(n log n) per iteration, it would be valuable to discuss how this affects the total cost to reach ε-optimality.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The method provides provable guarantees that previous adaptive sampling approaches lacked.
- The computational overhead is minimal and well-justified.
- The approach is general and applicable to a wide range of optimization problems in machine learning.
- The experimental results support the theoretical claims across multiple datasets and problem types.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The paper could better emphasize the comparison to uniform sampling (which is the most common baseline).
- The clarity issues in figures and notation slightly diminish the impact of the work.
- The complexity analysis of the total optimization process could be more complete.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a practical challenge in optimization with significant potential impact on machine learning applications. The paper makes a clear distinction from prior work by providing provable guarantees that the proposed method never performs worse than fixed importance sampling, while being efficiently computable. The experimental results validate the theoretical claims, demonstrating that the method outperforms fixed importance sampling in both iteration count and total computation time.

The minor clarity issues (notably in figure presentation) are easily fixable in the camera-ready version. The paper would be strengthened by adding a direct comparison to uniform sampling (as suggested in the key points) and providing a more complete analysis of the total computational complexity. However, these are relatively minor concerns for a paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution with strong practical implications.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria:
- **Quality:** Technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims.
- **Clarity:** Generally clear, with minor presentation issues.
- **Originality:** Novel approach to adaptive sampling with provable guarantees.
- **Significance:** Potentially impactful for speeding up optimization in large-scale machine learning.

I recommend acceptance for NIPS 2017. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, providing both theoretical insights and practical implementation that can benefit a wide range of applications.
