### Paper Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key challenge with adaptive methods, such as those based on full gradient information, is that they are often computationally too expensive to be practical, as they can negate the speed-up gained from using a stochastic method in the first place. Prior approximations often relied on upper bounds, which lack guarantees and can perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The main contribution of this work is to address this gap by formulating the problem of finding an optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem, given only "safe" lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem. The resulting sampling distribution is provably optimal with respect to the given bounds and is guaranteed to perform better than uniform or any fixed importance sampling scheme. The authors demonstrate how these bounds can be maintained efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs) and provide empirical evidence showing significant speed-ups over static sampling methods on several real-world datasets.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The core idea of formulating the optimal sampling problem as a minimax optimization problem (7) over the set of possible gradients defined by the bounds is highly novel. The derivation of an efficient O(n log n) solver (Algorithm 4) for this non-trivial problem is a significant technical contribution. The work is technically sound and provides a complete, well-motivated solution to a practical problem.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** A key advantage of the proposed method is its "safe" nature. The paper proves that the resulting sampling is always better than standard Li-based fixed importance sampling (and thus uniform sampling in the L-smooth case). This is a crucial guarantee that was missing from previous approximation schemes and makes the method robust and reliable.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and effectively motivates the problem. The authors build their argument logically, starting from the ideal but impractical optimal sampling to their efficient and provably safe approximation. The contributions are clearly stated and well-supported.

4.  **Practical Significance:** The proposed method has low computational overhead—O(n log n) per iteration—which is often negligible compared to the cost of a single gradient computation, especially when the feature dimension is high. This makes the method practical for real-world applications and a valuable contribution for practitioners seeking to accelerate CD and SGD.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While this is a strong paper, there are several points that could be clarified or improved:

1.  **Theoretical Details:** There are a few minor points in the theory that could benefit from clarification:
    *   In the proof of Lemma 2.1, the expression for the optimal α* appears to be missing a factor of 2 in the denominator. Please double-check this derivation.
    *   Example 3.1 is meant to motivate the sophisticated optimization in (7) by showing that naive choices (e.g., using `l` or `u` to construct the sampling) can be suboptimal. However, the explanation is a bit confusing, as the worst-case nature of the optimization in (7) seems to inherently favor solutions `c` that lie on the boundaries of the feasible set. A clearer explanation of why simply sampling based on `l` or `u` is insufficient would strengthen the motivation.
    *   The logic in Algorithm 4, line 4, is questionable. If `m` is initialized as `max(l^{sort})`, the condition `['l^{sort}']_l > m` may never be satisfied for the largest elements. Please clarify the initialization and loop logic to ensure correctness.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over uniform sampling and the computationally expensive "optimal" sampling. However, the evaluation could be more comprehensive:
    *   The paper does not compare against other adaptive sampling methods (e.g., [2], [5], [21]). Including such baselines is essential to properly position the work and understand its performance relative to the state of the art in adaptive sampling.
    *   Timing results are provided for the CD experiments but are notably absent for the SGD experiments. Since the practical utility hinges on low overhead, providing these timings is critical to fully validate the claims for SGD.
    *   While the comparison to fixed `L_i`-based sampling is included, expanding the comparison to other non-uniform fixed sampling heuristics could provide additional context.

3.  **Referencing:** The paper provides a good overview of related work, but there are minor issues. The title for reference [14] appears to be incorrect. More importantly, the highly relevant work of Csiba & Richtarik, "Importance Sampling for Minibatches" (2016, arXiv), is missing and should be cited and discussed.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a valuable and original contribution to the literature on importance sampling for large-scale optimization. The novel formulation of safe adaptive sampling as a tractable convex problem is a significant advance. The theoretical guarantees and strong empirical performance (despite the limited baselines) are compelling.

The identified weaknesses are largely addressable through revisions. I recommend **acceptance** for this paper. I strongly encourage the authors to address the points raised above in the final version, particularly by expanding the experimental comparisons to include other adaptive methods and adding timing results for the SGD experiments. These additions would substantially strengthen an already impressive paper.
