Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for randomized optimization methods, with a focus on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate ideal gradient-based adaptive sampling—known to maximize expected one-step improvement under a quadratic model—without computing full gradients. Instead, the method assumes per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (for CD) or component gradient norms (for SGD), and formulates the best sampling as a convex min–max problem over the probability simplex and a box of admissible gradients. The resulting sampling p̂ is provably optimal in the worst case with respect to these bounds, uniformly improves upon uniform or fixed Li-based sampling, and recovers the exact gradient-based sampling when bounds collapse.

Technically, the paper:
- Derives the best adaptive sampling for CD under full information, showing potentially up to n-fold speedups over uniform sampling and connections to steepest coordinate descent.
- Introduces the safe bounded-information min–max formulation min_p max_c V(p,c)/||c||^2 with V(p,c)=∑ Li c_i^2/p_i, and provides an efficient O(n log n) solver (Algorithm 4) via a thresholding structure c_i = clamp(√Li m, ℓ_i, u_i).
- Shows how to maintain safe bounds cheaply for GLMs, yielding per-iteration overhead O(n log n), often negligible relative to gradient computations.
- Presents experiments on GLMs (L1/L2, several losses) demonstrating that for CD, the proposed sampling nearly matches ideal gradient-based sampling and outperforms uniform/fixed importance sampling in iterations and wall-clock; for SGD, gains are smaller.

This work builds on and improves adaptive sampling literature including gradient-based sampling for SGD [Zhao & Zhang; Zhu], duality-gap and adaptive-probability CD [Csiba–Qu–Richtárik; Perekrestenko–Cevher–Jaggi], and non-uniform fixed probabilities [Richtárik–Takáč]. Its “safe” worst-case optimality relative to bounds and a practical O(n log n) solver appear novel.

Quality:
- Theory: The min–max formulation and guarantees that p̂ is (i) best for the given bounds and (ii) never worse than Li-based (hence uniform when Li=L) are clear and useful. The geometric characterization and thresholding solution (Theorem 3.4) are elegant.
- However, Lemma 2.1 seems incorrect as stated. From (3), the expected upper bound is f(xk) − α A + (α^2/2) B with A=||∇f||^2 and B=V(p,∇f). The minimizer is α* = A/B with minimum −A^2/(2B). Thus the decrease is (α*/2)·A = A^2/(2B), not α*^2·A as written; equivalently (4) should read E[f(xk+1)|xk] ≤ f(xk) − (αk(pk)/2) ||∇f||^2. Please correct the factor-of-2 (and the functional form) consistently throughout, including stepsize discussions.
- Minimax interchange: The use of Sion’s minimax theorem to justify (∗) in (8) is appropriate given convexity in p, concavity in c over a compact box Ck; making the exact conditions explicit in the main text would aid clarity.
- Example 3.1: The text first warns that naively setting c to ℓ or u can be poor, then introduces the min–max (7), where the worst-case c indeed lies on the bounds but in a structured way given by (9). Clarify this apparent tension: the “naive” choices ℓ or u used to build p are suboptimal, whereas the inner maximizer ĉ for (7) is a mixed clamped vector determined by m, not the raw ℓ or u.
- Algorithm 4: There are issues to fix for correctness/readability.
  - Line 4: With m initialized to max(ℓsort), the condition [ℓsort]ℓ > m can never hold. Either initialize m by solving the fixed-point threshold condition implied by (9), or compute m from the current tentative c before the first check (as in line 11). Please revise initialization and the logic of the first iteration.
  - Line 7: “Set corresponding [c]index := [√L usort]u” seems intended, but the text mentions csort—likely a typo. Please fix to use usort.
  - Consider adding a short proof sketch of correctness/termination in the main text, and clarify how indices are mapped back after sorting.
- Complexity and practicality: The O(n log n) overhead is reasonable when d = Ω(n) or when gradient computations dominate, but can be non-negligible for very sparse, low-dimensional features. A brief discussion of regimes where the overhead may matter (and potential amortization or periodic updates) would strengthen the practicality claims.

Clarity:
- Overall very well written. Minor issues: tighten the exposition around (6)–(9); define vk and its role in stepsizes earlier; ensure consistent notation (bold vs. scalar).
- The description of bound maintenance in Section 4 is helpful; consider adding a short pseudocode for updating ℓ,u and noting any numerical safeguards.

Originality:
- The “safe” bound-optimal sampling formulation and efficient algorithm, together with guarantees against uniform/fixed sampling and recovery of full-information optimal sampling, are novel and nontrivial. The work clearly differentiates from prior approximate/adaptive approaches that lack worst-case guarantees or require stale/full gradients.

Significance:
- For CD, the gains are substantial and robust, making this a practically impactful sampling policy that can be plugged into many solvers. For SGD, improvements are smaller in the reported settings; additional evidence on problems where gradient-norm heterogeneity is larger would be informative.

Empirical evaluation:
- Strong CD results, including wall-clock timings, and informative plots of vk/Tr[L].
- For fairness and completeness, please:
  - Compare against other adaptive baselines: gap-based CD [Csiba–Qu–Richtárik 2015; Perekrestenko–Cevher–Jaggi], gradient-based with stale values [Zhao & Zhang], and non-uniform fixed importance sampling (Li-based).
  - Report SGD wall-clock timings (currently missing) to support “negligible extra cost” in the SGD setting.
  - Discuss why SGD gains are modest and when the method helps more (e.g., high variance in ||∇fi||, heavy-tailed data).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, principled, and practically efficient adaptive sampling with strong guarantees.
- Clear theory with constructive algorithm and strong empirical gains for CD.
- Generic and easy to integrate; likely to influence practice.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Minor theoretical flaw (factor-of-2) needs correction.
- Algorithm 4 pseudocode has logical/typo issues and should be clarified.
- Empirical baselines and SGD timings could be more comprehensive.

Recommendation: Accept (after addressing the small theory and algorithm clarity issues). The contribution is original, technically sound after minor fixes, clearly written, and significant for randomized optimization.
