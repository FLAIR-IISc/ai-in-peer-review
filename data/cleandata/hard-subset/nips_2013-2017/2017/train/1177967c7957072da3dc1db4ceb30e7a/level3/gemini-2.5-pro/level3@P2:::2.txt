This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, named Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling, for randomized coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The key challenge in adaptive sampling is that computing the optimal, gradient-based sampling distribution is often as expensive as a full gradient step, negating the benefits of stochastic methods. While prior work has used approximations based on stale information or bounds, these approximations can sometimes perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The main contribution of this paper is to address this issue by proposing a "safe" approximation. The authors assume access to efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes for CD (or gradient norms for SGD). Given these bounds, they formulate the problem of choosing the best sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem: find the distribution that minimizes the worst-case objective progress over all possible true gradients consistent with the bounds. The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem. Crucially, the resulting sampling distribution is proven to be always better than standard fixed importance sampling (based on Lipschitz constants) and is, by construction, the optimal choice given the limited information. The authors demonstrate how to maintain the required bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs) and provide extensive experiments showing significant practical speed-ups over static sampling schemes.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary strength of this work is its theoretical foundation. Theorem 3.2 provides a "safety" guarantee that has been missing from many previous heuristic adaptive sampling methods. The proof that the proposed sampling `p̂` is always better than Li-based importance sampling (`V(p̂, c) ≤ Tr[L] ||c||^2`) is a significant result. This ensures that the method offers a robust improvement over standard practice.

2.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization problem (7) is elegant and well-motivated. Instead of picking an arbitrary point within the bounds (e.g., the upper bound), this approach robustly handles the uncertainty about the true gradient, leading to a sampling distribution with the best worst-case guarantee. This is a novel contribution to the literature on adaptive sampling.

3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The paper successfully bridges theory and practice. The authors provide an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the safe sampling distribution (Algorithm 4). Furthermore, they demonstrate in Section 4 how the necessary gradient bounds can be maintained with only O(n) overhead per iteration for the important class of GLMs. This makes the overall method practical and appealing for real-world applications, as corroborated by the timing experiments.

4.  **Generality:** The proposed framework is generic and can be readily integrated into a wide variety of existing CD and SGD algorithms. The paper also introduces a novel directionally-unbiased stepsize for CD, which is of independent interest.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Total Complexity Analysis:** The paper analyzes the per-iteration cost but does not provide a formal analysis of the *total complexity* to reach an ε-optimal solution. The improved sampling leads to a better expected progress (related to `1/v_k`), which should reduce the total number of iterations. However, there is an O(n log n) overhead per iteration. A theoretical analysis that combines these two aspects to show an overall reduction in computational complexity would significantly strengthen the paper's claims. While the empirical timing results are convincing, a supporting theoretical argument is currently missing.

2.  **Presentation Quality of Figures:** The figures are difficult to read and below the typical standard for a NIPS publication. The axis labels, tick marks, and legend text are all too small. This significantly hinders the interpretation of the empirical results. I strongly recommend that the authors remake all figures to improve their legibility, potentially by moving some plots to the appendix to allow the main figures to be larger.

### Questions and Suggestions for Improvement

*   Could the authors provide an explicit theoretical comparison to uniform sampling? Theorem 3.2(ii) gives a comparison to Li-based sampling. While Remark 3.3 covers the special case `L_i = L`, a more general corollary or discussion comparing `V(p̂, c)` with `V(p_unif, c)` would be valuable.

*   Please comment on the total complexity as mentioned in the weaknesses. For example, how does the improvement from the adaptive stepsize `α̂_k = 1/v_k` translate into a faster convergence rate, and how does this rate improvement balance the `O(n log n)` per-iteration overhead?

*   Line 85: In the definition of `α_k(p*_k)` in Example 2.3, the gradient `∇f(x)` should likely be `∇f(x_k)` for consistency.

*   Line 135: In Theorem 3.2, the variables `p̂` and `ĉ` are used without the iteration subscript `k`, which appears in equation (7). Using `p̂_k` and `ĉ_k` would be more consistent.

### Overall Evaluation

This is a well-written paper that makes a strong contribution to the important area of large-scale optimization. The proposed "safe" adaptive sampling method is novel, theoretically well-founded, and practically effective. The guarantee that the method will not perform worse than standard fixed importance sampling is a significant step forward for adaptive methods. The weaknesses are primarily in the presentation (figures) and a lack of a complete theoretical complexity analysis, but they do not undermine the core contribution. The work is interesting, original, and significant, and I believe it will be of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
