This paper introduces a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for accelerating coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, ensuring a provable improvement over uniform or fixed importance sampling while maintaining computational efficiency. The authors provide theoretical analysis demonstrating the benefits of their approach, particularly for CD, and support these claims with extensive numerical experiments.

The paper addresses a timely and important problem: accelerating optimization algorithms for large-scale datasets. Adaptive importance sampling is a popular technique, but existing methods often suffer from computational infeasibility or lack strong theoretical guarantees. This work directly tackles these limitations by proposing a practical and theoretically sound alternative. The proposed method is indeed generic and can be easily integrated into existing algorithms, a significant strength. The combination of theoretical results and practical validation enhances the credibility of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating the advantages of the proposed sampling scheme over static alternatives, particularly in the context of CD. The bounds on expected progress are well-defined and justify the approach.
*   **Practicality:** The method is designed to be computationally efficient, with overhead comparable to evaluating a single gradient component. This makes it suitable for large-scale problems where computational cost is a major concern.
*   **Generality:** The proposed sampling scheme is generic and can be applied to a wide range of algorithms (CD, SGD) and objective functions.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive numerical experiments on multiple datasets and with different loss functions and regularization techniques demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in practice.
*   **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper clearly relates its contributions to existing literature on importance sampling, coordinate descent, and stochastic gradient methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Figure Readability:** As noted in the paper itself, the figures in Section 5 (Figures 2-9) are difficult to interpret due to small font sizes and cramped labels. This hinders the ability to quickly grasp the experimental results. Enlarging these figures and improving label clarity is crucial.
*   **SGD Performance:** While the theoretical analysis extends to SGD, the experimental results show only marginal improvements over uniform sampling in the SGD experiments. The reasons for this discrepancy could be further investigated and discussed. A deeper analysis of why the theoretical gains are not fully realized in the SGD setting would be beneficial.
*   **Assumption of Safe Bounds:** The reliance on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradient requires careful consideration. The paper provides examples for GLMs, but the applicability of this approach to other model classes might be limited or require more sophisticated bound estimation techniques. A discussion of potential challenges and alternatives for obtaining these bounds would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons are made to uniform sampling and the optimal sampling (when feasible), a comparison to other existing adaptive importance sampling schemes (e.g., those based on duality gaps) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method's performance.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in large-scale optimization with a novel and theoretically sound solution.
*   The proposed method is practical and demonstrably effective in experiments, particularly for coordinate descent.
*   The generality of the approach allows it to be applied to a wide range of problems.
*   The theoretical analysis provides strong justification for the observed improvements.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The readability of the figures is poor and needs to be addressed.
*   The limited improvement in SGD performance raises questions about the practical benefits of the approach for all problem settings.
*   A more comprehensive comparison to existing adaptive importance sampling schemes would be valuable.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme is both theoretically sound and practically effective, particularly for coordinate descent. While the figure readability and the limited SGD performance are areas for improvement, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the figure readability issue and providing further discussion on the SGD results. Specifically, the authors should enlarge figures and labels for readability throughout Section 5.
