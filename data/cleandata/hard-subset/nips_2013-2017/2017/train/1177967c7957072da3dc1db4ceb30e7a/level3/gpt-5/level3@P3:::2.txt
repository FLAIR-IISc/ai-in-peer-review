Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for randomized coordinate descent (RCD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to maintain per-coordinate safe lower/upper bounds (`k, uk) on the (component) gradients and to compute, at each iteration, a sampling distribution p̂k by solving a convex min–max problem that minimizes a tight upper bound on the expected progress under all gradients consistent with (`k, uk). The method yields: (i) a distribution that is optimal given the partial information (best worst-case guarantee), (ii) a guarantee that it is always at least as good as fixed L-based importance sampling, and (iii) recovery of the full-information gradient-based sampling when bounds collapse to the exact gradient. An efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Alg. 4) is provided to compute p̂k. The paper also discusses how to maintain safe bounds with O(n) updates per iteration for GLMs, and reports empirical results showing substantial gains for CD and modest gains for SGD.

The work sits at the intersection of adaptive sampling for SGD (e.g., Zhao & Zhang 2015; Needell et al. 2014; Zhu 2016) and adaptive/importance sampling for CD (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Csiba et al.; Perekrestenko et al.), but adds a principled min–max formulation that turns partial gradient information into a provably safe and efficiently computable sampling policy.

Quality
- Theory: Strong. Theorem 3.2 (Conclusion 2) cleanly shows that p̂ is always better than L-based sampling: V(p̂, c) ≤ Tr[L]‖c‖2 for all c ∈ Ck, and vk ∈ [Lmin, Tr[L]]. The geometric characterization and the O(n log n) algorithm (Theorem 3.4) are elegant and practical. Competitive ratio bounds (Lemmas 3.5–3.6) provide additional insight.
- Algorithms: Clear specification for CD/SGD, including stepsize α̂k = 1/vk matched to the sampling.
- Experiments: CD results corroborate theory (large gains over fixed and close to full-gradient optimal sampling). SGD gains are smaller but consistent with prior observations.
- Weakness: The total complexity discussion is brief; see request below for an explicit end-to-end ε-complexity accounting.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The optimization viewpoint and safe-bounds construction for GLMs are clearly explained.
- Presentation issues:
  - Figures: many tick labels, axis labels, and numbers are too small or missing. Please enlarge and/or move some plots to the appendix to improve readability while keeping the main message in the paper.
  - Notation consistency and minor typos (see Specific comments).

Originality
- The safe min–max formulation over partial gradient information, with a best-possible (w.r.t. bounds) sampling and an efficient solver, is novel to my knowledge. Prior “approximate gradient” sampling heuristics lacked such worst-case guarantees. The result that the scheme provably dominates L-based importance sampling under limited information is new and useful.

Significance
- High for RCD: The guarantees and empirical speedups suggest this can become a default drop-in adaptive sampler when full gradients are infeasible.
- Moderate for SGD: Improvements are more modest; nevertheless, the safe-bounds view and min–max formulation are conceptually valuable and may spur further developments (e.g., tighter bounds, minibatching).

Specific comments, suggestions, and minor issues
- Overall positive: The paper is interesting and offers useful insights for both RCD and SGD. Theorem 3.2 (Conclusion 2) is especially compelling: it suggests the new sampling strictly improves upon L-based sampling.
- Line 85: Typo. In the expression for p∗i (Example 2.3), the denominator should involve xk, i.e., [p∗k]i = √Li|∇if(xk)|/‖√L∇f(xk)‖1, not x.
- Line 135: Consistency. In Theorem 3.2 and related text, remove the superfluous subscript k when referring to the generic optimizer (p̂, ĉ); reserve k for iteration-dependent quantities.
- Figures: Axis ticks, labels, and legend text are too small in several plots; some curves lack units/labels. Consider moving a subset of figures to the appendix to enlarge the remaining ones and improve readability.
- Analogue to uniform sampling: While Remark 3.3 covers the L = LIn case (uniform ≡ L-based), it would help to provide an explicit analogue of Conclusion 2 against uniform sampling p = 1/n for general L. For instance, since uniform is feasible in (7), one can add a corollary such as V(p̂, c) ≤ min{Tr[L]‖c‖2, n‖√Lc‖22} ≤ nLmax‖c‖2 for all c ∈ Ck, making the domination over uniform explicit. Empirical comparisons to uniform for nonuniform L would also be welcome.
- Total complexity to ε: Please add an end-to-end complexity discussion incorporating the O(n) maintenance of (`k, uk) and O(n log n) sampling computation.
  - For strongly convex f with parameter μ, standard analyses (cf. Appendix A.1) imply iteration complexity O((E[vk]/μ) log(1/ε)) with stepsize α̂k = 1/vk. Combine this with per-iteration cost Titer = Θ(d) (gradient component) + O(n) (bound updates) + O(n log n) (sampling) to get total time O(((E[vk]/μ) log(1/ε)) · (d + n + n log n)). Discuss regimes: when d = Ω(n), the overhead is effectively O(log n) relative to the gradient cost; when d ≪ n, the overhead can dominate unless bounds/sampling are updated less frequently (e.g., every few iterations, or in blocks).
  - For general convex f, similarly state O(E[vk]R2/ε) iteration complexity and the corresponding total time.
  - Memory: storing (`k, uk) is O(n); note this explicitly.
  - Practical refinements (optional): amortize or lazy-update (`k, uk), use sketches or block updates to reduce O(n) per step, and/or maintain p̂k less frequently, with theoretical guardrails via Lemma 3.6 (γ-accuracy).
- Empirics: Report time on a single machine or normalize to make timing across figures directly comparable. Consider including ablations on the frequency of p̂k/bounds updates to illuminate time–accuracy trade-offs.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled min–max formulation with clean guarantees; efficient algorithm.
- Strong theory including best-worst-case guarantee and dominance over L-based sampling.
- Solid empirical gains for CD; method is generic and easy to integrate.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited SGD gains in current experiments.
- Overhead can be substantial when d ≪ n; total ε-complexity not fully quantified in the paper.
- Presentation issues (figures) and a few notation inconsistencies.

Overall recommendation
I lean positive. With the minor fixes and a more explicit end-to-end complexity discussion, this will be a strong and useful contribution to adaptive sampling for RCD/SGD.
