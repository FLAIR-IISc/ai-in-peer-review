

# Review for "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that uses lower and upper bounds on gradient information. The authors formulate the problem of finding the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem with respect to these bounds, providing a provably better alternative to uniform or fixed importance sampling with negligible computational overhead. This work builds on previous adaptive importance sampling literature (e.g., [22, 23, 26, 36, 37]) but addresses the key computational infeasibility of gradient-based sampling by using a novel convex optimization formulation.

**Quality:** The paper provides solid theoretical guarantees that the proposed sampling is always better than uniform or fixed importance sampling. The convex optimization formulation for finding the optimal sampling distribution is well-motivated, and the complexity analysis is sound. The authors prove that the solution satisfies desirable properties (Theorem 3.2) and provide an efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) with O(n log n) complexity. However, there are some technical concerns: Lemma 2.1's proof appears to be missing a factor of 2 in the optimal alpha^*, and the logic in Algorithm 4 (lines 4 and 7) seems questionable (the condition in line 4 may never be satisfied, and line 7 likely contains a typo using c^sort instead of u^sort). The numerical results for CD are compelling, but the SGD experiments lack timing comparisons and fail to include relevant baselines like the adaptive methods referenced in [2, 5, 21].

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The main contributions are well-stated, and the theoretical development flows logically. The figures effectively illustrate the key findings. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, though the confusing Example 3.1 could be clarified (the paper claims naive bound-setting can be suboptimal, but the example calculation seems to contradict this).

**Originality:** The key contribution is the novel formulation of the optimal sampling problem as a convex optimization with respect to gradient bounds. This differs significantly from previous approaches that used simple approximations of gradient-based sampling (e.g., [36, 37]) which could be worse than uniform sampling. The paper correctly cites relevant prior work, though the reference list contains errors (incorrect title for [14] and missing reference to Csiba & Richtarik's "Importance Sampling for Minibatches" 2016).

**Significance:** The method has significant practical value as it can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations with negligible overhead (O(n log n) per iteration, comparable to computing a single gradient component). The experimental results demonstrate substantial speedups for CD on real-world datasets, with the adaptive sampling outperforming fixed importance sampling in both iteration count and total computation time. The theoretical guarantee that the method is always better than uniform sampling is particularly valuable for practitioners.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically useful contribution to importance sampling literature. The convex optimization formulation is elegant, the theoretical guarantees are strong, and the empirical results are convincing for CD applications. The method's generality and ease of integration make it broadly applicable.

**Weaknesses:** The technical issues with Lemma 2.1 and Algorithm 4 require clarification. The numerical evaluation lacks comparison to other adaptive sampling methods and to non-uniform fixed importance sampling. The SGD results are incomplete without timing comparisons. The reference list contains errors that need correction.

**Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical foundation is sound, the method is practical and well-motivated, and the empirical results demonstrate clear benefits. The identified weaknesses are technical issues that could be addressed with minor revisions, but they do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in adaptive importance sampling by providing a theoretically guaranteed, computationally efficient method that outperforms previous approaches.

The authors should address the technical concerns in Lemma 2.1, Algorithm 4, and the reference list, and include the missing comparisons in the experimental section. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
