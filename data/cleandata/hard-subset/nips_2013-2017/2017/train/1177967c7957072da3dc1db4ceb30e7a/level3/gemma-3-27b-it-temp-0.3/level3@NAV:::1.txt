## Review of “Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling”

This paper proposes a novel “safe” adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms, leveraging lower and upper bounds on gradient values. The core idea is to formulate optimal sampling as a convex optimization problem, solvable efficiently, ensuring the proposed method always outperforms uniform or fixed importance sampling with negligible computational overhead. 

The paper builds upon the existing literature on importance sampling, acknowledging the trade-off between theoretical guarantees and computational feasibility of adaptive methods like gradient-based sampling. It correctly identifies the challenge of approximating gradient-based sampling and proposes a solution grounded in safe gradient bounds. The theoretical analysis demonstrating the optimality of the proposed sampling distribution relative to the provided bounds is a significant contribution. The connection to convex optimization and the efficient solver further strengthens the work. The authors clearly articulate the benefits of their approach, particularly in scenarios where full gradient information is unavailable or computationally expensive.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The formulation of optimal sampling as a convex optimization problem with an efficient solver is a novel and valuable contribution to the importance sampling literature.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is provably optimal given the bounds, and the analysis demonstrating improvement over uniform sampling is convincing.
* **Efficiency:** The negligible computational overhead claim is appealing and supported by the analysis.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the core ideas.
* **Significance:** The method has the potential to improve the performance of widely used optimization algorithms (CD and SGD) in large-scale machine learning applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lemma 2.1:** The proof of Lemma 2.1 appears to be missing a factor of 2 in α*.
* **Example 3.1:** The example is confusing and the explanation doesn’t fully justify why setting c to the bounds can be suboptimal, contradicting the preceding claim.
* **Algorithm 4:** Line 4 has questionable logic – if m = max(l^{sort}), the condition may never be satisfied. Line 7 likely contains a typo – should use u^{sort} instead of c^{sort}.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical results lack comparison to other adaptive sampling methods (e.g., [2],[5],[21]) and to non-uniform fixed importance sampling. Timing results are missing for the SGD experiments.
* **References:** Incorrect title for [14]; missing reference to Csiba & Richtarik “Importance Sampling for Minibatches” (2016, arXiv).

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed method is theoretically sound, efficient, and well-motivated. The identified issues are relatively minor and can be addressed with revisions. 

**Recommendation:**

After reviewing the authors’ rebuttal and anticipated corrections, I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses, and the potential impact on the field is significant. Addressing the identified issues will further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution.
