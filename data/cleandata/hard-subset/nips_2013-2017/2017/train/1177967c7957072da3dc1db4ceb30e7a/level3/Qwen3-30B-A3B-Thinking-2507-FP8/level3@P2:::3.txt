

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address a timely and important problem in optimization: how to efficiently compute adaptive sampling distributions that can significantly speed up optimization algorithms while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The paper builds on previous work in adaptive importance sampling (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, 2015; Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2012) but introduces a novel approach that guarantees performance better than uniform or fixed importance sampling, which previous methods could not establish.

The main contribution is a "safe" adaptive sampling strategy that uses bounds on gradient information to efficiently compute a sampling distribution that is provably better than fixed sampling. The authors show that their method can be integrated into existing CD and SGD algorithms with minimal computational overhead (O(n log n) per iteration, which is often comparable to computing a single gradient component). They provide theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2) demonstrating that their sampling distribution is always better than fixed importance sampling, and they verify their approach through extensive experiments on real-world datasets.

The paper makes clear progress on the theoretical front by establishing a principled way to compute adaptive sampling distributions under limited gradient information. The authors' key insight is to formulate the problem of finding the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem that can be solved efficiently with the given bounds. This differs from previous approaches that used bounds in a less principled way (e.g., using only upper bounds as in Zhao & Zhang, 2015).

The experimental results (Section 5) convincingly demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms fixed importance sampling in terms of both iteration count and total computation time across various machine learning tasks (L1 and L2 regularized GLMs with square loss, hinge loss, and logistic loss). The authors carefully illustrate the behavior of their adaptive stepsize (vk) and show that even loose gradient bounds suffice for significant improvements.

Strengths:
- Strong theoretical guarantees with clear proofs
- The method is generic and can be applied to multiple optimization algorithms
- Practical implementation details with computational complexity analysis
- Comprehensive empirical validation on multiple datasets
- Clear contribution statement that distinguishes this work from prior art

Weaknesses:
- The figures in Section 5 are difficult to read (small fonts, low contrast) - the authors should enlarge the figures and labels for better readability
- The paper does not fully explain why the performance gains for SGD are less pronounced than for CD (the authors note this but don't provide deeper analysis)
- While the paper discusses GLMs as a primary application, it would be stronger to include more examples of how safe bounds could be derived for other problem classes

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and thorough experimental validation. The authors carefully address both the strengths and limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though the figures in Section 5 require improvement for readability. The notation is consistent and well-defined.

Originality: The paper makes a novel contribution by providing a safe way to compute adaptive sampling distributions that guarantees performance over fixed sampling strategies. This distinguishes it from previous work that used bounds in a less principled manner.

Significance: The results are significant because the proposed method can be integrated into existing optimization algorithms with minimal overhead, leading to substantial speedups in practice. The method is particularly valuable for large-scale optimization problems where computational efficiency is critical.

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The theoretical guarantees are compelling, the method is practical, and the empirical results support the claims. The main issue that needs addressing is the readability of the figures in Section 5.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel theoretical approach to adaptive importance sampling with provable guarantees
- Practical implementation with minimal computational overhead
- Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets and problem types
- Clear contribution that distinguishes from previous work

Arguments against acceptance:
- Figures in Section 5 need improvement for readability
- Limited discussion of why SGD benefits are less pronounced than CD benefits

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The method is theoretically sound, practically implementable, and empirically validated. The only significant issue is the poor quality of figures in Section 5, which can be easily addressed in the camera-ready version. I recommend acceptance.
