## Review of “Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling”

This paper addresses the important problem of safe adaptive importance sampling, a timely topic given the increasing scale of machine learning datasets. The authors propose a novel and efficient approximation of gradient-based sampling that utilizes safe bounds on the gradient, providing theoretical guarantees and demonstrating practical benefits in both coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). 

The paper clearly builds upon existing work in importance sampling, contrasting with fixed and uniform sampling approaches (references [18, 19, 2, 33, 16, 6, 25, 24]) and addressing limitations of previously proposed adaptive methods like gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37] and duality gap-based sampling [5, 23]. The authors effectively highlight the trade-off between theoretical guarantees and computational feasibility, positioning their contribution as a solution that balances both. The core idea of formulating the problem as a convex optimization problem (Section 3) and providing an efficient algorithm (Algorithm 4) is particularly strong. The analysis demonstrating that the proposed sampling is always better than uniform sampling and the theoretical gains over fixed sampling (Section 2) is compelling. 

A key strength of this work is its generality. The proposed method can be integrated into existing algorithms, as shown with both CD and SGD, making it readily applicable to a broad range of problems. The inclusion of both theoretical analysis and comprehensive numerical experiments adds to the paper’s credibility. However, the figures in Section 5 are indeed difficult to interpret due to their small size and indistinct labels, as noted by the supervisors, and should be enlarged and clarified. While the paper provides a strong theoretical and experimental justification for the proposed method, the performance gains for SGD seem more modest than those for CD. A deeper investigation into the reasons behind this difference could further strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a timely and relevant problem.
*   Proposes a novel and theoretically sound method with provable guarantees.
*   Demonstrates practical improvements in CD and SGD.
*   Method is generic and applicable to a wide range of problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Figures in Section 5 require improvement for readability.
*   Performance gains for SGD appear less significant than for CD.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The issues are minor and can be easily addressed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve the clarity of the figures.
