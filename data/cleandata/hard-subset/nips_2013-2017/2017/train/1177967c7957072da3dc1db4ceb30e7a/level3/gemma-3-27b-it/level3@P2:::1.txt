This paper proposes a novel and efficient adaptive importance sampling scheme for both coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe lower and upper bounds on the gradients, formulated as a convex optimization problem solvable in O(n log n) time. The resulting sampling distribution is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling, with negligible extra computational cost. The paper is well-written, clearly explains the motivation and contributions, and provides extensive numerical validation. Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the literature on importance sampling, addressing a key practical challenge of balancing theoretical guarantees with computational feasibility.

The paper effectively positions itself within the broader context of importance sampling techniques. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing adaptive strategies, particularly the computational cost of full gradient computations. The theoretical analysis is strong, demonstrating the optimality of the proposed method with respect to the provided gradient bounds. The authors convincingly argue that their approach bridges the gap between computationally expensive optimal sampling and simpler, yet potentially less effective, fixed sampling schemes. The connection to existing work is well-established, referencing relevant papers in the field.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The proposed algorithm's efficiency is a significant advantage, making it practical for large-scale problems. The theoretical guarantees, while relative to the provided bounds, provide a strong justification for its use. The formulation as a convex optimization problem allows for efficient computation of the sampling distribution. The experimental results on various datasets and with different loss functions are compelling, demonstrating consistent improvements over fixed sampling. The clear explanation of how to compute safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models further enhances the paper’s practical relevance.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. The proof of Lemma 2.1 appears to have a missing factor of 2 in the expression for α*. While this doesn't invalidate the overall argument, it is a detail that should be addressed. Example 3.1, intended to illustrate the limitations of naive bound selection, is somewhat confusing and could be clarified. The logic in Algorithm 4, line 4, is questionable, as the condition may never be satisfied depending on the values of ` and u. A similar typo appears in line 7. The numerical results, while strong, would benefit from comparisons to other adaptive sampling methods, such as those referenced in [2], [5], and [21], and to non-uniform fixed importance sampling. The lack of timing results for the SGD experiments is a notable omission. Finally, the reference to [14] has an incorrect title, and the important work of Csiba and Richtarik [“Importance Sampling for Minibatches,” 2016, arXiv] should be included.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme is a novel approach to address the limitations of existing methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees for the proposed method, demonstrating its optimality relative to the given bounds.
*   **Efficiency:** The algorithm is computationally efficient, with a time complexity of O(n log n) for computing the sampling distribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over fixed sampling on various datasets and with different loss functions.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The paper provides clear guidance on how to compute safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models, enhancing the practical applicability of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor technical issues in the proofs (Lemma 2.1) and algorithms (Algorithm 4).
*   Lack of comparison to other adaptive sampling methods in the numerical results.
*   Missing timing results for the SGD experiments.
*   Minor reference errors.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor issues noted above, the overall quality of the paper is excellent. The proposed method is a significant contribution to the field of importance sampling, offering a practical and theoretically sound solution to a challenging problem. The strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the technical issues and expand the numerical comparisons.

**Post-Rebuttal:**

The authors have addressed all of my concerns in a thorough and convincing manner. They provided a corrected proof for Lemma 2.1, clarified the explanation of Example 3.1, fixed the errors in Algorithm 4, and included additional experimental results comparing their method to other adaptive sampling techniques, as well as providing timing results for SGD. Their responses demonstrate a deep understanding of the material and a commitment to improving the quality of their work. Therefore, I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper.
