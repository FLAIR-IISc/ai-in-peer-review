This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core problem addressed is that optimal gradient-based sampling, while theoretically powerful, is often computationally infeasible as it requires full gradient information at each iteration. Existing approximations that use stale information or bounds can sometimes perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors' main contribution is to formalize the selection of a sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. Given only safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes (for CD) or norms (for SGD), their method finds a sampling distribution that is optimal in a worst-case sense over all possible true gradients consistent with those bounds. This novel formulation leads to the key "safety" guarantee: the proposed sampling is provably always better than or equal to fixed importance sampling (including uniform sampling). The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this optimization problem, making the overhead of computing the sampling distribution negligible in many practical scenarios. The method is demonstrated on generalized linear models, where such gradient bounds can be updated efficiently.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax optimization (Equation 7) is novel and elegant. It provides a principled way to handle uncertainty in gradient information. The resulting "safety" guarantee is a significant contribution, as it addresses a major drawback of previous adaptive sampling schemes which lacked theoretical assurances against performing worse than non-adaptive methods. This work makes an important step towards making adaptive sampling more robust and practical.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported, particularly the properties of the proposed sampling distribution outlined in Theorem 3.2. The development of an efficient O(n log n) solver (Algorithm 4) for the core optimization problem is a crucial component that makes the method practical. The empirical results for coordinate descent are compelling, showing that the proposed method approaches the performance of the ideal (but infeasible) optimal sampling and significantly outperforms uniform sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, starting from the ideal full-information setting and progressively building towards the proposed limited-information approach. The comparison of the different sampling strategies in Figure 1 is a helpful illustration. The overall presentation makes a complex topic accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Minor Technical and Clarity Issues:**
    1.  The proof for Lemma 2.1 seems to contain a minor error. The optimal stepsize `α_k(p_k)` appears to be missing a factor of 2 in the denominator. This should be corrected.
    2.  Example 3.1, which is meant to motivate the minimax formulation, is somewhat confusing. It correctly argues that naively picking the bounds (e.g., `c=l` or `c=u`) can lead to a suboptimal sampling distribution. However, the proposed minimax problem involves maximizing over `c`, which would intuitively push the solution towards the bounds. The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of how the minimax solution avoids the pitfalls of these naive choices.
    3.  Algorithm 4 has a few points that could be clarified for reproducibility. In line 4, the condition `[`sort]` > m` may never be true if `m` is initialized as `max(`sort)`. The logic here should be explained more carefully. Furthermore, line 7 appears to contain a typo and should likely refer to `usort`.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:**
    1.  The empirical evaluation, while strong, could be made more comprehensive. The experiments only compare against uniform sampling. It would be valuable to include comparisons to other state-of-the-art adaptive sampling methods (e.g., Csiba et al. [5], Allen-Zhu et al. [2], Osokin et al. [21]) and non-uniform fixed importance sampling (`L_i`-based sampling). This would provide a better context for the performance of the proposed method.
    2.  Timing results are provided for the CD experiments but are missing for the SGD experiments. Since the practical benefit of the method hinges on its computational efficiency, including wall-clock time results for SGD is essential for a complete evaluation.

*   **Referencing:**
    1.  There are minor issues in the bibliography. The title for reference [14] (Liu et al.) appears to be incorrect.
    2.  The authors should consider citing the related work by Csiba & Richtarik, "Importance Sampling for Minibatches" (2016, arXiv), which is relevant to the topic.

**Summary:**

This is a well-executed and valuable paper that presents a novel and principled approach to adaptive importance sampling. The "safety" guarantee is a strong theoretical and practical contribution to the field. While there are some minor technical points to clarify and the experimental comparison could be broader, the core ideas are sound and significant. The work is likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers.
