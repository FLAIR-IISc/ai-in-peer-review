

This paper introduces a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" approach for coordinate descent and stochastic gradient descent methods, addressing a timely and important topic in optimization for large-scale machine learning. The authors propose a method that efficiently approximates gradient-based sampling using safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information, which can be maintained in many practical scenarios. The key contribution is a theoretically guaranteed sampling scheme that is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling, while being computationally efficient.

The paper excels in several dimensions. **Quality** is strong with well-supported theoretical results (Theorem 3.2) showing the sampling distribution is always better than fixed importance sampling, along with extensive empirical validation on real-world datasets. **Originality** is clear, as the approach differs from prior work by using safe bounds in a novel optimization formulation rather than ad-hoc approximations. The method is **significant** as it provides a practical, generic improvement applicable to many existing algorithms, with demonstrated speed-ups in both iteration count and total computation time. The authors effectively relate their work to prior contributions on adaptive importance sampling (e.g., Zhao & Zhang 2015, Csiba et al. 2015), clarifying how their approach advances the state of the art.

The paper is generally **clear** and well-organized, though I note that the figures in Section 5 are difficult to read due to small text and labels. These should be enlarged and improved for better readability. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that even loose gradient bounds suffice for significant performance gains, with the adaptive stepsize strategy proving critical to the method's effectiveness.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the field of adaptive sampling with theoretically grounded, practical methods. The paper's main weakness is the poor figure quality in Section 5, which detracts from the otherwise excellent empirical results. With this issue addressed, the paper would be highly suitable for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve figure presentation.
