- Proposes Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling: an adaptive IS scheme to accelerate CD and SGD by approximating the optimal gradient-based sampling distribution.  
- Introduces a "safety" guarantee: the computed sampling distribution is provably at least as good as fixed importance sampling (including uniform).  
- Formulates selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax problem over true gradients constrained by safe lower/upper bounds.  
- Provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve the minimax problem per iteration.  
- Method is generic and can be integrated into various first-order methods (CD, SGD).  
- Demonstrates application to GLMs and shows how to maintain gradient bounds efficiently for that class.  
- Theoretical contributions are well-motivated and supported by proofs in the appendix.  
- Extensive experiments on real datasets show significant speed-ups in iteration count and wall-clock time over fixed sampling strategies.  
- High originality: novel safe/minimax formulation compared to previous heuristic bound-based approaches.  
- High significance: practical, drop-in improvement for large-scale optimization with potential broad impact.  
- Major clarity issue: experimental figures (Section 5, Figs. 2–9) are too small and illegible, severely hindering result interpretation.  
- Practical concern: O(n log n) per-iteration overhead could be a bottleneck when n is very large and per-coordinate gradient cost d is small.  
- Dependence on quality of bounds: performance requires reasonably tight, efficiently computable gradient bounds; applicability beyond GLMs (e.g., neural nets) is not explored.  
- Actionable suggestion: remake figures with larger fonts/size or move less critical plots to the appendix.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper worthy of acceptance assuming figure presentation is fixed for camera-ready.
