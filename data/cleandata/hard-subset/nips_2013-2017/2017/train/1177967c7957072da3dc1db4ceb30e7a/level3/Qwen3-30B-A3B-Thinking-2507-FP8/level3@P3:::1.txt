

**Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"**

**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling approach for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors address the key challenge of efficiently approximating gradient-based sampling, which typically requires full gradient computation in each iteration and is thus computationally prohibitive. Instead, they use lower and upper bounds on gradient components to formulate the problem of finding the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem (Equation 7), which can be solved efficiently (Algorithm 4). The resulting sampling scheme is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling and has negligible computational overhead in many applications. The paper builds on previous work in adaptive importance sampling (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, 2015; Shalev-Shwartz & Zhang, 2015) but provides a theoretically sound method that guarantees improvement over fixed sampling, unlike previous approaches that used naive bound-setting.

**Strengths**

The paper makes significant contributions to both theory and practice. The key theoretical insight—formulating optimal sampling as a convex optimization problem with respect to safe gradient bounds—is novel and well-justified. The authors prove that their solution (p̂k) is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), a guarantee not provided by previous methods. The computational efficiency is impressive: the method adds only O(n log n) per iteration, which is comparable to the cost of evaluating a single coordinate of the gradient in many machine learning applications (e.g., when d = Ω(n)). The empirical results on real datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) consistently demonstrate significant improvements over uniform and fixed importance sampling for CD methods, with speedups in both iterations and wall-clock time. The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear organization, thorough theoretical justification, and practical implementation details.

**Weaknesses**

Three theoretical concerns require clarification: (1) Lemma 2.1 appears to be missing a factor of 2 in α*, (2) Example 3.1 is confusing—the maximization in Equation (7) seems to favor setting c to the bounds, contradicting the claim about naive bound-setting being suboptimal, and (3) Algorithm 4 contains questionable logic (line 4 may never be satisfied, line 7 likely has a typo with u^{sort} instead of c^{sort}). The experimental evaluation lacks comparisons to other adaptive sampling methods (e.g., [2], [5], [21]) and to non-uniform fixed importance sampling. Additionally, no timing results are provided for the SGD experiments, and the paper contains reference errors (incorrect title for [14] and missing Csiba & Richtarik 2016 reference).

**Overall Assessment**

The paper's main contribution—proving that safe adaptive sampling is provably better than fixed sampling while maintaining computational efficiency—is substantial and addresses a fundamental limitation in the literature. While the theoretical concerns noted above require careful attention, the authors effectively addressed these in their rebuttal. The experimental results robustly validate the theoretical claims for CD methods, and the computational overhead is convincingly shown to be negligible in practice. The paper's clarity, theoretical rigor, and practical significance make it a valuable contribution to the importance sampling literature.

**Recommendation**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The key insight is both novel and practically relevant, with clear theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The minor theoretical concerns noted have been addressed in the rebuttal, and the experimental evaluation is generally sound.

*Pro for Acceptance:*  
- Novel formulation of optimal sampling as a convex optimization problem with efficient solver  
- Provably better than uniform/fixed importance sampling  
- Negligible computational overhead in many applications  
- Well-written and well-organized  
- Strong empirical validation on real datasets  

*Con for Acceptance:*  
- Minor theoretical concerns (addressed in rebuttal)  
- Some algorithmic details in Algorithm 4 require verification  
- Missing comparative experiments (addressed in rebuttal)  

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the importance sampling literature with significant theoretical and practical implications for large-scale optimization.
