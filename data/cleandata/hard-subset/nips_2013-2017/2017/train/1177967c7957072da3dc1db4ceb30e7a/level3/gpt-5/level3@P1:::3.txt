Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a generic, computationally efficient adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that uses safe lower/upper bounds on per-coordinate gradient magnitudes (for CD) or component gradient norms (for SGD). The core idea is to approximate the theoretically optimal gradient-based sampling—which is typically infeasible because it requires the full gradient at each step—by solving a minimax problem over the set of feasible gradients consistent with the bounds. This yields a sampling distribution p̂ that (i) is optimal under the available information, (ii) guarantees expected progress never worse than fixed Li-based (or uniform) sampling, and (iii) recovers the true gradient-based sampling when the bounds collapse to the true gradients. The resulting step size is α̂k = 1/vk where vk is the optimal value of the minimax problem, providing a tight, iteration-dependent choice.

The formulation connects to and extends prior adaptive strategies: gradient-based sampling for SGD [Zhao & Zhang ’15; Zhu ’16] and duality-gap-based sampling for CD [Perekrestenko et al.], as well as fixed importance sampling for CD/SDCA [Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.]. Unlike prior approximations that plug in upper bounds or stale gradients with no guarantees, the proposed approach uses safe bounds within a convex optimization that admits an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (via sorting) to compute p̂ (Theorem 3.4), supported by a Sion-type minimax argument. For CD, the authors show that full-information gradient-based sampling can yield up to an n-fold speed-up over uniform when Li = L. Section 4 shows how to maintain safe bounds cheaply for generalized linear models (GLMs) by tracking gradient changes and using Cauchy–Schwarz, keeping per-iteration overhead near-negligible when d = Ω(n). Experiments on rcv1, real-sim, news20 validate strong gains for CD and modest gains for SGD.

Quality:
- Theory: The one-step expected progress model is standard and the optimization objective V(p, c) = Σi Li c_i^2 / p_i is well motivated. The minimax formulation over c ∈ [`, u] is justified; the “always better than Li-based” guarantee is clean (Theorem 3.2) and the algorithmic construction via thresholding at √Li m is elegant. Competitive ratio bounds offer additional perspective. Assumptions are reasonable for convex smooth objectives; the GLM bounds are correct and easily implementable. A caveat is that guarantees are with respect to the quadratic model (and bounds), so “always better” holds for the surrogate progress bound rather than realized progress on f—this is consistent with the literature.
- Empirics: CD results show clear, practical speed-ups close to full-information sampling, confirming the theory. For SGD the gains are small, which the authors acknowledge. Baselines cover uniform and Li-based; a “full-information optimal” oracle is useful to contextualize the ceiling.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, with clear problem setup, assumptions, and algorithmic details. The use of consistent notation (V, vk, p̂) and pseudo-code helps. However, figures in Section 5 are hard to read; labels and legends should be enlarged, and axis scales clarified. It would also help to summarize the main convergence rates (now in the appendix) in the main text, and to provide an explicit SGD pseudo-code parallel to Algorithm 2.

Originality:
The safe minimax formulation for deriving an adaptive distribution under gradient bounds, along with an efficient O(n log n) algorithm and guarantees that it is never worse than fixed sampling, appears novel. Prior works used bounds or stale values heuristically; establishing optimality with respect to bound sets and a uniform improvement guarantee is a meaningful conceptual advance.

Significance:
Importance sampling is broadly used in large-scale optimization; making adaptive versions both safe and efficient is valuable. The method is generic, easily pluggable into CD/SGD, and the CD gains are substantial, suggesting real impact for practitioners working with convex sparse learning and GLMs. The limited empirical gains for SGD temper the overall impact there; nonetheless, the framework could inform future adaptive strategies beyond GLMs if safe bounds can be maintained.

Strengths:
- Clean, principled formulation that bridges infeasible gradient-based sampling and practical implementability via safe bounds.
- Strong theoretical properties: optimal under bounds, guarantees strictly better surrogate progress than fixed/uniform, and efficient computation.
- Generic integration into CD and SGD; good engineering insight on maintaining bounds for GLMs.
- Solid empirical improvements for CD across multiple datasets and losses; step-size coupling is well motivated and effective.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Overhead and scaling: O(n log n) per-iteration overhead may be noticeable when d ≪ n or for extremely large n. Discuss amortization (e.g., updating p̂ every few steps) and provide timings isolating the sampling cost.
- Dependence on bound quality: The use of Cauchy–Schwarz can be very loose. Include an ablation on tighter bounds (e.g., using approximate inner products or curvature estimates) to quantify trade-offs.
- Limited SGD benefits: Provide more challenging SGD scenarios (e.g., ill-conditioned finite sums, non-uniform Lipschitz constants, variance-reduced methods with importance sampling) and compare against adaptive baselines (e.g., Zhao & Zhang ’15; non-uniform SAG/SAGA; AdaSDCA) to better position the contribution.
- Scope: Focus is on convex problems. Discuss implications for nonconvex objectives (e.g., deep models) and whether safe bounds can be maintained there.
- Presentation: Enlarge figures and labels; ensure legibility. Include explicit SGD pseudo-code. Summarize main convergence rates in the main text.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel, principled approach with provable guarantees and practical efficiency.
- Strong, consistent improvements for CD with minimal code changes.
- Clear theoretical contribution likely to influence follow-up work on adaptive sampling.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Modest gains for SGD; limited diversity of adaptive baselines in experiments.
- Practical overhead may bite for very large n; bound maintenance may be problem-specific.
- Guarantees are with respect to a surrogate model; end-to-end complexity gains are empirical rather than theoretically tightened rates.

Questions for the authors:
- Can p̂ be updated less frequently (e.g., every τ iterations) with little loss? Any guidance on τ?
- How sensitive are results to bound tightness? Could one adaptively refine bounds where needed?
- How would the method integrate with variance reduction or minibatch sampling?
- Any memory or sparsity issues in maintaining bounds for very high-dimensional sparse data?

Overall, this is a solid and impactful contribution, especially for coordinate descent on convex problems. With improved presentation and broader experimental comparisons, it would make a strong NIPS paper.
