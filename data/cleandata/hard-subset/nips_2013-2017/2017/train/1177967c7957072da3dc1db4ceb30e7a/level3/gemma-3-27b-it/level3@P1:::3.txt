This paper introduces a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme designed to accelerate coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms, particularly in large-scale optimization problems. Adaptive importance sampling, which re-evaluates the importance of each data point during training, has demonstrated superior performance compared to static methods, but often suffers from computational infeasibility or weaker theoretical guarantees. This work bridges that gap by proposing an efficient approximation based on safe bounds on the gradient, provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling, and computationally feasible.

The core idea revolves around leveraging upper and lower bounds on the gradient to define a sampling distribution that is optimal within those constraints. The authors demonstrate that this scheme achieves a favorable trade-off between theoretical guarantees and computational efficiency. Specifically, they prove that for CD methods, gradient-based sampling can offer a speed-up factor of the dimension ‘n’.  They then present a generic adaptive sampling strategy applicable to both CD and SGD, providing a theoretical basis for its efficiency. The method is designed to be easily integrated into existing algorithms with minimal overhead.

The strengths of this paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and practical relevance. The authors establish clear theoretical guarantees for their proposed method, demonstrating its superiority over standard sampling strategies.  The approach is also highly practical, offering a computationally efficient solution that can be readily applied to a wide range of optimization problems, particularly those encountered in machine learning.  The inclusion of extensive numerical experiments further validates the theoretical findings and showcases the method's effectiveness on real-world datasets.  The presentation of Algorithm 1, 2, and 3 clearly delineate the proposed approach against existing methods, making it easier to understand the contribution. The discussion surrounding computational complexity and the connection to existing work provides valuable context.

However, the paper also has some areas for improvement. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the practical implications of the bounds on the competitive ratio (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) could be further elucidated.  A more intuitive explanation of how these ratios translate to expected performance gains would be beneficial. The description of how safe bounds are tracked in Section 4 is somewhat brief; expanding on this aspect with more concrete examples and potential challenges would strengthen the paper.  Most significantly, as noted in the key points, the figures in Section 5 are difficult to read. Enlarging the figures and ensuring clear labeling of axes and lines is crucial for effectively communicating the experimental results. The current size makes it difficult to discern trends and compare performance across different methods.  Additionally, the justification for choosing certain datasets and regularization parameters could be expanded upon.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme presents a novel approach to address the limitations of existing adaptive sampling methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees for the proposed method, demonstrating its superiority over standard sampling strategies.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The method is computationally efficient and can be readily applied to a wide range of optimization problems in machine learning.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Extensive numerical experiments validate the theoretical findings and showcase the method's effectiveness on real-world datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Figure Readability:** The small size and unclear labeling of the figures in Section 5 hinder the effective communication of the experimental results.
*   **Limited Elaboration:** The discussion on tracking safe gradient bounds in Section 4 could be expanded upon.
*   **Competitive Ratio Explanation:** A more intuitive explanation of the competitive ratio bounds would enhance the understanding of their practical implications.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses related to figure presentation and elaboration, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of optimization. The authors successfully address a challenging problem with a novel and theoretically sound solution. The practical relevance of the proposed method, coupled with the strong empirical validation, makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically focusing on improving the readability of the figures and providing further elaboration on the aspects mentioned above.
