- Problem: adaptive importance sampling can speed CD/SGD but optimal schemes need full gradients each iteration, which is computationally prohibitive; existing approximations can be worse than uniform sampling.
- Main idea: formulate sampling from maintainable lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes/norms as a min–max problem to maximize worst-case guaranteed progress.
- Algorithmic contribution: an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the robust sampling distribution and an associated adaptive step size.
- Theoretical guarantee: "safe" sampling provably performs at least as well as Lipschitz-based importance sampling (and hence uniform sampling in special cases) — Theorem 3.2.
- Practicality: shows how to compute gradient bounds efficiently for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).
- Empirical results: extensive experiments on real datasets show substantial iteration and wall-clock speedups, often approaching ideal full-information sampling.
- Strength — significance/originality: novel, principled min–max approach that bridges expensive optimal sampling and cheaper fixed sampling.
- Strength — technical quality: solid analysis with a key safety theorem and a practical O(n log n) method.
- Strength — clarity: well-written, motivates full-information case then limited-information solution; CD and SGD cases linked clearly.
- Strength — empirical validation: thorough experiments with timing results and visualizations that explain behavior.
- Weakness — figures: many plots (Figures 2–9) have fonts too small and need reworking or moving some to the appendix.
- Weakness — total complexity: per-iteration cost analyzed, but lacks a detailed discussion/trade-off of total cost to reach ε-optimality versus iteration-speed gains.
- Weakness — minor errors: small typos/inconsistencies (e.g., Example 2.3 denominator should use ∇f(xk); notation in Theorem 3.2 could be clearer).
- Suggestion: add an explicit corollary comparing performance to uniform sampling for the general non-uniform Li case.
- Acceptance arguments: novel principled approach, crucial safety guarantee enabling reliable adoption, and practical efficiency demonstrated empirically.
- Rejection arguments: poor figure presentation and insufficient discussion of total computational complexity.
- Overall: strong, theoretically sound, and practical contribution; recommend acceptance with revisions to presentation and discussion of total complexity.
