Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for stochastic optimization, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate the optimal gradient-based sampling distribution—which maximizes a standard one-step progress bound—using only safe lower/upper bounds on per-coordinate gradients (for CD) or per-component gradient norms (for SGD). The sampling at each iteration is chosen via a convex min–max problem over the uncertainty set defined by these bounds; the resulting distribution p̂k is provably the best (in a worst-case sense) given the bounds, strictly improves upon uniform and Li-based fixed importance sampling, and coincides with gradient-based sampling when full information is available. The paper also provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute p̂k and shows how to maintain safe bounds at negligible cost for generalized linear models (GLMs). Empirically, the method yields strong improvements for CD across losses and regularizers, and smaller but consistent gains for SGD.

This work builds on and connects several threads: fixed importance sampling for CD (e.g., Nesterov; Richtárik & Takáč), adaptive gradient-based sampling for SGD (Zhao & Zhang; Zhu and others), and gap-based/adaptive sampling for CD (Perekrestenko et al.; Csiba et al.). Unlike prior adaptive schemes that either require full gradients or use heuristics that can underperform uniform sampling, the proposed method provides safety and optimality guarantees relative to the available bounds, with efficient computation.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual advance: formulating sampling under uncertainty as a principled min–max problem with a tight analytic solution and efficient O(n log n) algorithm.
- Strong guarantees: p̂k is the best worst-case choice under the bounds; it always improves on uniform and Li-based fixed sampling; it recovers gradient-based sampling when bounds are exact.
- Practicality: bound maintenance for GLMs is simple and cheap; overall overhead is modest and can be negligible when d = Ω(n).
- Breadth: method is generic and integrates into both CD and SGD; applies to a wide class of convex objectives.
- Experiments: consistent improvements for CD across datasets and losses/regularizers; informative plots of vk showing how stepsizes adapt.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on bound quality: performance hinges on the tightness of the safe bounds. While the paper shows good results even with crude Cauchy–Schwarz bounds, more discussion/ablation on varying bound tightness (and its effect on vk and convergence) would strengthen the case.
- Computational overhead in some regimes: O(n log n) per iteration may be nontrivial for very large n and small d; a discussion of partial updates or amortization could help.
- SGD benefits are limited: as also observed by the authors, improvements over uniform in SGD are modest in the reported settings; adding cases where gradient-norm variability is larger (e.g., highly skewed datasets) or composite/finite-sum settings where prior work found larger gains would be helpful.
- Clarity issues:
  - Figures in Section 5 are hard to read; please enlarge plots and labels and ensure consistent axis scales.
  - Algorithm 4 reuses the symbol “u” for both an index and the upper-bound vector; rename indices to avoid confusion and explicitly address initialization when u0 = ∞ (finite surrogates are needed for sorting).
  - Provide an explicit SGD counterpart of Algorithm 2 (currently only described in text).
- Theory presentation:
  - The min–max interchange in (7) is central; while Sion/Komiya apply, please state the precise convexity/compactness conditions and continuity required and how they are met.
  - Competitive ratio bounds (wk, vk) are informative; a simple computable proxy bound or empirical reporting of ρk would be useful.

Evaluation against review criteria
- Quality: Theoretical developments are careful and compelling: expected progress bound (Lemma 2.1), optimal sampling with full gradients (Example 2.3), safety and dominance guarantees (Theorem 3.2), constructive algorithm (Theorem 3.4), and complexity analysis. The GLM bound-maintenance arguments are standard and sound. Empirical evaluation is solid for CD; the SGD case is less conclusive but honest.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized and readable, with helpful intuition (geometric view via max ⟨√l, c⟩/‖c‖2). Some notation overload and figure readability need attention (see suggestions above).
- Originality: The safe min–max formulation and its O(n log n) solution for sampling under gradient uncertainty are novel and distinct from prior adaptive importance sampling schemes that either require full gradients or rely on stale/heuristic proxies.
- Significance: Likely to be adopted in coordinate-wise methods due to plug-in nature and safety guarantees. Advances the state of the art in adaptive sampling for convex optimization. Broader impact may extend to minibatch selection and distributed settings where bounds are readily available.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and principled adaptive sampling under uncertainty with provable safety and efficiency.
- Strong theoretical guarantees and a constructive, practical algorithm.
- Clear empirical gains for CD across datasets and losses.
- Generic methodology with easy integration into existing solvers.

Cons:
- Benefits for SGD are modest in reported experiments.
- Performance depends on bound tightness; more analysis/ablations would help.
- Some clarity/organization issues (figure readability, notation in Algorithm 4).

Questions for the authors
- Can you report an ablation varying the tightness of bounds `, u (e.g., scaled CS bounds) and its effect on vk and convergence?
- How do you handle u0 = ∞ in practice for Algorithm 4’s sorting? Do you cap with data-dependent finite values?
- Do you envision amortized or partial updates of p̂k to reduce the O(n log n) overhead when n is very large?
- Could you include an SGD algorithm box mirroring Algorithm 2, and experiments on settings where gradient-norm skew is larger?

Minor comments
- Unify notation and avoid reuse of symbols (e.g., “u”).
- Fix typos (e.g., “preformance”, “It holds E[...] (1) ≤” formatting).
- Ensure consistent reporting of wall-clock times across machines, or focus on epochs where hardware differs.

Overall recommendation
A strong submission with a principled, practical contribution to adaptive importance sampling, clear theoretical advances, and solid empirical support—especially for coordinate descent. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing clarity and experimental coverage issues.
