This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, termed "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for stochastic optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The key motivation is to bridge the gap between fixed importance sampling, which is computationally cheap but suboptimal, and optimal adaptive sampling (e.g., gradient-based), which is theoretically powerful but computationally prohibitive as it requires full gradient information at each iteration.

The authors' main contribution is a principled method to approximate the optimal gradient-based sampling distribution using efficiently computable safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. Unlike previous heuristics that might use just the upper bounds and can perform worse than uniform sampling, this paper formulates the problem of choosing the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem. This approach finds a distribution that is optimal for the worst-case realization of the true gradient within the given bounds. The authors provide strong theoretical guarantees for their proposed sampling scheme, proving that it (i) is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling, (ii) is the best possible choice given the bounds under the worst-case criterion, and (iii) can be computed efficiently via a dedicated algorithm running in O(n log n) time. The paper demonstrates how to maintain the required gradient bounds for the important class of Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) with only O(n) overhead per iteration and validates the method's effectiveness through extensive experiments on several real-world datasets.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs, which are detailed in the appendix. The core idea of formulating the sampling problem as a min-max game (min_p max_c V(p,c)/||c||^2) is sound and leads to the desirable "safety" guarantee, which is a significant contribution. The paper presents a complete piece of work, providing the theoretical motivation, the algorithm for computing the sampling distribution (Algorithm 4), a practical application to GLMs, and thorough empirical validation. The authors are careful to evaluate their method against both the standard baseline (fixed sampling) and the ideal but infeasible oracle (optimal gradient-based sampling), which provides a clear context for the performance of their proposed method.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It logically progresses from the ideal full-information setting (Section 2) to the practical limited-information setting (Section 3), motivating the need for the proposed "safe" approach. The problem formulation in Section 3.1 is clear, and the properties of the proposed solution are well-summarized in Theorem 3.2.

A major point for improvement is the presentation of the figures in Section 5. The plots are quite small, and the text, labels, and legends are difficult to read. For the final version, the authors should significantly enlarge these figures to ensure they are legible and effectively communicate the experimental results, which are a key strength of the paper.

### Originality

The paper makes a significant and original contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. While the concepts of adaptive sampling and using gradient bounds are not new, the specific formulation of the problem as a min-max optimization to guarantee safety (i.e., provable improvement over fixed sampling) is highly novel. This principled approach contrasts with prior work that often relied on heuristics (e.g., sampling proportional to upper bounds) without such guarantees. The paper adequately situates its contributions within the existing literature, clearly differentiating its approach from prior methods.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a substantial impact. Importance sampling is a fundamental technique for scaling up optimization algorithms, and this work addresses a critical practical challenge: how to adapt the sampling distribution effectively without incurring prohibitive computational costs or risking performance degradation. The proposed method is:
*   **Safe:** The provable guarantee of improvement over standard baselines like uniform or Li-based sampling is a crucial feature that will encourage adoption.
*   **Practical:** The O(n log n) overhead for computing the sampling is manageable for many problems, especially high-dimensional ones where d >> n, as demonstrated by the wall-clock time improvements in the experiments.
*   **Generic:** The framework is applicable to both CD and SGD and can be integrated into existing solvers with relative ease, provided safe gradient bounds can be maintained.

Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale optimization will likely find these ideas useful and build upon them. The work clearly advances the state-of-the-art in adaptive sampling for stochastic optimization.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novel and Principled Method:** The min-max optimization framework for determining a "safe" sampling distribution from gradient bounds is a novel and elegant contribution.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides a rare provable guarantee that its adaptive sampling scheme will not perform worse than standard fixed importance sampling, addressing a key drawback of previous adaptive heuristics.
3.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The authors provide an efficient algorithm to implement their method and demonstrate its practical utility on an important class of machine learning models (GLMs).
4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments convincingly show significant improvements in both convergence rate per iteration and wall-clock time over fixed sampling baselines, validating the theoretical claims.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  **Clarity of Figures:** The figures are currently difficult to read and must be improved for publication. This is a correctable issue.
2.  **Limited Scope of Bound Derivation:** The paper details the maintenance of gradient bounds primarily for GLMs. While this is an important class of models, a brief discussion on the potential challenges or strategies for other models (e.g., with non-smooth activations or in deep learning) could strengthen the paper.

Overall, this is an excellent paper with strong theoretical and empirical contributions that addresses an important problem in large-scale machine learning. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be easily addressed. I recommend acceptance.
