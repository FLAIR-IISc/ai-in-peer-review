This paper introduces a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for Randomized Coordinate Descent (RCD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The work builds upon a rich literature of adaptive sampling methods, which are known to be powerful but are often computationally infeasible or, when approximated, lack guarantees of improving over static sampling. The authors address this gap by leveraging efficiently maintained lower and upper bounds on gradient information. Their core contribution is to formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem, which seeks the best distribution under the worst-case gradient realization within the given bounds. This approach yields an efficiently computable sampling strategy that is provably superior to standard fixed importance sampling.

The paper's strengths are considerable. The work is well-motivated, and its contributions are significant for large-scale optimization. The primary strength lies in its solid theoretical guarantees. The minimax formulation in Equation (7) is novel and elegant. The resulting "safety" guarantee in Theorem 3.2—proving the proposed sampling is always better than L-based sampling—is a crucial result that distinguishes this work from prior heuristic approximations. This makes the adaptive strategy highly practical and reliable. The paper's theoretical insights are well-supported by extensive experiments on several real-world datasets, which demonstrate clear improvements in both iteration complexity and wall-clock time over non-adaptive baselines.

While the paper is strong, it could be improved in a few areas. The presentation of the empirical results is a weakness; in many figures, the axis ticks, labels, and numbers are too small to be legible. The authors should revise these for clarity, perhaps moving some plots to an appendix to allow for larger, more readable figures. There are also minor typos and inconsistencies to correct (e.g., on Line 85, the denominator of p*_i should use x_k; on Line 135, subscripts for p̂ could be made consistent). Theoretically, the paper would be strengthened by providing an analogue of Conclusion 2 that explicitly compares the proposed sampling to uniform sampling, a very common baseline. Finally, the complexity discussion could be expanded. While the O(n log n) per-iteration overhead is analyzed, a more thorough discussion of the total computational cost, including the O(n) bound updates, required to reach an ε-optimal solution would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's practical impact.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with valuable theoretical and practical contributions. The weaknesses are addressable, and I recommend acceptance.
