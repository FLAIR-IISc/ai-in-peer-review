This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core idea is to address the high computational cost of optimal gradient-based sampling, a known challenge in the field. Instead of computing the full gradient at each step, the authors use efficiently maintained lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes. The main contribution is a novel formulation that finds the optimal sampling distribution, relative to these bounds, by solving a convex optimization problem. This approach provides a practical approximation that is provably superior to uniform or fixed importance sampling, a significant guarantee not always present in prior heuristic approximations.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The formulation of the optimal sampling problem as a maximin problem (7) is both novel and elegant, and its solution provides strong theoretical guarantees. The property that the proposed sampling is "safe"—always outperforming standard fixed sampling—is a particularly valuable result. The development of an efficient O(n log n) solver (Algorithm 4) ensures the method is practical, with a computational overhead that is often negligible compared to the gradient computation itself. The paper is well-written and makes a solid contribution to the literature on importance sampling for large-scale optimization.

However, there are several points that could be clarified or improved. In the theoretical analysis, the proof of Lemma 2.1 appears to be missing a factor of 2 in the derivation of α*. Example 3.1 is also confusing; the text argues that naively setting the unknown gradient vector `c` to its bounds can be suboptimal, yet the maximization in (7) seems to favor exactly that. A more detailed explanation would be beneficial. There are also potential logic issues in Algorithm 4: in line 4, the condition may never be met if `m` is initialized to `max(l^{sort})`, and line 7 likely contains a typo (`c^{sort}` should probably be `u^{sort}`).

The empirical evaluation, while promising, could be strengthened by including more comprehensive baselines. A comparison against other adaptive sampling methods (e.g., [2], [5], [21]) and non-uniform fixed importance sampling (e.g., sampling proportional to Lipschitz constants) is necessary to fully contextualize the performance gains. Furthermore, the absence of timing results for the SGD experiments leaves the claim of low overhead in that setting unverified. Finally, there are minor reference issues, including an incorrect title for [14] and a missing citation to Csiba & Richtarik’s 2016 work on importance sampling for minibatches.

Despite these concerns, this is a strong paper with a sound and significant contribution. The core method is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and practical. Assuming the authors can address these points in their rebuttal, the paper is a clear candidate for acceptance.
