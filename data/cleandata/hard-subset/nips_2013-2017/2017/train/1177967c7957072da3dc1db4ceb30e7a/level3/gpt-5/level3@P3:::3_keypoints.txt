- Proposal: Introduces Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling (SAIS) that uses safe lower/upper bounds on gradients to approximate gradient-based sampling for CD and SGD.  
- Core method: Formulates a min–max problem to choose a sampling distribution p that minimizes worst-case V(p,c)/||c||^2 over all c consistent with bounds, with an O(n log n) sorting-based solver.  
- Theoretical results: Recovers full-information gradient sampling, provably outperforms uniform and fixed Li sampling, and is optimal given the bounds; includes competitive-ratio analysis.  
- Algorithms: Integrates SAIS into coordinate descent and SGD with an efficient O(n log n) routine and justifies complexity.  
- Empirical results: Strong iteration/time improvements for CD on sparse GLMs; smaller but positive gains for SGD; vk/Tr[L] plots support theory.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on adaptive importance sampling literature but provides a principled minimax formulation with uniform guarantees not offered by prior stale/upper-bound approximations.  
- Quality: Technical development is careful and convincing; main theorems and algorithmic details are well-integrated and justified.  
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized, with clear motivation and GLM examples; some notation is dense and figures need higher resolution and larger labels.  
- Originality: The safe min–max formulation and efficient solver that guarantees improvement over fixed sampling are novel contributions.  
- Significance: Practical and broadly applicable to CD/SGD; particularly impactful for CD and large sparse datasets.  
- Strength: Generic, minimally assumptive method with provable guarantees and practical speedups, backed by clear experiments.  
- Weakness: Reliance on potentially loose safe bounds could reduce gains; sensitivity to bound tightness is not fully quantified.  
- Weakness: O(n log n) overhead may be costly when d ≪ n or updates are cheap; no experiments on amortizing/refreshing p.  
- Weakness: Limited baselines—missing comparisons to Gauss–Southwell variants, gap-based sampling, stale-gradient sampling, and mini-batch importance methods.  
- Weakness: SGD improvements are modest on presented datasets; scope limited to convex GLMs without experiments on variance-reduced, mini-batch, or non-convex settings.  
- Suggestions: Enlarge/high-res figures, ablate bound tightness and refresh frequency, add more baselines, report time breakdowns, test dense datasets and sparsity effects, and discuss/extensions to variance-reduction and mini-batches.  
- Acceptance arguments: Strong theoretical guarantees, practical and efficient algorithm, and convincing CD empirical gains make the paper impactful and adoptable.  
- Rejection arguments: Modest SGD gains, potential overhead and bound-sensitivity issues, and limited empirical baselines/robustness analyses.  
- Recommendation: Lean accept, contingent on addressing presentation and expanding empirical breadth.
