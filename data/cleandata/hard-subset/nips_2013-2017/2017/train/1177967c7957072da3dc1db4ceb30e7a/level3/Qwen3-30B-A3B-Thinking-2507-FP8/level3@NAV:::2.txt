

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, addressing the challenge of efficiently implementing gradient-based adaptive sampling. The authors demonstrate that their approach, which uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, provably outperforms uniform and fixed importance sampling while maintaining computational feasibility. The theoretical foundation is strong, with Theorem 3.2 establishing that the proposed sampling is always better than L-based sampling (which includes uniform sampling as a special case), and the empirical results validate significant speedups across multiple machine learning tasks.

The paper makes several notable contributions: it provides a theoretically sound method for adaptive sampling that avoids the computational infeasibility of exact gradient-based sampling, establishes clear theoretical guarantees, and demonstrates practical efficiency through extensive experiments. The connection to generalized linear models is well-motivated, and the computational complexity analysis (O(n log n) per iteration) is convincing for many practical applications.

However, several improvements would strengthen the paper. There is a typo on line 85 (denominator of p^*_i should be x_k, not x), and consistency issues with subscript k on \hat p and \hat c (line 135). The figures require significant improvement—ticks, labels, and numbers are too small or missing, and some should be moved to the appendix for better readability. The authors should also provide an explicit comparison between their method and uniform sampling (p=1/n) in Conclusion 2, as this is a natural baseline. Additionally, the paper would benefit from discussing the total complexity of RCD/SGD, including the O(n) cost for maintaining gradient bounds to reach ε-optimality.

The paper is technically sound (Quality), well-motivated (Significance), and addresses an important practical challenge in optimization (Originality). The clarity could be improved through the suggested figure revisions and notational consistency. Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in adaptive sampling for optimization algorithms, with clear potential for practical impact in machine learning. The minor issues noted do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution.
