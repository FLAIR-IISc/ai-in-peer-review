

This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that uses lower and upper bounds on gradient information to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling. The authors demonstrate that their sampling distribution is provably optimal with respect to the provided bounds, always outperforms uniform or fixed importance sampling, and can be computed at negligible extra computational cost in many applications.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes theoretical advantages of adaptive importance sampling over fixed sampling for CD methods, showing speed-ups that can reach a factor of the dimension n. Second, it formulates the problem of finding the optimal sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem with respect to gradient bounds, providing an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve it. Third, it demonstrates how the method can be integrated into CD and SGD for structured optimization problems, with numerical results showing significant speed-ups on real-world datasets. The paper's theoretical results are well-supported by empirical evidence across multiple machine learning tasks including regression, classification, and feature selection.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The main ideas are well-explained with appropriate notation, and the connection to prior work is well-maintained throughout. The authors properly cite related work in adaptive importance sampling (e.g., [22, 5, 26, 10, 21, 23]) while clearly distinguishing their novel contribution. The paper is well-structured, with each section building logically on the previous one.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical results are sound and well-justified, with the main theorems (Theorems 3.2 and 3.4) providing rigorous guarantees. The experimental results are thorough, covering multiple datasets and loss functions, and demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method. The authors carefully address the computational cost of their method, showing it's comparable to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component in many applications.

The originality is significant. While adaptive importance sampling has been studied before, the authors present a novel approach that uses safe bounds in a way that guarantees superiority over fixed sampling, which previous methods could not establish. The convex optimization formulation of the optimal sampling problem is particularly elegant and novel.

The significance is high. The paper addresses a fundamental problem in large-scale optimization with practical implications for machine learning practitioners. The proposed method can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations, offering substantial speed-ups without requiring major changes to the optimization framework.

Regarding weaknesses, there are a few minor issues that could be addressed in revision. The proof of Lemma 2.1 appears to be missing a factor of 2 in the optimal alpha^*, though this doesn't invalidate the main results. Example 3.1 is somewhat confusing as it seems to contradict the preceding claim about naive bound-setting being suboptimal. Algorithm 4 contains questionable logic in lines 4 and 7 (the condition may never be satisfied, and there's likely a typo in line 7). The numerical results would be strengthened by comparisons to other adaptive sampling methods like those in [2, 5, 21] and to non-uniform fixed importance sampling. Additionally, the SGD experiments lack timing results, making it difficult to assess computational efficiency. The reference list contains an incorrect title for [14] and omits Csiba & Richtarik's "Importance Sampling for Minibatches" (2016).

Despite these minor concerns, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the importance sampling literature. The proposed method is theoretically sound, practically efficient, and significantly outperforms existing approaches in the experiments presented. The authors' responses to the initial review questions were convincing, and the weaknesses identified are largely technical details that can be easily addressed in revision.

Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical foundation, practical significance, clear presentation, novel convex optimization formulation, thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets and problems.

Arguments against acceptance: Minor technical issues in proofs and algorithms, limited comparison to other adaptive methods in experiments, missing timing results for SGD.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling method is theoretically well-justified, practically efficient, and offers substantial improvements over existing approaches. The paper meets all the quality criteria for NIPS, with strong theoretical guarantees, clear writing, significant novelty, and important practical implications. The minor weaknesses identified are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's overall contribution. I recommend acceptance.
