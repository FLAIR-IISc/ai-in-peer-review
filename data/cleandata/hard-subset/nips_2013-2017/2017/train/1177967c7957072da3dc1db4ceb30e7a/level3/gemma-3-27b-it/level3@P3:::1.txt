This paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) utilizing lower/upper gradient bounds. The core idea is to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling, ensuring the resulting sampling distribution is provably optimal *relative* to the provided bounds, always outperforming uniform or fixed importance sampling with negligible extra computational cost. The method is framed as a convex optimization problem with an efficient solver, and the authors provide theoretical guarantees and empirical validation.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

Importance sampling is a crucial technique for scaling optimization to large datasets. While adaptive importance sampling, which adjusts sampling probabilities during optimization, offers theoretical advantages over static methods, it’s often computationally prohibitive, especially gradient-based approaches requiring full gradient computation. This work builds on this line of research (e.g., [22, 5, 26, 10, 21, 23]), addressing the computational challenge by leveraging safe gradient bounds instead of the full gradient. Previous attempts to approximate adaptive sampling often lacked strong theoretical guarantees. This paper distinguishes itself by providing such guarantees – the proposed method is *provably* better than uniform or fixed sampling given the bounds. The work also connects to coordinate descent acceleration techniques ([18, 19, 2, 33, 16, 6, 25, 24]) and variance reduction methods in SGD ([36, 37]).

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper presents a solid theoretical foundation, demonstrating the optimality of the proposed sampling scheme with respect to the given bounds. The proof of Lemma 3.2 is particularly noteworthy.
* **Efficiency:** The authors convincingly argue, and demonstrate empirically, that the proposed method introduces minimal overhead. The O(n log n) complexity for computing the sampling distribution is reasonable for many applications.
* **Generality:** The approach is generic and applicable to both CD and SGD, enhancing its practical relevance.
* **Novel Formulation:** Formulating the optimal sampling as a convex optimization problem is a clever approach, allowing for an efficient solution (Algorithm 4).
* **Well-Written:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the core ideas accessible. The notation is consistent, and the mathematical derivations are generally well-explained.
* **Valuable Contribution:** This work is a significant contribution to the importance sampling literature, bridging the gap between theoretical optimality and practical feasibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lemma 2.1:** The reviewer’s comment regarding a missing factor of 2 in α* within the proof of Lemma 2.1 is valid and needs correction. This impacts the theoretical guarantees presented.
* **Example 3.1:** The example is indeed confusing. Maximizing in (7) potentially favoring boundary values contradicts the claim that naively setting bounds can be suboptimal. Clarification is needed.
* **Algorithm 4, Line 4:** The condition `if m = max(l^{sort})` may never be satisfied, leading to a potential infinite loop. This is a serious logical flaw.
* **Algorithm 4, Line 7:** The use of `c^{sort}` on line 7 is likely a typo and should be `u^{sort}`.
* **Limited Comparison:** The numerical results, while comprehensive, lack comparison to other *adaptive* sampling methods like those in [2], [5], and [21]. Comparison to non-uniform *fixed* importance sampling would also strengthen the findings.
* **Missing Timing for SGD:** The absence of timing results for the SGD experiments limits the assessment of the method’s practical efficiency in that context.
* **Reference Errors:** The incorrect title for [14] and the omission of Csiba & Richtarik (2016) are minor but should be addressed.

**Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and guarantees.
* Practical efficiency and minimal overhead.
* Generality and applicability to both CD and SGD.
* Clear presentation and significant contribution to the field.

**Con:**

* Bugs in Algorithm 4 (lines 4 and 7) and potential error in Lemma 2.1’s proof.
* Limited comparison to state-of-the-art adaptive methods.
* Missing timing results for SGD.
* Minor reference errors.

**Overall Assessment & Recommendation:**

Despite the identified weaknesses, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The theoretical contributions are substantial, and the proposed method addresses a critical challenge in scaling optimization algorithms. The identified bugs in Algorithm 4 and the potential issue with Lemma 2.1 are concerning but appear fixable. The lack of comprehensive comparison to other adaptive methods and the missing timing results for SGD are weaknesses, but do not invalidate the core contributions.

Considering the authors' rebuttal and likely correction of the algorithmic issues and Lemma 2.1's proof, **I recommend acceptance** with the expectation that these corrections are implemented in the final version. Further enriching the empirical evaluation with comparisons to other adaptive methods would significantly strengthen the paper.
