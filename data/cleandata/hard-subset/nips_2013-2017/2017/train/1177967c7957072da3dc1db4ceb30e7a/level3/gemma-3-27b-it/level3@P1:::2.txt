## Review of Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling

This paper proposes a novel safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, resulting in a sampling distribution that is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling. The authors provide theoretical guarantees, demonstrate the integration of the scheme into CD and SGD, and validate its efficiency through extensive numerical testing. Overall, the paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of optimization, particularly for large-scale machine learning problems.

The paper builds upon existing work in importance sampling, adaptive importance sampling, and coordinate descent methods. The authors correctly position their work within the context of previous research, highlighting the drawbacks of existing adaptive strategies (computational cost, weaker theoretical guarantees) and motivating the need for a more efficient and theoretically sound approach. They draw connections to influential works like Nesterov’s accelerated coordinate descent [Nesterov, 2012], and the work of Zhao & Zhang [Zhao & Zhang, 2015] on stochastic gradient descent, providing a solid foundation for their contribution. The work also acknowledges the relationship to duality gap-based sampling for CD [Csiba et al., 2015] and gradient-based sampling for SGD [Zhao & Zhang, 2015; Papa et al., 2015].

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating the provable advantages of the proposed sampling scheme over fixed sampling strategies. Theorem 3.2 is a particularly strong result, establishing a guarantee on the worst-case performance.
*   **Practicality & Efficiency:** The proposed method is designed to be computationally efficient, aiming for minimal overhead compared to standard sampling techniques. The analysis of computational complexity, especially regarding the updating of the bounds `l_k` and `u_k`, is a valuable contribution.
*   **Generality:** The scheme is generic and can be readily integrated into existing CD and SGD algorithms. The authors demonstrate this versatility by applying it to a variety of objective functions and regularization techniques.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive numerical experiments on real-world datasets provide strong evidence supporting the theoretical findings. The figures clearly illustrate the performance improvements achieved with the proposed sampling scheme.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the main ideas and technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Minor Typographical Error:** There is a typo on line 85, where the denominator of p^*\_i should be ‖x\_k‖, not x.
*   **Inconsistency in Notation:** Line 135 presents an inconsistency with the notation; the subscript k should be removed from both \hat p and \hat c for clarity and consistency with previous definitions.
*   **Figure Readability:** The figures are somewhat difficult to read due to small tick labels, missing axis labels, and potentially dense plots. Moving some of the less critical figures to the appendix would improve the clarity of the main body of the paper and allow for larger, more detailed visualizations of the key results.
*   **Missing Comparison to Uniform Sampling:** While the paper demonstrates superiority over L-based sampling, a direct comparison to uniform sampling (p = 1/n) would be beneficial.  A conclusion analogous to Conclusion 2, explicitly stating the speed-up compared to uniform sampling, would strengthen the claims.
*   **Total Complexity Analysis:** The paper focuses on the per-iteration cost of the new sampling scheme. However, a more comprehensive analysis of the *total* complexity (e.g., number of iterations * per-iteration cost) to reach an ε-optimal solution would be valuable.  Specifically, discussing how the O(n) updates for l\_k and u\_k impact the overall complexity is important.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in large-scale optimization.
*   The proposed method is theoretically well-motivated and provides provable guarantees.
*   The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas and technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The readability of the figures could be improved.
*   The lack of a direct comparison to uniform sampling is a minor weakness.
*   A more detailed analysis of the total computational complexity would be helpful.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution and recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should address the typographical error, notation inconsistency, improve the readability of the figures, and add a comparison to uniform sampling and a discussion of the total complexity. These revisions would further enhance the clarity and impact of this valuable work.
