This paper introduces a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, offering theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, leading to a computationally efficient and provably effective approach. The paper builds upon existing work in importance sampling, adaptive sampling, and coordinate descent optimization, notably referencing [22, 5, 26, 36, 37] for gradient-based sampling and [18, 19, 2, 33] for fixed importance sampling. It correctly identifies the trade-off between theoretical guarantees and computational feasibility in adaptive methods and proposes a method that addresses this.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, presenting clear theoretical analysis and supporting it with extensive numerical experiments. The theorems (particularly 3.2) are well-stated and the proofs, while deferred to the appendix, appear rigorous. The proposed method is a complete piece of work, offering a practical and theoretically justified alternative to existing sampling strategies. The authors are careful to acknowledge the assumptions (safe bounds on the gradient) and discuss their implications. The analysis of computational complexity is thorough and realistic.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, although potentially dense for readers unfamiliar with optimization theory. The explanation of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings is clear, although some sections (e.g., the derivation of the optimal sampling distribution) could benefit from more detailed explanations. The connection between the theoretical results and the practical implementation is well-established.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel combination of ideas: using safe bounds on gradients to formulate a convex optimization problem for adaptive sampling. This approach differs significantly from previous methods that either rely on full gradient information or suffer from weaker theoretical guarantees. The proposed method represents a practical and theoretically sound advancement in the field of adaptive importance sampling. The application of Lemma 2.4 is also novel.

**Significance:** The results are important because they provide a practical and provably effective method for accelerating CD and SGD algorithms, particularly in large-scale machine learning applications. The proposed sampling scheme is likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners working on optimization problems where gradient information is costly to compute or access. The demonstrated speed-up and the theoretical guarantees represent a significant contribution to the state-of-the-art. The discussion of how to obtain safe gradient bounds for GLMs is also valuable.

**Strengths:**
*   Strong theoretical foundation with clear guarantees.
*   Computational efficiency – the overhead is minimal.
*   Practical applicability to a wide range of optimization problems.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating the benefits of the proposed scheme.
*   Well-defined connection between theoretical results and practical implementation.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Typo:** Line 85: The denominator of p^*_i should be x_k, not x.
*   **Consistency:** Line 135: Remove the subscript k for \hat p and \hat c.
*   **Figures:** Ticks, labels, and numbers are too small or missing in the figures, hindering readability. Several figures could be moved to the appendix to allow for enlargement.
*   **Missing Comparison:** The paper does not include a direct comparison to uniform sampling (p = 1/n), which would further strengthen the claims. Conclusion 2 states the new sampling beats L-based sampling but a comparison to uniform sampling is standard.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** A more detailed discussion of the impact on the *total* complexity of RCD/SGD – including the cost of updating l_k and u_k to achieve an ε-optimal solution – would be beneficial. How does the overhead of maintaining the bounds affect the overall running time?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper addresses a significant problem in large-scale optimization.
*   The proposed solution is both theoretically sound and computationally efficient.
*   The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The figures require improvement for readability.
*   The lack of a direct comparison to uniform sampling is a minor weakness.
*   A more detailed complexity analysis considering bound updates would be helpful.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be easily addressed with revisions. The strengths of the paper – its theoretical soundness, computational efficiency, and practical applicability – outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should address the typo and consistency issue, improve the figures, add a comparison to uniform sampling, and provide a more detailed analysis of the total complexity including bound updates.
