

# Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper addresses the timely and important problem of adaptive importance sampling for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The authors propose a method that efficiently approximates gradient-based sampling using safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information, providing theoretical guarantees that the sampling is always better than uniform or fixed importance sampling. This work builds on previous contributions to adaptive importance sampling [22, 36, 37] but addresses a critical limitation: previous approaches often used approximations that could be worse than uniform sampling, and provided no guarantees about their performance.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it establishes that adaptive sampling can theoretically outperform static sampling (Section 2), with speedups potentially reaching a factor of the dimension n. Second, it proposes a novel "safe" adaptive sampling scheme that can be efficiently computed in each iteration (Section 3), which is provably better than uniform sampling and recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting. Third, it demonstrates how to integrate this scheme into CD and SGD for structured optimization problems (Section 4), showing that safe gradient bounds can be maintained efficiently for generalized linear models. Finally, it provides extensive empirical evidence verifying the proposed method's effectiveness (Section 5).

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical results are well-supported by careful proofs (Theorems 3.2, 3.4), and the empirical evaluation on real datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with multiple loss functions (square loss, squared hinge loss, logistic loss) and regularizers (L1, L2) convincingly supports the theoretical claims. The authors carefully address both strengths (significant speedups over fixed sampling) and weaknesses (computational overhead), providing a balanced evaluation. The computational complexity analysis shows the overhead is O(n log n) per iteration, which is often comparable to or less than the cost of evaluating a single coordinate of the gradient.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The key contributions are well-articulated, and the algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 1-4) are precise and easy to follow. However, the figures in Section 5 (particularly Figures 2-9) are difficult to read due to small font sizes and low contrast, which significantly hinders the ability to interpret the results. The authors should enlarge these figures and labels to improve readability.

The work demonstrates significant originality. While previous papers have explored adaptive sampling [22, 36, 37], they did not provide guarantees that the approximation would outperform uniform sampling. The authors' key insight is formulating the sampling problem as a convex optimization problem with respect to the safe gradient bounds, which ensures the resulting sampling is always better than fixed importance sampling. This is a novel approach that distinguishes the work from previous contributions.

The significance of the paper is high. The proposed method is generic and can be integrated into many existing CD and SGD algorithms, with minimal computational overhead. The empirical results show that adaptive importance sampling can significantly outperform fixed importance sampling in both iterations and total computation time. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically sound and practically efficient method for adaptive importance sampling that overcomes previous limitations.

Strengths:
- Provably better than uniform and fixed importance sampling
- Efficient computation (O(n log n) per iteration)
- Generic method applicable to many optimization algorithms
- Strong theoretical analysis and empirical validation
- Clear presentation of algorithms and implementation details

Weaknesses:
- Figures in Section 5 are difficult to read (small font, low contrast)
- Limited discussion of applicability to non-GLM problems
- Theoretical analysis doesn't fully address the impact of very loose gradient bounds (though empirical results show robustness)

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of adaptive optimization methods. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme provides theoretical guarantees while maintaining practical efficiency, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its superiority over existing approaches. With minor improvements to the figure presentation, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
