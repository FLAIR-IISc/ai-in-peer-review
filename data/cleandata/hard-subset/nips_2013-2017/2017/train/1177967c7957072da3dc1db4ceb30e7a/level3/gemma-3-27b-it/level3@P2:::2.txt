This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms, aiming to improve their efficiency for large-scale optimization problems. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, providing provable guarantees and computational tractability. The authors demonstrate both theoretical advantages – showing the proposed scheme is superior to uniform and fixed importance sampling – and empirical validation on real datasets. Overall, this is a strong submission with the potential to be highly impactful.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. The authors effectively relate their work to prior research in adaptive importance sampling, highlighting the challenges of computationally infeasible gradient-based approaches and the limitations of existing approximations. The theoretical contributions, particularly Theorem 3.2, are significant, establishing a clear advantage over fixed importance sampling. The proposed algorithm is presented with sufficient detail, and the discussion of computational complexity is insightful. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed scheme, particularly in the CD setting.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach offers a principled and efficient way to approximate gradient-based sampling with provable guarantees.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a strong foundation for the proposed method. The proof of superiority over fixed sampling is particularly compelling.
*   **Practicality:** The algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient, with minimal overhead compared to standard sampling methods.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experimental results on real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed scheme in practice.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Figure Readability:** The figures are difficult to interpret due to small tick labels, axis labels, and numbers. Moving some to the appendix would improve clarity, allowing for larger, more detailed visualizations.
*   **Comparison to Uniform Sampling:** While the paper demonstrates superiority over fixed importance sampling (based on L-smoothness), a direct comparison to uniform sampling (p = 1/n) would further strengthen the results and provide a more intuitive understanding of the gains achieved.
*   **Complexity Analysis Depth:** While the authors discuss the computational complexity, a more detailed analysis of the total complexity – considering the cost of updating the lower and upper bounds (l\_k and u\_k) to reach an ε-optimal solution – would be valuable.
*   **Minor Typo:** Line 85 contains a typo: the denominator of p^*\_i should be ‖x\_k‖, not x.
*   **Minor Consistency:** Line 135 has a minor consistency issue: the subscript 'k' should be removed from \hat p and \hat c.

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The claims are well-supported by evidence. It represents a complete piece of work, rather than a mere position paper. The authors are careful to discuss the limitations and potential extensions of their work.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, the figure readability hinders understanding. Improved labeling and potential relocation to the appendix would greatly enhance clarity.
*   **Originality:** The proposed approach is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The combination of theoretical guarantees and practical efficiency is particularly noteworthy. The related work is adequately referenced.
*   **Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact a wide range of machine learning applications that rely on CD and SGD algorithms. The proposed method could lead to significant speedups and improved performance in these applications.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundations with provable guarantees.
*   Practical algorithm with low computational overhead.
*   Convincing empirical results on real datasets.
*   Addresses an important problem in large-scale optimization.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation (with minor exceptions regarding figure readability).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Figure readability issues detract from the overall presentation.
*   Lack of a direct comparison to uniform sampling.
*   Limited discussion of the overall complexity analysis, including the cost of updating bounds.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper. The contributions are significant, the technical quality is high, and the potential impact is substantial. Addressing the minor weaknesses regarding figure readability, comparison to uniform sampling, and a more detailed complexity analysis would further strengthen the paper. The proposed adaptive sampling scheme is a valuable addition to the toolkit for large-scale optimization and is likely to be widely adopted by the machine learning community.
