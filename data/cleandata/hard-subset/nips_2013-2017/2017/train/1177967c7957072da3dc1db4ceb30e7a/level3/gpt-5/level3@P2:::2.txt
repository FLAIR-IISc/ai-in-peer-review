Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling strategy for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate the (infeasible) fully adaptive gradient-based sampling by constructing, at each iteration, safe lower/upper bounds on per-coordinate gradient magnitudes (for CD) or per-component gradient norms (for SGD), and then solving a convex min–max problem to obtain the best sampling distribution guaranteed by these bounds. The method:
- Recovers the true gradient-based sampling when full information is available.
- Is provably never worse than fixed Li-based importance sampling, and in particular improves upon uniform sampling in the equal-Li case.
- Comes with an O(n log n) per-iteration procedure (Algorithm 4) for computing the sampling, plus O(n) updates for bounds in generalized linear models (GLMs).

The work connects to established lines of research on importance sampling for CD/SGD [Richtárik & Takáč; Qu et al.; Csiba et al.; Zhao & Zhang], gap-based sampling for CD [Perekrestenko et al.; Osokin et al.], and steepest coordinate rules [Nutini et al.; Stich et al.]. Unlike prior “stale” or heuristic approximations of adaptive sampling [Zhao & Zhang; Zhu], the proposed approach uses safe bounds and optimizes a principled min–max criterion with worst-case guarantees (leveraging minimax arguments à la Sion/Komiya). For GLMs, the paper shows how to maintain bounds cheaply via simple inequalities and Hessian-based arguments.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: The optimization formulation (Eq. 7) yields a sampling distribution that is optimal given only interval information. Theorem 3.2(ii) ensures it is always better than fixed Li-based sampling; the competitive-ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5–3.6) provides additional insight.
- Algorithmic practicality: Algorithm 4 computes the sampling in O(n log n), and the paper shows how to maintain safe bounds in O(n) for important classes (GLMs) for both CD and SGD.
- Clear link to ideal adaptive sampling: With full gradient information, the method reduces to the known optimal gradient-based distribution, yielding up to n-fold improvement over uniform sampling in CD (Section 2).
- Empirical support: On multiple datasets, the method substantially accelerates CD in iteration and wall-clock time; the overhead is small enough to pay off in practice.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- End-to-end complexity: While the paper analyzes per-iteration overhead (O(n log n) for sampling + O(n) for bound updates), it would be useful to discuss total complexity to reach ε-suboptimality, incorporating the improved per-iteration progress and the added costs. For CD, a theorem of the form: total time = (iterations to ε under vk) × (Θ(d) + O(n) + O(n log n)) would clarify net gains across regimes (e.g., d ≫ n vs. d ≪ n). For SGD, analogous statements would help interpret the smaller empirical gains.
- Uniform sampling comparison: Theorem 3.2(ii) gives a clean improvement over Li-based sampling; an explicit statement comparing against uniform sampling for general Li (not only Li = L) would strengthen the story. Even if a strict inequality cannot hold uniformly, bounding V(p̂, c) relative to V(punif, c) (e.g., in terms of Li heterogeneity) would contextualize typical improvements over uniform in practice.
- SGD impact: The SGD gains are modest in the experiments. A discussion of when the safe adaptive scheme is expected to help SGD (e.g., heteroscedastic component gradients, structured curvature, tight bounds) and when not would be valuable, as would experiments where SGD benefits more (e.g., strongly varying per-component gradient norms).
- Bound maintenance trade-offs: Since bound updates are O(n), a discussion of amortization strategies (e.g., updating bounds and recomputing p̂ every m > 1 iterations) and their practical effect on accuracy/speed would be helpful.
- Relation to other adaptive CD sampling: Please more explicitly contrast with gap-based adaptive sampling and “approximate steepest” variants, and clarify differences in guarantees and costs.
- Competitive-ratio interpretability: Lemma 3.6’s γ-condition is intuitive but somewhat opaque. Providing simple corollaries for common bound constructions (e.g., those in Section 4) would improve usability.

Clarity and presentation
- Generally well-written, with a clear algorithmic structure and theory-experiment alignment. However, several presentation issues hinder readability:
  - Figures: Axis ticks/labels and numbers are too small or missing. Please enlarge and standardize all plots; consider moving some to the appendix to improve readability of main results.
  - Notation consistency: At line 135, remove the subscript k in the definition of p̂ and ĉ when referring to the generic solution of (7), or state clearly whether they are iteration-specific.
  - Minor typo: Around line 85 in Example 2.3, the denominator for p*_i should depend on xk (i.e., ||√L ∇f(xk)||1), not x.
- It would help to restate the key functions V(p, c) and the role of L early in Section 3 for self-containment, and to add a short proof sketch for the min–max interchange used in (8).

Quality
- The main claims are well-supported by theorems and proofs, with appropriate use of convex analysis and minimax results. The empirical section is solid for CD and candid for SGD.

Originality
- The safe-bounds-based min–max formulation and the O(n log n) exact solver (Algorithm 4) appear novel and nontrivial. The integration into CD/SGD with directionally unbiased step sizes is thoughtfully designed.

Significance
- For CD, the method is practically impactful and theoretically sound, likely to be adopted where gradient bounds are accessible. For SGD, the benefit is more conditional, but the framework is general and may inspire further bound maintenance methods and applications (e.g., distributed settings).

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Principled, optimally safe adaptive sampling under limited information, with provable improvement over fixed Li-based sampling.
- Efficient and generic algorithm applicable to CD and SGD; reduces to optimal gradient-based sampling with full information.
- Strong CD empirical gains with minimal overhead; thorough GLM discussion.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited end-to-end complexity discussion (iterations-to-ε including overhead); practical trade-offs not fully quantified.
- Modest SGD improvements; guidance on when to expect benefits could be stronger.
- Presentation issues (figures, minor inconsistencies) and some missing comparative analyses (uniform sampling in general Li, more baselines).

Questions for authors
- Can you provide iteration and total time complexity to reach ε-suboptimality under your vk-based progress, explicitly accounting for O(n)/O(n log n) overheads?
- Can you add a comparison bound versus uniform sampling for general Li, or at least empirical comparisons highlighting regimes where your method dominates uniform?
- How sensitive are results to the tightness of the safe bounds, and how often do you recompute p̂ in practice?

Overall evaluation
This is a strong and well-motivated paper with a novel and practical adaptive sampling method. With improved presentation and a more explicit total-complexity discussion, it would be a valuable contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
