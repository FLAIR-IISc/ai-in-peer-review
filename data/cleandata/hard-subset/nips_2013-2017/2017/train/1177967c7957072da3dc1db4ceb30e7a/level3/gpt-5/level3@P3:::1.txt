Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and SGD that uses per-iteration lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes to approximate gradient-based sampling at negligible extra cost. It formulates the choice of sampling probabilities as a convex min–max problem over a box of admissible gradient values, proves that the solution is optimal relative to the provided bounds and always improves upon uniform or fixed importance sampling, and provides an O(n log n) algorithm (based on sorting) to compute the probabilities. The approach is generic and instantiated for GLMs, where safe bounds can be updated in O(n) per iteration.

This advances a line of work on non-uniform and adaptive sampling in randomized optimization (e.g., fixed L_i-based sampling [18,19,24], gradient-based sampling for SGD [22,36,37], and duality-gap/adaptive rules for CD [5,23]). The paper shows theoretically that fully gradient-based sampling for CD can yield up to n-fold improvements over uniform sampling, and then bridges to a practical, provably safe approximation via bounds—contrasting with prior heuristics using stale or upper bounds only.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: full-information adaptive sampling is ideal but impractical; safe bounded approximations are attractive if they can guarantee improvement over fixed sampling.
- Solid theory: the min–max formulation yields a distribution that (i) is optimal under the uncertainty set, (ii) dominates L_i-based/uniform sampling, and (iii) recovers full-information sampling when bounds are tight. Results for both CD and SGD are covered.
- Novel convex formulation and efficient solver: elegant reduction to a convex problem with an O(n log n) algorithm relying on sorting of scaled bounds; geometric interpretation enhances intuition.
- Practicality: bound updates for GLMs are simple (Cauchy–Schwarz-based) and cheap; empirical results show strong gains for CD in both iterations and time.
- Well written: the narrative is easy to follow, and empirical plots systematically include the evolving vk to diagnose stepsizes.

Weaknesses and questions
Technical correctness and clarity
- Lemma 2.1: The minimizer in (3) appears to miss a factor of 2 in α*. Deriving α by completing the square in (3) yields α* = ‖∇f(xk)‖22 / V(pk,∇f(xk)) with a factor-of-2 depending on the definition; please check constants and correct the proof and statement.
- Example 3.1: As written, optimizing (7) over c in a box does push c to faces aligned with l, which may appear to contradict the earlier warning that choosing c = l or c = u for defining p can be suboptimal. The resolution is that (7) is min_p max_c, whereas naively fixing p by plugging c = u (or l) is not solving the min–max. Please clarify this distinction in the text to avoid confusion.
- Algorithm 4, line 4: If m is initialized as max(l^{sort}), the condition [l^{sort}]_ℓ > m can never be true on the first pass, and may never trigger when equality holds. Consider initializing m using the current c (even if empty), or using ≥ in the violation check, or computing m from the undecided set at each iteration.
- Algorithm 4, line 7: Likely a typo—should use u^{sort} instead of c^{sort} in the comment and assignment.
- Competitive ratio: Lemma 3.5 gives a useful upper bound v_k/w_k, but w_k is non-convex to compute. A brief discussion on how loose this can be in practice (e.g., via proxy bounds) would help.

Experiments
- Comparisons: The empirical study lacks head-to-head comparisons against other adaptive schemes (e.g., [2], [5], [21] as cited; also [36], [23], and “stale-gradient” baselines). Including these would better position the contribution.
- Fixed non-uniform baselines: Please add results against non-uniform fixed importance sampling (L_i-based or other fixed distributions) in all plots, not only uniform.
- SGD timing: For SGD, iteration-wise plots are given, but timing results are missing. Timing is important to support the claim of negligible overhead; please include wall-clock comparisons similar to the CD experiments.
- Datasets/setup: A short note on the hardware for all timing plots would improve reproducibility and enable fair timing comparisons across CD and SGD.

References and presentation
- Reference [14] title appears incorrect; please fix.
- Please add Csiba & Richtárik, “Importance Sampling for Minibatches” (2016, arXiv), which is closely related to adaptive sampling for minibatches.
- Minor typos: a few small inconsistencies (e.g., Algorithm 4 comments/variables) and punctuation; a careful proofread is recommended.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Strong. Theoretical guarantees are clear and useful, the solver is efficient, and CD experiments convincingly support the claims. Some minor technical clarifications are needed (Lemma 2.1 constants; Algorithm 4 details).
- Clarity: Very good. The paper is well organized, with helpful intuition. Clarifications suggested above would further reduce potential confusion.
- Originality: High. The safe min–max formulation and the efficient solver for optimal sampling under bounds are novel, and the “always-better-than-fixed” guarantee is new relative to prior bounded/stale approximations.
- Significance: High. Importance sampling is widely used in CD/SGD; having a drop-in, provably safe adaptive version with negligible overhead is valuable. The work is likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., to minibatches, blocks, and distributed settings).

Pros
- Provably optimal safe sampling under bounds; dominates uniform/fixed sampling.
- Efficient O(n log n) solver; easy integration into CD/SGD.
- Strong theoretical and empirical support, especially for CD.

Cons
- Missing comparisons to other adaptive baselines and to fixed non-uniform sampling in all settings.
- Minor technical issues (Lemma 2.1 factor, Algorithm 4 edge cases/typos).
- No timing for SGD.

Recommendation
Before rebuttal: lean accept, pending clarifications and additional comparisons/timing if feasible. After authors’ rebuttal: recommend acceptance. The contributions are solid and useful, and the issues raised are minor and fixable. Confidence: high.
