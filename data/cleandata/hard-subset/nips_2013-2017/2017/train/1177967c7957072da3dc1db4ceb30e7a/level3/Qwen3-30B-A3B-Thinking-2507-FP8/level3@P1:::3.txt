

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" method for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms, addressing a key limitation in existing adaptive importance sampling approaches. The authors argue that while gradient-based adaptive sampling theoretically outperforms fixed importance sampling (e.g., by a factor of n in coordinate descent), it is computationally infeasible as it requires full gradient computation at each iteration. The proposed method overcomes this limitation by using safe upper and lower bounds on gradient components to construct a sampling distribution that is provably better than uniform sampling while remaining computationally efficient.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it provides a theoretical analysis showing that gradient-based sampling in CD can achieve speedups up to a factor of n compared to fixed importance sampling (Section 2). Second, the authors formulate a novel optimization problem to find the best sampling distribution given safe gradient bounds, proving that their solution (Algorithm 2) is always better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2). Third, they demonstrate how to efficiently compute these bounds for generalized linear models (Section 4), showing that the overhead is O(n log n) per iteration, which is comparable to evaluating a single gradient component for many problems. Finally, extensive empirical results on multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with various loss functions and regularization terms confirm the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach (Section 5).

The paper makes strong contributions to the literature on adaptive importance sampling. Previous work (e.g., Zhao and Zhang, 2015; Shalev-Shwartz and Zhang, 2015) has considered gradient-based sampling but often without provable guarantees relative to fixed sampling. The authors' key insight is that by solving a minimax optimization problem over the safe gradient bounds, they can guarantee performance better than fixed sampling, addressing a critical gap in the literature. The work builds appropriately on recent NIPS/ICML contributions on adaptive sampling (e.g., Allen-Zhu et al., ICML 2017; Csiba et al., ICML 2015), while making a distinct technical contribution.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with well-justified proofs (Lemma 2.4, Theorem 3.2, 3.4) that clearly establish the advantages of the proposed method. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with results on multiple datasets, loss functions, and regularization terms. The computational complexity analysis (Section 4) is particularly convincing, demonstrating that the overhead is minimal for many practical problems. The paper is well-organized, with clear exposition of the key ideas.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, though the figures in Section 5 would benefit from larger labels and improved readability as noted in the key points. The authors could also more explicitly address why the proposed method shows greater benefits for CD than for SGD (as observed in their experiments), though this is a minor point.

The originality of the approach is clear. While adaptive importance sampling has been studied before, the safe sampling method using minimax optimization over gradient bounds represents a novel technical contribution that advances the state of the art. The authors make a compelling case for how this differs from prior work that used approximations of the gradient-based sampling (e.g., [36, 37]).

The significance of the work is high. The method provides a practical solution to a theoretically appealing but computationally challenging problem. The results demonstrate significant speedups in practice (up to 4x faster convergence in some cases), and the method is broadly applicable to many machine learning problems. The computational overhead is minimal, making it suitable for large-scale applications where adaptive methods have previously been deemed impractical.

Strengths:
- Provably better than uniform and fixed importance sampling
- Theoretical guarantees with rigorous proofs
- Practical computational efficiency (O(n log n) per iteration)
- Extensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and problem settings
- Clear distinction from prior work on adaptive importance sampling

Weaknesses:
- Figures in Section 5 require better readability (larger labels, clearer presentation)
- Limited discussion of why CD benefits more than SGD from the proposed method
- Some implementation details about gradient bound tracking could be more thoroughly explained

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The safe adaptive importance sampling method provides a theoretically sound and computationally efficient approach to adaptive sampling that outperforms existing methods. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by comprehensive experiments. The method is generic and could be applied to a wide range of optimization problems beyond those considered in the paper.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution with provable guarantees, practical computational efficiency, extensive empirical validation, significant potential impact on optimization methods for large-scale machine learning.

Con: Minor issues with figure readability, limited discussion of CD vs. SGD performance differences, some implementation details could be more detailed.

Overall, this paper makes an important contribution to the field of optimization and would be of interest to the NIPS community. The method is both theoretically elegant and practically useful, addressing a key limitation in adaptive importance sampling that has hindered its adoption in practice. I recommend acceptance.
