This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to avoid recomputing full gradients by maintaining per-coordinate (for CD) or per-sample (for SGD) safe lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes and solving a convex min–max problem to obtain a sampling distribution that is provably optimal under these bounds. The method dominates uniform and fixed Li-based importance sampling in expected progress, recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information limit, and can be computed in O(n log n) per iteration. The work is well situated within prior NeurIPS literature on non-uniform sampling and adaptive probabilities (e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Zhao & Zhang; Csiba et al.), gap-safe screening and Gauss–Southwell rules, and connects cleanly to minimax reasoning.

Quality: The theory is solid and carefully executed: the worst-case optimality under bounds, guarantees vs. fixed sampling, and competitive-ratio analysis are valuable. The construction and correctness of the O(n log n) algorithm are convincing, and the link to practical bound maintenance for GLMs is clear. Experiments on rcv1/real-sim/news20 show strong speedups for CD and modest gains for SGD, consistent with the theory.

Clarity: Overall well-written and organized, but Figures in Section 5 are hard to read; please enlarge the plots and axis labels. It would also help to provide a clearer pseudocode summary with complexity and a table of symbols.

Originality: Framing adaptive sampling under safe bounds as a min–max problem and deriving the worst-case optimal distribution appears novel relative to prior gradient- or gap-based heuristics and stale-gradient approximations.

Significance: The approach is generic, easy to integrate, and practically relevant for large-scale convex learning. The guarantee “always at least as good as fixed sampling” is a strong selling point.

Suggestions:
- Improve figure readability; add time-breakdowns to quantify O(n log n) overhead vs. n and d, and an ablation on bound tightness vs. speedup (including empirical ρk).
- Compare against stale-gradient and Gauss–Southwell/approximate steepest-CD baselines; discuss minibatch SGD and non-convex models.
- Clarify memory costs and initialization of bounds; release code for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Timely topic; generic method with clear theoretical advantages.
- Strong, careful analysis and practically efficient algorithm.
- Empirical gains on standard benchmarks, especially for CD.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited domains for bound construction (primarily GLMs); modest SGD benefits.
- Missing some strong baselines and ablations; figure readability needs improvement.

Overall: Lean accept.
