This paper proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The key motivation is that while optimal gradient-based adaptive sampling can significantly accelerate convergence, it is computationally prohibitive as it requires full gradient information in every iteration. Existing approximations can sometimes perform worse than even uniform sampling.

The main contribution is a novel method to compute a sampling distribution based on efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes (for CD) or norms (for SGD). Instead of using a heuristic based on these bounds, the authors formulate the problem of choosing the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem. This problem seeks the distribution that maximizes the guaranteed progress in the worst case, over all possible true gradients that are consistent with the given bounds. The paper presents an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem and derives the corresponding adaptive step size.

The authors prove that the resulting sampling scheme is "safe" in the sense that it is guaranteed to perform at least as well as standard fixed importance sampling (based on Lipschitz constants), and by extension, uniform sampling. This is a crucial theoretical guarantee that was missing in prior work. The paper demonstrates how to compute the required gradient bounds efficiently for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs). Finally, extensive experiments on several real-world datasets show that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling strategies in both number of iterations and wall-clock time, often approaching the performance of the ideal but impractical optimal sampling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem in large-scale machine learning: how to benefit from adaptive sampling without incurring prohibitive computational costs. The proposed min-max formulation for finding a robust sampling distribution from uncertain gradient information is novel, elegant, and principled. It provides a clear and theoretically sound way to bridge the gap between expensive optimal sampling and cheaper but less effective fixed sampling.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically solid. The central result, Theorem 3.2, establishes a "safety" guarantee that the proposed sampling is provably superior to standard Li-based importance sampling. This is a strong and important result, as it ensures that practitioners can adopt this adaptive method without risking performance degradation compared to simpler methods. The derivation of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute the sampling distribution is another key technical contribution that makes the approach practical.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-structured. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem by first presenting the ideal "full information" case and then logically developing their "limited information" approximation. The connection between the CD and SGD cases is made clearly.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and provide convincing evidence for the method's effectiveness. The comparison against both fixed sampling and the ideal (but impractical) optimal sampling clearly situates the proposed method's performance. The inclusion of timing results (Figures 6 and 9) is crucial, as it demonstrates that the theoretical gains in convergence rate translate to real-world speedups, despite the computational overhead of the method. The plots visualizing the evolution of `vk` provide valuable insight into why the method works well.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Readability of Figures:** The figures are a weak point of the presentation. In Figures 2-9, the font size of the axis labels, ticks, and legends is far too small, making them very difficult to read. I would strongly recommend re-working the figures to improve their readability. Perhaps some of the less critical plots could be moved to the appendix to allow the main results to be displayed more clearly and in a larger format.
*   **Discussion on Total Complexity:** The paper analyzes the per-iteration complexity, which is O(n log n) for sampling plus O(n) for updating the bounds. While the empirical timing results are positive, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion on the *total* computational complexity to reach an ε-optimal solution. How does the improvement in iteration complexity (i.e., faster convergence) formally trade off against the per-iteration overhead? A more explicit discussion, even if not a full theoretical proof, would strengthen the paper's claims about overall efficiency.
*   **Minor Typos/Inconsistencies:**
    *   In Example 2.3 (around line 85), the denominator for the optimal probability `p*k` should likely involve `∇f(xk)`, not `∇f(x)`, for consistency.
    *   In the statement of Theorem 3.2 (line 135), the notation `(p̂, ĉ)` is used for the solution. It would be more consistent with the problem definition in (7), which defines `p̂k` as the optimal probability distribution, to drop the subscript `k` in the text preceding the theorem for clarity, or to use it consistently within the theorem.
*   **Explicit Comparison to Uniform Sampling:** Theorem 3.2(ii) and Remark 3.3 nicely show that the proposed sampling is better than Li-based sampling (and uniform sampling in the special case where all `Li` are equal). It would be beneficial to include a more general and explicit corollary that formally states the improvement over standard uniform sampling (`p_i = 1/n`) for the general case of non-uniform `Li`.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper introduces a novel and principled min-max approach to adaptive importance sampling under uncertainty, which is a significant conceptual contribution.
2.  It provides a crucial "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2), ensuring the method is provably better than standard fixed sampling schemes, which addresses a key drawback of prior heuristic approaches.
3.  The proposed method is practical, supported by an efficient algorithm and demonstrated to provide substantial wall-clock time speedups on real-world problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The presentation quality of the figures is poor and needs significant improvement.
2.  The theoretical discussion of total complexity could be more detailed.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The strengths in originality and significance far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are largely related to presentation and can be addressed in a final version. I recommend acceptance.
