## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper addresses the important problem of safe adaptive importance sampling, a technique for accelerating optimization algorithms, particularly in large-scale machine learning. The authors propose a novel method that approximates gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, aiming for a balance between theoretical guarantees and computational feasibility. This is a timely and popular topic, as adaptive importance sampling has shown promise but often suffers from computational overhead.

The strength of this work lies in its generality. The proposed method is designed to be easily integrated with existing algorithms like coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD), making it broadly applicable. Importantly, the paper provides both theoretical analysis demonstrating the benefits over uniform and fixed importance sampling, and practical experiments validating these findings. The theoretical results, particularly the analysis of the speed-up achievable with CD, are compelling. The inclusion of both CD and SGD demonstrates the versatility of the approach. 

However, the presentation could be improved. Figures in Section 5, particularly those showing the evolution of the objective function and iteration complexity, are difficult to interpret due to small font sizes and cramped labels. Enlarging these figures and improving label readability is crucial. While the paper provides a good overview, the connection between the proposed method and existing work on safe gradient bounds could be more explicitly drawn. A more detailed discussion of the assumptions required for the safe bounds (e.g., how they are obtained in practice beyond the GLM example) would also be beneficial. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and active research area.
*   Provides a theoretically sound and practically validated method.
*   Offers a generic approach applicable to multiple optimization algorithms.
*   Demonstrates clear improvements over baseline sampling strategies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Figures in Section 5 are difficult to read.
*   Discussion of practical considerations for obtaining safe gradient bounds could be more detailed.
*   Connection to related work on safe gradient estimation could be strengthened.

**Overall:**

Despite the minor presentation issues, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of adaptive importance sampling. The proposed method is well-motivated, theoretically justified, and empirically supported. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the figure readability and expanding the discussion on practical implementation details.
