- Proposes a "safe" adaptive importance sampling method for CD and SGD using lower/upper gradient bounds to avoid degrading performance.  
- Frames sampling as a min-max optimization (Eq. 7) to choose a distribution robust to worst-case gradient realizations.  
- Provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the safe sampling distribution.  
- Proves the sampling is never worse than fixed importance sampling (and uniform in the isotropic case).  
- Demonstrates practical speedups on GLMs in iterations and wall-clock time across real datasets.  
- Theoretical contributions are solid and well-motivated, with proofs supporting the safety guarantee (Theorem 3.2).  
- Algorithmic contribution (Algorithm 4) is non-trivial and makes the method practical.  
- Claims are supported by a comprehensive set of experiments.  
- Paper is generally well-written and logically structured, with clear exposition of core ideas.  
- Figures in Section 5 are too small/illegible, impairing interpretation of experimental results.  
- Method is highly original: min-max safety formulation and geometric angle interpretation are novel.  
- The approach gives a principled way to use partial gradient information without risking worse performance.  
- The contribution is significant and broadly applicable, likely easy to integrate into existing CD/SGD solvers.  
- Efficient maintenance of safe gradient bounds for GLMs is a strong practical grounding.  
- Co-optimizing adaptive step size with sampling is a useful addition confirmed by experiments.  
- Suggest adding discussion on sensitivity to bound tightness (how performance transitions as u→l or bounds loosen).  
- Note that speedups are less pronounced for SGD than CD; further analysis of why would be helpful.  
- Crucial fix: remake all Section 5 figures larger with bigger fonts for readability.  
- Pros summarized: novel principled method, strong guarantees, efficient algorithm, practical impact.  
- Con: main weakness is poor figure readability in experiments (fixable).
