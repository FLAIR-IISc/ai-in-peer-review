This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling with a method that relies on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients, ensuring theoretical guarantees while maintaining computational efficiency. The authors demonstrate that their proposed sampling strategy is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling and can achieve significant speed-ups in practice.

The paper is well-motivated, positioning itself within the growing body of work on adaptive importance sampling techniques. The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches, particularly the computational infeasibility of full gradient-based sampling. The authors appropriately reference related work, including papers from NIPS and other relevant conferences (e.g., ICML, AISTATS), demonstrating a good understanding of the field. The contributions are clearly stated and well-defined, outlining the theoretical and empirical advancements of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, proving the benefits of the proposed sampling scheme over uniform and fixed importance sampling. The derivation of optimal sampling distributions under bounded gradient information is a significant contribution. The connection to optimal sampling with full gradient information (Example 2.3) is well-established and provides a clear benchmark.
*   **Practicality and Generality:** The proposed method is designed to be computationally efficient and easily integrated into existing algorithms like CD and SGD. The analysis of the computational cost and the demonstration of how to track safe gradient bounds in GLMs are valuable for practical implementation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive numerical experiments on several datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed sampling scheme in improving convergence speed for both CD and SGD. The inclusion of plots showing the evolution of *vk*, the scaling factor for the stepsize, provides insights into the behavior of the algorithm.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are relatively clear, even for complex theoretical concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Figure Readability:** As noted in the key points, the figures in Section 5 are difficult to read. Enlarging the figures and increasing the font size for labels would significantly improve their clarity and impact. It's hard to discern details and accurately compare the performance of different methods with the current figure sizes.
*   **SGD Performance Gap:** The authors observe a smaller performance gain for SGD compared to CD. While they acknowledge this, a more in-depth discussion of the reasons behind this difference could be beneficial. Investigating the influence of different parameters (e.g., learning rate, batch size) or specific problem characteristics on the performance gap would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Bounding Assumptions:** The reliance on safe upper and lower bounds on the gradients requires additional assumptions. While the paper discusses methods for tracking these bounds in GLMs, it would be useful to discuss the limitations and challenges of obtaining such bounds in more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks).
*   **Limited Comparison:** While the paper compares against uniform sampling, a comparison to other recent adaptive sampling methods (beyond the ones directly referenced) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling that addresses a critical challenge in large-scale optimization.
*   The proposed method is practical, computationally efficient, and easily integrated into existing algorithms.
*   The extensive empirical evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the method on several real-world datasets.
*   The paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The figure readability issues hinder the assessment of the empirical results.
*   The performance gap between CD and SGD warrants further investigation and discussion.
*   The dependence on safe gradient bounds might limit the applicability of the method to certain problem settings.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. The theoretical contributions, practical relevance, and empirical validation make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. Addressing the figure readability issue would further enhance the presentation and impact of the work. The performance difference between CD and SGD is worth noting, but does not detract from the overall quality of the paper. The limitations related to bounding gradient information are acknowledged and do not invalidate the contributions within the stated scope of the work. The paper meets all the criteria for a good scientific contribution: quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
