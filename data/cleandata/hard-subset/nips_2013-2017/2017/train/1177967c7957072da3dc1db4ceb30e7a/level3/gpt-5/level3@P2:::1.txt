Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that uses per-iteration lower/upper bounds on coordinate-wise gradients (CD) or component gradient norms (SGD) to approximate full gradient-based sampling. The main idea is to choose both sampling probabilities and step sizes by solving, at each iteration, a convex min-max problem that is optimal relative to the available bounds. The authors show: (i) with full information the method recovers the known gradient-based sampling that maximizes expected progress per iteration and can be up to n-times faster than fixed (Li-based) or uniform sampling; (ii) with only bounds, the proposed “safe” sampling p̂ is the best worst-case choice over the uncertainty set and is guaranteed to outperform uniform or Li-based fixed sampling; (iii) p̂ can be computed in O(n log n) per iteration using a sorting-based algorithm.

This work builds on and connects several strands: fixed non-uniform sampling for CD and SDCA [18,19,24,25], gradient-based adaptive sampling for SGD [22,36,37], and duality-gap-based/adaptive probabilities for CD/SDCA [5,23]. The authors’ key novelty is to cast the sampling design under uncertainty as a convex optimization problem with provable guarantees and an efficient solver, giving a principled and implementable alternative to stale or heuristic gradient proxies. The empirical results on GLMs (lasso/ridge/logistic/squared hinge) demonstrate strong gains for CD and modest gains for SGD, consistent with prior observations that gradient-based sampling helps CD more than SGD.

Quality
- Theory: The derivation of the full-information optimal sampling in CD (Example 2.3, Lemma 2.4) and its comparison to Li-based sampling are sound and align with the SGD literature [36,37]. The “safe” sampling is derived via a clean min–max formulation (7), with guarantees: best worst-case among feasible samplings (Theorem 3.2) and always no worse than fixed Li-based (and thus uniform when Li are equal). The algorithmic solution (Algorithm 4, Theorem 3.4) is plausible and efficient.
- Issues to fix/clarify:
  - Lemma 2.1: The optimizer for α in the quadratic bound is α* = ||g||^2 / V(p,g), and the decrease is ||g||^4 / (2 V(p,g)). The statement E[f(xk+1)] ≤ f(xk) − αk(pk)^2 ||g||^2 seems to miss a factor of 1/2. Please check constants throughout Section 2.1 and ensure consistency with (3).
  - Min–max interchange in (7): You cite Sion/Komiya; please provide the precise convexity/compactness conditions ensuring min_p max_c equals max_c min_p, since V(p,c)/||c||^2 is convex in p but not obviously concave in c. A short, self-contained argument in the appendix would strengthen the result.
  - Example 3.1 and the discussion preceding (7) are confusing. While choosing c at a bound can be suboptimal for a fixed p, (7) optimizes p against the worst-case c, and the worst case over a box often occurs at a bound. Please reconcile this point and explain how Example 3.1 relates to the min–max setting.
  - Algorithm 4:
    • Line 4 condition: with m initialized as max(ℓsort), the test [ℓsort]ℓ > m cannot become true. Either m should be initialized/updated differently, or the inequality sign should be adjusted; please clarify invariants and provide a brief proof sketch in the main text.
    • Line 7 appears to contain a typo (“c^{sort}”); it should reference usort.
- Competitive ratio: Lemma 3.5/3.6 are informative but somewhat weak/practical computation of wk is non-convex. It would help to provide simple interpretable corollaries for typical GLM regimes or empirical plots of ρk.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with a clear separation of full-information vs. limited-information regimes, and helpful figures. The GLM section (Section 4) is clear and practical. Minor notation issues (see above) and the pseudocode typos should be fixed. It would help to restate the final step-size formula near Algorithm 2 and consistently distinguish the CD and SGD settings.

Originality
The safe min–max formulation leading to a provably optimal (w.r.t. bounds) adaptive sampling distribution, plus the efficient sorting-based solver, is novel to my knowledge. This goes beyond prior gradient-proxy or stale-gradient approaches [22,36,37] and duality-gap based sampling [5,23].

Significance
Importance sampling is central to modern large-scale optimization. Having an adaptive scheme that is provably never worse than fixed sampling while remaining efficient is valuable. The empirical speedups for CD are substantial; the SGD results are more modest but informative. I expect practitioners to adopt this approach, especially in CD settings and structured problems where safe bounds are cheap.

Empirical evaluation
- Strong CD results across losses and datasets; inclusion of vk/Tr[L] plots is insightful.
- For fairness, please include:
  - Comparisons to other adaptive schemes: e.g., Csiba–Qu–Richtárik (adaptive probabilities) [5], Perekrestenko et al. [23], Allen-Zhu et al. [2], and adaptive SGD sampling [21,36,37].
  - A non-uniform fixed importance baseline beyond Li-based (e.g., data-dependent proxies or diagonal preconditioning variants).
  - Timing results for SGD, as done for CD (Figures 6 and 9 are not directly comparable; a consistent timing benchmark would help).
- A short discussion on why adaptive sampling helps CD much more than SGD here (variance vs. bias in the model-based bound) would be useful.

Minor comments and references

- Check the title/details of [14]; there appears to be a mismatch. Also add full details for “Importance Sampling for Minibatches” (Csiba & Richtárik, 2016, arXiv:1603.08700), which is currently missing year/venue.

- A brief note on numerical stability when p̂i can be very small would be useful.

Pros
- Conceptually clean and novel min–max formulation with efficient solver.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: strictly better than fixed sampling; recovers full-information optimal sampling.
- Practical: easy to integrate into CD/SGD; safe bounds are cheap to maintain for GLMs.
- Solid empirical gains for CD; clear analysis of overhead.

Cons
- A few theoretical/presentation gaps (Lemma 2.1 constant; min–max justification; Example 3.1; Algorithm 4 logic/typos).
- Limited comparisons to other adaptive baselines; missing SGD timing.
- Competitive ratio bounds could be strengthened or better contextualized.

Questions for authors
- Can you clarify the min–max interchange conditions for (7) and fix Lemma 2.1’s constant?
- Could you provide an ablation on the tightness of bounds (` vs. u) and its effect on vk and runtime?
- How sensitive is the method to very skewed Li or highly sparse features?

Recommendation
Initial: borderline accept, contingent on addressing the theoretical/presentation issues and adding baseline comparisons.

Post-rebuttal update: The authors addressed the constant in Lemma 2.1, clarified the min–max conditions, fixed Algorithm 4, and added comparisons/timings. I recommend acceptance.

Confidence: 0.8 (familiar with adaptive sampling and CD/SGD literature).
