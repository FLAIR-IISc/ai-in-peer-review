This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, named "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling," for randomized coordinate descent (RCD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to approximate the optimal but computationally expensive gradient-based sampling distribution. Unlike prior work that often relies on heuristics or stale values, this paper introduces a principled approach. The authors assume access to safe lower and lower bounds on the gradient magnitudes (`l_k` and `u_k`) at each iteration `k`. They then formulate an optimization problem to find the sampling distribution that minimizes the worst-case variance of the update, considering all possible gradients consistent with these bounds.

This work builds upon a line of research that aims to accelerate large-scale optimization by adapting the sampling distribution during training. Previous adaptive methods, while often effective in practice, either required computing the full gradient at each step (which negates the benefit of stochastic methods) or used approximations that lacked theoretical guarantees of being superior to simpler, static sampling schemes (e.g., uniform or Lipschitz-based sampling). The main contribution of this paper is to bridge this gap by providing a practical adaptive scheme that is provably never worse than the standard fixed importance sampling based on Lipschitz constants.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a min-max optimization problem (Equation 7) is novel and provides a principled way to handle uncertainty in gradient information. This "safe" approach, which optimizes for the worst case given the available bounds, is a significant conceptual contribution. The resulting guarantee that the sampling is always better than standard `L_i`-based sampling (Theorem 3.2) is a strong and important result that was missing from prior work on approximate adaptive sampling.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method, including a proof that the min-max problem can be solved efficiently (Theorem 3.4) via a clever algorithm (Algorithm 4) with `O(n log n)` complexity. This makes the approach practical for high-dimensional problems. The connection between the ideal full-information setting and the proposed limited-information approach is well-motivated and clearly established.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method. The results on several real-world datasets show that the safe adaptive sampling scheme significantly outperforms uniform and fixed importance sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time. The plots visualizing the step-size parameter `v_k` provide excellent intuition and confirm that the method effectively captures useful information, even from loose bounds, to accelerate convergence.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Incomplete Complexity Analysis:** The paper's primary weakness is the lack of a complete theoretical analysis of the total computational complexity. The authors analyze the per-iteration cost (`O(n log n)` for sampling plus `O(n)` for bound updates) and the expected one-step progress. However, they do not combine these to provide a formal convergence rate in terms of total runtime to reach an ε-optimal solution. Such an analysis would be crucial for understanding the trade-offs involved. For instance, how much must the iteration complexity improve to offset the `O(n log n)` per-iteration overhead compared to an `O(1)` uniform sampling step? A discussion on the total complexity, especially in different regimes of `n` and `d`, would substantially strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation of Figures:** The figures are difficult to read due to very small fonts for axis labels, ticks, and numbers in the legends. For a paper focused on empirical performance, clear and readable plots are essential. I suggest increasing all font sizes significantly. If space is an issue, the authors could consider moving some of the less critical plots (e.g., for some of the specific loss/regularizer combinations) to an appendix to allow the main-text figures to be larger and more legible.

3.  **Comparison to Uniform Sampling:** Theorem 3.2 (ii) provides a direct comparison to `L_i`-based sampling. While Remark 3.3 handles the special case where this reduces to uniform sampling (`L_i = L`), a more explicit comparison for the general case would be valuable. Could the authors provide an analogue of Theorem 3.2 (ii) that directly compares `V(p̂, c)` with `V(p_unif, c)`? This would help readers better understand when the proposed method offers an advantage over the simplest baseline.

4.  **Minor Inconsistencies and Typos:**
    *   **Line 85 (Lemma 2.4):** The notation in Lemma 2.4, `arg min V(p,x)`, might be slightly confusing as `x` is also used for the optimization iterate `x_k`. Using a different symbol (e.g., `g` for gradient) might improve clarity. (Note: The user-provided point suggests a typo, but it appears to be a minor notational choice. In Example 2.3, the denominator of `p_k^*` is missing the L1-norm subscript `_1`, which is present in Lemma 2.4.)
    *   **Line 135 (Theorem 3.2):** The theorem statement drops the subscript `k` from `p̂`, `ĉ`, and `v_k`, which were defined with the subscript in Equation (7). While this is a common shortcut, retaining the subscript `k` would improve consistency and clarity.

**Summary:**

This is a well-written and interesting paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed "safe" adaptive sampling method is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically effective. The guarantee of being provably better than standard fixed importance sampling is a key result that addresses a significant limitation of prior work. The main weakness is the absence of a complete analysis of total runtime complexity. However, the strengths of the paper—its originality, technical quality, and convincing experiments—are substantial. I recommend acceptance. The suggestions above, particularly regarding the complexity analysis and figure clarity, should be addressed to further improve the manuscript.
