

This paper proposes a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors address a key limitation in existing adaptive importance sampling approaches: while gradient-based sampling theoretically outperforms uniform or fixed importance sampling (with potential speedups of up to n times), it is computationally infeasible as it requires full gradient computation in each iteration. The authors propose a practical approximation that uses safe lower and upper bounds on gradient information, which can be efficiently computed and provably guarantees better performance than static sampling strategies.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it rigorously establishes that adaptive gradient-based sampling in CD methods can achieve theoretically superior convergence rates compared to fixed sampling (Section 2), with speedups potentially proportional to the problem dimension n. Second, the authors propose a novel optimization framework (Equation 7) for computing the best possible sampling distribution given safe gradient bounds, which is provably better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2). Third, they demonstrate how to efficiently maintain these bounds for generalized linear models (Section 4), showing that the computational overhead is only O(n log n) per iteration - often comparable to the cost of evaluating a single gradient component. Finally, extensive empirical results on real datasets (Section 5) confirm significant speedups in both iteration count and total computation time for CD methods.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear proofs of the key properties (Theorem 3.2, Lemma 3.5). The proposed method is genuinely novel and addresses a well-known gap in the literature - previous approaches either failed to provide theoretical guarantees or required computationally prohibitive operations. The empirical validation is thorough, covering multiple loss functions and regularization schemes across three large datasets. The authors successfully demonstrate that the method works with very loose gradient bounds, requiring no special initialization and accelerating significantly after just one epoch.

Several minor issues should be addressed before publication. First, there's a typo in line 85 where the denominator of p^*_i should be x_k rather than x. Second, the notation consistency needs improvement: the authors should remove the subscript k from p̂ and ĉ to align with the rest of the paper. Third, the figures have readability issues - ticks, labels, and numbers are too small, which would make them difficult to interpret in a conference proceeding. The authors should consider moving some figures to the appendix to improve readability. Fourth, the paper would benefit from a direct comparison between the proposed sampling and uniform sampling (in addition to the comparison with L-based sampling), as uniform sampling is a common baseline in the literature. Finally, while the paper discusses the computational cost of the sampling scheme, it would strengthen the argument to explicitly discuss the impact on total complexity when including the O(n) cost of maintaining the gradient bounds for reaching ε-optimality.

The significance of this work is clear. For CD methods, the proposed sampling consistently outperforms fixed importance sampling, with convergence rates approaching those of the theoretically optimal (but computationally infeasible) gradient-based sampling. The method's computational efficiency (O(n log n) per iteration) makes it practical for large-scale problems, and the generic nature of the approach means it can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations. The empirical results demonstrate that the method works well in practice, with significant speedups observed on standard machine learning datasets.

The paper is well-written and clear, though some notation inconsistencies and figure issues detract from its presentation. The relationship to prior work is well-established, with appropriate citations to relevant NIPS papers (e.g., [22, 23, 36, 37]). The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach, particularly noting that the benefits are less pronounced for SGD compared to CD.

In summary, this paper presents a theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and practically effective approach to adaptive importance sampling. The method fills an important gap in the literature by providing a practical alternative to computationally infeasible gradient-based sampling with provable theoretical guarantees. While minor presentation issues need addressing, the technical contributions are significant and the empirical results are compelling. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, with clear potential for wide adoption in practical implementations of CD and SGD. The proposed method is a valuable addition to the toolkit of optimization algorithms for large-scale machine learning.
