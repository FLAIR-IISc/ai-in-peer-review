### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling strategy for first-order optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The core problem addressed is that optimal adaptive sampling (e.g., based on full gradient information) is computationally prohibitive, while common approximations can perform worse than simple uniform sampling. This work bridges the gap by proposing a method that uses efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on the gradient information to define a sampling distribution.

The main contribution is the formulation of the sampling problem as a min-max optimization problem (Eq. 7), which seeks the best sampling distribution for the worst-case gradient realization within the given bounds. The authors present an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem and prove that the resulting sampling distribution is guaranteed to be at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling (and uniform sampling in the isotropic case). This "safety" guarantee is a key differentiator from prior work on approximate adaptive sampling. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed scheme and demonstrates its practical effectiveness for training generalized linear models on several real-world datasets, showing significant speedups over non-adaptive methods in both iterations and wall-clock time.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical contributions are solid: the min-max formulation is well-motivated, and the proof that the resulting sampling is provably better than fixed sampling (Theorem 3.2) is a strong result. The development of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute this sampling distribution is a crucial and non-trivial contribution that makes the method practical. Claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and a comprehensive set of experiments.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. It effectively motivates the problem by first analyzing the ideal "full information" case and then transitioning to the practical "limited information" setting. The core ideas are conveyed clearly. However, a significant weakness in presentation lies in the figures in Section 5. They are rendered at a very small size, making the axes, labels, and legends nearly illegible. This severely impacts the reader's ability to interpret the otherwise compelling experimental results. This is an easily correctable but important issue.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While adaptive importance sampling is a well-studied area, the idea of formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a min-max game to provide a "safety" guarantee against uncertainty is novel. It provides a principled way to leverage partial gradient information without the risk of degrading performance, which distinguishes it from previous heuristics or approximations based on stale values or simple upper bounds. The connection made between the min-max problem and the geometric interpretation of minimizing the angle with the Lipschitz constants vector is insightful.

**Significance:** The paper's contribution is highly significant. It addresses a fundamentally important problem in large-scale machine learning: how to make optimization faster. The proposed method is generic, practical, and comes with strong theoretical guarantees. Given its ease of integration into existing CD and SGD frameworks and its demonstrated performance benefits, the ideas are likely to be widely adopted by both researchers and practitioners. The "safety" property is particularly attractive, as it lowers the barrier to adopting adaptive schemes. This work has the potential to become a standard technique for accelerating stochastic optimization methods.

### **Detailed Comments**

The authors do a great job of showing how safe gradient bounds can be maintained efficiently for an important class of problems (GLMs). This practical grounding is a major strength. The analysis of the adaptive step size, which is co-optimized with the sampling distribution, is also a nice contribution and the experiments clearly show its benefit (Figure 2).

While the paper demonstrates the method's effectiveness with simple Cauchy-Schwarz-based bounds, a brief discussion on the method's sensitivity to the tightness of the bounds (`l`, `u`) would be welcome. For instance, it's clear that as `u` approaches `l`, the method converges to the optimal sampling. Conversely, as the bounds become very loose, does the sampling gracefully degrade to uniform sampling? The theory (Theorem 3.2) guarantees it will be no worse than fixed sampling, but characterizing this transition would add further insight.

The experimental results in Section 5 are strong, but the observation that the speedup is less pronounced for SGD compared to CD is interesting and warrants more discussion. Is this due to the nature of the bounds being less informative in the SGD case, or is it a more fundamental property related to variance reduction in SGD versus progress in CD?

### **Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Crucially, please remake all figures in Section 5.** They should be significantly larger, with increased font sizes for all labels, legends, and axis ticks to ensure readability. This is essential for the final version of the paper.
*   Consider adding a short discussion on the impact of bound tightness on the performance of the proposed sampling scheme.
*   If space permits, elaborating on the potential reasons for the different performance gains between CD and SGD would strengthen the experimental analysis.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

**Pros:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Method:** The min-max formulation for safe adaptive sampling is a highly original and theoretically sound contribution.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The proposed method is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling, removing a key barrier to the adoption of adaptive methods.
3.  **Practical and Efficient:** The paper provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm for the sampling scheme and demonstrates its effectiveness in terms of wall-clock time on real-world problems.
4.  **High Significance and Broad Impact:** The method is generic and can be easily integrated into widely used CD and SGD solvers, promising broad impact across many areas of machine learning.

**Cons:**
1.  **Poor Figure Readability:** The figures in the experimental section are currently illegible, hindering the evaluation of the empirical results (though this is easily fixable).
