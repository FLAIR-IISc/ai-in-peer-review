### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme, named Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling, designed to accelerate coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The core idea is to approximate the optimal but computationally expensive gradient-based sampling distribution. Unlike previous approximations that could potentially perform worse than uniform sampling, this method provides a "safety" guarantee: the resulting sampling distribution is provably at least as good as fixed importance sampling (including uniform sampling).

The authors formulate the problem of choosing the best sampling distribution, given only safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient magnitudes, as a minimax optimization problem. They seek a probability distribution that minimizes the worst-case variance, considering all possible true gradients that lie within the given bounds. The paper presents an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this problem in each iteration. This approach is generic and can be integrated into various first-order methods. The authors demonstrate its application to Generalized Linear Models (GLMs), showing how the required gradient bounds can be maintained efficiently. The theoretical contributions are supported by extensive experiments on several real-world datasets, demonstrating significant speed-ups over fixed sampling strategies in both iteration count and wall-clock time.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-motivated and appear sound, with proofs provided in the appendix. The formulation of the sampling problem as a minimax objective (Equation 7) is a strong and elegant contribution. This work represents a complete research effort, spanning from theoretical motivation and the development of a novel algorithm to practical implementation and thorough empirical validation. The authors are careful to discuss the computational complexity and provide a clear pathway for applying the method to an important class of problems (GLMs).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, contrasting the benefits of ideal adaptive sampling with the practical pitfalls of existing approximations. The progression from the full-information setting (Section 2) to the proposed safe, limited-information approach (Section 3) is easy to follow.

However, a significant weakness in clarity comes from the presentation of the experimental results. The figures in Section 5 (Figures 2-9) are much too small. The axis labels, legends, and titles are nearly illegible, which severely hinders the reader's ability to interpret and verify the empirical claims of the paper. This is a critical issue as the experimental results are a key component of the paper's contribution.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While adaptive importance sampling is a well-studied topic, the proposed "safe" formulation is novel. Previous methods using bounds often relied on heuristics, such as sampling proportionally to the upper bounds, without guarantees of improvement over static sampling. This paper’s main contribution—solving a minimax problem to find a robust sampling distribution given the uncertainty encapsulated by the bounds—is a new and principled approach. The work is well-situated within the existing literature, and its unique contributions are clearly articulated.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a problem of high significance and practical importance in the machine learning community: accelerating large-scale optimization. The proposed method is generic and has the potential for broad impact, as it can be applied as a "drop-in" improvement for a wide range of CD and SGD algorithms. The fact that it offers theoretical guarantees of improvement is a major advantage that will likely encourage its adoption. The work provides a strong foundation that others can build upon, for example, by developing tighter or more efficient bounding strategies for different model classes. It demonstrably advances the state of the art in adaptive sampling by providing a method that is both practical and theoretically sound.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Soundness:** The core idea of formulating the sampling choice as a minimax problem over gradient bounds is novel, elegant, and technically sound.
*   **Strong Guarantees:** The method comes with a "safety" guarantee, ensuring it performs no worse than standard fixed importance sampling, which addresses a key weakness of prior adaptive heuristics.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The O(n log n) algorithm for computing the sampling is efficient, and the paper demonstrates its applicability to GLMs, a widely used model class. The wall-clock time improvements shown in experiments confirm its practical utility.
*   **Generality:** The framework is generic and not limited to a specific optimizer or problem class, suggesting broad potential impact.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Presentation Quality:** The poor quality of the figures is a major drawback. It makes the experimental section, a crucial part of the paper, very difficult to assess.
*   **Overhead:** The O(n log n) overhead per iteration, while often negligible, could become a bottleneck for problems where the dimension `n` is very large and the per-coordinate gradient cost `d` is small.
*   **Dependence on Bounds:** The method's performance relies on the availability of reasonably tight and efficiently computable gradient bounds. While demonstrated for GLMs, its applicability to more complex models (e.g., neural networks) is not explored.

### Detailed Comments

The primary suggestion for improvement is to remake all figures in Section 5. They should be made significantly larger, with readable font sizes for all labels, titles, and legends. If space is a concern, the authors could consider using a more compact layout (e.g., subplots) or moving some of the less critical experimental results to the appendix to allow the main figures to be larger.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel idea, solid theory, and promising results. The one major flaw is the presentation of the figures. Assuming the authors can fix this for the camera-ready version, the paper is a clear contribution to the field and warrants acceptance.
