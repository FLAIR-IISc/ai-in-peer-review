Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the known gap between theoretically appealing adaptive importance sampling (e.g., gradient-based sampling for SGD or gap-based sampling for CD) and its computational infeasibility due to the need to evaluate the full gradient at every iteration. The authors propose a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme that uses per-iteration lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes (CD) or component gradient norms (SGD) to solve a min-max convex problem that yields the best sampling distribution compatible with the available bounds. The scheme reduces to optimal gradient-based sampling in the full-information case and is provably never worse than fixed (Li-based) importance sampling, with an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Theorem 3.4). Theoretical guarantees (Theorem 3.2) show improved expected progress over fixed distributions; the method also yields an adaptive stepsize αk = 1/vk derived from the same optimization. Empirically, the method accelerates CD significantly on GLMs (square, squared hinge, logistic; L1/L2), and gives smaller gains for SGD—consistent with prior observations that gradients-based sampling is more beneficial in CD than in SGD. The work builds on and contrasts with prior importance sampling for CD and SGD [e.g., Richtárik & Takáč; Zhao & Zhang; Csiba et al.; Osokin et al.; Stich et al.] by providing a distribution that is both computationally efficient and with a “never worse than fixed sampling” guarantee based on safe bounds.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: casting sampling under partial information as a convex min-max and solving it exactly with O(n log n) time per iteration.
- Strong theoretical guarantees: Theorem 3.2(i–ii) shows best worst-case performance under bounds and strictly better expected progress than Li-based sampling; the scheme recovers optimal gradient-based sampling with full information.
- Practicality: Simple integration in CD/SGD; safe bounds can be maintained cheaply for GLMs and similar models. The adaptive stepsize αk=1/vk is well-motivated and empirically important.
- Empirical validation: Clear and consistent CD improvements in both iteration and wall-clock time; diagnostic plots of vk support the theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope of empirical gains in SGD is modest; the paper acknowledges this, but it would help to clarify when the method is expected to shine for SGD (e.g., problem classes where gradients are highly skewed).
- Complexity discussion is mostly per-iteration. Please provide an end-to-end complexity comparison to reach ε-suboptimality for CD/SGD, explicitly accounting for the O(n) updates of `k, uk and the O(n log n) sampling computation, and relate this to problem dimension d. For instance, under coordinate-wise L-smoothness and strong convexity, can you state rates in terms of vk (or bounds on its epoch-averages) and contrast with uniform/Li-based CD, including the additional O(n) maintenance cost?
- Uniform sampling comparison: While Theorem 3.2(ii) and Remark 3.3 imply an advantage over uniform when Li=L, it would be useful to add an explicit corollary paralleling Conclusion 2: for general L, an inequality showing how much better than uniform the safe sampling is, or at least a clear statement of conditions under which p̂ improves over p=1/n.
- Figures: axes ticks/labels are too small or missing in several plots, making it hard to read. Consider moving some figures to the appendix to enlarge the main ones and ensure all axes and legends are legible.
- Notational consistency: the paper uses c, ck, and hats somewhat interchangeably. Unify the use of subscripts and hats in the statement and proof of Theorem 3.2/Algorithm 4 to reduce cognitive load. See specific line comments below.

Quality
- Soundness: The min-max formulation is natural and the guarantees are clear. The O(n log n) algorithm is convincing and practical. The assumptions on safe bounds are reasonable in GLMs; it would help to discuss robustness when the bounds are loose or occasionally violated.
- Empirical support: Experiments reflect the theory; ablation on the tightness of bounds or on the frequency of recomputing p̂ (e.g., amortized updates every few iterations) would further strengthen the practical story.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but notational slips and figure readability issues detract. Suggest reordering some definitions (e.g., define Ck and c earlier) and adding a brief intuition for Lemma 3.5/3.6 in the main text.

Originality
- The safe-bounds min-max formulation and its exact, efficient solution appear novel relative to importance sampling literature. The “never worse than fixed sampling” guarantee under partial information is a distinctive contribution.

Significance
- High for CD: the method is simple, general, and provides substantial gains. Moderate for SGD: benefits are smaller but the guarantee and framework remain valuable. Likely to influence practical implementations where partial gradient information is cheap to maintain.

Detailed comments and questions
- Please add a discussion of total runtime to reach ε-optimality under standard assumptions (smoothness/strong convexity), including the O(n) per-iteration updates of `k, uk and the O(n log n) sampling cost. When d ≫ n, the overhead is minor; when d ≪ n, can you recommend amortization strategies (e.g., recomputing p̂ every m iterations) and quantify the trade-off?
- Provide a short discussion on the effect of bounds’ tightness: e.g., how do vk and the competitive ratio ρk behave when bounds are very loose initially and tighten over time? An ablation varying bound-tightness would be informative.
- For SGD, can you identify settings where your scheme yields larger gains (e.g., heteroscedastic component gradients, skewed Lipschitz constants), or combine with variance reduction?

Typos/notation
- Line 85: in the definition of p∗, the denominator should use xk (current iterate), not x.
- Line 135: consistency—remove the subscript k for p̂ and ĉ where they denote the solution of the per-iteration min-max problem; alternatively, keep k everywhere but be consistent across Theorem 3.2 and Algorithm 4.
- Minor: ensure all instances of norms in denominators are L1 vs L2 as intended; check Equation (3) signs and factors for αk.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled, and efficient solution to adaptive sampling under partial information with strong guarantees.
- Clear empirical improvements for CD; broadly applicable and easy to integrate.
- Solid theory that bridges full-information optimal sampling and practical, safe approximations.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical gains for SGD; broader set of tasks or guidance for when to expect benefits would help.
- End-to-end complexity to ε not fully articulated; figure readability issues and minor notational inconsistencies.

Overall evaluation
A well-motivated and technically solid contribution with practical impact for coordinate descent and a clean theoretical framework that is likely to inspire follow-up work. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved clarity (figures/notation) and a more explicit discussion of total complexity and uniform-sampling comparisons.
