This paper addresses the challenge of making adaptive importance sampling practical for large-scale optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). The authors correctly identify that optimal gradient-based sampling is often computationally infeasible, as it requires full gradient information at each step, while common approximations can perform worse than static sampling. The core contribution is a novel "safe" adaptive sampling scheme. This method relies on maintaining efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on the gradient information. The key novelty lies in formulating the choice of the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem: finding the distribution that offers the best worst-case performance over all possible gradients consistent with the bounds. This approach builds upon prior work in adaptive sampling but introduces a robust formulation that guarantees improvement over static methods like uniform or fixed importance sampling.

The paper's primary strengths are its theoretical soundness, originality, and practical significance.
*   **Quality & Originality:** The formulation of the sampling problem as a min-max objective (Eq. 7) is elegant and novel. The authors provide strong theoretical backing, proving that their proposed sampling is provably better than fixed Li-based sampling (Theorem 3.2). This is a crucial guarantee that previous approximations lacked. Furthermore, they present an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to compute this sampling distribution, making the method practical.
*   **Significance & Clarity:** The proposed scheme is generic and can be readily integrated into existing solvers. The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The claims are strongly supported by extensive experiments on several real-world datasets, which demonstrate significant improvements in convergence speed, both in terms of iterations and wall-clock time, over standard baselines.

The main weakness is that the performance of the method is inherently dependent on the quality of the gradient bounds. The paper demonstrates a simple, efficient method for maintaining these bounds for GLMs using a Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which can be quite loose. While the experiments show this is sufficient for a notable speed-up, the paper would be strengthened by a deeper discussion on the impact of bound tightness on performance. For instance, analyzing how the competitive ratio (ρk) behaves with looser bounds or exploring more sophisticated bounding techniques could provide valuable insights. Additionally, the authors note that the empirical gains for SGD are less pronounced than for CD, a point that could be explored further.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant and practical contribution to stochastic optimization. The theoretical guarantees combined with strong empirical results make it a valuable addition to the field. The identified weakness is minor and represents a clear avenue for future work rather than a flaw in the current contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
