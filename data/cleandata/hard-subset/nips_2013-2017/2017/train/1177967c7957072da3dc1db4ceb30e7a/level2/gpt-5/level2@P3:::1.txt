Summary
The paper addresses adaptive importance sampling for stochastic optimization, focusing on coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to approximate theoretically optimal gradient-based sampling—whose direct computation is typically infeasible—using safe lower and upper bounds on per-coordinate gradients (CD) or per-component gradient norms (SGD). A minimax formulation identifies the sampling distribution that is provably the best under the available bounds, never worse than uniform or fixed Li-based importance sampling, and recovers optimal gradient-based sampling when full information is available. The work provides:

- A theoretical justification that gradient-based sampling can yield up to an n-fold speedup over fixed sampling in CD.
- A safe adaptive scheme with stepsize αk = 1/vk where vk is the solution of a convex min-max, with guarantees: (i) best worst-case relative to bounds, (ii) uniformly better than Li-based sampling, (iii) efficient computation in O(n log n) via a sorting-based algorithm.
- Practical recipes for maintaining safe bounds `, u for GLMs in CD and SGD, with O(n) updates per iteration.
- Empirical evidence on standard sparse text datasets showing marked gains for CD, and smaller but consistent gains for SGD, with overhead small enough to improve wall-clock time.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to non-uniform and adaptive sampling for CD/SGD [18,19,2,33,16,6,25,24] and gradient-based or gap-based adaptive schemes [22,36,37,5,23]. Unlike prior upper-bound or stale-gradient approximations that can underperform uniform sampling [22,36], the proposed safe minimax distribution comes with worst-case guarantees relative to bound sets. The CD analysis relates to Gauss-Southwell and approximate steepest coordinate descent [20,32], and to adaptive SDCA probabilities [5,24]. The minimax derivations lean on Sion/Komiya minimax results [29,12].

Strengths
- Technical soundness: Clear derivation of the optimal sampling under bounds via a convex min-max; use of Lemma 2.4 and Theorems 3.2/3.4 establishes guarantees and computes p̂ in O(n log n). The competitive ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5–3.6) provides interpretability.
- Clear and general framework: The approach is generic for CD and SGD, recovers full-information optimal sampling, and provides uniform improvement over fixed Li-based sampling.
- Practicality: Efficient algorithm (sorting-based) and simple safe bound maintenance for GLMs via Cauchy-Schwarz and curvature arguments, with O(n) per-iteration updates. Stepsize coupling αk = 1/vk aligns with expected progress minimization.
- Empirical validation: Consistent CD speedups in iterations and wall-clock time across losses and regularizers; diagnostic plots of vk/Tr[L] corroborate the theoretical picture.

Weaknesses
- Overhead and scalability: Computing p̂ each iteration costs O(n log n), which may be significant for very large n, especially when gradient coordinate evaluation is cheap (small d) or when operations are sparse. The paper argues d = Ω(n) makes the overhead negligible, but a more nuanced analysis and experiments for regimes with d << n would be helpful.
- Bound tightness and generality: For GLMs, Cauchy-Schwarz yields very loose bounds; guarantees hold but could be conservative, and early iterations start near fixed sampling (u0 = ∞). More discussion or ablation on bound quality vs. performance would strengthen the practical story. Extension beyond GLMs or twice-differentiable losses is not explored empirically.
- Comparisons: Experiments primarily compare against uniform, fixed Li-based, and the full-information “optimal” oracle. Missing comparisons to alternative adaptive strategies such as gap-based CD [5,23], Gauss-Southwell rules [20], stale-gradient sampling [22,1], and adaptive SDCA [5] limit the empirical positioning.
- SGD results: Gains for SGD are modest. A deeper analysis of when the approach helps (e.g., variance structure, gradient norm heterogeneity) and strategies to amortize or occasionally update p̂ would be useful.

Quality
The theoretical development appears careful and sound, with appropriate use of minimax theory and convex analysis. Assumptions are standard (convexity, coordinate-wise smoothness). The safe-bounds-to-sampling pipeline is well-justified, and the algorithmic details are precise.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Minor notational inconsistencies and typos could be polished (e.g., overloaded symbols in Algorithm 4, clarity of stepsize choices and their dependence). The geometric interpretation of vk is useful; including a small illustrative example inline (in addition to Appendix B) would improve accessibility.

Originality
The safe minimax formulation for deriving a provably superior adaptive sampling under bounds, together with an efficient algorithm and guarantees, appears novel relative to prior approximations that may lack such guarantees. The coupling with an adaptive stepsize derived from the same minimax is also a neat contribution.

Significance
Given the wide use of CD/SGD and the importance of sampling schemes, a generic, safe, and efficient adaptive method with strong guarantees is valuable. The demonstrated CD improvements suggest practical impact. The approach could inform future adaptive sampling designs and safe screening-style tracking.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Include empirical comparisons to other adaptive CD strategies: duality-gap-based sampling [5,23], Gauss-Southwell/approximate steepest-CD [20,32], and adaptive SDCA [5]. This will better situate the gains.
- Analyze and report overhead vs. d, n, and sparsity; include experiments where d << n and where data are highly sparse. Evaluate periodic recomputation of p̂ (e.g., every T iterations) to amortize O(n log n), and quantify the trade-off.
- Provide an ablation on bound tightness: compare Cauchy-Schwarz, data-driven tighter inner product bounds, and different initialization strategies for (`0, u0), and show impact on vk and convergence.

Optional:
- Discuss memory and implementation details for maintaining `, u at scale; consider streaming or blockwise updates.
- Explore nonconvex objectives (at least empirically) to gauge robustness.
- Release code and scripts for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong and novel theoretical guarantees that the adaptive sampling is always at least as good as fixed Li-based sampling, with a principled minimax foundation.
- Efficient algorithmic instantiation with clear computational complexity and practical bound-maintenance schemes.
- Convincing empirical gains for CD in both iterations and wall-clock time on standard benchmarks.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical comparison to competing adaptive sampling methods, especially gap-based and Gauss-Southwell variants.
- Overhead considerations not fully explored for unfavorable regimes (small d, very large n, high sparsity).
- Modest benefits for SGD without a deeper diagnostic analysis.

Overall
A solid and well-argued contribution with theoretical and practical merit, particularly impactful for coordinate descent. With strengthened comparisons and overhead analysis, this work would be a strong addition to the literature. Recommended for acceptance.
