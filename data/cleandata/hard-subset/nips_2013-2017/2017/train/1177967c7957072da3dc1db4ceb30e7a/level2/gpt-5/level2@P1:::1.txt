Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a practical adaptive importance sampling scheme for randomized optimization methods, notably coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The core idea is to approximate the “optimal” gradient-based sampling—known to maximize a standard quadratic upper-bound model of expected decrease—but without computing full gradients at each iteration. The authors assume per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds on (i) coordinate gradients for CD or (ii) component gradient norms for SGD, and then solve a convex min-max problem to obtain the sampling distribution p̂ and a compatible global stepsize α̂ = 1/vk. They prove that p̂ is optimal w.r.t. the available bounds (best worst-case), is always at least as good as fixed (Li-based) importance sampling and uniform sampling, and recovers the full-information gradient-based sampling when bounds are tight. They give an O(n log n) algorithm (sorting plus a thresholding/water-filling style rule) to compute p̂, and describe how to maintain safe bounds efficiently for generalized linear models (GLMs). Experiments on standard text datasets show substantial gains for CD (both in iterations and time), while gains for SGD are modest.

This work connects to an extensive literature on non-uniform sampling for stochastic and coordinate methods, including fixed importance sampling [Nesterov ’12; Richtárik & Takáč ’14], gradient-based sampling for SGD [Zhao & Zhang ’15; Zhu ’16], gap-based sampling in CD [Perekrestenko et al. ’17; Csiba et al. ’15], and steepest/GS rules [Nutini et al. ’15; Stich et al. ’17]. The novelty here is a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient “safe” approximation to gradient-based sampling via a principled min-max formulation under uncertainty sets defined by safe bounds.

Quality:
- Theory: The paper provides a clear one-step expected decrease model, identifies the optimal adaptive sampling in the full-information case, and formulates the limited-information problem as min_p max_c V(p,c)/||c||^2 with c in a box defined by bounds. Theorem 3.2 (best worst-case sampling and guaranteed improvement over fixed Li-sampling) is a strong and clean result. The constructive O(n log n) algorithm and conditions in Theorem 3.4 are useful. Competitive-ratio bounds are given (Lemmas 3.5–3.6), though the γ^4 bound may be loose.
- Convergence: The main text focuses on one-step progress; full convergence rates are deferred to the appendix and rely on standard techniques. This is acceptable, but a concise statement of end-to-end rates in the main text would strengthen the presentation.
- Practicality: The paper explains how to maintain safe bounds for GLMs, with a simple Cauchy–Schwarz approach. Experiments indicate that even loose bounds suffice to provide gains for CD.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with concrete algorithms (Algs. 1–4), precise assumptions (coordinate-wise smoothness, safe bounds), and a helpful geometric interpretation. A few points would benefit from clarification (see questions below), especially around composite objectives and the cost to maintain bounds in sparse settings. Figures are informative; including more details on time accounting would help.

Originality:
The safe min-max formulation for sampling under uncertainty and the resulting algorithm appear novel. Prior works either compute exact adaptive sampling at high cost, rely on stale values, or use heuristics/upper bounds without the “always better than fixed” guarantee. The best-worst-case guarantee and the explicit construction via sorting/thresholding are new contributions.

Significance:
For CD, the method is compelling: it often approaches the performance of steepest/gradient-based CD while preserving low per-iteration cost, and provably dominates fixed sampling. For SGD, empirical improvements are smaller, so the impact there seems more modest. Nonetheless, the general framework may inspire further adaptive sampling designs and better bound maintenance strategies.

Strengths:
- Principled adaptive sampling under limited information with strong guarantees: optimal w.r.t. bounds and never worse than fixed sampling.
- Efficient O(n log n) algorithm with a simple structure.
- Clear demonstration of substantial practical gains for CD on standard datasets.
- Generic scheme applicable to both CD and SGD; recovers full-information optimal sampling when bounds tighten.
- Concrete guidance on maintaining safe bounds for GLMs.

Weaknesses and points to clarify:
- Computational overhead and scalability: The safe bounds are updated in O(n) per iteration and computing p̂ is O(n log n). For very high-dimensional, sparse problems where the cost of a coordinate update is sublinear in n (proportional to column nnz), the overhead may dominate. The paper argues this is negligible when d = Ω(n); please quantify the regimes (nnz per feature, n, and d) where the overhead is amortized, and report detailed timing breakdowns.
- Composite objectives and nonsmooth regularizers: The CD update in Eq. (2) uses gradient steps on f with coordinate-wise Lipschitz gradients. For L1-regularized problems, is the method applied to the smooth part only (proximal coordinate step), or to the full objective with subgradients? Please clarify the precise setting and state the assumptions used in the L1 experiments.
- Bound tightness and sensitivity: While experiments show good performance with loose bounds, a more systematic study of how bound accuracy affects vk and performance (e.g., controlled synthetic experiments varying γ in Lemma 3.6) would be informative.
- SGD results: Improvements are small. Can the authors provide insight into when the safe scheme is most beneficial for SGD (e.g., heterogeneity of component gradients, bound quality), and/or combine with variance-reduction to amplify gains?
- Convergence guarantees: Please include a concise theorem in the main text summarizing global convergence rates under standard convex assumptions for both CD and SGD variants with the proposed sampling and stepsizes.
- Dependence on Li: In practice, how are Li estimated for general h? For logistic loss, do you use global bounds on h'' or instance-dependent ones?

Minor comments:
- Provide pseudocode for the SGD variant analogous to Algorithm 2.
- Clarify initialization of bounds; with l0=0 and u0=∞, early iterations effectively use uniform sampling, which is fine, but stating this explicitly would help readers.
- Notation: Make clear early that V(p,x) uses |x| entrywise in the argmin characterization (Lemma 2.4).

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled method with strong theoretical guarantees (best worst-case and dominance over fixed sampling).
- Efficient and implementable algorithm; practical guidance for GLMs.
- Significant empirical gains for CD on real datasets.
- Broad relevance to randomized optimization and adaptive sampling.

Cons for acceptance:
- Overhead to maintain bounds/sampling may be significant in some sparse, high-dimensional regimes; needs clearer characterization and timing breakdown.
- Limited empirical gains for SGD; theoretical discussion for SGD is lighter in the main text.
- Some ambiguity around handling nonsmooth regularizers and composite settings.

Overall assessment:
A solid and thoughtful contribution that fills a gap between infeasible gradient-based adaptive sampling and fixed distributions, with clean theory and convincing CD results. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifications regarding composite objectives and a more detailed analysis of computational overhead and bound maintenance in sparse regimes.
