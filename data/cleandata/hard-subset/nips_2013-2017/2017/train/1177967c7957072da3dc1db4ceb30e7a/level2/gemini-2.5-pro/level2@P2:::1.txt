### Summary

This paper proposes a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The key challenge with adaptive sampling is that the optimal sampling distribution, which is often based on gradient magnitudes, requires full gradient information at every iteration, rendering it computationally prohibitive. Existing approximations can lack theoretical guarantees and may even perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The authors' main contribution is a "safe" adaptive sampling method that is provably at least as good as standard fixed importance sampling. The core idea is to work with efficiently computable safe lower and upper bounds on the gradient components. Instead of using an ad-hoc heuristic like sampling based on the upper bounds, the authors formulate the problem of choosing a sampling distribution as a minimax optimization problem. They seek a distribution that minimizes the worst-case value of the convergence upper bound, taken over all possible gradients consistent with the known bounds. The paper presents a solution to this problem, along with an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm to compute the sampling distribution. The proposed scheme is generic and is demonstrated on Generalized Linear Models (GLMs), where the required gradient bounds can be maintained with linear overhead per iteration. Extensive experiments on several real-world datasets show that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time, and performs nearly as well as the ideal (but computationally infeasible) optimal sampling.

This work fits well within the literature on accelerating first-order methods. It builds on prior work on adaptive importance sampling for CD (e.g., [5, 23]) and SGD (e.g., [22, 36]). The main point of departure and novelty is the principled minimax formulation for handling uncertainty in the gradient information, which provides a safety guarantee that was absent in prior approximation schemes.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The central idea of framing the selection of a sampling distribution as a minimax problem (Equation 7) is highly original and theoretically sound. It provides an elegant way to handle the uncertainty from having only bounds on the gradient, moving beyond heuristic approximations used in previous work.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper's claim of being "safe" is well-substantiated. Theorem 3.2 proves that the proposed sampling is always better than standard `L_i`-based importance sampling (which includes uniform sampling as a special case). This is a significant result, as it gives practitioners confidence that the method will not underperform simple baselines, a key concern with other adaptive schemes.

3.  **Computational Efficiency:** The authors provide an efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve their minimax problem. As they correctly argue, for many problems (e.g., where feature dimension `d` is large), this overhead is negligible compared to the cost of a single coordinate gradient computation, making the method highly practical. This is well supported by the timing experiments in Figures 6 and 9.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering different loss functions (square, squared hinge, logistic) and regularizers (L1, L2) on multiple standard datasets. The results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. It nearly matches the iteration complexity of the ideal "optimal" sampling and significantly outperforms uniform/fixed sampling in wall-clock time. The plots showing the evolution of the stepsize parameter `v_k` provide excellent insight into how the method adapts and achieves its speedup.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The performance of the method is necessarily tied to the quality of the safe bounds `l` and `u`. The approach presented in Section 4 for GLMs relies on a simple Cauchy-Schwarz inequality, which can be very loose. While the experiments show this is sufficient to achieve good performance, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion on the sensitivity of the method to bound tightness. It is unclear how the method would perform if the bounds were consistently loose (e.g., `l` close to 0 and `u` very large).

2.  **Limited Discussion on Broader Applicability:** The paper demonstrates how to derive bounds for GLMs, which is a very important class of models. However, a brief discussion on the challenges or potential strategies for applying this framework to other models, such as neural networks, would increase the paper's perceived impact. The generic nature of the sampling algorithm is a strength, but its utility hinges on the ability to efficiently maintain meaningful bounds for the problem at hand.

### Constructive Suggestions

*   The intuition behind Algorithm 4 could be made clearer in the main text. While the proof is rightly deferred to the appendix, a brief, high-level walkthrough of how the algorithm constructs the worst-case vector `c` by iteratively fixing components to their bounds `u` or `l` based on a threshold `m` would be very helpful for the reader.

*   The paper astutely observes that the performance gains for SGD are less pronounced than for CD. It would be insightful if the authors could offer some hypotheses for this. Does the difference in the structure of the optimal sampling distribution (`||∇fi(x)||` for SGD vs. `sqrt(L_i)|∇i f(x)|` for CD) play a role? Or is it related to how the bounds on vector norms evolve compared to bounds on scalar components?

*   The competitive ratio analysis in Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6 is a nice theoretical addition. However, its practical utility is not immediately obvious, especially since computing `w_k` is a non-convex problem. A short comment on how these results might inform the choice of algorithm or provide insight would be beneficial.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of stochastic optimization. The problem is important, the proposed solution is elegant and well-motivated, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the experimental results are convincing. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the core contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
