Summary
The paper proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling scheme for randomized optimization methods, primarily coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The key idea is to choose sampling probabilities using only interval bounds on gradient magnitudes (or component gradient norms in finite-sum problems), thereby avoiding full gradient evaluations. The sampling distribution is defined as the solution of a minimax problem: minimize over probabilities p the worst-case quadratic upper bound on expected decrease (V(p,c)/||c||^2) across all gradients c consistent with the bounds. The authors prove that:
- The resulting sampling p̂ is optimal for the given uncertainty set (best worst-case guarantee).
- It is never worse (in the same model-based sense) than uniform/fixed Li-based sampling, and recovers gradient-based sampling as bounds collapse.
- The solution admits an O(n log n) algorithm via a thresholding/water-filling–like construction.

They show for CD that the adaptive gradient-based sampling (with step-size α chosen to minimize the upper bound) yields potentially up to n-fold one-step progress over uniform. They instantiate efficient safe bounds for GLMs (both CD and SGD) based on Hessian structure and Cauchy–Schwarz. Experiments on several datasets show substantial speedups for CD versus uniform/fixed sampling, with less pronounced gains for SGD.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself among fixed importance sampling (e.g., p ∝ Li), adaptive importance sampling using (stale) gradients for SGD (Zhao & Zhang 2015; Needell et al. 2014), gap-based adaptivity for CD (e.g., Osokin et al.; Csiba et al.), and recent non-uniform CD (Qu & Richtárik; Allen-Zhu et al.; Perekrestenko et al.). The novelty here is the robust optimization formulation using safe bounds, guaranteeing improvement over fixed sampling without requiring full gradient access. The analysis is consistent with the quadratic-upper-bound model used in prior adaptive sampling works, but the minimax perspective and efficient construction appear new.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A principled min–max formulation for adaptive sampling under bounded gradient information, with clear properties (best worst-case, dominance over fixed sampling).
- Solid theory: Clean one-step progress bounds; recovery of full-information optimal sampling; geometric characterization; competitive ratio analysis; algorithm to compute p̂ in O(n log n).
- Practicality for CD: Efficient bound maintenance for GLMs via tracked inner products; plug-and-play integration with unbiased updates (α/p_i scaling).
- Empirical evidence: On multiple datasets and losses, CD with the proposed sampling achieves near-optimal (full-gradient) performance with small overhead and clear wall-clock gains; visualization of vk/Tr[L] helps interpret improvement.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scalability for SGD questionable: The proposed safe-bound maintenance for finite-sum SGD requires O(n) updates per iteration (to update bounds for all components), which can nullify the point of SGD. This limitation is acknowledged indirectly, and experiments indeed show only modest improvements. A more explicit discussion of when the approach is viable for SGD, or strategies to amortize/update bounds less frequently, would strengthen the claims.
- Overhead vs problem structure: For CD on high-dimensional sparse text data, n (#coordinates) can exceed d (#samples). The stated overhead O(n log n) per iteration may dominate the O(d) cost of a coordinate update when d ≪ n, contrary to the “negligible extra cost” statement. The paper could more carefully quantify regimes (sparsity, d/n ratios) where the method is actually time-efficient.
- Guarantees are model-based: The “always better than uniform/fixed” claim is in terms of the quadratic model (1) and worst-case over bounds Ck. While standard, it would help to discuss how loose bounds affect realized progress and whether there are instances where the robust choice is overly conservative.
- Limited baselines: For CD, a comparison against Gauss–Southwell and approximate steepest-CD heuristics (e.g., Stich et al., ICML’17) would be informative. For SGD, comparisons to practical adaptive sampling using stale gradients or mini-batch importance sampling would help contextualize the modest gains.
- Assumptions on Li: The method relies on coordinate-wise Lipschitz constants. Estimating Li can be non-trivial for some models; practical guidance or adaptive estimation would be useful.

Quality
The theoretical development is sound and well-motivated. The O(n log n) construction (Algorithm 4) is plausible; the characterization via thresholding and sorting is convincing. The competitive ratio bounds are somewhat loose (e.g., γ^4) but acceptable as worst-case. The empirical section supports the main CD claims; SGD results highlight limitations.

Clarity
Overall clear and well-organized. Suggestions:
- More explicit per-iteration complexity tables for CD vs SGD, including dependence on sparsity and d/n.
- A brief intuitive derivation of the minimax problem equivalence (√v = max ||√Lc||1/||c||2) in the main text.
- Clarify how often bounds are updated in practice and how updates interact with sparsity.

Originality
The robust-safe formulation and its efficient solution appear novel. The idea of using uncertainty sets for gradients to derive sampling distributions is a meaningful step beyond stale or heuristic approximations.

Significance
For CD on common GLMs, the method is a practical improvement with provable dominance over fixed sampling and strong empirical gains. For SGD, impact is more limited unless bound maintenance can be made sublinear or amortized.

Constructive suggestions
- For SGD, explore epoch-level updates of bounds, block updates, or sampling subsets to update per step; provide timing results including bound-updating overhead.
- Include comparisons with approximate Gauss–Southwell/steepest-CD heuristics and stale-gradient sampling.
- Discuss adaptive or data-driven estimation of Li and sensitivity to mis-specification.
- Provide an open-source implementation and profiling of overheads (sorting, bound updates) vs gradient computation across datasets with varying n,d,sparsity.
- Extend to block/mini-batch variants and asynchronous/distributed settings; the safe-bounds approach may be especially suitable there.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled adaptive sampling under uncertainty with clear guarantees.
- Efficient algorithm and practical integration for CD.
- Strong empirical improvements for CD; thorough analysis.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited practicality for SGD due to O(n) bound updates; modest empirical gains there.
- Overhead can be non-negligible when n ≫ d; need clearer guidance on when it pays off.
- Missing some practical baselines and more detailed complexity profiling.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Solid theoretical contribution and practical impact for coordinate descent; SGD claims should be tempered or supported by additional engineering to reduce overhead.

Confidence
High on the theoretical assessment; moderate on empirical generality beyond GLMs and on SGD practicality.
