This paper addresses the important and practical problem of designing efficient adaptive importance sampling schemes for large-scale optimization methods like Coordinate Descent (CD) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). While it is known that adapting the sampling distribution based on full gradient information can lead to significant speedups, computing this optimal distribution is often prohibitively expensive. The authors propose a novel "safe" adaptive sampling scheme that relies only on efficiently maintainable lower and upper bounds on the gradient information.

The core idea is to formulate the selection of the sampling distribution as a min-max optimization problem. Given the uncertainty captured by the lower and upper bounds on the gradient vector, the goal is to find a distribution that minimizes the worst-case variance term in the convergence analysis. This provides a principled and robust way to handle the limited information. The authors present an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to solve this min-max problem. Crucially, they prove that the resulting sampling distribution is guaranteed to perform better than standard fixed importance sampling schemes (e.g., uniform or Lipshitz-based sampling), a guarantee that was missing from prior work using similar bounding techniques. The paper demonstrates how to derive and update the required bounds for the important class of Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provides extensive empirical evidence showing that the proposed method significantly outperforms non-adaptive methods in both iteration count and wall-clock time.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem in large-scale machine learning. The formulation of the sampling problem as a min-max optimization problem (Eq. 7) is elegant, novel, and provides a theoretically sound way to derive an adaptive sampling distribution under uncertainty. This "safe" or "robust" approach is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical results are strong. The main result (Theorem 3.2), which proves that the proposed sampling scheme is always better than fixed Li-based sampling, is a powerful guarantee that distinguishes this work from previous heuristic or approximate adaptive methods. The provision of an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the non-trivial min-max problem makes the approach practical.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It effectively motivates the problem by first presenting the "ideal" but infeasible full-information sampling, and then developing the proposed "safe" limited-information alternative as a practical and principled approximation.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The method is shown to achieve convergence rates (in terms of iterations) close to the impractical optimal sampling scheme, while the timing results confirm that the computational overhead is low enough to yield significant wall-clock time improvements over fixed sampling.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Comparison to Other Practical Adaptive Methods:** The experiments compare the proposed method primarily against uniform sampling and the computationally expensive "optimal" sampling. While this effectively demonstrates the benefits over non-adaptive methods, a comparison against other practical adaptive schemes, such as those using stale gradients (e.g., Alain et al., 2015) or other bound-based heuristics (e.g., Zhao & Zhang, 2015), would be very valuable. This would help to better position the proposed method within the landscape of existing *practical* solutions.

2.  **Clarity of Algorithm 4:** While the algorithm for computing the safe sampling is a key contribution, its presentation in Algorithm 4 is quite dense and may be difficult for readers to parse. The main text could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the algorithm's mechanics, perhaps with a small worked example. The role and efficient update of the variable `m` inside the loop, in particular, could be elaborated upon for better clarity.

3.  **Discussion on Overhead and Scalability:** The paper argues that the O(n log n) overhead for computing the sampling is often negligible. While the timing plots support this for the tested settings, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs would be welcome. For instance, in very high-dimensional but sparse problems (like `news20`), the cost of a single coordinate gradient update can be very small (proportional to non-zeros), potentially making the O(n log n) cost more significant. A brief discussion of these different regimes would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Generality of Gradient Bounds:** The method for maintaining safe gradient bounds is demonstrated for GLMs. While this is an important and large class of models, the paper's claim of generality would be stronger with a brief discussion on the applicability and challenges of deriving such bounds for other models of interest at NIPS, such as neural networks or matrix factorization models.

5.  **Weaker SGD Performance:** The authors note that the performance gains for SGD are less pronounced than for CD. This is an interesting finding. Is there an intuitive or theoretical reason for this difference? For example, does the noise inherent in the SGD update interact with the sampling in a fundamentally different way? Expanding on this observation would add more depth to the experimental analysis.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Pros:**
    *   Novel and principled min-max formulation for adaptive sampling under uncertainty.
    *   Strong theoretical guarantee: The proposed method is provably better than standard fixed sampling.
    *   An efficient algorithm is provided to implement the method.
    *   Convincing empirical results on relevant machine learning problems.

*   **Cons:**
    *   Experimental comparison to other practical adaptive sampling methods is missing.
    *   The presentation of the core algorithm (Algorithm 4) could be clearer.
    *   The discussion on computational overhead and applicability beyond GLMs could be expanded.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a strong and novel contribution to an important area. The proposed "safe" sampling method is theoretically well-founded, practical, and empirically effective. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed to further improve the paper. I recommend acceptance.
