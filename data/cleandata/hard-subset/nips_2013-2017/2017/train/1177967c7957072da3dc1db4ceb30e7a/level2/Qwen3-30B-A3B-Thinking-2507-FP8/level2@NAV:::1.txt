

This paper proposes a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, addressing a key limitation of existing adaptive importance sampling approaches: the computational infeasibility of computing optimal gradient-based sampling distributions. The authors demonstrate that their approach, which uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling while maintaining computational efficiency.

The main contributions are well-structured and theoretically sound. The paper establishes that the proposed sampling distribution is the best possible with respect to the given bounds (Theorem 3.2), always outperforms fixed sampling, and can be computed efficiently in O(n log n) time per iteration. The theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful proofs of key properties and a clear connection to existing work on adaptive sampling (e.g., referencing Zhao & Zhang [36]). The empirical evaluation on standard datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with various loss functions and regularizers provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness, particularly for CD where speedups are significant.

The paper's greatest strength is its theoretical guarantee that the proposed sampling never performs worse than fixed importance sampling, a property not established for previous approximation schemes. The computational complexity analysis is convincing, showing that the overhead is minimal compared to the cost of gradient evaluations in practice. The examples of safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models (Section 4) demonstrate practical applicability.

A minor limitation is that the empirical benefits are less pronounced for SGD compared to CD, which the authors acknowledge but don't fully explore. While the paper provides sufficient details for reproducibility, the practical impact on very large-scale distributed systems could be further investigated. The writing is clear and well-organized, with helpful pseudocode and theoretical results.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important practical challenge in large-scale optimization. The theoretical guarantees are significant, and the empirical results support the claims. The paper advances the state of the art in adaptive importance sampling with a practical, provably better solution that can be easily integrated into existing algorithms. I recommend acceptance.
