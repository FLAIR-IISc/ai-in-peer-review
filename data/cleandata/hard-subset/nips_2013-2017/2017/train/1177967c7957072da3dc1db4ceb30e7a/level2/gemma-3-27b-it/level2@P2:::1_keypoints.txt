- Proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme to speed up coordinate descent (CD) and SGD by approximating gradient-based sampling using gradient bounds.  
- Ensures provable improvement over uniform/fixed importance sampling while remaining computationally efficient.  
- Provides strong theoretical guarantees and rigorous analysis showing superiority to static sampling.  
- Method designed with low overhead, making it practical for large-scale problems.  
- Scheme is generic and easily integrable into existing CD and SGD algorithms.  
- Empirical validation on real datasets shows significant speedups, especially for coordinate descent.  
- “Safe” approximation guarantees at least as good performance as static sampling, addressing risks of adaptive methods.  
- Clear writing, good motivation, logical presentation, and strong connection to existing importance-sampling and CD literature.  
- Relies on accurate gradient bounds (l and u); performance sensitive to bound quality and lacks full guidance for complex settings.  
- Empirical gains for SGD are modest compared to CD, and the paper doesn’t fully explain this discrepancy.  
- Competitive ratio (ρk) discussion is interesting but feels disconnected and lacks intuitive explanation; conditions for good ratio seem restrictive.  
- Experiments focus mainly on GLMs; broader evaluation (e.g., deep learning) would strengthen claims of generality.  
- Minor notation issues (e.g., unconventional [x]i) and some condensed equation derivations (notably Equation (3)).  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, theoretically sound, computationally efficient, compelling CD results, and well-presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: SGD results less convincing, sensitivity to gradient-bound estimation, and limited empirical scope.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to better discuss gradient-bound estimation and further investigate SGD performance.
