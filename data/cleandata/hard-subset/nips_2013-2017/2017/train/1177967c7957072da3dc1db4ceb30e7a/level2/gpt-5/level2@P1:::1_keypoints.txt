- Proposes a practical adaptive importance-sampling scheme for CD and SGD that approximates full-gradient sampling using per-iteration safe lower/upper bounds and a convex min-max formulation.
- Shows the estimator p̂ and stepsize α̂=1/vk are optimal w.r.t. available bounds, always at least as good as fixed Li-based and uniform sampling, and recover full-information sampling when bounds are tight.
- Provides an O(n log n) constructive algorithm (sorting + thresholding/water-filling) to compute p̂ and describes efficient bound maintenance for GLMs.
- Empirical results: substantial iteration/time gains for CD on text datasets; modest improvements for SGD.
- Theory: clear one-step expected-decrease model, strong Theorem 3.2 (best worst-case sampling and dominance over fixed sampling), and useful constructive conditions in Theorem 3.4.
- Provides competitive-ratio bounds (Lemmas 3.5–3.6), though the γ^4 dependence may be loose.
- Convergence analysis focuses on one-step progress; full convergence rates are deferred to the appendix—adding a concise main-text rate theorem would strengthen the paper.
- Practicality: explains simple Cauchy–Schwarz bound updates for GLMs and shows loose bounds can still yield CD gains.
- Computational overhead: bounds update is O(n) per iteration and computing p̂ is O(n log n), which may dominate for very high-dimensional sparse problems where coordinate updates cost is sublinear.
- Requests quantification of regimes (nnz per feature, n, d) where overhead is amortized and detailed timing breakdowns.
- Asks clarification on composite objectives/nonsmooth regularizers (e.g., L1): whether method applies to smooth part only (proximal CD) or to full objective with subgradients, and assumptions used in L1 experiments.
- Suggests a systematic study of bound tightness/sensitivity (e.g., controlled experiments varying γ) to show how bound accuracy affects vk and performance.
- Notes SGD improvements are small; requests insight on when the safe scheme helps SGD (gradient heterogeneity, bound quality) and potential combination with variance reduction.
- Requests a concise convergence theorem in the main text summarizing global rates for both CD and SGD with the proposed sampling and stepsizes.
- Asks how Li are estimated in practice for general h (global vs. instance-dependent bounds), with specifics for logistic loss.
- Clarity is generally good with concrete algorithms and helpful geometry, but asks for more details on time accounting and sparse-cost considerations.
- Originality: the safe min-max formulation and the sorting/thresholding construction appear novel compared to prior adaptive or heuristic samplings.
- Significance: very compelling for CD (near steepest performance at low cost), more modest impact for SGD; framework could inspire further adaptive designs.
- Strengths: principled limited-information adaptive sampling, best-worst-case guarantee, O(n log n) algorithm, recovers full-information sampling, practical GLM guidance, strong CD empirical gains.
- Weaknesses: potential overhead in sparse/high-dim settings, limited SGD gains, ambiguity on nonsmooth/composite handling, and need for clearer bounds/estimation practices.
- Minor requests: provide pseudocode for the SGD variant, clarify initialization of bounds (e.g., l0=0, u0=∞ implies initial uniform sampling), and clarify notation (V(p,x) uses |x| entrywise).
- Pros for acceptance: novel theory with strong guarantees, efficient implementable algorithm, convincing CD experiments, broad relevance.
- Cons for acceptance: need clearer characterization/timing of overhead in sparse regimes, light empirical/theoretical treatment for SGD, and ambiguity on composite settings.
- Overall assessment: solid, novel contribution bridging infeasible gradient-based sampling and fixed distributions with clean theory and strong CD results; recommend acceptance contingent on clarifications about composite objectives and computational overhead in sparse regimes.
