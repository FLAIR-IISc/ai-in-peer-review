- Proposed SAIS: adaptive sampling using safe lower/upper bounds on per-coordinate gradients via a convex min–max to approximate gradient-based sampling.  
- Key properties: worst-case optimal within bounds, never worse than fixed Li-based/uniform sampling, computable in O(n log n).  
- Unifies and improves prior adaptive schemes that need full gradients or use crude/stale bounds; recovers optimal gradient sampling with full information.  
- Theoretical development is careful: Sion’s minimax used, existence/optimality proven, and a constructive O(n log n) solver (Algorithm 4).  
- Meaningful guarantees: best worst-case within bounds, dominance over fixed sampling, and competitive-ratio bounds.  
- Empirical results support theory on GLMs (rcv1, real-sim, news20), showing clear gains for CD and smaller gains for SGD.  
- Experimental issues: timings reported on different machines, very loose bounds used, and missing variance bars reduce interpretability.  
- Clarity: generally well written with clear claims; Algorithm 4 is dense and would benefit from an intuitive derivation and a figure illustrating thresholding.  
- Presentation suggestion: unify CD and SGD treatments earlier to improve flow.  
- Originality: novel formulation of “safe bounds + min–max optimal sampling” and efficient solver; conceptually advances worst-case optimality under uncertainty.  
- Significance: practical for large-scale convex learning with negligible overhead when d = Ω(n); guarantees that SAIS is never worse than fixed importance sampling.  
- Suggested experiments/analysis: assess sensitivity to bound tightness, compare Cauchy–Schwarz vs tighter curvature/screening bounds, and include same-hardware timings with variance bars.  
- Baselines and robustness: explicitly compare to stale-gradient/upper-bound sampling and duality-gap CD sampling; discuss robustness when bounds are violated and propose conservative fixes.  
- Extensions requested: analysis/experiments for minibatching and parallel/distributed settings.  
- Pros: strong theory with safety guarantees, efficient algorithm, clear CD improvements, generic and easy to integrate.  
- Cons: limited evaluation breadth and weaker SGD gains, dependence on quality of bounds, and some experimental reporting issues.
