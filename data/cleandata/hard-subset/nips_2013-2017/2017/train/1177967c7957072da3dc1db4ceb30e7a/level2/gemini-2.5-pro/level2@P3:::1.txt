This paper presents a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The primary challenge with optimal adaptive sampling (e.g., gradient-based) is its prohibitive computational cost, as it requires full gradient information at each iteration. Existing approximations based on stale information or bounds can potentially perform worse than simple uniform sampling.

The core contribution of this work is a "safe" adaptive sampling method that relies on efficiently computable lower and upper bounds on the gradient information. Instead of using a point estimate from these bounds, the paper proposes a principled min-max optimization formulation. This approach finds a sampling distribution that is optimal in the worst case over all possible true gradient vectors consistent with the bounds. The key theoretical result is that this "safe" sampling distribution is provably never worse than fixed importance sampling (including uniform sampling) and is the best possible choice given the available bound information. An efficient O(n log n) algorithm is provided to compute this distribution. The paper demonstrates how to derive and maintain the necessary bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs) and provides extensive empirical evidence on several real-world datasets, showing significant performance improvements over standard baselines.

**Strengths and Reasons for Acceptance:**

1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The central idea of formulating the choice of sampling distribution as a min-max problem over an uncertainty set (defined by the gradient bounds) is highly original and elegant. It directly addresses the shortcomings of prior heuristics that could underperform, providing a robust and theoretically sound foundation for approximating the optimal sampling.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The work provides a crucial "safety" guarantee (Theorem 3.2), proving that the proposed sampling is always better than standard fixed importance sampling. This is a significant advantage over previous adaptive methods based on bounds, which lacked such a guarantee. The analysis leading to the efficient `O(n log n)` algorithm for solving the min-max problem (Theorem 3.4) is also a solid technical contribution.

3.  **Practicality and Broad Applicability:** The proposed method is generic and can be readily integrated into existing CD and SGD frameworks. The paper provides a concrete and practical example of how to maintain the required gradient bounds for the important class of GLMs with only a linear-time update cost per iteration. The overall `O(n log n)` overhead is often negligible compared to the per-coordinate gradient computation cost, especially for high-dimensional data.

4.  **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough, covering multiple datasets, loss functions, and regularizers for both CD and SGD. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed safe adaptive sampling significantly outperforms uniform and fixed sampling in both iteration count and wall-clock time. The inclusion of the (computationally expensive) optimal sampling as a reference point effectively shows that the proposed method comes very close to this ideal performance, validating the quality of the approximation.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Dependence on Bound Quality:** The effectiveness of the method relies on the availability of reasonably tight gradient bounds. The paper uses a straightforward application of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality for GLMs. While effective in the experiments, these bounds could be quite loose in some scenarios (e.g., for non-normalized data), potentially diminishing the performance gains. The paper could be strengthened by including a discussion on the relationship between the tightness of the bounds (`u_k / l_k`) and the quality of the resulting sampling distribution (e.g., relating it to the competitive ratio `rho_k`). This would provide users with better intuition about when the method is most likely to be effective.

2.  **Overhead for "Short and Fat" Data:** The `O(n log n)` overhead for computing the sampling distribution, while often manageable, could become a bottleneck for problems where the number of examples/coordinates `n` is extremely large and the cost of a single gradient computation (proportional to dimension `d`) is small. A brief discussion of this limitation and the problem regimes where the overhead is negligible versus those where it might be significant would make the paper more complete.

3.  **Discrepancy between CD and SGD Performance:** The empirical results show a more dramatic improvement for CD than for SGD. While the method still outperforms the baselines for SGD, the gap is smaller. The paper would benefit from a short discussion on potential reasons for this. Is it because the bounds on vector norms (for SGD) are inherently looser than bounds on scalar gradient components (for CD)? Or does the nature of the SGD update interact differently with the adaptive sampling?

**Qualitative Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical proofs (provided in the appendix) and strong experimental results. The work is complete and presents a well-rounded contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly, and the logical flow from the ideal but infeasible optimal sampling to the proposed practical and safe approximation is easy to follow. The algorithms and key results are presented clearly.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While building on the rich literature of importance sampling, the min-max formulation to guarantee safety and robustness is a novel and significant conceptual leap. It clearly distinguishes this work from prior art.
*   **Significance:** The results are important and likely to be impactful. The method addresses a key practical bottleneck in large-scale optimization. Given its strong theoretical guarantees and demonstrated efficiency, the proposed sampling scheme is a compelling choice for practitioners and a valuable building block for future research in optimization.
