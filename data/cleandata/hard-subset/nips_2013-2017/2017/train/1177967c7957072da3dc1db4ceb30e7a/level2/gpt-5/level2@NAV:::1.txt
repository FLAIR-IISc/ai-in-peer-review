Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling (SAIS), an adaptive sampling strategy for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that approximates gradient-based sampling using safe lower/upper bounds on per-coordinate (or per-component) gradient magnitudes. The key idea is to select probabilities by solving a convex min–max problem over a box of admissible gradients, yielding a distribution that is (i) optimal in the worst case given the bounds, (ii) guaranteed to be no worse than fixed Li-based/ uniform sampling, and (iii) computable in O(n log n). This unifies and improves upon prior adaptive schemes that either require full gradients [Zhao & Zhang ’15; Zhu ’16] or rely on crude upper bounds/stale values [Needell et al. ’14; Schmidt et al. ’15], and relates to duality-gap-based CD sampling [Perekrestenko et al. ’17; Csiba et al. ’15]. The paper also shows that with full information the scheme recovers optimal gradient-based sampling.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful. The min–max formulation, existence/optimality via Sion’s minimax, and the constructive O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) are solid. The guarantees (best worst-case within bounds; dominance over fixed sampling; competitive ratio bounds) are meaningful. The empirical study on GLMs (rcv1, real-sim, news20) supports the theory, especially for CD; for SGD the gains are smaller, consistent with prior observations. Some experimental choices reduce interpretability (timings on different machines, very loose bounds), but overall support is adequate for a conference paper.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear statements of claims and assumptions, and helpful comparisons to uniform/fixed sampling. Algorithm 4 is dense; a short intuitive derivation and a figure illustrating the thresholding condition (Eq. 9) would help. Unifying the CD/SGD presentations earlier would also improve flow.

Originality: The “safe bounds + min–max optimal sampling” formulation and its efficient solver appear novel relative to prior adaptive importance sampling methods that use point estimates or heuristics. The link to worst-case optimality within a bounded uncertainty set is a clear conceptual advance.

Significance: Practical and broadly applicable to large-scale convex learning, with negligible overhead when d = Ω(n). The guarantees that SAIS is never worse than fixed importance sampling are compelling.

Limitations and suggestions: 
- Empirically assess sensitivity to bound tightness; compare naïve Cauchy–Schwarz bounds to tighter curvature/screening-based bounds. 
- Provide results on the same hardware and add variance bars. 
- Compare against stale-gradient and upper-bound sampling baselines explicitly, and duality-gap CD sampling. 
- Discuss robustness when bounds are occasionally violated; propose conservative corrections. 
- Extend analysis to minibatching and parallel/distributed settings (motivated in the text).

Pros:
- Strong theory with safety guarantees and efficient algorithm.
- Clear improvements over uniform/fixed sampling, especially for CD.
- Generic and easy to integrate.

Cons:
- Limited evaluation breadth; weaker gains for SGD.
- Dependence on quality of bounds; little sensitivity analysis.
- Some experimental reporting issues (timing comparability).
