## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper addresses the challenge of efficiently implementing adaptive importance sampling (AIS) for large-scale optimization, specifically within coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) frameworks. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, ensuring theoretical guarantees and practical efficiency. 

The paper clearly identifies the limitations of existing adaptive strategies – computational cost and potential performance degradation compared to uniform sampling – and proposes a novel approach that mitigates these issues. The authors demonstrate that their proposed sampling scheme is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling and recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting.  The theoretical contributions, particularly the analysis of CD with adaptive sampling (showing a potential speed-up factor of *n*), are significant. The connection to existing work on gradient-based sampling [22, 36, 37] and duality gap-based sampling [5, 23] is well-established.

The strengths of the paper lie in its rigorous theoretical analysis and the clear presentation of the proposed algorithm. The authors provide a practical algorithm for computing the sampling distribution (Algorithm 4) with a reasonable computational complexity (O(n log n)). The empirical evaluation on several datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed scheme, particularly for coordinate descent. The inclusion of plots showing the evolution of *vk* (the scaling factor for the stepsize) provides valuable insight into the algorithm's behavior.

However, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the assumptions required for the safe gradient bounds. While the authors provide an example for GLMs, a broader discussion of applicability to other model classes would be beneficial. The performance gain in SGD was less pronounced than in CD, and a more detailed analysis of this discrepancy would be helpful.  Additionally, the paper could benefit from a comparison to other recent adaptive sampling techniques, such as those based on variance reduction.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and guarantees.
*   Novel and potentially impactful approach to adaptive sampling.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Empirical validation on real datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the applicability of the safe gradient bound assumption.
*   Less significant performance gains observed in SGD compared to CD.
*   Lack of comparison to other recent adaptive sampling techniques.

**Overall:**

This is a solid contribution with a clear and well-motivated approach. While there are minor areas for improvement, the theoretical and empirical results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding the assumptions and comparison to related work. The potential impact of this work on large-scale optimization algorithms is significant.
