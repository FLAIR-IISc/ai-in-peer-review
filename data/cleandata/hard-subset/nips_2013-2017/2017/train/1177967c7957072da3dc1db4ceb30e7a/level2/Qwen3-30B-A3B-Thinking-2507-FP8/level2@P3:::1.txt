

This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) that efficiently approximates gradient-based sampling while maintaining theoretical guarantees. The key insight is to formulate the sampling distribution as a convex optimization problem with respect to safe upper and lower bounds on gradient information, rather than requiring full gradient computation in each iteration. The proposed method provably outperforms uniform and fixed importance sampling while maintaining computational efficiency.

The paper makes several significant theoretical contributions. Theorem 3.2 establishes that the proposed sampling distribution is always better than Li-based sampling (which includes uniform sampling as a special case), and Theorem 3.4 provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the sampling distribution. The paper also demonstrates how to efficiently maintain safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models (GLMs), showing that the overhead per iteration is manageable (O(n log n)) compared to the cost of gradient evaluation.

The empirical evaluation on real datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) with L1 and L2 regularization demonstrates practical benefits. For coordinate descent, the adaptive sampling significantly outperforms fixed importance sampling in both iteration count and total computation time. The results show that even with very loose safe gradient bounds (derived from Cauchy-Schwarz), the method achieves substantial improvement over static sampling. The paper also provides a clear illustration of the theoretical guarantees through the evolution of the vk parameter, which determines the adaptive stepsize.

The paper's theoretical analysis is technically sound and well-structured. The connection between adaptive sampling and the optimal sampling distribution (Example 2.3) is clearly established, and the extension to safe bounds (Section 3) represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches that use approximate gradient bounds. The authors effectively address the tension between computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees, which is a critical challenge in adaptive importance sampling.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the safe bounds approach. While the GLM case is well-handled, the applicability to more complex models beyond GLMs is not fully explored. The empirical results for SGD (Figures 7-9) show less dramatic improvement compared to CD, but the paper does not sufficiently explain why this difference occurs or provide guidance on when the method might be particularly beneficial for SGD applications.

The presentation is clear and well-organized, with sufficient detail to allow for reproducibility. The notation is consistent, and the theoretical results are properly contextualized within the literature (e.g., comparing with Zhao and Zhang 2015 for SGD). The paper's contributions are clearly articulated in the introduction, and the experimental setup is well-motivated.

In terms of significance, the proposed method offers a practical and theoretically grounded approach to adaptive importance sampling that can be integrated into existing optimization frameworks with minimal overhead. For large-scale optimization problems where computational efficiency is paramount, this work provides a valuable tool that bridges the gap between theoretically optimal but computationally expensive adaptive schemes and the more practical but less effective fixed sampling approaches.

The paper's main contribution is the safe adaptive sampling scheme that maintains theoretical guarantees while being computationally feasible. This addresses a key limitation in previous adaptive sampling methods, which either lacked theoretical guarantees or were computationally prohibitive. The work advances the state of the art in optimization for large-scale machine learning problems, particularly for coordinate descent methods.

A minor weakness is that the paper focuses primarily on GLMs, and while it acknowledges that the approach could be extended to other models, it does not provide specific examples or discuss how the safe bound maintenance would work for non-GLM problems. This limits the perceived scope of the work, though the methodology itself is general.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the competitive ratio (ρk) in practical settings, particularly for cases where the bounds are tight versus loose. The theoretical bounds on ρk (Lemma 3.6) appear conservative compared to the empirical results, and a deeper analysis of this gap would strengthen the paper.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the method is efficiently implemented, and the empirical results demonstrate practical benefits. The paper meets all the NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), offers a novel approach to a well-known problem (Originality), and provides a practical solution with demonstrable advantages (Significance).

The work is significant because it resolves a fundamental tradeoff in adaptive importance sampling between computational efficiency and theoretical guarantees. The proposed method can be readily integrated into existing CD and SGD implementations with minimal overhead, making it immediately useful for practitioners. The paper represents a substantial step forward in making adaptive importance sampling both theoretically justified and computationally feasible for large-scale machine learning applications.
