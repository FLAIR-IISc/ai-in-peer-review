

This paper proposes a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, addressing a key limitation of prior adaptive sampling approaches: computational infeasibility due to requiring full gradient evaluations. The core contribution is a provably optimal sampling distribution derived from safe lower/upper bounds on gradient magnitudes, which avoids the pitfalls of previous approximations (e.g., stale gradients or naive bounds that can degrade performance below uniform sampling). The authors prove that their scheme (Algorithm 2) is always superior to fixed importance sampling (Example 2.2) and recovers the optimal gradient-based sampling (Algorithm 1) in the full-information limit. Empirical results on GLMs (with L1/L2 regularization) demonstrate significant speedups for CD (2-5x faster convergence) while incurring only O(n log n) per-iteration overhead—negligible compared to gradient computation (O(d) for d features).

**Strengths:**  
1. **Theoretical rigor**: The paper establishes strong guarantees (Theorems 3.2, 3.4) showing the sampling is *provably better than any fixed importance sampling* and provides tight bounds on the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5). This resolves a critical gap in prior work (e.g., [36, 22] lacked such guarantees).  
2. **Practical efficiency**: The O(n log n) computational cost (Theorem 3.4) is well-motivated and verified empirically. For large d (e.g., news20 with d=1.3M), the overhead reduces to O(log n), making it deployable in real-world settings.  
3. **Clear problem framing**: The distinction between "optimal but infeasible" (Algorithm 1) and "safe but efficient" (Algorithm 2) is compelling. The GLM examples (Section 4) concretely demonstrate how safe bounds can be maintained at low cost.  
4. **Strong empirical validation**: Results on rcv1, real-sim, and news20 (Figures 2–6) convincingly show CD speedups, with the adaptive stepsize (determined by v_k) dynamically improving over iterations. The "safe bounds" approach works even with naive Cauchy-Schwarz estimates (Section 5.3).

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**  
1. **SGD results lack depth**: The SGD experiments (Figures 7–9) show minimal gains over uniform sampling, contrasting sharply with CD. The authors should clarify *why* (e.g., problem structure, weaker gradient bounds in SGD vs. CD) and discuss whether this is inherent to the method or implementation. A brief analysis here would strengthen the significance claim.  
2. **Competitive ratio explanation**: The competitive ratio ρ_k (Equation 10) is theoretically important but under-explained. Adding a short numerical example (e.g., "For bounds with γ=2, ρ_k ≤ 16 per Lemma 3.6") would make this more accessible.  
3. **Minor clarity tweaks**: The transition to Section 3.1 (from Section 2) could better emphasize *why* previous bounds (e.g., [36]) fail to guarantee improvement. Also, the proof sketch for Lemma 2.4 (Appendix A.1) could be referenced more explicitly in the main text.

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a high-quality, well-motivated contribution that advances both theory and practice in adaptive sampling. The core idea—using safe bounds in an optimization framework to guarantee superiority over fixed sampling—is novel and impactful. While the SGD results warrant deeper discussion, the paper’s strengths in theoretical guarantees, computational efficiency, and empirical validation for CD (a dominant paradigm in large-scale ML) make it a strong fit for NIPS. The authors successfully bridge a gap between theoretically optimal but infeasible sampling and practical approximations with provable benefits. The paper is clear, well-structured, and addresses a well-recognized pain point in optimization.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The work is technically sound, original, and significant for practitioners. The minor weaknesses (mainly in SGD analysis) are easily addressable in revision and do not undermine the core contribution.
