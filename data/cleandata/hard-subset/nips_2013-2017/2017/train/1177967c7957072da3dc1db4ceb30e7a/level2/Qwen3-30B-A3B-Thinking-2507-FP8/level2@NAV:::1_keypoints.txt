- Proposes a novel "safe adaptive importance sampling" for CD and SGD that avoids the computational infeasibility of exact gradient-based sampling.  
- Uses safe upper and lower bounds on gradients to construct a sampling distribution that is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling.  
- Shows the proposed distribution is optimal with respect to the provided bounds (Theorem 3.2).  
- Proves the method always outperforms fixed sampling and can be computed efficiently in O(n log n) per iteration.  
- Theoretical analysis is thorough, with careful proofs and clear connections to prior adaptive sampling work (e.g., Zhao & Zhang).  
- Empirical results on rcv1, real-sim, and news20 across losses and regularizers demonstrate effectiveness, especially large speedups for CD.  
- Key strength: theoretical guarantee that the sampling never performs worse than fixed importance sampling, unlike prior approximations.  
- Computational overhead is minimal compared to gradient evaluation, making the approach practically efficient.  
- Provides practical examples of safe gradient bounds for generalized linear models, showing applicability (Section 4).  
- Limitation: empirical gains are less pronounced for SGD than for CD and this is not fully explored.  
- Suggests additional study of effects in very large-scale distributed systems.  
- Writing, pseudocode, and reproducibility details are clear; overall recommendation: accept.
