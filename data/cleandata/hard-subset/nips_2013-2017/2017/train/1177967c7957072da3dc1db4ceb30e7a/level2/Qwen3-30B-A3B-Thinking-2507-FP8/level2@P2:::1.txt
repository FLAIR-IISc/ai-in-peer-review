

This paper proposes a "safe adaptive importance sampling" scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The key contribution is a method to efficiently approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on gradient values, which guarantees the sampling is always better than uniform or fixed importance sampling while being computationally feasible.

The paper makes several strong theoretical contributions. The authors prove that their proposed sampling distribution is provably better than fixed importance sampling (Theorem 3.2), and they provide a clear optimization formulation for computing this distribution under safe gradient bounds (Equation 7). The computational complexity analysis shows the method requires O(n log n) time per iteration, which is often negligible compared to gradient computation costs (especially when d = Ω(n)). The paper also provides a thorough theoretical justification for adaptive sampling in CD methods (Section 2), showing potential speedups of up to n-fold compared to fixed sampling.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple datasets (rcv1, real-sim, news20) for CD methods with both L1 and L2 regularization. The results show that the proposed method significantly outperforms fixed importance sampling in terms of both iteration count and total computation time. The theoretical analysis of the competitive ratio (Lemma 3.5, Lemma 3.6) provides valuable insights into the method's performance guarantees.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with detailed algorithms (Algorithms 1-4) and thorough theoretical proofs. The connection between the theoretical guarantees and empirical results is well-maintained, and the authors provide a helpful geometric interpretation of the optimization problem (Appendix B).

However, there are some limitations worth noting. The empirical evaluation for SGD shows less dramatic improvements compared to CD (Figures 7-9), but the paper doesn't adequately explain this discrepancy. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, noting that further research is needed on maintaining safe gradient bounds for more complex models beyond generalized linear models (GLMs). While the paper demonstrates how to track bounds for GLMs in Section 4, it would be valuable to include a brief discussion of potential challenges for more complex models like deep neural networks.

The paper also cites important related work but misses a key recent contribution: "Faster Coordinate Descent via Adaptive Importance Sampling" by Perekrestenko et al. (AISTATS 2017), which also addresses adaptive importance sampling with theoretical guarantees. While the authors' approach differs in that it provides guarantees that the sampling is always better than fixed sampling (unlike previous approaches), this omission is notable as the authors claim their approach is "generic and can easily be integrated into existing algorithms."

The paper demonstrates significant practical value, as the computational overhead of the proposed method is minimal (O(n log n) per iteration) and the speedups observed in CD experiments are substantial. The method's ability to work with very loose safe gradient bounds (as shown in Figure 3-6) is particularly impressive, as it requires no initialization of gradient estimates.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that provides strong theoretical guarantees and practical improvements for CD methods. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of adaptive optimization methods by providing a safe and provably better alternative to previous adaptive sampling approaches.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the state of the art in optimization methods with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The method's generality and practical efficiency make it valuable to both researchers and practitioners.

Strengths: Strong theoretical guarantees, clear connection between theory and practice, comprehensive empirical evaluation, minimal computational overhead.

Weaknesses: Limited discussion of SGD performance discrepancy, omission of recent related work, limited scope to GLMs (though this is acknowledged as a direction for future work).

This paper is a significant contribution that advances the state of the art in adaptive importance sampling for optimization algorithms. I recommend acceptance.
