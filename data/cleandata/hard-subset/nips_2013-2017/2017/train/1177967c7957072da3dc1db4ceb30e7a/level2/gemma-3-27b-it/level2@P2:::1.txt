This paper proposes a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for speeding up coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using bounds on the gradient, ensuring a provable improvement over uniform or fixed importance sampling while maintaining computational efficiency. The authors demonstrate theoretical guarantees and empirical validation on several datasets.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the limitations of existing adaptive sampling methods. The contributions are clearly stated and the technical details are presented in a logical order. The notation is generally clear, and the authors provide sufficient context for the reader to understand the key concepts. The empirical evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides strong theoretical guarantees for the proposed sampling scheme, demonstrating its superiority over uniform and fixed importance sampling. The analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed method is designed to be computationally efficient, requiring only a small overhead compared to standard sampling techniques. This is a significant advantage, especially for large-scale problems.
*   **Generality:** The scheme is generic and can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD algorithms. This makes it a versatile tool for a wide range of machine learning applications.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive numerical experiments on real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in practice. The results consistently show significant speedups compared to other sampling techniques.
*   **Safe Approximation:** The focus on "safe" approximations, guaranteeing at least as good performance as static sampling, is a valuable contribution. This addresses a key concern with other adaptive methods that can sometimes degrade performance.
*   **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper places the research in the context of existing work on importance sampling and coordinate descent, and clearly articulates the novel contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Gradient Bound Estimation:** The paper relies on accurate gradient bounds (`l` and `u`). While the authors provide a basic method for estimating these bounds in the GLM case, the performance of the scheme is sensitive to the quality of these bounds. The paper doesn’t fully address how to effectively obtain such bounds in more complex settings where analytical derivation is intractable. It would be beneficial to discuss the robustness of the method to imperfect bounds.
*   **SGD Results:** The empirical results for SGD show only a slight improvement over uniform sampling, which is less impressive than the gains observed for CD. This discrepancy deserves further investigation. It's unclear why the theoretical advantages don't translate as strongly to the SGD setting in the experiments.
*   **Competitive Ratio Discussion:** While the discussion on competitive ratio (ρk) is interesting, it feels a bit disconnected from the main algorithm. A more intuitive explanation of how this ratio impacts performance would be helpful. The conditions for achieving a good ratio seem restrictive.
*   **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are primarily focused on GLMs. It would be valuable to evaluate the performance of the method on other types of problems, such as deep learning models.

**Minor Issues:**

*   The notation `[x]i` is a bit unconventional and could be clarified.
*   Some of the equations could benefit from more detailed explanations. For instance, the derivation of Equation (3) is somewhat condensed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in large-scale optimization.
*   The proposed method is theoretically sound and computationally efficient.
*   The empirical results are compelling, especially for coordinate descent.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The “safe” adaptive sampling approach is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The SGD results are less convincing.
*   The sensitivity to gradient bound estimation could be a limitation in practice.
*   The scope of the empirical evaluation is somewhat limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of large-scale optimization. The proposed adaptive importance sampling scheme is both theoretically sound and practically effective, particularly for coordinate descent. While the SGD results are less impressive and the reliance on gradient bounds could be a limitation, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly a more in-depth discussion of gradient bound estimation and a further investigation of the SGD results.
