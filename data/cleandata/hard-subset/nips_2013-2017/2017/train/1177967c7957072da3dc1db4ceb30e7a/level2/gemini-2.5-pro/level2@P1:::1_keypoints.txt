- Addresses adaptive importance sampling for large-scale CD/SGD using efficiently maintainable gradient lower/upper bounds instead of full gradients.

- Formulates sampling selection as a min-max optimization to minimize worst-case variance under gradient uncertainty.

- Core idea: find a “safe” distribution robust to gradient bounds, providing principled adaptation with limited information.

- Proposes an efficient O(n log n) algorithm (Algorithm 4) to solve the min-max problem in practice.

- Theorem 3.2: proves the proposed sampling is always better than fixed Lipschitz-based sampling, giving a strong theoretical guarantee.

- Shows how to derive and update the required gradient bounds for Generalized Linear Models (GLMs).

- Empirical results demonstrate substantial gains in iterations and wall-clock time, approaching impractical optimal sampling.

- Strength — originality/significance: novel, elegant min-max formulation for robust adaptive sampling.

- Strength — theoretical quality: strong guarantees and a practical algorithm make the approach sound and implementable.

- Strength — clarity/organization: paper is well-written, motivated, and logically structured.

- Strength — experiments: thorough validation showing low overhead and clear benefits over non-adaptive methods.

- Weakness — limited experimental comparisons: missing comparisons to other practical adaptive schemes (e.g., stale-gradient methods, other bound-based heuristics).

- Weakness — Algorithm 4 presentation: dense and hard to parse; needs a more intuitive explanation and a small worked example, especially clarifying updates to variable m.

- Weakness — overhead/scalability discussion: more analysis needed on regimes (e.g., very high-dimensional sparse problems) where O(n log n) cost may dominate.

- Weakness — generality of bounds: applicability beyond GLMs (e.g., neural networks, matrix factorization) is not discussed and may be challenging.

- Weakness — weaker SGD gains: observed smaller improvements for SGD than CD; paper should provide intuition or theory explaining this difference.

- Summary pros: principled min-max approach, provable improvement over fixed sampling, efficient algorithm, convincing empirical gains.

- Summary cons: missing practical-method comparisons, clearer algorithm exposition needed, and broader discussion of overhead and generality.

- Overall: high-quality, novel, and practical contribution; recommend acceptance.
