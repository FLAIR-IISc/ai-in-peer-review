- Proposes a novel "safe" adaptive importance sampling for CD and SGD using efficiently computable gradient bounds.  
- Addresses infeasibility of optimal adaptive sampling by formulating a principled min-max problem over an uncertainty set.  
- Derives a sampling distribution that is provably never worse than fixed importance (including uniform) given the bounds.  
- Provides an efficient O(n log n) algorithm to compute the worst-case-optimal sampling distribution.  
- Shows how to derive and maintain required lower/upper gradient bounds for GLMs with linear-time updates.  
- Empirically demonstrates substantial improvements over uniform and fixed sampling across datasets, losses, and regularizers.  
- Includes the computationally expensive optimal sampling as a reference, showing the proposed method closely approximates it.  
- Highly original and elegant formulation that remedies shortcomings of prior heuristic adaptive samplings.  
- Strong theoretical guarantees (safety theorem and algorithmic analysis) that back the method.  
- Practical and broadly applicable; overhead is often negligible relative to per-coordinate gradient costs in high-dimensional settings.  
- Weakness: performance depends on tightness of gradient bounds; loose bounds (e.g., unnormalized data) may reduce gains.  
- Weakness: O(n log n) overhead can be a bottleneck for extremely large n with cheap per-gradient costs ("short and fat" data).  
- Weakness: empirical gains are larger for CD than for SGD; paper lacks a detailed analysis explaining this discrepancy.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-written, clearly motivated, and likely to be impactful for large-scale optimization.
