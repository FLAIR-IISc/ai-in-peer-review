- Proposes a “safe” adaptive importance sampling for CD and SGD using interval bounds on gradients to avoid full gradient evaluations, via a minimax formulation.  
- Shows the resulting sampling p̂ is optimal for the uncertainty set, never worse than uniform/Li-based sampling, and recovers gradient-based sampling as bounds tighten.  
- Provides an O(n log n) algorithm to compute p̂ via a thresholding/water-filling construction.  
- For CD, adaptive sampling with optimal step-size can yield up to n-fold one-step improvement; efficient safe bounds for GLMs are derived.  
- Experiments show substantial speedups for CD and only modest gains for SGD.  
- Places work relative to fixed importance sampling, stale-gradient adaptive methods, gap-based CD, and recent non-uniform CD; novelty is the robust optimization viewpoint and efficient solution.  
- Strength — conceptual novelty: principled minimax formulation under bounded gradient information with best worst-case guarantee.  
- Strength — solid theory: clean one-step progress bounds, recovery of full-information sampling, geometric characterization, and competitive-ratio analysis.  
- Strength — practicality for CD: efficient bound maintenance for GLMs and straightforward integration with updates.  
- Strength — empirical evidence: CD achieves near-full-gradient performance with small overhead and clear wall-clock gains.  
- Weakness — SGD scalability: safe-bound maintenance for finite-sum SGD requires O(n) updates per iteration, undermining SGD’s efficiency.  
- Weakness — overhead vs problem structure: O(n log n) per-iteration cost can dominate when n ≫ d or for high-dimensional sparse data, contrary to “negligible” claims.  
- Weakness — model-based guarantees: “always better” claims are relative to the quadratic upper-bound model and may be conservative with loose bounds.  
- Weakness — limited baselines: missing comparisons to Gauss–Southwell, steepest-CD heuristics, and practical stale-gradient or mini-batch importance sampling.  
- Weakness — reliance on Li: method depends on coordinate-wise Lipschitz constants with little guidance on estimation or sensitivity.  
- Quality — theory is sound and the O(n log n) construction is plausible; some worst-case competitive bounds are loose but acceptable.  
- Clarity — paper is overall clear; would benefit from per-iteration complexity tables, an intuitive minimax derivation, and explicit bound-update frequency discussion.  
- Originality — the robust-safe formulation and efficient computation from gradient uncertainty sets appear novel.  
- Significance — practically impactful for CD on GLMs with provable dominance; limited impact for SGD unless bound updates can be amortized or made sublinear.  
- Suggestion — for SGD, explore epoch-level, block, or subset updates to amortize bounds and report timing including bound-update overhead.  
- Suggestion — include comparisons to approximate Gauss–Southwell/steepest-CD and stale-gradient sampling, and study adaptive Li estimation.  
- Suggestion — release open-source implementation and profiling of sorting and bound-update overheads across varying n, d, and sparsity.  
- Suggestion — extend analysis and experiments to block/mini-batch and asynchronous/distributed settings.  
- Pros for acceptance — novel principled approach, efficient algorithm, strong empirical gains for CD.  
- Cons against acceptance — SGD practicality concerns, potential overhead when n ≫ d, and missing practical baselines/profiling.  
- Recommendation — Weak accept: strong theoretical contribution and CD impact; temper or strengthen SGD claims with engineering/analysis.  
- Confidence — high for theoretical assessment; moderate for empirical generality and SGD practicality.
