## Review of "Safe Adaptive Importance Sampling"

This paper addresses the problem of accelerating optimization algorithms, particularly coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD), via adaptive importance sampling. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, resulting in a computationally efficient scheme with provable guarantees.

The paper clearly identifies the drawbacks of existing adaptive strategies – computational cost and potentially worse theoretical guarantees than uniform sampling – and positions its contribution as a solution to these issues. The authors convincingly argue that their proposed sampling scheme is the best possible given the available gradient bounds and is always superior to uniform or fixed importance sampling. The theoretical analysis, demonstrating the speed-up factor in CD and the bounds on performance, is a significant strength. The connection to existing work on gradient-based sampling and duality gap-based sampling is well-established.

A key strength is the practical focus. The authors not only provide theoretical results but also demonstrate how to efficiently compute the proposed sampling distribution and integrate it into CD and SGD. The algorithms presented (Algorithms 2 & 3) are clear and concise. The discussion on tracking safe gradient bounds for GLMs is particularly valuable, outlining a practical application of the proposed method. The experimental evaluation on several datasets and with different loss functions (squared hinge, logistic) validates the theoretical findings and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed sampling scheme.  The inclusion of plots illustrating the `vk` values (which influence the stepsize) is insightful, showing the benefit gained even with loose bounds.

However, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations. While the authors mention computational cost, a more detailed analysis of the overhead in different scenarios (e.g., very high-dimensional data) would be valuable. Furthermore, the experiments primarily focus on GLMs. Investigating performance on more complex models (e.g., deep neural networks) would strengthen the conclusions. The performance gap between CD and SGD with the proposed method could be better explained.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to adaptive importance sampling.
*   Provable guarantees of improvement over existing methods.
*   Practical and efficiently computable algorithm.
*   Strong experimental validation on relevant datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited exploration of performance on complex models beyond GLMs.
*   Could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of computational overhead.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a well-defined problem, a novel and well-supported solution, and convincing experimental results. I recommend acceptance, with a minor recommendation to address the limitations mentioned above in a revised version. The work has the potential to be highly influential in the field of large-scale optimization.
