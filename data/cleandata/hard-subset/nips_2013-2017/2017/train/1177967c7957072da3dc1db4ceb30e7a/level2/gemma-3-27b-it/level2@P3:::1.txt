This paper introduces a safe adaptive importance sampling scheme for speeding up coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) algorithms. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using bounds on the gradient, ensuring the proposed sampling is always better than uniform sampling and provably optimal given the available information. The paper provides theoretical guarantees and demonstrates the effectiveness of the scheme through extensive numerical experiments.

The work builds upon the existing literature on importance sampling, adaptive sampling, and coordinate descent methods. Previous work on adaptive sampling, such as [22, 5, 26], often suffers from computational infeasibility due to the need to compute full gradients. This paper addresses this limitation by proposing a computationally efficient approximation based on safe gradient bounds. It connects to work on duality gap-based sampling [5, 23] by aiming for theoretical guarantees while remaining computationally tractable. The analysis relating to CD builds on prior work establishing benefits of adaptive sampling [22, 36, 37] but presents a tighter bound on the potential speedup (factor of *n*). The connection to SGD builds on [22, 36, 37], providing a simpler complexity analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating the benefits of the proposed sampling scheme over uniform and fixed importance sampling. The proofs are detailed (deferred to the appendix) and the claims seem well-supported.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is designed to be computationally efficient, requiring only O(n log n) time per iteration for sampling, making it applicable to large-scale problems. This is a significant advantage over existing adaptive sampling methods.
*   **Generality:** The scheme is generic and can be easily integrated into existing CD and SGD algorithms.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The extensive numerical experiments on real datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed scheme in practice. The results consistently show improvements over fixed importance sampling and, in some cases, approach the performance of the optimal gradient-based sampling.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The performance of the proposed scheme relies on the availability of accurate safe gradient bounds. While the paper discusses how to obtain these bounds for GLMs, it does not address how to obtain them for more complex models. The practicality of obtaining these bounds in other settings could be a limitation. Further discussion and potentially investigation into robust methods for estimating these bounds would strengthen the paper.
*   **SGD Performance:** The experimental results for SGD show only modest improvements over uniform sampling. The authors acknowledge this and suggest that further investigation may be needed. Exploring the reasons for this limited improvement and potentially modifying the scheme for better SGD performance would be valuable.
*   **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** The competitive ratio analysis (Lemmas 3.5 and 3.6) is interesting, but the conditions for achieving a good competitive ratio (γ ≥ 1 in Lemma 3.6) feel a bit restrictive. A more detailed discussion of these conditions and their implications would be helpful.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While the experiments include comparisons to uniform sampling, a comparison to other state-of-the-art adaptive sampling methods (when feasible) would further strengthen the evaluation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant practical problem in large-scale optimization – accelerating CD and SGD.
*   The proposed solution is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically validated.
*   The work represents a novel contribution to the field of importance sampling and adaptive optimization.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on accurate gradient bounds could be a limitation in some applications.
*   The modest improvement in SGD performance warrants further investigation.
*   The restrictive conditions for the competitive ratio analysis could limit its practical applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The proposed safe adaptive importance sampling scheme is a practical and theoretically sound approach for accelerating CD and SGD algorithms. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendations:**

*   **Essential:** Discuss the challenges of obtaining accurate gradient bounds for more complex models and potentially explore robust methods for estimating them.
*   **Essential:** Investigate the reasons for the limited improvement in SGD performance and consider modifications to the scheme to address this issue.
*   **Optional:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the conditions for achieving a good competitive ratio.
*   **Optional:** Compare the proposed scheme to other state-of-the-art adaptive sampling methods when feasible.
