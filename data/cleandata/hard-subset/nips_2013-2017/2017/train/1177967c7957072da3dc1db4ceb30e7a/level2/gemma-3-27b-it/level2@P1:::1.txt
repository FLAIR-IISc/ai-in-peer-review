This paper introduces a novel adaptive importance sampling scheme for coordinate descent (CD) and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods, aiming to bridge the gap between theoretically strong but computationally expensive adaptive strategies and simpler, yet potentially less effective, fixed sampling approaches. The core idea is to approximate gradient-based sampling using safe bounds on the gradient, ensuring computational efficiency while maintaining provable performance guarantees.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction provides sufficient background and motivation, effectively highlighting the challenges with existing adaptive sampling methods. The contributions are clearly stated. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the notation is consistent. The connection to previous work, particularly on gradient-based sampling and duality gap-based sampling, is well-established. The empirical evaluation, while not extensive, provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness on several datasets and with different loss functions. 

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper provides strong theoretical results, demonstrating that the proposed sampling scheme is provably better than uniform or fixed importance sampling and recovers gradient-based sampling in the full-information setting.
* **Computational Efficiency:**  The proposed scheme is designed to be computationally efficient, with the overhead compared to fixed sampling being relatively small (O(n log n) per iteration). This addresses a major limitation of previous adaptive methods.
* **Generality:** The approach is generic and can be readily integrated into existing CD and SGD algorithms.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with the field of importance sampling.
* **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed is practically relevant, as it directly impacts the scalability of optimization algorithms for large-scale machine learning applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions on Gradient Bounds:** The reliance on “safe” upper and lower bounds on the gradient (`, u) is a potential limitation. While the paper discusses how to obtain these bounds for GLMs, the applicability to other model classes may require careful consideration. The quality of these bounds directly impacts the performance of the proposed method.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the empirical results are encouraging, the evaluation is somewhat limited.  Exploring a wider range of datasets and model architectures would further strengthen the claims. A more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art sampling methods (beyond uniform sampling) would also be beneficial. The experiments are primarily focused on coordinate descent. More emphasis on the SGD results would be valuable.
* **Competitive Ratio Analysis:** While the paper provides a competitive ratio analysis, the condition of non-overlapping bounds (γ) seems restrictive in practice.  Exploring the performance under less restrictive conditions would be useful.
* **Complexity of Algorithm 4:** Although stated as O(n log n), the practical overhead of sorting to obtain the bounds might be significant, particularly for very high-dimensional problems. A more in-depth analysis of the practical cost of this step would be beneficial.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in large-scale optimization—balancing theoretical guarantees with computational efficiency in adaptive importance sampling.
* The proposed approach is theoretically sound, with strong performance guarantees.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on relevant machine learning tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on safe gradient bounds might limit the applicability of the method to certain problem classes.
* The empirical evaluation could be more extensive and include a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The proposed adaptive importance sampling scheme offers a compelling combination of theoretical guarantees, computational efficiency, and generality. While the limitations regarding gradient bounds and empirical evaluation should be considered, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept.** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017. The paper presents a novel and valuable contribution that is likely to be of interest to a broad audience within the machine learning community.  I encourage the authors to address the limitations mentioned above in future work, potentially by exploring more robust methods for estimating gradient bounds and conducting more extensive empirical evaluations.
