- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), an estimator of distribution distance based on minimum weight matching of sample populations.
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric with clear mathematical derivations.
- Proposes an algorithm using PMD as a training objective and applies it to domain adaptation and generative modeling.
- Claims advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements.
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive across multiple datasets and tasks, showing practical benefits in some settings.
- Acknowledges computational cost of exact matching (Hungarian algorithm, O(N^3)) but argues it's acceptable relative to neural net training.
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise definitions, pseudocode, and helpful visualizations.
- Could provide more implementation details on matching algorithms and discuss trade-offs between exact and approximate matching.
- Original contribution: combines Wasserstein metric with minimum weight matching to avoid MMD’s bandwidth issue, distinct from related work.
- Significance: addresses a practical need for a differentiable, robust distribution distance estimator useful in deep learning.
- Experimental claims that PMD outperforms MMD are somewhat overstated; domain adaptation results are mixed across tasks.
- Lacks comprehensive comparisons with recent alternatives (e.g., Cramer distance) and limited analysis of PMD’s behavior in high-dimensional settings.
- Weakness: scalability concerns for very large datasets and potential limitations of matching in high dimensions are not fully explored.
- Overall assessment: strong, technically sound, original, and significant paper with minor experimental and discussion gaps.
- Recommendation: Accept.
