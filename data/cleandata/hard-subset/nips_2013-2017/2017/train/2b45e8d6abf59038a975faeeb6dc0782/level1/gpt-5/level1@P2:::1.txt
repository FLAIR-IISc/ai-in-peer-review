Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the distance between two distributions defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein metric (W1) via standard weak convergence and stability results. They propose using PMD as a differentiable objective for training parameterized models, arguing that gradients do not need to backpropagate through the discrete matching. They highlight practical advantages over MMD (no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and effective training with smaller SGD minibatches), and evaluate PMD on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), reporting faster convergence and better or comparable performance vs. MMD baselines.

The work connects to MMD-based training (GMMN) and WGAN, and to classical optimal transport (OT)/Earth Mover’s Distance. However, it omits key related work on entropic regularization and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) and subsequent differentiable OT methods that are computationally efficient and widely used by 2017, as well as sliced Wasserstein metrics and recent GAN/domain-adaptation competitors (DANN, JAN, CORAL, CMD). These omissions affect both the positioning and the empirical baselines.

Quality
- Theory: The consistency result follows directly from Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of OT (Villani), so it is correct but mostly a restatement of known facts for empirical OT. The central training claim—that one can differentiate the matching objective without propagating through the argmin—would benefit from a formal justification (e.g., Danskin’s theorem/Envelope theorem conditions: uniqueness and stability of the minimizer, subgradients at kinks). As written, “the matching should not change for infinitesimal changes” is intuitive but insufficiently rigorous. The minibatch subsampling with fixed matching is a heuristic whose bias is not analyzed.
- Algorithms: Using exact Hungarian (O(N^3)) or approximate O(N^2) matching is standard; the paper reports times and suggests GPU parallelization but does not implement it. The gradient-strength argument is plausible and supported by a small empirical analysis.
- Experiments: Domain adaptation experiments on Office compare only to MMD with fair model selection, showing advantages on some shifts and faster convergence. However, stronger baselines are missing (DANN/Ganin et al., JAN/Long et al., CORAL, CMD), which are standard by 2017. Generative experiments compare only to MMD/GMMN; images are blurry and lack quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception score/FID) or comparisons to GAN/WGAN. Claims about convergence and batch size are supported, but overall empirical evidence is not sufficient to demonstrate state-of-the-art significance.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Definitions and the connection to W1 are presented cleanly. Some statements around differentiability and unbiasedness are informal; pointing to Danskin’s theorem and explicitly acknowledging non-differentiable switching points would improve rigor. Important related work (e.g., Sinkhorn/entropic OT, sliced Wasserstein) is missing from the discussion. Experimental details are mostly adequate; adding precise hyperparameter settings and code would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Using empirical W1 via minimum-weight matching as a training loss is not new in OT; the consistency result and matching-based formulation correspond to classical EMD. The novelty here lies in: (a) advocating the “don’t backprop through matching” training recipe for deep models, (b) highlighting practical advantages over MMD (gradient strength, batch size), and (c) demonstrating utility in two tasks. Relative to contemporary work (Sinkhorn OT, WGAN, sliced Wasserstein), the conceptual novelty is limited.

Significance
The paper addresses a relevant problem—stable, hyperparameter-light sample-based discrepancies for deep learning. The practical insights (stronger gradients than MMD; smaller minibatches) are potentially useful. However, the impact is dampened by missing comparisons to widely adopted alternatives (Entropic/Sinkhorn OT and WGAN) and by modest generative quality. Without stronger baselines and broader experiments, it is hard to conclude that PMD advances the state of the art.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear formulation connecting PMD and W1; correctness of consistency.
- Practical training recipe with empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence than MMD.
- Simple objective with fewer hyperparameters; competitive results on Office and improved sample efficiency in training.
- Useful empirical observation on mini-batch versus population size trade-offs.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Limited novelty: PMD is empirical W1/EMD; consistency result is standard.
- Incomplete related work: omission of entropic/Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi 2013) and sliced Wasserstein; no discussion of backprop through Sinkhorn which addresses differentiability and scalability.
- Weak baselines: domain adaptation lacks comparisons to DANN/JAN/CORAL/CMD; generative modeling lacks GAN/WGAN comparisons and quantitative scores.
- The differentiability argument is informal; no analysis of bias when reusing matchings for sub-batches or of convergence under matching switches.
- Scalability concerns remain for large N without implemented parallel matching.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the work relative to entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) and sliced Wasserstein, and compare empirically (including GPU-friendly Sinkhorn with autograd).
- Strengthen the differentiability analysis using Danskin’s theorem; discuss subgradients at matching switches; clarify when gradients are unbiased.
- Add stronger baselines: DANN, JAN, CORAL, CMD for domain adaptation; GAN/WGAN (and report Inception/FID) for generation.
- Explore feature-space distances (e.g., learned or perceptual metrics) for PMD to mitigate blur, and report ablations.
- Provide code and GPU matching implementations, or at least complexity analyses on larger scales.

Overall assessment
- Quality: moderate; theory correct but limited, experiments supportive but not comprehensive.
- Clarity: good, with some missing rigor and related work.
- Originality: modest.
- Significance: moderate; practical insights are interesting but evidence vs. contemporary alternatives is insufficient.

Recommendation: borderline reject; promising practical idea, but needs stronger positioning and comparisons to be compelling for NIPS.
