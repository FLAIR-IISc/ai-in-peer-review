This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between samples drawn from the two distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. This work positions PMD as a direct competitor to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing it overcomes several of MMD's drawbacks. The use of the Wasserstein metric connects this work to the concurrent development of Wasserstein GANs (Arjovsky et al., 2017), but PMD tackles the primal optimal transport problem directly without requiring an auxiliary critic network. The paper then demonstrates the utility of PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep learning models in domain adaptation and generative modeling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—a practical, differentiable, and parameter-free estimator of the primal Wasserstein-1 distance for training deep neural networks—is both novel and significant. It offers a valuable alternative to MMD and critic-based Wasserstein approximations, addressing known issues like hyperparameter sensitivity (kernel bandwidth in MMD) and training instability (in GANs).
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claim of strong consistency is well-grounded in optimal transport theory. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with experiments in two distinct applications that convincingly demonstrate PMD's advantages over MMD in terms of both final performance and convergence speed. The ablation studies on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithms further strengthen the paper's claims.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The proposed method and its training algorithm are explained effectively, and the connections to prior work are well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The primary weakness is the computational complexity of the matching step, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. While the authors argue this is acceptable, it is a significant bottleneck compared to MMD and may limit scalability to very large population sizes.
*   **Experimental Baselines:** While the comparison to MMD is strong, the generative modeling experiments could have been strengthened by including comparisons to more state-of-the-art generative models from the same period, such as WGAN, to better contextualize PMD's performance in that application. The generated images, while demonstrating improvements over the MMD baseline, remain somewhat blurry.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and well-motivated distribution metric with compelling theoretical and empirical support. The practical benefits over MMD are clearly demonstrated, and it provides a valuable new tool for the deep learning community.
