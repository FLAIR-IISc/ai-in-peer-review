- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based metric using minimum-weight bipartite matching to estimate distance between distributions.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein (W1) metric.  
- Proposes a learning algorithm that uses PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep networks, updating parameters via SGD.  
- Computes gradients w.r.t. a fixed optimal matching that is periodically recomputed (practical inner-loop heuristic).  
- Positions PMD as an alternative to MMD, avoiding kernel bandwidth hyperparameters and providing stronger gradients.  
- Claims PMD allows smaller SGD mini-batches than the population used for matching.  
- Demonstrates effectiveness on unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing superior or competitive performance and faster convergence versus MMD.  
- Provides ablation studies on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithm choices to support practical claims.  
- Technical soundness: connects PMD to optimal transport with correct use of established propositions and proofs.  
- Minor theoretical weakness: gradient ignoring matching changes assumes matching stability under infinitesimal parameter perturbations, which may fail at match-change points.  
- Practical-theoretical gap: reusing fixed matchings for multiple SGD steps is a heuristic whose convergence/bias implications are not analyzed.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, organized, and explains PMD, algorithm, and distinctions from MMD clearly.  
- Originality: novel application of primal-form matching/Wasserstein as a differentiable objective for deep learning, distinct from MMD and WGAN approaches.  
- Significance: addresses fundamental distribution comparison problem and mitigates known MMD issues, offering a practical alternative with promising results.  
- Missing comparison to WGAN in generative modeling is a notable experimental omission.  
- Scalability concern: exact matching is O(N^3) (approx O(N^2)), raising practical limits versus MMD which is O(N^2) and more parallelizable.
