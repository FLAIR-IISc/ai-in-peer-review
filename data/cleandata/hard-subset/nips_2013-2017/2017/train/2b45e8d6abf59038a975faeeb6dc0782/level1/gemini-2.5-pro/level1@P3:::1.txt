### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based distance metric between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples (populations) drawn from the respective distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). A key contribution is a method to use PMD as a differentiable objective for training deep neural networks via stochastic gradient descent. This is achieved by assuming the optimal matching is locally constant, allowing gradients to be computed with respect to the sample locations.

The paper contrasts PMD with the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). It argues that PMD has several advantages: it is hyperparameter-free (no kernel bandwidth), provides stronger gradients that are less prone to vanishing, and allows for the use of small SGD mini-batches that are decoupled from the larger population size used for matching. The effectiveness of PMD is demonstrated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. In both applications, empirical results show that PMD outperforms or matches MMD, often with faster convergence.

**Critique**

This is a well-written and interesting paper that proposes an elegant and theoretically grounded alternative to MMD for comparing distributions in deep learning contexts. The connection to optimal transport theory and the first Wasserstein metric provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of using the primal form of the empirical Wasserstein distance (i.e., minimum weight matching) as a differentiable training objective for deep networks is novel and significant. While MMD is a common choice, this work provides a compelling alternative that directly addresses several of MMD's known practical shortcomings. This work is also nicely positioned relative to contemporaneous work like Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) [3], which uses the dual form of the Wasserstein distance, by offering a parameter-free, primal-form alternative.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper correctly establishes that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric by leveraging established results from optimal transport theory [49]. This provides strong theoretical justification for using PMD as a proxy for a true metric between distributions. The derivation of the gradient under the assumption of a locally fixed matching is a key and practical insight that makes the method viable.

3.  **Practical Advantages over MMD:** The paper makes a convincing case for PMD's practical benefits.
    *   **No Kernel Hyperparameter:** Eliminating the need to tune the kernel bandwidth `σ` is a significant practical advantage, as MMD's performance can be very sensitive to this choice.
    *   **Stronger Gradients:** The analysis and empirical validation (Fig. 2b) of PMD providing more uniform and non-vanishing gradients is a crucial point. This is a well-known issue with kernel-based methods like MMD, especially in the context of generative models [3], and PMD offers a clear solution.
    *   **Flexible Mini-batching:** The proposed training scheme, where the matching is computed on a larger population `N` but SGD updates are performed on smaller mini-batches `B`, is a clever and effective optimization. This allows for more parameter updates per epoch, leading to the observed faster convergence.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and well-executed. The choice of two distinct and important applications (domain adaptation, generative modeling) effectively demonstrates the versatility of PMD. The direct comparisons with MMD are fair and clearly show the benefits of the proposed method in terms of both final performance and convergence speed. The ablation studies in Section 5.3 further strengthen the paper's claims about the effects of population and mini-batch size.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The primary weakness of PMD is its computational cost. The Hungarian algorithm for exact matching is O(N^3), and the approximation is O(N^2). This is computationally more demanding than MMD's O(N^2) complexity, and this cost is incurred in every outer loop of training. While the authors rightly argue this can be acceptable compared to backpropagation costs and can be parallelized, it still presents a scalability challenge for very large population sizes (N > a few thousand), potentially limiting its application compared to MMD or WGAN.

2.  **Gradient Approximation:** The differentiability of the objective relies on the assumption that the optimal matching M* is locally constant. While this holds almost everywhere, the objective is non-smooth at points where the optimal matching changes. The paper does not discuss the potential implications of this approximation. Could this lead to optimization instabilities or biased gradients in certain regimes? A brief discussion on this point would strengthen the paper's technical rigor.

3.  **Choice of Ground Metric d(·,·):** The performance of PMD is inherently tied to the choice of the ground metric `d(x, y)` in the sample space (L1 was used in the experiments). For high-dimensional, structured data like images, simple pixel-wise distances like L1 or L2 are often poor measures of perceptual similarity. This is likely why the generated images in Fig. 4 appear blurry. In contrast, methods like WGAN can be interpreted as learning a more powerful "critic" metric. This is a critical trade-off (fixed simple metric vs. learned complex metric) that is worth discussing explicitly.

4.  **Comparison to WGAN:** Given that both PMD and WGAN are motivated by the Wasserstein distance and aim to solve similar problems in generative modeling (e.g., stable training, non-vanishing gradients), the lack of an empirical comparison to WGAN is a missed opportunity. While the paper clearly explains the methodological differences, a performance comparison would have better situated PMD within the state-of-the-art landscape of 2017.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper introducing a novel, well-motivated, and effective method. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and supported by convincing experiments. It makes a significant contribution by providing a strong alternative to MMD that addresses several of its practical limitations. While there are concerns about computational scalability and the choice of the ground metric, the strengths of the paper in terms of originality, theoretical grounding, and practical utility are substantial. I recommend acceptance.
