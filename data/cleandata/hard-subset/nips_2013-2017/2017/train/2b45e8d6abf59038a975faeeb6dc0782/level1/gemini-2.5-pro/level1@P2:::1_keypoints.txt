- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): a sample-based estimator using average distances from a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two sample sets.  
- Shows PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein (W1) metric.  
- Proposes a practical training algorithm treating the optimal matching as fixed during gradient updates to enable SGD optimization.  
- Positions PMD as a competitor to MMD, claiming no kernel bandwidth, stronger/stabler gradients, smaller mini-batches, and faster convergence.  
- Empirically evaluated on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), matching or outperforming MMD and converging faster.  
- Differentiates from WGANs by directly estimating the primal W1 without needing an auxiliary critic network.  
- Strength: novel, simple, parameter-free method likely to influence primal optimal-transport objectives in deep learning.  
- Strength: solid theoretical grounding with a proof of strong consistency to W1.  
- Strength: thorough and convincing empirical validation, including ablations on population/mini-batch size and matching algorithms.  
- Strength: clear exposition with pseudocode and helpful illustrations.  
- Weakness: computational complexity—exact matching O(N^3), approximate O(N^2), potentially limiting scalability.  
- Weakness: gradient computation relies on two approximations (fixed matching for infinitesimal changes and holding matching across inner-loop updates) with limited analysis of their effects.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of concurrent/related work (e.g., WGAN-GP, other direct W1 estimators like Bellemare et al.).  
- Weakness: generated images are blurry with pixel-wise distances, implying PMD alone may not achieve high-fidelity image synthesis without perceptual/feature-based losses.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, practical alternative to MMD and GAN approaches with strong empirical support.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability concerns due to matching cost and reliance on an insufficiently analyzed heuristic for fixing matchings.  
- Conclusion: overall an excellent, well-written paper with important contributions; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
