- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): a sample-based metric using minimum-weight bipartite matching between sample sets to estimate distribution distance.
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein-1 metric (supported by optimal transport and probability results).
- Proposes a differentiable training algorithm using PMD for deep networks, updated via SGD.
- Practical heuristic: compute expensive matching periodically and reuse fixed matching for several subsequent mini-batch updates.
- Positions PMD as an alternative to MMD: hyperparameter-free (no kernel bandwidth) and claims stronger gradients.
- Claims PMD allows smaller mini-batch sizes and often faster convergence than MMD.
- Relates PMD to Wasserstein estimators (e.g., WGAN) by approximating the primal form without an auxiliary critic network.
- Empirical validation on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) shows PMD outperforms or is competitive with MMD and converges faster.
- Technical quality is high; theoretical claims and experiments are comprehensive and well-supported.
- Main computational weakness: matching step complexity O(N^3) (exact) or O(N^2) (approx), which may be costly as N grows and for simpler models.
- The heuristic of fixing matchings lacks theoretical justification regarding its impact on convergence.
- Paper omits discussion of the curse of dimensionality and its effect on empirical Wasserstein estimation in high dimensions.
- Gradient-flow argument ignores potential issues when the optimal matching is non-unique; stability conditions (e.g., Danskin-type assumptions) are not explicitly addressed.
- Clarity is strong: well-motivated, intuitive PMD definition, clear algorithm pseudocode, and systematic relation to MMD/Wasserstein.
- Minor clarity issue: terminology for "population size N" vs. "mini-batch size |B|" can be confusing and could be renamed for clarity.
- Originality: novel application of bipartite matching as a direct differentiable training objective for deep learning, though underlying components are established.
- Significance: potential practical impact by removing kernel tuning and offering a simple, effective alternative to MMD for several tasks.
- Practical limitations: computational cost may limit scalability and choice of distance metric (e.g., pixel L1) can produce suboptimal/blurry outputs for images.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel and elegant method, practical advantages over MMD, strong empirical results, clear and sound presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability concerns, reliance on an unanalyzed training heuristic, and insufficient discussion of Wasserstein estimation limitations.
- Conclusion: overall strong, theoretically grounded, and empirically effective paper; recommendation for acceptance despite noted limitations.
