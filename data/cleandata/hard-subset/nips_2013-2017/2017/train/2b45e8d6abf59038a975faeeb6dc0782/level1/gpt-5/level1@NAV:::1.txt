This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of distributional distance defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching between two finite populations, and uses it as a differentiable training objective for deep models. The authors show PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance via standard results (Varadarajan, stability of OT) and report advantages over MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling: no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and the ability to train with smaller SGD minibatches. The work connects to prior NIPS literature on kernel two-sample testing and GMMNs (Gretton et al.; Li et al.) and to recent Wasserstein-based training (e.g., WGAN). However, it omits an important line of OT-based learning with entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) and their differentiable, GPU-friendly implementations widely used for learning with empirical measures.

Strengths:
- Clear formulation tying PMD to empirical OT with uniform weights; consistency proof is correct and succinct.
- Practical algorithmic considerations (freezing matchings across steps; approximate O(N^2) matching) and empirical evidence that smaller minibatches suffice.
- Convincing qualitative and some quantitative improvements over MMD on Office and small-scale image generation; useful gradient magnitude analysis.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty appears incremental: PMD largely recovers empirical 1-Wasserstein; the main contribution is to package assignment-based OT as a training loss. Missing comparisons to strong, closely related alternatives: Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi 2013), adversarial/domain-adversarial methods (DANN, JAN), and WGAN/Cramér distance on generation.
- Differentiability/gradient claims need care: Eq. (3) ignores the dependence of the optimal matching on parameters. The estimator is piecewise smooth with discontinuities when the assignment changes; “unbiased” is asserted but not justified. A subgradient argument or almost-everywhere differentiability discussion would strengthen the paper.
- Scalability is unclear: O(N^3) exact or O(N^2) approximate matching becomes costly beyond N~10^3; no end-to-end wall-clock comparison against Sinkhorn, which is GPU-friendly and differentiable.
- Evaluation is limited: no modern generative metrics (IS/FID), low-res grayscale datasets, and domain adaptation compares only to MMD regularization.

Quality: Sound core, but empirical scope and baselines are limited; gradient treatment needs clarification. Clarity: Generally well-written. Originality: Modest. Significance: Potentially useful, but impact uncertain without stronger baselines and scalable comparisons.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, well-motivated objective with consistency.
- Practical training benefits over MMD.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing key related work and comparisons (Sinkhorn, DANN/WGAN).
- Limited experimental rigor and scalability analysis.
- Unclear gradient justification.

Overall: Borderline reject; promising idea but requires broader comparisons and tighter analysis.
