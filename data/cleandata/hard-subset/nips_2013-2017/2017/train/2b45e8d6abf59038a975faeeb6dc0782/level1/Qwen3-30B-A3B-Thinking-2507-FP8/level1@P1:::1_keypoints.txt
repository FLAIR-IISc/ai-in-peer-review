- Introduces PMD: minimum-weight matching estimator for distribution distance, proven strongly consistent for the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Presents an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD and applies it to domain adaptation and generative modeling.  
- Claims advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and ability to use smaller mini-batches.  
- Theoretical strength: solid consistency proof and clear connections to Wasserstein and MMD theory.  
- Empirical validation: thorough experiments and ablations on population/mini-batch sizes and matching algorithms.  
- Clarity: well-organized paper with clear notation, useful figures, and helpful pseudocode.  
- Originality: novel application of minimum-weight matching as a direct estimator in deep learning, distinct from prior OT/MMD/WGAN work.  
- Significance: addresses practical MMD limitations and shows faster convergence and potential real-world utility.  
- Theoretical weakness: lacks finite-sample error bounds; a preliminary bound would strengthen the contribution.  
- Empirical limitation: blurry SVHN/LFW generative results blamed on L1 distance, with no exploration of alternative distances or comparisons to other Wasserstein-based GANs.  
- Computational concern: exact matching is O(N^3); paper does not provide detailed training-time comparisons vs. MMD across batch sizes.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-founded, practically advantageous, clearly written, and empirically validated.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete theoretical analysis, suboptimal generative results, and insufficient computational/benchmark comparisons.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to add finite-sample analysis and more comprehensive generative/computational comparisons.
