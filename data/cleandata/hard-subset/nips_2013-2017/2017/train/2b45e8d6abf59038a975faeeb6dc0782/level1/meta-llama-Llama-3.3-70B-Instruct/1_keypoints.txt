- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), estimating distribution distance via minimum-weight matching between two random populations.
- Shows PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.
- Introduces a stochastic gradient descent algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD.
- Paper is well-written and clearly presented.
- Provides thorough theoretical analysis of the relationship between PMD and the Wasserstein metric.
- Demonstrates advantages of PMD over MMD: fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch size.
- Experimental results show PMD outperforms MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling in performance and convergence speed.
- Strength: novel and theoretically grounded approach to estimating distribution distance.
- Strength: detailed analysis connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric.
- Strength: practical advantages over MMD (hyperparameters, gradients, mini-batch size).
- Strength: promising applications demonstrated in domain adaptation and generative modeling.
- Weakness: computational cost may be high for large population sizes.
- Weakness: lacks finite-sample error bounds for theoretical completeness.
- Weakness: limited experimental evaluation (only two tasks, not comprehensive comparisons).
- For acceptance: novelty, theoretical contributions, and promising empirical results support acceptance.
- Against acceptance: scalability concerns, missing finite-sample guarantees, and limited experiments argue for caution.
- Overall recommendation: accept, with a request that authors address computational cost and finite-sample bounds in future work.
