- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of distributional distance using minimum-weight bipartite matching as a differentiable training objective for deep models.
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance using standard empirical OT results.
- Claims advantages over MMD: no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and ability to train with smaller SGD minibatches.
- Connects to prior kernel two-sample testing and GMMN literature and to Wasserstein-based training (e.g., WGAN).
- Omits important related work on entropy-regularized Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi 2013) and GPU-friendly differentiable implementations.
- Strength: clear formulation linking PMD to empirical OT with uniform weights and a concise, correct consistency proof.
- Strength: practical algorithmic tricks (freezing matchings, approximate O(N^2) matching) and empirical evidence that smaller minibatches work.
- Strength: convincing qualitative and some quantitative improvements over MMD on Office and small-scale image generation, plus useful gradient magnitude analysis.
- Weakness: novelty appears incremental—largely recovers empirical 1-Wasserstein and packages assignment-based OT as a training loss.
- Weakness: missing comparisons to strong alternatives (Sinkhorn OT, adversarial/domain-adversarial methods like DANN/JAN, and WGAN/Cramér).
- Weakness: differentiability and gradient claims are under-justified; ignores dependence of optimal matching on parameters and needs subgradient or a.e. differentiability discussion.
- Weakness: scalability concerns—exact O(N^3) or approximate O(N^2) matching becomes costly beyond N~10^3 and no wall-clock comparison to GPU-friendly Sinkhorn is provided.
- Weakness: limited evaluation—no modern generative metrics (IS/FID), low-resolution grayscale datasets, and domain adaptation compared only to MMD.
- Assessment: sound core idea but limited empirical scope and baselines; clarity is good, originality modest, and significance uncertain without broader comparisons and scalability analysis.
- Arguments for acceptance: simple, well-motivated objective with consistency and practical training benefits over MMD.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing key related work and comparisons, limited experimental rigor and scalability analysis, and unclear gradient justification.
- Overall recommendation: borderline reject—promising idea but requires broader comparisons and tighter analysis to be convincing.
