- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): average cost of minimum-weight bipartite matching between sample sets, a sample-based distance estimator.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1).  
- Provides a method to backpropagate through PMD by assuming the optimal matching is locally constant, enabling SGD training.  
- Claims practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger/non-vanishing gradients, and flexible decoupling of population size and mini-batch updates.  
- Proposes a training scheme computing matching on a larger population N while updating on smaller mini-batches B to increase update frequency and speed convergence.  
- Empirically evaluates PMD on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, often outperforming or matching MMD with faster convergence; ablations support population and batch effects.  
- Original and significant: offers a novel primal-form Wasserstein alternative complementing dual approaches like WGAN.  
- Theoretical contribution: leverages optimal transport results for consistency and provides a practical gradient derivation under reasonable assumptions.  
- Main weakness — computational cost: exact matching is O(N^3) (Hungarian) and approximate O(N^2), posing scalability limits for large populations.  
- Main weakness — gradient non-smoothness: relying on locally constant matching ignores points where matching changes; potential optimization instability or bias is not analyzed.  
- Main weakness — dependence on ground metric: using simple metrics (L1/L2) is ill-suited for high-dimensional perceptual data (e.g., images), explaining blurry outputs versus learned critic metrics.  
- Empirical gap: lacks direct comparison to WGAN, missing an important benchmark given similar Wasserstein motivation.  
- Overall conclusion: technically sound, well-presented, and practically promising; recommended for acceptance despite scalability and metric-choice concerns.
