**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel, sample-based method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average distance of pairs in a minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples (populations) drawn from the respective distributions. The central theoretical contribution is proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). The authors propose an algorithm to use PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep learning models by treating the optimal matching as fixed during gradient updates, allowing for efficient optimization via SGD.

The paper positions PMD as a direct competitor to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a widely used metric in tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling. The authors argue that PMD overcomes several key limitations of MMD: it has no sensitive kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, provides stronger and more stable gradients, and allows for the use of smaller mini-batch sizes during training, leading to faster convergence. These claims are evaluated on two applications: unsupervised domain adaptation on the Office dataset and generative modeling on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW. In both settings, experiments show PMD outperforming or matching MMD in final performance while converging faster. This work is related to the concurrent rise of Wasserstein metric applications in deep learning, such as Wasserstein GANs (WGAN), but differentiates itself by directly estimating the primal form of the W1 metric without requiring an auxiliary critic network.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful idea. Using minimum weight matching as a direct, parameter-free estimator for the W1 distance in a deep learning context is a novel and significant contribution. It provides a compelling alternative to both kernel-based methods like MMD and adversary-based methods like WGAN. The proposed training procedure is elegant and practical. The idea is likely to be influential and could inspire further work on primal-based optimal transport objectives in deep learning.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is well-grounded in optimal transport theory. The proof that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the W1 metric provides a solid theoretical foundation for the approach. This formal connection lends credibility to the method beyond its empirical success.

3.  **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in two distinct and important applications (domain adaptation and generative modeling). The comparisons to MMD are direct and highlight the claimed advantages. For instance, the results in Table 1 show a clear performance gain in several domain adaptation tasks, and Figure 3 effectively visualizes PMD's faster convergence and reduced hyper-parameter sensitivity. The ablation studies in Figure 5 regarding population size, mini-batch size, and the choice of matching algorithm are excellent and strongly support the paper's claims about PMD's robustness and efficiency benefits.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The definition of PMD, its connection to the Wasserstein metric, and the parameter learning algorithm are explained logically and concisely. The inclusion of pseudocode and a graphical illustration (Figure 1) greatly aids understanding.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The primary drawback of PMD is the computational cost of solving the minimum weight matching problem, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm and O(N^2) for the approximation used. This is computationally more expensive than MMD, which is O(N^2) but with a smaller constant factor. While the authors argue this is acceptable relative to the cost of backpropagation and show good results with an N of a few thousands, this complexity could become a significant bottleneck for applications requiring very large population sizes. Mentioning that the matching algorithms can be parallelized on GPU is helpful, but providing such an implementation or profiling would have made the scalability argument more compelling.

2.  **Approximation in Gradient Computation:** The proposed learning algorithm relies on two key approximations: (i) the optimal matching M* is treated as a constant with respect to infinitesimal parameter changes, and (ii) M* is held fixed for several SGD updates in an inner loop. While the first point is a standard subgradient argument and is valid almost everywhere, the second is a heuristic. The paper does not analyze the sensitivity of the training to the number of inner-loop updates or provide guidance on setting this value. This could potentially affect training stability if the matching changes significantly between outer-loop iterations.

3.  **Limited Discussion of Concurrent Work:** The paper appropriately contrasts PMD with the original WGAN. However, given the NIPS 2017 timeline, a discussion of more stable variants like WGAN-GP would have strengthened the paper by placing it in the context of the latest advancements in Wasserstein-based training. Furthermore, other concurrent works aiming to directly estimate the W1 metric (e.g., Bellemare et al., 2017, cited as [2]) are mentioned in the references but not discussed, which is a missed opportunity for a more complete comparison.

4.  **Quality of Generated Images:** While the generated images are shown to be less noisy than those from GMMN, they remain blurry (Fig. 4), which is a known limitation of models optimized with pixel-wise L1/L2 distances. The authors acknowledge this and rightly suggest that performance improves in a learned feature space (the 'ae' models). However, this suggests that for high-fidelity image generation, PMD with a simple distance metric is not sufficient on its own and may need to be combined with perceptual losses, similar to GANs.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and practical method for distribution matching.
*   Provides a strong alternative to MMD, demonstrably overcoming several of its key weaknesses.
*   Offers a different perspective on using the Wasserstein metric compared to the popular GAN framework.
*   Empirical results are comprehensive and strongly support the paper's claims.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational cost of the matching algorithm is a notable limitation for scalability.
*   The training algorithm relies on a heuristic (fixing the matching) whose effects are not fully analyzed.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution. The proposed PMD method is elegant, well-motivated, and supported by both strong theory and compelling empirical results. The weaknesses, primarily concerning computational scalability, are acknowledged and do not detract significantly from the importance of the core contribution. The paper is well-written and will be of high interest to researchers working on generative models, domain adaptation, and statistical distance measures. I strongly recommend acceptance.
