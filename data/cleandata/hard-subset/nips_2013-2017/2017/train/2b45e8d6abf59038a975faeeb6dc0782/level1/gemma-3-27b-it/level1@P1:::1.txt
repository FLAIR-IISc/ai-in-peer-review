This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating distribution distance, particularly in the context of deep learning. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching between samples from two distributions and is shown to be a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors demonstrate empirically that PMD overcomes limitations of MMD—sensitivity to bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and large mini-batch requirements—and outperforms it in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

The core idea is well-motivated. MMD, while widely used, *does* suffer from the mentioned drawbacks, and the Wasserstein metric is a theoretically appealing alternative. Defining PMD as a discrete approximation of the Wasserstein metric via optimal transport is a sound approach. The theoretical connection established (strong consistency) is a significant contribution. The algorithmic proposal—using PMD as a training objective and detailing the gradient calculation—is clear and logical. The empirical evaluation on domain adaptation and generative modeling provides compelling evidence that PMD can indeed be more effective than MMD. The comparison regarding gradient behavior (Fig. 2b) is particularly insightful.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The proof of strong consistency is a valuable theoretical result linking PMD to the Wasserstein metric.
*   **Addresses Limitations of MMD:**  PMD tackles real practical issues with MMD (bandwidth selection, gradient issues, batch size).
*   **Clear and Well-Defined Algorithm:** The paper provides a clear description of the PMD calculation and how to incorporate it into a training pipeline.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate PMD's effectiveness in relevant deep learning tasks.  The convergence speed improvement (Fig. 3a) is noteworthy.
*   **Good Analysis:** The ablation studies concerning population size and mini-batch size (Fig. 5a) and the gradient analysis (Fig. 2b) provide valuable insights.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the O(N<sup>3</sup>) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, the practical implications for larger datasets aren’t fully addressed.  The move to an approximated matching algorithm is helpful but raises questions about the accuracy trade-off, and the impact of this approximation isn’t thoroughly investigated.
*   **Distance Metric Choice:** The paper consistently uses L1 distance for PMD. While this facilitates stronger gradients, it might not be optimal for all data modalities (e.g., images). A discussion of alternative distance metrics and their potential impact would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the results on domain adaptation and generative modeling are positive, expanding the experimental evaluation to other tasks where distributional distance estimation is critical (e.g., anomaly detection, representation learning) would strengthen the findings.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** The notation could be slightly more consistent (e.g., use of θX and θY vs. XθX and YθY).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to distribution distance estimation.
*   It addresses known limitations of a widely used method (MMD).
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical benefits of PMD.
*   The analysis provides useful insights into the behavior of PMD and MMD.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the optimal matching algorithm could be a limitation for very large datasets, despite the use of approximation.
*   The experimental evaluation, while strong, could be broadened to encompass a wider range of applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The proposed PMD offers a compelling alternative to MMD, addressing its shortcomings while maintaining strong theoretical guarantees. The experimental results are convincing, and the analysis is insightful. The computational concerns are valid, but the authors acknowledge them and propose an approximate solution.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity issues and to provide a more thorough discussion of the computational trade-offs associated with the matching algorithm and the impact of the distance metric choice.  Further experiments on additional tasks would be a strong addition to a revised version.
