Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets X and Y with equal cardinality, using a ground cost d (e.g., L1). PMD is thus the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (a.k.a. Earth Mover’s Distance) between the empirical measures p̂X and p̂Y when both are uniform over N points. The authors argue PMD is advantageous over MMD as a training loss: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches by reusing a fixed matching across several gradient steps. They show PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the population Wasserstein-1 distance, provide an SGD procedure that ignores gradients through the discrete matching, and evaluate on two settings: unsupervised domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), comparing mainly to MMD/GMMN.

The work sits at the intersection of OT-based discrepancies and sample-based training objectives for implicit models. It is closely related to classical EMD (Rubner et al. 2000) and the theory in Villani (2008). It is also related to WGAN (Arjovsky et al. 2017) and to computational OT advances such as entropic regularization and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi 2013), which provide smooth, GPU-friendly, differentiable approximations. In domain adaptation, aligning source/target feature distributions with MMD is common (e.g., DDC, JAN), and OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al., 2014; not cited) is highly relevant.

Quality
- Theory: The identification of PMD with empirical W1 is correct: with two uniform empirical measures of equal size, the Kantorovich LP reduces to an assignment problem, and the optimum is attained at a permutation. The consistency claim is plausible, but the proof sketch is incomplete and missing standard assumptions. To be precise, W1 is finite only if both distributions have finite first moments; the stability result requires integrability/tightness. Convergence of optimal plans is subtle when the optimal plan is non-unique; one typically shows that any limit point of γN is optimal, and the optimal cost converges—clarify uniqueness assumptions or argue convergence of costs rather than plans. The statement that the stochastic gradient in Eq. (3) is “unbiased” while ignoring dependence of M* on θ needs justification (e.g., envelope theorem for discrete argmin, subgradient calculus). As written, the estimator is best viewed as a valid subgradient, but unbiasedness of the Monte Carlo estimator of ∇ E D_N is not established.
- Algorithm: Using matching computed on populations of size N but applying several inner-loop SGD steps on smaller mini-batches with fixed M* is heuristic; a convergence argument is missing. Complexity-wise, O(N^3) Hungarian or O(N^2) approximate matching per outer iteration is nontrivial; practical training hinges on approximate matching and infrequent updates. The paper notes possible GPU acceleration but does not implement it.
- Empirics: Domain adaptation results compare only to MMD with VGG features; no comparison to stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD) or OT-based methods. Gains are modest and task-dependent. For generative modeling, comparisons are only to GMMN; no WGAN/WGAN-GP or Sinkhorn-OT baselines, and no quantitative metrics (Inception score/FID). Visuals show PMD reduces noise but is blurry with pixel L1; training in an AE code space helps, which weakens the claim of generality of PMD as a drop-in pixel-space loss.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful pseudocode and figures. Some notational issues (N as “population” vs mini-batch) could be cleaned up. Theoretical statements should include explicit assumptions (finite first moment, Polish space, continuity of d) and precise conclusions. The claim of unbiased gradients should be corrected.

Originality
The core notion—min-cost matching between empirical samples as a discrepancy—is the classical empirical Wasserstein-1 distance. The novelty lies in the training procedure that reuses a fixed matching over multiple SGD steps and the empirical observation of stronger gradients than MMD. Relative to prior art, the omission of entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi 2013) and OT-based domain adaptation is a significant gap; those methods address differentiability and computational efficiency and have been used in learning pipelines before 2017.

Significance
Using empirical W1 as a training objective is of interest, and the gradient-strength observation is useful. However, the lack of comparisons to the most relevant baselines (WGAN(-GP), Sinkhorn OT) and limited empirical scope reduce the impact. The approach may be valuable when exact matching is affordable and one desires a parameter-free, non-adversarial loss, but scalability and robustness in high dimensions remain concerns.

Pros
- Clear connection to Wasserstein-1 with a simple, parameter-free objective.
- Practical training recipe that often yields stronger per-sample gradients than MMD.
- Empirical evidence of faster convergence and reduced sensitivity to hyperparameters vs MMD.
- The consistency result (with proper assumptions) is standard and reassuring.

Cons
- Limited novelty: PMD equals empirical W1/EMD; key computational OT literature (Sinkhorn) and OT-based DA are not cited or compared.
- Theoretical gaps: missing moment assumptions; plan convergence vs cost convergence; unjustified “unbiased” gradient claim; no convergence guarantees for the inner-loop heuristic.
- Empirics lack strong baselines and quantitative metrics; image results are blurry in pixel space.
- Complexity may be prohibitive without GPU-accelerated matching; scalability analysis is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add the missing assumptions and a rigorous proof sketch (finite first moments; convergence of costs; clarify subgradient interpretation of Eq. (3)).
- Compare against Sinkhorn divergences (entropic OT) and WGAN/WGAN-GP on generation; use standard quantitative metrics (IS/FID).
- In domain adaptation, compare to DANN, JAN, CMD, and OT-based DA (e.g., Courty et al.), and consider class-conditional PMD to avoid mismatching across classes.
- Provide ablations on frequency of matching recomputation, effect of approximate vs exact matching on both accuracy and wall-clock, and scaling beyond N=2000 with GPU implementations.
- Discuss high-dimensional sample complexity of W1 and explore alternatives (sliced Wasserstein, learned feature spaces) more systematically.

Recommendation
Borderline. The idea is sensible and can be useful, but the paper in its current form lacks novelty relative to established OT methods and needs stronger theory and comparisons to be competitive for NIPS 2017.
