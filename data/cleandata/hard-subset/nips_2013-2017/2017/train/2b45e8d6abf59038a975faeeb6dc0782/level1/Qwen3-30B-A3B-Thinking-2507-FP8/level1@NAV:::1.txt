

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable estimator of distribution distance based on samples, as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors define PMD as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations, prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, and demonstrate its advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements. The paper applies PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing superior performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding with clear proofs connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric, addressing a key limitation of MMD. The empirical results are thorough, with comprehensive comparisons across multiple datasets and tasks, and the authors provide insightful analysis of gradient distributions and computational efficiency. The writing is clear and well-organized, with helpful pseudocode and figures illustrating key concepts.

Strengths include the novel approach to distribution distance estimation, solid theoretical foundation, and compelling empirical evidence of practical advantages over MMD. The work builds meaningfully on previous NIPS contributions like MMD (Gretton et al., 2012) and WGAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017), while addressing their limitations.

Weaknesses include limited comparison with other Wasserstein-based methods beyond MMD, and the generative modeling results on SVHN/LFW being described as "blurry" despite using L1 distance. The computational complexity discussion of the Hungarian algorithm could be expanded for larger N, though the authors note approximate algorithms can mitigate this.

Quality: Strong theoretical foundation and empirical validation. The authors carefully address strengths/weaknesses of PMD versus MMD.

Clarity: Well-written, well-organized, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Originality: PMD represents a novel approach to distribution distance estimation, distinct from previous NIPS work on MMD and Wasserstein metrics.

Significance: Addresses a practical limitation of widely used MMD, with demonstrated benefits across two important deep learning applications.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel approach with strong theoretical and empirical validation that overcomes significant limitations of MMD; well-motivated and well-executed work.

Arguments against acceptance: Limited comparison with other Wasserstein-based methods; minor limitations in generative modeling results.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in distribution distance estimation with practical benefits for deep learning applications.
