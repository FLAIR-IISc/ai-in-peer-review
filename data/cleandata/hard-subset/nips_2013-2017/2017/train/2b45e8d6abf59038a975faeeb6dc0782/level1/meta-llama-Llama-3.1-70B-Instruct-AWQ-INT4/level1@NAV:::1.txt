This paper proposes a new method for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples, called Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD). PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, and it is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors also propose an algorithm to learn the parameters of the distributions using PMD as an objective.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its advantages over existing methods, such as Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the theoretical properties of PMD and its relationship with other discrepancies.

The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in two deep learning tasks, domain adaptation and generative modeling. The results show that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed in both tasks.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a new method for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples, which is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the theoretical properties of PMD and its relationship with other discrepancies.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in two deep learning tasks, domain adaptation and generative modeling.
* The paper is well-written and well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its advantages over existing methods.

Weaknesses:

* The paper assumes that the samples are i.i.d., which may not be the case in practice.
* The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, which may be a concern for large-scale datasets.
* The paper does not provide a comparison with other methods, such as Wasserstein GAN (WGAN), which may be a relevant baseline for generative modeling tasks.
