- Paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) equal to the empirical 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between two equal-size sample sets.  
- Authors claim PMD is preferable to MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, and ability to reuse a fixed matching across multiple SGD steps.  
- PMD relates to classical EMD/optimal transport (Rubner et al., Villani) and to WGAN and entropic OT/Sinkhorn methods (Cuturi), but some relevant OT and domain-adaptation works are omitted.  

- Theory: identification of PMD with empirical W1 is correct, but the consistency proof sketch is incomplete and missing standard assumptions (e.g., finite first moments, tightness).  
- Theory: convergence of optimal plans is subtle; need to clarify whether claims refer to plan convergence or cost convergence and state uniqueness assumptions.  
- Theory: claim that the stochastic gradient ignoring dependence of the matching M* on θ is “unbiased” is not justified; should be framed as a valid subgradient or proven via envelope theorem/discrete argmin results.  

- Algorithm: reusing matchings computed on population size N for several inner-loop SGD steps is heuristic and lacks convergence guarantees.  
- Algorithm: exact matching complexity (Hungarian O(N^3)) or approximate O(N^2) per outer iteration is heavy; practical training depends on approximate/infrequent matching and GPU acceleration (not implemented).  

- Empirics: domain adaptation comparisons are limited (only MMD with VGG features) and miss stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD) and OT-based DA methods.  
- Empirics: generative modeling comparisons are limited to GMMN; missing WGAN/WGAN-GP and Sinkhorn-OT baselines and standard quantitative metrics (Inception score, FID).  
- Empirics: visual results show noise reduction but blur in pixel-L1 space; using AE code space helps but weakens claim of PMD as a drop-in pixel-space loss.  
- Empirics: gains are modest, task-dependent, and lacking thorough ablations (matching frequency, exact vs approximate matching, scaling).  

- Clarity: paper is generally clear with helpful pseudocode and figures, but notation (N as “population” vs mini-batch) and missing theoretical assumptions should be fixed.  
- Originality: core idea is classical empirical W1; novelty is in the training recipe (reusing matchings) and empirical observation of stronger gradients, but key OT literature (Sinkhorn) is omitted.  
- Significance: using empirical W1 is interesting and gradients are promising, but lack of comparisons and scalability analyses reduce impact.  

- Pros: clear connection to Wasserstein-1 and a simple, parameter-free objective.  
- Pros: practical training recipe often yields stronger per-sample gradients than MMD and shows faster convergence/reduced hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Pros: consistency result (with proper assumptions) is standard and reassuring.  

- Cons: limited novelty—PMD = empirical W1—and omission of key computational OT literature and OT-based DA methods.  
- Cons: theoretical gaps (missing moment assumptions; unclear plan vs cost convergence; unjustified unbiased gradient claim; no convergence guarantee for inner-loop heuristic).  
- Cons: empirical evaluation lacks strong baselines, quantitative metrics, and shows blurry pixel-space results.  
- Cons: computational complexity and scalability concerns without GPU-accelerated matching or approximate matching analysis.  

- Suggestions: add explicit assumptions and a rigorous proof sketch (finite first moments; cost convergence; subgradient interpretation of Eq. (3)).  
- Suggestions: compare to Sinkhorn/entropic OT and WGAN/WGAN-GP on generation tasks, with IS/FID metrics.  
- Suggestions: in domain adaptation, compare to DANN, JAN, CMD, and OT-based DA; consider class-conditional PMD to avoid class mismatch.  
- Suggestions: provide ablations on matching recomputation frequency, exact vs approximate matching, wall-clock cost, and scaling to larger N with GPU implementations; discuss high-dimensional sample complexity and alternatives (sliced Wasserstein, learned feature spaces).  

- Recommendation: Borderline — sensible idea and potentially useful, but currently lacks novelty relative to established OT methods, stronger theory, and comprehensive empirical comparisons.
