### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based metric for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples (populations) drawn from the respective distributions, where the cost is the average distance between matched pairs. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1).

The core contribution is a learning algorithm that uses PMD as a differentiable objective function for training deep neural networks. The parameters of the distributions (e.g., a generator network) are updated via stochastic gradient descent. The gradient is computed with respect to a fixed optimal matching, which is periodically re-computed. This approach is positioned as an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors argue that PMD offers several advantages over MMD, including having no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, providing stronger gradients during training, and allowing the use of smaller mini-batch sizes for SGD updates than the population size used for matching.

The effectiveness of PMD is demonstrated on two deep learning applications: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. In both cases, PMD is compared against MMD, showing superior or competitive performance and faster convergence. This work relates to a growing body of literature on using optimal transport and Wasserstein distances in machine learning, such as Wasserstein GANs (WGAN) [3], but proposes a direct, primal-form estimation of the W1 distance as a training objective, in contrast to the dual-form approximation used in WGAN or the kernel-based integral probability metric of MMD [17, 31].

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness)**

The paper is technically sound. The connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is clearly established by framing the problem as an optimal transport problem between two empirical distributions. The proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3) correctly relies on established results from optimal transport theory (Propositions 3.1 and 3.2). The proposed learning algorithm is well-defined, and the empirical evaluations are thorough in their comparison against MMD. The experiments include ablation studies on the effect of population size, mini-batch size, and the choice of matching algorithm, which supports the paper's claims regarding the practical advantages of PMD.

A minor weakness is the justification for the learning algorithm. The statement that the gradient does not propagate through the matching M* (Eq. 3) relies on the matching being stable under infinitesimal perturbations of the parameters. While generally true, this might not hold at points where the optimal matching changes. More significantly, the proposed algorithm re-uses a fixed matching for several SGD updates (the "inner loop" in Figure 1). This is a practical heuristic to reduce computational cost, but its theoretical implications on convergence and bias are not discussed.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The core concept of PMD is introduced with a simple and intuitive definition (Eq. 1), and its connection to the Wasserstein metric is explained logically. The algorithm for parameter learning is presented with a helpful graphical illustration and pseudocode. The distinctions between PMD and MMD are articulated clearly, both theoretically (Section 3.2) and empirically (Figure 2b). The experimental setup and results are presented in a way that is easy to understand.

**Originality**

The proposed approach is novel. While the Wasserstein metric and minimum weight matching are well-known concepts, their formulation as a direct, differentiable training objective for deep generative models and domain adaptation is a new contribution. It presents a compelling alternative to existing methods. It differs significantly from MMD-based approaches [31] by operating directly on sample distances rather than through a kernel embedding. It also differs from dual-form Wasserstein approximations like WGAN [3] by working with the primal problem, which avoids the need for a separate critic network and associated training stability challenges (e.g., enforcing the Lipschitz constraint).

**Significance**

The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of comparing probability distributions from samples, a fundamental task in modern machine learning. PMD offers a practical method that appears to overcome several well-known drawbacks of MMD, such as sensitivity to the kernel bandwidth and weak gradients. The experimental results, demonstrating improved performance and faster convergence on standard benchmarks for domain adaptation and generative modeling, suggest that PMD could be a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners. The work opens up a new avenue for leveraging optimal transport theory directly in deep learning optimization frameworks.

A notable weakness in this regard is the lack of an experimental comparison to WGAN [3] in the generative modeling section. Given that both PMD and WGAN are motivated by the Wasserstein metric, a direct comparison would have been highly informative for understanding their relative strengths and weaknesses. Additionally, the computational complexity of the matching step (O(N^3) for exact or O(N^2) for approximate matching) is a potential scalability concern compared to MMD, which is O(N^2) but highly parallelizable. While the authors argue the cost is acceptable and point to potential GPU acceleration, this remains a practical limitation for very large population sizes.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty:** Proposes a novel and well-motivated method (PMD) for using a sample-based primal form of the Wasserstein distance as a differentiable loss for deep learning.
2.  **Soundness:** The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is sound, and the claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments.
3.  **Significance:** Addresses a key problem in machine learning and provides a practical alternative to MMD that remedies some of its known deficiencies (hyperparameter tuning, gradient strength).
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, making the concepts and methods accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Missing Comparison:** The lack of an empirical comparison with WGAN, another prominent Wasserstein-based method for generative modeling, is a significant omission that weakens the paper's experimental evaluation.
2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The computational cost of the matching algorithm could be a bottleneck for scaling to very large population sizes, which is a relevant consideration for state-of-the-art deep learning.
3.  **Theoretical Gaps:** The learning algorithm relies on a practical heuristic (fixing the matching for multiple updates) whose theoretical properties are not analyzed.

Overall, the strengths of the paper, particularly its novelty and the practical advantages it demonstrates over MMD, outweigh its weaknesses. The introduction of PMD is a valuable contribution to the field.
