- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based distance estimator defined via minimum weight matching between sample populations.
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.
- Proposes an algorithm to use PMD as a training objective for learning distribution parameters.
- Demonstrates empirical advantages of PMD over MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling, with better performance and faster convergence.
- Situates PMD within prior work on MMD and Wasserstein metrics, highlighting limitations of MMD (kernel sensitivity, weak gradients, large mini-batch needs).
- Highlights PMD advantages over MMD and WGANs: parameter-free and no separate critic network required.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical foundation and proofs supporting PMD’s properties.
- Strength: practical relevance shown through real-world tasks and empirical validation.
- Strength: clear exposition, helpful pseudocode, and solid empirical analysis.
- Strength: originality in combining optimal transport ideas with sample-based divergence estimation.
- Strength: significance—PMD has potential for broad adoption in distribution comparison tasks.
- Weakness: higher computational cost from minimum weight matching (O(N^3) exact, O(N^2) approximate), limiting scalability.
- Weakness: generative outputs can be blurry on datasets like SVHN and LFW, suggesting L1 distance may be suboptimal for images.
- Weakness: limited application scope—evaluations restricted to domain adaptation and generative modeling.
- Weakness: comparisons with WGANs are less thorough; more empirical WGAN comparisons needed.
- Recommendation: accept, with suggestions to reduce computational cost, improve generative quality, and expand empirical comparisons (including WGANs).
