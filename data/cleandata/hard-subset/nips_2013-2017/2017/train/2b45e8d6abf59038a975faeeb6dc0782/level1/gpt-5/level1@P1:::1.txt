Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes population matching discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two equal-sized sample sets, as a differentiable, sample-based estimate of the distance between distributions. The authors show that PMD equals the empirical 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between the two empirical distributions with uniform weights and prove strong consistency to the population Wasserstein metric using standard results (Varadarajan; Villani’s stability). They then propose using PMD as a training objective in deep learning by (i) computing the matching (exact or approximate) on populations of size N, (ii) taking gradients that do not backpropagate through the matching (arguing the matching is locally constant), and (iii) optionally performing several SGD steps with a fixed matching and mini-batch subsampling. They compare PMD to MMD in domain adaptation (Office-31) and in deep generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), claiming PMD has no bandwidth hyper-parameter, provides stronger gradients, and works with smaller mini-batches, leading to better performance and faster convergence in their experiments.

Connections: PMD is the classic empirical OT/EMD objective (Kantorovich–Rubinstein W1 on empirical measures) solved by assignment (Hungarian) or approximate matching. Closely related deep learning works include WGAN (dual form), MMD-based generative modeling (GMMN, MMD-GAN), and domain adaptation with MMD (DAN/JAN) and with OT (e.g., JDOT/OTDA). The paper does not discuss entropic regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi 2013), which provide a fast, differentiable approximation widely used in ML and computer vision by 2017.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The connection to empirical OT and the consistency proof follow known results. The decision to not differentiate through the matching is reasonable: the loss is a pointwise minimum over a finite set of smooth functions, so Danskin’s theorem yields valid (sub)gradients almost everywhere; however, this justification is not made explicit, and the “frozen matching” multi-step SGD heuristic introduces bias with no convergence analysis.
- Experimental support: Results suggest PMD can work in practice and sometimes outperforms MMD. However, baselines are limited. In domain adaptation, only MMD is compared; stronger NIPS-relevant baselines (DANN, DAN/JAN, DSN, CMD, OT-based adaptation) are missing. In generative modeling, results are mainly qualitative; no standard metrics (FID/IS), and no comparisons to WGAN or MMD-GAN. The claim of stronger gradients is supported by a gradient-magnitude histogram under one kernel choice; alternative kernels or learned kernels (MMD-GAN) are not assessed. The scalability of O(N^2)–O(N^3) matching is a concern; GPU parallelization is discussed but not implemented.
- Clarity: Generally readable. Some notational ambiguities (e.g., N as “population size” vs mini-batch, then using |B| as a sub-batch) and minor typos. The algorithmic choice to fix a matching across several updates is described clearly, but theoretical implications are not discussed.
- Evaluation care: Hyper-parameter selection in domain adaptation uses 100 labeled target samples; this departs from the pure unsupervised protocol and may bias comparisons. Error bars/repetitions are only partially reported.

Originality
Conceptually, PMD is the empirical W1/EMD; renaming it “PMD” risks obscuring substantial prior art. Using empirical OT as a training loss is not new in 2017; the major novelty claims are practical (gradient strength, small sub-batches with frozen matching). The paper omits key, closely related works:
- Entropic-regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi 2013), differentiable and much faster; backprop through Sinkhorn was already used in ML by 2016.
- OT for domain adaptation (Courty et al., JDOT/OTDA), directly relevant to Sec. 4.1.
- Stronger MMD variants (MMD-GAN) and alternative distances with improved gradients (Cramér/energy distance).
- Sliced Wasserstein distances for generative modeling.

Significance
If robust and scalable, the observation that empirical OT with simple L1 ground cost can serve as a practical, parameter-free training objective is potentially useful. The empirical advantage over plain RBF-MMD is plausible and relevant. However, without comparisons to Sinkhorn-based OT and to stronger baselines, and without quantitative generative metrics, it is difficult to assess impact. Moreover, known sample complexity issues of W1 in high dimensions (rates degrading with dimension) are not discussed; the toy study indicates slow convergence (≈ N^-0.23 in 5D), suggesting large N may be needed.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive objective; parameter-free (beyond choice of ground metric).
- Correct connection to Wasserstein W1 and consistency proof.
- Practical training recipe (frozen matching, sub-batch updates) that can reduce per-iteration cost.
- Empirical evidence of better convergence and sometimes better accuracy than plain MMD on selected tasks.
- Useful diagnostic on gradient magnitudes.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: PMD is empirical OT/EMD; key prior work (Sinkhorn OT; OT for domain adaptation) is not cited or compared.
- Scalability: O(N^2) distance + matching each outer step; no GPU implementation; no comparison against Sinkhorn which is faster and differentiable.
- Evaluation gaps: Domain adaptation compared only to MMD; generative results lack FID/IS and comparisons to WGAN/MMD-GAN; qualitative images are blurry with pixel L1.
- Heuristic optimization (frozen matching) lacks theoretical guarantees; no analysis of bias or stability when matchings change.
- The claim of “stronger gradients” is kernel-dependent; MMD with adaptive/learned kernels or energy/Cramér distances could mitigate reported issues.
- Use of labeled target data for hyper-parameter tuning deviates from the strict unsupervised protocol.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include and compare against Sinkhorn OT losses (with/without debiasing) in both tasks; report wall-clock and accuracy/FID.
- Add state-of-the-art baselines: DANN/DAN/JAN/DSN/CMD and OT-based DA; WGAN/MMD-GAN in generative modeling; report FID/IS and training stability curves.
- Justify gradients formally via Danskin’s theorem; discuss convergence when matchings change; explore a “recompute matching every step” variant.
- Explore better ground costs (feature/perceptual spaces, learned metrics, sliced/Proj. Wasserstein) to address blurriness and improve gradients.
- Analyze sample complexity and dimensionality effects; report performance vs N with confidence intervals; discuss trade-offs vs MMD.
- Implement and report GPU-accelerated matching or adopt Sinkhorn for scalability.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear formulation and practical training procedure with some empirical benefits.
- Parameter-free alternative to MMD that may be easier to tune in practice.
- Highlights useful properties of empirical OT as a loss in deep learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Low conceptual novelty; significant prior art omitted.
- Insufficient comparisons to relevant baselines and metrics; limited empirical scope.
- Scalability and optimization concerns not fully addressed.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is useful and could be impactful with stronger empirical comparisons (especially to Sinkhorn-based OT and modern baselines) and clearer positioning relative to existing OT literature.
