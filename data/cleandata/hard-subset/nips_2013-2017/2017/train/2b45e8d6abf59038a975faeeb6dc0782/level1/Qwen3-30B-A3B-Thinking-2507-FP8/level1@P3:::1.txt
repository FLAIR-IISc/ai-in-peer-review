

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations, as a differentiable distribution distance estimator. The authors establish PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and propose an algorithm for learning distribution parameters using PMD. The paper demonstrates PMD's advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work:** The paper addresses a well-known limitation of MMD in deep learning: its sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and need for large mini-batches. While the paper correctly identifies these limitations, it overlooks a critical connection to existing work. The core concept of PMD is essentially the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) on empirical distributions, a well-established metric in computer vision and data mining since Rubner et al. (1998). The paper also fails to cite recent NIPS work on Wasserstein distance approximations, such as the Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017) and Cramer Distance (Bellemare et al., 2017), which address similar challenges.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper provides a clear and theoretically grounded connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, establishing strong consistency.
2. The empirical results consistently show PMD outperforms MMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling, with faster convergence.
3. The authors provide a practical algorithm for using PMD as a training objective, with a clever observation that the matching doesn't need to be recomputed at every gradient step.
4. The comparison of gradient magnitudes in Figure 2(b) effectively demonstrates the claimed advantage of stronger gradients.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Theoretical Gap:** The paper claims PMD is a "strongly consistent estimator" of the first Wasserstein metric, but the theoretical analysis only establishes equivalence to the Wasserstein metric on empirical distributions. The convergence rate (N^-0.23 in Figure 2a) is significantly slower than typical O(N^-1/2) rates for statistical estimators, which weakens the claim of strong consistency in practical settings.
   
2. **Computational Complexity:** The paper acknowledges the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm but downplays its significance by comparing it to "O(N × BackProp)." However, for large-scale applications (e.g., N=1000), the matching step becomes computationally expensive. The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how this impacts training time for large models or datasets.

3. **Missing Comparisons:** The paper fails to compare PMD with other Wasserstein approximations like the Sinkhorn algorithm (Cuturi, 2013) or the Cramer Distance (Bellemare et al., 2017), which also address MMD's limitations with different computational trade-offs.

4. **Generative Modeling Limitations:** The generative modeling results (Figure 4) show PMD generates "less noisy" images, but the paper doesn't provide quantitative metrics (e.g., FID scores) to substantiate this claim. The blurry images on SVHN and LFW suggest PMD may not be ideal for high-resolution image generation.

5. **Oversights in Related Work:** The paper fails to cite the foundational EMD work by Rubner et al. (1998) and doesn't properly contextualize its contribution relative to existing Wasserstein approximations.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a useful alternative to MMD with practical advantages, but the theoretical foundation is weaker than claimed, and the computational concerns are underplayed. The authors should address these limitations in a revision.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- PMD offers a practical solution to real problems with MMD in deep learning.
- The empirical results are convincing across two important tasks.
- The theoretical connection to Wasserstein metric is valid, even if not novel.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The theoretical claims about consistency are overstated given the slow convergence rate observed.
- The paper's failure to cite foundational work on EMD and recent Wasserstein approximations weakens its novelty claim.
- The computational complexity concerns are significant for large-scale applications but not adequately addressed.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper makes a useful contribution to the field of distribution distance estimation for deep learning, but the theoretical foundation needs strengthening, and the computational implications require more careful discussion. The paper is of moderate significance as it addresses a practical limitation of a widely used metric, but the novelty is somewhat diminished by the close relationship to established EMD concepts. With revisions addressing the theoretical and computational concerns and properly situating the work within the existing literature, this paper could be a strong contribution to NIPS 2017. As written, it's borderline for acceptance.
