This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the distance between two distributions, particularly in the context of deep learning. The core idea – minimizing the cost of matching samples between distributions – is intuitive and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify key weaknesses of MMD (bandwidth sensitivity, vanishing gradients, large batch size requirements) and position PMD as addressing these issues. The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is a significant contribution, establishing PMD as a strongly consistent estimator. The application to domain adaptation and generative modeling provides practical validation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PMD offers a fresh perspective on sample-based distribution divergence estimation, diverging from the kernel-based approach of MMD.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The proof establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric is a substantial result, grounding the method in solid mathematical principles.
* **Addressing MMD Limitations:** The paper convincingly argues how PMD overcomes the practical challenges associated with MMD – bandwidth tuning, gradient issues, and batch size limitations.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate PMD’s competitive and, in some cases, superior performance compared to MMD. The convergence speed analysis is also valuable.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are accessible. The inclusion of pseudocode and a graphical illustration for the learning algorithm are helpful.
* **Thorough Experimentation**: The experiments cover parameter sensitivity, convergence speed, and the impact of matching algorithm choice, providing a comprehensive evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While PMD avoids the bandwidth parameter, the cost of solving the minimum weight matching problem (O(N^3) for exact, O(N^2) for approximated) could be a bottleneck for very large datasets. The authors acknowledge this and discuss approximations, but a more detailed analysis of scalability for extremely high-dimensional data or large sample sizes would be beneficial. The GPU acceleration suggestion is good, but a preliminary implementation or benchmark would strengthen the claim.
* **Distance Metric Dependence:** The performance of PMD is directly tied to the choice of the distance metric *d(x, y)* used in the matching. The paper primarily uses L1 distance. While effective in the experiments, a broader exploration of other distance metrics (e.g., Euclidean, cosine) and their impact on performance, particularly in the generative modeling task, is warranted. The observation that L1 isn't ideal for natural images is important, but a more systematic investigation of alternatives is missing.
* **Generative Modeling Results:** The generative modeling results show improvements, but the generated images are still blurry. This suggests that while PMD is a better objective than MMD, further improvements are needed. A discussion of why PMD struggles with generating high-quality images (beyond the L1 distance limitation) would be helpful.  Comparing against more recent generative models (e.g., WGAN-GP) would provide a stronger benchmark.
* **Limited Theoretical Exploration of Gradient Properties:** While the authors claim stronger gradients, the analysis in Fig 2(b) is somewhat superficial. A more rigorous theoretical justification for the improved gradient behavior, beyond the observation that the L1 gradient is constant, would be valuable.
* **Minor Clarity Issue:** The notation in equation (3) is slightly confusing. It's not immediately clear why M* remains fixed when θ changes infinitesimally. A more explicit explanation would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to distribution divergence estimation.
* The identified limitations of MMD are significant, and PMD offers a compelling solution.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in practical applications.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the matching problem could limit scalability.
* The choice of distance metric is critical, and further exploration is needed.
* The generative modeling results, while improved over MMD, are not state-of-the-art.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The introduction of PMD is a valuable contribution to the field, and the theoretical and empirical results are convincing. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above (especially regarding scalability analysis, distance metric exploration, and generative modeling results). Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed discussion on the computational cost and scalability of PMD, explore alternative distance metrics, and potentially compare against more recent generative models.
