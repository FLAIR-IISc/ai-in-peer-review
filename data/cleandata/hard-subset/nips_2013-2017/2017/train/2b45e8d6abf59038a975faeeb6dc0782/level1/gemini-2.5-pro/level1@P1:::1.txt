**Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"**

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel, sample-based metric for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. They then propose an algorithm to use PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep neural networks, where parameters are updated via stochastic gradient descent. A key aspect of their training algorithm is an efficient heuristic where the expensive matching computation is performed periodically, and the resulting fixed matching is used for several subsequent gradient updates on smaller mini-batches.

The paper positions PMD as an alternative to the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). It argues that PMD overcomes several of MMD's drawbacks: it is hyperparameter-free (no kernel bandwidth), provides stronger gradients, and allows for the use of smaller mini-batch sizes for SGD updates, leading to faster convergence. The connection to other Wasserstein distance estimators, like that used in Wasserstein GAN (WGAN), is also discussed; PMD directly approximates the primal form of the Wasserstein metric without requiring an auxiliary "critic" network. The effectiveness of PMD is empirically demonstrated on two significant deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation on the Office dataset and deep generative modeling on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW. In these experiments, PMD is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD, often with faster convergence.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical claim that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein-1 metric is well-supported by established results from optimal transport and probability theory (Propositions 3.1 and 3.2). The claims are backed by a comprehensive set of experiments on standard benchmarks. The authors are careful to evaluate various aspects of their method, including sensitivity to population and mini-batch size (Fig. 5), computational time (Fig. 5c), and an empirical validation of the "stronger gradients" claim (Fig. 2b). The results generally support the paper's thesis.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    1.  The primary concern is the computational complexity of the matching step, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. While the authors argue this is acceptable compared to backpropagation, this may not hold for simpler models or as `N` grows. The proposed solution of fixing the matching for multiple inner-loop updates is a practical heuristic but lacks theoretical justification regarding its effect on convergence.
    2.  The paper does not discuss the "curse of dimensionality," which is a known challenge for estimating Wasserstein distances from samples. The convergence rate of the empirical Wasserstein distance to the true distance degrades severely in high dimensions. It would be valuable for the authors to acknowledge this limitation and discuss its potential impact.
    3.  The argument that the gradient does not flow through the optimal matching `M*` (Eq. 3) relies on the matching being stable under infinitesimal perturbations of the parameters. While this is generally true (related to Danskin's theorem), it can fail if the optimal matching is not unique. A brief mention of this condition would strengthen the claim.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

*   **Strengths:** The motivation is well-established in the introduction. The definition of PMD is intuitive, and the algorithm is presented clearly with pseudocode and a helpful illustration (Fig. 1). The relationship between PMD, the Wasserstein metric, and MMD is explained systematically in Section 3. The experimental setup and results are easy to follow.

*   **Weaknesses:** The terminology distinguishing "population size N" from "mini-batch size |B|" can be slightly confusing, as the term "mini-batch size" is often used to refer to `N` in the MMD literature. While the authors do clarify their usage, a more distinct naming convention (e.g., "matching set size" vs. "gradient mini-batch size") could have avoided potential ambiguity.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and valuable contribution.

*   **Strengths:** While the Wasserstein metric and its connection to bipartite matching on empirical samples are known in the optimal transport community, this paper appears to be the first to formulate and successfully apply this concept as a direct, differentiable training objective for deep learning models like generative networks and domain adaptation architectures. This provides a compelling, primal-form alternative to the dual-form approaches (e.g., WGAN) and kernel-based methods (e.g., MMD), representing a novel combination of established techniques in a new context.

*   **Weaknesses:** The core components (minimum weight matching, Wasserstein distance) are not new in themselves. The novelty lies entirely in their specific application and framing as a training loss for deep networks.

**Significance:** The paper has the potential for significant impact.

*   **Strengths:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of defining robust, sample-based distribution distances for training deep models. MMD is a widely used baseline, and its weaknesses (especially the sensitivity to kernel bandwidth) are a common frustration for practitioners. By offering a parameter-free alternative that demonstrates superior performance and faster convergence in several cases, PMD is likely to be adopted by researchers in generative modeling, domain adaptation, and other areas requiring distribution matching. The conceptual simplicity of the approach is also a major asset.

*   **Weaknesses:** The practical significance may be tempered by the computational cost of the matching step, which could limit its scalability to very large population sizes (`N`). Furthermore, as the authors note, the choice of the underlying distance `d(·, ·)` (e.g., L1 pixel distance) can be suboptimal for certain data types like natural images, leading to blurry generations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** Proposes a simple, elegant, and novel method for training deep models by directly minimizing a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein-1 distance.
2.  **Practical Advantages:** Addresses well-known practical issues with MMD, such as hyperparameter tuning and weak gradients, offering a more robust alternative.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to be effective on two important and distinct tasks, outperforming a strong baseline (MMD) in terms of both final performance and convergence speed.
4.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and provides a solid foundation for future work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The O(N^2) or O(N^3) complexity of the matching algorithm remains a practical concern that may limit adoption in settings requiring very large sample sets for accurate distance estimation.
2.  **Heuristic Training:** The proposed training algorithm relies on a heuristic (fixing the matching for multiple updates) that is not theoretically analyzed.
3.  **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of known limitations of Wasserstein distance estimation, such as the curse of dimensionality.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and valuable tool for the deep learning community. The proposed PMD method is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically effective. While there are potential limitations regarding scalability, the demonstrated advantages over MMD in terms of robustness and performance make this a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance.
