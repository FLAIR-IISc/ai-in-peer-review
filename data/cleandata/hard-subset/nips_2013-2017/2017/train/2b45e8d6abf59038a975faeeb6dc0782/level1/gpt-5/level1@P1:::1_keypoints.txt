- Proposes PMD (minimum-weight bipartite matching between equal-sized samples) as a differentiable, sample-based distance and shows it equals empirical 1-Wasserstein with consistency proofs.

- PMD is essentially empirical OT/EMD; close to WGAN, MMD approaches, and domain-adaptation OT methods; paper omits entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi 2013).

- Technical soundness: empirical OT connection and consistency are standard; not differentiating through matching is defensible but Danskin’s theorem is not cited and frozen-matching SGD introduces unanalysed bias.

- Experiments show PMD can outperform plain MMD, but baselines are limited and key comparisons are missing.

- Domain adaptation evaluation only compares to MMD; stronger DA baselines (DANN, DAN/JAN, DSN, CMD, OT-DA) are absent.

- Generative modeling results are mainly qualitative, lack standard metrics (FID/IS), and omit comparisons to WGAN and MMD-GAN.

- Claim of “stronger gradients” is weakly supported (single kernel); alternative or learned kernels and other distances (energy/Cramér) are not evaluated.

- Scalability concern: matching is O(N^2)–O(N^3); no GPU implementation or comparison to faster Sinkhorn methods is provided.

- Clarity: paper is readable but has notational ambiguities (population vs mini-batch) and minor typos; frozen-matching algorithm is described but theoretical implications are not discussed.

- Evaluation protocol issues: hyperparameter tuning used 100 labeled target samples (violates unsupervised protocol); error bars/repetitions are incompletely reported.

- Originality is limited: PMD is empirical W1/EMD under a new name and omits key related work (Sinkhorn OT, OT-based DA, MMD-GAN, sliced Wasserstein).

- Significance: could be useful as a parameter-free loss if robust and scalable, but lack of Sinkhorn comparisons and discussion of W1’s poor high-dimensional sample complexity limits impact.

- Strengths: simple, intuitive parameter-free objective; correct W1 connection and consistency proof; practical frozen-matching recipe that can reduce per-iteration cost; some empirical benefits and useful gradient diagnostics.

- Weaknesses: limited novelty and missing citations; scalability and computational cost; gaps in empirical evaluation and metrics; frozen-matching heuristic lacks theoretical guarantees; kernel-dependence of gradient claims; improper hyperparameter tuning.

- Suggested improvements: include and compare to Sinkhorn OT (wall-clock and performance), add SOTA baselines (DA and generative), report FID/IS and stability curves, justify gradients via Danskin, analyze matching-change convergence, explore learned/perceptual or sliced costs, study sample complexity vs N, and implement GPU or adopt Sinkhorn for scalability.

- Arguments for acceptance: clear formulation, practical training procedure, parameter-free alternative to MMD with some empirical advantages.

- Arguments against acceptance: low conceptual novelty, omitted prior art, insufficient empirical comparisons and metrics, and unresolved scalability/optimization concerns.

- Overall recommendation: Weak reject — idea has potential but needs stronger empirical comparisons (especially to Sinkhorn-based OT and modern baselines) and clearer positioning relative to existing OT literature.
