- Paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a differentiable distribution distance estimator based on minimum weight matching between sample populations.  
- Authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and propose an algorithm for learning distribution parameters with PMD.  
- Empirical results show PMD outperforms MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling with faster convergence.  
- Review notes the core idea is essentially Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD) on empirical distributions and misses citation of foundational EMD work.  
- Review criticizes omission of recent related work (Wasserstein GAN, Cramer Distance, Sinkhorn approximations) and poor contextualization.  
- Strength: clear, theoretically grounded connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric.  
- Strength: consistent empirical superiority of PMD over MMD across tasks and faster convergence.  
- Strength: practical training algorithm with the insight that matching need not be recomputed every gradient step.  
- Strength: effective empirical demonstration of stronger gradients (Figure 2b).  
- Weakness: theoretical claim of “strong consistency” is overstated—analysis only shows equivalence on empirical distributions and observed convergence rate (N^-0.23) is slow.  
- Weakness: computational complexity—Hungarian algorithm’s O(N^3) cost is downplayed and may be prohibitive for large N (e.g., N=1000).  
- Weakness: missing comparisons to other Wasserstein approximations (Sinkhorn, Cramer Distance) with different computational trade-offs.  
- Weakness: generative results lack quantitative metrics (e.g., FID); images appear blurry on SVHN and LFW, raising concerns for high-resolution generation.  
- Weakness: failure to cite Rubner et al. (1998) and properly situate contribution relative to established EMD literature.  
- Recommendation: useful alternative to MMD but theoretical foundation and computational implications need stronger treatment in a revision.  
- For acceptance: PMD addresses practical MMD limitations, has convincing empirical results, and a valid theoretical link to Wasserstein.  
- Against acceptance: overstated theoretical claims, inadequate novelty due to relation to EMD and missing citations, and significant unaddressed computational concerns.  
- Overall: moderate significance and borderline acceptance; with revisions on theory, computation, and related work the paper could become a strong contribution.
