- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): empirical minimum-weight bipartite matching as a sample-based estimator and training objective approximating W1, with claimed differentiability without backpropagating through the matching.
- Shows PMD is strongly consistent for W1 using standard weak convergence and OT stability arguments.
- Claims practical advantages over MMD: no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and effective training with smaller SGD minibatches; reports faster convergence and competitive results on Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW.
- Omits key related work: entropic/Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi 2013) and differentiable/efficient OT methods prevalent by 2017.
- Also omits comparisons to sliced Wasserstein metrics and important domain-adaptation/GAN baselines (DANN, JAN, CORAL, CMD, WGAN).
- The consistency theory is correct but largely restates known empirical OT results (Varadarajan, Villani).
- The central claim about differentiating the matching objective without backpropagating through the argmin is informal and needs formal justification (e.g., Danskin/Envelope theorem; uniqueness/stability conditions).
- The minibatch subsampling scheme with fixed matching is a heuristic and its bias or convergence properties are not analyzed.
- Algorithmically, using Hungarian (O(N^3)) or approximate O(N^2) matching is standard; the paper discusses GPU parallelization but does not implement it.
- The gradient-strength argument is plausible and partially supported by small empirical analysis, but scalability concerns for large N remain unaddressed.
- Domain-adaptation experiments compare only to MMD and show advantages on some shifts, but lack comparisons to stronger, standard baselines.
- Generative-modeling experiments compare only to MMD/GMMN; samples are blurry and lack quantitative metrics (Inception/FID) or GAN/WGAN comparisons.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized, with clean definitions and W1 connection, but statements on differentiability and unbiasedness are informal.
- Originality is limited: PMD corresponds to empirical W1/EMD; novelty mainly in advocating not backpropagating through matching and empirical observations about gradients and minibatch size.
- Significance is moderate: practical insights are useful, but impact is reduced by missing comparisons to widely adopted alternatives and modest generative quality.
- Pros: clear PMD–W1 formulation and consistency; practical training recipe with evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence than MMD; fewer hyperparameters and improved sample efficiency on some tasks.
- Cons: limited novelty, incomplete related work (Sinkhorn, sliced Wasserstein), weak empirical baselines, informal differentiability argument, and unimplemented scalability solutions.
- Suggestions: compare to entropic/Sinkhorn OT and sliced Wasserstein (and implement GPU Sinkhorn), strengthen differentiability analysis via Danskin, add stronger baselines (DANN/JAN/CORAL/CMD, GAN/WGAN) with quantitative metrics, explore perceptual/feature-space distances, and provide code/GPU matching.
- Overall assessment: moderate quality and clarity, modest originality and significance; empirical evidence interesting but insufficient versus contemporary alternatives.
- Recommendation: borderline reject — promising practical idea but needs stronger positioning, theory rigor, and comprehensive comparisons to be compelling.
