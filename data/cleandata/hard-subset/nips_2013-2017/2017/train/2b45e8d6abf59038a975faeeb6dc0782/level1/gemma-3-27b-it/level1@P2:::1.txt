This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the distance between two distributions. The core idea – minimizing the cost of matching samples from each distribution – is intuitively appealing and theoretically grounded as a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors convincingly argue that PMD addresses several drawbacks of MMD, including sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and the need for large mini-batches. The application to domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrates practical improvements over MMD, with faster convergence and comparable or better performance.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Foundation:** The proposed PMD offers a fresh perspective on sample-based distribution divergence estimation.  Its connection to the Wasserstein metric, and the proof of strong consistency, provide a solid theoretical basis.  This contrasts with empirical performance-driven methods.
* **Addressing MMD Limitations:** The paper directly targets and resolves well-known issues with MMD, which is a significant contribution. The arguments regarding gradient behavior and mini-batch size are particularly convincing.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling are well-presented and demonstrate the practical benefits of PMD. The convergence speed comparison is compelling. The ablation studies regarding population size and matching algorithm further strengthen the analysis.
* **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical notation is generally accessible, and the explanations are concise. The inclusion of a pseudocode snippet for the learning algorithm is helpful.
* **Thorough Comparison:** The authors provide a fair and detailed comparison with MMD, highlighting both the advantages and disadvantages of each approach.  The discussion of related work (e.g., WGAN) is also well-done.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the computational cost of the matching problem (O(N^3) for exact, O(N^2) for approximate), the practical implications are not fully explored. The comparison with MMD’s computational complexity in the specific context of training deep networks needs more nuance. The GPU acceleration mentioned is promising but not demonstrated in the experiments.  The overhead of the matching step, even with the approximation, could be substantial for very large datasets.
* **L1 Distance Choice:** The exclusive use of L1 distance might be a limitation. While justified by the gradient properties, the authors should acknowledge that other distance metrics might be more suitable for certain applications, and explore the sensitivity of the method to the distance function choice.  The comment that L1 isn't ideal for natural images is a good point, but not investigated further.
* **Limited Generative Modeling Results:** The generated images on SVHN and LFW are "blurry," and the improvement over MMD is not visually striking. While the authors identify the L1 distance as a potential cause, further investigation into alternative distance metrics or network architectures would strengthen this section.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Although PMD requires fewer hyperparameters than MMD, it still has parameters to tune. The discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity in the domain adaptation experiments is good, but similar analysis could be included for the generative modeling experiments.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*  The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to estimating distribution divergence.
*  It addresses significant limitations of a widely used method (MMD).
*  The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate practical improvements.
*  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The computational cost of the matching problem could be a bottleneck for large-scale applications.
*  The reliance on L1 distance might limit the applicability of PMD to certain types of data.
*  The generative modeling results are not particularly impressive, and further investigation is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The core idea is novel, the theoretical justification is sound, and the empirical results are encouraging. While the computational cost and the choice of L1 distance are valid concerns, they do not overshadow the overall contribution of the work.  The authors have convincingly demonstrated that PMD is a viable and potentially superior alternative to MMD for a variety of deep learning tasks.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, such as a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and potential extensions to other distance metrics.  Specifically, a more concrete analysis (perhaps with preliminary benchmarks) regarding GPU acceleration of the matching algorithm would be highly valuable.
