- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): average cost of minimum-weight bipartite matching; equals empirical W1 for uniform empirical measures.  
- Proves strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of W1 using Varadarajan (empirical measures) and Villani (OT stability).  
- Outlines a stochastic optimization scheme that fixes discrete matching during small parameter updates and uses subsampling for gradient estimates.  
- Claims advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger per-sample gradients, and effectiveness with smaller mini-batches.  
- Demonstrates applications in unsupervised domain adaptation (feature regularizer) and implicit generative modeling with faster convergence and better performance than MMD on selected tasks/datasets.  
- Fails to discuss and compare to entropic-regularized OT / Sinkhorn distances and prior OT-based adaptation or Sinkhorn-based generative training.  
- Theoretical equivalence to assignment problems and consistency results are standard; contribution here is mainly packaging/clarification.  
- Gradient treatment avoids differentiating through argmin; justification is informal and would benefit from formal Danskin-style argument (uniqueness/switch points).  
- Subsampling gradient estimator is a heuristic and biased; the paper does not analyze this bias or its effect.  
- Computational complexity is dominated by matching (O(N^3) exact, O(N^2) approximate), raising scalability concerns not addressed by GPU/Sinkhorn implementations.  
- Domain adaptation experiments compare only to MMD and use 100 labeled target samples for validation, which departs from strict unsupervised protocol and should be acknowledged.  
- Generative modeling comparisons are limited to MMD (GMMN), largely qualitative, and lack standard quantitative metrics (Inception Score, FID) or WGAN/MMD-GAN baselines.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; some notation overload and missing citation to Danskin’s theorem noted.  
- Originality is limited: PMD is empirical W1/EMD and theoretical results are largely known; novelty lies in advocating empirical OT in deep learning and a subsampling optimization scheme.  
- Significance is practical for settings using MMD (stronger gradients, smaller batches), but broader impact is limited by missing comparisons and scalability issues.  
- Strengths: clear OT connection, parameter-light objective, practical training strategy, empirical improvements over MMD, informative gradient-magnitude analysis.  
- Weaknesses: limited novelty, omitted key related work and baselines, informal gradient-through-matching justification, unanalysed subsampling bias, scalability concerns, and narrow experimental metrics.  
- Suggested fixes: cite/compare to Sinkhorn/Cuturi and OT-based adaptation; include a Sinkhorn-based GPU variant to address scalability.  
- Suggested analyses: provide formal Danskin justification for gradients and analyze bias/variance of subsampled gradients.  
- Suggested experiments: add WGAN and MMD-GAN baselines, domain adaptation methods (DANN, JAN, CMD), quantitative generative metrics, and computational cost vs. accuracy trade-offs.  
- Pro-acceptance arguments: principled and clear formulation, solid consistency, practical benefits over MMD, simple implementation, and empirical evidence of faster convergence.  
- Contra-acceptance arguments: incremental relative to existing empirical OT work, omission of key related work, limited experimental scope and baselines, and unresolved scalability issues.
