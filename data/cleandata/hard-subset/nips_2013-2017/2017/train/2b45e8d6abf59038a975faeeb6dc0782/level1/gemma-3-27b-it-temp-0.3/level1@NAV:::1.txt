## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel approach for estimating the distance between probability distributions based on samples, aiming to address limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The core idea is to define distance as the minimum weight matching of sample populations, proving its strong consistency as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors demonstrate PMD’s application to domain adaptation and generative modeling, showcasing improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

The paper builds upon a well-established body of work in sample-based divergence estimation, particularly MMD [Gretton et al., 2012]. It correctly identifies key drawbacks of MMD – sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and large batch size requirements – and proposes a compelling alternative. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is clearly articulated and theoretically justified. The proposed algorithm for parameter learning using PMD as an objective is straightforward and logically presented. The application to domain adaptation and generative modeling are relevant and demonstrate the practical potential of PMD.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated contribution. The theoretical analysis establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric is a significant result. The empirical results on both domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate the practical advantages of PMD over MMD, particularly in convergence speed. The authors are careful to acknowledge the computational cost of the matching problem and discuss potential optimizations. The writing is generally clear and well-organized.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical foundation is strong, the empirical evaluation could be more extensive. The comparison to MMD is limited to a few specific settings. Exploring different datasets, network architectures, and hyperparameter settings would strengthen the claims of PMD’s robustness. The paper doesn’t delve deeply into the limitations of using the L1 distance in generative modeling, only acknowledging it as a potential issue. A more thorough discussion of alternative distance metrics within the PMD framework would be beneficial. The computational complexity of the matching algorithm, even with approximations, could be a barrier to scaling to very large datasets.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Novel and theoretically grounded approach to distribution distance estimation.
* Demonstrated improvements over MMD in practical applications.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Addresses a relevant and important problem in deep learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* Limited scope of empirical evaluation.
* Potential computational bottleneck with the matching algorithm.
* Lack of exploration of alternative distance metrics within the PMD framework.

**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the theoretical soundness and demonstrated practical benefits of PMD warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
