

# Peer Review: Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning

## Summary

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper presents an algorithm for learning distribution parameters using PMD as an objective, and demonstrates its application to domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The authors compare PMD with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a widely used method for distribution distance estimation, highlighting PMD's advantages: no kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes. The work builds upon previous NIPS contributions on distribution distance estimation (e.g., MMD [17], WGAN [3]) and optimal transport theory [49], but introduces a fundamentally different approach to the problem.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The theoretical foundation is strong, with a clear proof that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper carefully establishes connections between PMD and existing methods (Wasserstein metric, MMD), and provides thorough empirical validation across multiple datasets and tasks. The ablation studies on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithms (exact vs. approximate) provide valuable insights into practical implementation. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., blurry images on SVHN/LFW due to L1 distance) and discuss potential future directions.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to experiments. Key concepts (PMD definition, parameter learning, matching problem) are well-explained with appropriate mathematical notation. The figures (particularly Fig. 2 and Fig. 5) effectively illustrate key findings about gradient magnitude and convergence behavior. The pseudocode in Fig. 1 provides a clear implementation guide.

**Originality:** PMD represents a novel approach to distribution distance estimation that combines minimum weight matching with distributional analysis. While optimal transport and Wasserstein metrics have been studied extensively (e.g., [40, 49, 50]), this is the first time minimum weight matching has been proposed as a direct estimator of the Wasserstein metric in the context of deep learning. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, including MMD-based approaches [17, 31] and WGAN [3], by highlighting PMD's unique advantages.

**Significance:** The work addresses a practical bottleneck in deep learning: the limitations of MMD as a training objective. The demonstrated advantages of PMD (no hyper-parameter tuning, stronger gradients, faster convergence) could significantly improve training efficiency across multiple domains. The empirical results on domain adaptation (Office benchmark) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) show clear improvements over MMD, suggesting this method could become a valuable tool in the deep learning practitioner's toolkit.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical depth:** While the consistency proof is solid, the paper lacks finite-sample error bounds for PMD, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. The authors mention this as future work, but a preliminary bound would have been helpful for readers to better understand the practical implications of their estimator.

**Empirical limitations:** The generative modeling results on SVHN and LFW show blurry images, and the authors attribute this to the L1 distance being unsuitable for natural images. However, they don't explore alternative distance metrics or how PMD would perform with them. The paper also doesn't compare PMD to other Wasserstein-based methods (e.g., WGAN, WGAN-GP) in generative modeling, which would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Computational complexity:** While the paper acknowledges that exact matching has O(N^3) complexity, the discussion of computational trade-offs could be strengthened. The authors note that "O(N^3) time complexity is acceptable" but don't provide a detailed comparison of training time for PMD vs. MMD across different batch sizes, which would help practitioners understand the practical cost-benefit.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel approach to distribution distance estimation with clear theoretical foundation
- Demonstrated practical advantages over MMD in multiple deep learning tasks
- Well-structured and clearly written paper with thorough empirical validation
- Addresses a meaningful limitation in current deep learning practice (MMD's hyper-parameter sensitivity and slow convergence)
- Strong connections to existing work and clear differentiation from prior contributions

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical analysis (lacks finite-sample error bounds)
- Generative modeling results could be improved with better distance metrics
- Some computational complexity claims need more empirical support
- Doesn't compare to other Wasserstein-based methods in generative modeling

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distribution distance estimation in deep learning. The introduction of PMD provides a theoretically sound alternative to MMD with clear practical advantages. While the paper has some limitations in theoretical depth and empirical scope, these are relatively minor compared to the significance of the contribution. The work is well-positioned to influence future research and practice in domain adaptation and generative modeling.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to address the theoretical limitations (adding a preliminary finite-sample bound) and to provide more comprehensive generative modeling comparisons. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and represents a significant advance over existing methods.
