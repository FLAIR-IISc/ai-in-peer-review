

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a new method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, and the authors prove that it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper then proposes an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD as a training objective, and applies it to domain adaptation and generative modeling, claiming advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) including no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements.

The paper builds on the growing body of work at NIPS addressing distribution distance estimation for deep learning applications, particularly extending the line of work on the Wasserstein metric (e.g., Arjovsky et al. 2017, "Wasserstein GAN") and MMD (Gretton et al. 2012, "A Kernel Two-Sample Test"). It distinguishes itself from these approaches by proposing a novel estimator that avoids the bandwidth parameter issue of MMD while maintaining the theoretical guarantees of the Wasserstein metric.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical proofs establishing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors provide clear mathematical derivations showing the relationship between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, and they carefully address the computational aspects of the matching problem. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, with experiments on both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks across multiple datasets. The paper acknowledges the computational cost of the Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)) but justifies it as acceptable compared to neural network training costs. However, the paper could have provided more detailed analysis of when PMD might be less suitable than MMD, particularly for high-dimensional data where matching might become problematic.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from theoretical foundations to applications. The mathematical definitions are precise, and the experimental setup is well-described. The authors provide pseudocode for the algorithm and include helpful visualizations (e.g., Fig. 1). The paper could be improved by adding more details about the implementation of the matching algorithms and providing a more thorough discussion of the computational trade-offs between exact and approximate matching.

**Originality**: The paper presents a novel approach to distribution distance estimation, combining the Wasserstein metric with minimum weight matching in a way that avoids the bandwidth hyper-parameter of MMD. This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as the bandwidth parameter in MMD is a well-known limitation. The paper clearly differentiates PMD from related work, including the Cramer distance (Bellemare et al. 2017) and Wasserstein GAN. The authors appropriately cite relevant NIPS work, including the MMD-based generative moment matching networks (Li et al. 2015) and domain adaptation methods.

**Significance**: The paper addresses an important practical challenge in deep learning: the need for a differentiable, robust distribution distance estimator. The claimed advantages of PMD over MMD—no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements—are significant for practitioners. The empirical results demonstrate that PMD outperforms MMD in domain adaptation (on some tasks) and generative modeling (producing less noisy images), which supports the theoretical claims. However, the paper's claim that PMD "outperforms MMD on both tasks" is somewhat overstated, as the domain adaptation results show PMD outperforming MMD on a→w and a→d tasks but being comparable on others. The significance would be further strengthened by comparing with more recent methods like the Cramer distance or other Wasserstein approximations.

**Strengths:**
- Theoretical foundation is strong, with a clear proof that PMD is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric.
- The advantages over MMD (no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size) are well-justified theoretically and empirically.
- The experimental results on both domain adaptation and generative modeling provide convincing evidence of PMD's practical benefits.
- The paper clearly articulates the relationship between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, which is valuable for the community.

**Weaknesses:**
- The computational complexity of the matching algorithm (O(N^3) for exact Hungarian) is not thoroughly addressed in terms of scalability to very large datasets.
- The paper doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with other recent methods for distribution distance estimation, such as the Cramer distance (Bellemare et al. 2017).
- The claim that PMD outperforms MMD on "both tasks" is not fully supported by the experimental results, as the domain adaptation results are mixed.
- The paper doesn't explore the limitations of PMD in high-dimensional settings, where matching might become less meaningful.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distribution distance estimation for deep learning. The theoretical foundation is solid, the empirical results are convincing, and the method has clear practical advantages over MMD. While the paper has some minor weaknesses in its experimental comparison and discussion of limitations, these do not detract significantly from the overall contribution.

The paper meets all the NIPS quality criteria: it's technically sound, well-written, original, and significant. The authors have provided a valuable new tool for practitioners working on domain adaptation and generative modeling, with clear advantages over the popular MMD approach. The paper is suitable for acceptance at NIPS 2017.

**Recommendation:**
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a theoretically sound method that addresses a practical limitation of current distribution distance estimators. The empirical results support the theoretical claims, and the method has clear advantages over MMD. The minor weaknesses identified do not undermine the paper's overall contribution.
