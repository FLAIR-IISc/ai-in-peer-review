This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the distance between probability distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations, and is proven to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors present an algorithm to learn distributions using PMD as an objective and demonstrate its advantages over MMD – namely, no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use normal mini-batch sizes – on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction provides sufficient motivation and background on the limitations of MMD, establishing the need for a new distance metric. The definition of PMD is concise and intuitive. The theoretical analysis, demonstrating consistency with the Wasserstein metric, is a strong point. The explanation of the learning algorithm and the discussion of gradient properties are clear. The empirical evaluation is well-designed, showcasing PMD's performance on two relevant tasks.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The strong consistency result relating PMD to the Wasserstein metric provides a solid theoretical basis for the proposed method.
*   **Addressing MMD Limitations:** The authors directly address the key drawbacks of MMD – bandwidth selection and vanishing gradients – providing a compelling motivation for PMD.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that PMD outperforms MMD on both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, supporting the claims of improved performance and convergence speed.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making the methodology accessible to a broad audience.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of computational complexity and the use of both exact and approximate matching algorithms are helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **L1 Distance Choice:** The exclusive use of the L1 distance in the experiments is a limitation. While the authors claim it's beneficial, the choice might not be optimal for all data types and tasks, and the impact of alternative distance metrics is not explored.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with other Wasserstein distance estimators (beyond MMD) is absent. This makes it difficult to assess the relative advantages of PMD in the broader landscape of Wasserstein-based methods. Specifically, the work doesn't compare to recent advances directly estimating the Wasserstein distance.
*   **Generative Model Blurriness:** The observation of blurry generated images on SVHN and LFW suggests potential limitations of PMD when combined with pixel-level distances. While the authors acknowledge this, a deeper investigation into addressing this issue would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Although mentioning MMD's parameter sensitivity, a similar sensitivity analysis for PMD (beyond mini-batch size) could strengthen the understanding of its robustness.
*   **Computational Cost:** Despite discussing the computational aspects, a more detailed profiling of the running time for different configurations (population size, matching algorithm) would provide a clearer picture of the practical scalability of PMD.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method (PMD) for estimating the distance between probability distributions.
*   PMD addresses significant limitations of MMD, a widely used technique in deep learning.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD on relevant tasks.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology and its advantages.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The reliance solely on the L1 distance may limit the applicability and performance of PMD.
*   The lack of comparison with other Wasserstein distance estimators weakens the assessment of PMD’s relative advantages.
*   The blurry image generation issue raises concerns about the method's performance in certain scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated contribution. The theoretical and empirical results are convincing, and the paper addresses a relevant problem in deep learning. While some limitations exist, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically including a discussion of different distance metrics and a more thorough comparison with other Wasserstein distance estimation methods.
