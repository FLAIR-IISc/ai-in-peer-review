Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based measure of distributional discrepancy defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between samples drawn from two distributions. PMD is equal to the empirical primal optimal transport (OT) cost for the first Wasserstein metric (W1) with uniform empirical measures. The authors prove strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of W1 using weak convergence of empirical measures (Varadarajan) and stability of OT (Villani). They outline a stochastic optimization scheme that treats the discrete matching as fixed within small parameter updates and allows subsampling for gradient estimation. Compared to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), the paper argues that PMD has no kernel bandwidth, yields stronger per-sample gradients, and works with smaller mini-batches.

Applications are demonstrated in unsupervised domain adaptation (as a feature-space regularizer) and implicit generative modeling (as a direct training loss), with experiments suggesting better performance and faster convergence than MMD on Office (Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and qualitative image generation on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW.

The work is closely related to: (i) empirical OT/earth mover’s distance (EMD) for discrete measures, long used in vision and retrieval; (ii) WGAN, which optimizes the dual formulation via a learned 1-Lipschitz critic; and (iii) prior use of MMD for generative modeling and domain adaptation. A key related line not discussed is entropic-regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013), which provide fast, differentiable approximations to W1 and have been applied in learning; there is also OT-based domain adaptation and Sinkhorn-based training of generative models (contemporaneous 2016–2017), which should be acknowledged and compared.

Quality (technical soundness):
- Theory: The equivalence of empirical W1 to a linear assignment (Hungarian) problem is standard. The consistency result follows from well-known stability of OT and convergence of empirical measures; the argument is sound. The gradient treatment rests on not differentiating through the argmin over matchings. This is justified almost everywhere by Danskin’s theorem for minimization over a finite set; formalizing this (uniqueness, measure-zero switch points) would strengthen Eq. (3). The subsampling gradient (Eq. 4) is a heuristic and biased but reasonable as an approximation; this limitation is not discussed.
- Algorithms: Complexity is dominated by matching (O(N^3) Hungarian, O(N^2) approximate). The paper argues the cost is acceptable relative to backprop; however, omitting Sinkhorn distances or other scalable OT solvers is a notable gap since they are GPU-friendly and differentiable.
- Experiments: Domain adaptation compares against MMD only, with reasonable protocol details. The use of 100 labeled target samples for hyperparameter selection departs from strict unsupervised adaptation; while applied equally to both methods, it should be acknowledged as such. Generative modeling comparisons are only to MMD (GMMN) and mainly qualitative, lacking standard quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) or comparisons to WGAN/MMD-GAN. The gradient magnitude analysis is informative.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Definitions and connections to OT are explicit, and empirical analyses illustrate claims. Some notation is overloaded (N for both sample size and elsewhere), and the justification for ignoring the derivative of the matching could cite Danskin’s theorem. Important related work (Sinkhorn/entropic OT, OT-based adaptation) is missing, which affects context.

Originality:
The core objective equals empirical W1/EMD, and the consistency result is standard. The contributions are primarily: packaging empirical OT as “PMD,” arguing for its practical advantages relative to MMD in deep learning training, and providing an optimization scheme with subsampling. This is an incremental advance; novelty is limited by substantial prior work on OT distances and their differentiable approximations for learning.

Significance:
Within tasks where MMD is commonly used, demonstrating that empirical W1 can give stronger gradients and work with smaller batches is practically useful. However, the broader impact is limited by missing comparisons to contemporary and stronger baselines (WGAN, Sinkhorn-based methods, DANN/JAN/CMD for domain adaptation) and by the computational scalability concerns of exact/approximate matching. Adding quantitative generative metrics and more comprehensive baselines would clarify significance.

Strengths:
- Clear connection to OT; correct and useful consistency statement.
- Simple, parameter-light objective (no kernel bandwidth) with intuitive gradients.
- Practical training strategy with evidence of faster convergence and smaller batch utility.
- Empirical improvements over MMD in two tasks; informative gradient magnitude analysis.

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: PMD is empirical W1/EMD; theory largely known.
- Missing key related work and comparisons (Sinkhorn/entropic OT; OT-based domain adaptation; WGAN/MMD-GAN).
- Gradient-through-matching justification informal; subsampling gradient bias not analyzed.
- Scalability: O(N^2–N^3) matching; no GPU or Sinkhorn implementation despite discussion.
- Domain adaptation uses labeled target validation; generative results lack standard quantitative metrics; baselines are narrow.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Cite and compare to Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) and OT-based adaptation; include a Sinkhorn-based variant with GPU to address scalability.
- Provide formal justification via Danskin’s theorem and discuss subgradient behavior at matching switch points; analyze bias/variance of the subsampled gradient.
- Add comparisons to WGAN and MMD-GAN for generative modeling with quantitative scores; for domain adaptation, include DANN, JAN, CMD.
- Report computational cost vs. accuracy trade-offs across methods.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation with solid consistency and practical benefits over MMD.
- Simple to implement and potentially useful as a drop-in replacement for MMD in some settings.
- Empirical evidence of faster convergence and improved performance on studied tasks.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Incremental contribution relative to established empirical OT; key related work omitted.
- Limited experimental scope and missing strong baselines and quantitative metrics.
- Scalability concerns not fully addressed; more efficient OT alternatives not evaluated.

Overall, the paper is clear and technically sound in its core, but the novelty and empirical breadth are limited.
