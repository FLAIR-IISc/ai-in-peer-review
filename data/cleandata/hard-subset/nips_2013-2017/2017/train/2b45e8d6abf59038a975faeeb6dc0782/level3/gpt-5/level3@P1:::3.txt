This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based, differentiable discrepancy that coincides with the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance when comparing two equally sized sample sets via bipartite minimum-weight matching. The authors prove strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of the 1-Wasserstein metric, and develop a practical SGD-based training algorithm that uses PMD as an objective for learning distribution parameters in deep models. PMD is positioned as an alternative to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), addressing known issues with MMD (kernel bandwidth tuning, weak gradients, and large batch requirements). Applications to unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling show that PMD tends to converge faster and/or reach better solutions than MMD, with advantages in gradient strength and reduced sensitivity to mini-batch size.

Relation to prior work: PMD directly implements the primal empirical optimal transport with equal weights, reducing to a linear assignment problem (Hungarian algorithm or Drake-Hougardy approximation). This connects closely to classical Earth Mover’s Distance (Rubner et al.) and modern optimal transport (Villani). Unlike dual approximations such as WGAN’s critic-based estimator or other dual estimators, PMD is parameter-free and has a known scale. Compared to MMD (Gretton et al.), PMD avoids kernel selection and provides stronger, more uniformly distributed gradients per sample. There is related work on entropic regularization for OT (e.g., Sinkhorn distances) that yields fast, GPU-friendly, differentiable approximations; while not discussed experimentally here, PMD is a compelling complementary approach with a simple estimator and clear theoretical grounding.

Quality: The theoretical connection and consistency result are sound and well-motivated, following from weak convergence and stability of OT. The optimization strategy—treating the matching as fixed across a few SGD steps and not backpropagating through the discrete assignment—is reasonable and standard in piecewise-constant objectives; the authors justify it and show it works in practice. Complexity and engineering trade-offs are discussed: O(N3) exact matching vs O(N2) approximation and the observation that matching cost can be comparable to backprop for moderate N; the authors note opportunities for GPU acceleration.

The experimental evaluation is adequate but not exhaustive. For domain adaptation (Office), PMD improves accuracy on several source→target pairs and converges faster, with reasonable hyperparameter handling. For generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), PMD yields cleaner samples than MMD, particularly outside autoencoder code space, though images remain blurry with pixel L1—consistent with the metric choice. The gradient magnitude analysis supports the claim that PMD provides stronger, more even gradients than MMD. Time breakdowns and sensitivity to N and mini-batch size |B| are informative.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and cites relevant literature. The algorithmic details and theoretical results are explained at the right level for NIPS. The assumptions behind not differentiating through the matching and the mini-batch subsampling heuristic are clearly stated.

Originality: While empirical OT via matching is classic, the contribution here is to reframe it as a differentiable, practical training objective for deep models with a principled stochastic optimization procedure and to analyze and demonstrate advantages over MMD. The consistency result and empirical findings on gradient strength/mini-batching are useful. The idea of subsampling |B|≪N while reusing a fixed matching is novel in this context.

Significance: A robust, parameter-free distribution discrepancy that yields strong gradients and works with modest batch sizes is valuable across generative modeling, domain adaptation, and beyond. PMD’s simplicity and theoretical guarantees make it an appealing alternative to MMD and adversarial critics, especially when stability and interpretability are priorities.

Strengths:
- Clear definition and proof that PMD equals empirical 1-Wasserstein and is strongly consistent.
- Practical, differentiable training objective with a simple optimization loop and useful |B|≪N heuristic.
- Stronger and more uniform gradients than MMD; no bandwidth hyperparameter.
- Competitive or better results on Office adaptation and on generative modeling benchmarks; faster convergence.
- Thoughtful discussion of computational trade-offs and approximate matching.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Generative modeling baselines are limited to MMD; missing comparisons to WGAN/Improved WGAN, Cramér distance, and Sinkhorn OT, which are standard by now and would strengthen claims about convergence and sample quality.
- Image quality remains blurry with pixel-space L1; evaluating PMD in learned feature spaces (e.g., perceptual or autoencoder features) and reporting standard metrics (e.g., FID, Inception score) would provide a clearer picture.
- Behavior on highly multi-modal data when N is smaller than the number of modes is not examined; experiments on datasets designed for mode coverage (e.g., Stacked MNIST, CIFAR-10) would address concerns about matching-induced mode undercoverage.
- The assumption that the assignment remains stable over multiple updates (when using |B|≪N) is heuristic; an analysis of its impact on bias/variance and convergence, or an adaptive strategy for refreshing the matching, would be valuable.
- Computational and memory costs scale as O(N2) for distance matrices; end-to-end GPU implementations and comparisons against linear-time MMD estimators (unbiased linear MMD) would give a fairer compute-normalized baseline.
- Finite-sample error bounds and sensitivity to the choice of ground metric (L1 vs L2 vs learned features) would strengthen the theoretical and practical guidance.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical grounding with a simple, broadly useful estimator and training objective.
- Clear empirical benefits over MMD in gradient behavior, hyperparameter simplicity, and convergence, with positive results on two tasks.
- Well-written, technically sound, and likely to influence practice in settings where MMD is currently used.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing head-to-head comparisons with state-of-the-art OT-based and adversarial generative baselines and domain-adversarial methods.
- Limited evaluation on complex, multi-modal generative tasks and lack of standard quantitative metrics.
- Heuristic aspects (fixed matching across updates) lack theoretical analysis.

Overall recommendation: This is a good paper with a clean idea, solid theory, and promising empirical results. With additional experiments against modern baselines and on more challenging generative settings, the work would be even stronger. As it stands, it offers a useful and principled alternative to MMD with practical advantages, and merits acceptance.
