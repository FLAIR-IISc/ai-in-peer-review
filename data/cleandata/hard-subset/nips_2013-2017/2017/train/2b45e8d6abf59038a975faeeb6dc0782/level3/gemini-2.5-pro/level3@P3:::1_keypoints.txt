- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a minibatch-based estimator of the primal Wasserstein-1 distance using minimum-weight bipartite matching.
- Matching solved exactly with Hungarian algorithm O(N^3) or approximately in O(N^2).
- Positions PMD as a parameter-free, kernel-free alternative to MMD and WGANs for distribution matching.
- Claims PMD has fewer hyperparameters, stronger gradients, and faster convergence than MMD.
- Original and simple idea: directly optimize a consistent minibatch estimator of the primal Wasserstein distance.
- Theoretical result: N-PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the true Wasserstein metric as N → ∞.
- Empirical analysis highlights benefits in domain adaptation and generative modeling and shows larger gradient magnitudes versus MMD.
- Generative sample quality is poor (blurry SVHN and LFW images) and shows mode collapse (many SVHN samples look like digit '8').
- Small-minibatch estimation is biased: PMD requires large N for accurate estimation on complex/high-dimensional/multimodal distributions.
- Gradient of PMD is biased w.r.t. the true Wasserstein gradient; stochastic gradients are unbiased only for the minibatch PMD, not W1.
- Lack of an unbiased gradient estimator for PMD is a significant limitation compared to MMD's unbiased U-statistic.
- Potential failure modes: one-to-one minibatch matching may fail to capture variances/modes (e.g., learning mixture of Gaussians).
- Criticism of MMD is incomplete—biased quadratic estimator needs large batches, but unbiased U-statistics allow small-batch SGD; comparison should be clarified.
- Suggestions: clarify bias of PMD and its gradient, quantify SVHN sample diversity (e.g., classifier labels), and test on mixtures of Gaussians with learnable variances.
- Minor issue: typo at Line 141 ("usally" → "usually").
- Overall: creative, theoretically grounded contribution, but practical usefulness limited by biased small-batch estimation and subpar sample quality; needs fuller discussion of limitations versus MMD.
