Paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based measure defined as the cost of the minimum-weight matching between two sample sets.  
PMD is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance.  
Positions PMD as an alternative to MMD and as a primal-form counterpart to dual-form Wasserstein approximations like WGAN.  
Evaluated on standard tasks (generative modeling, domain adaptation) and directly compared against MMD-based approaches (e.g., Generative Moment Matching Networks).  
Primary strength: PMD enables effective training with much smaller mini-batches than MMD, improving computational efficiency and memory use; empirical results support this.  
Paper is well-written and technically sound, offering theoretical justification and a clear algorithm for gradient-based optimization.  
Claim of “stronger gradients” over MMD is not fully substantiated because MMD gradient behavior depends critically on kernel bandwidth; comparisons lacked optimal bandwidth tuning or standard heuristics.  
Claim of “fewer hyperparameters” is tenuous since PMD relies on a distance metric (e.g., L-p norm) that introduces its own hyperparameters; this trade-off should be discussed.  
Overall recommendation: accept, but require revised experiments comparing MMD with proper bandwidth selection for a fairer evaluation.
