- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based estimator using minimum weight matching between populations.  
- Shows PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Argues PMD has advantages over MMD: fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and potentially smaller mini-batch sizes for optimization.  
- Demonstrates PMD applications in domain adaptation and generative modeling with improved performance and faster convergence versus MMD in experiments.  
- Paper is well-written with clear theoretical exposition and thorough experiments.  
- Main limitation: PMD requires large mini-batch size N to estimate Wasserstein for diverse, multimodal distributions.  
- PMD may fail with small N because optimization can have a global minimum that differs from the true distribution.  
- No known unbiased estimator of PMD gradient, complicating training with stochastic gradient descent.  
- In contrast, MMD admits an unbiased estimator that works with very small batches (batch_size=2) for SGD.  
- Generated images from PMD are inferior to Wasserstein GAN outputs, indicating weaker generative quality.  
- SVHN samples show low diversity (overrepresentation of digit '8'), suggesting mode collapse or sampling bias.  
- Originality: introduces a novel estimator, but builds on existing Wasserstein-based distribution matching ideas.  
- Significance: promising for multiple ML tasks (domain adaptation, generative modeling) but practical limits reduce immediate impact.  
- Recommendations: clarify PMD and MMD limitations, explore smaller mini-batch operation and unbiased gradient estimators, and analyze sample diversity/mode collapse.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel contribution, demonstrated effectiveness across tasks, and thorough analysis.  
- Arguments against acceptance: large batch-size requirement, lack of unbiased gradient estimator, and poorer image generation compared to WGAN.  
- Review scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 8/10.
