

**Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"**

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches computed via minimum weight matching. The authors establish PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, and propose its use as a training objective for domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The work builds on previous research in distribution distance estimation (MMD [17] and WGAN [3]), positioning PMD as addressing key limitations of MMD: kernel bandwidth sensitivity, weak gradients, and large minibatch requirements.

The paper's strengths include a clear theoretical foundation (Theorem 3.3 proving PMD's strong consistency with Wasserstein metric), well-executed experiments comparing PMD with MMD across domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), and practical implementation details for the matching algorithms. The empirical analysis of gradient magnitudes (Fig. 2b) provides compelling evidence for PMD's stronger gradients compared to MMD.

However, the paper has several significant limitations that undermine its claims. First, the generative modeling results are disappointing: PMD-generated images are blurrier than those from MMD (Fig. 4), and substantially worse than WGAN results, contradicting the claim that PMD "outperforms MMD on both tasks." Second, PMD requires large population sizes (N=2000 in experiments) to estimate Wasserstein for diverse distributions, contradicting the paper's claim about "smaller minibatch size" compared to MMD. Third, the paper fails to address a clear failure case: matching mixtures of Gaussians (including learning variances) would likely expose PMD's inability to capture complex distributional structure, as it only matches samples without modeling the underlying distribution.

The paper's claim that "PMD can use normal mini-batch size, such as 100" is misleading—the authors use N=2000 as population size with |B|=100, so PMD still requires large populations. The authors also do not provide an unbiased estimator for PMD gradients, unlike MMD which has a well-established unbiased estimator (from "A Kernel Two-Sample Test") that can be trained with SGD. The SVHN results (Fig. 4) show low diversity (digit '8' appears disproportionately often), suggesting the dataset may not be sufficiently challenging.

The paper contains a minor typo (Line 141: "usally" → "usually") and would benefit from a more thorough discussion of limitations. The authors should clarify that while PMD has advantages over MMD in some scenarios, it doesn't solve the fundamental challenges of using distribution distances as training objectives for generative models.

For quality, the paper is technically sound with proper theoretical grounding, but the experimental evaluation is limited and doesn't fully support the claimed advantages. The writing is clear and well-organized, though the discussion of limitations is inadequate. Originality is modest—while the application of minimum weight matching to estimate Wasserstein distance is novel for deep learning, the underlying connection to optimal transport has been known since [49]. Significance is limited: PMD represents an interesting alternative to MMD, but the practical advantages over established methods like WGAN are not demonstrated.

Pros for acceptance: Strong theoretical foundation, clear explanation of advantages over MMD, interesting empirical exploration of Wasserstein distance computation, good experimental setup for domain adaptation.

Cons for acceptance: Generative modeling results are inferior to WGAN, fails to address clear failure cases (mixture of Gaussians), misleading claims about minibatch size requirements, no unbiased gradient estimator, limited comparison with WGAN.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to distribution distance estimation, but the practical advantages over existing methods are limited and not fully supported by the evidence. The claims about performance advantages are overstated, particularly for generative modeling. The paper would benefit from more thorough evaluation, clearer discussion of limitations, and direct comparison with WGAN.

I recommend rejection. While the theoretical foundation is solid, the experimental results do not sufficiently demonstrate PMD's superiority over existing methods, and the paper fails to address key limitations that would be apparent to reviewers familiar with generative modeling literature.
