- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as Wasserstein distance via minimum weight matching and applies it to domain adaptation and generative modeling.  
- Theoretically strong: Theorem 3.3 establishes PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Claims PMD overcomes MMD issues (kernel bandwidth sensitivity, weak gradients, large minibatch needs).  
- Experiments compare PMD vs MMD on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW).  
- Provides practical implementation details for matching algorithms.  
- Empirical gradient analysis shows PMD yields stronger gradients than MMD (Fig. 2b).  
- Generative results are weak: PMD images are blurrier than MMD and much worse than WGAN.  
- PMD needs large population sizes (N=2000) for diverse distributions, contradicting claims of smaller minibatch requirements.  
- Fails to address clear failure cases (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians and learning variances), showing limits of sample-only matching.  
- Misleading claim: "normal mini-batch size" is |B|=100 but relies on large population N, so minibatch advantage is overstated.  
- No unbiased gradient estimator for PMD is provided, unlike established unbiased MMD estimators usable with SGD.  
- SVHN results show low diversity (digit '8' overrepresented), hinting dataset/experiment issues.  
- Minor typo ("usally" → "usually") and inadequate discussion of limitations.  
- Overall quality: technically sound and clear writing, but experimental evaluation is limited and doesn't support strong performance claims.  
- Originality: modest—application of minimum weight matching to deep learning is novel, but link to optimal transport is known.  
- Significance: limited—PMD is an interesting alternative but lacks demonstrated practical advantage over methods like WGAN.  
- Recommendation: reject—solid theory but insufficient empirical evidence and unaddressed limitations undermine claimed contributions.
