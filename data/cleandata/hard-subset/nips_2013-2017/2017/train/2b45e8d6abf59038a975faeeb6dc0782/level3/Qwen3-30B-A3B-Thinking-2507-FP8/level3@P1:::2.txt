

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable estimator of distribution distance based on samples, as a superior alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations, and the authors prove it's a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper demonstrates PMD's advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs MMD's typical 1000). The authors apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing improved performance and faster convergence.

The theoretical contribution is strong, with a rigorous proof establishing PMD's consistency as a Wasserstein estimator. The connection to optimal transport theory is well-motivated and properly contextualized within NIPS literature (e.g., Wasserstein GAN, MMD). The algorithm for parameter learning with PMD is clearly presented, with appropriate pseudocode and intuitive graphical illustration.

The paper's main strengths lie in its practical contributions. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate PMD's advantages: in domain adaptation, PMD significantly outperforms MMD on a→w and a→d tasks; in generative modeling, PMD produces less noisy images and converges faster. The ablation studies in Figure 5(a) provide compelling evidence that PMD's mini-batch size advantage is real and significant, with performance insensitive to population size N or matching algorithm choice (exact vs approximate).

However, the paper's claims about hyperparameters and gradients require closer scrutiny. The assertion that PMD has "fewer hyperparameters" is weakened by the fact that the choice of distance metric (e.g., L1 vs L2) introduces its own hyperparameters. The comparison of gradient strengths is less convincing because MMD's performance depends heavily on kernel bandwidth, which isn't consistently tuned across comparisons. The paper should have compared PMD to MMD using standard bandwidth heuristics or treated MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free (e.g., using a mixture of kernels as in GMMN).

The high-dimensional distance estimation problem remains unaddressed. While the paper shows PMD works well on image datasets, it doesn't explore whether PMD faces similar challenges to MMD in high-dimensional spaces (e.g., the curse of dimensionality). The authors acknowledge this limitation in the generative modeling results (blurry images on SVHN/LFW), suggesting the pixel-level L1 distance is suboptimal for natural images, but don't explore how this limitation might scale to higher dimensions.

The paper's significance is strong: it addresses real pain points in deep learning (MMD's bandwidth sensitivity, weak gradients, large mini-batch requirements) with a practical solution that demonstrates clear improvements in two important application areas. The work advances the state of the art in distribution distance estimation, providing practitioners with a more efficient alternative to MMD.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the theoretical contributions and thorough experimental validation. The comparisons to related work (Wasserstein GAN, MMD) are appropriate, and the authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., matching computation cost).

Pro arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical foundation connecting PMD to Wasserstein metric; convincing empirical evidence of practical advantages over MMD; clear applications to important deep learning tasks; well-executed experiments demonstrating faster convergence and smaller mini-batch requirements.

Con arguments against acceptance: Weaker-than-claimed hyperparameter advantage (distance metric choice introduces its own hyperparameters); less convincing gradient comparison (MMD's behavior depends on bandwidth tuning); insufficient exploration of high-dimensional limitations.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that addresses a meaningful practical problem in deep learning. While the claims about hyperparameters need refinement, the core idea of PMD is novel, well-supported, and demonstrates clear practical advantages over the widely-used MMD. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of distribution distance estimation, with potential to impact both research and practice in domain adaptation and generative modeling. The work meets the quality bar for NIPS, with strong theoretical grounding and compelling empirical results. The significance is high, as the proposed method addresses real limitations in current practice. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the hyperparameter discussion and address the high-dimensional distance estimation concerns.
