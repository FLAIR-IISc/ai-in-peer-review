- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based, population-level estimator of W1 using minimum-weight bipartite matching between i.i.d. samples.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 and provides a practical SGD-compatible learning procedure treating PMD as a differentiable loss.  
- Discusses exact O(N^3) Hungarian algorithm and a practical O(N^2) Drake–Hougardy approximation, showing the latter preserves empirical performance.  
- Positions PMD as a population-based alternative to MMD and compares PMD vs. MMD theoretically (gradients, hyperparameters, minibatching) and empirically (domain adaptation, generative modeling).  
- Relates PMD to Wasserstein GANs (dual formulation) and other Wasserstein estimators; emphasizes PMD as a direct, parameter-free primal estimator compatible with gradient-based training.  

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple and well-motivated: PMD aligns exactly with empirical W1 and the consistency result is well-grounded.  
- Differentiable training objective in practice: piecewise smooth objective supports SGD; not backpropagating through discrete matching is reasonable.  
- Clear advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger and more uniform gradients, and ability to use smaller minibatches.  
- Solid empirical work: thorough MMD comparisons, wins in domain adaptation tasks, competitive generative modeling with faster convergence.  
- Good writing, organization, and broadly cited related work; claims are mostly supported by analysis and experiments.  

Weaknesses and questions — Differentiability & optimization:
- Gradient ignores dependence of matching on parameters; need clarification on SGD convergence with stale matchings/subsampling.  
- Need empirical data on how often matchings change and whether changes induce optimization noise or bias.  
- Consideration of soft/entropic relaxations (e.g., Sinkhorn) to obtain smooth, GPU-friendly gradients and enable backprop through transport.  

Weaknesses and questions — Scalability:
- Both exact and approximate matchings require O(N^2) pairwise distances and memory; Hungarian is O(N^3); feasibility shown only up to a few thousand samples.  
- Request discussion of memory/runtime behavior for larger N and high-dimensional data and possible blockwise/Sinkhorn/sparsified approximations to reduce N^2 memory.  
- Ask for bounds or empirical reporting on the optimality gap of the approximate matching.  

Weaknesses and questions — Empirical breadth:
- Domain adaptation comparisons limited to MMD; stronger baselines like DANN/ADDA, JAN, CORAL, DSN, CMD should be included.  
- Generative modeling evaluation is largely qualitative; request quantitative metrics (Inception Score/FID, precision/recall, density/coverage).  
- Need multimodality stress tests (Gaussian grids, Stacked MNIST) to assess mode coverage and behavior when population size N < number of modes.  

Weaknesses and questions — Ground metric:
- Performance depends on ground metric; pixel L1 blurs images—study PMD on learned feature spaces and compare L1 vs L2 vs perceptual features.  

Weaknesses and questions — Finite-sample behavior:
- Strong consistency shown but rates/concentration not discussed; empirical rates (~N^-0.23) should be connected to known finite-sample OT results and implications for choosing N and batch size.  

Weaknesses and questions — Unequal sample sizes:
- Method assumes equal-sized populations with uniform weights; discuss extensions to unequal sizes or non-uniform empirical weights.  

Quality:
- Theory (OT connection and consistency) appears correct and well-cited; a remark about subgradients and measure-zero nondifferentiable points would help.  
- Empirics are adequate but need broader baselines and quantitative metrics; gradient magnitude and minibatch analyses are insightful.  

Clarity:
- Paper is clearly written, well-structured, and reproducible; an algorithmic appendix on the approximate matching and complexity would be helpful.  

Originality:
- Using minimum-weight matching as a population W1 estimator is natural but under-explored; the paper adds an end-to-end training recipe and careful MMD comparison—should discuss entropic OT/Sinkhorn as a close alternative.  

Significance:
- PMD is a practical, parameter-free divergence with promising optimization properties that could complement MMD and adversarial/dual Wasserstein methods if scalability and evaluation breadth are addressed.  

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, principled, consistent estimator with a practical training algorithm; clear empirical advantages over MMD in speed and stability; reproducible and useful to practitioners.  

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited baselines in domain adaptation and limited quantitative generative evaluation; missing multimodal and N < #modes experiments; scalability and differentiability through matching need better justification or alternatives.  

Requested additions/clarifications:
- Add multimodal generative experiments (Stacked MNIST, Gaussian grids) including N < #modes scenarios.  
- Include quantitative generative metrics (FID/IS, precision/recall).  
- Compare against at least one adversarial domain-adaptation baseline (e.g., DANN) and CMD/CORAL.  
- Discuss or test a soft/entropic matching variant and report approximate matching optimality gaps.  
- Comment on finite-sample rates and choice of ground metric/feature space.  

Overall assessment:
- A good paper proposing a potentially useful distribution-matching method; recommend acceptance if empirical breadth and scalability/soft OT discussion are strengthened.
