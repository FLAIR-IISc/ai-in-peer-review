Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets, as a sample-based surrogate for the 1-Wasserstein distance. The authors prove strong consistency and use PMD as a differentiable training objective for domain adaptation and implicit generative modeling, contrasting it with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). This connects to a substantial NeurIPS literature on kernel two-sample tests/MMD, OT/Wasserstein distances (including WGAN), and domain adaptation via distribution alignment.

Quality: The theoretical contribution—PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of W1—is sound and well-situated in optimal transport theory. The decision to ignore gradients through the discrete matching is standard and plausibly justified by piecewise-constancy. Empirically, the small mini-batch advantage is convincingly demonstrated and leads to faster convergence in practice. However, the “stronger gradients” claim is insufficiently supported: MMD’s gradient magnitude depends critically on kernel bandwidth, and fair comparisons should sweep standard bandwidths (e.g., median heuristic), multi-kernel variants, or report the best over a range. The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is also weak: PMD requires a ground metric and feature space choice, which introduce their own hyperparameters and scaling issues. High-dimensional distance estimation remains problematic (distance concentration, n−1/d sample complexity for OT), so PMD may face similar limitations as MMD.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. The dual use of “population” vs “mini-batch” could be unified earlier. Including complexity breakdowns is helpful.

Originality: Framing minimum bipartite matching as a practical, differentiable surrogate for W1 in deep training is a useful perspective. The consistency result is not novel per se but appropriately adapted; the empirical emphasis on mini-batch efficiency is a practical contribution.

Significance: PMD appears to be a valid improvement for fast training with small batches. Its reliance on ground metrics may limit performance in high-dimensional pixel spaces (as the authors observe). Broader significance would be strengthened by comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP, Cramér distance, MMD-GAN, Sinkhorn-regularized OT (Cuturi, 2013), and sliced Wasserstein.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid consistency result; simple, differentiable objective.
- Clear empirical advantage for small mini-batches and faster convergence.
- Competitive results on domain adaptation and generative modeling.

Cons for acceptance:
- Gradient-strength claim not fairly benchmarked against tuned MMD.
- “Fewer hyperparameters” argument is overstated; PMD depends on metric/feature choices.
- Limited analysis of high-dimensional behavior and sample complexity.
- Missing baselines (WGAN/WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn OT, sliced Wasserstein) and MMD with standard bandwidth heuristics/linear-time estimators.

Suggestions: Compare to MMD with median heuristic and multi-kernel choices, treating it as effectively hyperparameter-free; report best-over-sigma. Add baselines against WGAN(-GP), MMD-GAN, and Sinkhorn/sliced OT; include ablations over ground metrics/feature spaces and a high-dimensional synthetic study. Discuss practical guidance on metric choice and scalability.
