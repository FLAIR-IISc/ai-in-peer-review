The paper "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning" proposes a novel method, called Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching between two random populations from the distributions, and the authors show that it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a thorough comparison to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), the primary population-based divergence used in machine learning. The authors demonstrate the advantages of PMD over MMD, including fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes for gradient-based optimization.

The experimental analysis is adequate, with comparisons to MMD and other methods for domain adaptation and generative modeling. The results show that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed in both tasks.

The theoretical results appear correct, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric. The paper also provides a stochastic gradient descent algorithm to learn parameters of the distributions using PMD.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of experiments on generative modeling with many modes, which could be an important application of PMD. Additionally, the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of PMD's behavior when the sample size N is smaller than the number of modes.

Overall, this is a good paper that proposes a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the method and demonstrate its advantages over existing methods.
