- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) to address MMD’s sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, weak gradients, and large mini-batch requirements.
- Theoretically links PMD to the Wasserstein distance and proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.
- Provides a differentiable estimator without requiring kernel selection, claimed to improve gradient behavior and mini-batch efficiency.
- Empirical results show improved performance and faster convergence, particularly with smaller mini-batches, on standard benchmarks (Office, MNIST/SVHN/LFW).
- The “stronger gradients” claim is insufficiently supported because MMD was not compared across a range of kernel bandwidths.
- A fairer evaluation would tune MMD (or test common bandwidth heuristics) to assess PMD’s consistent advantage.
- The claim of “fewer hyperparameters” is questionable since PMD’s choice of distance metric (e.g., L1) introduces its own hyperparameter considerations.
- Authors acknowledge PMD may face similar high-dimensional distance-estimation issues as MMD and should discuss mitigation strategies or future work.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel and theoretically grounded approach, faster small-batch training, clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: need for stronger empirical validation of gradient claims, debatable hyperparameter advantage, and limited discussion of high-dimensional limitations.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address gradient validation, hyperparameter claims, and high-dimensional challenges.
