This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel, sample-based divergence for comparing probability distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations. The authors provide a solid theoretical contribution by proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance. This work positions PMD as a compelling alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a widely used metric in areas like domain adaptation and generative modeling (e.g., GMMNs). Unlike MMD, PMD has no sensitive kernel hyperparameters, and unlike recent adversarial methods for approximating the Wasserstein distance (e.g., WGAN), it is parameter-free and computed directly from samples.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper introduces a conceptually simple yet powerful method that addresses well-known limitations of MMD, such as kernel bandwidth tuning and vanishing gradients. The potential for PMD to become a standard tool for distribution matching is high, given its strong performance and ease of use.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical claims appear sound and are well-supported by connections to optimal transport theory. The authors pragmatically address the method's cubic complexity by proposing a quadratic approximation that, according to their experiments, preserves statistical performance. The experimental analysis is thorough, providing convincing comparisons against MMD on both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly written. The motivation is compelling, the method is presented lucidly, and prior work is appropriately cited and contextualized.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**
The primary weakness lies in the scope of the generative modeling experiments. While the results are promising, the chosen datasets do not sufficiently test PMD's ability to handle highly multi-modal target distributions.
1.  Could the authors conduct experiments on a synthetic dataset with many distinct modes to better understand how PMD captures the full diversity of a distribution compared to MMD or GAN-based approaches?
2.  The paper would benefit from a discussion on the behavior of PMD when the sample size N is small, particularly when N is less than the number of modes in the underlying distributions. It seems the matching could be less meaningful in this regime.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper proposing a novel and potentially very useful method. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be addressed with further experiments and discussion. I recommend acceptance.
