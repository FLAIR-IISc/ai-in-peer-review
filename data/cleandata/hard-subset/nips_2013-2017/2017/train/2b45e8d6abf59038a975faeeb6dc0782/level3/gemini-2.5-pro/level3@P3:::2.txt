### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples, one from each distribution. The authors provide a key theoretical contribution by showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein (or earth mover's) distance.

The paper positions PMD as a superior alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), particularly for training deep generative models. The main claimed advantages of PMD are: (1) it has fewer hyperparameters, avoiding the sensitive kernel bandwidth selection required by MMD; (2) it provides stronger, non-saturating gradients during optimization; and (3) it can be effectively optimized with small mini-batches, decoupling the SGD batch size from the population size used for distance estimation. This latter point addresses a significant practical limitation of MMD-based training, which typically requires large batches. The method is evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and generative image modeling, where it is shown to outperform MMD in terms of both final performance and convergence speed.

The work relates to a significant body of literature on integral probability metrics, including MMD, and recent work on using the Wasserstein distance for generative modeling, such as Wasserstein GANs (WGANs). Unlike WGANs, which approximate the dual form of the Wasserstein distance using a learned critic network, PMD directly estimates the primal form without requiring an auxiliary network.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Practical Approach:** The formulation of a differentiable distribution distance based on minimum weight matching is an original and elegant idea. It provides a direct, non-adversarial method for optimizing the Wasserstein distance, offering a valuable new tool for the deep learning community.

2.  **Strong Empirical Advantage on a Key Metric:** The paper's most convincing contribution is the empirical validation of its ability to train effectively with small mini-batches. Figure 5(a) clearly demonstrates that smaller SGD batch sizes lead to better optimization outcomes for PMD, confirming that it successfully decouples the optimization batch size from the population size `N`. This is a significant practical advantage over MMD, which requires large batches for stable estimation, leading to faster training and lower memory usage.

3.  **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The paper grounds the proposed PMD metric in the theory of optimal transport, proving that it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance. This theoretical link provides a solid justification for the method.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The method is explained intuitively with helpful illustrations and pseudocode, making it easy to understand and potentially reproduce.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Unconvincing "Stronger Gradients" Claim:** The claim that PMD provides stronger gradients than MMD is not rigorously supported. The analysis and experiments compare PMD (with L1 distance) against MMD with an RBF kernel, but the gradient behavior of MMD is critically dependent on the kernel bandwidth `σ`. A suboptimal `σ` can easily lead to either vanishing or exploding gradients. A fair comparison would require comparing PMD against MMD with a well-tuned `σ`, or at least one chosen by a standard heuristic (e.g., the median heuristic), which often yields robust performance. Without this, the observed difference in gradient distributions (Fig. 2b) may simply be an artifact of a poor hyperparameter choice for the baseline.

2.  **Oversimplified "Fewer Hyperparameters" Claim:** The paper claims PMD is superior for having fewer hyperparameters by eliminating the kernel bandwidth. However, this is a simplification, as PMD introduces a dependency on the choice of the distance metric `d(·, ·)`. While the paper uses L1 distance, the choice of metric is itself a crucial hyperparameter that can dramatically affect performance, especially for structured data like images. For instance, one could use L2 distance, a learned Mahalanobis distance, or perceptual distances, each with its own properties and potential parameters. Arguably, using a standard heuristic to set MMD's bandwidth makes it effectively as "hyperparameter-free" as PMD with a standard L1/L2 metric.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on High-Dimensionality:** The paper does not address the well-known challenges of using distance-based metrics in high-dimensional spaces (the "curse of dimensionality"). Metrics like L1 and L2, used by PMD, can become less meaningful as dimensionality increases. Since the experiments involve high-dimensional pixel and feature spaces (up to 4096-dim), the paper would be significantly stronger if it acknowledged this potential limitation and discussed how PMD's performance might degrade in even higher-dimensional settings, a problem that also affects MMD. The blurriness of the generated images on SVHN and LFW may be symptomatic of this issue, where pixel-wise L1 distance fails to capture perceptual similarity.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   To strengthen the claims, the authors should provide a more rigorous comparison against MMD. This should include results where MMD's bandwidth is selected using a standard data-driven heuristic. This would provide a much more compelling case for PMD's advantages.
*   The discussion on hyperparameters should be more nuanced, acknowledging that the choice of the distance metric `d(·, ·)` in PMD is a critical design choice analogous to the choice of kernel in MMD.
*   It would be beneficial to add a discussion section on the limitations of the proposed approach, particularly concerning the curse of dimensionality and the computational complexity of the matching algorithm (O(N³) for exact matching), even if approximations are used in practice.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practically useful method for comparing probability distributions. The connection between minimum weight matching and the Wasserstein distance is insightful, and the demonstrated ability to train with small mini-batches is a clear and significant advantage over existing MMD-based methods.

However, some of the claims regarding superiority over MMD (stronger gradients, fewer hyperparameters) are not fully substantiated and rely on comparisons that may not be entirely fair to the baseline. Furthermore, the work misses an opportunity to discuss the important challenge of applying distance-based methods in high-dimensional settings.

Despite these weaknesses, the core contribution is solid and provides a valuable new direction for non-adversarial generative modeling and other tasks requiring distribution matching. The work is of high quality and is likely to be of significant interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
