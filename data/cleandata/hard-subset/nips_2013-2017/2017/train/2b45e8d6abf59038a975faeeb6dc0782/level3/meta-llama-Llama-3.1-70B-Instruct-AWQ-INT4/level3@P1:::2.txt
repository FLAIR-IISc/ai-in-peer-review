This paper proposes a novel method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples, called Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD). The authors claim that PMD is a superior alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. In this review, we will summarize the main ideas of the paper, relate them to previous work, and evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.

The paper starts by introducing the problem of estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. The authors note that this problem is crucial in many deep learning tasks, such as generative modeling, domain adaptation, and model criticism. They then introduce PMD, which is defined as the minimum weight matching between two random populations from the distributions. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, which is a valid statistical distance.

The authors also propose an algorithm to learn the parameters of the distributions using PMD as an objective. They demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD on two deep learning tasks: domain adaptation and generative modeling. The empirical results show that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed in both tasks.

The paper relates to previous work on distribution matching, particularly MMD. The authors argue that PMD has several advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients, applicability with smaller mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters. However, we note that the claim of stronger gradients is less convincing because MMD's behavior depends on the kernel bandwidth (sigma), and comparisons should use the best or a range of sigmas. However, the paper also has some weaknesses. First, the reliance on distance metrics in high-dimensional settings may limit the applicability of PMD. Finally, the paper could benefit from more detailed discussions of the limitations and potential pitfalls of PMD, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
