This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based distance metric for comparing two probability distributions, and demonstrates its application in deep learning tasks such as domain adaptation and generative modeling. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two populations (minibatches) of samples, one from each distribution. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The core idea is to use this differentiable, sample-based approximation of the Wasserstein distance as a training objective for deep neural networks. This work is positioned as an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD avoids sensitive kernel bandwidth hyperparameters, provides stronger gradients, and allows for more efficient training with smaller SGD mini-batches. This approach relates to the recent surge of interest in optimal transport and Wasserstein distances for deep learning, such as in Wasserstein GANs (WGANs). However, unlike WGANs which approximate the dual form of the Wasserstein distance using a critic network, PMD directly computes an estimate of the primal form on batches of samples.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Soundness:** The primary contribution is the proposal of PMD, a direct and computationally tractable estimator for the Wasserstein-1 metric on minibatches, and its use as a loss function for deep learning. The connection to minimum weight bipartite matching is elegant, and the proof that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the true Wasserstein metric provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method.

2.  **Compelling Alternative to MMD:** The paper makes a strong case against the practical difficulties of MMD, particularly its sensitivity to the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter and the issue of weak or vanishing gradients, which can slow down training. The empirical analysis in Figure 2(b) effectively illustrates the claim that PMD provides more uniformly strong gradients across samples compared to MMD.

3.  **Good Experimental Analysis:** The experiments on domain adaptation are promising, showing that PMD can outperform MMD on several standard benchmark tasks. The analysis of convergence speed and parameter sensitivity in Figure 3 further strengthens the paper's claims about the practical advantages of PMD. The proposed optimization strategy of performing the expensive matching step less frequently than the SGD updates is a sensible and practical choice.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Sample Complexity and Potential for Mode Collapse:** A major concern is the sample complexity required to obtain a meaningful estimate of the Wasserstein distance, especially for diverse, high-dimensional, or multimodal distributions. With a small population size N, the matching may lead the optimization to a biased solution that does not reflect the true target distribution (e.g., a learned distribution with lower entropy). This could explain the observed low diversity in the generative modeling results (e.g., the over-representation of the digit '8' in SVHN samples). The paper would be stronger if it included experiments on challenging synthetic distributions (e.g., mixtures of well-separated Gaussians) to better characterize this limitation.

2.  **Quality of Generative Modeling Results:** While the idea is interesting, the empirical results for generative modeling are not as compelling as those for domain adaptation. The generated images for SVHN and LFW are noticeably blurry and lack the quality and diversity seen in contemporaneous work like WGANs. This weakens the claim that PMD is a superior method for this task.

3.  **Unfair Comparison with MMD:** The critique of MMD's batch size requirements is potentially misleading. The paper argues that MMD requires a large mini-batch size (N=1000), while PMD can use a smaller SGD update batch (|B|=100). However, MMD has a well-known unbiased estimator (Gretton et al., "A Kernel Two-Sample Test," JMLR 2012) that can be computed efficiently on very small mini-batches (as small as 2) and used effectively with SGD. The claim that PMD has an advantage in this regard is therefore not fully justified and should be revisited.

4.  **Biased Gradient Estimator:** The paper computes the gradient by assuming the optimal matching M* remains fixed (Eq. 3). While this makes the gradient tractable, it is a biased estimator of the gradient of the true objective, which is the *expected* PMD over all possible sample populations. Unlike the unbiased estimator for MMD, PMD does not appear to have a known unbiased gradient estimator. This is a subtle but important theoretical limitation that should be acknowledged and discussed.

### Summary of Arguments

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel and theoretically grounded method (PMD) for estimating the Wasserstein distance on minibatches.
*   Provides a compelling alternative to MMD, addressing some of its practical drawbacks like hyperparameter tuning and weak gradients.
*   Shows strong empirical results on domain adaptation tasks.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   The method's reliance on small-N matching is a significant limitation for complex distributions, likely leading to mode-seeking behavior and low-diversity solutions.
*   Generative modeling results are not competitive with state-of-the-art methods, questioning the method's effectiveness for this application.
*   The comparison with MMD regarding mini-batch size requirements is based on a questionable premise.
*   The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of its approach, particularly the biased nature of the gradient estimator and the high sample complexity needed for accurate Wasserstein estimation.

### Detailed Comments

- The authors should explicitly discuss the limitations of using a finite, and often small, sample population N to estimate the Wasserstein metric. How does this affect the optimization landscape and the properties of the learned distribution?
- Please clarify the claims regarding MMD and batch sizes, acknowledging the existence and utility of the unbiased MMD estimator for small-batch SGD.
- The SVHN samples in Figure 4 appear to show signs of mode collapse. A quantitative evaluation of sample diversity would be a valuable addition to the generative modeling experiments.
- Minor typo: On line 141 of the original PDF, "usally" should be "usually".

Overall, this paper introduces an interesting and original idea. The connection between minimum weight matching and the Wasserstein metric is well-established, and applying it as a loss for deep learning is a valuable contribution. However, the practical significance is hampered by limitations that are not fully addressed, and some of the claims against competing methods require more careful justification.
