- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets as a population-based divergence.  
- PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance (W1).  
- Estimator is conceptually simple and made differentiable for training by not backpropagating through the discrete matching.  
- Positioned as a practical, parameter-free alternative to MMD, avoiding kernel bandwidth tuning.  
- Claims stronger gradients and smaller effective mini-batch requirements compared to MMD, reducing gradient saturation issues.  
- Exact PMD computation is cubic via the Hungarian algorithm; authors propose a quadratic-time approximation that empirically preserves performance.  
- Demonstrated advantages over MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling: improved accuracy and faster convergence.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-structured, and situates itself appropriately with prior MMD and Wasserstein work.  
- Theoretical results are sound and appropriately scoped, using empirical-measure convergence and transport stability.  
- Empirical analysis supports claims about gradient strength and convergence.  
- Reviewer requests additional experiments: sensitivity vs. sample size when number of data modes exceeds N.  
- Reviewer requests more challenging multi-modal generative experiments (many-mode mixtures/datasets) with coverage/precision-recall/FID metrics.  
- Clarity suggestion: discuss subgradient behavior when matchings change and memory cost of the N×N distance matrix.  
- Originality: novel formulation using matching as a differentiable objective relative to kernel two-sample tests and adversarial Wasserstein training.  
- Significance: high — practical, parameter-free, strong-gradient alternative to MMD with a usable quadratic approximation.  
- Pros: principled W1-consistent estimator, no bandwidth, stronger gradients, smaller batches, solid empirical gains, clear theory and writing.  
- Cons / open questions: limited evaluation on highly multi-modal generative settings; behavior when N < number of modes unclear; computational and memory scaling concerns; no analysis of bias from the quadratic matching approximation.
