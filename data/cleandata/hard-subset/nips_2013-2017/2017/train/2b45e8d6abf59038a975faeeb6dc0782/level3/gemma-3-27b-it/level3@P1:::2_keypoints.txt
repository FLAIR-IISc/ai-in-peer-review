- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel sample-based distance estimator motivated by MMD’s limitations.
- Formulates distribution distance as minimum-weight matching of samples and shows consistency with the first Wasserstein metric.
- Proposes an algorithm for learning distribution parameters using PMD, with pseudocode and computational complexity discussion.
- Claims empirical advantages: stronger gradients, ability to use normal mini-batch sizes, and reduced hyperparameter tuning.
- Demonstrates improved performance and faster convergence in domain adaptation and generative modeling experiments.
- Provides theoretical grounding by connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric and optimal transport theory.
- Strength: solid theoretical justification, clear presentation, and empirical evidence supporting gradient and mini-batch benefits.
- Weakness: “fewer hyperparameters” claim is overstated because PMD depends on a chosen distance metric (e.g., L1) that can be sensitive.
- Weakness: Does not evaluate the impact of different distance metrics on PMD’s performance.
- Weakness: Comparison to MMD lacks nuance—MMD’s gradients depend on kernel bandwidth and should be evaluated with tuned bandwidths.
- Weakness: Unclear how PMD scales to high-dimensional settings; potential curse-of-dimensionality issues are not addressed.
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically sound alternative to MMD, practical mini-batch speedups, convincing empirical results, and strong Wasserstein connection.
- Arguments against acceptance: overstated hyperparameter benefits, insufficiently thorough MMD comparisons, and missing analysis for high-dimensional applicability.
- Overall recommendation: accept, with suggestions to study high-dimensional performance, metric choices, and a more nuanced MMD comparison.
