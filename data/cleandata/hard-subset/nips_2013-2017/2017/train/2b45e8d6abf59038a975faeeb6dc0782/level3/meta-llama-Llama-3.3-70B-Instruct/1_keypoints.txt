- Introduces a novel framework for personalized screening policies balancing screening cost against delay in disease detection.
- Models disease as an arbitrary finite-state stochastic process with an absorbing disease state.
- Incorporates external information that can trigger patient arrival before scheduled screening.
- Solution is based on point-based value iteration, leveraging established decision-theory methods.
- Framework is computationally tractable and can handle complex disease and information models.
- Experiments on breast cancer data show large reductions in screenings while maintaining the same detection delay.
- Assumes model parameters are known, which may not hold in practice.
- Discussion of estimation error is limited; more work is needed to address parameter uncertainty.
- Unclear how well the approach generalizes beyond the breast cancer case study.
- Paper is original, well-motivated, and makes a significant contribution to personalized screening policies.
- The manuscript is well-written and easy to follow with clear explanations.
- Potential public-health significance: reduces unnecessary screenings while preserving effective detection.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, strong experimental results, potential public-health impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on known parameters, limited handling of estimation error, uncertain generalizability.
- Reviewer summary: technically sound and promising; strong candidate for acceptance.
- Scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 9/10, Significance 9/10.
