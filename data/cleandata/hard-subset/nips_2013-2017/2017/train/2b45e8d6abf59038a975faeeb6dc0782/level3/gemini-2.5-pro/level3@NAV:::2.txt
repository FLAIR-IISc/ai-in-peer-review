This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based measure for comparing probability distributions. The authors show that PMD, defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two sets of samples, is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance. This work builds upon a rich history of using integral probability metrics in machine learning, positioning PMD as an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) and as a primal-form counterpart to recent dual-form Wasserstein approximations like WGAN. The proposed applications in generative modeling and domain adaptation are standard testbeds for such discrepancy measures, directly comparing against MMD-based approaches like Generative Moment Matching Networks.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its well-motivated and practical contribution. The authors convincingly demonstrate PMD's most significant advantage: its ability to train models effectively with much smaller mini-batches compared to MMD. This is a crucial benefit for computational efficiency and memory constraints, and the empirical results clearly support this claim. The paper is well-written and technically sound, providing both theoretical justification for PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance and a clear algorithm for its use in gradient-based optimization.

However, some of the paper's claims against MMD are not fully substantiated. The assertion of "stronger gradients" is weakened by the comparison methodology. MMD's gradient behavior is critically dependent on the kernel bandwidth (sigma), and a fair comparison would require evaluating MMD with an optimally tuned sigma or, at a minimum, using a standard bandwidth selection heuristic (e.g., the median heuristic). Without this, the observed weakness in MMD's gradients might stem from suboptimal hyperparameter choice rather than an inherent flaw. Similarly, the claim of having "fewer hyperparameters" is tenuous. While PMD avoids the kernel bandwidth, it introduces a reliance on a distance metric `d(·, ·)`, which can have its own hyperparameters (e.g., the `p` in an L-p norm). A more nuanced discussion acknowledging this trade-off would strengthen the paper.

In summary, PMD is a valuable and significant contribution, offering a practical method for distribution matching that excels in small-batch training regimes. While the claims of superiority over MMD in terms of gradients and hyperparameter simplicity are overstated, the method's core advantages are empirically validated. The work is original and likely to be influential for practitioners and researchers in generative modeling. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly encourage the authors to revise their experimental comparisons with MMD to account for proper bandwidth selection, which would provide a more rigorous evaluation.
