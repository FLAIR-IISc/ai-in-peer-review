Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based objective for matching probability distributions that computes the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. populations. The authors show that PMD equals the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (Earth Mover’s Distance, EMD) between the empirical measures with uniform weights and prove strong consistency via Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of OT. They then use PMD as a differentiable training objective by backpropagating through the matched pairs but not through the discrete assignment (arguing the matching is locally constant). The paper contrasts PMD with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) and claims three practical advantages: no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches than the matching population. Applications to unsupervised domain adaptation (Office-31) and implicit deep generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) show PMD can outperform MMD and converge faster.

The work sits at the intersection of kernel two-sample tests (Gretton et al., 2012), Wasserstein-based learning (Arjovsky et al., 2017), and OT-based distances more broadly. The equivalence of empirical 1-Wasserstein to a minimum-weight matching is classical (Villani, 2009), and fast/differentiable approximations to OT (e.g., entropic-regularized “Sinkhorn distances”, Cuturi, 2013) and energy/Cramér distances (Bellemare et al., 2017) are important nearby baselines. For domain adaptation, MMD/DANN/CMD-style regularizers are standard comparators (Ganin et al., 2016; Zellinger et al., 2017).

Strengths
- Clear formulation: PMD is a straightforward empirical OT objective with a practical optimization scheme.
- Useful empirical insight: convincingly demonstrates that using a larger matching population with smaller SGD mini-batches can speed convergence.
- Solid applications: shows competitive or improved performance on Office-31 and acceptable image generation on several datasets, with qualitative improvements over MMD in some settings.
- Practicality: approximate O(N^2) matching works well in practice; the gradient argument (holding the matching fixed for several steps) is simple and effective.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical novelty is limited: the strong consistency follows directly from standard OT results; no finite-sample rates or generalization bounds are provided (known rates exist for Wasserstein distances; e.g., Fournier & Guillin, 2015).
- Comparisons to MMD and gradient claims are not fully convincing: the observed “weak gradients” can depend heavily on kernel bandwidth/multi-kernel choices; comparisons should include standard heuristics (e.g., median heuristic) and report best-over-bandwidths.
- “Fewer hyperparameters” is debatable: PMD depends on the choice of ground metric (and potentially feature space), which itself introduces design choices/hyperparameters; in high dimensions, Euclidean/L1 distances can be problematic.
- Missing baselines: entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) offers differentiable, GPU-friendly, O(N^2) approximations and is a natural comparator; WGAN/WGAN-GP and Cramér distance are also important for generative modeling. For domain adaptation, DANN/CMD should be included.
- Differentiability/estimation details: ignoring derivatives through the assignment yields a subgradient almost everywhere, but the paper does not quantify the bias introduced by holding M fixed across inner-loop steps or by subsampling matched pairs.

Quality
- Soundness: The reduction of PMD to empirical 1-Wasserstein and the consistency argument are correct. The optimization heuristic (freeze M, subsample) is reasonable but lacks a bias/variance analysis. High-dimensional performance is acknowledged as sensitive to the ground metric (blurry images with pixel L1), but a more systematic study would help.
- Empirics: Results on Office-31 are promising on some transfers and comparable on others; convergence speedups are shown. For generative modeling, qualitative samples suggest PMD helps outside autoencoder code space, but quantitative metrics (e.g., FID/IS) are missing and comparisons to modern baselines are absent. Compute fairness should be clarified (PMD 100 epochs vs. MMD 500).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The connection to OT is clear. Suggestions:
  - Explicitly state assumptions under which the argmin matching is locally constant and discuss subgradient/differentiability at ties.
  - Clarify the training protocol when source/target dataset sizes differ (sampling equal-sized populations each iteration).
  - Discuss that PMD is an estimator of a metric; DN is not itself a metric for finite N (e.g., triangle inequality may fail).
  - Provide more detail on the approximate matching algorithm and its effect on gradients.

Originality
- The algorithmic package (empirical OT via matching + backprop through matched pairs + inner-loop freezing + small-batch subsampling) is a practical twist, but the core distance and consistency results are standard. Novelty is primarily empirical/pragmatic rather than theoretical.

Significance
- As a practical alternative to MMD when small mini-batches are desired, PMD is useful. However, without comparisons to Sinkhorn OT, WGAN(-GP), or Cramér distance, it is difficult to assess its broader impact for generative modeling. For domain adaptation, stronger baselines are needed to establish significance.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of ground metric and feature space? Could you report experiments with learned/perceptual metrics (e.g., feature-space L2) and discuss the resulting hyperparameters?
- Can you provide quantitative generative metrics (e.g., FID) and comparisons to WGAN(-GP) and Sinkhorn OT?
- How do your results change when MMD uses the median heuristic or multi-kernel bandwidths selected via validation?
- Can you quantify the bias introduced by holding M fixed for multiple steps and by subsampling matched pairs from the precomputed matching?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: Sinkhorn-regularized OT, WGAN(-GP), and Cramér distance for generative modeling; DANN/CMD for domain adaptation.
- Use standard bandwidth heuristics and best-over-bandwidths for MMD; report sensitivity plots.
- Provide quantitative metrics for generation (FID/IS) and matched compute budgets.
- Discuss known sample complexity rates for Wasserstein distances and situate your empirical N−0.23 observation relative to theory.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, practical objective with clear implementation path and useful empirical behavior (small-batch training).
- Competitive performance on domain adaptation and improved qualitative samples for some generators.
- Bridges the gap between theory (Wasserstein) and practice (matching-based SGD) for deep models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical novelty beyond standard OT facts; lacks finite-sample analysis.
- Missing key baselines (Sinkhorn, WGAN(-GP), DANN/CMD) and quantitative generative metrics.
- Claims about stronger gradients and fewer hyperparameters are not fully substantiated; high-dimensional distance choice remains a core limitation.

Overall
A promising and practically useful approach that improves upon MMD in certain training regimes, especially with small mini-batches. With stronger baselines, more thorough MMD comparisons, and quantitative generative evaluations, the paper would make a more compelling case. As it stands, I view it as solid but borderline for acceptance.
