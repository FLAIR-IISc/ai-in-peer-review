- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): average cost of minimum-weight bipartite matching between two sample sets.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein (W1) distance.  
- PMD is differentiable w.r.t. sample locations, enabling use as a training objective for deep networks via SGD.  
- Conceptually simple and parameter-free (no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter).  
- Claims advantages over MMD: no bandwidth tuning, stronger/non-saturating gradients, and more flexible mini-batching.  
- Exact PMD computation is cubic in population size N; authors propose a quadratic approximation that performs well empirically.  
- Empirical evaluation on unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling shows PMD outperforming MMD-based methods.  
- Strength: significant and original connection between bipartite matching and Wasserstein estimation, timely for OT-based ML methods.  
- Strength: technically sound with solid theoretical guarantees and a practical optimization scheme that decouples matching from SGD.  
- Strength: clear, well-organized presentation and thorough relation to prior work (Wasserstein, MMD).  
- Weakness: missing experiments on multi-modal generative targets; potential for mode collapse or slow mixing when batch size N < number of modes.  
- Weakness: limited experimental baselines—should compare to other Wasserstein-based methods (e.g., WGAN) for sample quality and stability.  
- Weakness: generated images are blurry (attributed to pixel-wise L1 ground metric); suggest trying perceptual (feature-space) distances.  
- Overall: solid, elegant, and useful contribution with empirical promise; experimental evaluation could be broadened but weaknesses are not fatal—recommend acceptance.
