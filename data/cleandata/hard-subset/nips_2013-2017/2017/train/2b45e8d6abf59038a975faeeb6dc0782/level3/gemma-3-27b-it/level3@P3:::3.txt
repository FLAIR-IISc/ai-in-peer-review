This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples, and demonstrates its application to deep learning tasks. PMD is presented as a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and offers advantages over the commonly used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in terms of hyperparameter sensitivity, gradient behavior, and mini-batch size requirements. The work builds upon existing literature in statistical divergence estimation, particularly MMD [Gretton et al., 2012], and extends it by leveraging optimal transport principles [Villani, 2008]. The motivation for developing PMD stems from the limitations of MMD, specifically its dependence on kernel bandwidth selection and potential gradient saturation issues, which hinder its effectiveness in training deep generative models [Li et al., 2017]. The connection to the Wasserstein metric and recent advancements like Wasserstein GANs [Arjovsky et al., 2017] are appropriately acknowledged.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. Technically, the paper is sound; the derivation of PMD and its consistency proof appear correct, and the theoretical results are well-supported. The authors clearly articulate the connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, establishing a strong theoretical foundation. The clarity of the writing is excellent, with a logical structure and concise explanations of complex concepts. The paper is well-organized, making it easy to follow the methodology and experimental setup. The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of related work, providing appropriate citations and contextualizing their contributions within the existing literature. The experimental validation, encompassing domain adaptation and generative modeling, provides convincing evidence of PMD’s efficacy. The comparison to MMD is comprehensive, highlighting the advantages of PMD in both performance and convergence speed. The inclusion of analysis on gradient behavior (Fig. 2(b)) is insightful and supports the theoretical claims.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The reviewer’s request for experiments on generative modeling with many modes is valid. While the authors demonstrate performance on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW, these datasets may not adequately represent the challenges posed by high-dimensional distributions with complex, multi-modal structures. Evaluating PMD’s behavior when the sample size (N) is smaller than the number of modes is a crucial consideration, as this scenario could lead to inaccurate matching and unreliable distance estimation. The discussion on this point is lacking. Furthermore, while the computational complexity analysis is provided, a more detailed breakdown of the runtime performance for different dataset sizes and matching algorithm choices would be beneficial. Although the authors note the potential for GPU acceleration, a quantitative assessment of the speedup achievable with GPU implementation would strengthen the practical impact of the work. Finally, the choice of L1 distance in PMD might be limiting for certain data types (e.g., natural images); exploring other distance metrics could potentially yield further improvements.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** PMD presents a novel approach to distribution distance estimation, offering a potentially valuable alternative to existing methods like MMD.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation of PMD is well-established, with a clear connection to the Wasserstein metric and a consistency proof.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that PMD outperforms MMD on both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making it easy to understand and follow.
*   **Practical Advantages:** PMD offers practical advantages over MMD, including reduced hyperparameter sensitivity, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Evaluation on Complex Distributions:** The lack of experiments on datasets with many modes raises concerns about PMD’s robustness in high-dimensional scenarios.
*   **Insufficient Discussion of Sample Size Limitations:** The paper does not adequately address the potential issues arising when the sample size is smaller than the number of modes.
*   **Lack of GPU Performance Analysis:** A quantitative assessment of GPU acceleration would strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper proposing a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. The theoretical contributions are solid, the experimental results are compelling, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. While the limitations regarding evaluation on complex distributions and the discussion of sample size limitations are valid concerns, they do not outweigh the overall strengths of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the reviewer’s comments, specifically adding experiments on generative modeling with higher modality datasets and further discussing the behavior of PMD when N is small relative to the number of modes.
