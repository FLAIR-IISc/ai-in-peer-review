- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable sample-based distribution distance defined via minimum weight matching cost.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, connecting to optimal transport theory.  
- Claims practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, and effective use of much smaller mini-batches.  
- Provides clear algorithmic presentation with pseudocode and intuitive graphical illustration for parameter learning with PMD.  
- Empirical results show PMD outperforms MMD in domain adaptation (a→w, a→d) and produces less noisy images and faster convergence in generative modeling.  
- Ablation studies indicate PMD’s mini-batch size advantage is real and performance is insensitive to population size N and matching algorithm (exact vs approximate).  
- Theoretical contribution is strong and well-contextualized within related NIPS literature (Wasserstein GAN, MMD).  
- Writing and organization are good, with appropriate related-work comparisons and acknowledgement of limitations (matching computation cost).  
- Hyperparameter claim is weakened because choice of distance metric (e.g., L1 vs L2) effectively introduces its own hyperparameters.  
- Gradient-strength comparison to MMD is less convincing because MMD’s performance depends heavily on kernel bandwidth tuning, which wasn’t consistently handled.  
- The paper lacks exploration of high-dimensional distance estimation issues and whether PMD shares MMD’s curse-of-dimensionality problems.  
- Generative modeling limitations (blurry SVHN/LFW outputs) suggest pixel-level L1 is suboptimal and high-dimensional scaling isn’t addressed.  
- Overall significance is high: addresses practical MMD pain points with a novel, well-supported method that benefits domain adaptation and generative modeling.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify hyperparameter discussion and investigate/address high-dimensional limitations.
