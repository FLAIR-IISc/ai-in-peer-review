- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based distance estimator defined via minimum weight matching between populations.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3).  
- Claims PMD advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and utility with smaller mini-batches.  
- Establishes theoretical connection between PMD and optimal transport/Wasserstein distance for empirical distributions.  
- Compares PMD and MMD theoretically, highlighting differences in gradient properties and computational needs.  
- Empirically shows PMD works better with smaller mini-batches and yields faster convergence in experiments (Figs. 5a, 3a).  
- Provides a clear PMD training algorithm and demonstrates effectiveness on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.  
- Acknowledges computational challenges and offers practical solutions (exact Hungarian for small N, approximate methods for larger N).  
- Weakness: claim of “stronger gradients” is not well substantiated because MMD’s gradient depends on kernel bandwidth σ and σ was not properly tuned.  
- Weakness: “fewer hyperparameters” claim is misleading since PMD requires choosing a distance metric (e.g., L1/L2), which affects performance.  
- Weakness: paper inadequately addresses high-dimensional estimation issues; reliance on L1/L2 may hurt performance for high-dimensional data (e.g., blurry generator outputs).  
- Weakness: comparison to MMD could be more rigorous by using standard bandwidth heuristics (e.g., median heuristic) and broader σ tuning.  
- Overall: PMD is a valid, theoretically sound improvement for small-batch training with good empirical support, but some claims need stronger justification.  
- Recommendation: Accept, with request that authors address concerns about gradient strength and hyperparameter claims in the camera-ready version.
