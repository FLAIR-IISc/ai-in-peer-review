## Review of “Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning”

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel estimator for the distance between two distributions, defined as the Wasserstein distance between minibatches. The authors motivate this work by highlighting limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), specifically its sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and need for large minibatches. They demonstrate the theoretical consistency of PMD as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and apply it to domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing improvements over MMD in both performance and convergence speed.

The core idea of PMD – leveraging optimal transport to define a distance between distributions based on sample matching – is interesting and builds upon established work in statistical divergence estimation (e.g., MMD, Wasserstein GAN). The theoretical justification, establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, is a valuable contribution. The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly superior, do demonstrate the potential of PMD, particularly in scenarios where MMD struggles. 

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The generated images from PMD are reported to be less visually appealing than those from Wasserstein GAN, a significant drawback for generative modeling. The authors acknowledge the need for large minibatches for reliable estimation with multimodal distributions, but don’t fully explore this limitation. The likely failure case of matching mixtures of Gaussians (especially with learned variances) is a crucial point that needs further investigation. The SVHN results are also concerning, with the observed bias towards the digit '8' potentially skewing the evaluation. Furthermore, the lack of an unbiased estimator for the gradient of PMD, contrasted with MMD’s unbiased estimator (as highlighted in the related work “A Kernel Two-Sample Test”), is a significant disadvantage. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to distribution distance estimation based on optimal transport.
*   Theoretical consistency guarantee for PMD as a Wasserstein metric estimator.
*   Demonstrated improvements over MMD in specific experimental settings.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited performance in generative modeling compared to established GANs.
*   Potential issues with scalability and sensitivity to distribution complexity.
*   Lack of an unbiased gradient estimator.
*   Concerns regarding the diversity of generated samples (SVHN).



I recommend the authors address the limitations outlined above, particularly the scalability concerns, the lack of an unbiased gradient estimator, and the potential for bias in the generated samples. A more thorough discussion of the limitations of PMD relative to MMD, and other relevant methods, would also strengthen the paper. Clarifying the conditions under which PMD is expected to perform well, and where it might struggle, is crucial. Overall, while the paper presents an interesting idea, it requires further refinement and analysis before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field.
