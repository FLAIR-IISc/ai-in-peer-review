- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based distance using minimum weight matching, and proves its strong consistency as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Applies PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, reporting improved performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.  
- Positions work within existing MMD and Wasserstein literature, noting MMD’s sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and large mini-batch requirements.  
- Theoretical strength: consistency proof gives a solid grounding linking PMD to Wasserstein theory.  
- Empirical strength: convincing experiments showing PMD matches or outperforms MMD, especially with much smaller mini-batches.  
- Presentation strength: generally clear exposition with logical flow and helpful pseudocode for implementation.  
- Hyperparameter concern: claim of “fewer hyperparameters” is overstated because PMD requires choosing a distance metric (and scaling) which affects performance.  
- Gradient analysis concern: comparison of gradient strength is superficial; results should be compared against MMD with optimized kernel bandwidths.  
- Scalability concern: computational cost of minimum weight matching and limitations of distance metrics in high dimensions raise questions about PMD’s scalability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, theoretical soundness, strong empirical validation, and practical significance for faster training.  
- Arguments against acceptance: misleading hyperparameter claim, insufficiently rigorous gradient comparisons, and inadequate treatment of high-dimensional scalability.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify hyperparameter choices, provide more thorough gradient comparisons with tuned MMD, and discuss limitations in high-dimensional settings.
