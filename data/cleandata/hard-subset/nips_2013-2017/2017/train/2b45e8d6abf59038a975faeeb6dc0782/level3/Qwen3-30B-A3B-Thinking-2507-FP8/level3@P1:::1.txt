

The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel approach to estimating the Wasserstein distance between two distributions using samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of two sample populations from the distributions, and the authors prove it's a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The work builds on previous NIPS contributions like WGAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017) and MMD (Gretton et al., 2012), but offers a different computational approach to estimating Wasserstein distance.

The main strength of this paper is its clear theoretical foundation. The authors rigorously establish that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), which provides a solid theoretical basis for using PMD as a training objective. The paper also makes a compelling case for PMD's practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients (Fig. 2b), and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (|B|=100 vs. N=1000 for MMD). The experimental results on domain adaptation (Table 1) and generative modeling (Fig. 4) demonstrate PMD's superiority over MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed, which is well-supported by the empirical evidence.

However, several important limitations need to be addressed. First, the paper claims PMD can use smaller mini-batch sizes, but the MMD literature (Gretton et al., 2012) shows that an unbiased estimator of MMD can be computed with batch_size=2 using the "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" approach, which contradicts the authors' claim about mini-batch size advantages. The paper should clarify this discrepancy and provide a fair comparison.

Second, the generative modeling results (Fig. 4) show PMD-generated images are less visually appealing than those from WGAN, which is a significant drawback given that WGAN has been the state-of-the-art for generative modeling. The paper doesn't adequately explain why PMD performs worse than WGAN despite being a Wasserstein distance estimator, which undermines the significance claim.

Third, the paper doesn't sufficiently address the critical failure case of matching mixtures of Gaussians. As noted in the key points, when learning a mixture of Gaussians (including variances), PMD would likely fail to capture the true distribution due to the discrete nature of the matching, especially with small N. The authors should provide experiments on this benchmark to validate their method's limitations.

The paper also doesn't adequately address the requirement for large N to estimate Wasserstein for diverse distributions. In Section 5.3, they show PMD can use N=500 with acceptable results, but the theoretical guarantee requires N→∞. For multimodal distributions like SVHN (which exhibits low diversity with disproportionate '8' digits), PMD would likely require much larger N to properly capture the distribution, contradicting the claimed advantage of smaller mini-batch sizes.

The theoretical analysis is strong, but the empirical analysis has notable gaps. The SVHN dataset analysis shows low diversity, which undermines the claim about PMD's ability to handle diverse distributions. The authors should analyze the diversity of their datasets more carefully and discuss how this impacts PMD's performance.

The paper contains a minor typo on line 141 ("usally" instead of "usually") and could benefit from a more thorough comparison with the recently published Cramer GAN (Bellemare et al., 2017), which also addresses Wasserstein distance estimation.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of Wasserstein distance.  
Pro: Clear demonstration of advantages over MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling.  
Pro: Practical algorithm with implementation details for parameter learning.  
Con: Underestimates MMD's ability to work with small minibatches (batch_size=2).  
Con: Generative results inferior to WGAN, a standard baseline.  
Con: Fails to address critical failure case of matching mixtures of Gaussians.  
Con: Overstates the advantage of smaller mini-batch size given the need for large N for diverse distributions.  
Con: SVHN dataset shows low diversity, undermining claims about handling complex distributions.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a solid theoretical contribution that addresses an important problem in deep learning. However, the experimental results don't fully support the claims about PMD's advantages, particularly regarding generative modeling performance. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations noted above, especially the comparison with MMD's small minibatch capabilities and the failure case for mixture distributions. Given these gaps, I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that with careful revision addressing these limitations, this could be a strong contribution to NIPS.
