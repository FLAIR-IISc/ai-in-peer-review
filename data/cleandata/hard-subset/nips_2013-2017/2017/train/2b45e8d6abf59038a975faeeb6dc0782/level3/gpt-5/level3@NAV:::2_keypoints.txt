- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as minimum-weight matching between two i.i.d. samples as a sample-based surrogate for the 1-Wasserstein distance.  
- Proves strong consistency of PMD and uses it as a differentiable training objective for domain adaptation and implicit generative modeling, contrasted with MMD.  
- Situates the work within existing literature on kernel two-sample tests/MMD, OT/Wasserstein (e.g., WGAN), and distribution-alignment domain adaptation.  
- Theoretical result (PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of W1) is sound and well-grounded in optimal transport theory.  
- Ignoring gradients through discrete matching is standard and plausibly justified by piecewise-constant behavior.  
- Empirically demonstrates a clear advantage for small mini-batches and faster convergence in practice.  
- The claim of “stronger gradients” is under-supported; MMD gradient strength depends on kernel bandwidth and requires fair tuning/sweeps.  
- “Fewer hyperparameters” claim is overstated because PMD requires choice of ground metric and feature space, introducing scaling/hyperparameter issues.  
- High-dimensional problems remain challenging (distance concentration, n−1/d OT sample complexity), so PMD may face similar limitations to MMD.  
- Presentation is generally clear; recommend unifying “population” vs “mini-batch” terminology earlier; complexity breakdowns are helpful.  
- Framing minimum bipartite matching as a practical differentiable W1 surrogate is a useful perspective; the consistency proof is an appropriate adaptation rather than wholly novel.  
- PMD shows practical significance for fast training with small batches but may be limited in high-dimensional pixel spaces.  
- Missing important baselines and comparisons (WGAN/WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn-regularized OT, sliced Wasserstein, MMD-GAN) and lack of MMD tuning (median heuristic, multi-kernel).  
- Suggestions: compare to MMD with median heuristic/multi-kernel or best-over-sigma, add WGAN(-GP)/Sinkhorn/sliced OT baselines, perform ablations over ground metrics/features, and include a high-dimensional synthetic study and practical guidance on metric choice.
