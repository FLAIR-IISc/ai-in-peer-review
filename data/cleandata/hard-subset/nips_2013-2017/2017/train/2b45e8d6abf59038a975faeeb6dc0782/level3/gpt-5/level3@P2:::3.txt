Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based discrepancy between two distributions defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample populations. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1st Wasserstein (W1) distance and develop a practical learning algorithm that treats the matching as fixed across several SGD steps, thereby not backpropagating through the discrete matching. They position PMD as a drop-in alternative to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for training deep models. Claimed advantages over MMD include the absence of a kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger (non-vanishing) gradients when samples are far apart, and the ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches than the population size used to compute the objective.

The paper connects PMD to the primal optimal transport (OT) formulation and contrasts it with MMD’s RKHS embedding and with approaches that approximate the dual W1 (e.g., WGAN). PMD is further related to exact Hungarian matching and a quadratic-time approximate matching algorithm. Empirically, PMD is applied to unsupervised domain adaptation on Office-31 (with a feature extractor and classifier) and to implicit generative modeling on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW, where it is compared primarily to MMD. Results indicate better or comparable accuracy in domain adaptation and improved qualitative samples and convergence speed in generative modeling.

Quality
- Theory: The identification of empirical PMD with the discrete OT problem is standard, and the strong consistency result follows from Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of OT; the argument appears correct. It would be helpful to explicitly state regularity assumptions on d and the sample space and to clarify that gradients are subgradients almost everywhere due to matching changes on measure-zero sets.
- Optimization: The gradient estimator fixes the matching within inner-loop updates. This heuristic is reasonable but potentially biased if matchings change frequently with θ; some empirical sensitivity analysis is provided and is reassuring. A short discussion of when this surrogate gradient approximates the true subgradient (e.g., Lipschitz continuity of d∘Tθ and stability of matchings) would strengthen the paper.
- Experiments: The domain adaptation experiments (Office-31) are solid but compare only to MMD-based regularization; compared to the contemporary literature, omitting domain-adversarial methods (e.g., DANN) and multi-kernel JMMD/JAN undercuts the significance claims. For generative modeling, results are qualitative; quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception/FID for SVHN/LFW or Parzen/2D likelihoods for MNIST) and comparisons to WGAN, MMD-GAN, or Cramér distance would provide a stronger case. The analysis of gradient magnitudes and ablations on N, |B|, and matching algorithm are useful. Timing breakdowns are reported; end-to-end wall-clock comparisons against MMD and (ideally) Sinkhorn-OT would be informative.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The objective, algorithm, and computational considerations are presented cleanly, with pseudocode and helpful figures. Related work is generally well covered; however, entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) should be discussed as they offer differentiable, scalable W1 approximations widely used in ML by 2017, and are a highly relevant baseline.

Originality
Using empirical OT (matching) directly as a differentiable training objective for deep implicit models and domain adaptation, with a practical approximate matching and an inner-loop reuse heuristic, is a nice, simple idea. The theoretical consistency itself is not new in OT, but its packaging as PMD and the emphasis on gradient behavior and mini-batch efficiency relative to MMD are useful contributions. The paper would benefit from positioning relative to Sinkhorn-based OT training, which targets the same goal with different trade-offs.

Significance
If PMD trains as robustly as shown, the contribution is practically valuable: it offers a principled, parameter-free alternative to MMD with stronger gradients and small-batch optimization, and it can serve as a baseline to dual-based OT (WGAN). The current empirical scope is moderate; broader, competitive baselines and quantitative metrics would increase the impact. The computational cost is nontrivial (O(N2)–O(N3) matching), but the approximate algorithm and potential GPU acceleration mitigate this.

Strengths
- Simple, well-motivated objective directly tied to W1; clear theory (strong consistency).
- Practical learning algorithm with an insightful gradient discussion; stronger gradients than MMD.
- Ablations: exact vs approximate matching; sensitivity to N and mini-batch size; timing breakdowns.
- Positive results on domain adaptation and generative modeling; improved convergence speed.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Missing comparison to Sinkhorn (entropic-regularized OT) and to strong baselines in both tasks (DANN/JAN for DA; WGAN/MMD-GAN/Cramér distance for generation).
- Generative modeling lacks quantitative metrics; datasets are small-scale; behavior on many-mode data is not probed.
- Limited theoretical development beyond consistency (e.g., finite-sample rates, stability of matchings under parameter updates).
- Clarify gradient estimator: conditions under which fixing M∗ yields an unbiased/consistent subgradient; discuss non-differentiable points and how they are handled in practice.

Questions for the authors
- How does PMD perform on synthetic multi-modal distributions when N is smaller than the number of modes? Does it match modes one-to-one or induce mode dropping?
- Can you report FID/Inception on SVHN/LFW and add WGAN/Sinkhorn baselines?
- How sensitive is training to the number of inner-loop steps with a stale matching?
- Any results with feature-space distances (e.g., learned features) to alleviate pixel L1 blur?

Pros/cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear idea, theoretically grounded; practical algorithm with tangible benefits over MMD.
- Empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence.
- Generally well written and easy to reproduce.

Cons:
- Missing key baselines (Sinkhorn/DA adversarial and modern generative baselines).
- Limited quantitative evaluation for generative modeling.
- Computational scaling may be challenging without GPU matching.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper proposes a useful, principled alternative to MMD with convincing analysis and promising results. Addressing the missing baselines and adding quantitative evaluations would solidify the contribution.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
