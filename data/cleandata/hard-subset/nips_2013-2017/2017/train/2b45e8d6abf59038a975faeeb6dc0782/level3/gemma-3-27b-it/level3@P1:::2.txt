This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel approach for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples, motivated by the limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The core idea is to formulate the distribution distance as the minimum weight matching of sample populations, demonstrating its consistency as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors propose an algorithm for learning distribution parameters using PMD as an objective and demonstrate its advantages – namely, stronger gradients, the ability to use normal mini-batch sizes, and a reduced need for hyperparameter tuning – through applications to domain adaptation and generative modeling.

The work builds upon a rich history of sample-based statistical divergence estimation, with MMD [Gretton et al., 2012] being a prominent example widely adopted in belief propagation, domain adaptation, and generative modeling due to its simplicity and theoretical properties. However, the paper rightly identifies the drawbacks of MMD – sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and large mini-batch requirements – which motivate the development of PMD.  The connection to the Wasserstein metric [Villani, 2008] is a significant contribution, providing a theoretical grounding for PMD and linking it to optimal transport theory. Recent work using the Wasserstein metric, such as Wasserstein GANs [Arjovsky et al., 2017], highlights the growing interest in this metric as a robust measure of distribution distance.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The theoretical justification linking PMD to the Wasserstein metric is well-presented, and the claim of stronger gradients is supported by empirical evidence. The demonstrated ability to effectively train with smaller mini-batches is a valuable practical advantage, potentially leading to significant speedups in training, particularly for large datasets. The experimental results on both domain adaptation and generative modeling provide compelling evidence that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of both performance and convergence speed. The clear presentation of the algorithm, including pseudocode and a discussion of computational complexity, is also commendable.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses.  While the claim of fewer hyperparameters is made, PMD still relies on a distance metric (L1 distance is used in the experiments) which itself can have parameters or be sensitive to the specific data. This nuance should be acknowledged. Furthermore, the paper does not explore the impact of different distance metrics on PMD's performance.  The discussion of stronger gradients lacks sufficient nuance. MMD's gradient behavior is highly dependent on the kernel bandwidth (σ). A fair comparison should involve evaluating MMD with optimized or a range of bandwidths, effectively treating MMD as a hyperparameter-tunable method rather than a fixed one. It's possible that a well-tuned MMD could achieve comparable gradient behavior.  

Another potential limitation is the applicability of PMD in high-dimensional spaces. Estimating distances becomes increasingly challenging in high dimensions (the “curse of dimensionality”), and PMD, like MMD, may suffer from similar issues. While the experiments demonstrate promising results, it's unclear how PMD will perform as dimensionality increases. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   PMD provides a theoretically sound and practically effective alternative to MMD for distribution matching.
*   The ability to train with smaller mini-batches offers a significant speedup in training time.
*   Empirical results convincingly demonstrate PMD’s superior performance on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
*   The connection to the Wasserstein metric adds a strong theoretical foundation to the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The claim of fewer hyperparameters is somewhat overstated.
*   The comparison to MMD could be more thorough by considering optimized kernel bandwidths for MMD.
*   The paper doesn't explicitly address the potential limitations of PMD in high-dimensional spaces.



**Overall, I recommend acceptance.**  PMD represents a valuable contribution to the field, offering a practical improvement over MMD for certain applications. While the paper has some minor weaknesses, the strengths outweigh them, and the results are compelling. Future work should explore the performance of PMD in higher dimensions and investigate the impact of different distance metrics. The authors should also provide a more nuanced comparison to MMD, considering optimized kernel bandwidths.
