## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel approach to distribution matching, aiming to address limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) – specifically, sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, weak gradients, and large mini-batch requirements. The core idea, elegantly presented, leverages the connection between PMD and the Wasserstein (Earth Mover's) distance, proving PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors demonstrate PMD’s efficacy in domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing improved performance and faster convergence, particularly with smaller mini-batches.

The work builds upon existing literature in distribution matching (e.g., MMD [Gretton et al., 2012]) and Wasserstein distance estimation (e.g., WGAN [Arjovsky et al., 2017]). It differentiates itself by providing a differentiable estimator *without* requiring kernel selection and demonstrating advantages in gradient behavior and mini-batch size. The theoretical grounding establishing the link to the Wasserstein distance is a significant contribution. 

A key strength is the convincing empirical validation of the small mini-batch advantage. The experiments are well-structured, employing standard benchmarks (Office dataset for domain adaptation and MNIST/SVHN/LFW for generative modeling). However, the claim of “stronger gradients” feels less substantiated. The authors acknowledge MMD’s performance dependence on kernel bandwidth (sigma), yet comparisons are not performed across a range of sigma values. A fairer assessment would involve tuning MMD to its best performance, or at least evaluating it with several common bandwidth heuristics to demonstrate a consistent advantage for PMD. The argument for “fewer hyperparameters” is also weak – PMD’s reliance on a distance metric (L1 in their experiments) inherently introduces its own potential hyperparameter considerations.

Furthermore, while the paper addresses the limitations of MMD in some scenarios, it acknowledges the potential for PMD to face similar challenges in high-dimensional spaces due to distance estimation issues.  It would be valuable to discuss potential mitigation strategies or future research directions to address this limitation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to distribution matching.
*   Demonstrated improvement in training speed, particularly with smaller mini-batches.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The claim of stronger gradients needs further validation through more thorough comparisons with optimized MMD.
*   The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is debatable.
*   Potential limitations in high-dimensional spaces require more discussion.

**Overall:** This is a valid improvement over MMD for fast training with smaller batches, and the theoretical justification is strong. However, the claims regarding gradient strength require more rigorous evaluation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.
