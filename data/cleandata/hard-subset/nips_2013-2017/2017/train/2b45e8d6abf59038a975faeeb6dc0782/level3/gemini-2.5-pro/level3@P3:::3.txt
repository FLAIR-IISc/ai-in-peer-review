This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel, population-based divergence for comparing probability distributions from their samples. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance. A key contribution is that this estimator is conceptually simple and differentiable with respect to the sample locations, which enables its use as a training objective for deep neural network models via stochastic gradient descent. The paper draws a thorough comparison to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a prevalent divergence in machine learning, arguing that PMD has advantages such as having no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, providing stronger gradients, and allowing for more flexible mini-batching during optimization. The authors acknowledge that the exact computation of PMD is cubic in the population size (N) and propose a quadratic approximation that empirically performs well. The method is evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, where it is shown to outperform MMD-based approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper presents a novel and principled approach to estimating the Wasserstein distance between continuous distributions. Given the growing importance of optimal transport metrics in machine learning (e.g., WGANs), providing a direct, differentiable, and parameter-free estimator is a significant and timely contribution. PMD is an elegant and intuitive idea that connects a classical computer science problem (bipartite matching) to a fundamental task in modern machine learning.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The theoretical connection to the first Wasserstein metric is well-established, and the claim that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator appears correct. The practical arguments for PMD's advantages over MMD—particularly the absence of the sensitive kernel bandwidth parameter and the potential for stronger, non-saturating gradients—are convincing and well-supported by empirical analysis (Figure 2b). The proposed optimization algorithm, which decouples the matching computation from the SGD updates, is practical and clever.
*   **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The core concepts are introduced intuitively before the formal definitions and theoretical results are presented. The relationship to prior work, especially the Wasserstein metric and MMD, is discussed in detail, which effectively situates the contribution within the field. The writing is accessible to readers familiar with deep learning and statistical divergences.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Generative Modeling for Multi-modal Distributions:** The experimental analysis, while adequate, could be more comprehensive. A critical scenario not explored is generative modeling for targets with many well-separated modes. The performance of a divergence in this setting is a strong indicator of its utility. Could the authors comment on or provide experiments showing how PMD performs when the population size N is smaller than the number of modes in the target distribution? Since PMD performs a one-to-one matching, it seems plausible that for any given batch, the model would only receive gradients to cover a subset of N modes, potentially leading to mode collapse or slow mixing over time. An experiment on a synthetic dataset, like a mixture of 2D Gaussians arranged in a grid or ring, would be highly illuminating.
*   **Experimental Comparison:** In the generative modeling experiments, the comparison is limited to an MMD-based method (GMMN). While this is a direct and relevant baseline, a comparison to other contemporary methods that also leverage the Wasserstein distance, such as WGAN, would provide a more complete picture of PMD's performance and trade-offs. Although PMD has the advantage of not requiring a separate critic network, it would be valuable to understand how its sample quality and training stability compare.
*   **Blurriness of Generated Images:** The authors note that the images generated by PMD are blurry and attribute this to the pixel-level L1 distance. While this is a reasonable hypothesis, it is also a potential limitation of the method as presented. The Wasserstein-1 distance with an L1 or L2 ground metric is known to favor blurry reconstructions over sharp but slightly misplaced ones. Have the authors considered using a perceptual distance as the ground metric `d(·, ·)` (e.g., distance in a pre-trained feature space) instead of pixel-wise L1, and would this improve image quality?

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper that introduces a simple, elegant, and potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. The work is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and clearly presented. The proposed PMD has compelling advantages over MMD, a widely-used baseline. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened, particularly concerning the behavior on multi-modal distributions and comparison to other Wasserstein-based generative models, the paper stands as a solid contribution. The weaknesses are not fatal flaws but rather opportunities for future work. The proposed method is likely to be of interest to researchers in generative modeling, domain adaptation, and anyone in need of a robust, differentiable statistical divergence. I recommend acceptance.
