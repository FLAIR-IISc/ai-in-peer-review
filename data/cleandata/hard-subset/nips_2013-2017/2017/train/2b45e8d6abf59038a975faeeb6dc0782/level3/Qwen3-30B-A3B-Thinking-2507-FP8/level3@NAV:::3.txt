

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel population-based divergence that serves as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations from respective distributions, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The work makes a compelling case for PMD's advantages over the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD): it eliminates the need for kernel bandwidth tuning, provides stronger gradients that don't saturate, and enables smaller mini-batch sizes during training (100 vs. MMD's typical 1000).

The paper excels in clarity and structure, with well-motivated theoretical analysis connecting PMD to optimal transport theory and a thorough comparison with MMD. The experimental evaluation is adequate, demonstrating PMD's superiority over MMD in both domain adaptation (on Office dataset) and generative modeling (on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW), particularly in convergence speed and performance. The authors appropriately address computational concerns by proposing a quadratic-time approximation to the cubic-time Hungarian algorithm, showing empirical performance is maintained.

The main weaknesses are that the generative modeling experiments don't include challenging multimodal distributions where PMD's behavior might be tested, and the paper doesn't address PMD's performance when the sample size N is smaller than the number of modes in the distribution. These are important limitations for a method claiming broad applicability.

In terms of quality, the theoretical results are sound and well-supported by experiments. The paper is clearly written with appropriate citations to prior work. The originality is strong, presenting a novel approach to distribution comparison that differs fundamentally from existing methods. The significance is high, as PMD addresses real pain points in using MMD for deep learning applications.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to distribution comparison methods. The proposed PMD is conceptually simple, theoretically grounded, and empirically effective. While the requested additional experiments would strengthen the claims, the paper is well-executed and makes a compelling case for PMD's utility in deep learning applications. I recommend acceptance.
