This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost (with a base metric such as L1) between two i.i.d. sample sets drawn from distributions pX and pY. PMD is thereby equivalent to the empirical first Wasserstein distance (W1) between equally weighted discrete measures supported on the two minibatches. The authors prove strong consistency of PMD for W1 via standard results on weak convergence and stability of optimal transport (Villani), and propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective without backpropagating through the matching. They provide both exact O(N^3) matching (Hungarian) and an O(N^2) approximate algorithm (Drake–Hougardy), and apply PMD to two tasks: (i) domain adaptation via matching source/target feature distributions, and (ii) training implicit generative models. Empirically, PMD is reported to have “stronger” gradients than MMD, fewer hyperparameters, and to converge faster, especially with small SGD minibatches. The work is related to prior NIPS work on MMD-based training (GMMN), WGAN (dual W1 with a critic), EMD in vision, and domain adaptation regularizers (DANN, JAN, CMD).

Strengths
- Clear statement that PMD equals the empirical W1 with one-to-one matching, and a clean consistency proof based on well-known OT results.
- Practical algorithms and an explicit recipe for using PMD as a loss, including reusing the matching across inner-loop updates.
- Interesting empirical observation that using small SGD minibatches while keeping a larger “population” N for matching can speed convergence.
- Applications to two relevant areas with reasonable baselines; some improvements over MMD in domain adaptation and sample quality in certain settings.
- Discussion of gradient magnitudes vs. MMD is useful, and the empirical gradient-magnitude histogram supports the intuition.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty/significance: Using empirical W1 via minimum-cost matching as a training loss is not conceptually new; the main contribution is to package and empirically validate it in deep learning workflows. Stronger positioning vs. prior scalable OT (e.g., entropically regularized Sinkhorn distances; Cuturi 2013, widely used since NIPS 2013) is needed. Sinkhorn yields differentiable, GPU-friendly O(N^2) computations and is a natural comparator here.
- Gradient estimator: Eq. (3) argues one can ignore the dependence of the discrete matching M* on parameters and obtain an unbiased gradient for a fixed batch under regularity (almost everywhere). However, the proposed inner-loop estimator (Eq. (4)) that reuses a stale matching and subsamples a minibatch is biased. Please clarify the bias/variance tradeoff and whether convergence guarantees hold. In contrast, for MMD there exists an unbiased U-statistic estimator (Gretton et al., “A Kernel Two-Sample Test”), which can be used with very small minibatches (even 2) and stochastic optimization; your claim that MMD requires large batches should be qualified or empirically revisited with the unbiased estimator.
- Sample complexity: Empirical error scales roughly as N^-0.23 in 5D, consistent with known slow rates for W1. This suggests that, for high-dimensional, multimodal data, PMD may require large population size N to produce stable gradients. In the generative modeling experiments (SVHN/LFW), the outputs are blurrier than WGAN-like baselines and exhibit low diversity (notably overproduction of digit ‘8’ on SVHN). Quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score/FID for SVHN) and class-frequency analysis would help assess mode coverage and diversity.
- Potential failure modes: Matching between minibatches can encourage low-entropy solutions when N is small (e.g., variance shrinkage in Gaussian mixtures), since the empirical W1 can be minimized by reducing spread to match batch realizations. A simple diagnostic on mixtures of Gaussians (including learning variances) would be illuminating.
- Computational aspects: The matching step can still be a bottleneck for larger N. GPU benchmarks and a comparison to Sinkhorn OT would strengthen the scalability claims.
- Comparisons: The paper compares primarily to MMD/GMMN but not to WGAN/Cramér distance or Sinkhorn GAN variants, which are natural baselines for W1-style training. Including at least WGAN with a 1-Lipschitz critic and Sinkhorn losses would contextualize PMD’s strengths and weaknesses.
- Clarity: While the paper is generally well written, some claims (e.g., “unbiased gradient” and “MMD requires large mini-batch”) should be qualified. Minor typo: line 141 “usally” → “usually.”

Quality
- Technical soundness is reasonable for the core consistency result. The estimator used in practice (stale matching, subsampling) introduces bias; more theoretical or empirical analysis of its impact would improve the paper.
- Experimental evaluation is suggestive but incomplete: missing key baselines (WGAN/Sinkhorn), quantitative metrics for generative quality/diversity, and diagnostics on challenging multimodal cases.

Clarity
- Definitions and algorithms are clearly presented. Please add a discussion of estimator bias, sample complexity in high dimensions, and a fair treatment of MMD with unbiased estimators.

Originality
- Moderate. The idea reduces to empirical W1 optimization via matching; the contribution is in adaptation to deep learning training loops and empirical observations on gradient strength and minibatch usage.

Significance
- Potentially useful as a simple, parameter-free alternative to MMD in some settings, but impact is limited without stronger baselines and scalability analysis. The domain adaptation gains are mixed; the generative results lag WGAN-like methods qualitatively.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare to entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi 2013) and WGAN/Cramér distance.
- Reevaluate MMD with unbiased estimators and small batches; report convergence and accuracy.
- Add quantitative metrics (FID/IS) and class-distribution analyses; address SVHN diversity issues.
- Include controlled experiments on mixtures of Gaussians (means and variances) to probe failure modes.
- Analyze the bias introduced by stale matching and subsampling; ablate number of inner-loop steps.
- Provide GPU implementations/benchmarks for matching or use Sinkhorn for scalability.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, broadly applicable loss; fewer hyperparameters than MMD.
- Clear connection to W1 with a consistency result.
- Empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence in some settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty vs. existing OT methods; missing key baselines (Sinkhorn, WGAN).
- Potential bias and sample-complexity issues not fully addressed.
- Generative results lack diversity and quantitative evaluation.

Recommendation: Weak reject (borderline). The idea is promising and practically appealing, but the paper needs stronger baselines, clearer treatment of estimator bias and sample complexity, and more thorough evaluation to meet NIPS standards.

Confidence: High.
