Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the minimum-weight bipartite matching (empirical W1) between minibatches and uses it as a differentiable training objective.

Proves strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric; result is standard from empirical OT but correctly invoked.

Implements matching via Hungarian (O(N^3)) or approximate O(N^2); experiments on minibatch Wasserstein are interesting.

Major clarification needed: the claimed “unbiased stochastic gradient” is not established—matching depends on θ, piecewise-constant matching induces non-smoothness; clarify if the estimator is unbiased, a valid subgradient a.e., or heuristic.

The statement that MMD “requires large mini-batches” is misleading—unbiased U-statistic estimators permit very small minibatches (even size 2) and converge under SGD.

Empirical generative results: PMD samples on SVHN show low diversity and are weaker than WGAN; dataset imbalance (e.g., digit ‘8’ dominance) may influence results.

Recommend a mixture-of-Gaussians experiment (including learning variances) to expose mode-seeking/low-entropy solutions when N is small and show differences from the true distribution.

Clarity: paper is generally clear and organized; fix minor typo (“usally”→“usually”) and more explicitly discuss limitations of PMD and MMD, including estimator variance/bias and minibatch-size trade-offs.

Originality: using empirical OT matching as a training loss is a natural but useful instantiation; should discuss related scalable OT methods (Sinkhorn/entropic regularization) and biased-Wasserstein-gradient work (e.g., Bellemare’s Cramér distance).

Significance: promising gains on some domain adaptation tasks but generative modeling lags WGAN; broader comparisons and stress tests are needed.

Pros: clear connection to W1 with consistency proof; interesting empirical study of minibatch Wasserstein; competitive domain adaptation results and a simple objective without a critic.

Cons: unclear gradient unbiasedness and non-smooth optimization; computational cost scales poorly with N and stronger baselines (WGAN, Sinkhorn) are missing; generative samples show mode collapse/low diversity; MMD limitations are overstated and need fair small-batch comparisons.
