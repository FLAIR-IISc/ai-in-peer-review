

# Review for NIPS 2017

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable estimator of the distance between two distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes an algorithm to learn parameters of distributions using PMD as an objective, and demonstrates its advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), the dominant sample-based divergence in deep learning.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions such as MMD (Gretton et al., 2012), Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017), and domain adaptation methods (Ganin et al., 2016). While the Wasserstein metric has been used in machine learning, it's typically applied to discrete distributions or approximated through dual formulations (e.g., WGAN). PMD provides a direct, sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein metric that is differentiable and suitable for gradient-based training, addressing key limitations of MMD.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is strong and well-executed. The authors prove PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, and provide a clear comparison with MMD. The experimental design is sound, with thorough comparisons on two important deep learning tasks (domain adaptation and generative modeling). The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the cubic time complexity of exact matching, and propose a quadratic approximation that maintains statistical performance. The empirical results demonstrate PMD's advantages over MMD in terms of both performance and convergence speed, with smaller mini-batch requirements. The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by both theory and experiments.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The concepts are clearly explained with appropriate mathematical notation, and the paper is structured logically from problem definition through theoretical analysis to applications. The pseudocode and figures (Fig. 1, 2, 3, 5) effectively illustrate key ideas. The authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce their results, including implementation specifics and experimental settings.

**Originality:** PMD represents a novel approach to estimating distribution distances. The key innovation is using minimum weight matching as a divergence measure, which differs fundamentally from MMD (which uses kernel mean embeddings) and other approaches. The authors clearly articulate how PMD differs from prior work, and the paper appears significantly different from previous NIPS publications. The absence of a bandwidth hyper-parameter and the stronger gradients are particularly valuable practical contributions that distinguish PMD from MMD.

**Significance:** PMD addresses practical limitations of MMD that hinder its effectiveness in deep learning applications. By eliminating the need for bandwidth tuning, providing stronger gradients, and allowing smaller mini-batch sizes, PMD enables more efficient training of deep models for distribution comparison tasks. The empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate tangible improvements over MMD, suggesting PMD could become a useful tool for practitioners. The work advances the state of the art in distribution distance estimation, with potential applications in many areas of deep learning.

**Strengths:**
- Novel approach to distribution distance estimation with clear theoretical foundation
- Practical advantages over MMD (no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size)
- Thorough empirical validation on two important deep learning tasks
- Well-structured, clearly written paper with strong technical content
- Comprehensive comparison with MMD and appropriate baseline methods

**Weaknesses:**
- The cubic time complexity of exact matching may limit applicability to very large datasets, though the authors propose a quadratic approximation that works well in practice
- The paper does not fully address how PMD behaves when the sample size N is smaller than the number of modes in the distribution (a concern raised in the key points)
- The generative modeling results on SVHN and LFW show blurry images, suggesting potential limitations for natural image generation (though the authors acknowledge this relates to the L1 distance choice rather than PMD itself)

**Additional Reviewer Requests:**
The authors should consider conducting experiments on generative modeling tasks with many modes (e.g., CIFAR-10) to better assess PMD's behavior in complex multimodal settings. It would also be valuable to analyze PMD's performance when the sample size N is smaller than the number of modes, as this is a common scenario in practical applications.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distribution distance estimation for deep learning. The theoretical foundation is strong, the practical advantages over MMD are compelling, and the empirical results are convincing. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and addresses important limitations of current approaches. This paper should be accepted to NIPS 2017.
