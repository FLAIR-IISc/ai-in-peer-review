This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel distance metric between probability distributions, grounded in the Wasserstein distance, and explores its application to domain adaptation and generative modeling. The core idea – defining the distance as the minimum weight matching of sample populations – offers a potentially advantageous alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), particularly concerning gradient behavior and mini-batch size requirements. The work builds upon a strong foundation of prior research in statistical divergence estimation, notably MMD [17] and Wasserstein GANs [3], and correctly positions PMD within that landscape.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the theoretical basis of PMD, demonstrating its consistency as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The proposed algorithm for parameter learning using PMD as an objective is logical and well-motivated. The experimental results on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) demonstrate competitive performance compared to MMD. The analysis of gradient characteristics (Fig. 2b) offers valuable insight into the potential benefits of PMD. The inclusion of implementation details regarding matching algorithms and computational costs is also appreciated.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge limitations, they aren't sufficiently explored. The claim of "stronger gradient" lacks comprehensive justification beyond the provided figure, and a deeper analysis of gradient flow during training (e.g., gradient norm evolution) would strengthen this point. The observed blurriness in generated images from PMD on SVHN and LFW, attributed to the L1 distance, is a significant drawback that requires further investigation.  Exploring alternative distance metrics within the PMD framework, or investigating perceptual losses, could mitigate this issue. The paper lacks a rigorous comparison with Wasserstein GAN [3] which, while acknowledged, is the state-of-the-art approach leveraging the Wasserstein distance. A direct comparison would be crucial to assess the true advantage of PMD.

A likely failure case, as hinted at by the authors, is matching mixtures of Gaussians with differing variances. PMD, relying on point-to-point matching, might struggle to capture the intricacies of varying scales and shapes within the distributions, leading to suboptimal results. This scenario deserves empirical validation. Furthermore, the paper mentions the possibility of optimization converging to a local minimum with lower entropy for small N. This is a critical limitation that needs more detailed discussion and potentially strategies to address it, such as regularization techniques. The SVHN results also raise concerns about sample diversity, with the digit '8' appearing disproportionately.  This highlights a potential sensitivity of PMD to imbalances in the training data.

The minor typo ("usally" → "usually" on line 141) is a minor point but should be corrected.  The reviewer's request for clarification on the limitations of PMD and MMD is partially addressed, but a more thorough and comparative analysis would be beneficial. The mention of an unbiased estimator for MMD using the kernel from Gretton et al. [17] is helpful, and contrasts effectively with the lack of a known unbiased estimator for PMD’s gradient.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: PMD presents a new approach to estimating distribution distance, building on the Wasserstein metric.
*   Theoretical grounding: The paper provides a theoretical justification for PMD’s consistency.
*   Empirical results: PMD demonstrates competitive performance on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
*   Clear writing: The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison: Lack of direct comparison with Wasserstein GAN.
*   Unexplored limitations: Insufficient discussion of failure cases (mixture of Gaussians, low N) and potential remedies.
*   Image quality: Blurry generated images on complex datasets (SVHN, LFW).
*   Sample diversity: Issues with sample diversity in SVHN experiments.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distribution distance estimation. The introduction of PMD offers a promising alternative to existing methods, with potential advantages in terms of gradient behavior and computational efficiency. However, the limitations outlined above need to be addressed to fully assess the practical applicability and robustness of PMD.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on addressing the weaknesses regarding the comparison to Wasserstein GAN, exploration of failure cases, and discussion of strategies to improve generated image quality and sample diversity.  A more in-depth analysis of the gradient behavior during training is also recommended.
