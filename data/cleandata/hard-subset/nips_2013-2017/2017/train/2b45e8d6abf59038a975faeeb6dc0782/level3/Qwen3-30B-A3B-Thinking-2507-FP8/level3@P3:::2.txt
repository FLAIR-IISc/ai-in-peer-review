

**Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"**

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. The authors propose PMD as a superior alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), which has been widely used in deep learning for tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, and the authors prove it's a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper claims PMD offers advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and applicability with smaller mini-batches.

The paper builds on prior work in distribution matching, particularly MMD [17] and Wasserstein distance [49]. It establishes a theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric by showing that the minimum weight matching problem is equivalent to the optimal transport problem when using empirical distributions. The authors also compare PMD with MMD theoretically, highlighting key differences in gradient properties and computational requirements.

**Strengths:**

1. The theoretical foundation is solid, with a well-established proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3) that demonstrates PMD's convergence to the Wasserstein metric.

2. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate PMD's advantage in using smaller mini-batch sizes (Fig. 5a) and faster convergence (Fig. 3a), addressing a genuine limitation of MMD in practice.

3. The paper provides a clear algorithm for using PMD as a training objective and demonstrates its effectiveness in two important applications: domain adaptation (Section 4.1) and generative modeling (Section 4.2).

4. The authors acknowledge computational challenges (Section 2.3) and provide practical solutions (exact Hungarian algorithm for small N, approximate algorithm for larger N), showing awareness of implementation considerations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The claim of "stronger gradients" is not sufficiently substantiated. MMD's gradient strength critically depends on the kernel bandwidth parameter (σ), which the authors fail to properly address. A fair comparison should use MMD with optimally tuned σ or a range of σ values, not just a single setting. Figure 2(b) shows gradient distributions, but without proper σ tuning, the claim remains unsubstantiated.

2. The assertion of "fewer hyperparameters" is misleading. While PMD avoids MMD's kernel bandwidth, it requires selecting a distance metric (e.g., L1, L2), which itself represents a hyperparameter choice that can significantly impact performance.

3. The paper does not adequately address high-dimensional distance estimation challenges. Both PMD and MMD suffer from the curse of dimensionality, and the reliance on distance metrics (L1, L2) may be problematic for high-dimensional data like natural images, as evidenced by the blurry images in Fig. 4.

4. The comparison with MMD could have been more rigorous by using standard bandwidth heuristics (e.g., median heuristic) and treating MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free, as suggested in the reviewer's comments.

**Overall Assessment:**

PMD is a valid improvement for fast training with small batches, as demonstrated by the empirical results. However, the paper's claims about gradient strength and hyperparameters need stronger justification. The high-dimensional limitations are particularly relevant for generative modeling applications.

The paper meets quality criteria with good theoretical analysis and solid empirical results. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the paper accessible to readers. The work shows originality by introducing PMD as a new distribution distance estimator, though it builds on established concepts of optimal transport and MMD. The significance is moderate: PMD offers a practical solution for specific use cases (small batch training), but may not be a universal solution to distribution matching challenges.

**Recommendation:**

Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated and theoretically sound method with compelling empirical results. While some claims could have been more carefully substantiated, the paper's contribution is significant for practitioners working with distribution matching in deep learning, particularly when mini-batch size is constrained. The authors should address the concerns about gradient strength and hyperparameter claims in the camera-ready version.
