

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel differentiable estimator of the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. The authors establish that PMD, defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. This is a significant contribution as it provides a theoretically grounded alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), which has been widely adopted in deep learning applications but suffers from several limitations including sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and the need for large mini-batch sizes.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong theoretical foundation. The authors provide a rigorous proof that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), building on Varadarajan's theorem and the stability of optimal transport. This theoretical contribution is particularly valuable as it addresses a fundamental limitation of existing distributional distance metrics. The authors also thoroughly compare PMD with MMD, highlighting three key advantages: (1) PMD requires no bandwidth hyperparameter, (2) it provides stronger gradients that do not vanish for outliers or clustered samples, and (3) it can operate with much smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs. 1000 for MMD), leading to faster convergence.

The paper demonstrates the practical utility of PMD through two compelling applications: domain adaptation and generative modeling. In domain adaptation, PMD outperforms MMD on the Office dataset, particularly on the a→w and a→d tasks, while converging significantly faster (Figure 3). For generative modeling, PMD generates less noisy images compared to MMD, especially in the pixel space, though the authors note that the L1 distance may not be ideal for natural images (Section 5.2). The empirical analysis is thorough, with careful ablation studies on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithm choice (Figures 5a-c), demonstrating that PMD's performance is robust to these parameters.

The paper's technical quality is high, with well-executed experiments that appropriately address the claims. The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of the matching problem (Section 2.3), including both exact and approximate algorithms, and demonstrate that the approximate matching algorithm (O(N^2)) performs well in practice, making PMD computationally feasible. The implementation details are transparent, including the use of TensorFlow, CUDA, and specific hardware configurations.

The originality of PMD is significant, as it represents a fresh approach to estimating distributional distances that directly addresses limitations of existing methods. While the Wasserstein metric has been used in machine learning, PMD provides a practical, differentiable estimator that avoids the need for a separate critic network (as in WGAN) and the kernel bandwidth tuning required by MMD. The connection to optimal transport theory is well-established, and the paper properly situates itself relative to prior work on both MMD (Section 3.2) and Wasserstein metrics (Section 3.1).

The significance of this work is substantial. The authors successfully demonstrate that PMD outperforms MMD in two major deep learning applications, with both improved performance and faster convergence. This has direct implications for practitioners, as the proposed method requires less computational resources and is easier to tune. The authors' claim that PMD can be used with standard mini-batch sizes (100 instead of 1000) is particularly valuable for large-scale applications.

However, there are a few areas for potential improvement. The paper could benefit from additional experiments on generative modeling with multiple modes to better understand PMD's behavior when the sample size N is smaller than the number of modes in the distribution. While the authors acknowledge this limitation in their discussion, a concrete experiment would strengthen their claims. Additionally, the paper would be more comprehensive if it included comparisons with other recent distributional distance metrics, such as the Cramer distance (Bellemare et al., 2017) or the Central Moment Discrepancy (Zellinger et al., 2017).

The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized, making it accessible to the NIPS audience. The authors properly cite prior work, including key papers on MMD (Gretton et al., 2012), Wasserstein metrics (Villani, 2009), and domain adaptation (Ganin et al., 2016).

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical foundation is sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the proposed method has clear practical advantages over the current state-of-the-art. The paper is well-written, properly contextualized, and addresses important limitations of existing approaches. I believe this work would be of interest to a broad audience at NIPS and would make a valuable addition to the conference proceedings. The strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its minor weaknesses, making it deserving of acceptance.
