**Paper Review: Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning**

**Summary**

This paper introduces a new method for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples, called Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD). PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors propose an algorithm to learn the parameters of the distributions using PMD as an objective and apply PMD to two deep learning tasks: domain adaptation and generative modeling. Empirical results demonstrate that PMD outperforms Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in terms of performance and convergence speed in both tasks.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of PMD and its relationship with the Wasserstein metric. The experiments are well-designed, and the results are presented in a clear and easy-to-understand manner. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the limitations of PMD, particularly in terms of its computational complexity and sensitivity to hyperparameters.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the technical details. The organization of the paper is logical, and the notation is consistent throughout. However, some of the sections, such as the introduction and conclusion, could be more concise and focused. Additionally, the paper could benefit from more visual aids, such as diagrams or flowcharts, to help illustrate the concepts and algorithms presented.

**Originality**

The paper presents a new method for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples, which is a significant contribution to the field of deep learning. The authors also provide a novel application of PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, which demonstrates the potential of PMD in real-world applications. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between PMD and existing methods, such as MMD and Wasserstein GAN.

**Significance**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, particularly in the areas of domain adaptation and generative modeling. The results demonstrate that PMD can outperform existing methods, such as MMD, in terms of performance and convergence speed. The paper also provides a new perspective on the estimation of distribution distances, which could lead to new insights and applications in the field. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the potential impact of PMD on real-world applications and the potential limitations and challenges of using PMD in practice.

**Arguments for acceptance**

* The paper presents a new and significant contribution to the field of deep learning.
* The results demonstrate that PMD can outperform existing methods in terms of performance and convergence speed.
* The paper provides a clear and concise explanation of the technical details.
* The authors provide a novel application of PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling.
