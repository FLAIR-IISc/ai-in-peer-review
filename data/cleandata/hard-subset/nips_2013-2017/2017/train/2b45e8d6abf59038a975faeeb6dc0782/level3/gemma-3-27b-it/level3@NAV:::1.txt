## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel approach to estimate distribution distance based on samples, framed as the minimum weight matching of sample populations and demonstrating its consistency with the first Wasserstein metric. The authors propose an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD as an objective and demonstrate its efficacy in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.  This work builds on the well-established field of sample-based distribution divergence estimation, addressing limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) like sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and potential gradient issues.  Recent NIPS conferences have featured numerous works utilizing divergence measures for generative modeling (e.g., WGAN) and domain adaptation, highlighting the relevance of this research area.

The primary strength of the paper lies in the theoretically sound definition of PMD and its connection to the Wasserstein metric. The experimental results, while not dramatically outperforming existing methods, are promising and demonstrate PMD's potential, particularly in scenarios where MMD struggles. The analysis of gradient behavior (Fig. 2b) provides valuable insight into why PMD might be more stable during training. However, a significant weakness is the limited scope of the evaluation. The generated images from PMD (SVHN and LFW) are reported as less impressive than those from WGAN, which the paper acknowledges. Furthermore, the SVHN sample diversity issue – the disproportionate frequency of the digit '8' – raises concerns about the robustness of the results.  

The authors correctly identify the need for large mini-batch sizes in PMD to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance for complex, multimodal distributions.  However, they don't thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of PMD to this parameter – the impact of *too small* a mini-batch size on convergence to a suboptimal (e.g., low entropy) solution requires more investigation, especially considering the lack of a known unbiased estimator for the PMD gradient.  I suspect a key failure case would be matching mixtures of Gaussians with differing variances, as accurately representing the shape of those distributions would be highly sensitive to sample size. A minor typo on line 141 (“usally” -> “usually”) has been noted. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Theoretically well-motivated and mathematically sound definition of PMD.
*   Demonstrated advantages over MMD in terms of gradient stability and potential for smaller mini-batch sizes.
*   Provides interesting empirical results in domain adaptation and generative modeling.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical evaluation; generated images not competitive with state-of-the-art.
*   Potential sensitivity to mini-batch size and lack of analysis of suboptimal convergence.
*   SVHN sample bias introduces concerns about experimental validity.

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should clarify the limitations of PMD compared to MMD, specifically addressing the issues raised above, and perform further experiments with mixtures of Gaussians to stress-test the method. A discussion of the computational cost of the matching algorithm for very large N would also be beneficial. They should also acknowledge the work on unbiased MMD gradient estimators (e.g., from “A Kernel Two-Sample Test”) and contrast it with the lack of such an estimator for PMD.
