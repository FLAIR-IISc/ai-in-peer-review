

# Review for "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel approach for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes using PMD as a training objective for deep learning tasks, particularly domain adaptation and generative modeling, claiming advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements.

The paper builds upon the NIPS tradition of developing practical distribution distance estimators for deep learning. It relates to recent work on Wasserstein distance (WGAN [3]), MMD (GMMN [31]), and domain adaptation [5, 47]. The authors effectively position PMD as a differentiable alternative to Wasserstein distance that avoids the need for a separate critic network while addressing MMD's limitations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous proof that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), which is a significant contribution. The connection to optimal transport theory is well-explained and properly contextualized.

2. **Practical Advantages:** The empirical results convincingly demonstrate PMD's advantages in domain adaptation (Table 1) and generative modeling (Fig. 4), particularly its faster convergence speed (Fig. 3a). The paper's claim about smaller mini-batch size requirements is well-supported by the experiments in Section 5.3.

3. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured with clear definitions, pseudocode (Fig. 1), and helpful figures (Fig. 2, 3, 5). The theoretical analysis is accessible to the NIPS audience.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Hyperparameter Claim:** The claim that PMD has "fewer hyperparameters" is questionable. While PMD avoids the MMD kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, it depends on the choice of distance metric (e.g., L1 vs L2), which could be considered a hyperparameter. The authors should clarify this distinction or acknowledge that the distance metric choice may require tuning.

2. **Gradient Strength Claim:** The claim about PMD having "stronger gradients" is less convincing than the paper suggests. MMD's gradient behavior depends heavily on the kernel bandwidth (σ), and the comparison should use the best possible σ values (not fixed bandwidths). The paper should compare PMD against MMD using standard bandwidth heuristics (e.g., median heuristic) to properly evaluate the gradient strength claim.

3. **High-Dimensional Limitations:** The paper doesn't sufficiently address the potential challenges of using PMD in high-dimensional spaces, where distance metrics become less meaningful (the "curse of dimensionality"). This limitation is shared with MMD, but the paper should acknowledge it and discuss how PMD might be adapted for high-dimensional data.

**Comparative Analysis:**

The paper's comparison between PMD and MMD is generally fair, but the hyperparameter discussion needs refinement. MMD is often used with bandwidth selection methods (e.g., median heuristic), which would make it effectively hyperparameter-free in practice. The authors should compare PMD against MMD with these standard bandwidth selection methods to make a stronger case.

The empirical results showing PMD's advantage in convergence speed (Fig. 3a) and smaller mini-batch requirements (Fig. 5a) are compelling. The generative modeling results (Fig. 4) also demonstrate PMD's practical utility, though the blurry images on SVHN/LFW suggest room for improvement.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a theoretically sound and practically useful alternative to MMD for distribution matching in deep learning. The key contributions—proving PMD's consistency with the Wasserstein metric and demonstrating its advantages in training speed and hyperparameter sensitivity—are well-supported by the experimental results. While the "fewer hyperparameters" claim needs qualification, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of distribution distance estimation.

The significance of this work is high, as it addresses a practical limitation of a widely used technique (MMD) with clear benefits for training deep learning models. The applications to domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate real-world utility.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The theoretical contribution is strong, and the empirical results are convincing. The authors should clarify the hyperparameter discussion and address the high-dimensional limitations more thoroughly.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation connecting to Wasserstein metric, practical advantages demonstrated in two significant applications, clear presentation, compelling convergence speed results.

Con: Questionable "fewer hyperparameters" claim (requires clarification), gradient strength claim could be better supported with standard MMD bandwidth selection, insufficient discussion of high-dimensional limitations.
