This paper introduces the concept of Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a differentiable estimation of the distance between two distributions based on samples. The authors define PMD as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches, which is computed using either an exact O(N^3) or approximate O(N^2) algorithm.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of PMD, establishing its connection to the Wasserstein metric and the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD). The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and highlight its advantages over MMD, including fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes for gradient-based optimization.

The authors apply PMD to two deep learning tasks: domain adaptation and generative modeling. The experimental results demonstrate that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed in both tasks. The paper also provides an empirical study of the approximation error of PMD and validates the claim of stronger gradients compared to MMD.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the experimental results are convincing.

However, there are some limitations and potential issues with the paper. Firstly, the generated images from PMD are not as good as those from Wasserstein GAN, which suggests that PMD may not be suitable for all generative modeling tasks. Secondly, PMD requires large minibatch sizes to estimate the Wasserstein distance for diverse, multimodal distributions, which can be computationally expensive. Thirdly, the paper does not provide a known unbiased estimator of the gradient of PMD, which can limit its applicability in certain scenarios.

Additionally, the paper has a minor typo in Line 141, where "usally" should be replaced with "usually".

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, the paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The writing is clear, and the organization is well-structured.
