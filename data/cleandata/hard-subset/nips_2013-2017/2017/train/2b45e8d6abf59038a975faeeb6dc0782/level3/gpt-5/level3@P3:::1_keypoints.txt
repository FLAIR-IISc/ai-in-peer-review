- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) = empirical W1 via minimum-weight bipartite matching, with proven strong consistency as N→∞.  
- Proposes using PMD as a differentiable training objective by fixing optimal matching and backpropagating paired distances.  
- Computes exact matching with Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)) and uses an O(N^2) approximation in practice.  
- Closely related to optimal transport/Wasserstein and MMD approaches; differs by requiring no critic and avoiding kernel bandwidths.  

Strengths
- Clear formulation and standard-consistent theoretical result linking PMD to empirical W1.  
- Practical minibatch-based algorithm with feasible CPU experiments and potential for acceleration.  
- Timely empirical study of minibatch Wasserstein objectives; domain adaptation results competitive and faster-converging than vanilla MMD in some settings.  
- Sensible ablations and analysis of gradient magnitudes, batch size, and matching approximations.  

Weaknesses / Concerns
- Sample complexity/minibatch bias: empirical W1 converges very slowly in high dimensions (≈N^−1/d), encouraging variance shrinkage and underdispersed generators at practical N.  
- Generated samples (SVHN, LFW) are blurrier and less diverse than WGAN; evidence of mode undercoverage (e.g., excess of digit “8”).  
- Claim of “unbiased stochastic gradient” is misleading: stopping gradients through discrete matching typically yields a biased estimator since matching depends on parameters.  
- Comparisons lack stronger baselines (WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn/entropic OT) and miss diversity/fidelity metrics (FID/IS).  
- Computational overhead is high: O(N^2) distances and O(N^3)/O(N^2) matching per step versus cheaper MMD or linear-time MMD estimators; no GPU implementation provided.  
- Using L1 pixel-space ground metric likely causes blurriness; advantage over feature-MMD or adversarial methods becomes unclear when using learned features.  

Quality
- Theoretical consistency is correct but standard; lacks finite-sample bounds or variance analysis.  
- Empirical claims of faster convergence on some tasks are supported, but generative quality lags stronger OT/adversarial baselines.  

Clarity
- Paper is well written overall but needs to clarify the non-differentiability/bias implications of matching and specify which MMD estimators (biased vs unbiased/linear-time) were used.  

Originality
- PMD is essentially empirical W1 packaged as a training objective; methodological novelty is modest though framing and empirical study are useful.  

Significance
- Offers a simple, no-critic OT baseline with strong per-sample gradients, but practical impact limited by large-N requirements, biased gradient estimation, and inferior generative performance.  

Requests for clarification / additional experiments
- Justify or correct the “unbiased stochastic gradient” claim and characterize bias or its vanishing with N.  
- Provide fair MMD baselines using unbiased/linear-time estimators and show training with small minibatches (e.g., 2–32).  
- Run controlled mixture-of-Gaussians experiments (including variances) to test variance shrinkage/entropy collapse.  
- Compare to entropic OT/Sinkhorn divergences and WGAN-GP/Cramér distance for quality/stability.  
- Report diversity/fidelity metrics (FID/IS) and class-balance statistics (e.g., SVHN digit frequencies).  
- Discuss dependence on N and batch-size for multimodal, high-dimensional data and give practical guidance.  

Minor
- Typo: “usally” → “usually”.  
- PMD is not entirely “parameter-free” — depends on ground metric and population/batch sizes.  

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated objective with solid OT grounding; timely experiments and clear gradient-magnitude analysis.  

Cons for acceptance
- Inferior generative quality/diversity vs WGAN; mischaracterizes MMD minibatch needs; lacks unbiased gradient estimator and incurs heavy computation with limited baseline comparisons.  

Overall recommendation
- Borderline reject: idea is sound and experiments interesting, but limited empirical advantages, important methodological issues, and missing comparisons need to be addressed.
