- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): average cost of minimum-weight bipartite matching between sample populations as a sample-based estimator of W1.  
- Shows PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1st Wasserstein distance and uses a practical algorithm that fixes matchings across inner SGD steps.  
- Positions PMD as a drop-in alternative to MMD, claiming no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients when samples are far, and ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches.  
- Connects PMD to primal optimal transport and contrasts it with RKHS-based MMD and dual W1 (WGAN) approaches.  
- Uses exact Hungarian and a quadratic-time approximate matching algorithm; evaluates on domain adaptation (Office-31) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW).  
- Theory: identification with discrete OT is standard; consistency argument appears correct but regularity assumptions and subgradient clarifications are needed.  
- Optimization: fixing matchings in inner-loop updates is a reasonable but potentially biased heuristic; more discussion on when surrogate approximates true subgradient would help.  
- Experiments (domain adaptation): solid results but comparisons are limited to MMD; omits adversarial DA methods (DANN) and multi-kernel JMMD/JAN.  
- Experiments (generative modeling): qualitative improvements shown, but lacks quantitative metrics (FID/Inception/Parzen) and comparisons to WGAN, MMD-GAN, Cramér distance.  
- Ablations and analysis: useful sensitivity studies on N, batch size, matching algorithm, and gradient magnitudes; timing breakdowns provided.  
- Clarity: paper is well written and organized with helpful pseudocode and figures; should discuss Sinkhorn/entropic-regularized OT as a key baseline.  
- Originality: packaging empirical OT as PMD and the inner-loop reuse heuristic is a neat practical idea, though theoretical consistency is not novel.  
- Significance: practically valuable if robust; offers parameter-free alternative to MMD with small-batch benefits, but empirical scope and baselines limit impact.  
- Computational cost: matching is O(N^2)–O(N^3) which is nontrivial, though approximate algorithms and GPU acceleration may mitigate this.  
- Strength: simple, well-motivated W1-tied objective with clear theory and practical algorithm; stronger gradients and useful ablations.  
- Strength: positive results in domain adaptation and generation with faster convergence observed.  
- Weakness: missing comparisons to Sinkhorn OT and strong baselines in both tasks (DANN/JAN; WGAN/MMD-GAN/Cramér).  
- Weakness: generative experiments lack quantitative metrics, use small datasets, and do not probe multi-modal behavior thoroughly.  
- Weakness: limited theoretical development beyond consistency (no finite-sample rates or stability of matchings analysis).  
- Weakness: gradient estimator needs clarification on unbiasedness/consistency and handling of non-differentiable points.  
- Questions for authors: behavior on synthetic multi-modal distributions when N < #modes (mode matching vs mode dropping)?  
- Questions for authors: please report FID/Inception on SVHN/LFW and add WGAN/Sinkhorn baselines.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to number of inner-loop steps with a stale matching?  
- Questions for authors: any results using feature-space distances (learned features) to reduce pixel L1 blur?  
- Pros for acceptance: clear idea, theoretically grounded, practical algorithm, stronger gradients, reproducible presentation.  
- Cons for acceptance: missing key baselines, limited quantitative generative evaluation, and potential computational scaling issues.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept.  
- Reviewer confidence: Medium.
