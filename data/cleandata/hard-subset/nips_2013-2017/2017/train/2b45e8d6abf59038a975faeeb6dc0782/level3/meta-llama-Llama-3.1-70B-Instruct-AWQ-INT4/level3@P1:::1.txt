This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a differentiable estimation of the distance between two distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors propose an algorithm to learn the parameters of the distributions using PMD as an objective and demonstrate its advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in terms of fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch size.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand the proposed method. The introduction provides a clear motivation for the work, and the related work section is comprehensive and well-referenced. The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is rigorous and provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.

The experimental results in Section 5 demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The comparison with MMD is fair, and the results show that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed. However, the generated images from PMD are not as good as those from Wasserstein GAN, which is a state-of-the-art method in generative modeling.

The reviewer raises some valid points about the limitations of PMD and MMD. For example, PMD requires a large minibatch size N to estimate the Wasserstein distance for diverse, multimodal distributions. Additionally, the optimization process may have a global minimum different from the true distribution when the minibatch size is small. The reviewer also points out that the SVHN samples exhibit low diversity, which may affect the performance of PMD.

To address these concerns, the authors should clarify the limitations of PMD and MMD in the paper. They should also discuss the potential failure cases, such as matching mixtures of Gaussians, and provide more analysis on the optimization process.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that proposes a new method for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples. The experimental results are promising, and the paper provides a good contribution to the field of deep learning.
