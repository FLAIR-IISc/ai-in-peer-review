

# Review for NIPS 2017

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable estimator of the Wasserstein distance between two probability distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two sample populations, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes using PMD as a training objective in deep learning tasks, specifically domain adaptation and generative modeling, and compares it with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD).

The work relates to several important NIPS contributions. It connects to the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) [3], which approximates the dual Wasserstein metric using a critic network, but PMD differs by not requiring a separate critic network. It also relates to MMD-based approaches like Generative Moment Matching Networks [31], which use MMD as a training objective for generative models. The paper builds on the growing body of work applying optimal transport theory to machine learning, including the use of Wasserstein metrics in computer vision [40] and information retrieval [50].

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by proving PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, a significant result that establishes the statistical foundation for the proposed method. The authors provide convincing empirical evidence that PMD outperforms MMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, with faster convergence and better performance on the Office benchmark. The method has practical advantages over MMD, including no kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients (as demonstrated in Figure 2b), and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100 instead of 1000), which leads to more efficient training. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical progression from theoretical foundations to empirical validation.

## Weaknesses

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of PMD's limitations. The authors do not adequately address the requirement for sufficiently large population sizes N to estimate the Wasserstein metric accurately for diverse, multimodal distributions. For instance, matching mixtures of Gaussians (including learning variances) would likely expose problems with PMD that the paper does not discuss. The authors should clarify under what conditions PMD is expected to work well and when it might fail.

The comparison with MMD's mini-batch requirements is not entirely fair. While the paper claims PMD can use smaller mini-batch sizes, MMD can also work with very small minibatches (e.g., batch_size=2) using the unbiased estimator from "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" [Gretton et al.], which the authors should acknowledge. The paper also does not address the lack of an unbiased gradient estimator for PMD, which is an important consideration for training stability.

The generated images using PMD are not as good as those from WGAN, as noted in the paper. The authors should discuss why this is the case and whether there are ways to improve the image quality with PMD. Additionally, the SVHN dataset exhibits low diversity (e.g., digit '8' appears disproportionately often), which may affect the generality of the results.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

*Pros:*
- Strong theoretical foundation with proof of consistency
- Demonstrated advantages over MMD in both performance and convergence speed
- Practical implementation with clear empirical validation
- Well-structured and clearly written

*Cons:*
- Insufficient discussion of limitations, particularly regarding population size requirements
- Unfair comparison with MMD regarding mini-batch size
- Generated images not as good as WGAN for generative modeling
- No discussion of the lack of unbiased gradient estimator

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting approach to estimating the Wasserstein distance between distributions using minimum weight matching. The theoretical results are sound, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over MMD in the tasks studied. However, the paper would be significantly improved by a more thorough discussion of PMD's limitations, especially regarding the required population size N for different distribution types. The comparison with MMD should be refined to acknowledge that MMD can also work with small minibatches using unbiased estimators.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations and improve the comparison with MMD. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing an alternative to MMD that addresses some of its key drawbacks, and with the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
