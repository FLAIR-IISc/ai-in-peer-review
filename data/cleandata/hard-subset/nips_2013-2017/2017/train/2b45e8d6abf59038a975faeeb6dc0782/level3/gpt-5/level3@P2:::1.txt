Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two samples (minibatches) drawn from two distributions, with d(·,·) as a ground metric (typically L1). PMD equals the empirical 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between the corresponding empirical measures, computed either exactly via the Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)) or approximately in O(N^2). The authors prove (via standard results on weak convergence and stability of optimal transport) that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance. They then use PMD as a differentiable training objective by (i) computing a matching for a population of size N and (ii) performing several SGD steps using mini-batches |B| ≪ N while freezing the matching. Applications include unsupervised domain adaptation (distance between source/target feature distributions) and deep generative modeling (matching generated to data samples). Empirically, PMD is argued to have stronger gradients, no kernel bandwidth, and to train with smaller |B| than MMD, with favorable results on Office domain adaptation and MNIST/SVHN/LFW generation.

This connects to extensive work on OT/Wasserstein distances (Villani, 2008) and their empirical use in vision and retrieval, to dual-approximation approaches such as WGAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017; Gulrajani et al., 2017), to kernel-based two-sample tests and MMD (Gretton et al., 2012), and to more recent divergences with improved gradients (e.g., Cramér distance; Bellemare et al., 2017). A notably relevant line not discussed is entropically regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013), which provide fast, differentiable approximations with GPU-friendly O(N^2) time.

Strengths
- Clear, simple objective: empirical W1 via matching; solid consistency argument grounded in standard OT theory.
- Practical training recipe (freezing matchings for several SGD steps) and thorough ablations on N, |B|, and exact vs. approximate matching.
- Empirical comparisons to MMD show faster convergence in some settings and modest accuracy gains on several Office transfer tasks; in generation, PMD is competitive with MMD, particularly outside autoencoder feature space.
- Useful qualitative analysis of gradient magnitudes, explaining potential advantages over RBF-MMD.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty is limited: PMD is the empirical W1 used as a loss; the core theorem is a direct application of known OT results. The main algorithmic novelty is the heuristic of freezing matchings and subsampling; no convergence analysis or unbiased gradient estimator is provided.
- Claims about MMD’s need for large minibatches are overstated. Unbiased/linear-time MMD estimators (Gretton et al., 2012) permit very small batches (even batch_size=2) and can be trained via SGD, albeit with higher variance. The comparison uses only RBF kernels with tuned bandwidths; recent MMD variants (e.g., MMD-GAN with learned kernels) address weak gradients.
- Computational cost and scaling: O(N^2)–O(N^3) matching per population is heavy, and the paper reports only single-threaded CPU implementations. While approximate matching helps, stronger baselines such as Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi, 2013) are not compared; these provide differentiable transport plans and GPU speedups.
- Sample complexity and multimodality: Empirical W1 in high dimensions converges slowly; the paper’s toy error rate (~N^-0.23) reflects this. For complex multimodal targets, small N can bias training toward low-entropy solutions or mode under-coverage. Indeed, SVHN samples appear to have low diversity (e.g., an overrepresentation of the digit “8”). A simple Gaussian mixture experiment (including variance learning) would likely expose such issues; this would clarify limitations and regimes where larger N is necessary.
- Generative quality lags behind WGAN/WGAN-GP; samples are blurrier and show reduced diversity, especially in pixel space with L1.
- Domain adaptation evaluation is limited: comparisons are only to MMD; stronger baselines such as DANN and JAN or CMD are not included, making it hard to assess significance.
- The gradient estimator is biased (freezing matchings, subsampling). The paper assumes matchings change slowly but does not quantify mismatch frequency or impact.

Quality
The technical components are correct and clearly derived; the consistency result is sound (if standard). Experimental methodology is mostly reasonable, but comparisons to stronger baselines (WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn OT, DANN/JAN) are missing, and diversity/coverage metrics (e.g., FID, class distribution on SVHN) are not reported. The optimization heuristic lacks theoretical support.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The connection to OT is clear, and the algorithm is described precisely. Minor typo: Line 141 “usally” → “usually.” It would help to explicitly state that the gradient does not backpropagate through the combinatorial matching and to discuss the implications.

Originality
Moderate. Using empirical W1 via matching as a deep learning loss is a natural idea; the main contribution is a pragmatic training scheme and an empirical study of its behavior. Missing discussion of entropic OT diminishes novelty positioning.

Significance
Mixed. PMD is a simple, potentially useful alternative to MMD/WGAN in some regimes. However, generative results lag SOTA, domain adaptation gains are modest and not compared to strong baselines, and computational and sample-complexity limitations are nontrivial.

Specific suggestions and questions
- Clarify the limitations: When does PMD require large N (e.g., multimodal targets, high dimension)? Provide a controlled Gaussian mixture experiment (with learned variances) to illustrate failure modes and the N needed to recover modes.
- Discuss and, if possible, include MMD baselines with unbiased/linear-time estimators and very small batch sizes; clarify that MMD does not inherently require large batches. Consider MMD-GAN or learned kernels to address gradient saturation.
- Compare against Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi, 2013), which offers differentiable, GPU-accelerated transport, and potentially yields less noisy gradients than hard matchings.
- Quantify diversity in generation (class balance on SVHN, FID/IS) and address the observed “8” overrepresentation. Analyze whether PMD encourages low-entropy solutions for small N.
- Report how often the matching changes across SGD steps and its effect on optimization; discuss bias/variance of the proposed gradient estimator.
- Broaden domain adaptation baselines (e.g., DANN, JAN, CMD) and report statistical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated empirical Wasserstein objective with a practical training scheme.
- Clear theoretical grounding (consistency) and useful empirical insights on gradients and minibatch usage.
- Positive results on some transfer tasks; competitive with MMD for generation without adversaries.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental contribution; missing key related baselines (WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn OT, modern domain adaptation).
- Overstated weaknesses of MMD; limited and sometimes weaker generative performance with diversity issues.
- Computational and sample-complexity concerns; biased optimization heuristic without analysis.
