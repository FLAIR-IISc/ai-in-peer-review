- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based minibatch estimator for comparing distributions via minimum-weight bipartite matching.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein-1 metric.  
- Uses PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep networks, computing a primal-form Wasserstein estimate on batches.  
- Positions PMD as an alternative to MMD that avoids kernel bandwidth tuning and claims to provide stronger, more uniform gradients.  
- Empirically shows promising results on domain adaptation benchmarks and analyzes convergence speed and parameter sensitivity.  
- Proposes a practical optimization strategy of performing expensive matching less frequently than SGD updates.  
- Main novelty and soundness: elegant connection to matching and solid theoretical foundation.  
- Compelling empirical and conceptual case against MMD’s sensitivity to kernel hyperparameters and weak gradients.  
- Concern about sample complexity: small minibatch matching can bias optimization and encourage mode-seeking / low-entropy solutions.  
- Generative modeling results are low-quality and low-diversity (e.g., blurry SVHN/LFW samples, over-represented digits), weaker than WGANs.  
- Comparison with MMD is potentially unfair: unbiased small-batch MMD estimators exist and can be used with SGD.  
- Gradient estimator is biased because it treats the optimal matching as fixed, unlike unbiased MMD gradients; this theoretical limitation needs discussion.  
- Reviewer requests experiments on challenging synthetic multimodal distributions to characterize mode-collapse risk and a quantitative diversity evaluation for generative samples.  
- Minor suggestions: explicitly discuss finite-N limitations, clarify MMD/batch-size claims, acknowledge biased gradient estimator, and fix typo ("usally" → "usually").
