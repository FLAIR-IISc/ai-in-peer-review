- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein metric using minimum weight matching as an alternative to MMD.

- Theoretically grounded: PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, linking to optimal transport theory.

- Practical advantages: PMD yields stronger gradients and can operate with smaller mini-batches, aiding efficient training.

- Empirical validation: PMD shows superior performance and faster convergence vs MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

- Clear presentation: paper is well-organized with pseudocode and detailed experimental setup improving reproducibility.

- Gradient claim limited: stronger gradients are demonstrated only in specific settings; broader evaluations (e.g., different distance metrics and MMD sigmas) are missing.

- Hyperparameter claim overstated: while kernel bandwidth is avoided, PMD depends on choice of distance metric (implicit hyperparameters like L1 vs L2).

- High-dimensional scalability concerns: reliance on distance metrics may hurt performance in high dimensions; experiments do not sufficiently probe this.

- Computational complexity: exact matching is O(N^3) (Hungarian algorithm); approximate methods help but computational trade-offs need deeper discussion.

- Pro: PMD is a theoretically sound, practically useful contribution with clear empirical benefits over MMD.

- Con: Uncertainty remains about PMD's scalability/robustness in high dimensions and the strength of its hyperparameter claims.

- Recommendation: Accept, but encourage further exploration of high-dimensional limitations and computational efficiency.
