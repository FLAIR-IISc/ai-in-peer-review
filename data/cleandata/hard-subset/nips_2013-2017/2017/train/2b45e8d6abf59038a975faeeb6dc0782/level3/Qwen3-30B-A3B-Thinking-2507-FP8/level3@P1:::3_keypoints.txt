- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel differentiable sample-based estimator of distance between distributions.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), using Varadarajan’s theorem and OT stability.  
- Presents PMD as a theoretically grounded alternative to MMD that avoids kernel bandwidth tuning and vanishing gradients.  
- Highlights three practical advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger non-vanishing gradients for outliers/clusters, and viability with much smaller mini-batches.  
- Demonstrates empirical gains in domain adaptation (Office dataset, notably a→w and a→d) with faster convergence.  
- Shows PMD yields less noisy generated images in pixel space for generative modeling, while noting L1 pixel distance limitations for natural images.  
- Provides thorough empirical analysis and ablation studies on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithm choice, showing robustness.  
- Describes matching problem and algorithms clearly; approximate O(N^2) matching performs well, making PMD computationally feasible.  
- Implementation details are transparent (TensorFlow, CUDA, hardware), supporting reproducibility.  
- Original contribution: practical differentiable Wasserstein estimator that avoids critic networks (WGAN) and MMD kernel tuning, grounded in optimal transport theory.  
- Significant practical impact: better performance, faster convergence, and lower computational/tuning cost (claims of using mini-batches of 100 vs. 1000).  
- Suggested improvements: add experiments on multimodal generative tasks when sample size N is smaller than mode count.  
- Suggested comparisons: include other recent distributional distances (e.g., Cramer distance, Central Moment Discrepancy).  
- Writing, organization, and citations are strong; reviewer recommends acceptance, citing strengths outweighing minor weaknesses.
