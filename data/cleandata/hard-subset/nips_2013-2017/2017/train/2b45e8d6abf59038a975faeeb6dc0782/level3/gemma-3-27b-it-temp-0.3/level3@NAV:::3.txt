## Review of “Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning”

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel estimator for the Wasserstein distance between probability distributions based on samples. The core idea – minimizing the cost of matching samples between distributions – is conceptually simple and offers a compelling alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a widely used divergence in machine learning. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and, crucially, is differentiable, enabling its use as a training objective for neural networks.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, presenting the theoretical foundations alongside practical applications in domain adaptation and generative modeling. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers on MMD, Wasserstein distance, and related techniques. The theoretical results appear sound, and the experimental evaluation provides a reasonable comparison to MMD, demonstrating PMD’s advantages in convergence speed and, in some cases, performance. The comparison to MMD is thorough, highlighting PMD’s lack of bandwidth parameter and potential for stronger gradients.

A key strength of this work lies in its potential to address limitations of MMD, particularly the sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and the need for large mini-batches. The authors convincingly argue that PMD’s differentiability and gradient properties are beneficial for training deep learning models. The inclusion of a quadratic approximation for computational efficiency is also a valuable contribution, as exact computation scales cubically with sample size.

However, as the reviewer notes, the paper would benefit from further investigation into PMD’s behavior with complex, multi-modal distributions. Specifically, experiments on generative modeling with datasets exhibiting many modes are needed. The question of how PMD performs when the sample size (N) is smaller than the number of modes is a valid concern, as the matching process might become ill-defined or unstable. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to estimating Wasserstein distance.
*   Clear advantages over MMD in terms of gradient behavior and mini-batch size requirements.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness in domain adaptation and generative modeling.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited exploration of PMD’s performance on highly multi-modal distributions.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of the quadratic approximation’s impact on statistical performance.



**Overall:** This is a good paper proposing a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. Addressing the reviewer’s concerns regarding multi-modal distributions would further strengthen the contribution and increase confidence in the method’s generalizability. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
