- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined via minimum weight matching of sample populations, and proves it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.
- Positions PMD as an alternative to MMD, claiming advantages: stronger gradients, effective with smaller mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters.
- Applies PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, reporting empirical improvements over MMD.
- Theoretical foundation is strong with a clear consistency proof linking PMD to the Wasserstein distance.
- Empirical results convincingly demonstrate faster convergence with small mini-batches (e.g., 100 vs. MMD’s typical 1000).
- Ablation studies on matching algorithms and population size give useful practical implementation guidance.
- Claim of “stronger gradients” is inadequately validated because MMD’s gradient depends on kernel bandwidth, which the paper does not systematically vary.
- Comparisons to MMD should explore a range of kernel bandwidths (sigma) rather than a single fixed value.
- The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is weakened by PMD’s dependence on choice of distance metric (e.g., L1 vs L2), introducing its own hyperparameters.
- Paper underestimates PMD’s difficulties in high-dimensional distance estimation, limiting real-world applicability.
- Well-organized, clearly written, and sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.
- Novel contribution relative to prior NIPS work, though the link to Wasserstein distance is somewhat straightforward.
- Significance: a practical improvement for small mini-batch training, but not a universal solution for distribution matching; some claims should be tempered and limitations acknowledged.
