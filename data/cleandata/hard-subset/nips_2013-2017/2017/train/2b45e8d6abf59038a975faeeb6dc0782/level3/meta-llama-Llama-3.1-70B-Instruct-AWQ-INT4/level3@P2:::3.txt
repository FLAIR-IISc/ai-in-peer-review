The paper "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning" introduces a novel method for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples, referred to as Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD). The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and propose an algorithm to learn the parameters of the distributions using PMD as an objective.

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and properly cites prior work. The authors provide a thorough comparison of PMD with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), the primary population-based divergence used in machine learning. The experimental analysis is adequate, with comparisons to MMD and other methods for domain adaptation and generative modeling.

The theoretical results appear correct, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the relationship between PMD and the Wasserstein metric. The use of PMD as a training objective for neural-network-based models is conceptually simple and enables efficient gradient-based training.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to balance theoretical rigor with practical considerations. The authors acknowledge the cubic computational complexity of exact PMD computation and propose a quadratic approximation that empirically maintains statistical performance. This is a significant contribution, as it makes PMD a viable option for large-scale applications.

The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The authors show that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed in both tasks. The visual results for generative modeling are also impressive, with PMD generating high-quality images that are comparable to those produced by MMD.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The reviewer requests experiments on generative modeling with many modes, as PMD's behavior in this setting is unclear. Additionally, the reviewer questions PMD's behavior when the sample size N is smaller than the number of modes. These are valid concerns, as PMD's performance in these scenarios could have significant implications for its practical applications.

Overall, this is a good paper that proposes a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field, and their work has the potential to impact a wide range of applications in machine learning.
