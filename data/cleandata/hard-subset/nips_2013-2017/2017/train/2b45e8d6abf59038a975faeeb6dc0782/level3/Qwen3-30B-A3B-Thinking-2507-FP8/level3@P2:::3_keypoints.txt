- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable sample-based estimator using minimum weight matching and proved strongly consistent for the first Wasserstein metric.
- Proposes an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD and demonstrates advantages over MMD.
- Positions PMD relative to prior work (MMD, WGAN, domain adaptation), offering a direct, sample-based, differentiable Wasserstein estimator.
- Provides strong theoretical analysis proving PMD's consistency and clear comparison with MMD.
- Experimental design is sound with thorough comparisons on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
- Acknowledges cubic time complexity of exact matching and proposes a quadratic approximation that preserves statistical performance.
- Empirical results show PMD outperforms MMD in performance and convergence speed, and requires smaller minibatches.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, with useful pseudocode, figures, and reproducibility details.
- Originality: novel use of minimum weight matching as a divergence, distinct from kernel-based MMD.
- Practical advantages: no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller minibatch requirements.
- Significance: addresses practical MMD limitations and could improve distribution-comparison training in deep learning.
- Strengths: solid theory, practical benefits, thorough empirical validation, clear presentation, and comprehensive baselines.
- Weaknesses: cubic complexity may limit large-scale use; unclear behavior when sample size N is smaller than number of modes.
- Additional weakness: blurry generative results on SVHN/LFW, possibly due to L1 distance choice rather than PMD itself.
- Reviewer requests: evaluate generative modeling on many-mode datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) and analyze PMD when sample size is smaller than number of modes.
- Overall recommendation: excellent contribution; should be accepted to NIPS 2017.
