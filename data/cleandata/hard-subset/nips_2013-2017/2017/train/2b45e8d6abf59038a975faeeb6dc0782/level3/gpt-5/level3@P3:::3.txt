Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based, population-level estimator of the first Wasserstein distance W1 defined as a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two sets of i.i.d. samples. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1, and develop a practical learning procedure that uses PMD as a differentiable loss to train neural network models with SGD. They discuss the O(N^3) exact cost (Hungarian algorithm) and a practical O(N^2) approximation with Drake–Hougardy, and show that the latter preserves empirical performance.

PMD is positioned as a population-based alternative to MMD, the most widely used sample-based divergence in ML. The paper compares PMD and MMD theoretically (gradients, hyperparameters, minibatching) and empirically (domain adaptation and deep generative modeling). It also relates to Wasserstein GANs (dual formulation) and other Wasserstein estimators; the paper’s contribution is a direct, parameter-free, primal estimator that remains compatible with gradient-based training.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated estimator: PMD is just min-cost matching and aligns exactly with empirical W1; the consistency result is correct and well-grounded in OT theory.
- Differentiable training objective in practice: the objective is piecewise smooth and supports SGD; the decision not to backprop through the discrete matching is reasonable and standard.
- Clear advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger and more uniform gradients, and the ability to use smaller SGD minibatches than the population size, which accelerates training.
- Solid empirical work: thorough MMD comparisons; wins on several domain adaptation tasks and competitive elsewhere; acceptable generative modeling quality with faster convergence.
- Good writing and organization; related work is broadly cited; claims are mostly supported by analysis and experiments.

Weaknesses and questions
- Differentiability and optimization: The gradient ignores the dependence of the matching on parameters (no backprop through argmin). While the objective is piecewise constant in the matching and differentiable almost everywhere, please clarify:
  - Are there convergence guarantees for SGD with stale matchings/subsampled pairs?
  - How often does the matching change in practice, and does this induce optimization noise or bias?
  - Could a soft/entropic relaxation (e.g., Sinkhorn) be used to obtain smooth, GPU-friendly gradients and enable backprop through transport?
- Scalability: Both exact and approximate matchings require O(N^2) pairwise distances and memory; Hungarian is O(N^3), the approximation O(N^2). The paper demonstrates feasibility up to a few thousand samples. Please discuss:
  - Memory/runtime behavior for larger N and high-dimensional data.
  - Whether blockwise/Sinkhorn or sparsified approximations could reduce the N^2 memory footprint.
  - Any bounds or empirical reporting on the optimality gap of the approximate matching.
- Empirical breadth:
  - Domain adaptation comparisons are limited to MMD. Stronger baselines such as DANN/ADDA, JAN, CORAL, DSN, and CMD (which you cite) would contextualize PMD’s practical value.
  - Generative modeling evaluation is largely qualitative. Please add quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score/FID on SVHN and LFW, and precision/recall or density/coverage).
  - Multi-modality stress tests: Please include synthetic experiments with many modes (e.g., Gaussian grids or Stacked MNIST) to assess mode coverage vs. MMD and WGAN/Cràmer GAN. Specifically, what happens when the population size N is smaller than the number of modes? Does PMD encourage covering modes or matching only a subset? Any degenerate pairing behavior?
- Choice of ground metric: Results suggest pixel L1 can blur images; PMD performance likely depends on the distance. Please study PMD on learned feature spaces (e.g., pretrained ConvNet embeddings) and/or compare L1 vs. L2 vs. perceptual features to understand sensitivity.
- Finite-sample behavior: While strong consistency is shown, rates and concentration are not discussed. Empirical rates (~N^-0.23) are informative; it would help to connect to known finite-sample rates for empirical OT (e.g., results by Fournier–Guillin) and discuss implications for choosing N and |B|.
- Unequal sample sizes and weighting: The method assumes equal-sized populations with uniform weights. Please discuss extensions to unequal sample sizes or non-uniform empirical weights, common in practice.

Quality
- Theory: The OT connection and strong consistency statement appear correct and are appropriately cited. The gradient rationale (no propagation through M) is standard for piecewise-constant combinatorial layers; a brief remark about subgradients and measure-zero nondifferentiable points would strengthen the presentation.
- Empirics: Adequate but could be broadened (baselines, quantitative metrics, multimodal stress tests). The analysis of gradient magnitudes and minibatch effects is insightful.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well-structured. Implementation details are sufficient to reproduce (code not required). A brief algorithmic appendix on the approximate matching and its complexity would be helpful.

Originality
- Using minimum-weight matching as a population-based differentiable estimator for W1 in deep learning is natural yet under-explored; the paper adds an end-to-end training recipe and a careful MMD comparison. Consider citing and discussing entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) as a closely related and highly relevant alternative.

Significance
- PMD offers a practical, parameter-free divergence with compelling optimization properties. If the scalability and evaluation breadth are addressed, this could become a useful tool for distribution matching tasks, complementing MMD and adversarial/dual Wasserstein methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, principled, and consistent estimator of W1 with a practical training algorithm.
- Clear empirical advantages over MMD on speed and stability; competitive accuracy.
- Good presentation; results are reproducible and likely useful to practitioners.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baselines in domain adaptation; limited quantitative evaluation for generative modeling.
- Missing discussion/experiments on highly multimodal data and N smaller than mode count.
- Scalability and differentiability through matching could be better justified or relaxed (e.g., Sinkhorn).

Requested additions/clarifications
- Add multimodal generative experiments (e.g., Stacked MNIST, Gaussian grids), including the N < #modes regime.
- Include quantitative generative metrics (FID/IS, precision/recall).
- Compare with at least one adversarial domain-adaptation baseline (e.g., DANN) and CMD/CORAL.
- Discuss or test a soft/entropic matching variant; report approximate matching optimality gaps.
- Comment on finite-sample rates and choice of ground metric/feature space.

Overall assessment
A good paper proposing a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. With stronger empirical breadth and a brief discussion of scalable/soft OT alternatives, I would be comfortable recommending acceptance.
