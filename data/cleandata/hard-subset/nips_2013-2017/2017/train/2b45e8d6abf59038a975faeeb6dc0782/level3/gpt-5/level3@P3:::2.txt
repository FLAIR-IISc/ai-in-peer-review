Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based objective for distribution matching defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two finite samples under a chosen distance (e.g., L1/L2). The authors show that PMD is the empirical optimal transport (OT) cost and prove strong consistency to the first Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance using standard results on weak convergence and stability of OT. They provide an SGD training procedure that alternates between computing a (fixed) matching and doing several gradient steps that do not backpropagate through the discrete matching.

The work is positioned as an alternative to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for deep learning scenarios such as domain adaptation and implicit generative modeling. Relative to prior NIPS work: MMD-based training (GMMN, MMD-GAN) and Wasserstein-based methods (WGAN) are the main comparators. PMD differs from WGAN by avoiding an auxiliary critic, and from MMD by replacing kernelization with sample-to-sample matching. It is also closely related to empirical OT with uniform weights; the algorithmic contribution is to exploit small-batch stochastic updates while reusing a fixed matching for several steps. The relationship to entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) and energy/Cramér distances could be discussed more fully.

Strengths
- Clear, simple objective that is a direct empirical estimator of W1, with a clean consistency argument.
- Practical training recipe that reuses matchings and empirically tolerates approximate O(N^2) matching.
- Convincing empirical evidence that PMD trains effectively with smaller mini-batches than MMD, yielding faster wall-clock convergence in tested setups.
- Competitive results on Office domain adaptation and reasonable results on MNIST/SVHN/LFW generative modeling; qualitative samples are less noisy than MMD in pixel space.
- Implementation details and ablations (population size N, mini-batch |B|, exact vs approximate matching) help reproducibility.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Stronger gradients claim not fully established. MMD’s gradients depend critically on kernel bandwidth; with the median heuristic or multi-kernel MMD, gradients can be much stronger than what is reported. The comparison should sweep σ (or use standard bandwidth heuristics and/or multi-kernel) and report best or range-of-σ results for fairness. As is, the gradient-strength conclusion is not convincing.
- “Fewer hyperparameters” claim is weak. PMD still requires choosing a ground distance d(·,·) and possibly a feature space in which distances are measured. Those design choices often bring their own hyperparameters (e.g., feature encoder, scaling), especially in high-dimensional data where raw L1/L2 on pixels is known to be poor. In the generative experiments the authors acknowledge that pixel distances lead to blur and move to code-space distances, undercutting the “parameter-free” narrative.
- High-dimensional distance concentration. Both MMD (with poor kernels) and OT with Euclidean costs suffer in high dimensions. PMD inherits the challenges of reliable distance estimation and sample complexity in high-D, so its practical advantage over well-tuned MMD may shrink there. This limitation should be discussed more explicitly.
- Computational and memory cost. Even with approximate matching, forming and storing the N×N distance matrix scales quadratically; this is nontrivial for larger N or higher D. The paper argues it’s acceptable versus backprop cost, but clearer wall-clock comparisons and GPU memory footprints would strengthen the case. A comparison with Sinkhorn-OT (which is GPU-friendly and linear in the number of pairwise distances) would also be informative.
- Theoretical novelty is modest. The consistency result follows directly from classic OT theory; there are no finite-sample bounds or gradient stability analyses. Claims like “unbiased stochastic gradient” when freezing M may need more careful justification (subgradient view, almost-everywhere arguments).
- Empirical baselines. Beyond MMD, comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn OT, energy/Cramér distance, or MMD-GAN would help contextualize PMD’s merits in generative modeling.

Quality
- Soundness: The PMD–W1 connection and consistency are correct, though standard. The training procedure is sensible; non-differentiability through matching is handled pragmatically. Claims about stronger gradients and hyperparameters are overstated without more careful MMD tuning.
- Empirics: The small mini-batch advantage is clearly demonstrated and is the most convincing empirical finding. Domain adaptation results are decent; generative results are mixed (blurry in pixel space), aligning with the metric-choice caveat.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The algorithmic description and ablations are clear. Please clarify: (i) treatment of non-differentiability at matching changes (subgradients), (ii) computational/memory scaling and GPU feasibility for large N, (iii) precise MMD tuning protocol (median heuristic, multi-kernel).

Originality
- The core idea—use empirical OT via minimum-weight matching as a learning objective—is natural and known; the contribution is its practicalization for SGD with empirical evidence of small-batch benefits. Novelty is moderate.

Significance
- PMD is a useful addition for practitioners seeking a simple, critic-free alternative to adversarial training that works with small batches. The reliance on a user-chosen metric limits impact in high-dimensional raw spaces unless combined with learned features. Still, the demonstrated speed benefits are practically relevant.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare MMD under standard bandwidth heuristics (median heuristic), report best-over-σ, and include multi-kernel MMD. If that narrows the gradient gap, recalibrate claims.
- Treat MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free under standard heuristics in the discussion, to fairly balance the “fewer hyperparameters” claim.
- Add comparisons to Sinkhorn-OT and WGAN/WGAN-GP (or Cramér distance), including wall-clock and sample quality.
- Explore feature-space distances learned jointly (e.g., a small encoder) and report sensitivity to metric choices to make the “distance hyperparameters” point explicit.
- Provide memory/runtime scaling plots for increasing N and D; include GPU implementations when possible.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, principled objective with clear link to Wasserstein-1.
- Strong empirical evidence for small mini-batch advantage and faster convergence.
- Competitive performance on domain adaptation; reasonable generative results without a critic.

Cons for acceptance
- Key claims (stronger gradients, fewer hyperparameters) are not convincingly established against well-tuned MMD.
- Limited theoretical novelty beyond standard OT consistency; no finite-sample or gradient analysis.
- Missing comparisons to strong OT and adversarial baselines; high-D limitations of distance metrics remain.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. PMD is a valid and practically useful alternative to MMD for fast training with small batches, with a clean link to Wasserstein distance. However, the paper should temper claims about gradient strength and hyperparameters, broaden baselines (including well-tuned MMD and OT/adversarial methods), and discuss high-dimensional limitations more candidly. If these issues are addressed, the contribution would be solidly above the bar.
