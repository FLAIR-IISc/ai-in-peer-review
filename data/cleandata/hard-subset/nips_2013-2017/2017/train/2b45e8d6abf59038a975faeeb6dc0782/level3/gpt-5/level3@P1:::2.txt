This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based discrepancy defined as the minimum-cost bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets, and proposes to use PMD as a differentiable training objective in deep learning. The authors show that PMD coincides with the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (earth mover’s distance) between empirical measures and prove strong consistency (convergence to the true W1) via standard results (Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport; cf. Villani). They compare PMD to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD avoids kernel bandwidth selection, provides stronger gradients, and allows smaller SGD mini-batches. Empirically, PMD is applied to domain adaptation (Office) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), showing faster convergence and often better performance than MMD, with additional analysis of mini-batch effects and approximate vs exact matching.

Relation to prior work: PMD is essentially the empirical W1 computed via assignment (Hungarian or approximate matching). This connects to long-standing OT methods in vision and IR (Rubner et al.) and to contemporary ML uses: kernel two-sample tests (MMD), implicit generative models via adversarial critics (GANs, WGAN), and entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi), as well as Cramér/energy distances and CMD for domain adaptation. The paper cites WGAN but not Sinkhorn OT; situating PMD against that line would strengthen the positioning. Compared to MMD-based GMMN, PMD replaces kernel-based IPM with OT-based IPM and shows practical training advantages.

Quality:
- Theory: The equivalence of (empirical) OT to minimum-weight matching and the strong consistency statement are correct and standard. The training algorithm uses gradients that do not backpropagate through the discrete matching, justified by local constancy of the permutation. This is a reasonable envelope-style argument for piecewise-constant matchings, but a short discussion of non-differentiability at permutation switches and the use of subgradients would improve rigor.
- Algorithms: Exact O(N^3) matching and O(N^2) approximations are appropriate; the observation that approximate matchings suffice is well supported. The claim of stronger gradients is motivated by analytical comparison (RBF kernel saturation) and a gradient magnitude histogram; however, this depends critically on kernel bandwidth. A more thorough study across bandwidths (including standard multiscale mixtures or the median heuristic) is necessary for a fair assessment.
- Experiments: The domain adaptation results on Office show PMD competitive or better than MMD and convincingly faster convergence. The generative modeling results suggest PMD helps in pixel space but benefits from learning in an autoencoder feature space; samples are still somewhat blurry, consistent with L1 costs being suboptimal perceptually. The analysis of mini-batch vs “population” size is insightful and supports the small-mini-batch advantage. Missing are comparisons to strong baselines in each domain (e.g., DANN/Domain-Adversarial, JAN for adaptation; WGAN, Cramér distance, Sinkhorn OT for generation) and quantitative generation metrics (e.g., FID, precision/recall).

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized. It would help to standardize terminology around “population size N” vs “mini-batch size |B|,” and to state upfront that PMD equals empirical W1. Including pseudocode with explicit update schedules and clarifying when the matching is recomputed would aid reproducibility. A discussion of the effect of the cost function d (L1 vs feature-space distances) on both theory and practice would also help.

Originality: The core discrepancy is the empirical W1, which is known; the novelty here is to package it as PMD, analyze its consistency in the deep learning context, and propose a practical training recipe that leverages sub-sampling and approximate matching. While not conceptually groundbreaking, the engineering insight and empirical demonstration are useful.

Significance: PMD is a compelling alternative to MMD as a training loss, particularly when small mini-batches are desirable and when kernel hyperparameters are a pain point. Its dependence on a distance function d, however, reintroduces design choices (e.g., L1 vs feature-space distances) that can act as hyperparameters in practice. In high-dimensional settings, Euclidean distances can be poorly behaved; adopting learned or perceptual feature spaces (or Sinkhorn with entropic regularization) may be more robust.

Specific suggestions:
- Compare MMD under standard bandwidth selection (median heuristic) and multiscale mixtures, and report best-over-bandwidth results to fairly stress-test the “stronger gradient” claim.
- Include comparisons to Sinkhorn-OT (Cuturi) and Cramér/energy distance (Bellemare et al.) as differentiable OT-like baselines; also CMD for domain adaptation.
- Report quantitative metrics for generation (FID, precision/recall) and compare to WGAN/WGAN-GP.
- Analyze sensitivity to d: L1 vs L2 vs cosine; and distances in learned feature spaces (e.g., conv features) to mitigate high-dimensional pixel-space issues.
- Discuss computational scaling with N and D; provide GPU implementations or complexity-vs-accuracy trade-offs when moving to higher-resolution images.
- Clarify and, if possible, bound the bias from not differentiating through the matching when permutations change.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled connection to Wasserstein distance with a simple, implementable objective.
- Strong empirical evidence that PMD enables small mini-batches and faster convergence than MMD.
- Useful analysis of gradient behavior and batch-size effects; approximate matching shown effective.
- Practical impact for domain adaptation and generative modeling.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited baselines; missing comparisons to Sinkhorn OT, Cramér distance, and strong domain adaptation methods.
- The “stronger gradients” claim is not fully substantiated across kernel bandwidths or multiscale MMD.
- “Fewer hyperparameters” is debatable; choice of distance metric and feature space can act as critical hyperparameters.
- High-dimensional behavior and perceptual quality not rigorously evaluated; no standard generation metrics.

Overall assessment: A solid, practically relevant paper that reframes empirical W1 as PMD and demonstrates meaningful advantages over MMD in optimization dynamics and batch efficiency. While the novelty is moderate and some claims need more robust empirical support, the contribution is useful to the community. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the MMD comparisons and adding a baseline like Sinkhorn or Cramér distance.
