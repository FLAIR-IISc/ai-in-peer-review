## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel estimator for the Wasserstein distance between probability distributions based on samples. The core idea – minimizing the cost of matching samples between distributions – is conceptually simple and provides a compelling alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a widely used divergence in machine learning. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, which is a valuable theoretical grounding. 

The paper effectively positions PMD within the existing landscape of sample-based divergence estimation, clearly outlining the limitations of MMD (bandwidth sensitivity, vanishing gradients, large batch size requirements) and how PMD addresses these issues. The theoretical analysis appears sound, and the experimental validation on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks provides initial evidence of PMD’s effectiveness. The comparisons to MMD are thorough and generally show PMD’s superior performance and faster convergence. The inclusion of a discussion on computational complexity and the proposed quadratic approximation for scalability are also positive contributions. 

However, I agree with the supervisor’s assessment that the experiments could be strengthened. Specifically, I’d like to see experiments on generative modeling with more complex, multi-modal datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10). The current experiments, while demonstrating improvements, do not fully stress-test PMD’s capabilities in scenarios where mode collapse or complex dependencies are present. A critical question raised is PMD’s behavior when the sample size N is smaller than the number of modes in the underlying distributions.  In such cases, the minimum weight matching might become unstable or less representative of the true distribution difference. Investigating this scenario experimentally is crucial. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Novel and conceptually simple approach to estimating Wasserstein distance.
*   Strong theoretical justification (consistency proof).
*   Clear presentation and well-structured paper.
*   Demonstrated improvements over MMD on standard tasks.
*   Practical consideration of computational complexity and approximation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding generative modeling with multi-modal distributions.
*   The behavior of PMD with N < number of modes warrants further investigation.



**Overall:** This is a good paper that proposes a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. While the experimental evaluation could be broadened, the theoretical contributions and initial results are promising. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the suggested experiments.
