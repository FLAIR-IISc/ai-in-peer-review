This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the distance between two probability distributions, and proposes its use as a training objective in deep learning. The core idea is to define the distance between two sets of samples as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between them. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (or Earth Mover's Distance). This work positions PMD as a direct competitor to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors claim several advantages for PMD over MMD, namely: the absence of a sensitive kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, the presence of stronger gradients during optimization, and the ability to use smaller mini-batches for stochastic gradient updates. These claims are evaluated in the context of two deep learning applications: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling. This work relates to a growing body of literature on using statistical divergences for training deep models, such as Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN), which use MMD, and Wasserstein GANs (WGAN), which approximate the Wasserstein distance via a learned critic network. PMD offers a third approach: a direct, non-parametric, and parameter-free (in the sense of not needing a critic network) estimator of the Wasserstein distance.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposal to use a direct, sample-based minimum weight matching estimator for the Wasserstein distance as a differentiable objective for training deep networks is novel and significant. It provides a principled and computationally tractable alternative to both kernel-based methods like MMD and adversarial methods like WGAN. The connection to optimal transport theory is well-established and clearly explained.

2.  **Practical Advantages:** The paper demonstrates a clear and practically important advantage of PMD: its ability to converge effectively with small mini-batch updates subsampled from a larger population. The experiments convincingly show that this leads to faster convergence compared to MMD, which typically requires large mini-batches (i.e., the population size) for stable estimation. This is a valuable contribution for practitioners seeking to train distribution-matching models more efficiently.

3.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is rigorously established. The proposed algorithm for parameter learning is straightforward and easy to understand. The empirical results on both domain adaptation and generative modeling are solid and generally support the claims of PMD being a competitive, and in some cases superior, alternative to MMD.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Overstated Claims on Hyperparameters and Gradients:**
    *   The claim that PMD has "fewer hyperparameters" is weak. PMD replaces the choice of kernel and its bandwidth (`sigma`) in MMD with the choice of a distance metric `d(·, ·)`. The paper uses the L1 distance, but this is a specific design choice. Other metrics, such as L2 or Mahalanobis distance, could be used and might be more appropriate in different settings. The choice of metric is a hyperparameter of similar importance to MMD's kernel choice. A more accurate statement would be that PMD avoids the specific issue of *tuning the kernel bandwidth*. It would strengthen the paper to compare against MMD where the bandwidth is set automatically using a standard heuristic (e.g., the median heuristic), which would make MMD effectively "hyperparameter-free" in practice and provide a fairer comparison.
    *   The claim of having "stronger gradients" is not fully convincing. The gradient behavior of MMD is highly sensitive to the kernel bandwidth. If `sigma` is poorly chosen (too large or too small), gradients will indeed vanish. The comparison in Figure 2(b) is likely against an MMD with a fixed `sigma` that may not be optimal. A thorough comparison would need to show that PMD's gradients are stronger than MMD's across a range of well-chosen `sigma` values, or when `sigma` is selected via a heuristic.

2.  **Lack of Discussion on High-Dimensional Spaces:** A critical weakness of the paper is the failure to discuss the behavior of PMD in high-dimensional spaces. It is well-known that distance metrics like L1 and L2 lose their meaningfulness in high dimensions (the "curse of dimensionality"). All points tend to become equidistant, which would make the matching problem ill-posed and the resulting distance estimate unreliable. MMD, through the kernel trick, implicitly maps data to a (potentially infinite-dimensional) RKHS where distances are better behaved, which is one of its primary strengths. The experiments use PMD on high-dimensional data (e.g., 4096-d VGG features), but the potential issues are not addressed. This is a fundamental limitation of PMD relative to MMD that deserves serious discussion.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** While the paper correctly notes that an O(N^2) approximate matching algorithm can be used, this is still computationally more demanding than the O(N^2) calculation for MMD, particularly due to higher constant factors. Although the authors argue this is acceptable compared to the cost of backpropagation, it remains a trade-off that should be clearly positioned as a potential disadvantage, especially as the population size `N` scales.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and principled method (PMD) for distribution matching based on the Wasserstein distance.
*   It demonstrates a significant practical advantage over MMD in terms of faster convergence due to its compatibility with small mini-batch updates.
*   The empirical results in domain adaptation and generative modeling are strong and show PMD is a viable and competitive approach.
*   The work provides a valuable new tool for researchers working on generative models and transfer learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The claims regarding fewer hyperparameters and stronger gradients compared to MMD are overstated and not fully substantiated by fair comparisons.
*   The paper completely ignores the critical issue of how the underlying distance metric behaves in high-dimensional spaces, a key problem that MMD is designed to mitigate.
*   The computational trade-offs, while mentioned, could be framed more clearly as a limitation.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and original contribution. The core idea of PMD and its empirical validation for faster training are significant. However, the paper would be much stronger if the authors tempered their claims about hyperparameters and gradients, and provided a thoughtful discussion of the fundamental limitations of using explicit distance metrics in high-dimensional spaces. Despite these weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh the negatives.
