This paper introduces the concept of Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable estimation of the distance between two distributions based on samples. PMD is proposed as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), which suffers from a sensitive kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, weak gradients, and large mini-batch size when used as a training objective. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and has several advantages over MMD, including no kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradient, and smaller mini-batch size for gradient-based optimization.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of PMD, establishing its connection to the Wasserstein metric and comparing its advantages and disadvantages with MMD. The authors also propose a stochastic gradient descent algorithm to learn parameters of the distributions using PMD. Empirical results demonstrate that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical foundations. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in practice.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The organization is logical, and the paper is easy to follow.

Originality: The problems or approaches are new, and the paper proposes a novel combination of familiar techniques. The authors clearly explain how this work differs from previous contributions and provide adequate references to related work.

Significance: The results are important, and other people (practitioners or researchers) are likely to use these ideas or build on them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a new and effective method for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples.
* The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-founded.
* The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in practice.
* The paper is well-written and well-organized.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The claim of stronger gradients is less convincing because MMD's behavior depends on the kernel bandwidth (sigma); comparisons should use best or a range of sigmas.
* The claim of fewer hyperparameters is weak: PMD depends on a distance metric that can introduce its own hyperparameters.
* High-dimensional distance estimation remains problematic, so PMD may face similar issues to MMD in high dimensions.

Overall, I believe that the paper is a valid contribution to the field and should be accepted. However, I would like to see the authors address the concerns mentioned above in the final version of the paper.
