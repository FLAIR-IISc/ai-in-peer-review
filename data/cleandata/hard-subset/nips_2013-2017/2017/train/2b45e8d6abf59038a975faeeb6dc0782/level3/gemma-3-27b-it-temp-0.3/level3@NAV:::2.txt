## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the distance between probability distributions, particularly in the context of deep learning. The core idea is to frame distribution matching as a minimum weight bipartite matching problem, demonstrating its consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein distance. The authors claim PMD overcomes MMD’s limitations – sensitivity to bandwidth, weak gradients, and large mini-batch requirements – and validate this through experiments in domain adaptation and generative modeling.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a known challenge in applying MMD to deep learning tasks. The theoretical connection established between PMD and the Wasserstein distance is a significant contribution, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate PMD’s advantage in training with smaller mini-batches, a practical benefit for large-scale models. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, with clear definitions and explanations of the core concepts.

However, the claim of “stronger gradients” is less persuasive. While the authors point to MMD’s potential for gradient saturation, the behavior of MMD is heavily dependent on the kernel bandwidth (σ). A more robust comparison would involve evaluating MMD across a range of σ values, including those selected using standard heuristics, effectively treating MMD as a less hyperparameter-sensitive method. The assertion of “fewer hyperparameters” is also weak; the choice of distance metric *within* PMD introduces its own hyperparameters, potentially offsetting the benefit of removing the kernel bandwidth. Furthermore, the paper acknowledges the challenges of distance estimation in high dimensions, suggesting PMD may inherit similar limitations to MMD in such settings.

The work builds upon existing literature in distribution matching (e.g., MMD, Wasserstein GANs) and appropriately references relevant publications. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the practical advantages of PMD. However, a comparison to more recent Wasserstein distance estimators beyond WGAN would strengthen the analysis. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to distribution matching with a strong theoretical grounding.
*   Demonstrated advantage in training with smaller mini-batches.
*   Clear presentation and well-conducted experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The claim of stronger gradients needs more rigorous justification.
*   The “fewer hyperparameters” argument is not entirely convincing.
*   Potential limitations in high-dimensional settings are not fully addressed.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valid and potentially useful improvement for fast training with small batches. While the claims regarding gradient strength and hyperparameter reduction are somewhat overstated, the core contribution of PMD as a sampled Wasserstein estimator is significant. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
