This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost (empirical W1) between two minibatches, and uses it as a differentiable training objective for deep models. The authors prove strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and evaluate PMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling, comparing primarily to MMD, and qualitatively to WGAN. The work relates to empirical optimal transport (Villani) and Wasserstein GANs (Arjovsky et al.), and to kernel two-sample tests (Gretton et al.) used in GMMNs.

Quality: The consistency result is standard from empirical OT stability and is correctly invoked. The practical algorithmic choices (Hungarian O(N^3) or approximate O(N^2) matching) are reasonable and the experiments on minibatch Wasserstein are interesting. However, two points need clarification: (i) the claimed “unbiased stochastic gradient” that ignores the dependence of the matching on θ is not established; PMD does not currently have a known unbiased gradient estimator, and the piecewise-constant matching induces non-smooth objectives. Please clarify whether your estimator is unbiased, a valid subgradient a.e., or a heuristic. (ii) The statement that MMD “requires large mini-batches” is misleading; the unbiased U-statistic estimator in “A Kernel Two-Sample Test” permits very small minibatches (even batch_size=2) and converges under SGD. Empirically, SVHN samples exhibit low diversity (e.g., digit ‘8’ dominates), and PMD-generated images are weaker than WGAN; a mixture-of-Gaussians experiment (including learning variances) would likely expose mode-seeking/low-entropy solutions when N is small, where the global minimum can differ from the true distribution.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. Please fix the minor typo (Line 141 “usally” → “usually”). It would help to more explicitly discuss limitations of both PMD and MMD, including variance/bias of estimators and minibatch-size trade-offs.

Originality: Using exact/approximate empirical OT matching as a training loss is a natural but useful instantiation. The novelty over prior empirical OT is in the deep-learning application and analysis, though related scalable OT (e.g., entropic regularization/Sinkhorn distances, Cuturi, NIPS 2013) should be discussed as competing accelerations; also see Bellemare et al. (Cramér distance) on biased Wasserstein gradients.

Significance: Gains on some domain adaptation tasks are promising, but generative results lag WGAN and show diversity issues. The approach is of interest, but broader comparisons and stress tests are needed.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear connection to W1 with consistency proof
- Interesting empirical study of minibatch Wasserstein
- Competitive domain adaptation results; simple objective without a critic

Cons for acceptance:
- Unclear gradient unbiasedness and non-smooth optimization
- Computational cost scales poorly with N; stronger baselines (WGAN, Sinkhorn) missing
- Generative samples show mode collapse/low diversity
- Overstated limitations of MMD; needs fair small-batch comparison
