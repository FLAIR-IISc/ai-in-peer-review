- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), estimating distribution distance via minimum-weight matching of sample populations.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3).  
- Positions PMD as a differentiable alternative to Wasserstein distance that avoids a separate critic network.  
- Claims practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements.  
- Empirical results show advantages in domain adaptation and generative modeling, with faster convergence and smaller mini-batch needs.  
- Paper is well-structured with clear definitions, pseudocode, helpful figures, and accessible theoretical analysis.  
- The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is questionable because PMD depends on the choice of distance metric (e.g., L1 vs L2).  
- The “stronger gradients” claim is not fully convincing; comparisons should use MMD with proper bandwidth selection (e.g., median heuristic).  
- Does not sufficiently address high-dimensional limitations where distance metrics lose meaning (curse of dimensionality).  
- Comparative analysis: PMD vs MMD comparison is generally fair but should include standard MMD bandwidth heuristics to be stronger.  
- Empirical generative results are promising, though some outputs (SVHN/LFW) appear blurry, indicating room for improvement.  
- Overall assessment: theoretically solid and practically useful alternative to MMD, with clear benefits for training deep models.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify hyperparameter claims, strengthen gradient comparisons, and discuss high-dimensional limitations.
