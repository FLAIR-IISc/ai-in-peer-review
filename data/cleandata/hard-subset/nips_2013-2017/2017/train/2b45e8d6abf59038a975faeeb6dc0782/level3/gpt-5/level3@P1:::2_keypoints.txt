- Paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based discrepancy using minimum-cost bipartite matching as a differentiable training objective for deep learning.

- PMD equals the empirical 1-Wasserstein (W1) distance; authors prove strong consistency to true W1 using standard OT results.

- PMD is positioned as an alternative to MMD, claiming to avoid kernel bandwidth selection, provide stronger gradients, and allow smaller SGD mini-batches.

- PMD is essentially empirical W1 via assignment (Hungarian/approximate matching), connecting to classical OT and recent ML uses (WGAN, Sinkhorn OT).

- Paper cites WGAN but omits discussion/comparison to Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi), weakening positioning versus entropic-regularized OT.

- Theoretical claims are correct and standard; the gradient argument avoids backprop through discrete matching but should discuss non-differentiability at permutation changes and subgradients.

- Algorithmic approach (exact O(N^3) and approximate O(N^2) matching) is appropriate; approximate matchings appear sufficient in practice.

- The “stronger gradients” claim is supported by analysis and histograms but is sensitive to kernel bandwidth choice and needs more thorough bandwidth studies.

- Experiments on domain adaptation (Office) show PMD competitive or better than MMD with faster convergence and convincing small-batch advantages.

- Generative modeling results (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) show PMD helps, especially in autoencoder feature space, but samples remain somewhat blurry and perceptual quality is limited.

- Missing experimental comparisons to strong baselines: DANN/JAN for adaptation; WGAN/WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn OT, and Cramér/energy distance for generation.

- Missing standard quantitative generation metrics (FID, precision/recall) to objectively assess sample quality.

- Paper clarity is good overall, but should standardize terminology (population N vs mini-batch |B|), state upfront that PMD = empirical W1, and include pseudocode and matching update details for reproducibility.

- Originality is moderate: empirical W1 is known, but packaging as PMD and practical training recipe with sub-sampling/approximate matching is a useful engineering contribution.

- Significance: PMD is a compelling alternative to MMD for small mini-batches and avoiding kernel tuning, but dependence on choice of distance d reintroduces hyperparameters and Euclidean distances can fail in high dimensions.

- Specific suggested experiments: compare MMD with median heuristic/multiscale mixtures, include Sinkhorn and Cramér baselines, report FID/precision-recall, and test sensitivity to L1/L2/cosine and feature-space distances.

- Implementation suggestions: discuss computational scaling with N and D, provide GPU implementations or trade-offs for higher-resolution images, and clarify bias from not differentiating through matching.

- Pros: principled link to Wasserstein distance, simple implementable objective, strong empirical evidence for small-batch efficiency, and useful analysis of gradient and batch-size effects.

- Cons: limited baselines (missing Sinkhorn/Cramér/DANN), incomplete substantiation of “stronger gradients” across bandwidths, debatable claim of fewer hyperparameters, and lacking rigorous high-dimensional/perceptual evaluation.

- Overall assessment: solid, practical paper reframing empirical W1 as PMD with meaningful optimization advantages; leans toward acceptance if MMD/Sinkhorn comparisons and stronger empirical support are added.
