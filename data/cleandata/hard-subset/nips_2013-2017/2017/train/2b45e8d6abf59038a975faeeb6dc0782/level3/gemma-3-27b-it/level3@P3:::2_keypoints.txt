- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to MMD for estimating distribution distance in deep learning.  
- Frames distribution comparison as a minimum weight bipartite matching problem and shows consistency as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Claims advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and effective use of smaller mini-batches for faster training.  
- Applies PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, reporting empirical improvements over MMD.  
- Correctly positions work within prior literature on MMD and Wasserstein distances, and contrasts PMD’s primal approach with WGANs’ dual/critic approach.  
- Theoretical analysis establishing PMD’s consistency as a Wasserstein estimator is a major contribution.  
- Empirical results convincingly show practical benefits, particularly reduced mini-batch sizes and training speed-ups.  
- Presentation is clear and pseudocode helps reproduce the method.  
- Gradient-strength comparison to MMD is potentially unfair because MMD’s kernel bandwidth (σ) was not thoroughly tuned or explored.  
- Claim of “fewer hyperparameters” is weak since PMD’s choice of distance metric (L1 in experiments) can introduce sensitivity or implicit hyperparameters.  
- Paper does not adequately address the curse of dimensionality; experiments are on relatively low-dimensional datasets (MNIST, SVHN, LFW features).  
- Lacks discussion of mitigation strategies for high-dimensional performance (e.g., dimensionality reduction).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, practical speed and mini-batch advantages, convincing empirical results, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unfair MMD comparison without bandwidth tuning, weak hyperparameter claim, limited treatment of high-dimensional limitations.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to include thorough MMD hyperparameter tuning and a more detailed discussion of high-dimensional behavior.
