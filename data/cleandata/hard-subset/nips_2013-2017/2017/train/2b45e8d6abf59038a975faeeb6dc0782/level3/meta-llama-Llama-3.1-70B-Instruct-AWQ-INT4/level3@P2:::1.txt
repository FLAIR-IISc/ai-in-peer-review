The paper "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning" proposes a novel method for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples, called Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD). PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.

The authors relate their work to previous research on maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) and the Wasserstein metric, highlighting the advantages of PMD over MMD, including fewer hyper-parameters, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes for gradient-based optimization. They also demonstrate the application of PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its theoretical foundations. The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in various tasks. However, there are some limitations and potential issues that need to be addressed.

One of the main concerns is that PMD requires a large mini-batch size N to estimate the Wasserstein distance for diverse, multimodal distributions. This can be a limitation in practice, especially when working with large datasets or limited computational resources. Additionally, the authors acknowledge that PMD may not work well with small N, as the optimization may have a global minimum different from the true distribution.

Another issue is that PMD does not have a known unbiased estimator of the gradient, which can make it difficult to train the model using stochastic gradient descent (SGD). In contrast, MMD can work with very small mini-batches (batch_size=2) using the unbiased estimator from "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" and can be trained with SGD to converge.

The generated images from PMD are not as good as those from Wasserstein GAN, which may indicate that PMD is not suitable for generative modeling tasks that require high-quality images. Furthermore, the SVHN samples exhibit low diversity, with the digit '8' appearing disproportionately often, which may not be representative of the true distribution.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel method for estimating the distance between two distributions, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, the idea of using the Wasserstein distance for distribution matching is not new, and the authors build upon previous research in this area.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential applications to various machine learning tasks, including domain adaptation and generative modeling. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in these tasks and provide a thorough analysis of its advantages and limitations.

In conclusion, the paper is well-written and proposes a novel method for estimating the distance between two distributions. However, there are some limitations and potential issues that need to be addressed, including the requirement for large mini-batch sizes and the lack of an unbiased estimator of the gradient. The authors should clarify the limitations of PMD and MMD and provide more insights into the potential applications and future directions of their work.
