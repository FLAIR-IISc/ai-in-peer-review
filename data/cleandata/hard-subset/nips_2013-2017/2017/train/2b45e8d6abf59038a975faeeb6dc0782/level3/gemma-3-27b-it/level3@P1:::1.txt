This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel method for estimating the distance between probability distributions based on samples, and proposes an algorithm for learning distributions using PMD as an objective. The core idea is to approximate the Wasserstein metric by finding the minimum weight matching between samples from two distributions. The authors claim PMD overcomes limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) – namely, sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and the need for large mini-batch sizes – and demonstrate improved performance on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

The work builds upon established concepts in statistical divergence estimation. MMD [Gretton et al., 2012] is a well-known non-parametric estimator, widely used in various deep learning applications.  Recent advancements have focused on utilizing the Wasserstein distance, notably through Wasserstein GANs [Arjovsky et al., 2017], to address the training instability issues of traditional GANs. This paper positions PMD as an alternative approach to approximating the Wasserstein metric, aiming to offer advantages in terms of computational efficiency and gradient behavior.  The connection to optimal transport theory and the theoretical grounding in strongly consistent estimation of the Wasserstein metric are strengths of the paper.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** PMD offers a fresh perspective on estimating distribution distances, moving away from kernel-based methods like MMD. The formulation based on optimal transport is theoretically sound and potentially offers benefits in gradient flow.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a proof demonstrating that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, lending credibility to the method.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate that PMD can outperform MMD. The convergence speed analysis provides evidence for the claimed benefits of stronger gradients.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and algorithms. The pseudocode is helpful for understanding the implementation.
*   **Addressing MMD Limitations:** The paper successfully identifies and attempts to address several known drawbacks of MMD, such as the bandwidth selection problem and the gradient vanishing issue.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors address the computational complexity, the O(N^2) or O(N^3) cost of the matching algorithm remains a potential bottleneck, especially for large datasets. The paper mentions GPU parallelization, but further details on the scalability would be beneficial.
*   **Mini-batch Sensitivity:** The discussion on mini-batch size suggests that PMD requires a reasonably large number of samples (though less than MMD) for reliable estimation. The performance of PMD with very small mini-batch sizes isn’t explored. While the authors show that convergence speed is improved with smaller batch sizes, a study of the relationship between batch size, population size, and final performance is missing.
*   **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The SVHN dataset, as mentioned in the paper, exhibits some biases (e.g., an overrepresentation of the digit '8'), which could influence the generative modeling results. Investigating performance on more diverse datasets is necessary.
*   **Gradient Analysis Detail:** The comparison of gradient magnitudes between PMD and MMD, while visually compelling, lacks a deeper quantitative analysis. A statistical comparison of gradient norms would strengthen the claim about stronger gradients.
*   **Lack of Unbiased Estimator:**  The absence of a known unbiased estimator for the gradient of PMD (compared to MMD's unbiased estimator discussed in Gretton et al, 2012) is a significant limitation, and the authors do not fully address the potential implications of this.  The reliance on the matching remaining stable during SGD updates could introduce instability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded method for estimating distribution distances.
*   The empirical results demonstrate that PMD can outperform MMD on relevant tasks.
*   The identified limitations of MMD and the attempt to address them are valuable contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the matching algorithm could limit scalability.
*   The lack of a detailed analysis of mini-batch size sensitivity is a concern.
*   The reliance on stable matching during optimization is a potential instability.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to distribution distance estimation. While the computational cost and potential instability are concerns, the theoretical justification and empirical results are encouraging. I believe the paper makes a worthwhile contribution to the field and is **weak accept**. 

I recommend the authors address the following points in a revision:

*   **Scalability:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the scalability of PMD, including experimental results on larger datasets or a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity.
*   **Mini-batch Sensitivity:** Investigate the performance of PMD with various mini-batch sizes and population sizes, and provide guidelines for choosing appropriate values.
*   **Gradient Stability:** Discuss the potential implications of the lack of an unbiased gradient estimator and how the assumption of stable matching is justified.
*   **Limitations Discussion:**  Explicitly discuss the limitations of both PMD and MMD in the conclusion, including potential failure cases and scenarios where one method might be preferred over the other.
*   **Typo:** Correct the typo on line 141 ("usally" to "usually").
