- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator using minimum-weight bipartite matching and proven strongly consistent with the first Wasserstein metric.
- Claims PMD outperforms MMD by having fewer hyperparameters, stronger gradients, and enabling smaller mini-batches for faster convergence, supported by theory and experiments (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW).
- Technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation and well-designed experiments demonstrating practical advantages of smaller mini-batch SGD.
- The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is tenuous because PMD requires choosing a distance metric (e.g., L1), trading one design choice for another; MMD bandwidth heuristics could neutralize this advantage.
- The “stronger gradient” claim is inconclusive since MMD gradient strength depends heavily on RBF bandwidth σ; comparisons appear to use a single σ without tuning or kernel mixtures.
- The paper lacks discussion of PMD’s potential limitations in high-dimensional spaces where explicit distance metrics suffer from the curse of dimensionality, unlike kernel-based MMD.
- Writing and organization are clear, figures/tables effective, and the work appears reproducible from provided details.
- Originality: presents a novel primal-form, sample-matching estimator distinct from kernel MMD and critic-based WGAN approaches.
- Significance: offers a practical, potentially impactful alternative to MMD by decoupling gradient mini-batch size from population size to speed training.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled novel method, significant practical advantage, strong empirical results, and clarity/reproducibility.
- Arguments against acceptance: some claims are overstated (hyperparameters, gradient strength) and the paper omits discussion of high-dimensional limitations.
- Overall recommendation: accept, with suggestions to temper claims about hyperparameters and gradient strength and add a discussion on high-dimensionality effects.
