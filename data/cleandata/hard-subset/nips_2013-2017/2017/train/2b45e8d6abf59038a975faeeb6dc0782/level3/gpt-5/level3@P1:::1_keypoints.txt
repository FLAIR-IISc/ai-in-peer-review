- PMD is the empirical first Wasserstein distance (W1) between two minibatches via minimum-weight bipartite matching.  
- The authors prove strong consistency of PMD for W1 using standard optimal transport results.  
- Paper provides practical algorithms (exact Hungarian O(N^3) and approximate O(N^2) Drake–Hougardy) and a recipe for reusing matchings in training.  
- Empirical claim: PMD gives stronger gradients than MMD, fewer hyperparameters, and faster convergence, especially with small SGD minibatches and larger population N.  
- Applications demonstrated: domain adaptation (matching source/target features) and training implicit generative models with some improvements over MMD.  
- Gradient-magnitude analysis is useful and supported by empirical histograms.  
- Novelty concern: using empirical W1 via matching is not conceptually new; need stronger positioning vs. entropic OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi 2013) which is GPU-friendly and O(N^2).  
- Theoretical/estimator concern: ignoring dependence of matching on parameters may hold a.e., but the practical inner-loop estimator that reuses stale matchings and subsamples is biased—bias/variance tradeoff and convergence need clarification.  
- MMD comparison is incomplete: an unbiased U-statistic estimator exists that works with very small minibatches; the claim that MMD requires large batches should be qualified or re-evaluated.  
- Sample-complexity worry: empirical error scales ~N^-0.23 in 5D, implying W1-based PMD may need large N for stable gradients in high-dim, multimodal data.  
- Generative results show blurrier outputs and low diversity (e.g., overproduction of digit ‘8’ on SVHN); quantitative metrics (FID/IS) and class-frequency analysis are missing.  
- Potential failure mode: matching can encourage low-entropy/variance-shrinking solutions when N is small; controlled Gaussian-mixture diagnostics are needed.  
- Computational bottleneck: matching can be expensive for larger N; GPU benchmarks and comparison to Sinkhorn OT are necessary for scalability claims.  
- Missing baselines: lacks comparisons to WGAN/Cramér distance and Sinkhorn-based GANs, which are natural W1-style competitors.  
- Clarity: generally well written but some claims (e.g., “unbiased gradient”, “MMD requires large mini-batch”) should be qualified; minor typo noted.  
- Technical soundness: core consistency result is reasonable, but the practical biased estimator needs more theoretical/empirical analysis.  
- Experimental evaluation: suggestive but incomplete—missing key baselines, quantitative generative metrics, and multimodal diagnostics.  
- Originality: moderate—main idea reduces to empirical W1; contribution lies in integrating it into DL workflows and empirical observations.  
- Significance: potentially useful as a simple alternative to MMD, but impact limited without stronger baselines and scalability/complexity analysis.  
- Suggested improvements: compare to entropic OT/Sinkhorn and WGAN/Cramér, re-evaluate MMD with unbiased estimators, add FID/IS and class analyses, run Gaussian-mixture failure tests, analyze bias from stale matching, and provide GPU implementations/benchmarks.  
- Pros for acceptance: simple, parameter-light loss; clear W1 connection and consistency proof; empirical evidence of stronger gradients and sometimes faster convergence.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited novelty vs. existing OT, unaddressed estimator bias and sample-complexity issues, and generative results lacking diversity and quantitative evaluation.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject (borderline)—promising idea but needs stronger baselines, clearer treatment of estimator bias/sample complexity, and more thorough evaluation.
