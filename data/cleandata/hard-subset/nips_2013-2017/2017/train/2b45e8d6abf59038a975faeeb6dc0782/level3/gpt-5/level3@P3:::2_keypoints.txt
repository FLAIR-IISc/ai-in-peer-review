- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): minimum-weight bipartite matching between finite samples as an empirical estimator of W1, trained with SGD that alternates fixed matchings and gradient steps without backpropagating through matching.

- Positions PMD as a critic-free alternative to MMD and WGAN, replacing kernelization with sample-to-sample matching and relating to empirical OT with uniform weights; links to Sinkhorn/energy/Cramér distances need more discussion.

- Strength: Simple, direct empirical estimator of W1 with a standard consistency proof.

- Strength: Practical training recipe that reuses matchings and tolerates approximate O(N^2) matching.

- Strength: Empirical evidence that PMD trains effectively with smaller mini-batches and yields faster wall-clock convergence in tested setups.

- Strength: Competitive domain adaptation results and reasonable generative modeling outcomes; pixel-space samples less noisy than MMD.

- Strength: Implementation details and ablations (population size, mini-batch, matching exactness) support reproducibility.

- Weakness: Claim of stronger gradients than MMD is not convincingly established—MMD gradients depend on kernel bandwidth and multi-kernel setups could close the gap.

- Weakness: “Fewer hyperparameters” claim is weak because PMD requires choosing a ground distance and possibly a feature space, introducing hidden hyperparameters.

- Weakness: PMD inherits high-dimensional distance concentration problems; advantages over well-tuned MMD may diminish in high-D spaces.

- Weakness: Computational and memory costs are significant (N×N distance matrix); clearer wall-clock and GPU memory comparisons, and comparison to Sinkhorn-OT, are needed.

- Weakness: Theoretical novelty is modest—consistency follows from classic OT theory and there are no finite-sample bounds or gradient stability analyses.

- Weakness: Some claims (e.g., “unbiased stochastic gradient” when freezing matching) need more careful justification.

- Weakness: Empirical baselines are incomplete—missing comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn-OT, energy/Cramér distance, and MMD-GAN.

- Quality: PMD–W1 connection and consistency are correct; training procedure is sensible, but some claims are overstated without stronger MMD tuning.

- Empirics: Small-mini-batch advantage is the most convincing empirical finding; domain adaptation is strong while generative results are mixed and blur in pixel space.

- Clarity: Paper is generally well written; algorithmic description and ablations are clear, but clarifications needed on subgradient treatment, scaling/GPU feasibility, and MMD tuning protocol.

- Originality: Idea is natural and known (empirical OT via matching); contribution lies in practicalizing it for SGD with empirical small-batch benefits—novelty is moderate.

- Significance: Useful practitioner tool as a critic-free alternative that speeds small-batch training, but impact is limited by metric choice dependence in high-dimensional raw spaces.

- Suggestions: Compare MMD under median heuristic and multi-kernel, report best-over-σ; treat MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free under standard heuristics in discussion.

- Suggestions: Add comparisons to Sinkhorn-OT and WGAN/WGAN-GP (and Cramér), include wall-clock and sample-quality metrics.

- Suggestions: Explore learned feature-space distances, report sensitivity to metric choices, and provide memory/runtime scaling plots for increasing N and D with GPU implementations.

- Pros for acceptance: Principled W1-linked objective, strong small-batch speed evidence, competitive domain adaptation and reasonable generative results without a critic.

- Cons for acceptance: Overstated claims about gradients and hyperparameters, limited theoretical novelty, missing strong OT/adversarial baselines, and high-D metric limitations.

- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept—valid, practical alternative with caveats that should be addressed (temper claims, broaden baselines, discuss high-D limitations).
