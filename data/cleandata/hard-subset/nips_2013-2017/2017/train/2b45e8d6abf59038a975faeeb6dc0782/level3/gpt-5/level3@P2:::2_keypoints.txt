- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): sample-based objective computing minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. populations, equivalent to empirical 1-Wasserstein (EMD).
- Uses PMD as a differentiable training objective by backpropagating through matched pairs while holding discrete assignment fixed (arguing local constancy).
- Claims practical advantages over MMD: no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches than matching population.
- Empirical results: competitive or improved performance on Office-31 domain adaptation and qualitative improvements/convergence speedups for generative modeling on MNIST, SVHN, LFW.
- Situates work within prior OT and kernel two-sample literature; equivalence to empirical Wasserstein and related fast/differentiable OT methods are classical.
- Strength: clear formulation and practical optimization scheme for empirical OT via matching.
- Strength: useful empirical insight that larger matching populations with smaller SGD mini-batches can speed convergence.
- Strength: solid application results and practical O(N^2) matching that works well with the freeze-M heuristic.
- Weakness: limited theoretical novelty—consistency follows from standard OT results with no finite-sample rates or generalization bounds provided.
- Weakness: MMD comparisons and gradient-strength claims are not fully convincing without careful bandwidth/multi-kernel baselines (e.g., median heuristic, best-over-bandwidths).
- Weakness: “fewer hyperparameters” is debatable since PMD depends on ground metric and feature space choices, which are critical in high dimensions.
- Weakness: missing key baselines—entropic-regularized (Sinkhorn) OT, WGAN/WGAN-GP, Cramér distance for generative modeling, and DANN/CMD for domain adaptation.
- Weakness: ignoring derivatives through the assignment yields a subgradient almost everywhere, and the paper does not quantify bias from holding M fixed or subsampling matched pairs.
- Quality: reduction to empirical 1-Wasserstein and consistency claim are correct; optimization heuristic is reasonable but lacks bias/variance analysis and high-dimensional sensitivity study.
- Empirics: promising domain adaptation transfers and convergence speedups, but generative results lack quantitative metrics (FID/IS) and comparisons to modern baselines; compute budget fairness unclear.
- Clarity: generally well written; should explicitly state assumptions for local constancy of argmin matching, clarify unequal dataset sampling, note PMD is an estimator (finite-N metric issues), and detail approximate matching effects on gradients.
- Originality: practical algorithmic package is useful, but core distance and theoretical results are standard—novelty is mainly empirical/pragmatic.
- Significance: potentially useful practical alternative to MMD for small-batch training, but broader impact uncertain without comparisons to Sinkhorn, WGAN(-GP), and Cramér distance.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to ground metric/feature space; experiments with learned/perceptual metrics; quantitative generative metrics and comparisons to WGAN(-GP)/Sinkhorn; MMD with median heuristic or multi-kernel; quantify bias from freezing M and subsampling.
- Suggestions: add baselines (Sinkhorn, WGAN(-GP), Cramér, DANN/CMD), use standard bandwidth heuristics and sensitivity plots for MMD, report FID/IS and matched compute budgets, and discuss sample-complexity rates relative to theoretical results.
- Arguments for acceptance: simple, practical objective with clear implementation and useful behavior for small-batch training; competitive domain adaptation and improved qualitative generative samples.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited theoretical novelty, missing key baselines and quantitative metrics, and unsubstantiated claims about gradients and hyperparameter simplicity.
- Overall: promising and practically useful approach that improves upon MMD in some regimes, but currently solid yet borderline for acceptance without stronger baselines and more thorough evaluation.
