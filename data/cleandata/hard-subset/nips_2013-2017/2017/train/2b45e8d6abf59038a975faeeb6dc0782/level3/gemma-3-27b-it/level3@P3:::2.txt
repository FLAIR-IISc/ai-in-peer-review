This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the distance between probability distributions, particularly within the context of deep learning. The core idea is to frame distribution comparison as a minimum weight bipartite matching problem, demonstrating its consistency as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors claim advantages including elimination of a kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to effectively utilize smaller mini-batches – a key benefit for training speed. The method is applied to domain adaptation and generative modeling, with empirical results showing improvements over MMD.

The paper builds upon existing work in statistical divergence estimation, notably MMD [Gretton et al., 2012], which has become a standard technique in various deep learning applications like domain adaptation [Ben-David et al., 2010] and generative modeling [Dziugaite et al., 2015]. The paper correctly positions PMD within this landscape, acknowledging MMD’s strengths while addressing its limitations. The connection to the Wasserstein metric [Rubner et al., 2000; Villani, 2008] is well-established and provides a strong theoretical foundation. Recent related work utilizing Wasserstein distances, such as WGANs [Arjovsky et al., 2017], is also acknowledged, although the differences in approach (primal vs. dual estimation and lack of a critic network in PMD) are highlighted.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The theoretical analysis establishing the consistency of PMD as a Wasserstein metric estimator is valuable. The empirical demonstration, particularly regarding the ability to use smaller mini-batches, is convincing and practically significant. The speed-up observed in training, coupled with comparable or improved performance, makes PMD a potentially attractive alternative to MMD. The presentation is generally clear, and the pseudocode provides a useful overview of the implementation.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the claim of "stronger gradients" is made, the comparison to MMD is not entirely fair. MMD’s performance is heavily dependent on the kernel bandwidth (σ), and the authors don't demonstrate results across a range of optimized σ values. A proper comparison would involve tuning σ for MMD using standard heuristics (e.g., median distance) or reporting results across a spectrum of bandwidths to illustrate the sensitivity. Treating MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free, as suggested by standard practice, might reveal a less dramatic gradient advantage for PMD.

The claim of "fewer hyperparameters" is also weak. While PMD avoids the kernel bandwidth of MMD, it relies on a distance metric (L1 in the experiments) which *can* introduce its own hyperparameters or sensitivity to the choice of metric, particularly in high-dimensional spaces. The choice of L1 is justified, but the potential for alternative distance metrics to influence performance should be acknowledged.

A significant concern is the potential for PMD to suffer from the “curse of dimensionality.” Like MMD, estimating distances in high-dimensional spaces remains a challenging problem. The paper doesn't adequately address this limitation; the empirical results focus on relatively low-dimensional datasets (MNIST, SVHN, LFW features). While the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, a discussion of potential mitigation strategies (e.g., dimensionality reduction) would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to distribution matching.
*   Demonstrated practical benefit of smaller mini-batch sizes for faster training.
*   Convincing empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Unfair comparison of gradient strength to MMD without proper bandwidth tuning.
*   Weak claim of fewer hyperparameters; distance metric choice can be critical.
*   Limited discussion of high-dimensional performance concerns.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valid and potentially useful improvement to existing methods for distribution matching, particularly for scenarios where fast training with small batches is crucial. The theoretical grounding is solid, and the empirical results are encouraging. However, the limitations regarding the comparison to MMD and the potential issues in high dimensions need to be addressed. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above, specifically a more thorough comparison to MMD with appropriate hyperparameter tuning and a more detailed discussion of the method's behavior in high-dimensional settings.
