- Paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a Wasserstein-based minibatch matching distance estimator.  
- Authors prove PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3).  
- Empirical applications shown in domain adaptation and generative modeling.  
- Strength: clean theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric.  
- Strength: PMD avoids MMD’s kernel bandwidth hyperparameter and is claimed to work with smaller minibatches.  
- Empirical claim: PMD outperforms MMD on certain domain adaptation tasks (a→w, a→d) and converges faster.  
- Major limitation: PMD requires large minibatches (N=1000+) for reliable estimation on multimodal distributions.  
- Evidence: SVHN results show low diversity and poor image quality, indicating multimodal failure and need for large N.  
- Critical missing point: no unbiased gradient estimator like MMD’s batch_size=2; PMD’s claim of “normal mini-batch size” is misleading.  
- Missing experiments: failure modes such as matching mixtures of Gaussians (including learning variances) are not tested.  
- Incorrect / overstated empirical claim: PMD-generated images are claimed better than WGAN but are actually blurrier.  
- Overall verdict: technically sound but practical limitations (large N, poor multimodal performance) outweigh contributions; MMD remains more practical.  
- Minor correction: typo “usally” → “usually.”
