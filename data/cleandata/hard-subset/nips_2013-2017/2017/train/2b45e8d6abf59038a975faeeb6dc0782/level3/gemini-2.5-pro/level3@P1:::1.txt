This paper proposes the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein distance, and demonstrates its use as a training objective for deep learning models. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples (or "populations") drawn from two distributions. The paper proves that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the true Wasserstein-1 distance. To use PMD as an objective, the parameters of the underlying generative models are updated via stochastic gradient descent, where the gradient is computed by backpropagating through the distance term of the matched pairs, while treating the matching itself as fixed. The authors relate PMD to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) and Wasserstein GANs (WGAN), arguing that PMD avoids the kernel hyperparameter tuning of MMD and the need for a separate critic network as in WGAN. The method is evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Simplicity:** The core idea of using a direct, sample-based approximation of the primal form of the Wasserstein distance as a differentiable training objective is novel and conceptually appealing. It provides a parameter-free alternative to the dual-form approximation used in WGAN, which requires training a separate, carefully constrained critic network, and a non-kernel-based alternative to MMD.

2.  **Theoretical Grounding:** The paper provides a clear theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein-1 metric, proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator. This places the method on a solid theoretical foundation.

3.  **Empirical Demonstration:** The authors apply PMD to two relevant deep learning problems. The results on the Office dataset for domain adaptation are promising, showing that PMD can outperform MMD on some tasks and converge faster. This suggests the method may be effective in settings where feature distributions need to be aligned.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **High Sample Complexity and Potential for Mode Collapse:** The quality of the PMD as an estimator for the true Wasserstein distance is highly dependent on the population size N. For high-dimensional and multimodal distributions, such as those of natural images, a very large N is required for the sample-based matching to accurately reflect the geometry of the true distributions. With a small N, the optimization objective may be a poor proxy for the true distance. This can lead to a global minimum that does not correspond to the target distribution. For example, the optimizer might find it easier to reduce the matching cost by generating samples with low diversity (mode collapse), rather than matching a complex, multimodal target distribution.

    This weakness appears to be reflected in the generative modeling experiments. The generated SVHN samples in Figure 4 show a significant lack of diversity, with the digit '8' appearing disproportionately often. The generated images are also qualitatively inferior to those from other contemporary generative models like WGAN, which is a highly relevant baseline that is not compared against.

2.  **Questionable Claims Regarding MMD:** The paper's claimed advantages over MMD are not entirely convincing.
    *   The authors argue that MMD requires a large mini-batch size (~1000) to get a reliable estimate, whereas PMD can be optimized with a smaller SGD mini-batch (`|B|`) than its population size (`N`). This is a misleading comparison. MMD has a well-known unbiased U-statistic estimator that allows for effective training with very small mini-batches (e.g., as small as 2). This is standard practice and is described in Gretton et al., "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" (NIPS 2012). Thus, the claim that PMD has an advantage in its mini-batch size requirement is not well-supported.
    *   The argument about MMD's "weak gradients" due to kernel saturation is a known issue, but it is often mitigated in practice by using a mixture of kernels with different bandwidths, which the authors themselves employ in their MMD baseline. The empirical gradient distribution in Figure 2(b) is interesting but may not fully capture the dynamics of optimization over many iterations.

3.  **Biased Gradient Estimator:** The paper computes the gradient of the PMD objective by fixing the optimal matching M* and differentiating only the distance term. While the matching may not change for an infinitesimal parameter update, the stochastic gradient computed on a mini-batch is likely a biased estimator for the gradient of the true expected objective, E[DN(X, Y)]. The optimal matching M* is a function of the entire populations X and Y, which in turn depend on the model parameters θ. Ignoring this dependency in the gradient calculation introduces bias. A more rigorous analysis of the properties of this stochastic gradient estimator is needed.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

*   The authors should provide a more thorough discussion of the limitations of PMD, especially concerning the sample complexity (the population size N) needed to accurately approximate the Wasserstein distance for complex distributions. A toy experiment showing how PMD behaves when matching a unimodal to a multimodal Gaussian distribution for various N would be very insightful.
*   The comparison with MMD should be revised to reflect standard practices for MMD optimization, particularly regarding the use of unbiased estimators with small batch sizes.
*   The generative modeling results should be compared against stronger and more relevant baselines, such as WGAN, to better position the contribution of PMD.
*   The quality of the generated samples could potentially be improved by using a more perceptually relevant distance metric `d(·, ·)` in the feature space of a pre-trained network, rather than L1 distance in pixel space.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel, simple, and theoretically-grounded method for estimating distribution distance. The application to domain adaptation shows promising results.
*   **Arguments against acceptance:** The method appears to have significant limitations for complex generative modeling tasks, as evidenced by low-quality samples and signs of mode collapse. Key claims about advantages over MMD are based on a questionable setup. The gradient estimator is likely biased.

Overall, while the core idea of PMD is interesting, the current experimental validation and analysis are not sufficient to demonstrate its effectiveness as a general-purpose tool, especially in comparison to strong existing methods. The weaknesses regarding sample complexity and the questionable comparison to MMD are significant concerns.
