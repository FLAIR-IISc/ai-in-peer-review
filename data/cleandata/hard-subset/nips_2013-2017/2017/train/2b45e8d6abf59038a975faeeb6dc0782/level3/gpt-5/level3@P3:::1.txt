Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-cost bipartite matching cost between two size-N minibatches drawn from two distributions, using a chosen ground metric (L1 in the experiments). PMD is exactly the empirical first Wasserstein distance between the two empirical (minibatch) measures; the authors prove strong consistency: as N grows, PMD converges almost surely to the true W1 distance. They propose using PMD as a differentiable training objective by fixing the optimal matching and backpropagating through the paired distances, and compare it to MMD and adversarial/Wasserstein approaches in domain adaptation and generative modeling. Exact matching is computed with the Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)); an O(N^2) approximation is also used in practice.

The work is closely related to optimal transport/earth mover’s distance and recent uses in deep learning (e.g., WGAN), as well as MMD-based training (GMMN). Unlike WGAN, PMD does not require a critic; unlike MMD, PMD avoids kernel bandwidth selection and yields stronger pointwise gradients.

Strengths
- Clear formulation: PMD as empirical W1 with minimum-weight matching, with a clean consistency result via standard OT stability.
- Practical contribution: a minibatch-based algorithm with either exact or approximate matching; experiments show it is feasible on CPU and could be further accelerated.
- Empirical interest: the study of computing Wasserstein on minibatches and using it directly as an objective is timely; the domain adaptation results are competitive against MMD and converge faster.
- Sensible analysis: discussion of gradient magnitude (PMD vs MMD) and ablations on N, |B|, exact vs approximate matching.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Sample complexity and minibatch bias: Empirical W1 in high dimension has slow convergence rates (∼N^−1/d), requiring large N to faithfully capture diverse, multimodal distributions. The paper acknowledges using N≈1000–4000 in places; this limits practicality and can bias training toward lower-entropy, underdispersed solutions with small N. A concrete likely failure case is matching mixtures of Gaussians while learning both means and variances: finite-N PMD encourages variance shrinkage (lower entropy) because pointwise matching rewards peaky generators, potentially yielding a global minimum different from the true distribution.
- Generated sample quality: On SVHN and LFW, PMD samples are blurrier and less diverse than WGAN; on SVHN, digit “8” appears disproportionately often, suggesting mode undercoverage. This weakens the generative modeling claim relative to state of the art.
- Gradient unbiasedness and differentiability: The paper states an “unbiased stochastic gradient” while stopping gradient through the discrete matching (Eq. 3). Because the matching is a function of parameters and samples, ignoring its dependency typically yields a biased gradient estimator. In contrast, MMD admits unbiased and linear-time estimators of MMD^2 (Gretton et al., “A Kernel Two-Sample Test”) that can be trained with very small minibatches (even batch_size=2) using SGD to converge. The comparison claiming MMD “requires large mini-batch size” is therefore misleading unless one fixes a biased, quadratic estimator and does not use the known linear/unbiased variants.
- Comparisons and baselines: WGAN (and related OT relaxations such as entropic Sinkhorn) provide scalable, differentiable OT surrogates and deliver higher-fidelity images. The paper would benefit from a stronger baseline comparison (e.g., WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn divergences) and from reporting diversity metrics (e.g., FID/IS).
- Computational costs: Matching requires O(N^2) distances and O(N^3) or O(N^2) matching per population, which can dominate training for practical N. While acceptable in some regimes, this is a heavier overhead than kernel MMD’s O(N^2) with small constants or the linear-time unbiased MMD estimator. GPU implementations are suggested but not provided.
- Objective choice: L1 in pixel space is known to produce blur; this likely contributes to the visual results. Using learned perceptual features or code space helps, but then PMD’s advantage over feature-MMD or adversarial methods is less clear.

Quality
- The theoretical result (strong consistency) is correct but standard given the OT literature; no finite-sample bounds or variance analyses are provided. Claims about “unbiased gradients” need correction or proof. Empirical results support faster convergence than MMD in some settings, but generative quality lags behind stronger baselines.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, the gradient discussion should be more careful about the non-differentiability of the matching and the implications for estimator bias. Please clarify the precise estimators used for MMD (biased vs unbiased, quadratic vs linear-time) and for PMD (whether any control variates or averaging over matchings are used).

Originality
- PMD is essentially empirical W1 with matching; the contribution is to package it as a training objective with some empirical analysis. Relative to WGAN and Sinkhorn-based OT, the methodological novelty is modest but the framing is useful.

Significance
- As a practical alternative to kernel MMD, PMD is a useful baseline and offers an appealing “no-critic” OT objective with strong per-sample gradients. However, the need for large N in high dimension, lack of unbiased gradient estimators, and inferior generative quality limit impact. The domain adaptation gains are promising but incremental.

Requests for clarification and additional experiments
- Please correct or justify the “unbiased stochastic gradient” claim; if the matching depends on parameters, the estimator is typically biased. Can you characterize this bias or show it vanishes as N→∞?
- Provide fair MMD baselines using the unbiased/linear-time estimator and demonstrate training with very small minibatches (e.g., 2–32).
- Include a controlled MoG experiment (including learning variances) to probe variance shrinkage/entropy collapse under finite-N PMD.
- Compare to entropic OT/Sinkhorn divergences, which provide differentiable transport plans and scalable approximations, and to WGAN-GP or Cramér distance for stability/quality.
- Report diversity metrics (FID/IS) and class balance (e.g., SVHN digit frequencies) to quantify the observed “8”-dominance and low diversity.
- Discuss the dependence on N and |B| for multimodal, high-dimensional data and provide guidance on practical choices.

Minor
- Typo: line 141 “usally” → “usually”.
- PMD is “parameter free” only modulo the choice of ground metric and population/batch sizes; please nuance this claim.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated objective; solid theoretical grounding as empirical OT.
- Interesting and timely experiments showing feasibility and some advantages over vanilla MMD.
- Clear analysis of gradient magnitudes and convergence.

Cons for acceptance
- Inferior generative quality and diversity vs WGAN; mode undercoverage on SVHN.
- Mischaracterization of MMD’s minibatch requirements; lack of unbiased gradient estimator for PMD.
- Heavy computation and no GPU implementation; limited comparison to modern OT relaxations.

Overall recommendation: Borderline reject. The idea is sound and the experiments are interesting, but the empirical advantages over stronger baselines are limited, and important limitations (finite-N bias, gradient estimator, fair baselines) need to be addressed.
