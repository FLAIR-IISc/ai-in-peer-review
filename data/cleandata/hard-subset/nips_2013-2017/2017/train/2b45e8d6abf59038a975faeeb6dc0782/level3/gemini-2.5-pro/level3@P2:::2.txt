### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel, sample-based estimate of the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between samples drawn from the two distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The primary motivation is to provide a superior alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for training deep learning models, particularly in domain adaptation and generative modeling. The paper claims that PMD overcomes key limitations of MMD by having fewer hyperparameters, providing stronger gradients for optimization, and enabling the use of smaller mini-batches during training, leading to faster convergence. These claims are supported by theoretical analysis and empirical results on standard benchmarks for domain adaptation (Office dataset) and image generation (MNIST, SVHN, LFW).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The connection drawn between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is well-established and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The experiments are well-designed and cover relevant applications, providing a direct comparison to MMD-based approaches like Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN). The empirical evidence strongly supports the claim that PMD allows for faster training by using smaller mini-batches for the gradient updates than the full population size, which is a significant practical advantage.

However, some of the claims regarding PMD's superiority over MMD could be better substantiated:

1.  **"Fewer hyperparameters":** This claim is tenuous. The authors argue PMD avoids the kernel bandwidth parameter `σ` required by MMD. However, PMD introduces a dependency on the choice of the distance metric `d(·, ·)`. While the paper uses the L1 distance, this is itself a design choice. Other metrics like L2, or more complex, parameterized metrics could be used. A more balanced comparison would acknowledge this trade-off or compare against MMD using a standard bandwidth selection heuristic (e.g., the median heuristic), which would also render MMD effectively "hyperparameter-free".

2.  **"Stronger gradient":** The analysis of gradient strength in Figure 2(b) is interesting but potentially inconclusive. The gradient of MMD with an RBF kernel is highly sensitive to the bandwidth `σ`. The presented comparison appears to be for a single choice of `σ`. Without knowing if this `σ` was optimal for the MMD baseline, it is difficult to conclude that PMD's gradients are inherently stronger. The claim would be more convincing if it were shown to hold against MMD with a well-tuned `σ` or a mixture of kernels with different bandwidths.

3.  **High-Dimensionality:** The paper does not discuss the potential limitations of PMD in high-dimensional spaces. PMD relies on an explicit distance metric (e.g., L1) in the native feature space. Such metrics are known to behave poorly in high dimensions (curse of dimensionality), which may limit PMD's effectiveness. In contrast, MMD, via the kernel trick, operates in a potentially richer reproducing kernel Hilbert space, which can be an advantage. A discussion of this aspect would provide a more complete picture of the method's scope.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and clearly organized. The definition of PMD, its connection to the Wasserstein metric, and the proposed learning algorithm are explained lucidly. The figures and tables effectively support the paper's claims and experimental results. The work should be reproducible given the details provided.

**Originality:** The use of the Wasserstein distance in deep learning is not new (e.g., Wasserstein GANs). However, this paper proposes a direct, primal-form estimator based on optimal transport's sample matching formulation. This is a novel approach that is distinct from both the kernel-based MMD and the dual-formulation, critic-based approach of WGAN. It presents a new, practical tool for distribution matching in deep learning.

**Significance:** The work is significant and likely to be impactful. It offers a compelling and practical alternative to MMD, a cornerstone technique in this area. The demonstrated ability to accelerate training by decoupling the gradient mini-batch size from the population size is a major practical contribution. Researchers and practitioners working on generative models and domain adaptation are likely to find PMD useful and build upon these ideas.

### Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Method:** PMD is a novel, intuitive, and theoretically grounded method for estimating distribution distances, based on a consistent estimation of the Wasserstein metric.
2.  **Significant Practical Advantage:** The ability to use small mini-batches for SGD updates leads to demonstrably faster convergence, a highly valuable property for training large deep learning models.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method outperforms or is competitive with MMD-based baselines in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally clear and provides a solid basis for others to implement and use the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Overstated Claims:** The claims of superiority regarding hyperparameters and gradient strength are not fully convincing and would benefit from more rigorous and nuanced comparisons with MMD.
2.  **Lack of Discussion on Limitations:** The paper does not address the potential challenges of using an explicit distance metric in high-dimensional spaces, which is a key consideration for a method intended for deep learning applications.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and valuable method. While some of the claims against MMD are slightly overstated, the core contribution—a practical, Wasserstein-based discrepancy measure that enables faster training—is significant and well-supported by the experiments. The weaknesses are addressable and do not detract from the overall merit of the work. I recommend acceptance. I encourage the authors to tone down the claims about hyperparameters and gradient strength in the final version and to add a brief discussion on the potential effects of high dimensionality on the chosen distance metric.
