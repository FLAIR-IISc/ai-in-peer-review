This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel and theoretically grounded method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and propose an algorithm for learning distributions using PMD as an objective function. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and offers a compelling alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a widely used technique in deep learning.

The core idea behind PMD – finding the minimum weight matching between samples from two distributions – is conceptually simple yet powerful. This simplicity translates into practical advantages over MMD, specifically the elimination of a bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients during training, and the ability to utilize standard mini-batch sizes. The theoretical analysis establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric is a significant contribution. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature on statistical divergence estimation, citing relevant papers on MMD, Wasserstein distance, and optimal transport.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The technical soundness is evident in the rigorous theoretical derivation and the clarity of the proposed algorithm. The writing is exceptionally clear, making the paper accessible to a broad audience within the NIPS community. The experimental validation, encompassing both domain adaptation and generative modeling, provides strong empirical support for the claims. The comparison with MMD is thorough and convincingly demonstrates PMD’s advantages in terms of both performance and convergence speed. The authors also acknowledge and address the computational cost of the matching problem, proposing an efficient approximation that maintains performance. Furthermore, the inclusion of a detailed analysis of gradient behavior (Fig. 2b) offers valuable insight into the method’s benefits.

However, some potential weaknesses and areas for improvement could be addressed. While the experiments on generative modeling are promising, they are limited to relatively simple datasets (MNIST, SVHN, LFW). A key limitation of generative models is their ability to capture multi-modal distributions. The reviewer wonders how PMD would perform when the true data distribution exhibits a significantly larger number of modes than the sample size *N*. In such scenarios, the minimum weight matching might be prone to suboptimal pairings, potentially leading to mode collapse or inaccurate distance estimation. Experiments demonstrating PMD’s performance on more complex, multi-modal datasets would strengthen the paper considerably.

Additionally, while the authors mention the possibility of GPU acceleration for the matching algorithms, they do not provide any empirical results demonstrating the speedup achieved. Including such results would further highlight the practical applicability of PMD, particularly for large-scale datasets.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** PMD presents a novel approach to estimating distributional distance, offering a compelling alternative to established methods like MMD.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The strong consistency proof establishes a solid theoretical foundation for PMD.
*   **Practical Advantages:** PMD addresses key limitations of MMD, offering improvements in hyperparameter sensitivity, gradient behavior, and mini-batch size requirements.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate PMD's effectiveness in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Generative Modeling Experiments:** The lack of experiments on complex, multi-modal datasets raises concerns about PMD's performance in challenging scenarios.
*   **Lack of GPU Acceleration Results:** The absence of empirical evidence demonstrating GPU acceleration limits the assessment of practical scalability.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of distributional distance estimation. While the generative modeling experiments could be more comprehensive, the strong theoretical foundation, clear writing, and compelling empirical results justify acceptance. The potential practical benefits of PMD, particularly its improved gradient behavior and reduced hyperparameter sensitivity, make it a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners in deep learning. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the concerns raised regarding generative modeling and GPU acceleration. Specifically, adding experiments with more complex datasets and reporting GPU speedups would further strengthen the paper.
