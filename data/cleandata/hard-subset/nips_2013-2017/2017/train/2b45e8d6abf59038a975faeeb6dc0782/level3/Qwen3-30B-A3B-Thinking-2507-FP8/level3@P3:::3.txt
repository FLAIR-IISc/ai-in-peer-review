

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning" (NIPS 2017)

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel approach for estimating the Wasserstein distance between two distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution and is proven to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors demonstrate PMD's application in domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), the dominant population-based divergence in machine learning.

The paper builds upon the growing interest in distributional distances for deep learning, particularly following the success of Wasserstein GANs (WGANs) and MMD-based approaches. While WGANs approximate the dual Wasserstein metric with a critic network, PMD offers a primal approach that is parameter-free, differentiable, and computationally tractable. Unlike MMD, which requires careful tuning of kernel bandwidth and suffers from weak gradients, PMD has no hyperparameters, provides stronger gradients, and can use smaller mini-batch sizes during training.

The paper's main contributions include: (1) a theoretically grounded definition of PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric; (2) a practical algorithm for using PMD as a training objective with neural networks; and (3) thorough empirical validation across domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The theoretical analysis is sound, with clear connections to optimal transport theory and proper citations to relevant prior work, including the seminal work on Wasserstein distances by Villani.

The paper excels in clarity and structure. The problem definition, theoretical results, and experimental methodology are well-explained, making it accessible to readers familiar with deep learning and distributional distances. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation specifics about matching algorithms and hyperparameter choices. The experimental section thoroughly compares PMD with MMD across multiple datasets and tasks, demonstrating consistent improvements in both performance and convergence speed.

The paper's main strength lies in its practical advantages over MMD: PMD eliminates the need for kernel bandwidth tuning, provides stronger gradients that avoid the "vanishing gradient" problem in MMD, and allows for smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs. 1000), significantly improving training efficiency. The authors also address computational concerns by proposing a quadratic-time approximation to the cubic-time Hungarian algorithm, which maintains statistical performance.

However, the paper has a few limitations. The generative modeling experiments, while showing promise, do not fully address the reviewer's concern about PMD's behavior with many modes or when sample size N is smaller than the number of modes. The authors observe blurry images on SVHN and LFW, which they attribute to using L1 distance at the pixel level rather than addressing the fundamental challenge of multimodal distributions. Additionally, while the paper demonstrates PMD's advantages over MMD, it does not compare with other Wasserstein approximations like WGAN or the more recent CramerGAN.

The theoretical results appear correct and well-supported, with appropriate references to Varadarajan's theorem and stability of optimal transport. The experimental analysis is adequate but could be strengthened with more comprehensive generative modeling experiments on challenging multimodal datasets.

The paper's significance is high, as it offers a simple yet effective solution to a persistent challenge in deep learning: comparing distributions without explicit parametric assumptions. By removing the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter and providing stronger gradients, PMD has the potential to simplify training of generative models and domain adaptation systems. The authors' claim that PMD is "conceptually simple, differentiable, and enables training of neural-network-based models" is well-supported by the experiments.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributional distances for deep learning. The method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The paper is clearly written, properly contextualized within the literature, and addresses practical concerns about computational efficiency. While the generative modeling experiments could be expanded to address multimodality concerns, the paper's core contributions are compelling and likely to be useful to practitioners.

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear consistency proof
- Conceptually simple and practical implementation
- Clear advantages over MMD in both theory and practice
- Well-structured, clearly written, and properly cited
- Adequate experimental validation across multiple tasks

**Weaknesses:**
- Generative modeling experiments don't fully address multimodal challenges
- Limited comparison with other Wasserstein approximations (e.g., WGAN)
- The paper doesn't explore the theoretical limits of PMD when N < number of modes

**Recommendation:** Accept. This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The proposed method is novel, well-supported by theory and experiments, and addresses important practical limitations of existing approaches. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS 2017 proceedings and likely to influence future work in distributional distances for deep learning.
