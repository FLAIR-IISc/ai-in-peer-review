This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel method for estimating the distance between probability distributions based on samples, offering an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The core idea is to define distance as the minimum weight matching of sample populations, proving its strong consistency as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, demonstrating improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

The work builds upon the established field of sample-based statistical divergence estimation, particularly MMD [Gretton et al., 2012]. MMD has become a cornerstone in various deep learning tasks, including those mentioned by the authors (generative modeling [Li et al., 2017], domain adaptation [Long et al., 2015]), due to its simplicity and theoretical guarantees. However, the authors rightly identify MMD’s drawbacks – sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, potential for vanishing gradients, and the need for large mini-batches – as motivating factors for their approach. The connection to the Wasserstein metric [Villani, 2008; Rubner et al., 2000] is also a valuable contribution, as the Wasserstein distance offers a different perspective on distributional similarity and has gained prominence, particularly with Wasserstein GANs [Arjovsky et al., 2017].

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The theoretical grounding, demonstrating PMD’s consistency as a Wasserstein metric estimator, is a solid foundation. The application to both domain adaptation and generative modeling provides a comprehensive evaluation. The empirical results are convincing, particularly showcasing the ability of PMD to achieve comparable or superior performance to MMD with significantly smaller mini-batches – a practical advantage for efficient training. The clarity of the paper is generally good, with a logical flow from introducing the concept to presenting the theoretical analysis and experimental results. The inclusion of pseudocode is helpful for understanding the implementation.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The claim of "fewer hyperparameters" is somewhat overstated. While PMD avoids the kernel bandwidth of MMD, the choice of distance metric *d(x, y)* in the PMD definition itself introduces a hyperparameter (e.g., the scaling of the L1 distance used here). The performance of PMD will inherently depend on this choice, and different distance metrics may be more suitable for different datasets or applications. A more nuanced discussion of this aspect would be beneficial. The analysis of gradient strength, while presented with supporting figures, feels slightly superficial. The behavior of MMD gradients is heavily influenced by the kernel bandwidth, and a more rigorous comparison would involve testing PMD against MMD using a range of bandwidths optimized for each task. Simply stating that MMD’s gradients can be weak doesn't fully address the fact that proper bandwidth selection mitigates this issue. Finally, while the paper acknowledges the computational cost of solving the minimum weight matching problem, the scalability of PMD to very high-dimensional data remains a concern. The limitations of distance metrics in high dimensions are well-known, and PMD may encounter similar challenges to MMD in such scenarios.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** PMD offers a genuinely new approach to distribution matching, grounded in Wasserstein metric theory.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The consistency proof provides a strong theoretical basis for the method.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages, especially regarding mini-batch size, making it more practical for large-scale training.
*   **Significance:** The potential for faster training and improved performance in key deep learning tasks is significant.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Hyperparameter Claim:** The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is weak and potentially misleading.
*   **Gradient Comparison:** The comparison of gradient strength is not fully rigorous and could benefit from more systematic evaluation with optimized MMD bandwidths.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The limitations of distance metrics in high-dimensional spaces are not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While not without its minor flaws, the theoretical grounding, empirical validation, and practical benefits (particularly the smaller mini-batch requirement) make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. The authors effectively demonstrate that PMD is a viable and often superior alternative to MMD for estimating distributional distance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should clarify the hyperparameter aspects of the distance metric choice, provide a more thorough comparison of gradient behavior with optimized MMD parameters, and briefly discuss potential limitations in high-dimensional settings.
