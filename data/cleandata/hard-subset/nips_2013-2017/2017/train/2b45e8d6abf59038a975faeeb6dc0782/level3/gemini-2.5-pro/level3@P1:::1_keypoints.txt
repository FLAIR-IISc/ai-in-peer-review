- Proposal: Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the primal Wasserstein-1 distance using average cost of minimum-weight bipartite matching between sample populations.  
- Training: Uses PMD as a differentiable objective by backpropagating distances of matched pairs while keeping the matching fixed during parameter updates.  
- Theory: Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the true Wasserstein-1 distance.  
- Relation to prior work: Positions PMD as an alternative to MMD (no kernel tuning) and WGAN (no separate critic network).  
- Evaluation: Applied to unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling; shows promising and faster convergence on some domain adaptation tasks (Office dataset).  

Strengths
- Novelty & simplicity: Conceptually appealing, parameter-free primal approximation avoiding critic networks and kernel tuning.  
- Theoretical grounding: Strong consistency proof links PMD to Wasserstein-1.  
- Empirical domain adaptation: Outperforms MMD on some tasks and converges faster in feature alignment settings.  

Weaknesses
- Sample complexity & mode collapse risk: PMD requires large population size N in high-dimensional, multimodal settings; small N can yield poor proxies and encourage low-diversity mode collapse.  
- Poor generative results: SVHN samples show low diversity and visual quality; no WGAN baseline comparison.  
- Questionable claims about MMD: Mischaracterizes MMD’s mini-batch requirements and overlooks unbiased U-statistic estimators and mixture-of-kernels practical fixes.  
- Biased gradient estimator: Fixing optimal matching during differentiation ignores matching’s dependence on parameters, likely introducing bias in stochastic gradients.  

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss sample complexity limitations and include toy experiments varying N (e.g., unimodal vs multimodal Gaussians).  
- Revise MMD comparisons to reflect standard practice (unbiased estimators, small batches, kernel mixtures).  
- Compare generative results to stronger baselines like WGAN.  
- Consider perceptual/feature-space distances (pretrained networks) instead of pixel L1 for better sample quality.  

Summary arguments
- For acceptance: Novel, simple, theoretically grounded method with promising domain adaptation results.  
- Against acceptance: Significant limitations for complex generative tasks, questionable MMD comparisons, biased gradient estimation, and insufficient experimental validation.
