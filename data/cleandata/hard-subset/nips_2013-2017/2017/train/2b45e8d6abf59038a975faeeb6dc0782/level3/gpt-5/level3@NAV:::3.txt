This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a population-based divergence that equals the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets and serves as a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance. The estimator is conceptually simple, differentiable with respect to distribution parameters (by not backpropagating through the discrete matching), and thus amenable to training neural-network-based models. The work positions PMD as a practical alternative to MMD, the predominant population-based divergence in ML, and contrasts PMD’s lack of kernel hyperparameters, stronger gradients, and smaller effective mini-batch requirements with MMD’s bandwidth sensitivity and gradient saturation. Exact computation is cubic via the Hungarian algorithm; the authors propose a quadratic-time approximation that empirically preserves statistical performance. Applications in domain adaptation and generative modeling show advantages over MMD in accuracy and convergence speed. The paper is clearly written, well-structured, and appears to properly situate itself with prior work in MMD and Wasserstein methods.

Quality: The theoretical results are sound and appropriately scoped: PMD is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of W1 via standard empirical-measure convergence and transport stability. The empirical analysis is adequate and supports the claims (notably, gradient strength and convergence). I encourage the authors to add: (i) sensitivity vs. sample size when the number of data modes exceeds N; (ii) more challenging multi-modal generative modeling (e.g., many-mode mixtures or datasets known to test mode coverage), reporting coverage/precision-recall/FID.

Clarity: Excellent presentation; algorithms and complexity trade-offs are explicit. A brief discussion of subgradient behavior when matchings change and memory cost of the N×N distance matrix would improve completeness.

Originality: The formulation—using exact/approximate minimum matching as a differentiable training objective for deep models—is novel relative to prevailing kernel two-sample tests and adversarial Wasserstein training. The connection to W1 is clean and compelling.

Significance: High. A practical, parameter-free, strong-gradient alternative to MMD is valuable, and the quadratic approximation makes PMD potentially usable at scale. Additional experiments on highly multi-modal data would further solidify impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, principled estimator with strong consistency to W1
- Clear advantages over MMD (no bandwidth, stronger gradients, smaller batches)
- Solid empirical improvements and convergence benefits
- Clean theory and clear writing

Cons / requests:
- Limited evaluation on highly multi-modal generative settings
- Open questions on behavior when N < number of modes and on computational/memory scaling
- No analysis of approximation-induced bias from the quadratic matching heuristic
