This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based metric for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between samples drawn from the two distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. A key contribution is showing that this estimator is differentiable with respect to the parameters of underlying generative models, enabling its use as a training objective in deep learning frameworks via stochastic gradient descent.

The work is situated in the context of other integral probability metrics used in machine learning, most notably the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The paper provides a thorough comparison, arguing that PMD overcomes several practical drawbacks of MMD, such as the need to tune a sensitive kernel bandwidth parameter, the issue of weak or vanishing gradients, and the requirement for large mini-batch sizes. PMD is also contrasted with recent methods for approximating the Wasserstein distance based on its dual formulation, such as in Wasserstein GANs, noting that PMD works with the primal form and does not require a separate critic network. The utility of PMD is demonstrated on two applications: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The connection between the minimum weight matching of empirical samples and the first Wasserstein metric is well-established in optimal transport theory, and the authors correctly leverage this to prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator. The proposed learning algorithm, which uses mini-batches for gradient updates while keeping the matching fixed over several iterations, is a practical and reasonable approach. The experimental evaluation is well-executed, with appropriate baselines and analyses that support the paper's central claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The definition of PMD is introduced intuitively, and the algorithm for parameter learning is described clearly with pseudocode. The theoretical analysis connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric is easy to follow. The experimental sections are detailed enough to understand the setup and the conclusions drawn from the results.

**Originality:** The idea of using the solution to the assignment problem as a distance between point sets is not new, but its formulation as a differentiable, stochastic objective for training deep generative models is novel and timely. While MMD has been the dominant sample-based metric for this purpose, this paper presents a compelling and well-motivated alternative derived from the Wasserstein distance. It clearly articulates its relationship to and advantages over MMD and recent adversarial methods, adequately referencing prior contributions.

**Significance:** The results presented are important and likely to be impactful. PMD offers a practical, parameter-free (in the sense of no kernel bandwidth) alternative to MMD for a range of tasks that require comparing distributions. The demonstrated benefits—faster convergence and potentially better performance—could lead to its wide adoption by researchers and practitioners in areas like generative modeling, domain adaptation, and model criticism. It advances the state of the art by providing a new and effective tool for distribution matching in deep learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Usefulness:** PMD is a conceptually simple, elegant, and potentially very useful method for comparing distributions. It provides a direct and differentiable estimator for the Wasserstein-1 distance, offering a valuable alternative to both MMD and adversarial losses.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is well-grounded in theory, with a clear proof of its consistency as an estimator for a true metric (Wasserstein-1).
*   **Practical Advantages:** The paper convincingly argues and demonstrates that PMD addresses significant practical limitations of MMD, including hyperparameter tuning (kernel bandwidth), gradient strength, and mini-batch size flexibility, leading to improved convergence and performance.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The empirical study is comprehensive, covering two important applications (domain adaptation and generative modeling) and providing insightful ablations on computational time and sensitivity to hyperparameters.

**Arguments against Acceptance / Questions for Authors:**
*   **Computational Cost:** A potential weakness is the computational complexity of the matching step. While the authors correctly point out that an O(N^2) approximate solver is sufficient and comparable to the SGD updates, this is still computationally more intensive than M-MMD, especially as linear-time estimators for MMD exist. Further discussion on scalability to very large population sizes (N) would strengthen the paper.
*   **Behavior with Multi-modal Distributions:** A key question is how PMD behaves when the number of samples (N) is smaller than the number of modes in the underlying distributions. In such a scenario, the minimum weight matching might only pair samples within corresponding modes, potentially underestimating the true distance between the distributions and failing to enforce coverage of all modes. An experiment on a synthetic dataset (e.g., a mixture of Gaussians) that explicitly tests this scenario would be very illuminating and would help characterize the estimator's limitations.
*   **Choice of Ground Metric:** The paper highlights that PMD avoids MMD's kernel hyperparameter. However, it introduces a dependency on the choice of the ground distance `d(·, ·)` (L1 is used in the experiments). The blurry results on image generation tasks suggest that this choice is critical. This could be viewed as replacing one hyperparameter (kernel bandwidth) with another (the choice of a perceptually meaningful distance metric). A brief discussion of the sensitivity to this choice would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable new technique to the machine learning community. The strengths in novelty, theoretical grounding, and practical utility far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
