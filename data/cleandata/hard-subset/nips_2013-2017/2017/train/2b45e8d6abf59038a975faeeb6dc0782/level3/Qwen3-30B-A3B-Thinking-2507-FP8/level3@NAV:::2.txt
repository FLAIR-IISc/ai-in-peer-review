

The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel approach for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, and the authors prove it's a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper positions PMD as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), claiming advantages including stronger gradients, applicability with smaller mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters. The authors apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, reporting empirical improvements over MMD.

The theoretical foundation is strong, with a clear connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric established through a consistency proof. The empirical results are well-executed, with convincing demonstrations of PMD's convergence speed advantage using small mini-batches (100 vs. MMD's typical 1000). The ablation studies on matching algorithms and population size provide valuable practical insights for implementation.

However, several claims require more rigorous support. The assertion of "stronger gradients" is not thoroughly validated, as MMD's gradient behavior critically depends on kernel bandwidth (sigma), which the authors do not systematically vary. Comparisons should use best or a range of sigmas for MMD rather than a single fixed setting. The claim of "fewer hyperparameters" is also weakened by PMD's dependence on distance metric choice (e.g., L1, L2), which introduces its own hyperparameters. The paper overlooks that high-dimensional distance estimation remains problematic for PMD, potentially limiting its real-world applicability.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The contributions are novel compared to previous NIPS work on distribution matching, though the connection to Wasserstein distance is somewhat straightforward. The significance lies in providing a practical alternative to MMD for fast training with small batches, though the high-dimensional limitations may constrain broader adoption.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with meaningful empirical results. The authors should address the gradient comparison more carefully and acknowledge the distance metric dependency as a limitation. PMD represents a valid improvement for specific applications requiring small mini-batch training but may not be a universal solution to distribution matching challenges. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, though with some overstated claims that could be tempered in revision.
