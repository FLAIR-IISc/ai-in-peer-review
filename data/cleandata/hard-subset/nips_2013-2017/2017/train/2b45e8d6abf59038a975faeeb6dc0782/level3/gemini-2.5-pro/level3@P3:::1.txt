This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric, and proposes its use as a training objective for deep learning applications like domain adaptation and generative modeling. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two minibatches of samples, one from a model distribution and one from a target distribution. The matching problem is solved either exactly using the Hungarian algorithm in O(N^3) time or approximately in O(N^2). The paper positions PMD as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD has fewer hyperparameters, provides stronger gradients, and allows for faster convergence. This work relates to a line of research on using statistical divergences for training generative models, such as Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) which use MMD, and Wasserstein GANs (WGAN) which approximate the dual form of the Wasserstein distance. PMD is distinct in that it directly approximates the primal form of the Wasserstein distance on minibatches.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Simplicity:** The core idea of directly optimizing a consistent estimator of the primal Wasserstein distance between minibatches is novel and elegant. It provides a parameter-free (unlike WGAN's critic network) and kernel-free (unlike MMD) method for distribution matching, which is an interesting contribution to the field.
2.  **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a clear theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, proving that N-PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the true metric as N approaches infinity. This provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
3.  **Interesting Empirical Analysis:** The experiments demonstrating the use of minibatch Wasserstein computations are valuable. The comparisons to MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling highlight potential benefits, such as faster convergence and robustness to hyperparameter tuning (i.e., no kernel bandwidth σ). The analysis of the gradient magnitudes (Fig. 2b) provides some empirical support for the claim that PMD provides stronger learning signals than MMD.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Quality of Generated Samples:** While the approach is interesting, the empirical results for generative modeling are not on par with contemporary methods. The generated images for SVHN and LFW (Fig. 4) are quite blurry and lack the quality and diversity seen in other works, including WGAN, which also optimizes a form of the Wasserstein distance. The SVHN samples seem to exhibit mode collapse, with a disproportionate number of samples resembling the digit '8'.
2.  **Limitations of Minibatch Estimation:** The paper's primary weakness lies in the statistical properties of estimating the Wasserstein distance on small minibatches (populations).
    *   **Bias with Small N:** PMD requires a large population size N to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance for complex, high-dimensional, or multimodal distributions. With a small N, the minimum weight matching objective may find a global minimum that does not correspond to the true data distribution. For instance, the optimizer might favor a generated distribution with lower entropy (fewer modes) because it is easier to match a small batch of target samples with a less diverse set of generated samples.
    *   **Gradient Bias:** The paper states that the gradient of the PMD objective is unbiased. This appears to be a misunderstanding. While the stochastic gradient `∇θ(1/N Σ d(x_i, y_{M*i}))` is an unbiased estimate of the gradient of the minibatch PMD `E[DN(X,Y)]`, it is not an unbiased estimate of the gradient of the true Wasserstein distance `W_1(p_X, p_Y)`. This is a critical point of comparison with MMD, for which a truly unbiased U-statistic estimator exists (Gretton et al., "A Kernel Two-Sample Test," JMLR 2012) that allows MMD to be optimized effectively with very small minibatches (e.g., batch size of 2). The lack of a known unbiased gradient estimator for PMD is a significant limitation that should be discussed.
3.  **Potential Failure Modes:** The method's reliance on one-to-one matching within a batch seems susceptible to certain failure modes. A challenging but informative experiment would be to learn a mixture of Gaussians, where both the means and variances are learnable parameters. It is likely that PMD would struggle to correctly capture the variances of the modes, as the matching objective may not penalize incorrect variance as strongly as it penalizes incorrect means, especially with small N.
4.  **Clarity on MMD Limitations:** The paper's criticism of MMD should be more nuanced. The claim that MMD requires a large minibatch size (e.g., 1000) is only true for the biased, quadratic-time estimator. As mentioned above, unbiased estimators allow MMD to converge with SGD using very small minibatches. The authors should clarify this and provide a more direct comparison of the statistical properties of their estimator versus the standard estimators for MMD.

**Questions and Suggestions for the Authors:**

*   Could you please clarify the limitations of using a finite, and often small, population size N? Specifically, could you discuss the bias of the PMD objective and its gradient with respect to the true Wasserstein distance and its gradient?
*   The SVHN samples appear to have low diversity. Have you quantified this, for example, by looking at the distribution of class labels if they were passed through a pre-trained classifier?
*   Could you comment on how PMD might perform on the suggested task of learning a mixture of Gaussians with learnable variances? This could help clarify the practical limitations of the matching objective.
*   Please consider adding a discussion that contrasts PMD with the unbiased U-statistic estimator for MMD, which would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between the two methods.

**Minor Issue:**

*   Line 141: "usally" should be "usually".

In summary, this paper presents a creative and theoretically grounded approach for distribution matching. The idea of using the primal form of the Wasserstein distance on minibatches is a valuable contribution. However, the practical application is hampered by the biased nature of the small-minibatch estimator and the resulting sample quality, which does not advance the state of the art. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough and frank discussion of the limitations of this estimation strategy, particularly in comparison to the well-understood properties of estimators for MMD.
