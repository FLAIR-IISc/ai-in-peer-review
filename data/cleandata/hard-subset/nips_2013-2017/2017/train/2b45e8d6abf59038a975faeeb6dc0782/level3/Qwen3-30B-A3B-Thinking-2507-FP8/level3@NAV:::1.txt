

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum weight matching between two minibatches, as a Wasserstein-based distribution distance estimator. The authors prove PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and demonstrate applications in domain adaptation and generative modeling. While the theoretical foundation is sound and the paper makes a technically valid contribution to distribution distance estimation, several critical limitations undermine its significance.

The main strength lies in the clean theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), and the empirical demonstration that PMD avoids MMD's bandwidth hyperparameter and can use smaller minibatch sizes (N=100 vs MMD's typical N=1000). The domain adaptation results (Table 1) show PMD outperforms MMD on a→w and a→d tasks, and converges faster (Fig. 3a).

However, the paper significantly underestimates PMD's limitations. PMD requires large minibatch sizes (N=1000+) for reliable estimation on multimodal distributions, as evidenced by the SVHN dataset's low diversity (digit '8' dominates) and poor image quality (Fig. 4). Crucially, the authors fail to address that PMD does not have an unbiased gradient estimator like MMD's batch_size=2 capability (from "A Kernel Two-Sample Test"). The claim that PMD "can use normal mini-batch size" is misleading—PMD's convergence depends on large N for diverse distributions, while MMD works with tiny batches. The failure case of matching mixtures of Gaussians (including learning variances) is not tested, which would expose PMD's fundamental weakness.

The paper also incorrectly claims PMD-generated images are better than WGAN, when they're actually blurrier. While the theoretical analysis is correct, the practical limitations (large N requirement, poor multimodal performance) aren't adequately addressed, and the significance is overstated. The minor typo ("usally" → "usually") should be corrected.

Overall, while technically sound, the paper's limitations outweigh its contributions. It does not advance the state of the art sufficiently to warrant acceptance, as MMD remains more practical for most applications due to its small-batch capability and proven multimodal performance. The authors should clarify these limitations before reconsideration.
