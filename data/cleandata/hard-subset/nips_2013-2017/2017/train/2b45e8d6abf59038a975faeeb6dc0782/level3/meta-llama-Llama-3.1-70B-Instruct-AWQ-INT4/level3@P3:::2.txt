This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a superior alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein distance, which is a well-established metric for measuring the distance between two probability distributions. The main advantages of PMD over MMD are its stronger gradients, applicability with smaller mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters.

The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of PMD, including its relationship with the Wasserstein metric and MMD. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, which is a valid statistical distance. They also compare the advantages and disadvantages of PMD with MMD, highlighting the benefits of using PMD for distribution matching.

The empirical results presented in the paper convincingly verify the advantages of PMD over MMD. The authors demonstrate that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The results also show that PMD can be used with smaller mini-batches, which can lead to faster training times.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The claim of stronger gradients is less convincing because MMD's behavior depends on the kernel bandwidth (sigma), and comparisons should use the best or a range of sigmas. Additionally, the claim of fewer hyperparameters is weak, as PMD depends on a distance metric that can introduce its own hyperparameters. Furthermore, high-dimensional distance estimation remains problematic, and PMD may face similar issues to MMD in high-dimensional settings.

Overall, the paper presents a valid improvement for fast training with small batches, but its applicability may be limited by its reliance on distance metrics in high-dimensional settings. The authors suggest comparing PMD to MMD using standard bandwidth heuristics and treating MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free.
