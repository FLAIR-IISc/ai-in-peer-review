- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel distance metric between distributions grounded in the Wasserstein distance.
- Core idea: distance defined as minimum weight matching of sample populations, offering an alternative to MMD.
- Claims potential advantages over MMD in gradient behavior and mini-batch size requirements.
- Builds on prior work (MMD, Wasserstein GANs) and correctly positions PMD in that literature.
- Theoretical contribution: proves PMD is a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.
- Learning algorithm using PMD as an objective is logical and well-motivated.
- Experimental results show competitive performance on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW).
- Analysis of gradient characteristics (Fig. 2b) provides useful insight into PMD’s gradient behavior.
- Includes helpful implementation details on matching algorithms and computational costs.
- Weakness: limitations are acknowledged but not sufficiently explored or empirically validated.
- Claim of “stronger gradient” lacks comprehensive justification; needs deeper gradient-flow analysis (e.g., gradient norm evolution).
- Generated images on SVHN and LFW are blurry (attributed to L1), a significant drawback needing investigation or alternate losses.
- Lacks rigorous, direct comparison with Wasserstein GAN despite WGAN being the relevant state-of-the-art.
- Potential failure case: matching mixtures of Gaussians with differing variances may be problematic and needs empirical testing.
- Optimization risk: possible convergence to low-entropy local minima for small sample sizes (N), needing discussion and remedies (e.g., regularization).
- Sample diversity concern: SVHN results show imbalance (digit '8' overrepresented), suggesting sensitivity to training data imbalances.
- Minor typo noted ("usally" → "usually").
- MMD has an unbiased gradient estimator (Gretton et al.), while PMD lacks a known unbiased estimator for its gradient.
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, theoretical grounding, competitive empirical results, and clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited comparison to WGAN, unexplored failure cases and small-N issues, poor image quality on complex datasets, and sample diversity problems.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on addressing WGAN comparison, failure-case exploration, image-quality and diversity improvements, and deeper gradient analysis.
