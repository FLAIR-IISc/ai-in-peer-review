This paper proposes the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based distance metric for training deep learning models, particularly in domain adaptation and generative modeling. PMD is defined as the first Wasserstein distance between two minibatches of samples, computed by solving a minimum weight matching problem. This approach connects to a rich body of work on distribution matching, positioning PMD as a parameter-free alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) and a direct optimization alternative to the critic-based Wasserstein GAN (WGAN).

The primary strength of the paper is the novelty of using an explicit, sample-based Wasserstein distance computation as a training objective. The experiments demonstrating this on minibatches are interesting and provide a new perspective on distribution matching. The theoretical connection showing PMD is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein-1 metric is sound.

However, the work has several weaknesses that limit its practical significance. First, the quality of the generative modeling results is not competitive. The generated images are blurrier than those from WGANs, and the SVHN samples show a lack of diversity, with a disproportionate number of "8"s, suggesting a form of mode collapse. This may stem from a fundamental limitation: PMD requires a large minibatch size N to reliably estimate the distance between diverse, multimodal distributions. The O(N^3) or O(N^2) computational cost thus becomes a serious bottleneck. With small N, the optimization landscape may change, leading to a global minimum that does not match the true data distribution (e.g., a learned distribution with lower entropy). A simple failure case, such as matching mixtures of Gaussians, would likely expose this problem.

Furthermore, the comparison with MMD is not entirely balanced. The authors should clarify the limitations of both methods more thoroughly. While PMD avoids kernel selection, MMD can be effectively trained with SGD using an unbiased estimator that works for very small minibatches (batch_size=2), a significant advantage not shared by PMD, which has no known unbiased gradient estimator.

In summary, the paper introduces a conceptually interesting idea, but its practical application seems limited by computational cost and potential optimization issues with smaller batches. I would encourage the authors to conduct further experiments on challenging synthetic distributions and provide a more nuanced discussion of the method's limitations.
