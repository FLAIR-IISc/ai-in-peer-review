- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based differentiable surrogate for 1-Wasserstein using minimum-weight bipartite matching.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of 1-Wasserstein via Varadarajan’s theorem and OT stability.  
- Proposes an SGD algorithm that keeps matching fixed over inner-loop updates and optionally sub-samples matched pairs for cheaper gradients.  
- Claims advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, and smaller effective mini-batch needs.  
- Empirically evaluates on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), often outperforming MMD and converging faster.  
- Timing and sensitivity analyses indicate approximate O(N^2) matching is competitive with SGD and small sub-batches help.  
- PMD is essentially empirical OT/assignment with uniform weights — a well-studied special case of OT.  
- Missing comparisons to key related methods: Sinkhorn/entropic OT, stochastic OT, sliced Wasserstein, OT-based domain adaptation, and Cramér/energy distance.  
- Clear, simple formulation and a straightforward, provable consistency result.  
- Practical training heuristic (fixed matching + sub-sampling) with good empirical behavior.  
- Shows PMD yields stronger, more uniform gradients than MMD, addressing saturation issues.  
- Provides solid ablations on population/mini-batch sizes, matching algorithm choice, and timing.  
- Demonstrates utility on two tasks with qualitative and some quantitative convergence results.  
- Lacks crucial baselines (Sinkhorn, sliced Wasserstein, WGAN(-GP), energy/Cramér; DANN/JAN/CMD/CORAL/OTDA) limiting claims of superiority.  
- Generative evaluation is mostly qualitative, lacks standard metrics (FID/Inception), uses small datasets, and samples are blurry in pixel space.  
- Optimization ignores derivatives through matching; the “unbiased stochastic gradient” claim needs more careful theoretical justification.  
- Scalability concerns: N^2 costs for large N/high dimensions, memory/time not fully addressed and GPU matching not implemented.  
- “Parameter-free” claim is overstated: ground metric, population size, inner-loop steps, and matching parameters matter.  
- Theory stops at consistency; no finite-sample rates or generalization guarantees as a training loss.  
- Technically sound on main claims; optimization strategy is plausible but experimental scope is too narrow.  
- Writing is generally clear; optimization section should clarify when ignoring matching gradients is justified and how often re-matching occurs.  
- Originality is moderate: PMD is a natural empirical OT approach; novelty lies in the specific training heuristic and MMD comparisons.  
- Potentially useful in practice as a simpler alternative to MMD, but impact is unclear without comparisons to modern OT/adversarial methods.  
- Requested experiments/suggestions: compare to Sinkhorn and sliced Wasserstein (speed/performance), add FID/Inception and WGAN-GP baselines (CIFAR-10), formalize gradient justification, report memory/time for largest N, include DANN/JAN baselines, and analyze sensitivity to ground metric and matching quality.  
- Pros for acceptance: simple, theoretically grounded alternative to MMD with convincing gradient behavior and empirical speedups.  
- Cons for acceptance: missing key baselines/metrics, limited datasets, scalability and differentiability-through-matching not fully addressed.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject.  
- Reviewer confidence: Moderate.
