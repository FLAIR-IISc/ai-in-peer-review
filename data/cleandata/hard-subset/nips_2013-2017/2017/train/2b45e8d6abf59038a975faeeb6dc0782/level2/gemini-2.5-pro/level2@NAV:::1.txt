This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel, sample-based metric for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. The core idea is to frame the distance as the cost of a minimum weight bipartite matching between samples from the two populations. The authors provide a solid theoretical grounding by proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. This work is well-situated within existing literature, presenting PMD as a practical alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) and a parameter-free alternative to methods like Wasserstein GANs that approximate the dual form of the Wasserstein distance.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its clear motivation and strong empirical validation of PMD’s advantages over MMD. The claims that PMD has fewer hyperparameters (no kernel bandwidth to tune), provides stronger gradients, and allows for smaller mini-batch sizes are convincingly supported. The experiments are comprehensive, covering two distinct and important deep learning applications: domain adaptation and generative modeling. In both cases, PMD demonstrates performance that is competitive with or superior to MMD, often with significantly faster convergence. The ablation studies on population and mini-batch sizes further strengthen the paper's claims. The work is original, well-written, and the results are significant for practitioners looking for a robust and efficient distribution matching objective.

Despite these strengths, the paper has a few weaknesses. The main concern is the computational complexity of the matching step (O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm), which may limit scalability to very large population sizes. While the authors show that an O(N^2) approximation works well and mention the potential for GPU parallelization, this remains a practical bottleneck compared to MMD's quadratic complexity. Secondly, while the generative models trained with PMD produce less noisy samples than MMD, the results on natural images (SVHN, LFW) are still quite blurry. This is likely a limitation of using a simple L1 distance for matching, as the authors briefly acknowledge. The work could be strengthened by exploring the use of a learned perceptual metric as the distance function `d(·, ·)` for the matching cost.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper introducing a valuable new tool for the deep learning community. The strengths in originality, clarity, and empirical significance strongly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
