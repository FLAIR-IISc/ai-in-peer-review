

This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel differentiable estimator of distribution distance based on samples, as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors define PMD as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from two distributions and prove it's a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. They apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, claiming advantages over MMD including no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch size requirements.

The paper's main strengths include a clear theoretical foundation connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric, a well-structured presentation of the algorithm, and compelling empirical results demonstrating PMD's advantages over MMD on several benchmarks. The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is sound, and the comparison with MMD is well-motivated. The empirical results in Sections 5.1-5.2 show PMD outperforming MMD on domain adaptation tasks (a→w and a→d) and generating less noisy images in generative modeling. The demonstration of stronger gradients (Fig. 2b) and reduced parameter sensitivity (Fig. 3b-3c) are particularly convincing.

However, several issues need addressing. First, the claim that PMD is a "strongly consistent estimator" relies on Propositions 3.1 and 3.2, but the proof in Theorem 3.3 is somewhat superficial. The authors should explicitly show how these propositions apply to their specific case of PMD, especially regarding the convergence of the optimal transport plan.

Second, the paper lacks comparison with more recent distribution distance estimators beyond MMD. It should compare against Wasserstein GAN variants (e.g., WGAN-GP) and other alternatives like Cramer distance [Bellemare et al., 2017], which addresses similar issues with Wasserstein gradients. This would better establish PMD's position in the current literature.

Third, the computational complexity analysis is incomplete. While the authors note PMD's O(N^3) Hungarian algorithm complexity, they don't compare it to MMD's O(N^2) complexity or discuss how this impacts training time in practice. The timing results in Fig. 5(c) suggest approximated matching can be accelerated on GPU, but this needs more thorough discussion.

Fourth, the generative modeling results are somewhat mixed. PMD generates blurry images on SVHN and LFW (Fig. 4), and the authors attribute this to L1 distance not being suitable for natural images. However, they don't provide a comprehensive comparison between PMD in pixel space versus code space (as suggested in the text), which would strengthen their claim that PMD is generally better than MMD.

The claim that PMD "can use normal mini-batch size, such as 100" is under-supported. While the paper states this advantage in Section 3.2, the empirical evidence (Fig. 5a) shows only that decreasing mini-batch size improves PMD's final value, not that it converges faster than MMD with the same mini-batch size. A direct comparison of convergence speed with identical batch sizes would be more convincing.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of when PMD might not be preferable. For instance, with high-dimensional data where the cost matrix becomes prohibitively large, PMD's computational cost could become a significant drawback compared to MMD.

Overall, this is a solid paper with clear theoretical contributions and promising empirical results. The core idea of using minimum weight matching for distribution distance estimation is novel and well-motivated. The main weaknesses are the incomplete theoretical justification, lack of comparison with recent alternatives, and insufficient empirical evidence for some key claims.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distribution distance estimation in deep learning. While not revolutionary, it provides a practical alternative to MMD with clear advantages in specific scenarios. With minor revisions to address the theoretical and empirical shortcomings, this paper would be a strong candidate for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with the expectation that the authors address the concerns raised.
