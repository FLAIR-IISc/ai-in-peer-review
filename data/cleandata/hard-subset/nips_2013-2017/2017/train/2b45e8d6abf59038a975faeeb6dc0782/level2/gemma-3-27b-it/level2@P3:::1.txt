This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel distance metric between probability distributions, offering an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). PMD is framed as a minimum weight matching problem and demonstrated to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors present a stochastic gradient descent algorithm for learning with PMD and showcase its application to domain adaptation and generative modeling, demonstrating improvements over MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation for PMD is well-articulated, highlighting the drawbacks of MMD—sensitivity to bandwidth selection, vanishing gradients, and large mini-batch requirements. The definition of PMD is mathematically sound, and the connection to the Wasserstein metric is convincingly established through rigorous theoretical analysis. The proposed learning algorithm is also clearly explained, along with justifications for its advantages (e.g., reduced mini-batch size). The experimental setup is adequate, and the results demonstrate the practical benefits of PMD. The inclusion of ablation studies regarding population size and matching algorithms (in Section 5.3) adds significant value. The references are comprehensive and relevant.

A key strength lies in the theoretical grounding of PMD. The proof of strong consistency provides a solid foundation for its use as a reliable distance metric. Furthermore, the analysis comparing PMD and MMD provides valuable insights into their respective strengths and weaknesses. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate PMD’s effectiveness in the chosen applications. The discussion regarding the gradient behavior is particularly insightful.

Several areas could benefit from further clarification or expansion. While the paper mentions the computational complexity of PMD (O(N^3) for exact matching, O(N^2) for the approximation), a more detailed analysis of the practical runtime performance would be beneficial, particularly when scaling to larger datasets. A comparison with other Wasserstein distance estimators (beyond MMD) would strengthen the contribution. While the paper mentions limitations related to the L1 distance in image generation, exploring alternative distance metrics within the PMD framework could be a worthwhile direction for future work. Furthermore, the sensitivity analysis in section 5.4 is limited to a simple toy dataset. Performing a similar analysis on the more complex datasets used in the application sections would give greater confidence in the robustness of the results. The discussion of GPU acceleration is brief; detailing the potential speedups achieved with a GPU implementation would be helpful. While the claim of stronger gradients is supported by the distribution plots, quantifying the improvement in convergence speed specifically *due to* stronger gradients would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to estimating distribution distance.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Demonstrated improvements over MMD in practical applications.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis establishing connections to the Wasserstein metric.
*   Empirical validation with ablation studies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison with other Wasserstein distance estimators.
*   Detailed runtime analysis and GPU acceleration results are lacking.
*   Sensitivity analysis could be extended to more complex datasets.
*   The benefit of stronger gradients is not quantitatively assessed.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to the field. The proposed PMD metric is a valuable addition to the toolkit for estimating distribution distances, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated its advantages. Addressing the minor concerns raised above would further strengthen the paper. A strong accept recommendation is given.
