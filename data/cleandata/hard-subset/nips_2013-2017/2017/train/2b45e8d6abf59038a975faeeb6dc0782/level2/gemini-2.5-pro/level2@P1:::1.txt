This paper proposes a new method, Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), for estimating the distance between two probability distributions from samples. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The main contribution is the application of PMD as a differentiable objective function for training deep neural networks. The proposed training algorithm involves an outer loop that computes the optimal matching and an inner loop that performs several SGD updates using this fixed matching.

The paper positions PMD as an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). It argues that PMD has several advantages: it is hyperparameter-free (unlike MMD's kernel bandwidth), it provides stronger and more uniform gradients, and it allows for smaller mini-batch sizes during optimization, leading to faster convergence. The method is evaluated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. In both applications, PMD is shown to be competitive with or outperform MMD-based approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel and Elegant Idea:** The core idea of using the minimum weight matching cost directly as a differentiable loss for training deep networks is novel and intuitive. It provides a new, practical way to leverage the power of the Wasserstein metric, complementing other popular approaches like MMD and the dual formulation used in Wasserstein GANs.
*   **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric. The proof that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator (Theorem 3.3) gives the method a solid theoretical grounding.
*   **Clear Practical Advantages:** The paper makes a compelling case for the practical benefits of PMD over MMD. The elimination of the sensitive kernel bandwidth hyperparameter is a significant advantage. The analysis of the gradient properties (Sec 3.2, Fig 2b) provides a convincing explanation for why PMD might lead to more stable and efficient training. The ability to decouple the population size for matching from the SGD mini-batch size is a clever and effective optimization strategy.
*   **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and thoroughly executed. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of PMD on two distinct and important problems (domain adaptation and generative modeling). The results consistently show that PMD achieves strong performance, often outperforming the MMD baseline in both final accuracy/quality and convergence speed. The ablation studies on population size and mini-batch size (Fig. 5) further validate the claims made in the paper.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The primary drawback of PMD is the computational cost of solving the assignment problem, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm and O(N^2) for the approximation used. While the authors argue this is acceptable and provide timing breakdowns, it remains significantly more expensive than the O(N^2) cost of MMD and could become a bottleneck for larger population sizes (N). While GPU parallelization is mentioned as a possibility, providing such an implementation or more detailed analysis would have strengthened the paper's claims of practicality.
*   **Dependence on Ground Metric:** The performance of PMD is inherently tied to the choice of the ground metric `d(x, y)`. The experiments exclusively use the L1 distance. The authors acknowledge that the blurriness of generated images on SVHN and LFW may be due to the pixel-wise L1 distance being a poor perceptual metric. A discussion on the sensitivity of the method to this choice, or an exploration of using learned feature-space distances as the ground metric, would be a valuable addition.
*   **Comparison to Wasserstein GANs (WGANs):** Given that PMD is an estimator of the Wasserstein metric, a direct empirical comparison to WGANs in the generative modeling experiments would have been highly informative. While the paper discusses the conceptual differences (primal vs. dual, parameter-free vs. critic network), understanding how PMD's performance stacks up against the state-of-the-art in Wasserstein-based generative models would help better position its contribution.
*   **Training Heuristic:** The proposed training procedure (computing matching once, then running multiple SGD steps) is a reasonable heuristic, but it relies on the assumption that the optimal matching M* remains stable across several updates. It would be useful to understand the sensitivity of the algorithm to the number of inner-loop iterations and whether this assumption holds in practice.

**Clarity, Quality, Originality, and Significance:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The concepts are explained clearly with helpful illustrations (Fig. 1) and pseudocode.
*   **Quality:** The work is of high quality. The theoretical claims are sound, and the experimental evaluation is rigorous and supports the paper's conclusions.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it builds on established theories of optimal transport and distribution metrics, its formulation as a direct, differentiable loss for deep learning is novel and distinct from prior work.
*   **Significance:** This paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a compelling new tool for comparing distributions in deep learning. PMD offers a strong alternative to MMD, addressing some of its key practical limitations. The method is general and could likely be impactful in many other areas beyond the two applications explored, such as reinforcement learning and model criticism.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated, and effective method with strong theoretical and empirical support. The weaknesses are minor compared to the strengths and mostly point towards interesting avenues for future research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
