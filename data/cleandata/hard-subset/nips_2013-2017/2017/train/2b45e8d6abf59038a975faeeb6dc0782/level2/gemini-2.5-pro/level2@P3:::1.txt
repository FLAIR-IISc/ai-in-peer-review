This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based metric for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples, or "populations," drawn from the respective distributions. The work establishes a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator. A learning algorithm is proposed to minimize PMD via stochastic gradient descent, which is then applied to unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. The paper contrasts PMD with the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD avoids sensitive kernel bandwidth hyperparameters, provides stronger learning gradients, and allows for more efficient mini-batch updates, leading to faster convergence.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The theoretical connection drawn between the N-PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is a key strength, grounding the proposed empirical measure in the established theory of optimal transport. The derivation, which links the linear programming formulation of the Wasserstein metric on empirical distributions to the minimum weight matching problem, is correct and well-explained. Claims are well-supported by a combination of theoretical analysis and extensive empirical evaluation. The experiments include not only direct comparisons on downstream tasks but also targeted studies validating specific claims, such as the analysis of gradient magnitude distribution (Fig 2b) and the approximation error convergence (Fig 2a).

One point for potential improvement regards the gradient computation in Equation (3). The argument that the optimal matching M* remains constant for infinitesimal changes in parameters θ is central to the proposed learning algorithm. While this is intuitively plausible, a brief discussion of the conditions under which this holds (e.g., uniqueness of the optimal matching) or a reference to relevant literature (e.g., envelope theorems like Danskin's theorem) would further strengthen the technical rigor of this step. Additionally, while the main text states that the proof of Theorem 3.3 is straightforward, including a more detailed derivation in an appendix would be beneficial for completeness.

**Clarity:**

The paper is very well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for a new distribution metric is clearly articulated by outlining the drawbacks of MMD. The definition of PMD is intuitive, and the learning algorithm is presented clearly with both a textual description and a helpful diagram (Fig 1). A particularly clear point is the distinction between the "population size" N used for matching and the smaller "mini-batch size" |B| used for SGD updates, which is a key practical advantage of the method. The experimental setup and results are described in sufficient detail to understand the comparisons being made.

**Originality:**

The proposed approach is novel and makes a significant contribution. While the Wasserstein metric and its connection to the assignment problem are known, the formulation of PMD as a practical, end-to-end differentiable objective for training deep neural networks is original. This work provides a compelling primal-form alternative to other methods for leveraging the Wasserstein distance in deep learning, such as the dual-form, adversarial approach of Wasserstein GANs (WGAN) [3]. It also presents a solid alternative to kernel-based methods like MMD [17, 31]. The paper does a good job of positioning its contribution with respect to these key related works.

**Significance:**

The results presented suggest that PMD could have a substantial impact. It offers a new, principled tool for a fundamental problem in machine learning: comparing distributions from samples. The method addresses several well-known practical issues with MMD, a very popular technique in the field. The strong performance shown in both domain adaptation and generative modeling highlights its versatility. The fact that PMD is parameter-free (unlike MMD's kernel bandwidth) and appears more robust to optimization challenges (stronger gradients, faster convergence) makes it an attractive method for practitioners.

A potential limitation on its significance is the computational cost of the matching step, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm and O(N^2) for the approximation used. While the paper rightly notes that this can be acceptable and that parallel implementations exist, this complexity may still be a barrier for applications requiring very large population sizes (N) where MMD's O(N^2) complexity might be faster. Furthermore, while the generated images show an improvement over the MMD-based baseline, they are not state-of-the-art for the time, suggesting that while PMD is a powerful loss function, it may need to be combined with architectural advances for top-tier generative performance. This could be acknowledged more explicitly.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically grounded discrepancy measure (PMD) as a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.
*   Directly addresses several practical limitations of the widely-used MMD, such as hyperparameter tuning and weak gradients.
*   The proposed learning algorithm is simple, practical, and demonstrates strong empirical performance and faster convergence than MMD on important tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling.
*   The paper is clearly written, and its contributions are significant and likely to be of broad interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The computational complexity of the matching algorithm is a potential drawback for scalability to very large population sizes compared to MMD or WGAN.
*   The justification for the gradient computation could be made more formally rigorous.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It introduces a novel and valuable method with strong theoretical and empirical support. The work is a solid contribution to the field and is likely to inspire follow-up research.
