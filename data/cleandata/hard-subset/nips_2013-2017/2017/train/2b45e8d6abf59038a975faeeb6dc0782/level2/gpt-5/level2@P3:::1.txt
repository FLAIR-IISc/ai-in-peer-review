Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based, differentiable objective for measuring distances between probability distributions in deep learning contexts. PMD is defined as the minimum-cost perfect matching between two i.i.d. samples of equal size (empirical measures), using a ground distance d (L1 in experiments). It is shown that PMD equals the empirical first Wasserstein distance (W1) and is a strongly consistent estimator of the population W1, via standard optimal transport (OT) results (Villani 2009; Varadarajan 1958). An algorithm is presented that alternates between computing a matching on a “population” (outer loop) and performing several SGD steps on smaller mini-batches that reuse the fixed matching. Compared to MMD, the paper argues that PMD avoids kernel bandwidth tuning, provides stronger gradients (less saturation), and works with smaller mini-batches. Empirical evaluations on domain adaptation (Office) and image generation (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) suggest better or comparable accuracy to MMD and faster convergence. Practical issues of solving the assignment are discussed, using Hungarian (exact, O(N^3)) and Drake–Hougardy (approximate, O(N^2)) algorithms.

Relation to prior work
PMD is equivalent to the empirical W1 (a.k.a. EMD with uniform weights), a well-studied object in OT and computer vision (Rubner et al., 2000) and statistics. Consistency of empirical OT is known; finite-sample rates have been studied (e.g., Fournier & Guillin 2015, not cited). Entropic-regularized OT and the Sinkhorn algorithm (Cuturi 2013) provide a differentiable, GPU-friendly, O(N^2) approximation widely used in machine learning by 2017, including for domain adaptation (Courty et al., 2016) and generative modeling (Genevay et al., 2016). The paper contrasts with MMD and WGAN, but does not compare to or discuss Sinkhorn-based OT, which is arguably the most relevant baseline/alternative.

Strengths
- Clear formulation connecting PMD to empirical W1 and a solid (if standard) consistency argument.
- Practical training procedure with outer-loop matching and inner-loop SGD using smaller mini-batches; simple to implement and parameter-free beyond choice of ground metric.
- Insightful discussion of gradient behavior; the analysis explaining why MMD gradients saturate with RBF kernels is accurate and supported empirically.
- Empirical evidence that PMD enables smaller mini-batches and accelerates convergence relative to MMD; useful timing breakdown and ablations on population size, batch size, and matching approximations.
- Applications to both domain adaptation and generative modeling; results on Office are competitive, and generation quality appears less noisy than MMD-GMMN in pixel space.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: PMD equals empirical W1/EMD, and the consistency theorem follows directly from standard OT stability results. The main contribution is algorithmic usage in deep learning with a particular training loop, rather than a new discrepancy or theory.
- Missing comparisons to key contemporaneous OT methods, especially entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn). This omission affects both positioning and experimental baselines. Sinkhorn yields differentiable soft couplings, is faster on GPU, scales better, and has been applied to domain alignment and generative modeling by 2016–2017.
- Experimental baselines are narrow. In domain adaptation, only MMD regularization is compared, whereas methods such as DANN (Ganin et al., 2016), Deep CORAL, JAN (Long et al., 2017), CMD (Zellinger et al., 2017), and OT-based alignment (Courty et al., 2016) are relevant. In generative modeling, evaluation lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) and comparisons to WGAN/Improved GANs.
- Optimization subtlety: gradients are not propagated through the discrete matching, and the method assumes the assignment remains stable for several SGD steps. This introduces non-smoothness; behavior when matchings flip could be discussed more rigorously (e.g., subgradient interpretation, stability conditions).
- Scope limitations: only equal-mass, one-to-one matchings are considered. Many practical settings benefit from unbalanced OT, partial matchings, or class-aware couplings (important for domain adaptation to avoid cross-class matchings).
- The L1 ground metric in pixel space leads to blur; perceptual or feature-space distances are only used via an autoencoder, but this is not systematically explored.

Quality
The technical content is sound; the PMD–W1 connection and consistency follow from standard OT results. The algorithm is reasonable and well-explained. However, the lack of strong baselines (especially Sinkhorn OT and modern DA methods) limits the strength of the empirical claims. The gradient-strength analysis is convincing; a theoretical treatment of assignment stability would further improve rigor.

Clarity
Overall clearly written with helpful figures and ablations. Some notation overloading (N as “population size” vs “mini-batch” in MMD literature) is acknowledged. It would help to explicitly name PMD as empirical W1 to situate the contribution, and to discuss unequal sample sizes and weighting.

Originality
Conceptually modest; the originality lies in the specific training recipe and empirical demonstration against MMD. Missing discussion of closely related OT practice (Sinkhorn) reduces perceived novelty.

Significance
Aligning distributions with stronger gradients and smaller batches is practically useful. With broader baselines and quantitative metrics, the significance could be substantial. As it stands, the contribution is incremental but potentially impactful for practitioners who currently rely on MMD.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Add comparisons to entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) for both tasks, including runtime on GPU, stability, and performance. Discuss the trade-offs vs PMD (regularization bias vs discrete matching).
- For domain adaptation, include baselines such as DANN, Deep CORAL, JAN, CMD, and OTDA (Courty et al., 2016) on the same feature backbone to contextualize gains.
- For generative modeling, report standard quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score/FID) and compare to WGAN/Improved GANs on SVHN. Include code-space vs pixel-space analyses with matched architectures.

Optional but valuable:
- Analyze assignment stability: frequency of matching changes, impact on gradients, and whether a subgradient view applies. Consider a relaxed/differentiable coupling (e.g., Sinkhorn) as a variance-reduction warm start for hard matching.
- Explore unbalanced/partial OT and class-conditional constraints to reduce harmful cross-class matchings in domain adaptation.
- Evaluate alternative ground costs (e.g., LPIPS/perceptual distances) and feature-space PMD beyond autoencoder codes.
- Discuss scaling to larger N with GPU-parallel matching or block-wise strategies; include memory/time profiles.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Simple, well-motivated objective with practical advantages over MMD.
- Clear algorithm with empirical evidence of faster convergence and stronger gradients.
- Solid connection to W1 and thorough ablations.

Cons:
- Limited novelty relative to established empirical OT; missing key related work (Sinkhorn) and baselines.
- Empirical evaluation lacks state-of-the-art comparisons and quantitative generative metrics.
- Theoretical and practical aspects of non-differentiable assignment are underexplored.

Overall
A solid and useful contribution that demonstrates practical benefits over MMD, but the absence of comparisons to mainstream OT approaches and stronger baselines reduces confidence in the general advantage of PMD. With the suggested additions, the work could make a stronger case for acceptance.
