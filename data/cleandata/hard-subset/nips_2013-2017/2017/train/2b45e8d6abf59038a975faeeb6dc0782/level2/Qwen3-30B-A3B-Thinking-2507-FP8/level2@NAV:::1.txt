

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel approach for estimating distribution distance based on samples, with applications in deep learning tasks. The main claim is that PMD, defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from two distributions, provides a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, while offering practical advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) including no kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes.

The theoretical foundation is strong, with Theorem 3.3 establishing PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, supported by clear connections to optimal transport theory. The authors provide thorough comparisons with MMD in Section 3.2, demonstrating PMD's advantages through gradient analysis (Figure 2b) and empirical validation. The experimental evaluation on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) is comprehensive, showing consistent performance improvements over MMD across multiple metrics and tasks. The ablation studies (Section 5.3) effectively validate the claimed advantages regarding mini-batch size and population size.

The paper excels in technical quality with solid theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The writing is clear, and the method is well-motivated by practical limitations of existing approaches. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that L1 distance may not be optimal for natural images and suggesting potential improvements through code-space learning.

The main weakness is that the paper doesn't sufficiently address scenarios where PMD might be less advantageous than other approaches. While they mention L1 distance limitations for natural images, they don't explore alternative distance metrics or compare with other Wasserstein approximations. Additionally, the computational complexity of the matching algorithm (O(N³) for Hungarian) could be a concern for very large datasets, though the authors acknowledge this and propose approximations.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that makes a meaningful advancement in distribution distance estimation for deep learning. The theoretical results are sound, the empirical evidence is convincing, and the practical advantages over MMD are well-demonstrated. The paper advances the field by providing a more practical alternative to MMD that addresses several key limitations.

Strengths: Strong theoretical foundation, comprehensive experiments, clear advantages over MMD, practical usefulness.
Weaknesses: Limited discussion of scenarios where PMD might be suboptimal, insufficient exploration of alternative distance metrics.

The paper should be accepted as it makes a significant contribution to the field with both theoretical and practical value.
