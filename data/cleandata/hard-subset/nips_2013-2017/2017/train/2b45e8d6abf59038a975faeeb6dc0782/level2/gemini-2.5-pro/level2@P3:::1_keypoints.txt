- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based metric defined by average cost of minimum weight bipartite matching between two sample sets.  
- Proves a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, showing PMD is a strongly consistent estimator.  
- Correctly derives the link between the linear programming formulation of empirical Wasserstein distance and minimum weight matching; derivation is clear and sound.  
- Proposes a practical learning algorithm that minimizes PMD via stochastic gradient descent and applies it to domain adaptation and generative modeling.  
- Extensive empirical evaluation supports claims, including gradient magnitude analysis and approximation error convergence studies.  
- Claims PMD avoids kernel bandwidth tuning required by MMD, provides stronger learning gradients, and enables efficient mini-batch updates for faster convergence.  
- The argument that the optimal matching remains constant under infinitesimal parameter changes (used for gradient computation) needs a brief discussion of conditions (e.g., uniqueness) or references to envelope/Danskin-type theorems.  
- Recommends including a more detailed derivation of Theorem 3.3 in an appendix for completeness.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow, with clear motivation and helpful figures (e.g., Fig 1).  
- Clearly distinguishes population size N for matching from mini-batch size |B| for SGD, an important practical advantage.  
- The approach is novel: a practical, end-to-end differentiable primal-form use of Wasserstein via matching, positioned as an alternative to WGANs and MMD.  
- PMD is potentially significant: parameter-free, robust optimization behavior, and strong empirical performance across tasks.  
- Main limitation is computational cost of matching (exact O(N^3), approximation O(N^2)), which may hinder scalability to very large populations.  
- Generated sample quality improves over MMD baselines but is not state-of-the-art, suggesting PMD may need architectural enhancements for top-tier generative performance.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded PMD; addresses MMD shortcomings; simple practical algorithm with strong empirical results; clear writing and broad interest.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability concerns due to matching complexity and need for more formal rigor in gradient justification.
