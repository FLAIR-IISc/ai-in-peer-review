## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, intended to overcome limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in deep learning applications. The core idea – formulating distribution distance as a minimum weight matching problem – is well-motivated, particularly concerning MMD’s sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and gradient issues. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and present promising empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling, showcasing improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The theoretical analysis establishing the connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is a significant strength, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method. The comparison with MMD is thorough, highlighting PMD's advantages in terms of hyperparameter tuning, gradient behavior, and potential for smaller mini-batch sizes. The inclusion of experiments on both domain adaptation and generative modeling provides a reasonable assessment of PMD’s versatility.  The empirical evaluation, including convergence studies and an analysis of gradient magnitudes, supports the theoretical claims.

However, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the computational cost of the matching algorithm, particularly for large sample sizes. While the authors mention an approximated matching algorithm, a more detailed comparison of its performance and accuracy against the exact Hungarian algorithm would be valuable.  Furthermore, the choice of the L1 distance metric for PMD feels somewhat arbitrary and a discussion of other potential distance metrics and their impact on performance is missing. The impact of PMD on the generated image *quality* in generative modeling could be better quantified beyond a visual comparison. While it's mentioned images are less noisy, metrics like FID or IS would strengthen the claim.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Novel and theoretically sound approach to estimating Wasserstein distance.
* Demonstrable improvements over MMD in empirical evaluations.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Addresses a relevant problem in deep learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Limited discussion of computational complexity for large datasets.
* Justification for the L1 distance metric could be improved.
* More quantitative evaluation of generative model performance is needed.

Overall, this is a solid contribution with the potential to impact research in areas requiring distribution distance estimation. Despite the minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above. The authors should be encouraged to explore the computational scalability of PMD and investigate the sensitivity to the choice of distance metric.
