This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two empirical populations, and uses it as a differentiable objective for training deep models. The authors prove strong consistency of PMD to the 1-Wasserstein distance via classical optimal transport stability results, and argue PMD remedies common drawbacks of MMD (bandwidth tuning, weak gradients, large batch requirements). Applications to domain adaptation and implicit generative modeling show improvements over MMD in accuracy and convergence speed. The work situates itself relative to MMD and WGAN; however, connections to entropic regularization (Sinkhorn distances) and stochastic OT methods, widely used at NIPS since 2013, are missing experimentally and largely absent from the related work.

Quality: The theoretical contribution is sound but limited (consistency of empirical assignment OT is standard in OT literature). The training algorithm (freeze matching, update parameters; sub-sample with fixed matching) is plausible and empirically effective, though the non-smooth objective and no gradient through matching deserve more analysis. Experimental support is mixed: domain adaptation on Office with MMD comparisons shows gains on several shifts, and convergence curves are informative. Generative results are qualitatively better than MMD in pixel space but still blurry; no quantitative metrics (FID/IS) or comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn OT, or JAN/DANN in domain adaptation are provided. Runtime analysis is helpful, but O(N2)–O(N3) matching remains a concern at scale; no GPU-parallel matching experiments are included.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and organized. Algorithms and complexities are described, and implementation details are provided, though full reproducibility would benefit from released code and more training hyperparameters.

Originality: Using exact/approximate assignment OT as a training loss with a practical matching-freeze trick is a useful engineering contribution, but the core discrepancy is essentially empirical Wasserstein-1/EMD. The lack of discussion and comparisons to Sinkhorn/stochastic OT lowers perceived novelty.

Significance: PMD could be a practical drop-in where MMD is used, with stronger gradients and smaller minibatches. Broader impact requires demonstrating competitiveness with contemporary OT-based methods and GAN baselines.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, parameter-free objective with stronger gradients and faster convergence than MMD
- Clear theoretical grounding and practical training algorithm
- Solid empirical improvements over MMD in two tasks

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to Sinkhorn OT, WGAN(-GP), JAN/DANN; limited metrics
- Scalability concerns and no GPU matching results
- Generative samples remain blurry; no quantitative evaluation

Constructive suggestions:
- Add Sinkhorn/stochastic OT baselines and WGAN(-GP) comparisons with FID/IS
- Evaluate end-to-end CNN adaptation vs. fixed VGG features; include DANN/JAN/CMD baselines
- Report wall-clock vs. accuracy trade-offs with GPU-parallel matching
- Analyze sensitivity to ground cost and provide finite-sample error bounds or variance estimates
- Release code and detailed hyperparameters

Overall: promising and practical idea with incomplete empirical positioning; encourage revision with stronger baselines and metrics.
