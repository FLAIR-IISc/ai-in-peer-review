This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the distance between two distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching between samples from the two distributions, and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors demonstrate empirically that PMD overcomes the drawbacks of MMD (sensitivity to bandwidth, vanishing gradients, large mini-batch sizes) and achieves better performance in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

The core idea is well-motivated. MMD, despite its widespread use, suffers from practical difficulties as highlighted by the authors. The Wasserstein metric offers an appealing alternative, and PMD provides a practical, sample-based estimator. The theoretical result establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric is a significant contribution. The application to domain adaptation and generative modeling are relevant and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method. The empirical results are presented clearly and show consistent improvements over MMD.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The consistency proof is a valuable contribution, grounding the method in established statistical theory.
* **Addresses Practical Limitations of MMD:** The paper directly tackles the common issues with MMD, offering a more stable and efficient alternative.  The claim about stronger gradients is supported by Fig. 2(b), which provides compelling visual evidence.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with optimal transport.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on both domain adaptation and generative modeling show consistent improvements over MMD, indicating practical relevance. The ablation studies (Fig. 5) provide further insights into the impact of different hyperparameters and computational choices.
* **Efficient Implementation Consideration**: The authors discuss the trade-offs between exact and approximate matching algorithms and the impact of mini-batch size, demonstrating an understanding of practical considerations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Comparison to WGAN:** While the paper briefly mentions Wasserstein GAN (WGAN), the comparison is superficial. A more detailed discussion of the differences and trade-offs between PMD and WGAN would be beneficial. For instance, how does PMD handle the Lipschitz constraint issues that WGAN addresses?
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The paper consistently uses the L1 distance within PMD.  The justification for this choice is not fully elaborated. Could other distance metrics lead to better performance in specific applications?  A sensitivity analysis regarding the distance metric would be valuable.
* **Generative Modeling Results:** While the results show that PMD generates less noisy images, the visual comparison (Fig. 4) lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID). This makes it difficult to assess the magnitude of the improvement.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors discuss the time complexity, a more detailed profiling of the computation time for PMD and MMD with varying population sizes would be useful. Is the gain in convergence speed sufficient to offset the computational cost of the matching algorithm?
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the PMD model demonstrated less sensitivity to parameters overall, a more thorough exploration of the impact of different settings on the domain adaptation results would be helpful.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for estimating the distance between distributions.
* PMD addresses practical limitations of existing approaches like MMD.
* The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over MMD in relevant applications.
* The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The comparison to related work (particularly WGAN) could be more comprehensive.
* The evaluation of generative modeling results lacks quantitative metrics.
* A more detailed analysis of the computational cost and parameter sensitivity would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are solid, and the empirical validation is convincing. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly expanding the comparison to WGAN and including quantitative metrics for the generative modeling experiments.
