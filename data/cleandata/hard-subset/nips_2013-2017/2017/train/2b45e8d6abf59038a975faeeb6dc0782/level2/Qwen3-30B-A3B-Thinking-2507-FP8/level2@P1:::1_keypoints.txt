- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based distance estimator using minimum-weight matching between sample populations.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3).  
- Provides a practical training algorithm with implementation details (matching algorithms, mini-batch handling).  
- Applies PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, comparing against MMD.  
- Demonstrates PMD overcomes MMD drawbacks: no kernel bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and effective with smaller mini-batches.  
- Empirical results show improved performance and faster convergence on multiple tasks/datasets (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW).  
- Figures effectively illustrate gradient distributions and convergence behavior (notably Fig. 2 and Fig. 5).  
- Theoretical analysis is sound and well-situated in optimal transport literature.  
- Strengths: novel approach, solid theory, practical benefits over MMD, comprehensive experiments, clear writing and organization.  
- Weakness: domain adaptation gains are not uniform—significant improvement only on 2 of 6 tasks, needing deeper analysis of task-specific differences.  
- Weakness: generative outputs are blurry on SVHN and LFW due to pixel-level L1 loss; more concrete guidance on learning in code space would help.  
- Weakness: computational complexity of matching is noted but not deeply analyzed; scalability to very large datasets needs more discussion.  
- Overall recommendation: accept for NIPS — original, well-supported contributions with clear practical significance for distribution distance estimation.
