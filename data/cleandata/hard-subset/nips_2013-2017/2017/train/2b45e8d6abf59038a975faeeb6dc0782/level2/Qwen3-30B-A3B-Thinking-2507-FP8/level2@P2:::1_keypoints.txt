- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable sample-based distribution distance estimator as an alternative to MMD.  
- Defines PMD as minimum weight matching between sample populations and links it to the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (claims supported by Propositions 3.1–3.2 and Theorem 3.3).  
- Well-structured algorithmic presentation and sound theoretical analysis in Section 3.  
- Empirical results show PMD outperforming MMD on domain adaptation tasks (a→w, a→d) and producing less noisy generative samples.  
- Demonstrates stronger gradients and reduced parameter sensitivity for PMD (Fig. 2b, Fig. 3b–3c).  
- Theorem 3.3’s proof is superficial; authors should explicitly show how Propositions 3.1–3.2 apply to PMD, especially convergence of the optimal transport plan.  
- Lacks comparison to recent alternatives like WGAN-GP and the Cramér distance, limiting positioning in current literature.  
- Computational complexity analysis is incomplete: Hungarian algorithm’s O(N^3) cost isn’t adequately compared to MMD’s O(N^2) or its practical impact on training time.  
- Timing results hint at GPU-accelerated approximate matching but need more thorough discussion and experiments on training-time tradeoffs.  
- Generative modeling results are mixed (blurry SVHN and LFW outputs); missing systematic comparison of PMD in pixel vs code space to justify claims.  
- Claim that PMD works with normal mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100) is under-supported; need direct convergence-speed comparisons with identical batch sizes to MMD.  
- Should discuss scenarios where PMD is disadvantageous (e.g., high-dimensional cost matrices and scalability concerns).  
- Overall: solid, novel contribution with promising empirical and theoretical aspects; recommend acceptance conditional on addressing theoretical gaps, broader baselines, and more complete empirical/complexity analyses.
