- Proposal: Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based distance using minimum-weight bipartite matching as a loss for deep networks.  
- Theory: Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3).  
- Novelty/Significance: Using assignment solutions as a differentiable, parameter-free alternative to MMD and adversarial WGAN approaches is original and impactful.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, with clear motivation, concise method description, helpful pseudocode, and structured theoretical analysis.  
- Empirical performance: PMD matches or outperforms MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling in final performance and convergence speed.  
- Ablations: Parameter-sensitivity studies show PMD requires fewer critical hyperparameters than MMD.  
- Mini-batch/Population findings: Experiments indicate PMD benefits from smaller SGD mini-batches and varying population sizes, enabling faster convergence.  
- Gradient analysis: Empirical gradient-magnitude results suggest PMD yields stronger, more uniform gradients than RBF-kernel MMD.  
- Scalability concern: Exact/approximate matching is computationally expensive (O(N^3) exact, O(N^2) approx), raising practical limits on population size and needing wall-clock comparisons to MMD.  
- Matching approximation: Reusing a single matching for multiple inner-loop SGD updates may lead to a "stale" matching; more discussion/analysis of inner-loop iterations and stability is needed.  
- Ground metric choice: PMD performance hinges on the ground metric (authors use L1); for images, pixel-wise L1 is inadequate and learned perceptual feature distances (e.g., VGG activations) should be discussed.  
- WGAN comparison: Comparison to WGAN is brief; the trade-off between PMD's fixed metric and WGAN's learned critic metric needs a more nuanced discussion.  
- Recommendation: High-quality, well-justified work whose originality, theory, and experiments merit acceptance despite practical scalability concerns.
