- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to MMD for sample-based distribution distance estimation in deep learning.
- Core idea: formulate distance as a minimum-weight matching between samples and prove strong consistency to the first Wasserstein metric.
- Claims practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, and potential for smaller mini-batches.
- Experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling show improved performance and faster convergence vs. MMD.
- Paper is well-motivated, addressing MMD’s sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and gradient issues.
- Theoretical connection to optimal transport/Wasserstein is a strong point and gives solid foundation to PMD.
- Presentation is generally clear, with helpful definitions and pseudocode (Fig. 1).
- Experimental evaluation is limited to two tasks, reducing evidence of broad applicability.
- The “stronger gradients” claim is illustrated but lacks detailed analysis of impact on optimization (e.g., learning rates, initialization).
- Time-complexity discussion cites GPU parallelization but provides no empirical GPU speedup results.
- Choice of L1 distance for PMD is not well-justified; alternatives are not explored.
- Description of the SGD update (Eqs. 3–4) is dense; explanation of why matching is invariant to infinitesimal θ changes is needed.
- More detail is required on the approximate matching algorithm used in generative experiments.
- Experimental setup is terse; exact network architectures, learning rates, and hyperparameters should be specified for reproducibility.
- Relating sample distance to optimal transport is not entirely novel, but the minimum-weight matching formulation and gradient analysis are meaningful contributions.
- Theoretical consistency proof adds value and credibility to the method.
- PMD could be a useful tool for generative models and domain adaptation, reducing hyperparameter tuning and improving stability.
- Broader experimental validation across datasets/tasks and exploration of other applications (e.g., metric learning) would increase significance.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel matching formulation, theoretical Wasserstein link and consistency, empirical gains vs. MMD, and practical benefits (less tuning).
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experimental scope, insufficient analysis of stronger gradients’ effects, lack of alternative distance discussion, and missing GPU empirical results.
- Overall recommendation: accept conditional on addressing weaknesses—expand experiments, analyze practical benefits (gradients, hyperparameters), justify L1 choice, and report GPU acceleration results.
