Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): average cost of minimum-weight bipartite matching between sample sets used as a differentiable loss for training deep networks.  
Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3).  
Training algorithm uses an outer loop to compute optimal matching and an inner loop of several SGD updates with the matching fixed.  
Positions PMD as an alternative to MMD: hyperparameter-free, yields stronger/more uniform gradients, allows smaller mini-batches and faster convergence.  
Empirical results show PMD is competitive with or outperforms MMD on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling.  
Novel and elegant idea: direct use of matching cost as a training loss leveraging Wasserstein properties.  
Solid theoretical foundation: clear connection to Wasserstein metric and consistency proof.  
Clear practical advantages: elimination of kernel bandwidth, improved gradient behavior, and decoupling of population vs. SGD mini-batch sizes.  
Strong empirical evaluation with thorough experiments and ablations validating population and mini-batch effects.  
Computational complexity is a drawback: exact Hungarian O(N^3) (approx O(N^2)) may be a bottleneck and lacks GPU implementation/analysis.  
Dependence on the ground metric (used L1) may harm perceptual quality; needs study of learned or feature-space metrics.  
Missing direct empirical comparison to Wasserstein GANs to position PMD among Wasserstein-based generative methods.  
Training heuristic concern: computing matching once then multiple SGD steps assumes matching stability; sensitivity to inner-loop iteration count is unclear.  
Paper is very clear, high-quality, highly original, and potentially significant across multiple domains.  
Overall recommendation: strong accept.
