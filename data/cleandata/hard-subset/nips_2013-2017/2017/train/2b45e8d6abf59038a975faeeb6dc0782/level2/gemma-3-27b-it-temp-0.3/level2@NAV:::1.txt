## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the distance between probability distributions, particularly within the context of deep learning. The core idea – framing the distance estimation as a minimum weight matching problem – is well-motivated by the limitations of MMD (sensitivity to bandwidth, vanishing gradients, large batch sizes). The authors demonstrate a theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, proving strong consistency. They then apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively highlights the challenges of sample-based divergence estimation and positions PMD as a solution. The mathematical formulation of PMD is concise and the connection to the Wasserstein metric is clearly established. The experimental setup is reasonable, utilizing standard datasets and comparing against a strong baseline (MMD). The empirical results demonstrate a clear advantage for PMD, particularly in convergence speed, which is a significant practical benefit. The inclusion of ablation studies (Fig. 5) regarding population size and matching algorithm is valuable.

The novelty of the work lies in the formulation of PMD and its theoretical grounding. While the idea of using optimal transport for distribution comparison isn’t new, framing it as a minimum weight matching problem and demonstrating its advantages over MMD in a deep learning context is a valuable contribution. The authors adequately reference relevant work, including MMD, Wasserstein GANs, and optimal transport theory.

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity of the matching algorithm, especially for large-scale datasets. While the authors mention approximations, a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy and speed would be helpful. Additionally, the choice of the L1 distance for PMD seems somewhat arbitrary; exploring other distance metrics and their impact on performance could strengthen the results. Finally, the blurry images generated in the generative modeling experiments suggest limitations of using L1 distance in pixel space, and this could be discussed further.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to distribution distance estimation.
*   Demonstrated improvements over MMD in both performance and convergence speed.
*   Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
*   Relevant to a broad audience in deep learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity of the matching algorithm could be a limitation for very large datasets.
*   Limited exploration of alternative distance metrics within PMD.
*   Generative modeling results show some limitations with the chosen distance metric.

**Overall:**

Despite some minor limitations, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution and compelling experimental results. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. The authors should elaborate on the computational complexity and explore the impact of different distance metrics.
