Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets from two distributions under a ground metric d(·,·). PMD is exactly the empirical Wasserstein-1 (Earth Mover’s) distance for equal sample sizes, obtained by solving an assignment problem. The authors use PMD as a differentiable training objective for implicit models by not backpropagating through the discrete matching (arguing the matching is locally constant almost everywhere), and introduce a practical training scheme that recomputes the matching on a larger “population” and performs several SGD steps using smaller minibatches of matched pairs. They prove strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of W1 by combining standard results on weak convergence of empirical measures and stability of optimal transport. Empirically, PMD is compared against MMD on Office domain adaptation and MNIST/SVHN/LFW generation, and is reported to (i) avoid the kernel bandwidth tuning of MMD, (ii) provide stronger gradients, (iii) work with smaller minibatches, and (iv) converge faster with better or comparable performance.

Relation to prior work
PMD is the classic empirical Wasserstein-1 distance with one-to-one matching. The consistency statement follows directly from standard OT results (e.g., Villani). The paper positions PMD against MMD and WGAN; however, it omits highly relevant developments on computational OT, notably entropically regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013), which provide differentiable, GPU-friendly approximations with O(N^2) complexity and have been used in learning settings. Also related are energy/Cramér distances and recent MMD-GANs. In domain adaptation, there are strong baselines beyond MMD (e.g., DANN, JAN, CMD) that are not empirically compared.

Quality
- Theory: The connection to W1 and strong consistency are correct but largely standard. The decision to ignore derivatives through the matching is reasonable (the mapping is piecewise constant; subgradients exist almost everywhere), but the paper should more carefully justify unbiasedness claims (Eq. 3) and clarify that the inner-loop updates with stale M introduce bias. No finite-sample bounds or rates are provided (known to be dimension-dependent for W1).
- Algorithms: The training heuristic (compute matching on a large population, then do multiple small-minibatch SGD steps) is sensible and practical; the complexity discussion is honest. The approximate matching is a useful engineering choice, but the impact on optimization and variance is not analyzed.
- Empirics: The Office results show improvements over MMD on some shifts and parity on others; the convergence speed plot is convincing. For generation, comparisons are only to GMMN/MMD; the absence of WGAN/WGAN-GP or Sinkhorn-based baselines makes it hard to assess real significance. Visuals on SVHN/LFW show blur (as expected with pixel L1); quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception/FID) are missing. Reporting wall-clock time vs. dataset size and population N would strengthen the scalability claim.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The definition of PMD, algorithmic description, and empirical sections are accessible. Missing or imprecise points:
- Precisely state when gradients are unbiased (single-step with fixed matching) vs. biased (multiple inner steps).
- Discuss the curse of dimensionality for W1 sample complexity; the toy study suggests slow convergence (N^−0.23).
- Provide more detail on the approximate matching algorithm and its accuracy.

Originality
The core construct (empirical W1 via minimum-cost matching) and its consistency are known. The originality lies in the particular training recipe (population matching with subsampled updates) and the empirical observation that it yields stronger gradients and works with smaller minibatches than MMD. Given existing differentiable OT methods (Sinkhorn) and adversarial Wasserstein training, the conceptual novelty is modest.

Significance
PMD is a simple, parameter-free, and broadly applicable objective that practitioners might find attractive, especially for domain adaptation. However, without comparisons to stronger contemporary baselines (WGAN variants, Sinkhorn) and without addressing scalability beyond a few thousand samples, the impact is limited. The method’s reliance on O(N^2–N^3) matching, even with approximations, may constrain usage in large-scale settings where adversarial critics or Sinkhorn are prevalent.

Pros
- Simple, principled objective directly tied to W1; no kernel bandwidth.
- Clear training procedure; practical insight that smaller minibatches suffice.
- Empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence vs. MMD.
- Positive results on Office domain adaptation and acceptable generation quality.

Cons
- Core idea is essentially empirical W1; theoretical results are standard.
- Missing key related work (Sinkhorn/entropic OT) and no empirical comparison to it or to WGAN(-GP).
- Limited quantitative evaluation for generation; blurry outputs with pixel L1 and no standard metrics.
- Scalability concerns; matching remains a bottleneck, and GPU-accelerated OT alternatives are not discussed.
- Unclear guarantees for the inner-loop updates with stale matchings.

Questions for the authors
- How many inner-loop SGD steps are performed per matching recomputation? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- How does PMD scale in high dimensions and on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet-sized features) with respect to N and runtime?
- Can you report quantitative generation metrics (Inception/FID) and wall-clock comparisons vs. MMD and WGAN?
- How does PMD perform when using a learned feature space or perceptual cost d? Could PMD be combined with a learned embedding?
- What is the effect of approximate matching quality on final performance?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP and to Sinkhorn-regularized OT, both in accuracy and speed.
- Add quantitative generative metrics and fair compute budgets across methods.
- Discuss and, if possible, provide rates or cite known finite-sample bounds for W1; analyze variance vs. N and dimensionality.
- Clarify gradient properties and biases introduced by stale matchings; consider subgradient interpretations.
- Explore alternative ground metrics (feature-space distances, learned metrics) to mitigate pixel-space blur.
- Cite and discuss relevant OT literature (Cuturi 2013 and follow-ups) and stronger domain adaptation baselines.

Overall assessment
A useful and practical application of empirical Wasserstein distance to deep learning objectives, with clear advantages over MMD in some settings. However, novelty is limited and important related work and baselines are missing. With additional comparisons and analysis, this could be a solid contribution; in its current form, I lean toward a borderline/weak reject.
