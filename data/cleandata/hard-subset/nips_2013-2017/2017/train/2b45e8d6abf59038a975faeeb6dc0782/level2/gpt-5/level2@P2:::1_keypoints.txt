- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): average cost of minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. samples (empirical W1 for equal sizes).
- Uses PMD as a differentiable training objective by not backpropagating through discrete matching and recomputing matchings on a larger population with small-minibatch SGD updates.
- Proves strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of W1 by combining weak convergence of empirical measures and OT stability.
- Empirically claims PMD avoids MMD kernel bandwidth tuning, provides stronger gradients, works with smaller minibatches, and converges faster or comparably.
- PMD is essentially the classic empirical Wasserstein-1 with one-to-one matching; consistency results are standard from OT literature.
- Paper omits key related work on computational OT, notably entropic/Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi 2013) and differentiable, GPU-friendly approximations.
- Relation to other distances (energy/Cramér) and stronger domain-adaptation baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD) is not discussed or compared empirically.
- Theory section is correct but standard; claims about unbiased gradients need clearer justification and the inner-loop stale matching introduces bias.
- No finite-sample rates are provided; W1 sample complexity and the curse of dimensionality are not analyzed despite empirical hints of slow convergence.
- The proposed training heuristic (population matching + multiple small SGD steps) is practical, but the optimization/variance impact of approximate/stale matchings is unanalyzed.
- Complexity discussion is honest; matching is O(N^2–N^3) and may limit scalability despite approximations.
- Office domain adaptation shows some improvement over MMD, but generation experiments compare only to GMMN/MMD, lacking WGAN/WGAN-GP or Sinkhorn baselines.
- Generative visuals on SVHN/LFW are blurry (expected with pixel L1); no standard quantitative metrics (Inception/FID) are reported.
- Wall-clock runtime vs. dataset size and population N is not reported, weakening scalability claims.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized but should explicitly state when gradients are unbiased vs. biased and detail the approximate matching algorithm and accuracy.
- Originality is modest: core idea known, novelty lies in the specific training recipe and empirical observations about minibatch behavior.
- Significance is limited without comparisons to modern OT/Wasserstein methods and tests on larger-scale datasets; computational cost may hinder adoption.
- Pros: simple parameter-free objective tied to W1; practical training recipe; empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence vs. MMD; positive domain-adaptation results.
- Cons: core idea is known; missing key OT and adversarial baselines; limited quantitative generative evaluation; scalability and inner-loop guarantees unclear.
- Key questions for authors: number/sensitivity of inner-loop SGD steps per matching, scaling to higher dimensions/larger datasets, quantitative metrics and wall-clock comparisons, performance with learned/perceptual costs, and effect of approximate matching quality.
- Suggested improvements: compare to WGAN/WGAN-GP and Sinkhorn OT, add quantitative generative metrics and compute budgets, discuss finite-sample rates and dimensionality effects, clarify gradient bias from stale matchings, and explore learned/feature-space ground metrics.
- Overall assessment: useful and practical application of empirical W1 with some advantages over MMD, but limited novelty, missing critical baselines/analysis, and borderline/weak reject in current form.
