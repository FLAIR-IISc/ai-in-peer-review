

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel approach for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. The authors define PMD as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution and prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. They apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, demonstrating that it overcomes several drawbacks of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), including the need for kernel bandwidth tuning, weak gradients, and large mini-batch sizes.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a theoretically rigorous foundation for PMD, with a clear proof of Theorem 3.3 establishing its strong consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein metric. Second, it offers a practical algorithm for using PMD as a training objective, with careful attention to implementation details like matching algorithms and mini-batch considerations. Third, it provides thorough empirical comparisons with MMD across multiple deep learning tasks, demonstrating PMD's advantages in both performance and convergence speed.

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The theoretical analysis is sound, with appropriate references to related work in optimal transport theory and distribution distance estimation. The experimental section is comprehensive, with detailed results on the Office dataset for domain adaptation and on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW for generative modeling. The figures (particularly Fig. 2 and Fig. 5) effectively illustrate key points about gradient distributions and convergence behavior.

The paper's strengths include its clear theoretical contribution, strong empirical validation, and practical advantages over MMD. The authors effectively address the limitations of MMD, such as the sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and weak gradients, by demonstrating that PMD provides stronger gradients (Fig. 2b) and can use smaller mini-batch sizes (Fig. 5a). The comparison with MMD is fair and well-supported by experiments, and the authors acknowledge limitations, such as the blurry images generated on SVHN and LFW due to using pixel-level L1 distance.

A few areas for improvement could enhance the paper. While the paper claims PMD "outperforms MMD on both tasks," the domain adaptation results (Table 1) show that PMD only significantly outperforms MMD on two of six domain adaptation tasks (a→w and a→d), while being comparable on the others. A more detailed analysis of why PMD performs better on some domain adaptation tasks than others would strengthen the paper. Additionally, while the paper mentions that the blurry images in generative modeling could be addressed by learning in code space, it would be beneficial to provide more specific guidance on how to implement this improvement.

The computational complexity of the matching algorithm is mentioned but not deeply analyzed. While the authors note that both exact and approximate matching can be parallelized on GPU, a more detailed discussion of how this affects the practical scalability of PMD with very large datasets would be valuable.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field. The theoretical foundation is solid, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the practical advantages over MMD are clearly demonstrated. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in deep learning with a novel and well-supported solution. The writing is clear, the organization is logical, and the presentation of results is thorough.

The paper's significance is evident in its potential to improve training efficiency for various deep learning tasks, particularly those requiring distribution distance estimation. The results demonstrate that PMD can achieve better performance with faster convergence, which is valuable for both researchers and practitioners.

Strengths:
- Novel approach to distribution distance estimation
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear proof
- Practical advantages over MMD (no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size)
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple tasks
- Clear writing and organization

Weaknesses:
- Limited analysis of task-specific performance differences in domain adaptation
- Insufficient discussion of solutions for blurry generative images
- Need for more detailed computational complexity analysis

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The contribution is original, well-supported by theory and experiments, and has clear significance for the field. The paper makes a valuable addition to the literature on distribution distance estimation and has practical implications for deep learning practitioners. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the overall quality of the work.
