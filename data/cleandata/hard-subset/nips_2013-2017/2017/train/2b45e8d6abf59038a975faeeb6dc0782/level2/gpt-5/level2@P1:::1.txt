Summary
The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based, differentiable surrogate for the 1-Wasserstein distance that computes the minimum-weight bipartite matching between two sample sets. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein metric by appealing to Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport. They propose an SGD-based learning algorithm that treats the matching as fixed over a small number of updates (no gradient through the discrete assignment) and optionally sub-samples matched pairs for cheaper gradients. Compared to MMD, PMD is claimed to have (i) no kernel bandwidth, (ii) stronger gradients, and (iii) smaller effective mini-batch requirements. Empirically, the paper evaluates PMD on domain adaptation (Office benchmark) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), finding that PMD often outperforms MMD and converges faster. Timing and sensitivity analyses suggest approximate O(N^2) matching is competitive with SGD cost and that small sub-batches are beneficial.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely related to optimal transport (OT)-based distances and their use in learning. PMD is essentially OT between empirical measures with uniform weights and 1-1 assignments (i.e., the assignment problem), a well-studied special case. The paper contrasts PMD with MMD and WGAN, noting differences in hyperparameters and scaling. However, there are important missing connections: entropically regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) provide GPU-friendly, differentiable approximations widely used since 2013; stochastic OT for large-scale settings (e.g., Genevay et al., 2016) and sliced Wasserstein distances offer alternative scalable, differentiable formulations; OT for domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al., 2016) is also highly relevant. The Cramér/energy distance (Bellemare et al., 2017) is cited but not compared empirically.

Strengths
- Clear and simple formulation with a crisp connection to Wasserstein-1, and a straightforward proof of strong consistency.
- Practical training algorithm that leverages fixed matching over inner-loop SGD updates and sub-sampling, with good empirical behavior.
- Insightful discussion and evidence that PMD provides stronger and more uniform gradients than MMD, addressing known saturation issues.
- Solid ablation on population/mini-batch sizes, matching algorithm choice, and timing breakdowns.
- Demonstrates utility on two tasks (domain adaptation and generation) and provides qualitative and some quantitative convergence results.

Weaknesses
- Missing key baselines and related work. No comparisons to Sinkhorn OT, sliced Wasserstein, WGAN(-GP), or energy/Cramér distance on generation; no comparison to established domain adaptation methods (e.g., DANN, JAN, CMD, CORAL, OTDA). This limits claims of significance.
- Generative modeling evaluation is mostly qualitative; no standard metrics (e.g., Inception score, FID) and small datasets. Reported samples are blurry in pixel space and improvements rely on autoencoder codes.
- The optimization relies on ignoring derivatives through the matching. While reasonable almost everywhere, the claim of “unbiased stochastic gradient” would benefit from a more careful statement (piecewise-constant assignment; subgradients; measure-zero assignment switches; stability of the inner loop).
- Computational scalability remains a concern for large N and high dimensions; memory and time for N^2 cost matrices are nontrivial. The paper mentions GPU parallelization but does not implement it; faster alternatives (e.g., Sinkhorn) are not discussed experimentally.
- “Parameter-free” is overstated: the choice of ground metric (L1), population size N, inner-loop steps, and approximate matching parameters matter and can affect results.
- Theory stops at consistency; no finite-sample rates or generalization guarantees as a training loss.

Quality
Technically sound in its main claims (PMD = empirical OT/assignment; strong consistency under standard assumptions). The optimization strategy is plausible and empirically validated. Experimental comparisons, however, are too narrow to support broad claims of superiority beyond MMD.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The connection to OT is clearly developed. The optimization section could clarify conditions under which ignoring gradients through M is justified and how often re-matching occurs. Experimental details are mostly sufficient; releasing code would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. Using empirical OT/assignment as a training loss is natural and has been explored in OT literature. The contributions are in the specific PMD training heuristic (fixed matching over inner loops, sub-sampling) and the comparative analysis vs. MMD. Novelty would be stronger with broader comparisons and analysis against established OT approximations.

Significance
Potentially useful in practice as a simple, parameter-light alternative to MMD with stronger gradients. However, without comparisons to Sinkhorn OT, sliced Wasserstein, and WGAN(-GP), the impact is unclear. The domain adaptation results are promising but limited.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please compare to entropically regularized OT (Sinkhorn) and sliced Wasserstein losses, both in speed and performance. These are natural, scalable, GPU-friendly baselines.
- Provide quantitative generative metrics (FID/Inception) and add a stronger baseline (e.g., WGAN-GP). Include CIFAR-10 or a similar benchmark.
- Clarify the theoretical status of the gradient estimator when the matching changes. Can you formalize conditions for an envelope-theorem-type argument or provide a subgradient justification?
- Report memory/time for cost matrix construction and assignment for your largest N, and discuss scalability to higher dimensions and datasets.
- For domain adaptation, please include DANN and JAN/CMD baselines; discuss class-conditional alignment.
- Analyze sensitivity to the ground metric and to approximate matching quality; can learned feature spaces or perceptual distances mitigate blurriness without AEs?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, theoretically grounded alternative to MMD with convincing gradient behavior.
- Empirical evidence of faster convergence and competitive performance.
- Clear exposition and practical algorithmic insights.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing key baselines (Sinkhorn, WGAN-GP, sliced Wasserstein; DANN/JAN) and quantitative metrics.
- Limited datasets; generative results remain qualitative with blurriness in pixel space.
- Scalability and differentiability-through-matching not fully addressed.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is sound and promising, but the experimental scope and baselines fall short for NIPS. Strengthening comparisons to modern OT-based and adversarial methods and adding quantitative evaluations would likely make this work publishable.

Reviewer confidence
Moderate. Knowledgeable about OT, MMD, and deep generative modeling; not privy to the authors’ code.
