- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based metric framing distribution distance as minimum-weight bipartite matching cost.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Positions PMD as a practical, parameter-free alternative to MMD and dual-form Wasserstein approximations (e.g., WGANs).  
- Main strengths: clear motivation, strong theoretical grounding, and convincing empirical validation.  
- Advantages over MMD: fewer hyperparameters (no kernel bandwidth), stronger gradients, and effective with smaller mini-batches.  
- Comprehensive experiments in domain adaptation and generative modeling show PMD is competitive or superior and often converges faster.  
- Ablation studies on population and mini-batch sizes support the method’s claims.  
- Original, well-written, and of significant practical interest to deep learning practitioners.  
- Primary weakness: computational cost of exact matching (O(N^3)) may limit scalability; O(N^2) approximations and GPU parallelization help but remain a bottleneck versus MMD.  
- Generative results on natural images (SVHN, LFW) remain blurry, likely due to using simple L1 distance for matching.  
- Suggested improvement: explore learned perceptual metrics for the matching cost d(·,·).  
- Overall assessment: high-quality work with strengths outweighing weaknesses; recommendation to accept.
