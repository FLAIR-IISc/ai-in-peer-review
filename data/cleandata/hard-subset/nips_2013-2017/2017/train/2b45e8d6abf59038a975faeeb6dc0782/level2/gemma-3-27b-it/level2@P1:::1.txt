This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the distance between two distributions based on samples, particularly in the context of deep learning. The core idea is to formulate the distance as a minimum weight matching problem between samples from the two distributions, proving its strong consistency as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors demonstrate the advantages of PMD—no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and potentially smaller mini-batch sizes—through experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing genuine drawbacks of MMD, namely the sensitivity to the kernel bandwidth and potential gradient issues. The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is a strong point, providing a solid foundation for PMD. The experimental results, while limited to two tasks, are encouraging, demonstrating PMD's potential. The authors carefully compare PMD with MMD and highlight the practical benefits like reduced hyperparameter tuning and faster convergence. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, with clear definitions and explanations. The inclusion of pseudocode (Fig. 1) is helpful.

However, there are several areas for improvement.

**Quality:** While the theoretical arguments are sound, the practical implications of those arguments could be further explored. For example, the statement about “stronger gradients” is supported by Fig. 2(b), but the precise impact of these stronger gradients on optimization is not thoroughly investigated. Does PMD allow for larger learning rates? Does it require less careful initialization? Showing this would strengthen the claims.  The time complexity analysis mentions GPU parallelization, but doesn't present any empirical results demonstrating those speedups.  The choice of L1 distance for PMD is not extensively justified.  While it’s mentioned that L1 distance may not be ideal for natural images, the paper doesn’t explore alternative distance metrics within the PMD framework.

**Clarity:**  The description of the stochastic gradient descent update (Eq. 3 & 4) is somewhat dense. Elaborating on *why* the matching doesn't change for infinitesimal changes in θ would improve understanding.  More detailed explanations on the approximate matching algorithm used in the generative modeling experiments would also be beneficial.  The experimental setup section feels a bit terse. Specifying the exact network architectures used, learning rates, and other hyperparameters would enhance reproducibility.

**Originality:** The idea of relating sample distance to optimal transport is not entirely novel. However, the specific formulation as a minimum weight matching problem and the demonstration of its advantages over MMD, particularly its gradient behavior, represent a significant contribution.  The work builds upon existing concepts but provides a concrete and practical alternative. The theoretical consistency proof is also a valuable addition.

**Significance:** PMD has the potential to be a valuable tool for researchers working with generative models and domain adaptation, where estimating distributional distances is crucial. Overcoming the limitations of MMD could lead to more stable and efficient training.  The reduced hyperparameter tuning requirement is also a practical benefit. However, the current experiments are somewhat limited. Demonstrating PMD's effectiveness on a wider range of datasets and tasks would significantly increase its impact.  Exploring the applicability of PMD to other areas like metric learning could also broaden its significance.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of distributional distance as a minimum weight matching problem.
*   Theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric and strong consistency proof.
*   Empirical evidence of improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.
*   Practical advantages such as reduced hyperparameter tuning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of the impact of stronger gradients.
*   Insufficient discussion of alternative distance metrics for PMD.
*   Missing empirical results on GPU acceleration.



Overall, this is a solid paper with a promising approach. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly expanding the experimental evaluation and providing more detailed analysis of the practical benefits of PMD. Addressing the points about GPU acceleration and justification for the L1 distance would further strengthen the work.
