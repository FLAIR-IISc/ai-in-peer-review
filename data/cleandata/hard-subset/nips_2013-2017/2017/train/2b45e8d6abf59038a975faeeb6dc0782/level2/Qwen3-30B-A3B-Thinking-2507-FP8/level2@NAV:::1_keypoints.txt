- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based distribution distance using minimum weight matching, claimed to consistently estimate the 1-Wasserstein metric and be practical for deep learning.

- Theorem 3.3 establishes PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, with solid optimal transport foundations.

- PMD offers practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and effectiveness with smaller mini-batches.

- Section 3.2 and Figure 2b provide gradient analysis showing PMD yields stronger gradients than MMD.

- Empirical evaluation on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) shows consistent improvements over MMD across metrics.

- Ablation studies confirm the claimed benefits regarding mini-batch size and population size.

- Technical quality is high: rigorous theory, well-designed experiments, clear writing, and strong practical motivation.

- Authors acknowledge limitations, notably that L1 distance may be suboptimal for natural images and suggesting code-space learning as an improvement.

- Main weakness: limited discussion of situations where PMD may be suboptimal and insufficient exploration of alternative distance metrics or other Wasserstein approximations.

- Computational complexity of matching (O(N^3) Hungarian) is a potential concern for large datasets, though approximations are discussed.

- Overall verdict: high-quality contribution with sound theory and convincing empirical evidence; recommend acceptance.

- Strengths: strong theoretical foundation, comprehensive experiments, clear practical advantages over MMD; Weaknesses: limited exploration of suboptimal scenarios and alternative metrics.
