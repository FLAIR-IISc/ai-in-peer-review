This paper proposes the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based statistical distance for comparing two distributions, and demonstrates its application as a loss function for training deep neural networks. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the respective distributions. The central theoretical contribution is a proof that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). The paper positions PMD as an alternative to the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD avoids MMD's sensitive kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, provides stronger gradients for optimization, and allows for more efficient stochastic gradient updates using smaller mini-batches. These claims are evaluated on two tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, where PMD is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD in terms of both final performance and convergence speed. The work connects to the rich literature on integral probability metrics (like MMD) and optimal transport (like the Wasserstein metric), offering a direct, primal-form, parameter-free estimator for the W1 distance, which contrasts with recent dual-form, critic-based approaches like the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of using the solution to the assignment problem (minimum weight matching) as a differentiable loss function for training deep networks is novel, elegant, and well-motivated. It provides a direct computational bridge between the theory of optimal transport and practical deep learning, offering a distinct alternative to both kernel-based methods (MMD) and adversary-based methods (WGAN). The potential for a parameter-free, robust distribution distance is highly significant for the field.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. Theorem 3.3, which establishes that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, is a key result that justifies the use of PMD as a principled proxy for a true metric on probability distributions.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the method is explained concisely in Section 2 with a helpful pseudocode and illustration (Fig. 1), and the theoretical analysis in Section 3 is well-structured. The connection to and advantages over MMD are articulated persuasively.

4.  **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides compelling support for the paper's claims.
    *   The comparison with MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling shows competitive or superior performance.
    *   The ablation studies are particularly effective. The analysis of parameter sensitivity (Fig. 3b, 3c) convincingly demonstrates that PMD has fewer critical hyperparameters to tune than MMD.
    *   The study on the effect of population size and mini-batch size (Fig. 5a, 5b) strongly supports the claim that PMD benefits from smaller SGD mini-batches, leading to faster convergence.
    *   The gradient magnitude analysis (Fig. 2b) provides a clear and intuitive empirical validation for the theoretical argument that PMD provides stronger, more uniform gradients than MMD with an RBF kernel.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The primary weakness of PMD is the computational cost of solving the assignment problem, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. This is computationally more demanding than MMD, which is O(N^2). While the authors argue this is acceptable and show timing breakdowns in Fig 5c, this remains a significant concern for scaling to larger population sizes (N). The paper would be strengthened by a more direct wall-clock time comparison to convergence against MMD and a discussion of the practical limits of N. While GPU acceleration is mentioned as future work, its current absence limits the immediate practical appeal for very large-scale problems.

2.  **Approximation in the Learning Algorithm:** The algorithm in Figure 1 computes the matching M* once and then performs several SGD updates on mini-batches sampled from the matched pairs. The assumption is that M* does not change much during these inner-loop updates. While this is a reasonable heuristic for efficiency, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on its validity. For instance, how many inner-loop iterations are typically performed? Is there a risk of the matching becoming "stale," and how might that affect training stability or convergence?

3.  **Discussion of the Ground Metric d(x, y):** The performance of PMD is fundamentally tied to the choice of the ground metric `d(·, ·)`. The paper uses the L1 distance in all experiments. As the authors correctly note, the blurry results in the generative modeling task are likely due to the inadequacy of a pixel-wise L1 distance for natural images. While this is not a flaw of the PMD framework itself, the paper should more explicitly discuss the importance of this choice. A brief discussion on how PMD could be combined with learned perceptual feature spaces (e.g., using distances between activations in a pre-trained VGG network) as the ground metric would strengthen the paper and highlight promising avenues for future work.

4.  **Comparison to WGAN:** The comparison to WGAN is somewhat brief. The paper should elaborate more on the trade-offs. PMD's key advantage is being a parameter-free, direct estimator of W1 for a *fixed* ground metric. In contrast, WGAN approximates W1 with respect to a metric that is *learned* by the critic network, which can adapt to the data's underlying structure but introduces a complex minimax optimization problem. A more nuanced discussion of this "fixed metric vs. learned metric" trade-off would better situate PMD in the current landscape of generative models.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper introducing a novel, principled, and effective method for comparing distributions. The strengths in its originality, theoretical grounding, and strong empirical validation far outweigh the weaknesses. The primary concern regarding scalability is a practical trade-off rather than a fundamental flaw. The paper is a valuable contribution to the NIPS community and should be accepted. The suggestions above are intended to help improve the final version of the manuscript.
