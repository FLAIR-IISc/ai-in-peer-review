

The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable estimator of the distance between two distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD as an objective and demonstrates its application to domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, claiming advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) including no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by establishing the connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, providing a rigorous proof of strong consistency. The theoretical analysis clearly demonstrates the relationship between PMMD and both the Wasserstein metric and MMD, with well-justified comparisons of their properties. The experimental results are comprehensive, showing PMD outperforms MMD on both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, with the advantage being particularly pronounced in convergence speed. The empirical validation of gradient strength (Figure 2b) and the analysis of mini-batch size effects (Figure 5a) provide convincing evidence for the claimed advantages.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail to allow reproducibility. The theoretical claims are properly supported, and the experimental setup is appropriate for the claims being made. The connection to prior work is adequate, though the paper could better distinguish itself from recent related work like the Cramer distance (Bellemare et al., 2017) and other Wasserstein approximations.

The main weakness is the lack of theoretical guarantees on the optimization process using PMD. While the paper demonstrates empirical advantages in convergence speed, it does not provide theoretical analysis of the optimization landscape or convergence rates. The claim about smaller mini-batch size requires more justification; while Figure 5a shows PMD benefits from smaller mini-batch sizes, the paper doesn't explain why this is theoretically advantageous compared to MMD's requirement for large mini-batches. The paper also doesn't thoroughly explore the limitations of PMD, particularly regarding high-dimensional data where the matching problem becomes computationally challenging.

Another minor weakness is the use of L1 distance in the matching without exploring alternative distance metrics that might be more suitable for specific applications. The paper mentions that pixel-level L1 distance is not ideal for natural images (Section 5.2), but doesn't explore how PMD might be adapted for different data types.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distribution distance estimation in deep learning. PMD addresses practical limitations of MMD that have been recognized in the literature (e.g., kernel bandwidth tuning, weak gradients), and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages. The theoretical foundation provides confidence in the estimator's properties, and the applications to domain adaptation and generative modeling are well-chosen and relevant to current deep learning research.

The significance of this work is strong, as it offers a practical alternative to MMD that could simplify training procedures in many deep learning applications. The claims about avoiding bandwidth hyper-parameter tuning and achieving faster convergence are particularly valuable for practitioners. While the paper doesn't claim to be a complete solution to all distribution distance estimation problems, it makes a compelling case for PMD as a useful addition to the toolkit of deep learning practitioners.

The paper would benefit from addressing the optimization theory gap and providing more detailed analysis of the mini-batch size claims. The authors could also explore the performance of PMD with different distance metrics and investigate its behavior on higher-dimensional data to better understand its limitations. However, these are reasonable suggestions for future work rather than essential revisions for the current paper.

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound approach to distribution distance estimation with clear empirical advantages over MMD. The contributions are significant enough to warrant acceptance, with the main limitations being areas for future work rather than flaws in the current presentation. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a practical, differentiable estimator that overcomes key limitations of existing methods.
