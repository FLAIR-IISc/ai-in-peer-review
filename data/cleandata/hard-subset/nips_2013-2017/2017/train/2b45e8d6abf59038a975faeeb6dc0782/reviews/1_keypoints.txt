- Paper defines Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches.
- Wasserstein distance is computed by an exact O(N^3) or approximate O(N^2) algorithm.
- Pros: experiments computing Wasserstein on minibatches are interesting.
- Generated images from PMD are not as good as those from Wasserstein GAN.
- PMD requires large minibatch size N to estimate Wasserstein for diverse, multimodal distributions.
- Likely failure case: matching mixtures of Gaussians (including learning variances) would expose problems.
- With small N, optimization may have a global minimum different from the true distribution (e.g., learned distribution with lower entropy).
- SVHN samples exhibit low diversity; digit '8' appears disproportionately often.
- Minor typo: Line 141 "usally" → "usually".
- Reviewer requests authors clarify limitations of PMD and MMD.
- MMD can work with very small minibatches (batch_size=2) using the unbiased estimator from "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" and can be trained with SGD to converge.
- PMD does not have a known unbiased estimator of the gradient.
