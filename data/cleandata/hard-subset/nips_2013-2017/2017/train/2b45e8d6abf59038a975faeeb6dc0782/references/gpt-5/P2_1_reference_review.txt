Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets from two distributions, as a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance. The authors (i) formalize PMD as empirical optimal transport (OT) on equal-mass empirical measures, (ii) argue and prove (via standard OT stability) that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein metric, (iii) propose using PMD as a differentiable training objective where gradients do not backpropagate through the discrete matching (invoking continuity/Danskin-like reasoning), with an inner-loop SGD that reuses a fixed matching and allows sub-sampling, and (iv) provide experiments on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), comparing to MMD. They report that PMD needs no bandwidth tuning, produces stronger gradients, can use smaller minibatches, and converges faster than MMD, with competitive or improved performance.

Relation to prior work
- PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (a.k.a. Earth Mover’s Distance, EMD) for equal-size empirical measures and L1 ground cost; the linear assignment equivalence is classical. The consistency proof follows standard results (Varadarajan + Villani’s stability).
- The paper compares to MMD and WGAN, but omits key related work on computational OT and learning with OT, notably Cuturi’s Sinkhorn distances (2013) and follow-ups that enable fast, differentiable OT with entropic regularization (and backprop-through-Sinkhorn), as well as OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al., 2016) that use OT plans/regularization for aligning domains. These omissions make the novelty less clear and the positioning incomplete.
- Claims about differentiability vis-à-vis dual formulations are somewhat misleading: dual OT via a critic (WGAN) is differentiable; entropic OT and Sinkhorn are also differentiable and widely used for training.

Quality
- Theory: The “strong consistency of PMD” is correct but largely a restatement of known facts about empirical OT; no new rates or bounds are given. The gradient treatment relies on the matching being locally constant; this is essentially Danskin’s theorem. The paper asserts unbiased stochastic gradients (Eq. 3) without a formal statement of assumptions (uniqueness, measure-zero nondifferentiabilities); Eq. 4 introduces a biased sub-sampling approximation whose effect is only empirically explored. These points deserve a precise statement and citation (e.g., Danskin) to avoid overclaiming.
- Algorithms and scalability: Exact Hungarian O(N^3) and approximate O(N^2) matching are used; distance computation is O(N^2D). While acceptable at N≈10^3, this is still costly relative to training, and the paper does not compare to Sinkhorn OT which is the standard scalable OT baseline and GPU-friendly. The claim that smaller |B| helps PMD is intriguing but not fully explained theoretically (it stems from freezing a plan, hence bias/variance trade-offs).
- Experiments: 
  • Domain adaptation: Only compared against MMD with tuned bandwidth; not compared to strong baselines (DANN/GRL, JAN, DSN, CMD, OTDA). Gains are modest and not consistent across all shifts. Using a labeled target validation set for hyperparameter tuning is standard in this line but should be clearly justified and harmonized across methods (the paper does so for MMD vs PMD).
  • Generative modeling: Results are qualitative; no standard quantitative metrics (FID, IS), no comparison to WGAN, Cramér GAN, MMD-GAN, or Sinkhorn-based training. Images on SVHN/LFW are blurry; authors attribute this to pixel L1 and partially mitigate via AE codes. This underscores the importance of feature-space costs, which are well explored in prior work.
  • Analyses (gradient magnitude histograms, population-size sensitivity) are useful but limited; the observed empirical error rate N^{-0.23} is consistent with known slow rates for Wasserstein in high-dim, but no discussion of sample complexity/curse of dimensionality is provided.
- Reproducibility: Implementation details are given at a reasonable level (hardware, solvers, architectures), but code availability is not mentioned.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The definitions and connections to OT are straightforward. However:
  • The “unbiased gradient” statement should be replaced with a careful Danskin/subgradient argument.
  • The positioning against dual OT and differentiability is imprecise.
  • Missing key related citations (Sinkhorn OT; OT for domain adaptation) harms clarity of novelty.
  • Some figures (e.g., pseudocode) are referred to but not fully described in text.

Originality
- Conceptually, PMD is not new: it is empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD with a new name. The learning recipe (periodically recomputing the assignment and freezing it for several updates, with sub-batches) is a pragmatic variant but similar ideas have been explored in OT-based learning. The theoretical contribution (consistency) is standard. The claimed “stronger gradients” is a useful observation but mostly heuristic and context-dependent.

Significance
- As presented, the contribution is incremental. Without new algorithmic advances (e.g., faster/differentiable OT) or stronger empirical evidence (state-of-the-art results or broad comparisons), the impact is limited. The idea that OT-type distances can be effective and require fewer hyperparameters than MMD is valuable, but this has been explored elsewhere (notably via Sinkhorn/OTDA and WGAN variants).

Pros
- Clear exposition of empirical OT as a training loss; simple and implementable.
- Correct consistency statement; useful practical tricks (freezing plans, sub-batches).
- Empirical evidence that PMD can be more stable than MMD and less sensitive to bandwidths.
- Useful analysis plots (gradient magnitudes; sensitivity to N and |B|).

Cons
- Novelty is limited; PMD largely renames empirical OT/EMD; missing key related work (Sinkhorn, OTDA).
- Theoretical claims about gradients are informal; sub-batch estimator is biased without analysis.
- Scalability discussion incomplete; no comparison to Sinkhorn distances.
- Experiments are limited in scope and baselines; no standard metrics for generative modeling; domain adaptation gains modest.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the work within computational OT: cite and compare to Sinkhorn OT (including backprop), and OT-based domain adaptation.
- Provide a precise gradient justification (Danskin) and discuss non-differentiable points; analyze bias/variance of the frozen-plan sub-batch estimator.
- Include stronger experimental baselines (WGAN/Cramér/MMD-GAN; DANN/JAN/DSN/CMD/OTDA) and standard metrics (FID/IS).
- Explore feature-space ground costs and/or learned costs; discuss sample complexity and dimensionality effects.

Overall assessment
The paper is clear and technically sound on well-known foundations, but the novelty and significance are limited, and the empirical evaluation lacks breadth against established OT-based methods. As written, it does not sufficiently advance the state of the art to meet the NeurIPS bar.
