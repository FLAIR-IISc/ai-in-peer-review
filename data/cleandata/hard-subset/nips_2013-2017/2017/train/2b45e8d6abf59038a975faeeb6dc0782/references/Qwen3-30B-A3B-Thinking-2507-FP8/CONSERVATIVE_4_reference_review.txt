

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper proposes a new statistical distance called "Population Matching Discrepancy" (PMD), defined as the minimum weight matching between two sets of samples from different distributions. The authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and demonstrate its use as a training objective for deep learning tasks, particularly domain adaptation and generative modeling. They compare PMD to the established Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) method, arguing that PMD has advantages including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes.

The paper makes a theoretically interesting connection between the minimum weight matching problem and the Wasserstein metric, and the theoretical analysis in Section 3 is largely sound, correctly establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance. The authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm and its implementation, and the empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate some performance improvements over MMD in specific cases.

However, the paper falls short of the rigorous validation expected at NIPS for a new method with claims of superiority over established approaches. The most significant weakness is the lack of comprehensive experimental validation. The paper only evaluates PMD on four datasets (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW) across two tasks, which is insufficient to justify the claim that PMD "outperforms MMD in terms of both performance and speed of convergence." The results are inconsistent - PMD outperforms MMD on a→w and a→d domain adaptation tasks but is comparable on others. The paper also fails to provide statistical significance testing for these improvements.

The computational complexity claims are concerning. The authors acknowledge the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm for exact matching, noting that "when N is larger, e.g., a few thousands, the Hungarian algorithm takes seconds to run." For large-scale applications, this is likely prohibitive. While they mention approximate matching algorithms, they provide no thorough comparison of approximation quality versus computational cost. The paper claims PMD "can use normal mini-batch size, such as 100," but fails to demonstrate how this translates to practical speed advantages in real-world applications.

The theoretical claim about "stronger gradients" is problematic. The authors provide a simple gradient calculation for L1 distance but do not establish this as a general property. Their Figure 2(b) shows a distribution of normalized gradients, but this is insufficient to make a strong theoretical claim about gradient strength across different distance metrics and datasets.

The paper also fails to adequately contextualize its contribution relative to existing work on Wasserstein distance estimation. They briefly mention WGAN and the "dual" approach, but do not properly contrast PMD with these methods, nor do they acknowledge that the Wasserstein metric itself has been extensively studied in machine learning. The connection to optimal transport theory is noted but not sufficiently developed.

The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of thorough validation against established baselines. While the authors compare PMD to MMD, they don't compare to other state-of-the-art methods for domain adaptation and generative modeling. The paper also lacks ablation studies to isolate the specific contribution of PMD versus other potential improvements.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting connection between minimum weight matching and Wasserstein distance estimation, it fails to provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that PMD is a superior alternative to established methods. The theoretical justification is reasonable but incomplete, and the empirical validation is limited and inconsistent. The paper would benefit from significantly more thorough experiments, including comparisons to additional baselines, statistical significance testing, and a more comprehensive analysis of computational trade-offs.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical connection between PMD and Wasserstein metric
- Well-organized presentation of the algorithm
- Some empirical improvements on specific tasks

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient experimental validation across datasets and tasks
- No statistical significance testing for performance claims
- Overstated computational advantages without thorough analysis
- Incomplete theoretical justification for "stronger gradients" claim
- Inadequate comparison to existing work on Wasserstein estimation

This paper presents an interesting idea but does not meet the high bar of evidence required to justify replacing established methods with a new approach. The claims of superiority over MMD are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. I recommend rejection.
