This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence measure proposed as an alternative to the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from two distributions. The authors establish a connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of it. They propose a learning algorithm that uses PMD as a training objective, which involves finding an optimal matching and then performing stochastic gradient descent updates on the matched pairs. The key advantages claimed over MMD are the absence of a kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, the presence of stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches. The method is evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling tasks, where it is compared against MMD.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Grounding:** The paper provides a clear and sound theoretical connection between the proposed PMD and the well-established first Wasserstein metric. Framing PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of W1 gives the method a solid theoretical foundation, which is a significant strength.

2.  **Novelty of the Approach:** While the Wasserstein metric is not new to machine learning, using its primal, sample-based matching formulation directly as a differentiable loss for deep learning models is a novel and interesting contribution. It presents a different perspective compared to dual-form approaches like Wasserstein GANs (WGANs) [3] or kernel-based methods like MMD.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core concepts are explained clearly. The derivation from the Wasserstein metric to the PMD definition via empirical distributions is easy to follow. The proposed learning algorithm is also presented in an understandable manner.

4.  **Practical Considerations:** The argument for PMD's advantages over MMD is compelling from a practical standpoint. The removal of the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter is a welcome simplification. The analysis of the gradient behavior (Fig. 2b) provides some intuition for why PMD might avoid the vanishing gradient problem sometimes associated with RBF kernels in MMD.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The primary weakness of the proposed method is its computational complexity. The minimum weight matching problem requires O(N^3) time for an exact solution with the Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. This is a significant computational burden compared to MMD, which is O(N^2) but highly amenable to GPU parallelization. While the authors argue this cost is acceptable compared to backpropagation, this may not hold for very deep models or when larger population sizes (N) are needed. The experiments are limited to N=4000, and the scalability to larger-scale problems remains a major unaddressed concern.

2.  **Insufficient Baselines and Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating PMD's potential, is not sufficiently rigorous in its comparison to the state of the art.
    *   **Generative Modeling:** The primary baseline is the Generative Moment Matching Network (GMMN) [31], which is an MMD-based method from 2015. A much more relevant and established baseline would be the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) [3], which also seeks to optimize a form of the Wasserstein distance. Without a comparison to WGAN, it is difficult to assess the significance of PMD in this application. The qualitative results in Figure 4 are not convincing; the generated images are blurry and do not appear to be competitive with modern generative models.
    *   **Domain Adaptation:** The comparison is again limited to MMD. Other well-known adversarial methods like Domain-Adversarial Training of Neural Networks (DANN) [13] should have been included as baselines to properly contextualize the performance.

3.  **Methodological Rigor of the Learning Algorithm:** The proposed training algorithm (Fig. 1) relies on the assumption that the optimal matching M* does not change significantly over several inner-loop SGD updates. This is a heuristic that lacks justification. How sensitive is the training to this assumption? An analysis of the stability of the matching matrix M* during training would be necessary to validate this approach. Without it, the learning algorithm feels more like an ad-hoc procedure than a principled optimization strategy. Using a "stale" matching could potentially introduce significant bias into the gradient estimates.

4.  **Hyperparameter Re-introduction:** While PMD removes the kernel bandwidth parameter, it introduces a dependency on the ground metric `d(·, ·)`. The authors acknowledge that the pixel-wise L1 distance may be a reason for blurry images, suggesting that learning in a different feature space is preferable. This implies that the choice of the metric or feature space is critical, effectively re-introducing a design choice that requires tuning and domain knowledge, similar to kernel selection in MMD.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-Acceptance:**
    *   The paper introduces a novel, parameter-free (no kernel) discrepancy measure with strong theoretical ties to the Wasserstein metric.
    *   The connection is well-established and the method is presented clearly.
    *   The proposed training optimization of decoupling population size from SGD mini-batch size is a clever practical idea.
    *   It shows promising results against its chosen baseline, MMD, in terms of convergence speed and final performance in some domain adaptation tasks.

*   **Con-Acceptance:**
    *   Significant concerns about computational scalability due to the O(N^3) or O(N^2) matching step, which is less parallelizable than MMD.
    *   The experimental evaluation is weak due to the absence of key contemporary baselines like WGAN and DANN.
    *   The core assumption of the training algorithm (fixed matching M*) is not theoretically or empirically justified, raising questions about the method's soundness.
    *   The quality of the generative modeling results is not competitive, and the method's sensitivity to the ground metric undermines the "fewer hyperparameters" claim.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting and theoretically grounded idea, its claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous empirical validation against established, state-of-the-art methods. The significant computational drawbacks and the unjustified heuristics in the training algorithm make it difficult to endorse this approach in its current form. The work is a promising first step, but it is not a complete piece of research ready for a top-tier conference like NIPS. A stronger contribution would require a more thorough experimental comparison and a more principled analysis of the proposed learning algorithm.
