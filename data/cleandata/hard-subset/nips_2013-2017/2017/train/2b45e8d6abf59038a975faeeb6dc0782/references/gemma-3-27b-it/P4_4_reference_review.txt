This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, proposing it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for training deep learning models. The core idea is to minimize the cost of matching samples between two distributions, offering advantages like parameter-free optimization, potentially stronger gradients, and the ability to use standard mini-batch sizes. The authors provide theoretical justification for PMD's consistency and demonstrate its performance in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, outperforming MMD in these experiments.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the PMD concept. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is established through a formal derivation, and the advantages over MMD are articulated convincingly. The inclusion of theoretical analysis regarding consistency and empirical validation through experiments are strengths. The paper builds on existing work in statistical divergence estimation (specifically MMD) and applies it to relevant deep learning areas, extending the toolbox of techniques available to researchers.  The experimental results are thorough, including convergence speed comparisons and sensitivity analysis.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. While the theoretical results are stated, a more intuitive explanation of *why* PMD avoids the gradient saturation issues of MMD would be helpful. The paper claims stronger gradients without a comprehensive theoretical or empirical demonstration beyond Fig. 2(b), which shows a distribution of gradient magnitudes. A more direct comparison of gradient norms during training for specific tasks would be more persuasive. The choice of the L1 distance for PMD is somewhat arbitrary; a justification or exploration of other distance metrics would be beneficial. Furthermore, the limited scope of the experiments—only two application areas—leaves open questions about PMD’s generalizability.  The claim of GPU acceleration potential for the matching algorithms is mentioned but lacks detail.  Finally, while the related work section is adequate, it could be broadened to include more recent developments in optimal transport and adversarial training, especially within the context of generative modeling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** PMD offers a novel approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric and utilizing it as a training objective.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a theoretical justification for PMD’s consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein metric.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the concepts accessible.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments demonstrate PMD’s effectiveness in two relevant deep learning tasks, outperforming MMD in most cases.
*   **Practical Advantages:** PMD avoids the need for kernel bandwidth tuning and potentially allows for faster convergence with standard mini-batch sizes.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The evaluation is restricted to only two application domains, limiting the assessment of generalizability.
*   **Gradient Explanation:**  The explanation for the stronger gradients is somewhat superficial.
*   **Distance Metric Choice:** The justification for using the L1 distance is lacking.
*   **GPU Acceleration Details:** Lack of specific details regarding GPU acceleration potential.
*   **Related Work Breadth:** The related work section could benefit from including more recent advancements in the field.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for estimating statistical divergence.
*   The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods (MMD) in specific applications.
*   The problem addressed is relevant to several areas within deep learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is limited in scope.
*   The explanation for key advantages (e.g., stronger gradients) is not fully convincing.
*   The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work and a broader exploration of the design choices.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of statistical divergence estimation and its application to deep learning. While the limitations mentioned above exist, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and promising empirical results outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised regarding gradient explanation, distance metric justification, and broadening the related work.
