## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, positioning it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in deep learning applications. The core idea – using a minimum weight bipartite matching to estimate the distance between distributions – is conceptually straightforward. The authors demonstrate its application to domain adaptation and generative modeling, reporting performance gains over MMD.

While the concept of leveraging optimal transport for distribution comparison isn’t novel (e.g., WGAN), the authors correctly identify limitations of MMD – bandwidth tuning, gradient saturation – and propose PMD as a remedy. The theoretical justification, establishing PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, is a key strength. However, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet persistent, issues that collectively detract from its overall quality and readability.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty (Incremental):** PMD offers a potentially useful alternative to MMD, particularly in scenarios where gradient stability is a concern.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The proof of strong consistency provides a solid theoretical basis for the proposed method.
* **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrating improvements over MMD in two different tasks adds credibility to the approach.
* **Clear Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the drawbacks of MMD that PMD aims to address.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Writing Quality:** The manuscript contains numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing (e.g., "Without making explicit assumption…", “PMD has no bandwidth hyper-parameter,” the repeated use of "e.g." followed by a list of only one item). This hinders readability and gives an impression of sloppiness.
* **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** The parameter tuning process for MMD is mentioned but not detailed adequately. Simply stating "validated on a random 100-sample labeled dataset" is insufficient; a more comprehensive explanation of the search space and validation procedure is needed.
* **Limited Ablation Studies:**  The effect of the mini-batch size (|B|) on PMD performance is explored, but a more systematic ablation study varying *both* |B| and population size (N) across tasks is required.
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The exclusive use of the L1 distance feels arbitrary. The paper does not adequately justify this choice, nor does it explore the impact of other distance metrics (e.g., Euclidean) on PMD's performance.
* **Computational Details:** The claim of GPU parallelization potential for the matching algorithms is mentioned but lacks any concrete details. Providing approximate speedups or implementation specifics would strengthen the argument.
* **Minor Technical Inaccuracies:** The statement that MMD’s population size is “around 1000” is a generalization and not universally true. Also, equating N in PMD to the mini-batch size in MMD literature isn’t entirely accurate and can be misleading.
* **Figure Quality:** Figure 1 is visually cluttered and difficult to understand.  Figure 2(a) lacks axis labels for the y-axis. The figures would benefit from clearer annotations and improved visual clarity.
* **Referencing:** There are inconsistencies in citation style.



**Specific Concerns:**

* **Equation (2):** The expectation operator seems unnecessary and doesn't significantly contribute to the clarity of the objective.
* **Equation (3):** The claim that the matching *M* doesn’t change for infinitesimal changes in θ is overly strong and potentially inaccurate. While the matching is relatively stable, it can change, especially with higher-dimensional data and complex transformations.
* **Section 3.1:** The proof sketch relies heavily on existing propositions without providing sufficient context or explanation. The connection to previous work on optimal transport could be more explicitly stated.
* **Section 5.3:** The discussion of timing results is superficial. More detailed benchmarks, including breakdown of time spent on different components (distance computation, matching, SGD), would be valuable.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The theoretical contribution is solid and establishes a firm foundation for PMD.
* Empirical results demonstrate promising performance improvements over MMD.
* PMD addresses legitimate drawbacks of MMD.

**Con:**

* The manuscript suffers from numerous writing and presentation flaws.
* The experimental evaluation lacks rigor and comprehensiveness.
* The paper presents an incremental improvement rather than a groundbreaking advance.
* The overall polish and attention to detail are insufficient for NIPS.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially valuable contribution of PMD, the numerous shortcomings in writing, presentation, and experimental evaluation lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the core idea is sound, the paper requires significant revision to meet NIPS standards. The authors need to address the grammatical errors, improve the clarity of the exposition, conduct more thorough experiments, and provide more detailed technical justifications for their choices. The current manuscript feels rushed and lacks the level of polish expected for a top-tier conference. A major rewrite focusing on clarity, rigor, and completeness is necessary before this work could be considered for publication.
