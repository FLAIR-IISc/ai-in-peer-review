- Overall: Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric and recommends accept with minor revisions.  
- Contribution: PMD minimizes sample matching cost to estimate Wasserstein distance, positioned as an alternative to MMD.  
- Motivation: Addresses MMD limitations (bandwidth tuning, gradient saturation, large batch needs) with a matching-based approach.  
- Theoretical result: Provides a strong consistency proof for PMD.  
- Empirical gains: Shows faster convergence and comparable/improved performance in domain adaptation and generative modeling.  
- Gradient behavior: Claims PMD yields stronger gradients than MMD, supported by experimental figure.  
- Practical advantages: No kernel bandwidth parameter and smaller mini-batch size requirements compared to MMD.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-structured, written, and figures are informative.  
- Scalability concern: Insufficient discussion of matching algorithm computational cost and practical implications for large N.  
- Metric choice: Experiments only use L1 distance; sensitivity to other distances (e.g., L2) is not explored and L1 justification is weak.  
- Generative modeling weakness: Results are blurry; causes (L1 vs. PMD limitation) are not investigated.  
- Ablation studies: Lacks comprehensive ablations (e.g., effect of mini-batch size) especially for generative tasks.  
- Gradient explanation clarity: The stronger-gradient claim needs a more intuitive or visual explanation.  
- Error analysis: Limited analysis of the types of errors PMD makes relative to MMD in applications.  
- Significance: Novel and potentially impactful if scalability issues are addressed.  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel estimator, strong theory, promising empirical results, addresses MMD limitations, clear presentation.  
- Rejection concerns: Computational cost, blurry generative outputs, narrow distance-metric exploration, missing ablations.  
- Required revisions: Expand complexity discussion, investigate blurry images, test alternative distances, add ablations (batch size/hyperparams), clarify gradient intuition, and include comparative error analysis.
