

# Peer Review: "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based statistical distance measure between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sets of samples from the distributions, and the authors show it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes an algorithm to use PMD as a training objective in deep learning, demonstrating advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD): no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes. The authors evaluate PMD on two deep learning tasks - domain adaptation and generative modeling - showing consistent improvements over MMD.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear and well-supported theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric. The proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3) is well-constructed, building on established results in optimal transport theory. This provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.

2. **Practical Advantages**: The authors convincingly demonstrate PMD's practical advantages over MMD: no bandwidth tuning required, stronger gradients (Fig. 2b), and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (Sec. 3.2). These are significant practical concerns in deep learning applications.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The paper provides thorough empirical evaluation across two distinct deep learning tasks (domain adaptation and generative modeling) with multiple datasets (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW). The results consistently show PMD outperforms MMD in both performance and convergence speed.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The theoretical foundations, algorithmic details, and experimental results are presented in a logical flow that makes the contributions clear to readers.

5. **Implementation Details**: The authors provide valuable insights into the practical implementation, including the choice of matching algorithms (Hungarian vs. approximate) and their computational trade-offs (Fig. 5c).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Bound**: While the paper establishes strong consistency, it lacks a finite-sample error bound for PMD. This would strengthen the theoretical contribution and provide practitioners with guidance on how the approximation error scales with sample size.

2. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper focuses primarily on comparing with MMD, but does not thoroughly compare with other recent advances in distributional distances, such as WGAN-GP, Cramer GAN, or other optimal transport-based methods. This weakens the significance claim by not fully situating the work within the current landscape.

3. **Gradient Strength Claim**: The claim about stronger gradients for PMD compared to MMD is important but lacks sufficient theoretical justification. The paper provides empirical evidence (Fig. 2b) but could benefit from a more detailed theoretical analysis.

4. **Distance Metric Limitation**: The paper primarily uses L1 distance for PMD, which the authors acknowledge may not be ideal for natural images (Sec. 5.2). A more thorough investigation of different distance metrics (L2, Mahalanobis, etc.) would strengthen the work.

5. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper mentions computational trade-offs, it could provide a more detailed analysis of how PMD's computational complexity scales with population size, especially for very large datasets.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distributional distance metrics in deep learning. The introduction of PMD is novel, well-theorized, and empirically validated across multiple applications. The practical advantages over MMD are significant and well-documented.

The paper addresses an important practical problem in deep learning: the need for a sample-based statistical distance that is differentiable, efficient to compute, and requires minimal hyperparameter tuning. PMD offers a compelling solution to this problem, with clear advantages over the widely used MMD.

The main limitations are the lack of a finite-sample error bound and limited comparison with more recent methods beyond MMD. These are important but do not undermine the core contribution of the paper.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The practical advantages of PMD over MMD are significant and well-documented through thorough experiments across multiple tasks.

The paper's clear writing, strong theoretical foundation, and convincing empirical results make it a valuable contribution to NIPS. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened with a finite-sample error bound, the current work is substantial enough to warrant acceptance.

## Final Thoughts

The authors have made a strong case for PMD as a practical alternative to MMD. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between different statistical distance measures for deep learning. The empirical results across multiple tasks demonstrate the real-world relevance of the proposed method.

For future work, the authors could explore extending PMD to handle larger datasets by developing more efficient matching algorithms, investigating the impact of different distance metrics on performance, and providing a finite-sample error bound for PMD. These would be valuable extensions to an already strong paper.

This paper represents a solid contribution to the field of deep learning and distributional metrics, and I believe it would be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
