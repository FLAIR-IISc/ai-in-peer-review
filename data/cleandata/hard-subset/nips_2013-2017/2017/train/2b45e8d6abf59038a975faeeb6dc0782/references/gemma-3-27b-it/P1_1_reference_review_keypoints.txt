- Overall: Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based Wasserstein estimator that outperforms MMD in practice; recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Problem & novelty: Estimates divergence from samples via minimum-weight matching, offering a primal Wasserstein approach without a critic network.  
- Relation to prior work: Positions PMD as an alternative to MMD and complements WGAN-style approaches by focusing on direct sample matching.  
- Theoretical contribution: Provides a clear consistency proof for PMD as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Practical advantages: No bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and effective with smaller mini-batch sizes.  
- Empirical validation: Shows better performance and faster convergence on domain adaptation and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW).  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, organized, and explains concepts and algorithms clearly.  
- Code: Recommends releasing code to strengthen the contribution.  
- Computational cost: Scalability concerns due to O(N^3) Hungarian algorithm; needs more discussion of GPU parallelization and speedups.  
- Theoretical depth lacking: Deserves deeper analysis on convergence rates and approximation error bounds.  
- Distance metric choice: Exclusive use of L1 is limiting; should explore other metrics or learned/feature-space distances.  
- Generative results: SVHN and LFW outputs are blurry; suggests L1 may be inappropriate for natural images and alternatives were not explored.  
- Ablation studies: Limited; more systematic experiments (e.g., varying mini-batch size) would solidify claims.  
- Originality & significance: Moderately original application of matching to Wasserstein with significant practical impact potential.  
- Qualitative scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 7/10, Significance 8/10.  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, theoretically grounded, practically advantageous, empirically validated, and well-presented.  
- Rejection arguments: Needs deeper theory, better scalability analysis, and exploration of alternative distance metrics.  
- Final recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; address scalability and metric choices and consider releasing code.
