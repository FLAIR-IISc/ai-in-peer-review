Summary
The paper proposes “Population Matching Discrepancy” (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. samples from two distributions (with d(·,·) a ground metric), and uses it as a sample-based, differentiable training objective. The authors (i) relate PMD to the 1-Wasserstein distance (optimal transport, OT), show strong consistency via Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of OT, (ii) propose a practical SGD procedure that reuses a fixed matching for several inner steps (no gradient through the matching), and (iii) present empirical comparisons against MMD on domain adaptation (Office) and on image generation (MNIST, SVHN, LFW). They argue PMD has no kernel bandwidth, yields “stronger gradients,” and admits smaller mini-batches.

Relation to prior work
The core object is the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (EMD) between two equally weighted empirical distributions; the reduction to an assignment problem is classical. Similar use of OT in machine learning and deep learning is extensive: Cuturi (2013) Sinkhorn distances (accelerated, differentiable OT), OT for domain adaptation (Courty et al., 2016), WGAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017) and follow-ups, sample complexity of OT (e.g., Fournier & Guillin 2015; Genevay et al. 2017), and numerous practical applications. Using EMD/OT as a loss is not new; the paper’s theoretical result (consistency) is standard. The main novelty claimed is a specific training heuristic (reusing matchings across SGD steps and subsampling a batch from a matched population). This feels incremental.

Strengths
- Clear link between PMD and 1-Wasserstein; the assignment-based estimator is a faithful, standard discretization.
- Simple training recipe; code-level details (CUDA kernel for pairwise distances) are appreciated.
- Sensible empirical observations: MMD gradients saturate with RBF kernels; PMD with L1 tends not to; smaller inner mini-batches can help throughput.
- Some practical measurements on time and convergence.

Weaknesses and concerns
Originality
- PMD is simply empirical OT/EMD; renaming it obscures substantial prior art. The consistency statement and linear program equivalence are well-known. This needs to be framed as “using empirical OT as a loss with a particular optimization scheme,” rather than proposing a new discrepancy.
- Major related work is missing or under-discussed: Cuturi (2013) Sinkhorn (NIPS), stabilized GPU implementations; OT for domain adaptation (Courty et al., NIPS 2016); sample complexity/rates of empirical OT; Sliced Wasserstein distances; Wasserstein Auto-Encoders; linear-time MMD estimators and improvements (e.g., using unbiased linear-time MMD, spectral MMD, or improved kernels). These omissions hinder positioning and fairness.

Technical correctness
- The “unbiased stochastic gradient” claim (Eq. 3) is not justified. The function θ → min_M ∑ d(xθ, yθ; M) is non-smooth where the optimizer changes; treating M* as locally constant yields a subgradient, not necessarily an unbiased gradient estimator in SGD when M* can change between steps. The inner-loop heuristic (reusing M* for multiple updates) introduces bias with no analysis. Please formalize as subgradient descent and provide conditions for convergence or at least bounds on mismatch-induced error.
- Subsampling a mini-batch B from a fixed matched population (Eq. 4) further biases the estimator; no analysis of the bias/variance trade-off is provided.
- “Stronger gradient” comparisons pit L1-OT vs RBF-MMD. This is not controlled: (a) L1 is non-smooth; (b) MMD with mixtures/multi-scale kernels, energy/Cramér distance, or learned kernels/critics can mitigate saturation; (c) Sinkhorn-regularized OT yields smooth, scalable, differentiable alternatives. Claims should be toned down and comparisons broadened.
- Complexity statements omit modern O(n^2) Sinkhorn-GPU alternatives that are widely used; assignment O(n^3) or approximate matching is not the only route. The proposed approach is not the most scalable option known.

Empirical evaluation
- Domain adaptation: no comparison to strong baselines (DANN, JAN, Deep CORAL, OTDA). Reporting only against MMD is insufficient. Details are thin: exact λ/σ grids, matched population sizes, number of inner-loop steps, and selection protocol are not fully specified. The claim “PMD converges faster” is not meaningful without matched compute budgets and fair early-stopping protocols.
- Generative modeling: evaluation is qualitative; no Inception Score, FID, precision/recall, or NFE curves. Images are described as “blurry,” suggesting the pixel L1 ground metric is suboptimal; experiments in a learned feature space (e.g., VGG/AE) are only briefly mentioned. Comparisons to WGAN/Improved WGAN, Sinkhorn-based generative models, Sliced Wasserstein, or MMD-GAN are missing.
- Fairness: MMD admits linear-time approximators and multi-scale kernels; the paper uses mixture kernels in some cases but not consistently, and does not leverage linear-time estimators that allow small batches. Training for 100 epochs (PMD) vs 500 (MMD) is also asymmetric.

Theory/claims vs. evidence
- The main theorem provides only consistency (expected and unsurprising). No finite-sample bounds, no sensitivity analysis of approximate matching, no treatment of gradient stability under changing matchings.
- The empirical rate N^−0.23 in 5D matches known OT rates ~N^−1/d but is not contextualized; the paper could acknowledge known rates and dimensional dependence.

Clarity and presentation
- Several notational/formatting issues: inconsistent indices and spacing (p X N vs pXN), γ(xi, yMi)=1/N phrasing (should be γij), occasional grammar errors, “st Conference” typo, misspellings (“retrival”), and inconsistent capitalization.
- Mis-citations: “see Proposition 5.4 in [6]” seems incorrect; [6] in the list is “Domain separation networks,” not an OT reference. This is serious and must be fixed.
- References are frequently incomplete (missing years/venues), and key OT references (Cuturi 2013) are absent.

Pros
- Simple, practical loss using a well-understood metric.
- Some empirical speedups in their setup.
- Insightful gradient-magnitude histogram.

Cons
- Limited novelty; renaming empirical OT as PMD.
- Theoretical development is minimal and standard; optimization heuristics lack guarantees.
- Incomplete related work and unfair comparisons.
- Weak empirical rigor (missing baselines/metrics) and several presentation/citation issues.

Questions for authors
1) Please compare against Sinkhorn-regularized OT (Cuturi 2013) for both tasks, and OT-based domain adaptation baselines (e.g., Courty et al. NIPS 2016).
2) Can you formalize your training as subgradient descent and provide conditions under which reusing M* for K steps is safe? How sensitive are results to K?
3) How do results change with learned/perceptual ground costs (feature-space L2) or sliced Wasserstein?
4) Can you add quantitative generation metrics (FID/IS) and ablate multi-scale MMD/linear-time MMD?
5) Fix the mis-citations and provide complete bibliographic details.

Recommendation: Reject
While the empirical OT objective is useful in practice, the paper offers limited novelty beyond repackaging a known estimator, lacks key related work and baselines, makes overstated claims about gradients without controlled comparisons, and contains technical and bibliographic inaccuracies. With stronger positioning (as a practical engineering study), fair comparisons (Sinkhorn/OTDA/WGAN/MMD variants), quantitative evaluations, and corrected citations, this could become a solid workshop/short paper.
