

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric that uses minimum weight matching between two samples from distributions. The authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, has no bandwidth hyperparameter, provides stronger gradients than MMD, and allows smaller mini-batch sizes. They apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, claiming superior performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.

## Strengths
The paper presents a novel approach to estimating statistical distances between distributions that addresses some limitations of MMD. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is theoretically grounded, and the empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling are promising. The implementation details of the matching algorithm and the comparison with MMD are thorough, and the paper provides valuable insights into gradient properties that could inform future work.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Presentation
- The paper incorrectly states "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" where "st" should be "The" (typo in title).
- The paper claims to be from NIPS 2017 but references "NIPS 2023" in the instructions, creating confusion.
- In Section 2.1, the definition of DN(X,Y) uses a factor of 1/N but describes it as "the cost of the minimum weight matching" (which would be the sum, not the average). This is inconsistent with the description.

### Theoretical Inconsistencies
- Theorem 3.3 claims strong consistency but the proof is only sketched ("applying Proposition 3.1 and 3.2"). The connection between the empirical PMD and the population Wasserstein metric requires more rigorous justification.
- The paper states PMD "has no bandwidth hyper-parameter" but doesn't specify the distance function d(·,·) used. If they use Euclidean distance, this is true, but this should be explicitly stated.
- The claim that PMD "is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric" is misleading. PMD is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric between empirical distributions, not the population Wasserstein metric. The paper needs to clarify this distinction.

### Methodological Issues
- Section 2.2 states "PMD has no bandwidth hyper-parameter" but doesn't address how the distance function d(·,·) might introduce implicit hyperparameters (e.g., L1 vs L2 distance).
- The paper claims PMD "can use normal mini-batch size, such as 100" but fails to clarify the relationship between the mini-batch size |B| and the population size N. In Eq. (4), they use |B| as a subsample, but the paper never specifies how |B| relates to N.
- The paper states "the gradient does not propagate through the matching" (Eq. 3), but this is not generally true for the Hungarian algorithm. The gradient would propagate through the matching when the optimal matching changes with parameter updates.
- Section 5.3 claims "the solution is insensitive to neither the population size N nor the choice of the matching algorithm" but Figure 5(a) shows clear dependence on N (performance improves with larger N).

### Experimental Concerns
- The paper claims "PMD outperforms MMD" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the results (e.g., p-values or confidence intervals).
- The generative modeling experiments (Section 5.2) show "blurry" images for PMD on SVHN and LFW, but the paper doesn't address this limitation or provide a clear explanation.
- The paper references "Drake and Hougardy's approximated matching algorithm [11]" but doesn't specify which approximation they used in the experiments (exact vs approximate matching in Section 5.3 is unclear).
- The claim that "PMD gradients have similar magnitude for each sample, while MMD has many samples with small gradients" (Section 3.2) is based on a single experiment (Fig. 2b) without statistical validation.

### Related Work
- The paper doesn't compare with WGAN [3] despite mentioning it in Section 3.1. WGAN is a relevant approach that also addresses Wasserstein metric estimation.
- The paper cites "Generative Moment Matching Networks" [31] as an ICML paper but it was actually published at ICML 2015, not a conference paper.
- The paper doesn't adequately distinguish PMD from other Wasserstein distance estimators (e.g., [45]).

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting approach with promising empirical results, it contains significant theoretical inconsistencies, methodological ambiguities, and experimental shortcomings that undermine its claims. The theoretical justification for PMD being a "strongly consistent estimator" of the Wasserstein metric is insufficient, and the paper's claims about gradient strength and mini-batch size are not rigorously supported. The experimental results lack statistical significance testing and fail to address limitations like blurry generated images.

The paper would benefit from a more careful theoretical treatment of the consistency properties, clearer explanation of the relationship between population size and mini-batch size, and additional experiments to validate the gradient strength claims. The formatting issues and citation inconsistencies also detract from the paper's professionalism.

Given these substantial issues, I recommend rejection. The paper has potential but requires significant revisions to address the theoretical and methodological weaknesses before it can be considered for publication.
