- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric using bipartite matching.
- PMD claimed to avoid kernel bandwidth tuning, provide stronger gradients, and work with standard mini-batch sizes.
- The paper proves strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of the first Wasserstein distance.
- Empirical results show improved performance and faster convergence in domain adaptation and generative modeling vs. MMD.
- Ablation studies and figures (e.g., Fig. 2b, Fig. 3a, Fig. 5a) support empirical claims about gradients and convergence.
- Paper is well-written, logically organized, and includes helpful pseudocode for PMD.
- Novel contribution: a new perspective on estimating Wasserstein distance via minimum-weight matching.
- Technical soundness: theoretical results are correctly stated and experiments are well-designed.
- Practical benefit: reduced hyperparameter tuning compared to kernel-based MMD.
- Computational cost concern: reliance on the Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)) raises scalability questions for large N.
- GPU acceleration discussion is brief and the approximate matching algorithm’s trade-offs are insufficiently explored.
- Authors consistently use L1 distance, but the impact of other distance metrics is not investigated and the L1 claim may be overstated.
- Limited comparison scope: mainly compared to MMD, lacking comparisons to other Wasserstein estimators (e.g., WGAN, OT approximations).
- Hyperparameter sensitivity critique of MMD is valid but may overemphasize MMD’s weaknesses without acknowledging well-tuned baselines.
- Theoretical depth could be improved by providing convergence rates or sample-size error bounds.
- Recommendation for acceptance with minor revisions emphasizing scalability, metric choice, and broader comparisons.
- Suggested revisions: expand complexity/scalability discussion, test other distance metrics, compare to recent Wasserstein techniques, and note L1 limitations.
