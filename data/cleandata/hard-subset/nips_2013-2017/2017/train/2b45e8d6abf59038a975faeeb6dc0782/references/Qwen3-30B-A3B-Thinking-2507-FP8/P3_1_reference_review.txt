

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical distance measure defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two samples sets from two distributions. The authors establish that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and propose it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for deep learning applications. The main claims are that PMD has no bandwidth hyperparameter, provides stronger gradients, and can use smaller mini-batch sizes than MMD.

## Strengths

The paper makes a valuable contribution by connecting the minimum weight matching problem to the Wasserstein metric, which has been widely adopted in machine learning. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is sound, establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric through Varadarajan's theorem and the stability of optimal transport. The empirical results in Sections 5.1 and 5.2 convincingly demonstrate PMD's advantages over MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, particularly in terms of convergence speed and parameter sensitivity. The claim about stronger gradients is well-supported by the distribution of normalized gradients in Figure 2(b), showing PMD gradients maintain consistent magnitude across samples, unlike MMD which suffers from vanishing gradients for outliers.

## Weaknesses

The primary weakness is the lack of comprehensive theoretical analysis. While the paper establishes consistency, it omits finite-sample error bounds, which would provide crucial insights into the practical performance of PMD. The authors claim PMD "can use normal mini-batch size, such as 100," but don't provide a theoretical justification for why this is possible when the matching problem requires O(N^3) time complexity. The comparison with MMD is limited - they don't compare with other Wasserstein approximations like WGAN or EMD, which are more commonly used in practice. The computational complexity of the Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)) is a significant concern for large-scale applications, and while the authors mention approximate algorithms, they don't adequately address how these affect the quality of the estimate.

The experimental setup has inconsistencies. For generative modeling, the authors train PMD for 100 epochs but MMD for 500 epochs, which makes the comparison misleading. While they claim PMD converges faster, this is partly due to the different training durations. The paper also doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of using L1 distance for image generation, which likely explains the blurry images shown in Figure 4.

## Originality and Significance

While the connection between matching and Wasserstein metric is not entirely novel (the Wasserstein metric itself is defined through optimal transport), the specific application of this concept as a training objective for deep learning is a reasonable contribution. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from related work on Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) or other matching-based approaches. The significance is somewhat limited because the improvements over MMD are modest in some settings (Table 1 shows comparable performance on 4 out of 6 domain adaptation tasks), and the computational advantages may be offset by the matching cost for large N.

## Recommendations

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with promising empirical results, but it needs stronger theoretical grounding and a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods. The authors should:

1. Provide finite-sample error bounds for PMD to better understand its practical performance
2. Address the computational complexity concerns with a thorough analysis of the trade-offs between exact and approximate matching
3. Compare PMD with other Wasserstein approximations (like WGAN) to better establish its position in the landscape of distribution distance metrics
4. Address the training duration inconsistency in generative modeling experiments

## Final Recommendation

This paper presents a reasonable extension of the Wasserstein metric for practical deep learning applications with some empirical advantages over MMD. However, the lack of theoretical depth and incomplete experimental comparison limit its contribution. The paper would be strengthened by addressing these weaknesses, but as it stands, it represents a modest incremental advance rather than a significant breakthrough for NIPS. I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the theoretical and experimental shortcomings before publication.

The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of statistical distances in deep learning, but it doesn't quite meet the bar for a top-tier NIPS publication due to the missing theoretical components and incomplete experimental comparison.
