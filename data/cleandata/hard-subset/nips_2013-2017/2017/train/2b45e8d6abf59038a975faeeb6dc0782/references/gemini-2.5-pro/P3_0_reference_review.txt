This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence measure, and demonstrates its application as a training objective in deep learning. The core idea is to define the distance between two distributions as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from them. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). They propose a learning algorithm based on stochastic gradient descent where the gradient is computed through the matched pairs, under the assumption that the optimal matching is locally constant. The paper's main contribution is presenting PMD as a practical alternative to the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD has fewer hyperparameters, provides stronger gradients, and allows for more efficient stochastic optimization with smaller mini-batches. These claims are evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling tasks, where PMD is shown to be competitive with or outperform MMD.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Formulation:** The connection between the minimum weight matching of empirical samples and the first Wasserstein metric is elegant and well-established in optimal transport theory. Grounding the proposed discrepancy measure in this solid theoretical framework is a major strength. The authors provide a clear and concise derivation connecting the discrete matching problem to the continuous optimal transport problem (Sec 3.1).

2.  **Novel Application:** While the Wasserstein metric and its estimation are not new, its direct application as a differentiable, sample-based training objective for deep networks in this primal form is novel. It provides a compelling alternative to both MMD and the dual-formulation approaches used in Wasserstein GANs (WGAN).

3.  **Compelling Practical Advantages over MMD:** The paper makes several strong, well-motivated arguments for PMD's superiority over MMD in a practical deep learning context:
    *   **Hyperparameter Reduction:** Eliminating the kernel bandwidth parameter (`sigma`) is a significant practical benefit, as MMD's performance is often highly sensitive to this choice.
    *   **Gradient Strength:** The analysis of MMD's vanishing gradients for distant or very close samples is insightful. The argument that PMD (with an L1/L2 ground distance) provides a more consistent, non-saturating gradient signal is convincing and is well-supported by the empirical gradient distribution shown in Fig. 2(b). This could be crucial for stable training of deep models.
    *   **Optimization Efficiency:** The ability to decouple the population size `N` (for a stable matching) from the SGD mini-batch size `|B|` is a clever and important practical insight. This allows for more frequent parameter updates per population, potentially leading to faster convergence, as demonstrated in the experiments.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and cover two relevant application areas. The results in both domain adaptation and generative modeling convincingly show PMD's advantages. The ablation studies in Sec 5.3 effectively validate the claims regarding the impact of population size, mini-batch size, and the choice of matching algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The primary concern with PMD is the computational cost of solving the assignment problem, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. While the authors argue this is acceptable compared to backpropagation, this can become a significant bottleneck as the population size `N` grows. For high-dimensional and complex distributions, a larger `N` may be required for a reliable estimate, making the matching step prohibitively slow. The paper mentions GPU parallelization as future work, but the current single-threaded CPU implementation represents a practical limitation on the method's scalability.

2.  **Stability of the Gradient Approximation:** The derivation of the gradient in Eq. (3) relies on the assumption that the optimal matching M* is locally constant with respect to infinitesimal changes in the parameters `theta`. While this is an application of the envelope theorem and is often valid, it may not hold at points where the optimal matching is non-unique or changes abruptly. Could this lead to optimization instabilities? A discussion on the limitations of this assumption and its potential impact would strengthen the paper.

3.  **The Choice of Ground Metric `d(·, ·)`:** The paper argues that PMD has fewer hyperparameters than MMD. However, the choice of the ground distance `d(·, ·)` in the sample space is itself a critical, problem-dependent choice that functions like a hyperparameter. The authors use L1 distance, but as they note, this may be suboptimal for image data, leading to blurry generations. For complex data, one might need a more sophisticated metric, perhaps even a learned one, which reintroduces complexity. A more detailed discussion on the sensitivity to `d` would be valuable.

4.  **Comparison to Wasserstein GAN (WGAN):** The paper was written around the same time WGAN became prominent. The comparison in Sec 3.1 is brief. While PMD has the advantage of being parameter-free (not requiring a critic network), WGAN's dual approach scales to much larger mini-batch sizes and can implicitly learn a powerful metric through the critic. A more direct comparison or discussion would be helpful. For instance, how does the sample complexity of PMD compare to that of WGAN for achieving a certain quality of generated samples?

5.  **Finite-Sample Analysis:** The paper proves asymptotic consistency but lacks finite-sample error bounds, which are available for MMD. The empirical study (Fig 2a) suggests a convergence rate of roughly N^-0.23, which is quite slow. Understanding how the estimation error depends on both the sample size `N` and the data dimensionality would be an important theoretical contribution.

**Summary:**

This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel and well-motivated method for comparing distributions in deep learning. The proposed PMD is theoretically sound, practically motivated, and demonstrates strong empirical performance against a relevant baseline (MMD). The core ideas are clearly presented, and the connections to established theory are well-articulated. The weaknesses, primarily concerning scalability and the gradient approximation, are acceptable limitations for a novel method and open up interesting avenues for future research. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a new, practical tool for the deep learning community. It is likely to inspire follow-up work on scalable optimal transport methods for training deep models.
