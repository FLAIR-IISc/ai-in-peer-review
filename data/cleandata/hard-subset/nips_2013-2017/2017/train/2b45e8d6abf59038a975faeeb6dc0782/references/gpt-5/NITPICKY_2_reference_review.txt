Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight perfect matching between two i.i.d. sample sets from distributions pX and pY under a ground metric d (typically L1). The authors show PMD equals the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance for uniform empirical measures, and by invoking classical weak convergence and the stability of optimal transport plans, they claim strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of W1. They propose to optimize model parameters by SGD, not differentiating through the discrete matching (arguing the matching is locally constant), and further reuse the matching for several steps and/or subsample the matched pairs. They argue PMD has advantages over MMD (no bandwidth, stronger gradients, smaller minibatches), and demonstrate applications to unsupervised domain adaptation (Office) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), reporting qualitative samples and speed/convergence comparisons mostly against MMD-based baselines.

Relation to prior work
- PMD is exactly the Earth Mover’s Distance/empirical 1-Wasserstein distance between two equally weighted empirical measures (Rubner et al., Villani). This is a standard construction in OT and has been used extensively. The renaming obscures this connection.
- Theoretical consistency results and rates for empirical W1 are well studied (e.g., Fournier & Guillin 2015; del Barrio et al.; Bobkov & Ledoux), but not cited; the toy rate observed roughly matches the known N^{-1/d} behavior.
- Computational approaches to differentiable OT with samples via entropic regularization (Sinkhorn; Cuturi 2013) are conspicuously missing, despite being directly relevant, GPU-friendly, and differentiable, offering a strong baseline to both PMD and MMD.
- On generative modeling, the discussion of WGAN is incomplete/misleading; with a proper 1-Lipschitz critic (e.g., gradient penalty), the dual objective estimates W1 without an “unknown scale factor”.

Strengths
- Simple objective with a clear connection to W1; the consistency claim follows from classical results.
- Sensible engineering observations: gradient saturation issues in RBF-MMD, and that pairwise-matching-based objectives can admit smaller minibatches during parameter updates.
- Empirical study indicates PMD can train implicit generators directly in pixel/code space and may converge faster than their MMD setup in some cases.
- Implementation of both exact and approximate matching and a brief timing breakdown is useful.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness
- Differentiation: The paper treats the objective as differentiable in parameters while ignoring the dependence of the optimal matching M* on the parameters. The claim of an “unbiased stochastic gradient” (Eq. 3) is not justified; the objective is non-differentiable on sets of non-zero measure where matchings switch, and ignoring the derivative of argmin yields, at best, a subgradient almost everywhere, not an unbiased gradient. Reusing a stale matching (Eq. 4 and the inner-loop heuristic) introduces additional bias.
- Theoretical section: Conditions needed for W1 finiteness and convergence (e.g., finite first moments, tightness) are not stated; the “proof is straightforward” is too cursory. The equivalence of (6) to a permutation solution should be tied to the Birkhoff–von Neumann theorem/vertex optimality more precisely; ties and non-uniqueness are ignored.
- Complexity/memory: The O(N^2D) distance computation and storage of an N×N cost matrix is glossed over; memory implications (e.g., N=4000) and streaming strategies are not discussed.

Novelty and positioning
- The core “PMD” contribution is a relabeling of empirical W1/EMD with uniform weights. Using EMD as a training loss is not new in computer vision and ML. The paper does not engage with entropic OT/Sinkhorn or recent sample-complexity results, which substantially limits novelty and places the work as incremental.

Experimental methodology
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited almost exclusively to a single flavor of MMD. For domain adaptation, no comparisons to strong contemporaries (DAN, DANN, JAN, CMD) are reported; for generative modeling, no GAN/WGAN/PixelCNN comparisons and no quantitative metrics (IS, FID, NLL). Conclusions about “outperforming MMD in performance and convergence” are not strongly substantiated.
- Fairness: PMD trained for 100 epochs vs MMD for 500; population/minibatch settings differ; linear-time unbiased MMD estimators are not considered. The claim that “MMD requires large minibatches” is not accurate in general; linear-time MMD estimators and MMD-GAN practice routinely use moderate minibatches.
- Protocol: For Office, hyperparameters are selected using 100 labeled target samples, which deviates from strict unsupervised DA protocols and inflates numbers; while both methods use this, the evaluation is then less informative for the broader community.
- Reporting: No variance across runs, no statistical tests, and qualitative samples only for generators.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous notational/formatting errors: Eq. (1) mixes normalization (N outside vs 1/N later), inconsistent subscripts/superscripts; typos (“st Conference…”, leftover “∗Corresponding author”), missing/unclear Fig. 1 pseudocode, missing crucial algorithmic details (how costs are stored), and many incomplete references (venues/years). Some claims are overstated (“parameter free”) ignoring choice of ground metric d, N, and |B|.

Pros
- Clear connection to W1 with a simple objective that can be implemented and can work in practice.
- Empirical hints that pairwise matching can mitigate MMD gradient saturation in some settings.
- Attempts to reduce matching cost via approximation and minibatch reuse.

Cons
- Very limited novelty; essentially empirical W1/EMD rebranded.
- Incomplete and partially incorrect treatment of differentiation and gradient unbiasedness.
- Missing key related work (entropic OT/Sinkhorn) and strong baselines; weak/biased experimental protocol and lack of quantitative evaluation.
- Presentation issues and many small technical/notation/formatting mistakes.

Suggestions for improvement
- Properly position the work as empirical W1; cite and compare against entropic OT (Cuturi 2013) and recent stochastic/automatic differentiation OT methods. Analyze and/or use subgradient methods or smoothing to handle matching non-differentiability; discuss bias from stale matchings and minibatch subsampling.
- Provide formal conditions for consistency; discuss sample complexity/rates and curse of dimensionality; connect to known results.
- Add strong baselines and quantitative metrics (IS/FID) for generative modeling; adhere to unsupervised DA protocols without target labels for selection; report multiple runs with variance.
- Address computational/memory complexity and provide GPU implementations or streaming details.
- Fix notation, equations, and references.

Overall evaluation
- Quality: below threshold due to methodological gaps and weak evaluation.
- Clarity: needs significant polish.
- Originality: low.
- Significance: limited without stronger baselines and theory.
