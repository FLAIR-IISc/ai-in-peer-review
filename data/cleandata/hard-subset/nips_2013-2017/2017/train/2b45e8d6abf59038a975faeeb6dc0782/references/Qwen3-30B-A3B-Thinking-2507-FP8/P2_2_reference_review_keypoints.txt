- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): a sample-based divergence defined by minimum-weight matching, proposed as a training objective for deep models.  
- The authors prove PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Propositions 3.1, 3.2; Theorem 3.3).  
- PMD claimed advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and ability to use smaller mini-batches.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW) across domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing better performance and faster convergence than MMD.  
- The Hungarian matching step has O(N^3) complexity; authors argue it's acceptable relative to NN training but it may bottleneck large-scale use.  
- Theoretical contribution is incremental: PMD–Wasserstein connection reuses standard optimal transport results without deep new theory.  
- Gradient analysis is interesting but lacks deeper theoretical justification and is empirically validated on only one dataset.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with useful pseudocode, figures, and reproducibility details.  
- Using minimum-weight matching as a divergence in deep learning is a novel practical perspective, distinguishing the work from prior MMD-focused methods.  
- The paper does not sufficiently compare PMD to other recent Wasserstein approximations that also approximate the primal problem.  
- If PMD delivers claimed advantages, it could have substantial practical impact by addressing MMD’s tuning and batch-size issues.  
- The trade-off between matching computational cost and reduced mini-batch size is not thoroughly explored for large-scale settings.  
- Strengths: solid theoretical justification, well-designed experiments, strong empirical results, excellent writing, and practical insights.  
- Weaknesses: limited theoretical novelty, incomplete comparisons to related Wasserstein methods, shallow gradient theory/empirics, unclear large-scale cost trade-offs, and insufficient PMD hyperparameter tuning details.  
- Overall assessment: solid, well-executed paper with meaningful practical contribution; recommendation: accept.
