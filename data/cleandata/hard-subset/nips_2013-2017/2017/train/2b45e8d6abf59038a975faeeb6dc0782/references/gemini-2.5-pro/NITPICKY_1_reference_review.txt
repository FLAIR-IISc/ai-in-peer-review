This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric, and proposes its use as a training objective in deep learning. The core idea is to compute the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from two distributions. The average cost of this matching, the PMD, is then minimized via stochastic gradient descent to learn the parameters of one or both distributions. The authors argue that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein-1 distance and offers several advantages over the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), such as having no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, providing stronger gradients, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes during optimization. The method's efficacy is demonstrated on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling tasks, where it is shown to be competitive with or outperform MMD-based approaches. This work relates to the broad literature on integral probability metrics, particularly MMD (Gretton et al., 2012) and the Wasserstein metric, the latter of which has seen a recent surge in popularity through methods like Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017).

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The proposal to use the primal form of the empirical Wasserstein-1 distance (i.e., minimum weight matching) directly as a differentiable training objective for deep neural networks is a novel and interesting contribution. It provides a parameter-free alternative to both kernel-based methods like MMD and critic-based methods like WGAN.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is clearly established. Showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
*   **Practical Advantages:** The potential benefits over MMD are compelling. Eliminating the need to tune a kernel bandwidth simplifies the experimental setup. The analysis suggesting that PMD-L1 provides stronger, more uniform gradients (Fig. 2b) offers a plausible explanation for the observed faster convergence.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the promising core idea, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from critical citation errors to significant theoretical omissions and a general lack of rigor in its presentation.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper exhibits a general lack of polish and attention to detail.
    *   **Formatting:** There are numerous formatting inconsistencies. The footer contains a typo ("st Conference" instead of "31st"). Equations that are part of sentences consistently lack terminal punctuation. Section 3.2 uses unusual bolded headings within a paragraph ("Fewer hyper-parameter", which is also grammatically incorrect).
    *   **Citations:** The handling of citations is exceptionally poor and constitutes a major flaw. The text repeatedly cites [3] for Wasserstein GAN, but the reference list entry for [3] is a paper by Ben-David et al. on domain adaptation theory. The seminal WGAN paper (Arjovsky et al., 2017) is not cited at all. This misattribution of foundational work is unacceptable. Furthermore, the reference list is poorly formatted, with many entries being incomplete (e.g., [22], [25]) or containing extraneous commas (e.g., [1], [2]).

2.  **Technical Quality and Rigor:**
    *   **Gradient Calculation:** The paper's central technical claim—that one can differentiate the PMD objective—is not rigorously justified. The argument in Eq. (3) hinges on the assumption that "the discrete matching M* should not change for infinitesimal change of θ". This is a non-trivial application of the envelope theorem, but the authors provide no formal justification. The objective function is piecewise differentiable, and the gradient is undefined at points where the optimal matching changes. This critical detail is entirely ignored, leaving a significant gap in the theoretical underpinnings of the proposed learning algorithm.
    *   **Omitted Proof:** The proof of Theorem 3.3 (Strong Consistency) is dismissed as "straightforward," with no details provided even in an appendix. While the result may follow from the cited propositions, a formal proof requires explicitly verifying that all necessary conditions of those propositions are met. This omission detracts from the paper's completeness.
    *   **Overstated Claims:** The claim that PMD has "Fewer hyper-parameters" is misleading. While it lacks a kernel bandwidth, it introduces the population size `N`, the SGD mini-batch size `|B|`, and the choice of distance metric `d(·, ·)` as new, important hyperparameters that require careful selection (as evidenced by Fig. 5a). Similarly, the "stronger gradient" argument is specific to the L1 distance and does not generalize to all possible distance metrics (e.g., L2).

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   **Generative Modeling:** The qualitative comparison of generated images is insufficient. For a 2017 paper, the lack of quantitative metrics like FID or Inception Score is a notable omission. The authors' own admission that the generated images are "blurry" and that this is a flaw of the pixel-level L1 distance weakens the argument for PMD's general utility in this domain.
    *   **Timing Analysis:** The timing results in Fig. 5(c) are difficult to interpret. Key experimental details, such as the number of inner-loop SGD iterations (`k` from the pseudocode in Fig. 1), are not specified. Without this information, the comparison between matching time and SGD time is not meaningful.
    *   **Domain Adaptation Results:** The results in Table 1 are mixed. PMD shows a large margin on two tasks but is only comparable on the other four. This should be presented as a more nuanced finding rather than a clear overall victory.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The core idea of using minimum weight matching as a differentiable loss for deep learning is novel and elegant.
2.  The paper connects two important fields: deep learning and optimal transport, in a practical way.
3.  The method shows promising empirical results in certain settings.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  Critical and extensive citation errors, including the misattribution of WGAN, undermine the paper's scholarly credibility.
2.  A core theoretical claim—the differentiability of the objective—lacks rigorous justification, ignoring the subtleties of differentiating through a `min` operator over a discrete set.
3.  The manuscript is rife with minor errors in formatting, grammar, and presentation, suggesting a lack of care in its preparation.
4.  Experimental evaluation is incomplete, lacking standard quantitative metrics for generative models and sufficient detail in the timing analysis.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the central idea presented in this paper is interesting and potentially significant, the execution is deeply flawed. The combination of critical citation errors, a lack of theoretical rigor on the central gradient computation claim, and a general sloppiness throughout the manuscript prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper does not meet the high standards of technical soundness and careful scholarship expected at NIPS. A major revision would be required to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication.
