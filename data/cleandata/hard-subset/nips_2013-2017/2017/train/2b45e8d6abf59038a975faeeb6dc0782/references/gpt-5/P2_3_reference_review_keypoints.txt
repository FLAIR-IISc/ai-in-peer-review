- PMD is the average cost of minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. samples — effectively the empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD — and the paper proposes training with fixed-match differentiation plus match reuse and subsampled minibatches.
- The consistency proof is straightforward via standard OT results; the training heuristic uses piecewise-constant matchings and treats the matching as fixed when differentiating.
- The paper fails to acknowledge that PMD = empirical W1/EMD is classic and that estimator consistency is already known.
- Important prior work is omitted: entropic regularization/Sinkhorn (Cuturi 2013), OT-based domain adaptation (Courty et al.), WGAN/Cramer GAN, and point-set EMD usages.
- The MMD comparison ignores linear-time unbiased MMD estimators and other MMD/GAN variants that mitigate minibatch issues.
- Theoretical contribution is limited: no finite-sample bounds or rates; the subgradient/unbiased gradient claims need formal qualification (switching regions, measure-zero caveats).
- Algorithmically, assignment per population is heavy compared to Sinkhorn; approximate matching lacks accuracy guarantees and no empirical comparison to entropic OT is provided.
- Experiments compare mainly to MMD; they omit stronger DA baselines (DANN, JAN, CORAL/CMD, OTDA) and OT-based methods.
- Generative modeling results lack quantitative metrics (IS/FID) and rely on qualitative samples and internal curves.
- Claims of faster convergence require wall-clock timing including matching cost and GPU-friendly implementations; partial timing is insufficient.
- Empirical observations (e.g., stronger per-sample gradients) are plausible but limited in scope and do not explore alternative MMD settings or linear-time estimators.
- The paper is readable and organized, but the PMD naming obscures its equivalence to empirical W1 and training assumptions (subgradients, match reuse) should be explicitly stated.
- Originality is limited: main novelty is a practical training heuristic (reuse of matches, small minibatches) rather than a new estimator or theory.
- Potential significance exists (assignment-based W1 can give stronger gradients and smaller minibatches), but impact is limited by missing comparisons, computational cost, and lack of clear advantages over existing OT methods.
- Pros: clear W1 formulation, simple to implement with off-the-shelf matchers, sensible heuristic, and competitive results versus MMD in some settings.
- Cons: limited novelty, missing related work/comparisons (Sinkhorn, OT-DA, WGAN/Cramer, linear-time MMD), questionable unbiased gradient claims, no convergence/finitesample analysis, and weak experimental scope.
- Suggestions: explicitly reframe as empirical W1/EMD; cite/compare to Sinkhorn and OT-DA; justify subgradient treatment and add finite-sample/statistical analysis; expand experiments (DANN/JAN/OTDA, WGAN/Sinkhorn, IS/FID, wall-clock times, linear-time MMD); explore feature-space metrics and Sinkhorn for scalability.
- Recommendation: Reject (borderline) — clear writing and useful empirical insights but core idea is a rebranding of a standard estimator with incomplete positioning and insufficient experimental baselines.
