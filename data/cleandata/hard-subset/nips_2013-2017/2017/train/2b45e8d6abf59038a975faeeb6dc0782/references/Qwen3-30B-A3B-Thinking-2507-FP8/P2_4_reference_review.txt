

# Review: "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical distance that estimates the Wasserstein metric by solving a minimum weight matching problem between two sets of samples. The authors prove PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and demonstrate its advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD): no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs. 1000 for MMD). They apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing consistent performance improvements over MMD in both tasks.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Soundness (Quality):** The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by establishing PMD's connection to the Wasserstein metric. Theorem 3.3 on the strong consistency of PMD as an estimator is well-proven using Varadarajan's theorem and stability of optimal transport. This provides a solid foundation for using PMD as a statistical distance, addressing a key limitation in prior work. The comparison with MMD (Section 3.2) is particularly compelling, with clear theoretical explanations for why PMD's gradients are stronger and less sensitive to outliers.

**Practical Advantages (Significance):** The paper identifies and validates several practical advantages of PMD over MMD that address real pain points in deep learning. The ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs. 1000) is a significant practical benefit for training efficiency. The empirical validation in Section 5 (Fig. 3, 5) clearly demonstrates PMD's faster convergence speed and reduced hyperparameter sensitivity compared to MMD. The domain adaptation results (Table 1) are particularly convincing, showing PMD outperforms MMD on multiple tasks.

**Clear Exposition (Clarity):** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The definitions, theoretical results, and algorithmic details are presented with appropriate mathematical rigor while remaining accessible. Figures 1-5 effectively illustrate key concepts, and the pseudocode in Fig. 1 makes the algorithm easy to understand. The authors consistently connect their theoretical claims to practical implications.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Theoretical Bounds (Quality):** While the strong consistency result is valuable, the paper lacks finite-sample error bounds for PMD, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but for a NIPS submission, including even preliminary finite-sample analysis would significantly enhance the paper's quality.

**Generative Modeling Limitations (Quality/Significance):** The paper shows PMD generates blurry images on natural image datasets (SVHN, LFW) when using L1 distance, a notable limitation that isn't adequately addressed. While the authors suggest learning in code space might help, they don't explore alternative distance metrics (e.g., perceptual distance) that could improve image quality. This limitation reduces the practical significance for generative modeling applications.

**Computational Complexity (Quality):** The paper acknowledges the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm but doesn't sufficiently address how this scales to very large datasets. While they mention using approximate matching (O(N^2)), the computational trade-offs between exact and approximate matching for large-scale applications aren't thoroughly analyzed. For context, modern generative models often operate on datasets with millions of samples, where N=2000 (used in their experiments) might be too small.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of statistical distance estimation for deep learning. The theoretical foundation is sound, the practical advantages of PMD over MMD are clearly demonstrated, and the applications to domain adaptation and generative modeling are well-executed. The paper is well-written and clearly addresses the problem space.

The main weaknesses are the lack of finite-sample error bounds and the blurry image generation limitation in natural image tasks. However, these don't undermine the paper's core contribution, which is the introduction of a practical, theoretically grounded alternative to MMD with demonstrable advantages.

Compared to recent NIPS papers on statistical distances (e.g., WGAN, MMD-GAN), this paper offers a genuinely different approach with clear theoretical justification. The work builds on but significantly extends the understanding of statistical distances for deep learning applications.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical results are compelling, and the practical advantages of PMD over MMD are well-documented. The minor weaknesses (lack of finite-sample bounds, image quality limitation) are acknowledged by the authors and don't detract from the paper's overall contribution.

The paper advances the state of the art in statistical distance estimation for deep learning by providing a practical alternative to MMD with demonstrable benefits. The authors have clearly demonstrated that PMD is not only theoretically sound but also practically superior in key aspects for deep learning applications. This work is likely to be valuable to practitioners working on domain adaptation, generative modeling, and other tasks requiring distribution comparison.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), presents a novel approach (Originality), and addresses a significant practical problem (Significance). The authors have made a meaningful contribution that advances the field.
