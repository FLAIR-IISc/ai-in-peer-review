

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for estimating the Wasserstein metric between two distributions. The authors claim PMD has advantages over MMD including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements.

## Summary and Context

The paper introduces PMD as the minimum weight matching between two samples from distributions, establishing its connection to the Wasserstein metric. The authors compare PMD to MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, claiming superior performance and faster convergence. The work builds on previous work on statistical divergences (MMD, Wasserstein metric) and applications in domain adaptation and generative modeling.

## Major Weaknesses

### Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies
The paper exhibits numerous notational inconsistencies that undermine its technical rigor:
- Equation (1) has confusing notation: "DN (X,Y) = min M N N∑ i=1 d(xi, yMi)" should be "D_N(X,Y) = min_M (1/N) ∑_{i=1}^N d(x_i, y_{M_i})"
- Inconsistent use of "population size" and "mini-batch size" (Section 2.2 incorrectly claims "the mini-batch size for PMD can be smaller than the population size" when these are distinct concepts)
- Inconsistent parameter notation: "pX(x; θX)" vs "pX(x)" (parameters should consistently be outside the distribution notation)
- "PMD" is not defined early in the paper, only in Section 2.1
- "MMD have been widely adopted" (subject-verb agreement error)

### Technical Flaws in Key Claims

The paper makes several technically questionable claims:

1. **Gradient Claim**: The paper states "the gradient does not propagate through the matching" because "the discrete matching M* should not change for infinitesimal change of θ." This is incorrect - small changes in θ can cause discontinuous changes in the matching (due to the discrete nature of the assignment problem), making the gradient undefined in the standard sense. The paper fails to address this critical issue.

2. **Gradient Magnitude Claim**: The paper claims "the L2 norm of the term between two samples x and y is k(x, y) ‖x−y‖^2 / σ^2" when it should be k(x, y) * ‖x-y‖ / σ^2. This mathematical error undermines the justification for PMD's stronger gradients.

3. **Wasserstein Consistency**: The paper claims PMD is a "strongly consistent estimator" of the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), but the proof is superficial and doesn't address the discrete matching problem's discontinuities that could invalidate the consistency result.

4. **PMD vs MMD Comparison**: The paper claims PMD "only considers the distance between a sample and its matched sample, while MMD considers the distance between all pairs of samples." This is factually incorrect - MMD considers all pairs between the two distributions, while PMD considers only the matched pairs (a subset of all pairs), not that MMD considers all pairs within each distribution.

### Experimental Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Statistical Significance**: The paper claims PMD "outperforms MMD" but provides no statistical significance tests for the results (Table 1, Figure 3).

2. **Confusing Figures**: Figure 5(a) labels "Exact N=500" as using the Hungarian algorithm while the rest use approximated matching, but the x-axis is "Mini-batch size |B|," creating confusion between population size and mini-batch size.

3. **Contradictory Claims**: The paper claims PMD "generates acceptable images on pixel space" but then admits "the generated images of PMD on the SVHN and LFW datasets are blurry," without explaining this contradiction.

4. **Lack of Comparison to WGAN**: The paper claims PMD "differs from WGAN by not requiring a separate critic network" but doesn't compare to WGAN-GP or other Wasserstein distance approximations that also avoid critic networks.

## Minor Weaknesses

- Typos in figures: "Re la tiv e" in Figure 2(a), "Fr eq ue nc y" in Figure 2(b), "Fi na l" in Figure 5(a)
- Inconsistent use of "PMD" vs "population matching discrepancy"
- The paper claims PMD "has no bandwidth hyper-parameter" but uses L1 distance without addressing scale issues
- The paper doesn't clarify what "large size" means when claiming "the Wasserstein metric itself of two empirical distribution is tractable, it can be too expensive to compute due to the large size of the dataset"

## Strengths

- The connection to the Wasserstein metric is theoretically interesting
- The paper provides empirical evidence for PMD's advantages in domain adaptation and generative modeling
- The algorithmic approach for using PMD as a training objective is well-motivated

## Conclusion

While the paper presents an interesting idea with potential theoretical and practical value, the numerous technical errors, notational inconsistencies, and unaddressed theoretical issues significantly undermine its credibility. The paper's core claims about PMD's advantages over MMD are not properly justified, and the experimental results lack statistical rigor. These issues are not merely minor formatting problems but represent fundamental technical flaws that would lead to incorrect implementations and interpretations.

The paper does not meet NIPS's standards for technical soundness and clarity. The numerous minor flaws collectively indicate a lack of careful review and attention to detail that is essential for a top-tier machine learning conference.

**Recommendation: Reject**
