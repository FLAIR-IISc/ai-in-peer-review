## NIPS 2017 Review: Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, termed Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD). It offers a compelling alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) with potential advantages in gradient behavior and computational efficiency. The paper is well-written, the theoretical grounding is sound, and the empirical results demonstrate promising performance gains in domain adaptation and generative modeling. However, the practical impact feels somewhat limited by the use of the L1 distance (which performs poorly on complex images), and the computational advantage isn’t dramatically shown – relying on a C++ implementation for matching. Despite these limitations, the theoretical contribution and demonstrated empirical benefits warrant acceptance.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper addresses the problem of estimating statistical divergence between probability distributions when only samples are available. It introduces PMD, which approximates the first Wasserstein metric by minimizing the cost of a perfect matching between samples from the two distributions. The authors demonstrate the strong consistency of PMD, highlighting its advantages over MMD: no bandwidth parameter tuning, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes. They showcase the benefits of PMD in two deep learning tasks: domain adaptation and generative modeling, achieving improved performance and faster convergence.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear and concise derivation of PMD, establishing its strong consistency as an estimator for the Wasserstein metric. The connection to optimal transport theory is well-explained.
* **Novelty:** While Wasserstein distances are not new, PMD offers a novel and practical approach to estimating it from samples, particularly suited for gradient-based optimization in deep learning.
* **Clear Advantages over MMD:**  The authors convincingly argue for the advantages of PMD over MMD, particularly regarding gradient behavior (avoiding saturation) and computational scalability through smaller mini-batches. The analysis in Figure 2(b) clearly illustrates the improved gradient signal.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate the practical effectiveness of PMD, showing improvements in performance and convergence speed.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is clearly structured, with well-defined sections and a logical flow of ideas.  The pseudocode and illustrations (Fig. 1) enhance understanding.
* **Honest Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of using the L1 distance and discuss potential avenues for future work, such as exploring alternative distance metrics.

**Weaknesses:**

* **L1 Distance Limitation:** The choice of L1 distance as the underlying metric for PMD is a significant limitation, especially in high-dimensional spaces like images. As acknowledged by the authors, it leads to blurry generated images in the generative modeling experiments. The results would be significantly strengthened by exploring alternatives like Euclidean distance or learned metrics.
* **Computational Advantage Not Fully Demonstrated:** While the authors claim PMD enables smaller mini-batches, the reported speedups aren't dramatic. The bottleneck remains the matching algorithm, and the reliance on a C++ implementation (without detailed GPU optimization) limits the demonstration of true scalability. A more detailed performance comparison including timings for the matching step on a GPU would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope.  Further validation on a broader range of datasets and tasks would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (MMD Comparison):** While PMD is presented as having fewer hyperparameters, the comparison with MMD highlights the sensitivity of MMD to *both* bandwidth and regularization strength. It feels a bit unfair to highlight this as a PMD advantage without a similar sensitivity analysis for the (fewer) parameters of PMD when applied to a complex task.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a novel and theoretically sound approach for estimating the Wasserstein metric.
* Addresses a key challenge in deep learning – estimating statistical divergence between distributions.
* Demonstrates clear advantages over MMD in terms of gradient behavior and potential scalability.
* Empirical results show improved performance in relevant applications.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The use of L1 distance limits the applicability of PMD to complex data like images.
* The computational advantage is not fully demonstrated, and the matching algorithm remains a potential bottleneck.
* The scope of experiments is somewhat limited.


**Overall Score:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Further Comments to Authors:**

*  **Explore alternative distance metrics:** Investigate the impact of using Euclidean distance, learned metrics, or other suitable distance functions within the PMD framework, especially for image generation tasks.
* **GPU Acceleration:** Prioritize GPU acceleration of the matching algorithm to fully realize the potential computational advantages of PMD. Provide a detailed performance comparison with and without GPU acceleration.
* **Wider Evaluation:** Evaluate PMD on a more diverse set of datasets and tasks to demonstrate its generalizability.
* **Sensitivity Analysis:**  Conduct a sensitivity analysis for PMD's hyperparameters in a complex task to provide a more complete comparison with MMD.
* Consider adding a discussion on the potential challenges of using PMD in very high-dimensional spaces (curse of dimensionality) and how these challenges might be addressed.
