

# Peer Review: "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a new sample-based statistical divergence for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations from the distributions, and the authors prove it's a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes using PMD as a training objective for deep learning tasks, claiming advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD): no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100 instead of 1000). The authors demonstrate PMD's effectiveness on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing improvements in both performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

The paper builds on prior work on statistical divergences (MMD [17]), optimal transport (Wasserstein metric [49]), and applications in domain adaptation [5] and generative modeling [31]. It makes a clear connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, which is a significant contribution to the growing literature on statistical divergences for deep learning.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with solid theoretical foundations. The authors provide a rigorous proof showing PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), supported by Propositions 3.1 and 3.2. The experimental section is comprehensive, with careful comparisons between PMD and MMD across multiple datasets and tasks (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW). The ablation studies on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithms (Fig. 5) provide valuable insights into PMD's practical implementation.

The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the blurry images generated on SVHN/LFW due to using L1 distance at pixel level, and notes that GPU acceleration would be beneficial for the matching algorithms. The authors are honest about the trade-offs between exact and approximate matching algorithms.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and the proposed solution. The theoretical analysis (Section 3) is presented logically, with clear definitions and proofs. The experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including implementation specifics, hyperparameters, and computational resources.

The figures (particularly Fig. 2, 3, 4, and 5) effectively illustrate key findings about gradient magnitudes, convergence speed, and performance comparisons. The pseudocode in Fig. 1 provides a clear implementation guide.

## Originality
The core idea of PMD represents a novel approach to estimating statistical divergence between distributions. While the connection between minimum weight matching and optimal transport is known, the authors successfully adapt this to create a practical, parameter-free divergence that can be used as a training objective in deep learning. The paper clearly differentiates PMD from existing methods, particularly MMD and WGAN, by highlighting its advantages in terms of hyperparameter requirements, gradient properties, and mini-batch size.

The paper's originality is further strengthened by its application to domain adaptation and generative modeling, where it outperforms MMD. While the concept of using matching for divergence estimation isn't entirely new, the authors' specific formulation and practical implementation are novel contributions.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. The practical advantages claimed for PMD (no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size) address real pain points in deep learning practice. Domain adaptation and generative modeling are active areas of research, and the paper demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods in these domains.

The paper advances the state of the art in statistical divergence estimation for deep learning, providing a new tool that could be widely adopted. The authors' empirical findings about gradient strength (Fig. 2b) and convergence speed (Fig. 3a) offer practical insights that will benefit the deep learning community.

## Strengths
- Strong theoretical foundation connecting PMD to Wasserstein metric
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple domains
- Clear demonstration of practical advantages over MMD
- Well-written with clear figures and pseudocode
- Honest discussion of limitations (e.g., blurry images on SVHN/LFW)

## Weaknesses
- The paper doesn't fully address how the choice of distance metric d(x,y) affects PMD's performance. While L1 is used, other metrics might require tuning.
- The blurry images on SVHN/LFW (Fig. 4) are a significant drawback for generative modeling, and the paper's explanation (pixel-level L1 distance not being good for natural images) feels somewhat superficial.
- The GPU acceleration claim (Section 2.3) lacks concrete timing results comparing CPU vs GPU implementations.
- The comparison with WGAN is limited to a brief mention; a more detailed discussion would strengthen the paper.

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field. The theoretical analysis is sound, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the practical advantages over MMD are compelling. While the blurry image issue in generative modeling is a notable weakness, it's acknowledged by the authors and could be addressed in future work. The paper's focus on practical implementation details (mini-batch size, matching algorithms) makes it immediately useful to practitioners.

The paper is clearly within NIPS scope as it addresses fundamental statistical problems with important applications in deep learning. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a new tool for the deep learning community.

## Arguments for Acceptance:
- Novel concept (PMD) with strong theoretical foundation
- Clear practical advantages over existing methods (MMD)
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple tasks
- Well-written and well-organized
- Addresses important pain points in deep learning practice

## Arguments Against Acceptance:
- Blurry images in generative modeling results (though acknowledged as a limitation)
- Superficial discussion of distance metric choice impact
- Limited comparison with WGAN

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement:
1. In Section 5.2, provide more detailed analysis of why L1 distance at pixel level leads to blurry images, and explore alternative distance metrics or architectures (e.g., code space) for generative modeling.
2. Include a brief discussion of how PMD might relate to WGAN, particularly in terms of the Wasserstein metric approximation.
3. Consider adding a sentence about how PMD could be extended to other distance metrics beyond L1.
4. Include a small experiment or timing comparison showing the GPU acceleration benefit for the matching algorithms.

This paper represents an important contribution to the field of statistical divergences in deep learning, with clear theoretical foundations and practical advantages that will likely be adopted by practitioners. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
