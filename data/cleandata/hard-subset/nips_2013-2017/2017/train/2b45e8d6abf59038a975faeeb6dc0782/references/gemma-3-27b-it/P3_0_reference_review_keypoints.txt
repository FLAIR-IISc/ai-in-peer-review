- Proposal of Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric tailored for deep learning.
- PMD is strongly consistent and grounded in optimal transport theory.
- PMD avoids MMD bandwidth tuning, yields stronger gradients, and works well with standard/smaller mini-batches.
- Empirical results show competitive or improved performance in domain adaptation and generative modeling, with faster convergence than MMD.
- Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically organized.
- Core idea leverages existing optimal transport/matching concepts, so novelty is limited.
- PMD’s use of L1 distance exclusively may hurt performance on complex data (e.g., blurry LFW results); other distances were not explored.
- Computational complexity and scalability to very large datasets are insufficiently detailed despite mention of GPU parallelization.
- Comparison between exact and approximate matching lacks a detailed ablation on the impact of approximation quality.
- Paper lacks discussion of scenarios where PMD might fail or not outperform MMD.
- Arguments for acceptance: theoretically sound, practical advantages, convincing experiments, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited novelty, reliance on L1 distance, and insufficient analysis of scalability and approximation effects.
- Recommended experiments/analyses: test alternative distance metrics (Euclidean, Cosine), detailed scalability/parallelization discussion, and ablation on matching algorithm quality.
- Recommended addition: explicit discussion of potential failure cases and limitations.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions; overall score 8/10, reviewer confidence 8/10.
