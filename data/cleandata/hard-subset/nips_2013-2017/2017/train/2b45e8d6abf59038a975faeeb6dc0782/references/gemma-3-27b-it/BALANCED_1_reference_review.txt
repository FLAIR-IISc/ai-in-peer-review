This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, offering an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) commonly used in deep learning tasks requiring distributional comparisons. The core idea is to estimate the Wasserstein distance by solving a minimum weight matching problem between samples from two distributions. The authors demonstrate theoretical consistency and propose an SGD-based learning algorithm utilizing PMD as a loss function, highlighting advantages such as the absence of bandwidth parameters, stronger gradients, and the ability to use standard mini-batch sizes. They validate their approach on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The use of a population-matching approach to estimate the Wasserstein metric, particularly in the context of deep learning and gradient-based optimization, is a novel contribution. While the Wasserstein metric itself is well-established, PMD provides a practical, sample-based estimator with potentially significant advantages.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper establishes the strong consistency of PMD as an estimator for the Wasserstein metric, providing a solid theoretical basis for its use.
* **Practical Advantages:** The identified benefits of PMD – no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes – are significant from a practical standpoint. These can translate to faster training and more stable learning dynamics.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD and support the claimed advantages over MMD. The convergence speed comparison is particularly compelling.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology. The pseudocode in Figure 1 is helpful for understanding the implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors address the computational cost of the matching problem, the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm for exact matching remains a concern for large N. The approximation used might introduce inaccuracies, though the experiments suggest this isn't a major issue. A more detailed discussion of the practical limitations regarding N would be valuable.
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The exclusive use of the L1 distance (Manhattan distance) in the experiments is a limitation. The impact of different distance metrics on the performance of PMD is not explored. The authors acknowledge L1 might not be ideal for natural images but don’t investigate alternatives.
* **Limited Baselines:** The comparison is primarily with MMD. While MMD is a strong baseline, it would be beneficial to compare PMD against more recent Wasserstein distance estimation techniques like WGAN, especially considering the authors acknowledge WGAN's limitations.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** While some parameter sensitivity analysis is presented (Fig. 3), a more comprehensive investigation of the hyperparameters in the domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** The phrase "population size N" is used somewhat interchangeably with "mini-batch size." This can be confusing. A clearer distinction between the overall population used for distance estimation and the mini-batch used for SGD updates would improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for estimating the Wasserstein metric.
* The practical advantages of PMD are well-motivated and supported by empirical results.
* The experiments demonstrate clear improvements over MMD in relevant deep learning tasks.
* The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational complexity of the matching problem could limit the scalability of PMD.
* The exclusive use of the L1 distance and limited baseline comparisons weaken the strength of the empirical results.
* The sensitivity analysis could be more thorough.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed PMD offers a promising alternative to existing methods for estimating distributional distances, with potential benefits for a range of deep learning applications. While the computational complexity and limited exploration of distance metrics are legitimate concerns, the novelty, theoretical grounding, and empirical validation outweigh these drawbacks. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Expand the discussion of computational complexity:** Provide more detailed analysis on the practical limitations of using the Hungarian algorithm for large N and potential strategies for further optimization (e.g., GPU parallelization beyond what's mentioned).
2.  **Investigate alternative distance metrics:** Explore the impact of different distance metrics (e.g., Euclidean distance) on the performance of PMD.
3.  **Include a comparison with WGAN:** Add a comparison to Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) as a more recent and relevant baseline.
4.  **Clarify terminology:** Distinguish more clearly between "population size" and "mini-batch size" throughout the paper.
5. **Expand sensitivity analysis:** A more exhaustive hyperparameter study will enhance the results' credibility.
