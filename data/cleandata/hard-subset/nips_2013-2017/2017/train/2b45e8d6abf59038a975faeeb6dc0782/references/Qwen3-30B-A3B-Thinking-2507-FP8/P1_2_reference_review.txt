

# Peer Review: "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a new statistical divergence for measuring the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two samples from the distributions, and the authors establish its connection to the first Wasserstein metric. They propose using PMD as a training objective for deep learning tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling, arguing it has advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes. The paper provides theoretical analysis supporting PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, along with empirical results demonstrating superior performance compared to MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), establishing PMD as a strongly consistent estimator. The relationship to optimal transport theory is well-explained.

2. **Practical Advantages**: The authors convincingly demonstrate several practical advantages of PMD over MMD, particularly the absence of a bandwidth hyperparameter and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs. 1000). The gradient analysis in Section 3.2 is particularly insightful.

3. **Well-Designed Experiments**: The empirical evaluation on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) is thorough and well-organized. The results consistently show PMD outperforming MMD in both performance and convergence speed.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-written, logically structured, and includes helpful figures (e.g., Fig. 2 showing gradient distributions) that clarify key concepts.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Connection to Existing Work**: The paper fails to properly acknowledge the connection to Earth Mover's Distance (EMD), which is essentially the same concept as the Wasserstein metric used in PMD. EMD has been widely used in computer vision (e.g., [40]) and image retrieval (e.g., [50]), and the authors' PMD is closely related to this established literature. This is a significant omission that undermines the claimed novelty.

2. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: While the paper establishes consistency, it lacks finite-sample error bounds for PMD, which would strengthen the theoretical claims. The authors mention this as future work but it's a notable gap in the current paper.

3. **Generative Modeling Limitations**: The paper acknowledges that PMD with L1 distance produces blurry images for natural images (Section 5.2), but doesn't explore solutions. This is a significant drawback for a generative modeling application, and the paper could have investigated alternatives (e.g., using perceptual loss functions).

4. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper doesn't address how PMD would scale to very large datasets (beyond the MNIST/SVHN/LFW experiments). The O(N^3) Hungarian algorithm could become prohibitive for large N, and while the authors mention an approximate O(N^2) algorithm, they don't provide concrete evidence of scalability for large-scale applications.

## Evaluation Against Review Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is appropriate, and the experimental results are convincing. However, the lack of finite-sample error bounds and the limited discussion of scalability are notable weaknesses. The authors are careful in acknowledging limitations (e.g., blurry images with L1 distance), which is commendable.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The pseudocode and figures significantly enhance understanding. The connection between the matching problem and the Wasserstein metric could be clarified with a more explicit statement of how PMD relates to the standard EMD.

**Originality**: While the core idea of using matching for statistical distance is novel in the context of deep learning applications, the connection to EMD/Wasserstein metric is not sufficiently distinguished from existing work. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough literature review that properly contextualizes their contribution relative to EMD-based approaches in computer vision.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a practical problem in deep learning (estimating distributional distances) with clear advantages over MMD. The demonstrated improvements in both domain adaptation and generative modeling are significant for practitioners. However, the limited scalability and the blurry image generation issue somewhat temper the significance for the most challenging generative modeling tasks.

## Specific Recommendations

1. **Expand Literature Review**: Properly cite and discuss the relationship between PMD and existing EMD work in computer vision. Acknowledge the conceptual similarity and clarify how PMD differs in its application to deep learning training objectives.

2. **Provide Finite-Sample Bounds**: Include finite-sample error bounds for PMD to strengthen the theoretical contribution. This would address a key gap in the current analysis.

3. **Address Image Quality Issue**: In the generative modeling experiments, discuss potential solutions to the blurry image problem (e.g., using perceptual loss functions or other distance metrics). This would significantly improve the practical utility of the approach.

4. **Include Scalability Analysis**: Add experiments demonstrating PMD's performance on larger datasets or with larger population sizes to address scalability concerns.

## Final Recommendation

The paper presents a compelling alternative to MMD with clear theoretical and empirical advantages. While the core idea is valuable and the experiments are well-conducted, the lack of proper context regarding existing EMD work and the limited theoretical analysis reduce the paper's novelty and completeness. With the suggested revisions, particularly the expanded literature review and additional theoretical analysis, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper has significant merit but requires some clarification of its relationship to existing work and additional theoretical depth to fully justify its contribution.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Clear theoretical foundation connecting PMD to Wasserstein metric
- Demonstrated practical advantages over MMD (no bandwidth, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch)
- Thorough experimental evaluation showing consistent improvements
- Well-written and well-organized paper

**Con Acceptance:**
- Significant omission of connection to Earth Mover's Distance literature
- Lack of finite-sample error bounds for PMD
- Limited discussion of scalability for large datasets
- Blurry image generation issue not adequately addressed

The paper's core contribution is valuable, but the missing context and theoretical gaps need to be addressed before final acceptance.
