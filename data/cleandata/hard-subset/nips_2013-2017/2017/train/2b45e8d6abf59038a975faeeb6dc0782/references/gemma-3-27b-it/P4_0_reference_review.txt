This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, positioning it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for tasks involving statistical divergence estimation, particularly within deep learning. The core idea is to minimize the cost of a bipartite matching between samples from two distributions. The authors demonstrate theoretical consistency and propose an SGD-based learning algorithm, highlighting advantages of PMD over MMD – namely, the absence of a bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use standard mini-batch sizes. Experimental validation is provided for domain adaptation and generative modeling.

The paper is well-structured and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the broader context of statistical divergence estimation and deep learning applications. The mathematical formulation of PMD is concise and well-defined. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is established with appropriate theoretical backing (propositions and theorem). The comparison with MMD is thorough, covering both theoretical and empirical aspects. The experiments are well-designed and provide evidence supporting the authors' claims. The inclusion of ablation studies (varying N and |B|) and a discussion of computational complexity strengthens the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** PMD offers a novel approach to estimating statistical divergence based on optimal transport, providing a potentially advantageous alternative to MMD.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for PMD, demonstrating its consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein metric.
*   **Practical Advantages:** The identified benefits of PMD (no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch sizes) are compelling and address known limitations of MMD.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate that PMD can outperform MMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors address the computational cost of the matching algorithm, it remains a potential bottleneck, especially for large-scale datasets. While approximated algorithms are mentioned, the overhead compared to MMD’s simpler formulation could be significant in certain applications.  More detailed benchmarking comparing the *actual* runtime for comparable performance on the tasks would be beneficial.
*   **Distance Metric:** The choice of L1 distance seems somewhat arbitrary and might not be optimal for all data modalities (e.g., images). A discussion on the impact of different distance metrics on PMD’s performance would be valuable.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the chosen tasks demonstrate the potential of PMD, further evaluation on a broader range of datasets and applications would strengthen the findings.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** The transition between mathematical notation and verbal explanation could be smoother in some sections. For example, the explanation of Equation (4) could be clearer.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for estimating statistical divergence.
*   The proposed PMD addresses practical limitations of existing approaches like MMD.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in relevant deep learning tasks.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the matching algorithm could limit the scalability of PMD.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope, and further validation is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a strong contribution to the field of statistical divergence estimation. The proposed PMD method is theoretically well-founded, offers practical advantages over existing approaches, and demonstrates promising empirical results. While the computational cost and limited scope of experiments are potential concerns, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions addressing the clarity issues and potentially including a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and the impact of different distance metrics.
