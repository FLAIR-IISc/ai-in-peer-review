### Summary

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions. The authors establish that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). A key contribution is a stochastic gradient-based learning algorithm that uses PMD as a training objective, where the gradient is computed by fixing the optimal matching for several updates. The paper contrasts PMD with the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD has several advantages: it is hyperparameter-free (no kernel bandwidth), provides stronger gradients that do not vanish, and allows for more efficient training with smaller mini-batch sizes. These claims are evaluated on two deep learning applications: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling, where PMD is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD in both performance and convergence speed.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Intuitive Formulation:** The paper provides a clear and well-motivated connection between minimum weight matching and the Wasserstein-1 distance. While estimating W1 on empirical samples via the assignment problem is a known result in the optimal transport literature, its framing as "PMD" and its application as a practical, differentiable loss for training deep neural networks is a valuable contribution. The method is intuitive and parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage over methods like MMD that require careful kernel selection and bandwidth tuning.

2.  **Insightful Comparison with MMD:** The paper offers a compelling critique of MMD, particularly regarding the issue of vanishing gradients. The argument that the RBF kernel can saturate when samples are either too far apart or too close together, weakening the gradient signal, is insightful. The proposed PMD with an L1 ground metric avoids this by providing a constant-magnitude gradient signal (the sign function), which can lead to more stable and faster training. This claim is well-supported by the gradient distribution analysis in Figure 2(b).

3.  **Novel and Practical Optimization Scheme:** The proposed training algorithm, which decouples the population size `N` (for a stable distance estimate) from the SGD mini-batch size `|B|` (for efficient updates), is a clever and practical heuristic. By computing the matching on a larger population and then performing several SGD steps on smaller subsamples of the matched pairs, the method mitigates the high computational cost of the matching step while benefiting from the efficiency of small-batch SGD. The experimental analysis in Section 5.3 effectively validates this design choice.

4.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and cover two important application areas. In domain adaptation, PMD shows competitive or superior performance to MMD, and crucially, faster convergence and less sensitivity to hyperparameters (Figure 3). In generative modeling, the results demonstrate that PMD can generate reasonable images, particularly in the challenging pixel space where MMD-based methods often struggle. The ablations on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithms further strengthen the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Novelty of the Estimator:** The paper presents PMD as a novel discrepancy, but it is precisely the empirical W1 distance obtained by solving the assignment problem, a standard approach in computational optimal transport. The authors should more explicitly acknowledge this and frame their contribution as the novel application and optimization of this estimator in a deep learning context, rather than the invention of the estimator itself. Citing foundational work like Villani's "Optimal Transport: Old and New" or Peyré and Cuturi's "Computational Optimal Transport" would better situate the work.

2.  **Gradient Approximation and Theoretical Justification:** The core of the learning algorithm relies on the assumption that the optimal matching `M*` remains constant during gradient updates. This makes the objective piecewise smooth, and the gradient is technically undefined at points where the optimal matching changes. The paper states this assumption but does not discuss its implications. While this approximation appears to work well empirically (as is common with non-smooth objectives in deep learning), a discussion of the potential pitfalls or a theoretical justification for why this is a reasonable approximation would strengthen the paper. For instance, how frequently does the matching change in practice, and does this cause instability?

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The computational complexity of the matching step, O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation, remains a significant bottleneck compared to alternatives. While the authors correctly argue that backpropagation can be more expensive, the matching cost can become dominant for large population sizes (`N`). The paper mentions that GPU implementations exist but does not use them. This leaves scalability as a potential concern, limiting the practical size of `N` to a few thousands, which might not be sufficient for capturing the structure of very complex distributions.

4.  **Choice of Ground Metric:** The performance of PMD is inherently tied to the choice of the ground distance `d(x, y)`. The paper exclusively uses the L1 distance. While simple and effective, this metric may not be perceptually meaningful for high-dimensional data like images, which could explain the blurriness of the generated samples in Section 5.2. In contrast, GANs (including WGAN) learn a metric through the discriminator, and MMD can use sophisticated kernels, potentially offering more flexibility in capturing relevant notions of similarity.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is a well-written and well-executed paper that introduces a valuable and practical tool for comparing probability distributions in deep learning. The proposed PMD method is principled, intuitive, and offers clear advantages over the popular MMD baseline, particularly regarding hyperparameter tuning and gradient stability. The proposed optimization scheme is a clever way to balance statistical accuracy with computational efficiency.

While the novelty of the estimator itself is limited, its successful application and the thorough empirical investigation of its properties for training deep networks represent a significant contribution. The weaknesses, primarily concerning the gradient approximation and scalability, are reasonable limitations for a first paper on this topic and open up clear avenues for future work. The work is likely to be of high interest to researchers in generative modeling, domain adaptation, and representation learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper addresses an important problem, presents a clean and effective solution with clear benefits over existing methods, and supports its claims with strong empirical evidence.
