Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-cost bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets from two distributions, with cost given by a ground distance d(·,·). This is exactly the optimal transport cost (Wasserstein-1) between the empirical (uniform) measures supported on the two sample sets. The authors (i) formalize PMD, (ii) show strong consistency to the population W1 using weak convergence (Varadarajan) plus stability of optimal transport (Villani), (iii) propose using PMD as a differentiable training objective for parameterized generators or feature extractors by computing a matching on “outer-loop” populations and running a few SGD steps treating the matching as fixed, and (iv) report empirical advantages versus MMD in domain adaptation and implicit generative modeling, highlighting fewer hyperparameters, stronger per-sample gradients, and the ability to use smaller inner-loop minibatches.

PMD connects directly to the classical OT literature and to recent ML uses of Wasserstein distances (e.g., WGAN). Compared to MMD (Gretton et al.), PMD avoids kernel bandwidth tuning and pairwise repulsion terms. Compared to adversarial approximations of W1 (WGAN), PMD has no critic network and yields an absolute-scale cost, but it requires solving a matching problem. The proposed learning strategy relates to minimizing empirical OT between batches; however, the paper does not compare to entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn distances, Cuturi 2013) or sliced Wasserstein approaches, both commonly used for speed and differentiability.

Strengths
- Clear formulation: PMD is precisely empirical W1 between batches with uniform marginals; the connection to OT is correctly established, and the strong consistency follows directly from standard results.
- Practical algorithm: Using a fixed matching across several SGD steps and sub-sampling matched pairs for inner-loop updates is simple and effective in practice.
- Gradient analysis: The paper gives an intuitive and empirical comparison showing PMD’s per-sample gradients are more uniformly strong than MMD’s when using an RBF kernel.
- Empirical evidence: On Office-31 domain adaptation, PMD matches or exceeds MMD on several transfers and converges faster. In generative modeling on MNIST/SVHN/LFW, PMD produces less noisy samples than MMD in pixel space and trains faster, with timing analyses that separate matching vs. SGD costs. Results suggest approximate matching is adequate.

Weaknesses
- Novelty: PMD equals the standard empirical Wasserstein-1 distance with uniform weights; viewing it as “minimum weight matching” is a restatement. Using empirical OT as a loss for training generators or features has been explored in OT-based ML, and the paper lacks comparisons to closely related OT solvers (Sinkhorn, sliced Wasserstein) that are widely used for differentiable and scalable training.
- Missing related work/comparisons: No experimental or theoretical comparison to:
  - Entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi 2013) which are O(N^2) per iteration, GPU-friendly, and differentiable.
  - Sliced/Projected Wasserstein distances, known to scale well in high dimensions.
  - OT-based domain adaptation methods (e.g., Courty et al.) or popular domain adaptation baselines (DANN, CORAL, JAN, CMD), despite citing some of them.
  - OT-based generative methods (e.g., Wasserstein Auto-Encoders), and WGAN baselines on the same datasets.
- Theory is minimal: The main theorem is known; no finite-sample rates, variance bounds, or concentration are provided (e.g., rates for empirical W1; known to suffer from curse of dimensionality). The differentiability argument for ignoring ∂M*/∂θ could be formalized via Danskin’s theorem; conditions and subgradient behavior at matching changes are not discussed.
- Evaluation metrics: Generative modeling is evaluated mainly via visuals and training loss; no standard quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) or likelihood proxies. Reported images on SVHN/LFW are blurry with L1 pixel costs, acknowledging the metric mismatch.
- Scope and rigor in experiments: Domain adaptation compares only to MMD on Office-31 with fixed VGG features; gains are mixed and modest on some shifts. Hyperparameter search uses target labels for validation (albeit equally for both methods), which may not reflect unsupervised adaptation practice.

Quality
- Technical correctness: The equivalence to empirical OT and strong consistency are correct. The optimization procedure is plausible but lacks theoretical convergence guarantees in the presence of discrete matchings that change with parameters. The computational analysis is honest and shows feasibility with approximate matching.
- Empirical support: Evidence supports “stronger gradients” and “smaller minibatches” claims versus MMD, but broader baselines and standard metrics would strengthen conclusions.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the objective and algorithms are clear. The gradient treatment could reference Danskin’s theorem and discuss non-smoothness. Matching frequency, inner-loop step counts, and the impact on bias/variance could be specified more precisely. Related work on OT solvers is under-cited, which obscures the method’s position in the literature.

Originality
- Limited. The core discrepancy is standard empirical W1; the training heuristic (fixing matchings across steps and sub-sampling pairs) is straightforward. Novelty lies mainly in the empirical investigation and practical recipe.

Significance
- Moderate. Showing that empirical OT-based matching can be used as a drop-in loss with practical efficiency and stable gradients is useful. However, without comparisons to established OT solvers and stronger baselines/metrics, the impact is constrained.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare against Sinkhorn OT (entropic regularization), sliced Wasserstein, and WGAN/WAE on the same tasks; report standard generative metrics (e.g., FID).
- For domain adaptation, include DANN, CORAL, JAN, CMD, and OT-based DA baselines; report comprehensive results on Office-31.
- Theoretically justify the gradient step with changing matchings using Danskin’s theorem; discuss subgradients and conditions for correctness.
- Provide finite-sample error/concentration bounds for PMD estimation or cite known results (e.g., convergence rates for empirical W1).
- Use feature-based costs (perceptual/learned embeddings) to address pixel-level metric mismatch; evaluate sensitivity to choice of ground metric.
- Discuss and, if possible, implement GPU-accelerated matching or Sinkhorn as a scalable alternative.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, principled objective tied to Wasserstein-1 with a practical training algorithm.
- Clear empirical advantage over MMD in convergence speed and gradient properties.
- Useful insights on minibatch sizing and approximate matching.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty compared to existing empirical OT and modern OT solvers; misses key related work and baselines.
- Theoretical contribution is minimal beyond standard consistency; no rates or convergence analysis of the training procedure.
- Experimental evaluation is narrow; generative results are qualitative and not competitive with contemporary OT/GAN baselines.

Overall: Promising practical recipe and analysis, but gaps in related work, baselines, and theory reduce its suitability without further strengthening.
