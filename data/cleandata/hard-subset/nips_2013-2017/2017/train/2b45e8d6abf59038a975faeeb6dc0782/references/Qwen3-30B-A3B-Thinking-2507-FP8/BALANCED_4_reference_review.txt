

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for statistical divergence between distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching between two sample populations from the distributions, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes an algorithm to use PMD as a training objective, and compares it with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The work builds on optimal transport theory and connects to existing work on statistical divergence estimation, particularly MMD and Wasserstein GAN.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The consistency proof of PMD as an estimator of the Wasserstein metric is rigorous, leveraging established results in optimal transport theory. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is well-motivated and clearly explained.

2. **Practical Advantages:** The paper convincingly demonstrates key practical advantages of PMD over MMD: no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients (supported by empirical evidence in Figure 2b), and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes during training.

3. **Clear Experimental Design:** The experiments on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) are well-designed, with appropriate baselines and careful hyperparameter tuning. The results consistently show PMD outperforming MMD in convergence speed and sometimes final performance.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear definitions, good organization, and helpful figures that effectively illustrate the concepts and results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Complexity Concerns:** While the authors claim the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm is acceptable, this becomes problematic for large N (e.g., N > 1000). The paper lacks detailed discussion of when PMD would become computationally prohibitive compared to other methods. The claim that GPU acceleration can significantly improve performance (Section 5.3) needs more concrete evidence.

2. **Limited Comparison Scope:** The paper focuses exclusively on comparing with MMD, ignoring other relevant divergence estimators like Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) or Jensen-Shannon divergence. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the claims about PMD's advantages.

3. **Generative Modeling Limitations:** The paper notes PMD produces blurrier images on SVHN and LFW than MMD, attributing it to the pixel-level L1 distance. However, this is a significant limitation for image generation tasks that needs more thorough discussion. The authors should explore whether using feature space distances (like MMD) would resolve this issue.

4. **Lack of Finite-Sample Error Bounds:** While the paper establishes consistency, it does not provide finite-sample error bounds for PMD, which would be valuable for understanding its statistical properties and trade-offs.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

1. Conduct additional experiments comparing PMD with WGAN and other divergence estimators to better establish its relative advantages.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of the computational trade-offs, including runtime measurements for different N values and comparison with other matching algorithms.

3. Address the generative modeling limitations by exploring feature space distances or other modifications to PMD that might improve image quality.

4. Include finite-sample error bounds for PMD to strengthen the theoretical foundation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that introduces a novel divergence estimator with clear practical advantages over MMD. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the empirical results are convincing for the tasks evaluated. The main weaknesses relate to computational complexity concerns and limited comparison scope, but these are addressable with additional experiments and analysis.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of statistical divergence estimation for deep learning applications, and the proposed method has potential for broader adoption. While the current results are promising, the paper would benefit from addressing the computational considerations more thoroughly and expanding the comparison to other relevant methods.

**Decision:** Accept

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with strong theoretical foundations, clearly written, makes a novel contribution to the field, and demonstrates significant practical value. The minor weaknesses identified are not severe enough to outweigh the paper's strengths and contributions.
