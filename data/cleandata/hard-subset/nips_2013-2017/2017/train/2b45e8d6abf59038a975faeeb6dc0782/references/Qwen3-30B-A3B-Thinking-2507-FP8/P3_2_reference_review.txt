

# Peer Review: "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two populations drawn from two distributions. The authors establish that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and propose using it as a training objective for deep learning tasks. The paper claims PMD has advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes. The authors evaluate PMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, reporting superior performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper correctly establishes PMD's connection to the Wasserstein metric, showing it's a strongly consistent estimator. This is a solid theoretical contribution that fills a gap in understanding sample-based approximations of the Wasserstein metric.

2. **Practical Advantages**: The arguments regarding PMD's advantages over MMD are well-supported and convincing. The demonstration that PMD gradients remain strong regardless of sample proximity (unlike MMD's gradient saturation) is particularly valuable for training stability.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) provide compelling evidence that PMD outperforms MMD in both performance and convergence speed. The ablation studies on population size and mini-batch size effectively support the paper's claims.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear definitions, logical flow, and helpful figures that effectively communicate key concepts.

## Weaknesses
1. **Theoretical Gap**: The paper lacks finite-sample error bounds for PMD. While the consistency result is valuable, the absence of convergence rates or error bounds limits the practical utility of the theoretical contribution. In deep learning contexts, understanding how quickly PMD converges to the true Wasserstein distance is crucial for practitioners.

2. **Missing Comparison with WGAN**: The paper mentions WGAN in Section 3.1 but fails to compare PMD with this influential approach to generative modeling. WGAN has been widely adopted and shows strong performance, making this comparison essential for a comprehensive evaluation of PMD's practical utility.

3. **Limited Generative Modeling Metrics**: The generative modeling experiments rely on qualitative visual assessment without quantitative metrics (e.g., FID, Inception Score). The claim that PMD generates "less noisy" images needs stronger quantitative support.

4. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper mentions the O(N³) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, it doesn't provide sufficient empirical evidence of how this scales in practice for large N (e.g., N=1000-2000). The claim that "the time complexity for computing matching is still acceptable" requires more substantial justification.

5. **Narrow Comparison Scope**: The paper only compares PMD with MMD, ignoring other relevant sample-based divergences like Cramer GAN or Energy Distance. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's position.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing PMD as a practical alternative to MMD. The core insight that PMD avoids the bandwidth hyperparameter issue of MMD is particularly compelling. However, the theoretical contribution is incomplete without finite-sample analysis, which is standard for such methods in machine learning.

The experimental evaluation is generally strong but has notable gaps. The omission of WGAN comparison is especially problematic since WGAN has become the de facto standard for generative modeling with Wasserstein metrics. The paper's claim that PMD "differs from WGAN by not requiring a separate critic network" is valid, but the lack of direct comparison weakens the paper's position.

The generative modeling results are intriguing but need quantitative validation. The statement that "PMD generates acceptable images on pixel space" while MMD only works well on "autoencoder code space" requires more objective evidence. The paper's supplementary material showing circular interpolation is helpful but doesn't replace standard generative metrics.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field with a novel approach to statistical divergence estimation that addresses practical limitations of existing methods. The main issues that need addressing are:

1. Provide finite-sample error bounds or at least discuss convergence rates for PMD
2. Add a comparison with WGAN in the generative modeling experiments
3. Include quantitative metrics (FID, IS) for generative modeling results
4. Expand the discussion of computational complexity with empirical evidence

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in deep learning. With these revisions, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS.

## Final Verdict
The paper presents a novel and practically useful approach to statistical divergence estimation that addresses significant limitations of MMD. While the theoretical analysis requires strengthening and the experimental evaluation needs expansion, the core contribution is valuable and well-executed. I believe this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field and should be accepted with minor revisions.
