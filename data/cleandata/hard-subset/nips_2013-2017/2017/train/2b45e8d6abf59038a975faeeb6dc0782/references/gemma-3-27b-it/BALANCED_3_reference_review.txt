This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, aiming to address limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in deep learning applications. The core idea is to minimize the cost of optimally matching samples from two distributions, offering advantages such as the absence of bandwidth parameters, potentially stronger gradients, and suitability for standard mini-batch sizes. The authors demonstrate PMD’s effectiveness in domain adaptation and generative modeling, showcasing improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly identifying the drawbacks of MMD and proposing a reasonable alternative. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is explicitly established through theoretical analysis, proving strong consistency. The presentation is generally clear, with a logical flow of ideas. The experimental results are encouraging, demonstrating PMD’s benefits in practice. The inclusion of theoretical grounding alongside empirical validation is a significant strength.  The comparison to MMD is well-structured, highlighting the advantages of PMD, particularly regarding gradient behavior and mini-batch size requirements.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved. 

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty of the Core Idea:** While the application to deep learning and the specific analysis of PMD are novel, the core idea of using optimal transport/matching for distribution comparison is not entirely new. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of existing work leveraging optimal transport, especially in the context of generative modeling (e.g., WGAN). Simply mentioning WGAN and dismissing it due to its scale factor issue feels insufficient; a deeper comparison of PMD’s advantages *specifically* concerning the challenges of training a critic network would be valuable.
* **Distance Metric Choice:**  The exclusive use of the L1 distance for PMD is a limitation.  While justified by stronger gradients, its suitability for complex data distributions (like images) is questionable.  The authors acknowledge this in the generative modeling experiments but don’t explore other distance metrics within the PMD framework. Investigating different distances (e.g., Euclidean, cosine) and their impact on performance is crucial.
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:**  While the paper mentions the computational complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, the practical implications for scaling to higher-dimensional data or larger sample sizes are not fully addressed.  The claim that the matching cost is acceptable compared to backpropagation is somewhat vague.  More detailed timing results, especially scaling experiments with increasing N, are needed to substantiate this claim.  The GPU parallelization potential is mentioned but lacks detail.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation, while solid, is limited to two tasks. Expanding the evaluation to other relevant tasks (e.g., semi-supervised learning) would strengthen the paper's claims of generalizability.  Ablation studies, specifically demonstrating the benefit of the smaller mini-batch size, would be beneficial.
* **Clarity on “Stronger Gradient”:** The discussion of stronger gradients is somewhat hand-wavy. The empirical evidence presented (Fig. 2(b)) is helpful but lacks a quantitative metric to define "stronger".  Connecting the gradient properties directly to improved convergence speed or final performance would be more compelling.
* **Minor Issues:**  The writing could benefit from minor edits for clarity and conciseness. For example, the repeated mention of "Eq. (X)" is slightly distracting.



**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The proof of strong consistency provides a solid theoretical basis for PMD.
* **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:**  The paper effectively articulates the limitations of MMD and the need for alternative divergence measures.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results demonstrate PMD’s practical benefits in domain adaptation and generative modeling.
* **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of computational complexity and potential GPU acceleration is valuable.
* **Well-Organized and Readable:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated alternative to MMD for estimating Wasserstein distance.
* Demonstrates practical improvements in domain adaptation and generative modeling.
* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in deep learning.
* Clear presentation and well-motivated approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Incremental novelty; core idea of optimal transport is not entirely new.
* Limited exploration of distance metrics beyond L1.
* Incomplete discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
* Relatively narrow experimental evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While not groundbreaking, it offers a valuable improvement over existing methods for estimating distribution divergence, with clear theoretical justification and empirical support. The weaknesses identified are not fatal, and addressing them would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:**  **Weak Accept**. I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should address the points raised regarding novelty, distance metric exploration, computational complexity, and experimental scope. A more thorough comparison to related work on optimal transport, especially WGAN, is crucial.  Additionally, a more quantitative analysis of the gradient properties would be beneficial. Addressing these issues will elevate the paper’s impact and ensure its lasting contribution to the field.
