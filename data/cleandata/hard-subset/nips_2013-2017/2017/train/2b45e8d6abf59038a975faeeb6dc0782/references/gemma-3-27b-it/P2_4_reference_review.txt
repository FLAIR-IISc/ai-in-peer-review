## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric. PMD calculates the minimum cost matching between samples from two distributions. The authors demonstrate theoretical consistency and propose a stochastic gradient descent algorithm for learning parameters using PMD as a loss function. They highlight advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) – namely, lack of bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes. The method is evaluated on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing competitive or improved performance compared to MMD.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** PMD offers a new approach to estimating Wasserstein distance, particularly appealing in scenarios with intractable distributions and a desire for gradient-based learning. While the core idea of matching is not entirely new, applying it directly as a differentiable loss function with theoretical justification is a valuable contribution.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, establishing strong consistency. This is a significant strength, grounding the method in established mathematical principles.
*   **Practical Advantages:** The claimed benefits over MMD – no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch size – are compelling and potentially impactful for practical applications.  The empirical results support these claims, showing faster convergence in the domain adaptation experiment.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions of PMD and the learning algorithm are clearly explained.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate the potential of PMD. While not a massive performance leap, the improvements and faster convergence are encouraging.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:**  While the authors acknowledge the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm for exact matching and mention an approximation, the computational cost remains a potential bottleneck, especially for large datasets.  The experiments used relatively small population sizes (N=2000).  Scaling to much larger N remains an open question. The computational analysis in section 5.3 is useful, but more extensive benchmarking on larger datasets would strengthen the claims.
*   **Limited Generative Modeling Results:** The generative modeling results are somewhat underwhelming, with the generated images being blurry. The authors attribute this to the L1 distance, but a deeper exploration of alternative distances or architectural choices might be warranted. A more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art generative models (beyond MMD) would be valuable.
*   **Sensitivity Analysis:** While the authors perform a sensitivity analysis, it's somewhat limited. Exploring the impact of different distance metrics (beyond L1) on the performance of PMD would be beneficial.
*   **Related Work Discussion:** The discussion of related work could be more thorough, particularly regarding existing approaches for estimating Wasserstein distance from samples (e.g., those mentioned in the references) and their limitations.  A more detailed comparison of PMD with these methods would clarify its unique advantages.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a novel and theoretically grounded contribution. The proposed PMD offers a promising alternative to MMD for estimating Wasserstein distance and has the potential to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of gradient-based learning in various applications. The empirical results are encouraging, and the authors clearly articulate the advantages of their approach. While the computational cost and the limited generative modeling results are areas for improvement, the overall quality of the work is high.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and consistency guarantee.
*   Demonstrated practical advantages over MMD in domain adaptation.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Addresses an important problem in machine learning – estimating distances between distributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Computational cost could be a limiting factor for large-scale applications.
*   Generative modeling results are not fully convincing.
*   Limited comparison with a wider range of Wasserstein distance estimation techniques.



**Rating:**  **Strong Accept** (7/10)

**Recommendations:**

*   The authors should address the computational cost concerns more thoroughly, potentially exploring GPU acceleration or alternative matching algorithms.
*   They should expand the generative modeling experiments, exploring different distances and architectures.
*   A more comprehensive discussion of related work, including a detailed comparison with other Wasserstein distance estimation methods, would strengthen the paper.
*   Consider including a discussion on the choice of the distance metric 'd' and how it impacts the results (beyond just L1).
