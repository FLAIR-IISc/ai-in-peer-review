- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based divergence defined via minimum weight matching and proven consistent for the 1-Wasserstein metric.  
- Proposes using PMD as a training objective in deep learning with applications to domain adaptation and generative modeling.  
- PMD advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and requires smaller mini-batches.  
- Empirical results show PMD outperforms MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling benchmarks.  
- Theoretical contribution: a technically sound consistency proof linking PMD to optimal transport/Wasserstein theory.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and ablation studies on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithms.  
- Paper is clear and well-organized, with accessible theory, useful pseudocode, and informative figures.  
- Originality: PMD is a novel formulation distinct from MMD and prior Wasserstein approaches.  
- Significance: practical implications for faster convergence and easier hyperparameter tuning in deep learning.  
- Computational concern: exact matching via the Hungarian algorithm is O(N^3) and the paper underanalyzes scaling to very large datasets and timing comparisons.  
- Limited comparison with other Wasserstein approximations (e.g., WGAN, Sinkhorn) in terms of complexity, gradient behavior, and empirical performance.  
- Generative modeling results are mixed: PMD images are less noisy but can be blurry on natural images, and the paper does not explore alternative distance metrics or code-space matching sufficiently.  
- Recommendation: accept — solid theoretical and practical contribution with minor addressable weaknesses.  
- Suggested improvements: expand comparisons to WGAN/Sinkhorn, provide detailed analysis of exact vs. approximate matching trade-offs and timings, investigate why PMD struggles on natural images, and consider extensions to other distance metrics or higher-order Wasserstein metrics.
