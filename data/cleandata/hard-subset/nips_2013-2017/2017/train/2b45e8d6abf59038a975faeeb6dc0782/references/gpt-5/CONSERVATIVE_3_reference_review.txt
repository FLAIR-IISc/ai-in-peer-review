Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets drawn from distributions pX and pY with a chosen ground metric (e.g., L1). The authors show that PMD equals the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (EMD) between empirical measures and, via standard weak convergence and OT stability results (Varadarajan; Villani), argue that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the true Wasserstein-1 distance. They propose using PMD as a training objective for parameterized distributions, computing a matching per “population,” and then taking SGD steps while assuming the matching stays fixed over a few iterations. They argue PMD has advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batches. Experiments compare PMD to MMD on domain adaptation (Office) and on implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), reporting better or comparable performance and faster convergence in epochs.

Relation to prior work
- PMD is essentially the empirical OT (Earth Mover’s Distance) with uniform masses and a chosen ground metric (Hungarian for exact; Drake-Hougardy for approximate). The “strong consistency” result is a direct corollary of well-known results in OT; the connection to Wasserstein-1 is standard.
- There is extensive literature on efficient and differentiable approximations to OT for learning, notably entropic-regularized OT / Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) and their use in deep learning; this is not discussed or compared. Also relevant are sliced Wasserstein methods, WGAN/WGAN-GP, Cramer distance, and MMD-GAN; the baselines and discussion are incomplete.
- The use of Danskin’s theorem (or subdifferential calculus) is the usual way to justify ignoring derivatives through the argmin in min over a finite set; the paper appeals informally to “matching does not change for infinitesimal θ” without a formal statement/assumptions.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive objective (empirical W1 via matching) with a clear connection to OT.
- Practical recipe to use PMD in SGD by “freezing” matchings across a few steps; the idea is easy to implement.
- Empirical results suggest PMD can be less sensitive to hyperparameters than RBF-MMD and may yield stronger per-sample gradients.
- Experiments include two applications (domain adaptation, implicit generative modeling) and some timing/ablation on population/mini-batch sizes.

Weaknesses
- Novelty: The objective is the standard empirical W1; the theoretical “strong consistency” is a re-statement of known OT results. The algorithmic contribution (SGD with periodic match recomputation and ignoring gradients through the matching) is incremental and not sufficiently justified theoretically.
- Missing baselines: For generative modeling, comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP, Cramer distance, energy distance, MMD-GAN, and sliced Wasserstein are absent. For domain adaptation, comparisons to DANN, CORAL, JAN, CMD beyond MMD are needed. Without these, claims of superiority are unconvincing.
- Differentiability/gradient correctness: The paper claims an “unbiased stochastic gradient” by not propagating through the matching. A formal justification via Danskin’s theorem (uniqueness of the minimizer; smoothness of d; subgradients at ties) is missing. The subsequent “subsampled” gradient (Eq. 4) is explicitly approximate and biased; the impact on convergence is not analyzed.
- Sample complexity and scaling: Empirical W1 is known to converge slowly in high dimensions (rates ~ N^{-1/d}). The toy study shows ~N^{-0.23} in d=5, aligning with this concern. No finite-sample error bounds or practical guidance are provided; the paper claims PMD works with N≈100–1000, but robustness beyond small images/features is unclear.
- Computational efficiency: Exact matching is O(N^3); approximate matching is O(N^2). Even with GPU distance computation, matching dominates and is implemented in single-thread CPU in the experiments. There is no thorough wall-clock comparison versus MMD and Sinkhorn OT; epoch counts alone do not address overall time-to-quality, especially at larger N.
- Empirical evaluation: 
  - Generative modeling is evaluated largely with qualitative images; no standard quantitative metrics (FID/KID, Inception score) or likelihood proxies. Datasets are small/low-res (MNIST, SVHN, 32×32 gray LFW).
  - Domain adaptation uses pretrained VGG features and small MLPs; gains over MMD are modest and inconsistent across shifts. Strong contemporary methods are not compared. 
  - Hyperparameter tuning uses 100 labeled target samples for validation in an “unsupervised” adaptation setting; this needs careful discussion and alignment with established protocols.
- Related work gaps: No discussion of Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi 2013) and their differentiability/efficiency; no comparison to stochastic/regularized OT methods that address gradient saturation and computational load.

Quality
Technically sound where it reuses known OT theory, but the gradient estimator justification is informal, and the subsampled matching heuristic is unanalysed. Experimental rigor is limited by weak baselines and lack of quantitative metrics.

Clarity
Generally clearly written and easy to follow. Theoretical claims should be stated with proper conditions (e.g., Danskin/subgradient). Clearer separation between estimator consistency and optimization properties would help.

Originality
Limited. The core idea is empirical W1 via matching; the “PMD” naming does not hide that. The learning recipe is incremental.

Significance
Potentially useful as a simple drop-in loss, but given missing baselines, scalability concerns, and weak empirical validation, the contribution is not yet compelling for NeurIPS.

Questions for the authors
1. Please formalize the gradient claim using Danskin’s theorem (assumptions, uniqueness, subgradient at ties). Is the estimator unbiased? What is the impact of using fixed matchings across mini-batches on convergence?
2. Why no comparison to Sinkhorn (entropic-regularized) OT, which is differentiable, fast on GPU, and widely used? Similarly, compare to WGAN-GP and Cramer distance.
3. Provide wall-clock time-to-target-quality comparisons vs MMD and Sinkhorn at various N, including GPU-accelerated matching if feasible.
4. Add quantitative generative metrics (FID/KID) and stronger datasets (CIFAR-10, CelebA). 
5. Clarify the domain adaptation protocol regarding target labels for validation; add standard baselines (DANN, CORAL, JAN, CMD) and report target error without target labels for tuning if claiming unsupervised DA.

Pros
- Simple, interpretable objective with known theoretical grounding.
- Potentially stronger gradients and less hyperparameter sensitivity than RBF-MMD.
- Some positive empirical indications.

Cons
- Minimal novelty; known estimator repackaged.
- Missing key baselines (Sinkhorn, WGAN-GP, etc.) and quantitative metrics.
- Weak theoretical treatment of optimization/gradients; no sample complexity analysis.
- Scalability and runtime comparisons incomplete.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is straightforward and known; the paper lacks rigorous optimization analysis and comprehensive empirical validation against established OT-based and adversarial methods. Strengthening theory (gradient justification, finite-sample analysis), adding strong baselines (Sinkhorn, WGAN-GP, Cramer), quantitative evaluations, and wall-clock comparisons could make a future version competitive.
