Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets from distributions pX and pY, using a chosen ground metric (typically L1). PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between equally weighted empirical measures. The authors (i) re-derive PMD as the optimal transport primal on empirical distributions, (ii) prove almost-sure consistency via Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport, (iii) propose a training loop that alternates computing a matching on a “population” of size N and doing several SGD steps on mini-batches of size |B| using the fixed matching, and (iv) demonstrate applications to domain adaptation (Office dataset) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), reporting better convergence and performance than MMD.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: many deep learning tasks need sample-based, differentiable distributional distances. PMD alleviates kernel bandwidth tuning and gradient saturation issues common with RBF-MMD.
- Proper grounding in OT: PMD equals the 1-Wasserstein between empirical distributions. The consistency statement is correct (albeit standard).
- Practical training recipe: fixing the matching for several SGD steps and subsampling matched pairs (|B| ≪ N) is simple and seems to work empirically. The observation that gradients with L1 cost remain “strong” for outliers is useful.
- Empirical evidence: On Office domain adaptation, PMD matches or improves over MMD on several shifts; for generation, PMD produces cleaner samples than MMD in pixel space and converges faster.
- Discussion of computational choices: Hungarian vs. O(N^2) approximate matching, timing breakdowns, and insensitivity to N and matching algorithm are informative.

Weaknesses and missing related work
- Novelty is limited. PMD is the standard empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD with uniform masses; the “strong consistency” result follows directly from well-known results (Villani). The main novelty is a particular training scheme that fixes the assignment during inner-loop updates.
- Major omissions in related work:
  - Entropic-regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) and their GPU-parallelizable, scalable variants widely used for learning with OT; this is a fundamental baseline for both accuracy and speed that should be compared.
  - Sliced Wasserstein distances and projections (Rabin et al., Kolouri et al.), which are computationally lighter and used in generative modeling.
  - Sample complexity rates for empirical OT (e.g., Fournier & Guillin 2015; Weed & Bach 2017), which would contextualize the N^-0.23 empirical rate the authors observed.
  - OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al., 2016–2017) is directly relevant but not discussed.
- Gradient treatment lacks rigor. Eq. (3) states an “unbiased stochastic gradient” and claims the gradient does not propagate through the matching. The proper tool here is Danskin’s/Envelope theorem: the gradient of min_M f(θ, M) equals ∇θ f(θ, M*) when the argmin is unique; otherwise one gets subgradients. The paper should formalize differentiability conditions, clarify that non-differentiabilities occur at assignment changes, and avoid calling this unbiased without justification. The inner-loop heuristic that fixes M* for several steps introduces bias; no analysis is provided on its impact.
- Computational claims versus alternatives. Even O(N^2) matching is expensive for large N or high D; computing all pairwise distances is O(N^2 D). In contrast, Sinkhorn OT has highly optimized GPU implementations and can trade accuracy for speed via entropic regularization. No comparisons are shown.
- Experimental scope and metrics:
  - Domain adaptation comparisons are limited to MMD; stronger baselines (DANN/GRL, CORAL, JAN, CMD, DSN) should be included. Gains are modest and inconsistent across shifts.
  - Generative modeling lacks standard quantitative metrics (Inception Score, FID), available at the time (Salimans et al., 2016). Only qualitative results are shown, with acknowledged blur due to pixel L1.
  - No comparison to WGANs despite an extended discussion; WGAN with GP was already competitive in 2017.
- Claims need nuance:
  - “Parameter free”: PMD still requires choosing the ground cost (L1 vs L2) and its scaling, which acts as a hyperparameter. The contrast with kernel bandwidths should be presented more carefully.
  - “Smaller mini-batch size”: PMD still needs a large “population” N to compute a good matching periodically; the training-time and memory implications should be reported relative to MMD and Sinkhorn OT. MMD also has linear-time unbiased estimators that permit small batches (albeit higher variance).

Quality
Technically mostly sound, but (i) the gradient argument needs proper citation to Danskin and discussion of subgradients/assignment changes; (ii) the training heuristic’s bias is not analyzed; (iii) theoretical contribution is incremental (consistency known). Empirical evidence is suggestive but lacks strong baselines and standard metrics.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. However:
- Using a new name (PMD) for empirical 1-Wasserstein may confuse; explicitly acknowledge equivalence to EMD/OT between empirical measures and position your contribution as a training strategy using discrete OT.
- Clarify the optimization logic (Danskin theorem, subgradients) and when the gradient is valid.
- Expand related work to include Sinkhorn, sliced Wasserstein, OT for domain adaptation, and empirical OT rates.

Originality
Moderate. The core distance is well-known; the contribution is a specific way to use it in SGD with fixed assignments and subsampling. The gradient-strength analysis vs. MMD is a nice empirical observation, but not fundamentally new.

Significance
Potentially useful as a simple recipe for practitioners who want an OT-based objective without training a critic. However, without comparisons to Sinkhorn OT and stronger baselines in both tasks, it is hard to assess its practical advantage. As presented, the impact is limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, intuitive method that often works better than MMD and converges faster.
- Clear connection to OT and a practical training loop that others can implement.
- Useful empirical observations about gradients and batch sizes.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; missing key related work and baselines (especially Sinkhorn).
- Incomplete experimental evaluation (no FID/IS; weak DA baselines).
- Insufficient theoretical treatment of the optimization (Danskin/subgradients; bias from fixed matches).

Questions for authors
- Can you formalize the gradient claim via Danskin’s theorem and discuss subgradients when the assignment changes?
- How does PMD compare to entropic OT (Sinkhorn) in speed/accuracy on your tasks?
- How sensitive are results to the ground metric and its scaling? Have you tried learned feature spaces for the cost?
- For domain adaptation, how does PMD compare to DANN/CORAL/JAN/CMD on Office?
- Can you report quantitative generative metrics (FID/IS) and include WGAN-GP as a baseline?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is practical and the paper is clear, but key related work/baselines and a more rigorous optimization treatment are missing. Strengthening these points could make this a solid contribution.
