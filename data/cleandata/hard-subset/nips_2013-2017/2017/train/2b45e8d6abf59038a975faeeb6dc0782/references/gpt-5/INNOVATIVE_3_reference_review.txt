Summary
The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets, as a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance. The authors show PMD equals the optimal transport between empirical measures with uniform weights and provide a (standard) strong consistency argument via Varadarajan + stability of optimal transport. They propose a practical training procedure for deep models that (i) computes a matching once per “population,” (ii) backpropagates through the distances but not through the discrete matching, and (iii) performs several SGD steps on smaller mini-batches while keeping the matching fixed. They use exact Hungarian or an O(N^2) approximate matching and claim advantages over MMD: no bandwidth, stronger per-sample gradients, and the ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches. Empirically, PMD is evaluated on Office domain adaptation and on generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) against MMD-based baselines. PMD converges faster and often performs better; it yields less noisy samples than MMD, though pixel-space L1 leads to blurriness.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and appealing idea: train deep models by directly minimizing the primal empirical Wasserstein via matching, avoiding an adversary/critic.
- Useful practical training heuristic (freeze-and-update) that decouples expensive matching from SGD steps and allows smaller mini-batches.
- Clear (if standard) theoretical connection: PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein and is strongly consistent.
- Empirical results show better convergence and often better performance than MMD, and the gradient-strength argument is insightful and plausibly explains faster fitting.
- The paper targets two important applications (domain adaptation and generative modeling) and discusses computational aspects, including approximate matching and potential GPU acceleration.

Weaknesses
- Related work gaps: missing major prior art on differentiable, scalable approximations to Wasserstein/OT, especially entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn; Cuturi 2013) and subsequent GPU-friendly formulations that are widely used in deep learning. Also missing comparisons to WGAN baselines for generation and OT-based/sliced-Wasserstein/domain-adaptation methods.
- Novelty is somewhat limited: PMD is the empirical Wasserstein distance; the main new pieces are the specific training heuristic and empirical observations. The theoretical result is standard.
- The gradient treatment is informal: “matching does not change for infinitesimal θ changes” is not a proof. The objective is piecewise smooth with non-differentiabilities at matching changes; ignoring the dependence of M* on θ can be justified via envelope theorem/subgradient arguments under uniqueness, but this should be stated precisely. The “unbiased stochastic gradient” claim also needs care once subsampling and frozen matchings are used.
- Experimental scope is narrow: comparisons only to MMD. For DA, no comparisons to DANN/JAN/CMD or OT-based DA, and the validation with 100 labeled target samples weakens the unsupervised DA claim. For generation, no quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception/FID), no comparison to WGAN/LSGAN or Sinkhorn-based losses. Different epoch counts across methods (100 vs 500) further complicate fairness.
- Scalability concerns: matching needs O(N^2) memory/time; even with O(N^2) approximation, building the full distance matrix is costly. Memory/runtime constraints and batch-size limits in high dimensions should be discussed more concretely. Also, Wasserstein sample complexity degrades severely with dimension; the paper only gives a toy N^-0.23 empirical rate and does not discuss the implications.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; the core estimator is correct and consistency follows from classic OT measure convergence. However, the optimization claims (unbiasedness, differentiability) need more rigorous justification, and the experimental evaluation lacks stronger baselines and quantitative metrics. The implementation details for approximate matching and its effect on bias/variance could be fleshed out.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. Figures are informative. Some statements are too casual (e.g., on gradients/unbiasedness). The paper would benefit from a dedicated related-work section contrasting PMD with Sinkhorn OT, stochastic OT, sliced Wasserstein, WGANs, MMD-GAN, and domain-adaptation methods beyond MMD.

Originality
Moderate. Framing empirical OT (minimum matching) as a training loss is not new per se; the main originality is the mini-batch/matching-freeze training recipe and the empirical case for stronger gradients vs MMD. The lack of comparison to established OT approximations diminishes the novelty.

Significance
Potentially useful as a simple, critic-free alternative for distribution matching with better optimization behavior than MMD. Could spur more work on matching-based objectives with efficient GPU solvers. However, without comparisons to Sinkhorn/WGAN and more thorough evaluations, it’s hard to assess impact on state of the art.

Questions/suggestions for the authors
- Please compare to Sinkhorn (entropic OT) both in speed and performance; it’s differentiable, GPU-friendly, and widely used. Discuss ε’s role vs your “parameter-free” claim.
- Provide a formal justification for not backpropagating through the argmin (envelope theorem/Clarke subgradient) and clarify when the gradient is unbiased vs biased due to freezing/subsampling.
- Add quantitative metrics for generation (FID/IS) and baseline comparisons to WGAN or Sinkhorn-based generators.
- For domain adaptation, include DANN/JAN/CMD and OT-based DA baselines; clarify the protocol using target labels for validation.
- Discuss computational/memory scaling with N and D, and any tricks (e.g., blockwise matching, approximate nearest-neighbor pre-pruning).
- Consider feature-space distances (e.g., learned perceptual metrics) to mitigate pixel-space L1 blurriness, and report results.

Pros
- Simple, bold idea; avoids adversarial training and kernel tuning
- Empirically strong optimization behavior; faster convergence than MMD
- Practical training heuristic that reduces matching frequency and mini-batch size
- Clear link to Wasserstein with consistency

Cons
- Missing key related work and comparisons (Sinkhorn, WGAN, DA baselines)
- Limited novelty beyond a known estimator plus a training heuristic
- Informal gradient/unbiasedness arguments
- Scalability and high-dimensional sample complexity not fully addressed
- Evaluation lacks standard quantitative metrics for generation

Recommendation
Score: 6 (weak accept)
Confidence: 4/5

Rationale: While parts are incremental and some comparisons are missing, the paper proposes a simple, potentially impactful direction—directly minimizing empirical Wasserstein via matching with a practical training scheme—and shows promising empirical behavior. Given my preference for novel ideas, I’m inclined to accept if the authors can strengthen related work, add key baselines (especially Sinkhorn/WGAN), and clarify the optimization theory.
