### **Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"**

#### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples, each drawn from one of the distributions. The authors establish a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric (W1), showing that N-PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 as the sample size N approaches infinity.

The core of the contribution is a method for using PMD as a differentiable objective function in deep learning. The parameters of generative models or feature extractors are optimized via stochastic gradient descent (SGD), where the gradient is computed on the cost of a fixed matching. To manage computational complexity, the paper proposes a training scheme where a matching is computed on a moderately sized "population" of samples, and then multiple SGD updates are performed on smaller "mini-batches" drawn from this matched population.

The paper positions PMD as an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). It argues that PMD has several advantages: it is parameter-free (lacking the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter of MMD), provides stronger and more uniform gradients during training, and allows for the use of smaller mini-batches for parameter updates, leading to faster convergence. These claims are evaluated empirically on two deep learning applications: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling. The experimental results generally show PMD outperforming or performing competitively against MMD.

#### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Conceptual Simplicity:** The paper presents a novel and elegant approach to estimating a statistical divergence. Framing the problem as a minimum weight matching is intuitive and provides a direct, parameter-free estimator for the primal form of the first Wasserstein metric. This contrasts with MMD, which relies on kernel methods, and with Wasserstein GANs, which approximate the dual form using an auxiliary network.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the PMD is defined precisely, and its connection to the Wasserstein metric is rigorously established by citing foundational results in optimal transport theory. The proposed learning algorithm is presented with a clear pseudocode and illustration.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical claims are well-supported. The assertion that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 is a direct consequence of established theorems in probability theory and optimal transport. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and directly supports the paper's central claims. The experiments include:
    *   A direct comparison against a strong baseline (MMD) in two relevant applications.
    *   Ablation studies on the effect of population size (N), mini-batch size (|B|), and the choice of exact vs. approximate matching algorithms.
    *   Empirical verification of theoretical claims, such as the faster decay of approximation error and the distribution of gradient magnitudes (Fig. 2).

4.  **Significance and Practical Utility:** PMD offers a valuable and practical alternative for training deep models that require comparing distributions. The absence of a kernel bandwidth parameter is a significant practical advantage over MMD, removing a sensitive hyperparameter that often requires careful tuning. The demonstrated ability to train effectively with smaller update batches can lead to faster convergence, which is a key consideration in large-scale deep learning. These properties make the proposed method likely to be adopted and built upon by the community.

#### **Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The primary weakness of PMD is its computational cost. The minimum weight matching problem requires O(N^3) time for an exact solution (Hungarian algorithm) or O(N^2) for the suggested approximation. While the paper argues this is acceptable for moderate N (e.g., thousands), this is computationally more demanding than the standard O(N^2) evaluation of MMD, especially considering the higher constant factors involved in matching algorithms compared to kernel matrix computations. The proposed training scheme amortizes this cost over several SGD updates, but the matching step remains a potential bottleneck, limiting scalability to larger population sizes.

2.  **Omission of Relevant Prior Work:** The paper’s discussion of tractable Wasserstein distance estimators could be more complete. A significant body of work on making optimal transport computationally efficient via entropy regularization (e.g., Sinkhorn distances, as introduced to machine learning by Cuturi, NIPS 2013) is not discussed or cited. The Sinkhorn-Knopp algorithm provides a highly parallelizable, GPU-friendly method for approximating the Wasserstein distance with nearly O(N^2) complexity. A comparison or discussion of PMD in relation to this popular alternative would have significantly strengthened the paper's positioning.

3.  **Quality of Generative Modeling Results:** While the paper reports that PMD outperforms MMD in generative modeling, the qualitative results (generated images in Fig. 4) are quite blurry and do not represent the state-of-the-art for that time period. The authors correctly attribute this to the use of a pixel-wise L1 distance, which is known to favor blurry averages. However, this weakens the empirical evidence for PMD's effectiveness as a general-purpose objective for high-quality generative modeling, as the choice of ground distance `d(·,·)` appears critical.

#### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   The distinction between "population size N" (for matching) and "mini-batch size |B|" (for SGD updates) is a key part of the proposed algorithm. While explained, this terminology can be slightly confusing, as N is what many other papers would refer to as the batch size. Clarifying this distinction earlier and more prominently could improve readability.
*   The empirical results in Fig. 5(a) showing that smaller |B| leads to better final PMD values are compelling. This supports the claim that frequent updates based on a stable matching are beneficial. This is an interesting finding that contrasts with the typical requirement for large batches in other sample-based divergence estimators.
*   It would be beneficial to include a discussion on the choice of the ground metric `d(·, ·)`. The paper uses L1 distance throughout the experiments. How does the choice of metric (e.g., L2) affect performance? As noted with the blurry images, this choice is crucial, and some guidance for practitioners would be valuable.

In summary, this is a strong paper introducing a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically sound method. Its strengths in originality, clarity, and practical utility are significant. The main concerns relate to computational scalability and a minor gap in the discussion of related work on efficient optimal transport. However, the contributions are solid and represent a valuable addition to the literature on statistical divergences in deep learning.
