## Peer Review: Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric. The core idea is to minimize the cost of matching samples from two distributions, offering an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors demonstrate strong consistency of PMD, highlight advantages like the absence of bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and the potential for smaller mini-batch sizes, and validate these claims through experiments in domain adaptation and generative modeling. The paper argues that PMD offers a compelling alternative to MMD, particularly in scenarios where gradient stability and computational efficiency are crucial.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on a rich literature surrounding statistical divergence estimation. MMD [Gretton et al., 2012] is a foundational work in this area. The authors correctly position PMD within this context, explicitly drawing comparisons to MMD’s strengths and weaknesses.  The work also connects to the growing interest in the Wasserstein metric (Earth Mover's Distance) in machine learning [Rubner et al., 2000; Villani, 2008], particularly spurred by WGANs [Arjovsky et al., 2017].  Unlike WGANs, PMD doesn't rely on a critic network, which is a significant distinction. The theoretical analysis connects PMD to optimal transport theory [Villani, 2008] and leverages results regarding the consistency of empirical distributions [Varadarajan, 1998].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  While the Wasserstein metric is not new, directly framing it as a population matching problem and proposing PMD as a practical estimator is a novel contribution.  The focus on the gradient properties of PMD compared to MMD is a valuable insight.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating the strong consistency of PMD as an estimator for the Wasserstein metric.  This adds substantial credibility to the proposed method.
* **Practical Advantages:**  The identified advantages – no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and potentially smaller mini-batch sizes – are compelling. These factors could significantly improve training stability and speed, particularly in deep learning settings.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling provide empirical evidence supporting the claims.  The results demonstrate that PMD can outperform MMD in these tasks, and the convergence speed analysis is insightful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the explanations are relatively easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:**  While the experimental results are positive, they are limited to two specific tasks. Expanding the empirical evaluation to more diverse applications would strengthen the paper.
* **L1 Distance Choice:** The exclusive use of the L1 distance for PMD feels somewhat arbitrary.  While justified as leading to strong gradients, exploring the impact of other distances (e.g., Euclidean) might reveal further insights.  The paper doesn’t fully explore the trade-offs.
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the authors mention the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, the practical implications aren’t fully explored. The discussion of GPU parallelization feels somewhat cursory. More detailed performance benchmarks with varying N would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to WGAN:**  The paper briefly mentions WGAN, but a more detailed comparison, highlighting the specific advantages and disadvantages of PMD versus WGAN, would be valuable.  Specifically, addressing the scalability of PMD to higher-dimensional data compared to WGAN's critic training.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** The phrase “strongly consistent estimator” is used without a very clear layman’s explanation of what that means in the context of the paper. Some readers might benefit from a more intuitive description.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for estimating the Wasserstein metric.
* The empirical results demonstrate the practical benefits of PMD over MMD in specific deep learning tasks.
* The identified advantages of PMD (gradient stability, no bandwidth tuning) are significant and address known limitations of MMD.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is limited in scope.
* The choice of the L1 distance is not thoroughly justified.
* The discussion of computational complexity could be more detailed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a novel contribution. The theoretical analysis is strong, and the empirical results are encouraging.  While the scope of experiments could be broadened and some aspects of the discussion deepened, the paper addresses a relevant problem and offers a promising solution. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance at NIPS.
