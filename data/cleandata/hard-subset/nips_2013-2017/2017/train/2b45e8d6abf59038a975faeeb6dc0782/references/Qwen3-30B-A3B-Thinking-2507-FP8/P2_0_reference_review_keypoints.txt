- Introduces PMD, a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein metric that avoids MMD's bandwidth hyperparameter.  
- Provides a theoretical consistency result for PMD as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3).  
- Connects PMD to optimal transport via a minimum weight matching formulation.  
- Empirical evaluation on domain adaptation and generative modeling shows PMD outperforming MMD on several benchmarks.  
- Claims PMD yields stronger gradients and allows smaller mini-batches; evidence is empirical but lacks rigorous theoretical justification.  
- Does not provide finite-sample error bounds for PMD (missing detailed error bounds for practical N).  
- Fails to adequately address computational complexity of the matching step (Hungarian O(N^3)) for large-scale deep learning.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; mathematical definitions and figures are effective.  
- Needs clearer distinction between population size N and mini-batch size |B| to avoid conflation.  
- Should explain in more detail why gradients do not propagate through the matching (Eq. 3).  
- Needs a clearer comparison and discussion of the relationship to Earth Mover’s Distance and prior matching-based work.  
- Originality: PMD formulation and its use as a training objective is novel, despite conceptual overlap with EMD-based methods.  
- Significance: Potentially important for practitioners by removing bandwidth tuning and improving training dynamics, but practical impact limited by scaling concerns.  
- Strengths: solid theoretical foundation, clear empirical advantages over MMD, practical implementation details, and good exposition.  
- Weaknesses: limited theoretical analysis of gradient strength, insufficient computational scaling discussion, some overlap with EMD literature, and limited exploration of matching algorithm trade-offs.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to strengthen computational analysis and theoretical justification for the gradient claims.
