Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-cost bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets, as a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance. It shows PMD equals the empirical Wasserstein distance with uniform masses and proves strong consistency via standard results (Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport). The authors then use PMD as a differentiable training objective by backpropagating through the sample costs while treating the assignment as fixed (piecewise constant in parameters). They argue PMD has practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger/less-saturated gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batches than the “population” used to form the matching. Experiments compare PMD to MMD on domain adaptation (Office) and image generation (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), with qualitative improvements and faster convergence, plus some timing analysis and ablations (exact vs approximate matching).

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity: PMD is the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance; the formulation and its use as a loss are straightforward.
- Clear computational recipe leveraging approximate matching; practical trick of reusing assignments for multiple SGD steps and sub-sampling within a matched population.
- Empirical observation that PMD yields stronger per-sample gradients than RBF-MMD and tolerates smaller mini-batches, translating to faster convergence in their settings.
- Domain adaptation results on Office show improvements on several transfer tasks; convergence plots and sensitivity analyses are helpful.
- Implementation and timing details (exact vs approximate matching, GPU distance kernel) increase practical relevance.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: PMD is identical to the empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD between equal-sized samples. Using OT/EMD as a training objective is not new, and closely related, influential work is not cited or compared against:
  - Entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances for fast, differentiable OT (Cuturi, NIPS 2013).
  - OT for domain adaptation (Courty, Flamary, Tuia, Rakotomamonjy; e.g., “Optimal Transport for Domain Adaptation,” NIPS 2014; JDOT variants).
  - Stochastic/Sinkhorn-based OT for large-scale deep learning (e.g., Genevay et al., 2016–2017), and recent GAN alternatives (Cramér/MED).
  - WGAN comparisons are discussed in text but no empirical comparison is provided.
- Theory is standard: the “strong consistency” result follows directly from well-known OT theory for empirical measures; no finite-sample rates, no analysis of the bias introduced by approximate matching or by ignoring gradients through the assignment, and no convergence guarantees for the proposed SGD procedure with assignment reuse.
- Experimental evaluation is limited:
  - Generative modeling lacks quantitative metrics (Inception Score, FID), baselines (WGAN, Sinkhorn), and stronger datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10). The qualitative results are blurrier in pixel space; the paper notes L1’s inadequacy but doesn’t explore feature-space OT beyond an autoencoder.
  - Domain adaptation experiments omit comparisons to OT-based DA methods and adversarial DA baselines beyond MMD-type regularization, and tuning uses a small labeled target set.
- Computational trade-offs are incompletely characterized: although approximate matching appears affordable, the asymptotic cost and GPU-parallel OT alternatives (Sinkhorn) should be thoroughly discussed; the reliance on piecewise-constant assignments can induce non-smooth objectives and potential instability.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level but largely rederives known facts. The optimization procedure (no gradient through matching, sub-sampling within a fixed assignment) is heuristic; while plausible, it deserves more careful justification and empirical stress-testing. Experimental design demonstrates some benefits but lacks key baselines and metrics to substantiate claims against modern alternatives.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The connection to OT is explained; pseudocode and figures help. However, the positioning vis-à-vis the OT literature is incomplete; related work should be expanded. Provide more details for reproducibility (architectures/hyperparameters, exact approximate matching variant and parameters) and report quantitative metrics for generative tasks.

Originality
Low to moderate. The core idea is established OT/EMD on empirical measures. The claimed contributions are mainly engineering/empirical: applying empirical OT matching as a deep learning loss with assignment reuse and showing convergence/memory benefits against MMD. Relative to existing OT-in-deep-learning work (Sinkhorn, OT-DA), novelty is not clearly articulated.

Significance
Moderate potential practical interest: the observation that a hard-matching Wasserstein estimator can train with small mini-batches and stronger gradients versus RBF-MMD could influence practitioners who currently rely on MMD. However, without comparisons to Sinkhorn/WGAN and stronger evaluations, the broader impact is limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, parameter-free alternative to MMD with observed optimization benefits.
- Practical recipe that may be useful in some settings (DA, small-batch training).
- Empirical improvements over MMD on Office and qualitative gains in some generative setups.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; effectively rebranding empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD.
- Missing key related work and baselines (Sinkhorn OT, OT-DA, WGAN), weakening significance claims.
- Theoretical results are standard; optimization heuristics lack guarantees.
- Generative results lack quantitative evaluation and stronger datasets.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the work within the OT literature: cite and compare to Sinkhorn (Cuturi, 2013), OT-DA (Courty et al., 2014+), and stochastic OT methods. Include empirical comparisons to Sinkhorn divergences and WGAN/Cramér GAN on the same tasks.
- Provide quantitative metrics (FID/Inception) and stronger datasets (CIFAR-10) for generation; explore feature-space costs (e.g., pretrained perceptual features) and sliced/Proj-Wasserstein variants.
- Analyze the bias/variance trade-off of approximate matching and assignment reuse; provide ablations on how often assignments change and the impact on convergence.
- Consider differentiable OT relaxations to allow gradient flow through the transport plan and amortized training on GPU.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is clear and potentially useful, but the conceptual novelty is limited and the experimental/theoretical support is not yet at the level needed for NIPS, especially given omitted related work and baselines. Strengthening comparisons and positioning could make this a solid contribution.
