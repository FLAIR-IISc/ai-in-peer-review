Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-cost bipartite matching between two i.i.d. samples from distributions pX and pY with respect to a ground metric d. This is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (a.k.a. Earth Mover’s Distance) between uniform empirical measures with equal sample sizes. The authors use PMD as a training objective for deep models by (i) drawing two populations, (ii) solving a min-cost matching (Hungarian O(N^3) or an O(N^2) approximation), and (iii) updating parameters by backpropagating only through the distances of matched pairs (no gradient through the matching). They also propose a heuristic inner-loop where the matching computed on a larger “population” is reused for several small mini-batch updates. The paper proves that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance using standard results (Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport). Empirically, PMD is compared to MMD on domain adaptation (Office dataset with VGG features) and on deep generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), with claims of better stability, stronger gradients, fewer hyperparameters, and faster convergence at comparable or better accuracy.

The work connects to several lines: integral probability metrics (MMD), Wasserstein distance and its empirical estimator (Rubner et al. for EMD; foundational OT results in Villani), GANs with Wasserstein-like objectives (WGAN, Cramer distance), and OT-based domain adaptation. Notably, entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn distances) provides a scalable, differentiable approximation widely used in ML that is not discussed or compared.

Strengths:
- Clear articulation of a simple, parameter-free, sample-based divergence with an intuitive training procedure.
- Theoretical connection to 1-Wasserstein is correct and the consistency statement is standard but useful for framing.
- Practical observations on gradients: matched-pair L1 gradients remain strong vs. RBF-MMD’s vanishing gradients for outliers; supported by an empirical gradient magnitude analysis.
- The mini-batch inner-loop heuristic is practical and shows some speed gains; sensitivity plots are informative.
- Experiments indicate PMD can outperform or match MMD on some Office transfer tasks and can produce less noisy samples than MMD on small-scale generative benchmarks.

Weaknesses:
Quality:
- Novelty is limited: PMD is the empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD with uniform masses; both the estimator and its consistency are well known. Renaming it as a new discrepancy obscures this fact.
- Theoretical section mostly restates known results; missing key references and tools (e.g., Danskin’s theorem for differentiating argmin; finite-sample convergence rates for Wasserstein).
- Computational story is incomplete. Exact matching is O(N^3) and approximate O(N^2); while acceptable for N~10^3 in small settings, scaling remains a bottleneck. The suggested GPU acceleration is not implemented. No comparisons to strongly relevant, scalable OT variants like entropic Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi) which are differentiable and GPU-friendly.
- Experimental comparisons are too narrow. For domain adaptation, there are no baselines against DANN/gradient reversal, Deep CORAL, CMD, JAN, or OT-based methods (e.g., Courty et al.). For generative modeling, there is no comparison to WGAN (GP), improved MMD-GAN, or modern likelihood-based models; reported samples are blurry and evaluation is qualitative (no FID/IS).
- Hyperparameter selection for domain adaptation uses 100 labeled target examples for validation, which weakens the “unsupervised” claim and complicates fairness.

Clarity:
- The paper introduces PMD as if novel, while it is the standard empirical 1-Wasserstein. The relationship section should more explicitly acknowledge identity with EMD and cite prominent scalable OT literature (e.g., Sinkhorn).
- The differentiation-through-matching assumption (“matching does not change for infinitesimal changes”) should be formalized via subgradient/Danskin analysis; discuss nondifferentiability when matchings change.
- Some claims (e.g., “parameter-free” advantage vs. WGAN) ignore that regularized OT has a tunable ε and that WGAN-GP constrains the Lipschitz constant to fix scaling.

Originality:
- Limited. The main idea—using empirical OT (min-cost matching) as a deep training objective—is natural and has been explored in various forms; the paper does not introduce a new discrepancy nor a fundamentally new algorithmic technique.

Significance:
- Potentially useful insight that matched-pair objectives yield stronger gradients than kernel MMD in practice, and that inner-loop small minibatches can speed training. However, without comparisons to Sinkhorn/WGAN and stronger baselines, the impact is uncertain. The datasets are small; no clear state-of-the-art improvements are demonstrated.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, intuitive objective with reasonable empirical behavior.
- Highlights practical gradient issues with MMD and offers a viable alternative in some regimes.
- Clear exposition and useful sensitivity analyses.

Cons for acceptance:
- Low novelty; essentially rebranding empirical Wasserstein/EMD.
- Missing key related work and comparisons (Sinkhorn OT, WGAN/GP, OT domain adaptation).
- Limited scalability demonstration; GPU acceleration not implemented.
- Weak experimental scope and evaluation metrics; reliance on target labels for validation.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Acknowledge explicitly that PMD equals empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD; reposition contributions as engineering/practical insights for using empirical OT in deep learning.
- Add and compare to strong, relevant baselines: Sinkhorn-regularized OT (tuning ε, GPU implementation), WGAN-GP, MMD-GAN, Cramer distance. Provide runtime-accuracy trade-offs.
- Strengthen theory: cite Danskin’s theorem for gradients; discuss subgradients at matching changes; add known finite-sample rates (e.g., Fournier–Guillin; Weed–Bach) and discuss dimensional dependence.
- Improve domain adaptation protocol: avoid using labeled target data for tuning; compare against DANN, JAN, CMD, Deep CORAL, and OT-based DA; consider larger benchmarks (Office-Home, VisDA).
- For generative modeling, include quantitative metrics (FID/IS) and higher-resolution datasets; consider feature-space distances (e.g., perceptual or learned) or regularized OT to reduce blurriness.
- Implement and report GPU-parallel matching or Sinkhorn to address scalability.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is sound and practically relevant, but the novelty is limited and the experimental/theoretical scope is insufficient for NeurIPS. With stronger positioning, broader comparisons (especially Sinkhorn/WGAN), and improved evaluations, a future version could be impactful.
