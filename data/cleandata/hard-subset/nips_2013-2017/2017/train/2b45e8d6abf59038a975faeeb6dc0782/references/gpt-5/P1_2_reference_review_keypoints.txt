- PMD is just the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (minimum-weight bipartite matching) between two sample sets, and the paper proposes using it as a training objective for deep models.  
- The consistency result is standard: PMD converges to W1 by empirical measure convergence and OT stability.  
- Main technical novelty is pragmatic: reuse matchings across SGD steps and subsample matched pairs to reduce computation.  
- Authors use exact Hungarian or an O(N^2) approximate matcher and claim faster convergence and stronger gradients than MMD.  
- Paper omits key prior work: entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi 2013) and stochastic large-scale OT (Genevay et al.), and OT-based domain adaptation (Courty et al.).  
- No comparisons to Sinkhorn OT, OT-based DA methods, or adversarial WGAN/WGAN-GP for generative modeling — a critical omission.  
- Novelty is limited: PMD = empirical W1 is well-known in OT; the reuse/subsampling heuristics are incremental and lack theoretical analysis.  
- Claims of “unbiased stochastic gradient” (Eq.3) and no-bias for stale match reuse (Eq.4) are unsupported; reuse/subsampling likely introduces bias.  
- Computational practicality is questionable: exact matching is O(N^3), approximate O(N^2); reported timings use single-thread CPU matching with no fair wall-clock comparison to GPU-friendly alternatives.  
- Experimental baselines are insufficient: domain adaptation compares only to MMD, missing DANN/CORAL/JAN/CMD/DSN and OT-DA baselines.  
- DA protocol unclear/possibly invalid: hyperparameters tuned using 100 labeled target samples (violates unsupervised DA), and no results reported for strictly unsupervised selection.  
- Experimental reporting lacks rigor: no error bars/confidence intervals despite multiple runs, and mixed improvements (2/6 shifts).  
- Generative modeling experiments are weak: only qualitative images (blurred SVHN/LFW), no standard quantitative metrics (FID/Inception), and pixel L1 is suboptimal.  
- Paper overstates MMD limitations: MMD has unbiased/linear-time estimators and can be trained with minibatches; mini-batch advantages claimed for PMD are overstated.  
- Theoretical contribution is minimal: consistency is a direct corollary of standard results and no finite-sample rates or bounds are provided.  
- Clarity is generally good, but some claims need more careful wording (e.g., “unbiased gradient”, “parameter-free”).  
- Suggestions: add Sinkhorn and OT-DA comparisons, include WGAN baselines, report FID/ Inception scores, provide wall-clock/matched-compute comparisons, ablate matching refresh/subsampling bias, clarify DA validation protocol, add error bars, and discuss ground-cost choices.  
- Pros: clear exposition, practical algorithmic notes, empirical evidence of stronger gradients vs MMD, some favorable empirical results and faster convergence.  
- Cons: limited novelty, missing key related work and baselines, insufficient experimental rigor, and unproven optimization/differentiability claims.  
- Overall: interesting empirical insight that empirical W1 can outperform MMD in some settings, but paper needs stronger positioning within OT literature and more comprehensive, fair experiments to be persuasive.
