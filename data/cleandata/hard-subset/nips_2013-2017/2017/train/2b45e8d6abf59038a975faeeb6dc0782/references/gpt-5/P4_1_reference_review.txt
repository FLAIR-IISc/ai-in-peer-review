Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-cost bipartite matching between two equally sized i.i.d. samples from distributions pX and pY with respect to a ground metric d. PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between the two empirical measures with uniform weights. The paper proves strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of the population W1 by invoking Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport. It then proposes a learning algorithm that uses PMD as a differentiable objective by reparameterizing samples through neural networks and applying SGD. The implementation uses exact Hungarian matching (O(N^3)) or an O(N^2) approximate assignment algorithm; in practice the authors keep the assignment fixed for several inner SGD steps and optionally use a minibatch |B| significantly smaller than the “population” size N to compute gradients. Empirically, PMD is compared against MMD on domain adaptation (Office dataset, VGG features) and on implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW). The authors report that PMD achieves competitive or better accuracy on some adaptation tasks and yields faster convergence and visually cleaner samples than MMD, especially with smaller minibatches.

Relation to prior work: PMD coincides with the classical EMD/Wasserstein-1 distance on empirical measures (Rubner et al. 2000; Villani 2009). In deep learning, WGAN (Arjovsky et al. 2017) estimates W1 via the dual with a 1-Lipschitz critic; this paper instead uses the primal discrete OT with exact/approximate assignment and no critic. The paper discusses MMD-based training (GMMN) and notes gradient attenuation issues. Major related OT computation work not cited includes entropic regularization and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) and stochastic optimization for large-scale OT (Genevay et al., NIPS 2016), both highly relevant as tractable, differentiable alternatives to exact matching. Rates of convergence for empirical Wasserstein distances (e.g., Fournier & Guillin 2015; Weed & Bach, NIPS 2017) are also pertinent for the theoretical section and empirical scaling analysis.

Evaluation

Quality
- Theory: The consistency result follows standard OT stability arguments and is correct. However, the claim that the gradient “does not propagate through the matching” would benefit from a formal justification via Danskin’s theorem/Clarke subdifferential for min over discrete assignments; uniqueness and measure-zero non-differentiability should be discussed. The “unbiased stochastic gradient” statement is too strong without conditions; the proposed inner-loop strategy that keeps assignments fixed introduces bias relative to the true objective unless analyzed as a valid stochastic subgradient scheme.
- Algorithms: The matching-based loss is well-defined, but scalability is limited by O(N^2)–O(N^3) costs and O(N^2) memory for distance matrices. The paper sensibly uses approximate matching, smaller |B|, and shows timing splits; however, it omits strong baselines that address scalability, notably entropic Sinkhorn OT and the auction algorithm, which are GPU-friendly and commonly used.
- Experiments: Domain adaptation compares only PMD vs MMD within the same architecture, which isolates the discrepancy but not against stronger baselines (e.g., DANN/GRL, JAN, CMD, and OT-based DA methods such as Courty et al., NIPS 2016). Generative modeling results are qualitative; established metrics (e.g., Inception/FID for SVHN/LFW, Parzen/2-sample tests for MNIST) are missing, making it difficult to assess significance. The gradient magnitude analysis is useful, although reported only as histograms.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with precise definitions and an accessible algorithmic description. Notational choices around “population size N” vs “minibatch size |B|” could be clarified earlier to avoid confusion with standard minibatching. Theoretical claims should cite Danskin and standard empirical OT rates, and the relation to empirical OT/EMD should be stated upfront rather than framed as a new discrepancy. Important related work (Cuturi 2013; Genevay 2016) is missing, and some references are incomplete.

Originality
Conceptually, PMD is not new—it is the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance. The novelty lies in advocating and engineering its direct use as a training objective with approximate matching and small minibatches, and in contrasting its gradient properties with MMD. Given prior widespread use of OT/EMD and the emergence of WGAN, the incremental novelty is moderate unless accompanied by stronger theoretical or empirical advances (e.g., convergence guarantees with fixed-assignments inner loops, or state-of-the-art results).

Significance
A practical, parameter-free, sample-based discrepancy with stronger gradients than MMD could be valuable. Demonstrating that minibatches smaller than N still work is a useful observation. However, the omission of comparisons to Sinkhorn OT, WGAN, and stronger domain adaptation baselines limits the impact. Without quantitative generative metrics and broader baselines, it is hard to conclude that PMD offers a clear advantage beyond MMD in modern settings.

Pros
- Simple objective with clear interpretation; no kernel bandwidth tuning.
- Correct consistency connection to W1; empirical error scaling aligns with known rates.
- Gradient analysis highlights a real issue with RBF-MMD saturation.
- Empirical evidence that smaller |B| can accelerate training while retaining quality.
- Practical approximate matching and timing breakdowns.

Cons
- Rebrands empirical W1/EMD as a new discrepancy; missing key OT literature (Cuturi 2013; Genevay 2016; OT for domain adaptation).
- Limited baselines: no comparisons to Sinkhorn OT loss, WGAN/Cramér distance, or stronger DA methods.
- The gradient treatment through the argmin needs a formal justification; unbiasedness claim and bias from fixed assignments should be addressed.
- Scalability and memory use for large N not fully resolved; GPU implementations suggested but not provided.
- Generative results lack standard quantitative metrics; domain adaptation tuning uses target labels (as in prior work) but should be clearly stated as semi-target supervision.

Suggestions for improvement
- Cite and compare with entropic Sinkhorn OT (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) and stochastic OT (Genevay et al., NIPS 2016); consider Sinkhorn divergences as a differentiable, scalable alternative and include empirical comparisons.
- Provide theoretical justification via Danskin’s theorem for gradients; clarify subgradient issues when assignments change; analyze or empirically ablate the inner-loop fixed-assignment bias.
- Add quantitative generative metrics (e.g., Inception/FID, 2-sample tests) and compare with WGAN/Cramér distance and Sinkhorn losses on the same architectures.
- For domain adaptation, include comparisons to DANN/GRL, JAN, CMD, and OT-based DA methods; report statistical significance across runs.
- Discuss memory footprint and provide GPU implementations for distance computation and matching; consider blockwise or sliced OT variants to reduce O(N^2) memory.
- Reference empirical OT convergence rates (e.g., Fournier & Guillin 2015; Weed & Bach, NIPS 2017) and align the empirical rate discussion with theory.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper offers a clear and useful perspective but lacks essential related work, formal gradient justification, and stronger empirical baselines/metrics. With these issues addressed, the contribution could be solid.

Confidence
Medium.
