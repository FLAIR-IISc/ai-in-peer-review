This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, presenting it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling. The core idea is to minimize the cost of matching samples from two distributions, offering advantages like parameter-free operation, potentially stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batches. The authors provide theoretical justification for PMD’s consistency and demonstrate its effectiveness through empirical evaluation on standard benchmarks.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a relevant problem in deep learning where estimating distributional divergences is crucial. The introduction clearly outlines the limitations of MMD, setting the stage for the proposed PMD. The theoretical analysis, establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, is a significant contribution. The presentation of the method itself is reasonably clear, although the notation could be slightly more consistent (e.g., clarifying the use of X and Y to represent both samples and distributions). The experiments demonstrate the potential of PMD, showing improvements in both performance and convergence speed in certain scenarios.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PMD offers a fresh perspective on estimating distributional divergences compared to established methods like MMD and Wasserstein GANs. It provides a direct approximation of the Wasserstein metric without requiring a critic network or careful tuning of a Lipschitz constant.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical justification for PMD, demonstrating its consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein metric.
* **Potential Advantages:** The reported advantages of PMD – parameter-free operation, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes – are highly desirable in practical deep learning applications.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling provide initial evidence of PMD’s effectiveness, showing improvements over MMD in certain cases.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow of ideas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are well-conducted, they are limited to two tasks (domain adaptation and generative modeling) and a relatively small set of datasets.  More extensive evaluation on diverse tasks and datasets would strengthen the claims about PMD's generalizability.
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The use of the L1 distance in PMD is somewhat arbitrarily chosen.  While justified in the paper, it might not be optimal for all data types (as acknowledged in the discussion of generative modeling). The impact of different distance metrics should be investigated.
* **Computational Cost of Matching:**  Although the authors address the computational complexity of the matching problem, it remains a potential bottleneck, particularly for large datasets. While they utilize approximations, the overhead could be significant compared to MMD's closed-form solution. A more thorough analysis of the computational cost, including scaling behavior with dataset size, is needed.
* **Sensitivity to Matching Algorithm:** The results show that the choice of matching algorithm (exact vs. approximated) can impact performance. A discussion on criteria for selecting the appropriate algorithm, and a more detailed comparison of their trade-offs, would be helpful.
* **Lack of Comparison to Wasserstein GAN:** The paper does not compare PMD to Wasserstein GAN (WGAN), a prominent method for approximating the Wasserstein metric. A comparison would be crucial to position PMD within the existing landscape of Wasserstein distance estimation techniques.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically grounded method for estimating distributional divergences.
* The proposed PMD offers potential advantages over existing methods like MMD.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD on relevant tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The computational cost of the matching problem remains a concern.
* The lack of comparison to WGAN hinders a comprehensive evaluation of PMD's position in the field.
* The choice of the L1 distance may not be universally optimal.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising new approach to estimating distributional divergences with potential benefits over existing methods. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and the computational cost needs further consideration, the theoretical soundness and initial empirical results warrant serious consideration. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to expand the experimental evaluation, provide a comparison to WGAN, and further investigate the computational aspects of the proposed method. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field. The authors should also explore the sensitivity of PMD to the choice of distance metric.
