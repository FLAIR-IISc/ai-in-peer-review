Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance that computes a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets and uses the average pairwise cost as a discrepancy. The authors (i) formalize PMD as the empirical OT problem with uniform masses, (ii) show “strong consistency” by invoking Varadarajan’s theorem (empirical measures converge a.s.) and stability of optimal transport (Villani), and (iii) propose a practical training scheme that alternates recomputing the matching on populations of size N and taking several SGD steps without differentiating through the matching (arguing the permutation is locally constant). They empirically compare PMD to MMD on domain adaptation (Office dataset with VGG features) and on implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), claim advantages (no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, ability to use smaller minibatches than population size), and provide ablations on matching algorithms and batch/population sizes.

Quality
- Theory: The connection of PMD to the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance is correct, and the consistency result follows directly from standard OT results (Varadarajan + OT stability). However, there is no new theoretical contribution beyond restating known results; no finite-sample bounds or rates (which are known to be dimension-dependent for W1) are provided. The empirical N−0.23 rate in 5D is consistent with N−1/d, but this is anecdotal.
- Differentiation and optimization: The paper relies on the fact that the optimal matching is locally constant and does not backprop through the permutation. This is essentially an application of Danskin’s theorem/envelope theorem for a minimization over a finite set; a formal statement and conditions (e.g., uniqueness, measure-zero tie events) would strengthen the argument. The “unbiased stochastic gradient” statement is asserted but not proven; rigor here would be helpful.
- Algorithmic choices and scalability: Exact Hungarian is O(N^3); approximate O(N^2) is used, with CPU implementations. The authors note GPU acceleration is possible, but do not provide it. Memory/time costs for computing and storing pairwise distances scale as O(N^2D). While the ablation suggests acceptable overhead at N up to a few thousand, the method may be challenging to scale to larger problems without additional accelerations.
- Experiments: 
  - Domain adaptation: Regularizing a shallow classifier on VGG features with PMD yields improvements over MMD on some Office tasks and similar performance on others. However, baselines are limited to MMD; stronger contemporaneous baselines (e.g., DANN, CMD, and OT-based DA) are missing, which weakens claims of significance.
  - Generative modeling: Qualitative samples show PMD is less noisy than MMD in pixel space, but images are blurry; the autoencoder-code setting performs better. There are no standard quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score/FID for SVHN/LFW), so it’s hard to gauge how competitive PMD is against GAN/WGAN or other OT-based generative methods.
  - Claims on stronger gradients are supported by gradient magnitude histograms, which are suggestive but indirect; an analysis of optimization stability/convergence would be valuable.
  - The claim that MMD “must” use mini-batches equal to the population size overlooks linear-time unbiased MMD estimators; this should be acknowledged to fairly represent MMD’s minibatch behavior.

Clarity
The paper is generally clearly written and well organized. The connection to OT and the training algorithm are explained clearly. That said:
- The justification for stopping gradients through the matching would benefit from a formal citation (Danskin) and a short lemma/warning about ties and non-differentiability.
- More details on the approximate matching algorithm used, its approximation guarantees, and practical settings would help reproduction.
- Training details (frequency of recomputing matchings, number of inner-loop steps, learning rates, seeds) should be more explicit. Releasing code would help.

Originality
Moderate. Using the empirical OT/assignment matching as a training objective is natural and has been explored in related areas (EMD in vision; OT for domain adaptation). The novelty here is in packaging it as PMD, arguing practical training advantages vs MMD, and demonstrating its use in deep learning tasks. However, major related work is missing:
- Entropic-regularized OT (“Sinkhorn distances”, Cuturi 2013, NIPS) provides a fast, differentiable approximation to OT with GPU-friendly matrix operations, and has been used as a loss in deep models. This is a significant omission.
- OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al., 2014/2016) and later differentiable OT training works are not discussed.
- Comparisons to WGAN are qualitative; no empirical comparisons are provided.
These gaps make it harder to position PMD’s contributions relative to existing OT-in-deep-learning methods as of the time.

Significance
The idea is potentially useful: a simple, parameter-free empirical OT objective that yields strong, well-scaled gradients and can work with standard minibatches. If adopted broadly, PMD could be an attractive alternative to MMD in some settings. However, the experimental scope and baselines are limited, the computational cost is nontrivial, and the lack of quantitative generative metrics reduces impact. Given the strong OT literature (especially Sinkhorn) already offering differentiable, scalable approximations, the marginal significance is unclear without a direct comparison.

Pros
- Clear formulation connecting assignment matching to empirical W1; correct consistency argument.
- Practical training recipe with evidence of stronger per-sample gradients and tolerance to smaller minibatches than the matching population.
- Empirical gains over MMD on several tasks; simple and parameter-free objective.
- Useful ablations on matching algorithm choice, N vs |B|, and timing.

Cons
- Missing key related work on entropic OT/Sinkhorn and OT-based domain adaptation; no comparisons to these strong baselines.
- No new theoretical results beyond known consistency; no finite-sample guarantees or analysis of optimization dynamics.
- Limited experimental evaluation: few baselines in DA; no quantitative metrics for generation; blurry samples; no comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP.
- Scalability concerns (O(N^2) memory/time) not addressed with practical GPU implementations; claims about minibatch advantages over MMD partly overstated.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper contains a reasonable idea, is clearly executed, and shows promising evidence against MMD. However, the omission of core OT literature (especially Sinkhorn) and limited empirical comparisons reduce confidence in the contribution’s novelty and significance. Strengthening related work, adding direct comparisons to entropic OT-based losses and modern DA/GAN baselines, providing quantitative generative metrics, and clarifying the differentiation argument would substantially improve the paper.
