Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. samples from distributions pX and pY using a ground metric d (typically L1). PMD is exactly the 1-Wasserstein distance between the corresponding empirical measures. The authors (i) formalize PMD, (ii) show strong consistency to the true W1 via standard results (Varadarajan + Villani’s stability of OT), (iii) propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective by computing a matching once per population and taking gradients that do not back-propagate through the discrete matching, and (iv) argue empirically that PMD has “stronger gradients” and can use smaller minibatches than MMD. They present applications to domain adaptation (Office) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), with exact or approximate matching, and provide convergence/timing analyses and gradient magnitude histograms.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and well-grounded: PMD is the empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD; the consistency statement is correct and the empirical convergence rate matches known OT rates in moderate dimension.
- Practical training recipe: computing a batch matching and optimizing for several SGD steps is simple to implement; approximate O(N^2) matching makes the approach feasible at moderate N.
- Clear empirical message about gradients: histograms suggest fewer vanishing gradients than RBF-MMD in their setting; the toy error-rate study is informative.
- Applications: domain adaptation and generative modeling with systematic sensitivity/timing studies; the Office results are competitive with MMD and sometimes better; analysis of batch size vs population size is useful.

Weaknesses and missing related work
- Novelty/positioning: PMD as defined is the classical Earth Mover’s Distance/Wasserstein-1 between empirical measures; computing it by Hungarian or approximate matching is standard. Training with empirical OT has appeared in numerous works; seminal and highly relevant contributions (not cited) include Cuturi (2013) “Sinkhorn distances: Lightspeed computation of optimal transport” (NIPS), which provides a fast, differentiable OT approximation with GPU-friendly matrix operations, and subsequent stochastic/mini-batch OT methods (e.g., Genevay et al., 2016/2017). Also closely related are Sliced Wasserstein distances for generative modeling (Rabin et al., Bonneel et al., and follow-ups, some pre-2017). The paper should acknowledge these and position PMD relative to entropic/sliced OT.
- Differentiability and gradient correctness: The paper assumes the optimal matching M* is locally constant and ignores gradients through the combinatorial argmin. While true almost everywhere, this leads to non-smooth objectives; no theoretical justification is provided that the proposed SGD (especially with multiple inner updates per fixed matching) is unbiased or convergent. The subsampled mini-batch gradient in Eq. (4) is biased with respect to DN but this is not analyzed.
- Claims about MMD and minibatching are incomplete: It is not true that “mini-batch size for MMD must equal the population size.” Unbiased/low-variance linear-time MMD estimators exist (e.g., Gretton et al., 2012; Dziugaite et al., 2015; Li et al., 2017 MMD-GAN) that are explicitly designed for SGD with small minibatches. This weakens the comparative advantage argument.
- Experimental scope and baselines: In domain adaptation, comparison is only to MMD; stronger contemporary baselines exist (DANN, JAN, CMD, DSN), some cited but not compared. In generative modeling, evaluation is largely qualitative; no standard metrics (Inception score, FID, Parzen) are reported; datasets are small; pixel-space L1 produces blur and is acknowledged as suboptimal. Comparison to WGAN/critic-based methods and to entropic OT (Sinkhorn) is missing.
- Computational considerations: Although approximate matching helps, the cost of building pairwise distances and matching is still O(N^2) or worse, and scaling beyond a few thousand samples per population is unclear. The paper mentions possible GPU acceleration but does not demonstrate it; a comparison to Sinkhorn (which is GPU-friendly) would be illuminating.

Quality
- Theory: The consistency result follows directly from standard OT stability and empirical measure convergence; correct but incremental. No finite-sample bounds or generalization analysis are provided (stated as future work).
- Algorithms: The training heuristic (freezing matchings for multiple steps, subsampling pairs) is reasonable but lacks theoretical guarantees; claims of “unbiased” gradients are not fully justified in the presence of the non-differentiable matching and subsampling.
- Empirics: Generally careful in reporting architectural and optimization details; timing breakdowns are helpful. However, the experimental comparisons are limited and the generative modeling evaluation lacks quantitative metrics.

Clarity
- The paper is largely clear and well-organized, with helpful figures. Some notational clutter (XθX, YθY) could be streamlined. The claim about MMD minibatches should be corrected or nuanced. Please clarify the number of inner-loop updates per fixed matching and its effect on bias/convergence.

Originality
- Low to moderate. The main idea is to use empirical W1 (via matching) as a loss for deep models; both the distance and its empirical computation are well-known. The contribution is an engineering recipe and empirical study rather than a new discrepancy or algorithmic breakthrough.

Significance
- Moderate. The simplicity and practical observations (gradient behavior, ability to use smaller minibatches) may interest practitioners. However, the lack of stronger baselines, missing related work (entropic/sliced OT), and limited evaluation reduce impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add and discuss entropic OT (Cuturi 2013) and stochastic/mini-batch OT; compare experimentally and discuss trade-offs (speed, smoothness, bias).
- Compare against DANN/JAN/CMD on Office; report full results with error bars.
- For generative modeling, include quantitative metrics (FID/IS) and stronger baselines (WGAN-GP, MMD-GAN, Sinkhorn/Wasserstein autoencoders). Consider feature-space costs (e.g., pretrained conv features) systematically.
- Provide analysis of the bias introduced by freezing matchings and by subsampling matched pairs; or adopt differentiable OT surrogates to avoid this issue.
- Clarify claims about MMD minibatching and include linear-time MMD estimators in comparisons.
- Discuss scalability and, if possible, include GPU-accelerated matching or Sinkhorn results.

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated objective with clear connection to W1
- Clean consistency statement and useful empirical convergence/timing studies
- Practical and easy-to-implement training recipe; often competitive with MMD

Cons
- Limited novelty; reinvention of empirical OT without key related work (Sinkhorn)
- Incomplete theoretical justification for the training heuristic; biased gradients
- Narrow baselines and qualitative evaluation in generative modeling
- Some inaccurate statements about MMD minibatching

Recommendation: Weak reject (borderline). The idea is useful, but novelty and empirical rigor need strengthening; adding comparisons to entropic/stochastic OT and stronger baselines would significantly improve the paper.

Confidence: High.
