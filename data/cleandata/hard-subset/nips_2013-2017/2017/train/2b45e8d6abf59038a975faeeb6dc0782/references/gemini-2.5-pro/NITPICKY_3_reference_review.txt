### Summary

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric (W-1). PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from two distributions. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the W-1 metric. They propose an optimization procedure to use PMD as a training objective for deep learning models, where the optimal matching is computed and then held fixed for several stochastic gradient descent (SGD) updates to improve efficiency. PMD is presented as an alternative to the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), with claimed advantages including having no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, providing stronger gradients, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes during optimization. The method is evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, where it is shown to be competitive with or outperform MMD-based methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The core idea of using the primal form of the Wasserstein distance, approximated via minimum weight matching, as a loss for deep learning is interesting and well-motivated. The connection to optimal transport theory is clearly laid out, and the proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3), while a direct application of existing results, correctly grounds the method in theory.

However, the paper suffers from numerous technical and methodological weaknesses that undermine its overall quality.

1.  **Core Algorithmic Heuristic is Unjustified:** The proposed learning algorithm hinges on the approximation in Eq. (4), where the optimal matching M* is computed once and then used for multiple SGD updates. The justification provided is that "M* does not change much within a small number of gradient updates." This is a strong claim with no theoretical or empirical support. How many inner-loop updates are appropriate? How does the stability of M* depend on the learning rate or the current proximity of the two distributions? Without an ablation study on the number of inner-loop steps, this critical part of the algorithm remains an un-validated heuristic.
2.  **Underwhelming Gradient Analysis:** The argument for "stronger gradients" is a key selling point, but the analysis is superficial. The claim that the L2 norm of the MMD gradient term is small for large or small distances contains a potential typo (`||x-y||^2` vs `||x-y||` in the numerator). More importantly, the empirical validation in Fig. 2(b) is performed on a *converged* model, which does not adequately support the claim about training dynamics, especially during the initial phase where MMD is purported to suffer from vanishing gradients. Furthermore, using the L1 distance results in a `sgn` gradient of constant magnitude, which can be problematic for fine-tuning when samples are already close and may lead to optimization instability, a nuance completely ignored by the authors.
3.  **Scalability Concerns Downplayed:** The paper acknowledges the O(N^3) or O(N^2) complexity of matching but dismisses it as acceptable compared to backpropagation costs. Fig. 5(c) shows that for N=4000, the approximate matching time is comparable to the SGD time. This suggests that for larger population sizes or shallower networks, matching will become the dominant bottleneck. Citing that parallel GPU versions exist is not a substitute for demonstrating that the proposed method is practical for large-scale problems.
4.  **Experimental Flaws:** The experimental evaluation is not entirely convincing. In the generative modeling experiment, PMD is trained for 100 epochs while MMD is trained for 500. This disparity is unexplained and makes a direct comparison of the final generated images difficult to interpret. The visual results in Fig. 4 show that PMD-generated images are noticeably blurrier than MMD's, a significant drawback the authors attribute to the pixel-wise L1 distance. This highlights that PMD is not "hyper-parameter free" as claimed; the choice of the ground distance `d(.,.)` is a critical, task-dependent decision that is not explored in the paper.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally readable, but it is marred by a lack of attention to detail, including numerous typographical errors, grammatical mistakes, and formatting inconsistencies.

*   **Typos:** "information retrival" (Sec 3.1), "upon to" (Sec 3.1), "Fewer hyper-parameter" (Sec 3.2), "epoches" (Sec 5.2), "insensitive to neither" (Sec 5.3).
*   **Formatting:** The footer on page 1 reads "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems", where "st" is clearly an unfilled placeholder. The references are poorly formatted, with many entries (e.g., [1], [2], [4]) ending in stray commas, indicating a lack of care in preparation.
*   **Clarity of Claims:** The claim that PMD is "parameter free" (Sec 3.1) is misleading. It is free of a *critic network's* parameters but not free of hyperparameters, as the choice of ground metric `d(·, ·)` is crucial and un-tuned.

This consistent lack of polish detracts significantly from the paper's professionalism and makes one question the diligence with which the research itself was conducted.

**Originality:**

By 2017, using Wasserstein distances in deep learning was an active area of research, with WGAN [3] being a prominent example. This paper's primary originality lies in proposing a practical algorithm based on the *primal* formulation of the W-1 distance using finite-sample matching, contrasting with WGAN's dual approach. While the connection between empirical OT and min-weight matching is known, its packaging into a differentiable (almost everywhere) loss for deep learning applications like domain adaptation is a novel contribution.

**Significance:**

The paper presents an interesting alternative to MMD and GANs. Its conceptual simplicity is appealing. However, the practical significance is limited by the identified weaknesses. The high computational cost of matching for large populations, the critical and unaddressed choice of the ground metric (which led to blurry images), and the heuristic nature of the training algorithm are substantial barriers to widespread adoption. While the idea is promising, the work in its current state does not feel complete or robust enough to have a major impact.

### Summary of Review

The paper proposes an interesting method (PMD) for learning distributions by directly minimizing a sample-based approximation of the Wasserstein-1 distance. The core idea is sound and the connection to optimal transport is well-established. However, the paper is severely let down by a lack of methodological rigor, questionable experimental choices, and a surprising number of presentation errors. The central training algorithm relies on an un-validated heuristic, the analysis of its main advantages is superficial, and its practical limitations (computational cost, choice of ground metric) are not adequately addressed. The sheer volume of minor flaws in formatting, grammar, and referencing suggests a rushed preparation, which undermines confidence in the work as a whole. While the underlying concepts have merit, the paper in its current form does not meet the high standards of NIPS.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Presents a novel and intuitive algorithm for applying the primal W-1 distance as a loss in deep learning.
2.  Provides a plausible alternative to MMD and GANs, with some compelling conceptual advantages (e.g., no kernel bandwidth tuning).
3.  Empirical results, while flawed, show promise in two distinct application areas.

**Arguments for rejection:**
1.  The paper is poorly polished, with numerous typos, formatting errors, and incomplete references, reflecting a lack of care.
2.  The core optimization algorithm is based on a key heuristic (fixing the matching M*) that is not theoretically or empirically justified.
3.  The analysis of claimed benefits, such as "stronger gradients," is weak and not fully convincing.
4.  The experimental comparison is weakened by unequal settings (e.g., number of epochs) and fails to address critical hyperparameters like the choice of ground distance `d(·, ·)`.
5.  Significant concerns about computational scalability for large population sizes are not adequately resolved.

**Recommendation:** Reject.
