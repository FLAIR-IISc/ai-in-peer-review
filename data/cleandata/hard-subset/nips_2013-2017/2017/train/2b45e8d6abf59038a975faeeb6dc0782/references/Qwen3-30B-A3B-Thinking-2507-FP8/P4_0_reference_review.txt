

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric that avoids the limitations of existing methods like Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations from two distributions, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD as an objective, demonstrating advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements. The authors apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing improved performance and faster convergence compared to MMD. The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on statistical divergences (e.g., MMD [17], WGAN [3]) but addresses key limitations of these methods.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric using Varadarajan's theorem and stability of optimal transport. The algorithmic approach for parameter learning is well-justified, with careful treatment of gradient computation that avoids backpropagating through the matching. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, including multiple datasets (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW) and thorough ablation studies on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithms.

2. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized with clear definitions, mathematical formulations, and helpful figures (e.g., Fig. 1 for the learning algorithm, Fig. 2 for gradient analysis). The distinction between population size and mini-batch size is carefully explained, though this could be made more explicit in the main text. The connection between PMD and optimal transport theory is clearly articulated.

3. **Originality**: The paper introduces PMD as a novel approach to estimating statistical divergence without kernel bandwidth hyper-parameters. The authors clearly differentiate their method from MMD (which requires bandwidth tuning) and WGAN (which requires a separate critic network). The theoretical analysis establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric represents a significant contribution to the field.

4. **Significance**: The advantages claimed for PMD (no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size) address practical limitations of existing methods. The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements in both performance and convergence speed across multiple deep learning applications. This work has potential to benefit practitioners in domain adaptation and generative modeling who need reliable statistical divergence estimators.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Theoretical Bounds**: While the paper proves PMD is a consistent estimator, it lacks finite-sample error bounds for the approximation error. The empirical study in Section 5.4 shows the approximation error decreases with population size, but theoretical guarantees would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Distance Metric Limitations**: The paper primarily uses L1 distance for natural images, which leads to blurry generated images (Section 5.2). While the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest using code space instead, a more systematic investigation of distance metrics for different data types would be valuable.

3. **Scalability Concerns**: The exact Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)) is used for small N, but the paper doesn't thoroughly explore the trade-off between matching accuracy and computational cost for larger datasets. While approximate matching is mentioned, the paper could better quantify the error introduced by approximation.

4. **Comparison Scope**: The paper focuses on comparing PMD with MMD, but doesn't compare with other recent divergence estimators like the Cramer distance [Bellemare et al., 2017] or the kernelized MMD with adaptive bandwidths [Gretton et al., 2012], which could provide more comprehensive context.

## Recommendation

**Pro Acceptance**:
- Novel approach to estimating statistical divergence without kernel bandwidth hyper-parameters
- Strong theoretical foundation for PMD as a consistent estimator of Wasserstein metric
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation showing clear advantages over MMD in both performance and convergence
- Practical implementation details and ablation studies that demonstrate the method's viability

**Con Acceptance**:
- Limited theoretical bounds on approximation error
- Limited investigation of distance metrics for different data types
- Some scalability concerns with the exact matching algorithm

This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of statistical divergence estimation for deep learning. While the theoretical bounds could be strengthened, the practical advantages demonstrated in the experiments are compelling. The work addresses a genuine limitation of existing methods (MMD's bandwidth tuning requirement) with a theoretically sound and empirically effective solution. The applications to domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate clear significance.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written with good organization, presents a novel approach that differs meaningfully from previous work, and addresses a problem with significant potential impact on practitioners. The weaknesses noted are not fundamental flaws but areas for potential future work.

**Overall Recommendation**: Accept. This is a strong, well-executed paper that advances the state of the art in statistical divergence estimation for deep learning.
