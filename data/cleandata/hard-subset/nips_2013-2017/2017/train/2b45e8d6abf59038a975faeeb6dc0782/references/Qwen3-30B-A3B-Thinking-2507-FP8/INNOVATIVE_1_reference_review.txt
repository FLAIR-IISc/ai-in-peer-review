

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces a novel statistical discrepancy measure called Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), which is defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two sets of samples from two distributions. The authors establish that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, and propose using it as a training objective in deep learning tasks. The key novelty lies in using the minimum weight matching as a statistical divergence, which differs fundamentally from existing methods like Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) that rely on kernel functions.

The paper makes several compelling claims about PMD's advantages over MMD: it has no bandwidth hyperparameter, provides stronger gradients (making it less prone to vanishing gradients), and can operate with smaller mini-batch sizes. These claims are supported by theoretical analysis showing PMD's connection to the Wasserstein metric and empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, where PMD outperforms MMD in terms of both performance and convergence speed.

The theoretical foundation is solid, with clear connections to optimal transport theory and a proof of strong consistency. The authors effectively distinguish PMD from related work, particularly WGAN (which approximates the dual Wasserstein metric) and MMD (which uses kernel-based estimators). The paper's position on the landscape of distributional divergence estimation is well-motivated and clearly articulated.

The empirical results are convincing for the claims made. The domain adaptation experiments on Office datasets show PMD significantly outperforming MMD on two tasks (a→w and a→d), while matching its performance on others. The generative modeling results on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW show PMD produces less noisy images than MMD, especially when operating directly on pixel space rather than on autoencoder codes. The ablation studies on mini-batch size and population size further support the paper's claims about PMD's efficiency.

I find the paper's core idea highly novel and potentially impactful. The concept of using minimum weight matching as a statistical divergence is bold and represents a fundamentally different approach from the kernel-based methods that dominate current practice. While the computational cost of the matching step (O(N^3) with Hungarian algorithm) is acknowledged, the authors convincingly argue that this cost is acceptable compared to the cost of neural network training, and they propose practical approximations that work well in practice.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with good technical depth and appropriate experimental validation. The authors acknowledge limitations (such as the need for distance metric selection) without overstating their claims.

**Strengths:**
- Truly novel approach to statistical divergence estimation
- Clear theoretical connection to Wasserstein metric
- Strong empirical results demonstrating advantages over MMD
- Practical implementation details and ablation studies
- Well-motivated position in the existing literature

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited exploration of different distance metrics beyond L1 (e.g., for image generation)
- Computational cost of matching for extremely large N (though acknowledged)
- Limited comparison with other Wasserstein approximation methods

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in the field of statistical divergence estimation. While the methodological details could be refined further, the core idea is sufficiently novel and promising to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors have identified a fundamentally different way to approach an important problem, which could inspire new directions in generative modeling, domain adaptation, and other areas requiring distributional divergence estimation.

The paper's main contribution is the conceptual shift from kernel-based to matching-based divergence estimation. This is not just an incremental improvement over existing methods but a genuinely new direction. The authors have provided a compelling argument for why this direction is valuable, with both theoretical and empirical support.

Given the reviewer's preference for novelty and bold approaches, I believe this paper should be accepted. The core idea is sufficiently original to warrant publication, and the empirical results provide reasonable validation. The minor limitations noted above do not undermine the paper's fundamental contribution, which is a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem.

The paper's significance is high: if PMD proves to be widely applicable, it could change how practitioners approach distributional divergence estimation in deep learning. The potential for improved training efficiency (smaller mini-batch sizes) and elimination of hyperparameter tuning (bandwidth selection) are particularly appealing for practitioners.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that introduces a genuinely novel concept with significant potential impact. The theoretical foundation is sound, the empirical results are convincing, and the paper is well-written. I believe it represents a meaningful contribution to the field that deserves publication at NIPS.
