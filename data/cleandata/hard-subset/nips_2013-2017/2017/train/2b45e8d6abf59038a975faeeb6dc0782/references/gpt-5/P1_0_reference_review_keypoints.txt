- PMD is defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. samples and is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (EMD).
- Authors prove PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 (using Varadarajan’s theorem and OT stability) and train deep models by not backpropagating through discrete matching.
- Claimed advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger/non-saturating gradients, and ability to use smaller minibatches than the full matched population.
- Experiments: demonstrated on unsupervised domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), with PMD often faster/more stable and yielding better or comparable results to MMD.
- Relation to prior work: PMD is equivalent to empirical W1/EMD; prior OT work (WGAN, Sinkhorn/entropic OT) is relevant but under-discussed.
- Theory quality: consistency result follows from classic results; missing explicit finiteness/finite-first-moment assumptions for unbounded metrics.
- Gradient claim: “no backprop through matching” is intuitive but lacks rigorous subgradient discussion and ignores nondifferentiability at matching boundaries.
- Algorithms: matching solved exactly via Hungarian (O(N^3)) or approximated (Drake-Hougardy O(N^2)); proposed outer-loop recompute + inner-loop fixed-matching SGD is practical.
- Subsampling bias: using minibatches |B|<N induces biased gradients; paper observes empirical success but provides no analysis of the bias.
- Experimental limitations: evaluations are largely qualitative for generative models and lack standard quantitative metrics (FID, Inception, likelihood proxies).
- Missing baselines: comparisons are only to MMD; no comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn OT, sliced Wasserstein, or stronger DA methods (DANN, CORAL, CMD, JAN).
- Clarity: paper is generally clear, well-organized, with good figures and pseudocode; should state PMD ≡ empirical W1 upfront to avoid rebranding.
- Some claims are too strong/inaccurate (e.g., dual-based approaches “not differentiable”) given differentiable entropic OT methods.
- Originality: core discrepancy and theoretical results are standard; contributions are mainly advocating empirical W1 as a training loss, a practical SGD recipe, and empirical observations.
- Significance: showing empirical W1 can work competitively vs MMD is useful, but impact is limited without comparisons to contemporary Wasserstein methods and larger-scale experiments.
- Strength: simple, parameter-free objective with clear OT connection and practical training procedure yielding stronger gradients and faster convergence than RBF-MMD empirically.
- Strength: useful sensitivity, timing analyses, and gradient-magnitude study; observed empirical N−0.23 error rate consistent with W1 theory in 5D.
- Weakness: limited novelty and rebranding of empirical W1 as PMD.
- Weakness: underplayed related work on entropic regularization (Sinkhorn) and differentiable OT solvers that are GPU-friendly.
- Weakness: heuristic treatment of gradients through matching without theoretical support; nondifferentiability and subgradient issues not discussed.
- Weakness: domain adaptation experiments use labeled target validation and omit stronger DA baselines.
- Suggested revision: explicitly acknowledge PMD ≡ empirical W1 and frame contribution as a practical recipe and empirical study.
- Suggested revision: include comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn OT (with autodiff), and sliced Wasserstein; report standard generative metrics.
- Suggested revision: add discussion on subgradients for piecewise-constant matchings and conditions justifying frozen-assignment gradients.
- Suggested revision: clarify assumptions for Theorem 3.3 (finite first moments) and quantify how often matchings change during inner-loop SGD.
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to ground metric (L1 vs L2 vs feature distances), frequency/stability of matching changes, computational trade-offs vs GPU Sinkhorn at similar N, and quantitative generative metrics with WGAN/Sinkhorn baselines.
- Pros for acceptance: practical, simple method consistent with OT theory, empirical benefits over MMD, and clear exposition.
- Cons for acceptance: limited novelty, missing key related work and stronger baselines, weak quantitative generative evaluation, and heuristic gradient treatment.
- Recommendation: Weak reject; reviewer confidence: high.
