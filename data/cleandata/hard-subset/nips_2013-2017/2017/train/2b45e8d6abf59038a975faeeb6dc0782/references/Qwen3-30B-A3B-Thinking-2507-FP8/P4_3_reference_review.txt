

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein metric for comparing probability distributions. The authors define PMD as the minimum weight matching cost between samples from two distributions and prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. They then propose an algorithm to use PMD as a training objective in deep learning tasks, comparing it with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in domain adaptation and generative modeling.

**Strengths:**

The paper's main strength lies in its clear theoretical contribution. The authors rigorously establish PMD's connection to the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), demonstrating its consistency as a statistical estimator. This theoretical foundation is well-supported by the Varadarajan theorem and stability of optimal transport. The comparison with MMD is particularly strong, with clear theoretical arguments about PMD's advantages: no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients that don't vanish for outlier samples (Fig. 2b), and smaller mini-batch requirements. These advantages are well-validated through empirical results on standard benchmarks (Office dataset, MNIST, SVHN, LFW).

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem within existing deep learning challenges, and the theoretical analysis (Section 3) is logically developed with appropriate citations to prior work on optimal transport [49, 50] and MMD [17, 31]. The experimental section (Section 5) provides sufficient detail on implementation, with clear comparisons between PMD and MMD across multiple tasks. The pseudocode (Fig. 1) effectively illustrates the parameter learning algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

The primary theoretical limitation is the absence of finite-sample error bounds for PMD, despite the paper's focus on practical applications. While the authors empirically study approximation error (Fig. 2a), a theoretical analysis of convergence rates would strengthen the contribution. The paper mentions this as future work (Section 6), but it's a significant gap for a NIPS submission.

The paper omits comparison with Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) [3] in the generative modeling experiments, despite discussing WGAN in Section 3.3. Since WGAN is a prominent approach for generative modeling using Wasserstein metrics, this omission weakens the empirical evaluation. The paper claims PMD "differs from WGAN by not requiring a separate critic network," but doesn't demonstrate this advantage in practice.

The discussion of matching algorithms (Sec. 2.3) is somewhat superficial. While the authors mention using the Hungarian algorithm and Drake-Hougardy approximation, they don't thoroughly analyze how these choices affect the quality of the estimator or the trade-offs between exact and approximate matching. The paper states that both algorithms "work well" (Sec. 2.3), but this claim lacks empirical support beyond the timing results in Fig. 5(c).

**Novelty Assessment:**

The core idea of using minimum weight matching as a direct estimator of the Wasserstein metric is novel within the context of deep learning applications. While the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) has been used in computer vision [50], PMD differs by being a population-based estimator rather than a distance between histograms. The paper clearly distinguishes PMD from MMD (Sec. 3.2) and WGAN (Sec. 3.3), and the theoretical proofs of consistency (Theorem 3.3) provide a solid foundation for novelty. The paper cites relevant prior work, including recent NIPS papers on MMD [31] and WGAN [3], and effectively positions its contribution as a new approach with practical advantages.

**Significance Assessment:**

PMD addresses a practical challenge in deep learning: estimating statistical divergence between distributions when only samples are available. The advantages over MMD (no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, smaller batch size) have clear implications for training efficiency and model quality. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple tasks and datasets (Table 1, Fig. 3a), suggesting PMD could be broadly useful in deep learning applications. However, the paper's significance is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of experiments (only domain adaptation and generative modeling) and the omission of comparison with WGAN.

**Conclusion:**

This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing PMD as a theoretically sound and practically advantageous alternative to MMD for statistical divergence estimation. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical results are convincing, and the paper is clearly written. The main limitations are the absence of finite-sample error bounds and the omission of WGAN comparison in experiments. Despite these weaknesses, the paper's strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and it represents a meaningful advancement in the field of statistical distance estimation for deep learning.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, clear comparison with MMD, practical advantages demonstrated in experiments, well-written.
Con: Lack of finite-sample error bounds, no WGAN comparison in experiments, superficial analysis of matching algorithms.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a novel contribution that advances the state of the art in statistical distance estimation for deep learning applications. It should be accepted.
