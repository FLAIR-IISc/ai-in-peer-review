

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence that estimates the Wasserstein metric through minimum weight matching of two sample populations. The authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, has no bandwidth hyper-parameter, provides stronger gradients than MMD, and can use smaller mini-batch sizes during training. They apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, reporting improved performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.

The paper demonstrates some technical merits. The theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is sound, and the experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling appear promising. The authors have made an effort to compare with relevant work, including MMD and WGAN.

However, numerous methodological and presentation issues significantly undermine the paper's quality and credibility. These include:

1. **Critical Terminology Confusion**: The paper uses "population size" to refer to the number of samples N used in the matching problem (N-PMD definition), but this conflicts with standard statistical terminology where "population" refers to the entire set of items being studied. This is confusing and should be clarified.

2. **Technical Inaccuracies**: The claim that "the mini-batch size for MMD must be equal with the population size" is incorrect. MMD can be computed with mini-batches smaller than the population size (though the estimate may be noisier). This misrepresentation weakens the paper's comparative analysis.

3. **Theoretical Ambiguities**: The paper claims PMD "has no bandwidth hyper-parameter" but fails to acknowledge that the choice of distance metric d(·, ·) may introduce parameters (e.g., Lp distance with parameter p). The statement "PMD is parameter free" is therefore inaccurate.

4. **Formatting and Citation Errors**: The footnote with "st Conference" is clearly a formatting error. Reference [3] is incorrectly cited for WGAN (WGAN is actually Arjovsky et al., 2017, not Goodfellow et al., 2014). The reference to "Proposition 5.4 in [6]" is invalid as [6] does not appear in the reference list.

5. **Experimental Ambiguities**: The paper states "PMD outperforms MMD on the a→w and a→d tasks by a large margin" without providing statistical significance testing. The claim about "stronger gradients" lacks quantification and justification.

6. **Inconsistent Parameterization**: The paper sets "population size N = 2000 for both PMD and MMD" but fails to clarify what N represents in the MMD context (MMD typically uses a different parameterization where N would be the sample size used for the estimate).

7. **Missing Technical Details**: The paper mentions using "approximated matching" in generative modeling but doesn't specify which approximation algorithm was used. The exact implementation details of the matching algorithms are insufficient for reproducibility.

8. **Misleading Claims**: The statement that "PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric" is technically correct, but the paper fails to clarify that this holds only for continuous distance metrics d(·, ·), which should be explicitly stated.

9. **Figure Mislabeling**: Figure 5(c) has "Time (seconds)" on the y-axis but doesn't specify the units for the x-axis (population size N), making interpretation difficult.

10. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for parameterized transformations (e.g., "xi;θX" vs "xi;θX"), which creates confusion.

These issues collectively represent a significant number of minor but critical flaws that substantially undermine the paper's credibility. The paper's technical contributions are overshadowed by these presentation and methodological inconsistencies. While the core idea of using minimum weight matching as a divergence estimator has potential, the paper fails to present it with the rigor expected at NIPS.

The paper's strengths include a clear connection to the Wasserstein metric, promising experimental results on domain adaptation, and a well-structured theoretical analysis. However, the numerous technical inaccuracies and presentation issues outweigh these strengths.

The paper is not sufficiently novel compared to recent work on Wasserstein distance estimation (e.g., Genevay et al., 2016; Kolouri et al., 2019), and the claimed advantages over MMD are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented.

I recommend rejection due to the numerous technical inaccuracies and presentation flaws that prevent the paper from meeting NIPS's standards for quality and rigor. While the core idea has potential, the current version is not ready for publication at NIPS.
