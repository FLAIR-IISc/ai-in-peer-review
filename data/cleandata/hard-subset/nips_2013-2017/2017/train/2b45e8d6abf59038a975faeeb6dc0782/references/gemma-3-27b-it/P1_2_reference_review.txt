## NIPS 2017 Review: Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning

**Summary:**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric. Unlike Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), PMD doesn’t require kernel bandwidth tuning, exhibits stronger gradients, and can function effectively with standard mini-batch sizes. The authors demonstrate PMD’s advantages over MMD in both theoretical analysis (strong consistency) and empirical evaluation on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The core idea is elegantly simple: directly minimize the cost of matching samples between distributions, offering a compelling alternative to kernel-based methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  PMD offers a fresh perspective on measuring distribution divergence, moving away from kernel methods and leveraging optimal transport concepts. This is a valuable contribution as it addresses some known limitations of MMD.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, demonstrating that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric. This grounding in optimal transport theory adds significant credibility.
* **Practical Advantages:** The identified advantages - no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes – are highly practical. These directly translate to easier implementation, faster training, and potentially improved performance. The claim regarding gradient strength is supported by empirical evidence (Fig. 2b).
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate PMD’s superior performance and convergence speed compared to MMD. The experiments are well-designed and the comparisons are fair.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise.  The inclusion of pseudocode (Fig. 1) further enhances understanding.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the computational cost of the matching problem and propose using an approximation algorithm for larger sample sizes, demonstrating an awareness of practical limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors address the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, it remains a potential bottleneck for extremely large sample sizes. The approximated algorithm (O(N^2)) helps, but further optimization or GPU acceleration of the matching process could be a worthwhile future direction.
* **Distance Metric Limitation:** The current implementation relies on the L1 distance. While justified, it's not universally optimal for all data types (as noted in the generative modeling section). Exploring other distance metrics and their impact on performance could strengthen the work.
* **Limited Scope of Comparison:** The paper focuses solely on comparing PMD with MMD.  A comparison with other Wasserstein distance estimators (e.g., those based on the dual formulation as mentioned in section 3.1) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.  Including comparisons with other domain adaptation or generative modeling techniques would also be beneficial.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** While the authors demonstrate PMD's superiority, an ablation study focusing on the mini-batch size and the choice of matching algorithm (exact vs. approximate) could further elucidate the impact of these parameters on performance.
* **Generative Modeling Results:** The generated images on SVHN and LFW are described as blurry. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth analysis of why PMD struggles with these datasets and potential solutions would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* This paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound method for estimating distribution divergence.
* PMD addresses key limitations of MMD, offering practical advantages in training and performance.
* The empirical results demonstrate PMD's effectiveness in relevant deep learning applications.
* The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and logically organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the matching problem remains a potential concern for very large datasets.
* The comparison is limited to MMD; a broader evaluation against other Wasserstein estimators would be ideal.
* The blurry image generation results raise questions about the method’s suitability for complex data.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distribution divergence estimation. The proposed PMD offers a compelling alternative to existing methods, addressing several key limitations while providing strong theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. While the computational complexity and limited scope of comparison are minor concerns, the overall quality and potential impact of the work outweigh these drawbacks. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.

**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
