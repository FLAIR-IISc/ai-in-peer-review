This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based statistical divergence, and demonstrates its application as a training objective in deep learning. The core idea is to estimate the distance between two distributions by calculating the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from them. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). A key contribution is a practical algorithm for minimizing PMD via stochastic gradient descent, where the matching is computed on a larger "population" of samples, while gradient updates are performed on smaller mini-batches, assuming the matching remains locally constant.

The work is well-situated within the context of existing methods. It is presented as a direct alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), and the authors draw a clear theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric, distinguishing their primal-form approximation from dual-form approaches like the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN).

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Elegance:** The central idea of using the cost of bipartite matching as a differentiable loss for deep neural networks is a bold, simple, and elegant one. While estimating the Wasserstein distance via optimal transport on empirical samples is known in statistics, framing and operationalizing this as a practical, end-to-end trainable objective for generative modeling and domain adaptation is a highly novel contribution in the context of deep learning. It presents a fundamentally different approach to distribution matching compared to kernel-based methods (MMD) or adversarial training (GANs).

2.  **Significant Practical Advantages:** The paper convincingly argues for several practical benefits over MMD. The elimination of the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter is a significant advantage, removing a sensitive and often crucial tuning parameter. The argument for stronger, non-saturating gradients is compelling, supported by both intuition (L1 distance vs. RBF kernel) and empirical evidence (Fig. 2b). This could directly address training instabilities and slow convergence issues seen in other methods. Furthermore, the proposed training scheme, which decouples the population size for matching (N) from the SGD mini-batch size (|B|), is a clever practical insight that allows for faster updates.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors apply PMD to two distinct and important problems: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling. In both cases, PMD demonstrates competitive or superior performance to MMD, often with faster convergence. The generative modeling results are particularly interesting, as PMD produces reasonable images even when applied directly in pixel space, a setting where MMD-based methods often struggle. The ablation studies regarding population size, mini-batch size, and the choice of matching algorithm are thorough and support the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The primary weakness is the computational cost of the minimum weight matching, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. While the authors argue this is acceptable relative to the cost of backpropagation, this complexity can become a serious bottleneck as the required population size N increases. This might limit the applicability of PMD in scenarios where very high-precision estimates of the divergence are needed, which in turn require very large N. MMD, while also O(N^2), is often more amenable to massive parallelization on GPUs.

2.  **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical contribution is solid but not deeply original, primarily leveraging established results from optimal transport theory to show strong consistency. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a non-asymptotic analysis providing finite-sample error bounds, which would give practitioners a better handle on how to choose the population size N. The authors acknowledge this as future work. Additionally, the assumption that the optimal matching M* remains fixed during gradient steps is treated somewhat lightly; a more formal discussion of the implications of this piece-wise constant approximation would be beneficial.

3.  **Choice of Ground Metric:** The experiments exclusively use the L1 distance as the ground metric `d(·, ·)`. While this choice has nice properties for the gradient, it is well-known to be a poor perceptual metric for structured data like images. This may contribute to the blurriness observed in the generated samples. The PMD framework is general to any `d`, and exploring more perceptually-aligned metrics could be a simple way to dramatically improve results, but this is not discussed.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

The core idea of PMD is a genuinely novel and thought-provoking contribution to the field. It offers a new, parameter-free tool for a fundamental problem in machine learning: comparing probability distributions from samples. The weaknesses, particularly regarding scalability and the depth of theoretical analysis, are present but do not undermine the core contribution. They represent clear and exciting directions for future research that this paper will undoubtedly inspire. In line with valuing bold new directions, this work's conceptual novelty and potential impact far outweigh its current methodological limitations. It is precisely the kind of creative and significant paper that NIPS should showcase.
