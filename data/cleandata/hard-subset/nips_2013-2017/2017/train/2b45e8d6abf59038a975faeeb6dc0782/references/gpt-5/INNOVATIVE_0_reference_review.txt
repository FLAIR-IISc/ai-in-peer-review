Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets of equal size using a ground metric d. PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (EMD) between uniform empirical measures; the authors emphasize its use as a differentiable training objective for deep models. They show PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the population W1 via classical results (Varadarajan’s theorem + stability of optimal transport), and propose a practical SGD procedure that (i) computes a matching on a “population” of size N, (ii) freezes the matching for several inner steps, and (iii) uses much smaller update minibatches B by subsampling matched pairs. They implement exact Hungarian and O(N^2) approximate matching, and compare PMD against MMD in unsupervised domain adaptation and implicit generative modeling. They report faster convergence, stronger gradients, and reduced hyperparameter tuning, with some improvements over MMD on Office (feature-based) and qualitative gains for image generation.

Strengths
- Clear and simple formulation that directly leverages the primal OT problem; easy to implement and reason about.
- Solid consistency argument (albeit based on known OT results), plus an informative toy study of convergence with N.
- Practical training heuristic (freeze matching + small B) that appears to accelerate optimization in practice.
- Empirical evidence that PMD yields stronger, more uniformly distributed gradients than RBF-MMD and is less sensitive to tuning.
- Comparisons on two applications suggest PMD can be a viable, parameter-free alternative to MMD, with faster convergence.

Weaknesses and missing context
- Novelty: PMD is equivalent to the classical empirical 1-Wasserstein distance/EMD with uniform weights. The consistency result is a direct corollary of standard OT theory; no new theory or estimator is introduced. The main novelty is the training recipe. Renaming EMD as PMD risks rebranding.
- Related work omissions: Key OT literature is missing, especially Cuturi’s entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances (2013) and subsequent differentiable OT losses used in deep learning (e.g., Sinkhorn-based training, JDOT/OT for domain adaptation). Also missing stronger generative baselines (WGAN/WGAN-GP, Cramér distance/energy distance, MMD-GAN). These methods address some of the very issues highlighted (stability, gradients, scalability).
- Methodological concerns:
  - The gradient is taken w.r.t. a piecewise-constant matching; “no backprop through matching” and freezing the assignment yield a biased estimator with potentially discontinuous updates. No analysis of the induced bias or convergence.
  - Computational scalability remains an issue: computing an N×N cost matrix and running O(N^2) matching per outer step is expensive; experiments use N up to 2000 on small images. No results on higher-res or larger-scale settings.
  - The “small minibatch” advantage requires precomputing large-population matchings and reusing them; in non-stationary training this assumption may fail as parameters move.
- Empirical evaluation:
  - Domain adaptation: only compared to MMD on top of pretrained features; no comparison to adversarial/domain-adversarial approaches (DANN) or OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., JDOT). Gains over MMD are mixed.
  - Generative modeling: largely qualitative; images are blurry in pixel space, and performance hinges on moving to AE code space. No standard metrics (IS/FID/precision-recall), no comparison to WGAN/Sinkhorn-based OT losses or Cramér GAN. Using different training epochs for PMD vs MMD complicates fairness.
  - “Stronger gradient” claim is demonstrated post hoc on one setup; multi-kernel MMD and MMD-GAN variants can mitigate vanishing gradients but are not evaluated.

Quality
- Technically sound in its use of known OT theory; the algorithmic proposal is reasonable but lacks theoretical guarantees for the frozen-matching stochastic updates.
- Experimental support is suggestive but not conclusive due to missing strong baselines and quantitative metrics in the generative setting.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and easy to follow. Figures are helpful. Some notational overload (N as “population” vs minibatch) could be clarified earlier. The paper would benefit from an explicit statement that PMD equals empirical W1/EMD with uniform weights, and a clearer discussion of computational trade-offs.

Originality
- Limited. Using empirical W1 as a loss is known; the theoretical results are standard. The main new element is the practical training recipe with frozen matching and subsampled updates.

Significance
- As presented, the contribution is incremental. It may be useful in practice as a drop-in alternative to MMD in some settings, but without comparisons to modern OT-based or adversarial methods and without scaling results, the impact is uncertain.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the work within OT for deep learning: compare and cite entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) and differentiable OT losses; include WGAN/WGAN-GP and Cramér/energy distance, and OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., JDOT).
- Provide quantitative generative metrics (FID/IS) and fair training budgets across methods.
- Analyze the bias and stability of the frozen-matching SGD; consider strategies to update matchings adaptively, or approximate differentiable matchings (e.g., Sinkhorn).
- Explore feature-space ground metrics (learned perceptual features) and ablate L1 vs L2 vs feature distances.
- Discuss and demonstrate GPU implementations and scaling beyond N=2K.

Pros
- Simple, parameter-free loss with intuitive gradients.
- Practical heuristic that often speeds up training.
- Clear connection to Wasserstein distance and consistency.

Cons
- Limited novelty; essentially reuses empirical W1/EMD.
- Missing key related work and strong baselines.
- Computational and theoretical shortcomings not fully addressed.
- Empirical gains over stronger alternatives unproven.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is clean and potentially useful, the conceptual novelty is modest and the empirical evaluation lacks comparisons to the most relevant OT/adversarial baselines and standard metrics. Strengthening related work, theory for the training procedure, and experimental comparisons could make this a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
