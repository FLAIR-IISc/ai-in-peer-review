Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of the minimum-weight bipartite matching between N i.i.d. samples from two distributions under a ground metric d (typically L1). PMD is thus the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance between empirical measures, computed by solving a linear assignment problem. The authors:
- Prove strong consistency by appealing to standard results on weak convergence of empirical measures and stability of optimal transport (Villani).
- Propose using PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep models, avoiding backpropagation through the discrete matching (arguing the matching is locally constant almost everywhere).
- Introduce a practical training procedure with occasional recomputation of the matching on large populations but using smaller mini-batches in inner-loop SGD updates by “freezing” the matching.
- Compare PMD to MMD in domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW), claiming fewer hyperparameters, stronger/less vanishing gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batches.

Relation to prior work
PMD is the classical empirical Wasserstein-1 distance computed via assignment; the paper positions it against MMD and WGAN. However, key related OT literature is missing or under-discussed: entropic regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) and their GPU-friendly, differentiable implementations; stochastic/mini-batch OT for learning; optimal transport for domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al., 2016); sliced Wasserstein distances for generative modeling; and WGAN-GP. The theoretical consistency result is well-known in OT and follows directly from standard theorems.

Quality
- Theory: The strong consistency result is correct but straightforward; no finite-sample bounds or new analysis. The claim that the gradient estimator is “unbiased” when ignoring the dependence of M* on parameters is not rigorously justified; what is used is effectively a valid subgradient almost everywhere (the matching is piecewise constant except on measure-zero manifolds). The inner-loop scheme that reuses a stale matching and subsamples a mini-batch introduces bias; no convergence guarantees are provided.
- Algorithms: Computing exact matching is O(N^3); the approximate O(N^2) matching helps but still requires O(N^2) distance computation and memory. The approach’s scalability and memory footprint for large N and high-dimensional data are not deeply evaluated. Importantly, the paper does not compare to Sinkhorn OT, which is typically faster, differentiable, and GPU-accelerated.
- Experiments: 
  - Domain adaptation: Only compares to MMD on Office using fixed ImageNet features; gains are modest (PMD better on 2 of 6 shifts). No comparison to stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, CORAL, CMD, OTDA with Sinkhorn) that are standard in this setting. Validation uses 100 labeled target samples, which departs from strict unsupervised DA and may bias selection.
  - Generative modeling: Evaluation is mostly qualitative; no standard metrics (Inception Score/FID) are reported. Comparison to WGAN/WGAN-GP or Sinkhorn-based losses is missing. PMD models trained for fewer epochs than MMD, which complicates fairness of time-to-convergence claims. Images trained with pixel-level L1 are blurry (acknowledged), and performance improves in AE code space, mirroring prior observations for MMD.
  - Ablations: The gradient magnitude histogram and mini-batch sensitivity are interesting, but more rigorous ablations (e.g., effect of ground metric, dimension, population size vs. wall-clock) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well organized. The presentation of PMD and its relation to Wasserstein-1 is clear, though the novelty is mostly in packaging and empirical insights. Important implementation details could be clarified:
- Precise treatment of non-differentiability and justification for ignoring the dependence of M* on parameters (subgradient view).
- The potential bias introduced by reusing matchings over multiple updates.
- Memory/time complexity and GPU practicality.
There are some missing citations to core OT literature (see below).

Originality
The core idea—using the empirical Wasserstein-1 distance via minimum-cost matching as a training loss—is not new. The paper’s contributions lie in:
- An explicit comparison to MMD’s optimization dynamics and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- A practical training recipe (frozen matching with smaller inner-loop batches) and empirical observations about gradient strength and convergence.
However, in light of prior work on differentiable, scalable OT (Sinkhorn), OT-based domain adaptation, and Wasserstein-based generative modeling, the novelty is limited and the positioning incomplete.

Significance
Estimating distributional discrepancy from samples matters widely. PMD is a simple, parameter-free alternative to MMD that can work well in small to medium batch regimes, and the empirical gradient-strength observation is useful. That said, without rigorous convergence guarantees for the training scheme, without comparisons to stronger and more scalable OT alternatives (Sinkhorn/sliced OT), and with limited, partially qualitative experiments, the impact is constrained. The work may still be of practical interest to some practitioners.

Pros
- Clear formulation connecting matching-based discrepancy to Wasserstein-1; simple to implement.
- Useful practical insight: PMD’s per-sample gradients don’t vanish as easily as RBF-MMD and allow smaller inner-loop batches.
- Empirically improves over MMD in some settings and converges faster in the reported experiments.
- Theoretical consistency (albeit standard).

Cons
- Novelty is limited; key OT literature (Cuturi/Sinkhorn, OTDA) and strong baselines (WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn losses) are missing from experiments and related work.
- Claims about unbiased gradients and the inner-loop frozen-matching heuristic lack theoretical support.
- Experimental scope is limited: DA comparisons only to MMD and on one benchmark; generative modeling lacks quantitative metrics and SOTA baselines.
- Scalability concerns (O(N^2) memory/time, CPU matching) and practical GPU implementations not demonstrated.
- Pixel-space L1 leads to blur; the “code space” fix echoes prior MMD/GMMN practice.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please compare to entropic OT (Sinkhorn) and/or sliced Wasserstein, both in runtime and performance; these are natural, scalable, differentiable baselines.
- Clarify the gradient treatment: frame the estimator as a subgradient; discuss conditions under which the frozen-matching inner loop converges, or provide empirical evidence over more tasks.
- For DA, compare to DANN/JAN/CMD/OTDA and avoid target labels for validation (or justify).
- For generative modeling, report FID/IS and include WGAN-GP and Sinkhorn losses; align compute budgets for fair convergence-time comparisons.
- Discuss memory footprint and GPU implementation details for large N.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is clearly written and contains some useful empirical insights, but the theoretical contribution is minimal, related work/baselines are incomplete (notably excluding Sinkhorn OT and modern Wasserstein-based methods), and the experimental evidence is not strong enough to support significant impact at NeurIPS without broader, more rigorous comparisons and analysis.
