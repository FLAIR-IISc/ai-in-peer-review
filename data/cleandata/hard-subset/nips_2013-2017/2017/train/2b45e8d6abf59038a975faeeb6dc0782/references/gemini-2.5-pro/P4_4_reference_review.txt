This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence measure for comparing two probability distributions. The PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples, each drawn from one of the distributions. The core theoretical contribution is to establish that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). Building on this, the authors propose a method to use PMD as a differentiable objective function for training deep neural networks via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key insight enabling this is that the gradient of the PMD with respect to the sample-generating parameters can be computed by holding the optimal matching fixed, as the discrete matching assignment does not change with infinitesimal parameter updates.

This work relates to a significant body of research on estimating statistical divergences from samples. It is presented as an alternative to the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), which has been used extensively in prior NIPS work for tasks like generative modeling (e.g., Generative Moment Matching Networks, GMMN) and domain adaptation. The paper also connects to the recent line of work using optimal transport and the Wasserstein metric in deep learning, most notably the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN). However, while WGAN approximates the dual form of the W1 distance using a learned critic network, PMD directly estimates the primal form through bipartite matching on finite samples. The idea of using the solution to the assignment problem to approximate the W1 distance on empirical measures is well-established in the optimal transport literature, but its application as a direct, differentiable loss for training deep generative models and for domain adaptation appears to be novel.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Simplicity:** The paper proposes a novel and conceptually elegant method for integrating a direct primal estimator of the Wasserstein distance into deep learning frameworks. The approach is simple to understand and implement, avoiding the complexities of adversarial training (as in GANs) or kernel selection (as in MMD).

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is clearly articulated and theoretically grounded. The claim that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 is supported by established results from probability theory (Varadarajan's theorem) and optimal transport theory (Villani's stability theorem). This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.

3.  **Practical Advantages over MMD:** The paper makes a compelling case for PMD's advantages over MMD.
    *   **Fewer Hyperparameters:** PMD is parameter-free, removing the need to tune a kernel bandwidth, which is a significant practical challenge for MMD.
    *   **Stronger Gradients:** The authors provide both a theoretical argument and empirical evidence (Fig. 2b) that PMD provides stronger, non-saturating gradients compared to the RBF kernel MMD. This can lead to more stable and efficient training, especially in deep models.
    *   **Optimization Efficiency:** The proposed training scheme, which re-uses a fixed matching for several mini-batch SGD updates, allows for faster convergence per epoch compared to MMD, which requires the full population for each gradient step.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the method is explained precisely with helpful illustrations (Fig. 1), and the theoretical analysis is presented coherently.

5.  **Empirical Validation:** The claims are well-supported by experiments in two different applications: domain adaptation and generative modeling. In both cases, PMD is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD, both in final performance and convergence speed. The ablation studies on the population size and mini-batch size further reinforce the paper's claims about the method's properties.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The primary weakness of PMD is the computational cost of solving the minimum weight matching problem, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm and O(N^2) for the approximation used, where N is the population size. While the paper argues this is acceptable, and experiments show good performance with N in the low thousands, this could become a significant bottleneck if larger population sizes are required for accurate estimation in higher-dimensional spaces. The paper mentions the possibility of GPU acceleration but does not provide results using it.

2.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The comparison is almost exclusively against MMD. Given the prominence of WGANs, which also optimize a form of the Wasserstein distance, a comparison to WGAN or WGAN-GP would have been highly valuable for situating PMD's performance within the state-of-the-art for generative models. While the authors correctly point out conceptual differences (primal vs. dual, no critic network), an empirical comparison is necessary to assess the practical trade-offs. The quality of the generated images, while showing an improvement over the GMMN baseline, does not appear to be on par with modern GAN results.

3.  **Optimization Heuristic:** The algorithm relies on the assumption that the optimal matching M* remains stable across several SGD updates. This introduces a new, unanalyzed hyperparameter: the number of inner-loop SGD updates before the matching is recomputed. The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the training to this choice or provide guidance on how to set it.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel, simple, and well-founded method for comparing distributions in deep learning.
*   It provides a compelling alternative to MMD, with clear practical advantages (no kernel tuning, stronger gradients).
*   The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is sound and well-explained.
*   The empirical results strongly support the claimed advantages over a relevant and widely used baseline (MMD).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational complexity of the matching algorithm raises scalability concerns.
*   The experimental comparison is narrow, lacking a comparison to other state-of-the-art Wasserstein-based methods like WGAN.
*   The optimization procedure introduces a new hyperparameter (frequency of matching re-computation) that is not analyzed.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution. The proposed PMD method is novel, theoretically sound, and demonstrates significant practical advantages over MMD in the conducted experiments. While concerns about scalability and the scope of experimental comparison exist, they represent avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws. The paper's clarity, originality, and potential significance make it a strong candidate for acceptance.
