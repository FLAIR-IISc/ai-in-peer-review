### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based statistical divergence measure for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average distance between pairs of samples under an optimal bipartite matching, i.e., the solution to a minimum weight matching problem. The authors establish a strong theoretical connection between their proposed measure and the well-known first Wasserstein metric (W1), proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1.

The paper then proposes a practical algorithm for using PMD as a differentiable objective function in deep learning models trained with stochastic gradient descent. A key insight is to amortize the computational cost of finding the optimal matching (O(N^3) or O(N^2)) over several smaller mini-batch updates, under the assumption that the matching remains locally constant.

The work positions PMD as an alternative to the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). It argues that PMD offers several advantages, including having no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, providing stronger, non-saturating gradients, and enabling faster convergence through the use of smaller mini-batch sizes. These claims are evaluated empirically on two challenging deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. The experimental results generally show that PMD is competitive with, and often outperforms, MMD in both final performance and convergence speed.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper introduces a simple yet powerful idea: using the primal form of the empirical Wasserstein-1 distance directly as a loss function for deep learning. This provides a compelling and principled alternative to existing methods like MMD and the concurrent work on Wasserstein GANs (WGANs), which estimate the dual form. The motivation for overcoming the limitations of MMD (hyperparameter tuning, vanishing gradients) is clear and convincing.

2.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is clearly established. The proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3), which leverages established results from optimal transport theory, provides a firm theoretical grounding for the proposed estimator. This gives confidence that PMD is measuring a meaningful statistical distance.

3.  **Significant Practical Advantages:** The paper highlights several practical benefits of PMD over MMD that are highly relevant to practitioners. The elimination of the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter simplifies the model tuning process. The argument for stronger gradients (empirically verified in Fig. 2b) is well-made and addresses a known issue with saturated kernels in MMD. Furthermore, the proposed training scheme that decouples the population size `N` from the SGD mini-batch size `|B|` is a clever and effective way to manage the computational trade-offs, enabling faster convergence (Fig. 5a).

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD on two distinct and important applications (domain adaptation and generative modeling). The results on the Office dataset and on generating images from MNIST, SVHN, and LFW are strong, generally showing improvements over the MMD baseline (GMMN). The ablation studies in Section 5.3 and 5.4 are particularly valuable, supporting the claims about the impact of `N` and `|B|`, the effectiveness of approximate matching, and the nature of the PMD gradients.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The primary concern with PMD is its computational complexity. The minimum weight matching requires O(N^3) (exact) or O(N^2) (approximate) time, which is significantly more expensive than MMD's O(N^2) evaluation for the entire batch. While the paper's amortization strategy is effective, the matching cost remains a bottleneck, especially as the population size `N` needs to grow to get a reliable estimate in high dimensions. The authors mention that matching algorithms can be parallelized on GPU but provide no results or detailed discussion. This leaves a critical question about the method's practical scalability unanswered.
    *   **Suggestion:** To strengthen the paper, even preliminary timing results from a prototype GPU implementation or a more detailed discussion of the parallelization strategy for the chosen matching algorithms would be highly beneficial.

2.  **Differentiability of the Matching:** The training algorithm relies on the assumption that the optimal matching M* does not change for infinitesimal changes in the parameters θ (Eq. 3). While this holds "almost everywhere," the gradient is technically undefined at points where the optimal matching switches. This is a non-trivial detail, as the loss surface may have discontinuities in its gradient.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would be more complete with a brief discussion of this issue. Acknowledging that the objective is only subdifferentiable and justifying why SGD is still effective in practice (e.g., such points are rare or do not hinder convergence) would add technical rigor.

3.  **Curse of Dimensionality:** PMD relies on a base distance `d(·, ·)`, which is L1 in the experiments. It is well-known that L1/L2 distances can become less meaningful in very high-dimensional spaces like raw image pixels. This may contribute to the blurry results seen in the generated SVHN and LFW images. Critic-based methods like WGAN implicitly learn a feature representation where the distance is more semantically meaningful.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors correctly note that training on an autoencoder's latent space helps. It would be valuable to expand this into a broader discussion about the trade-offs between a fixed-distance metric like PMD and a learned-distance metric like WGAN in the context of high-dimensional data. This would better position PMD in the landscape of optimal transport-based methods.

4.  **Comparison to WGAN:** Given that this paper tackles the same core problem as the highly influential WGAN paper (using the Wasserstein distance for generative modeling), the lack of an experimental comparison is a notable omission. While the paper provides a good conceptual comparison in Section 3.1, an empirical one would have provided a much clearer picture of the relative strengths and weaknesses.

### Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, theoretically sound, and intuitive sample-based divergence (PMD).
*   Clearly demonstrates practical advantages over the widely used MMD baseline (no hyperparameters, stronger gradients, faster convergence).
*   The proposed training algorithm with amortized matching is a valuable practical contribution.
*   Experiments are thorough and convincingly support the paper's main claims across multiple tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   Significant concerns about computational scalability, especially for large population sizes `N`.
*   The technical assumption about the differentiability of the matching operator is not fully addressed.
*   The use of L1/L2 distance may limit effectiveness in very high-dimensional spaces.
*   Lacks an experimental comparison to the highly relevant WGAN baseline.

### Final Recommendation

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and novel contribution to the field. The proposed PMD method is elegant, well-grounded in theory, and demonstrates clear practical benefits over established baselines like MMD. While there are valid concerns regarding scalability and the choice of the base metric in high dimensions, these feel more like limitations and avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws. The strengths in novelty, theoretical contribution, and empirical validation are significant. Therefore, I recommend **acceptance**.
