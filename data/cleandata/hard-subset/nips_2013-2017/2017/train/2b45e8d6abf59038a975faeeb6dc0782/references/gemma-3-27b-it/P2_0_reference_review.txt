## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric (Earth Mover's Distance).  PMD is defined as the minimum cost matching between two sample populations, offering an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors argue PMD avoids MMD’s bandwidth tuning, exhibits stronger gradients, and allows for smaller mini-batch sizes, leading to faster convergence. They demonstrate PMD’s effectiveness in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing performance improvements over MMD.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound contribution. The core idea of PMD is relatively simple, yet has potentially significant benefits for training generative models and performing domain adaptation.  The theoretical justification connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric is clearly laid out, and the empirical results support the claims of improved performance and faster convergence. While the improvements demonstrated aren’t *massive*, they are consistent and the advantages in gradient behavior and mini-batch size are compelling. The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.  

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** While building upon existing work on optimal transport and discrepancy measures, PMD presents a genuinely new approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric, particularly in the context of deep learning.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper clearly establishes the theoretical link between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, proving strong consistency.  This is a significant contribution.
*   **Practical Advantages:**  The claimed advantages of PMD – no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size – are all highly desirable in deep learning applications.  The empirical evidence supports these claims, particularly regarding gradient behavior.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling, while not exhaustive, provide convincing evidence that PMD can outperform MMD.
*   **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise.
*   **Discussion of limitations:** The authors acknowledge the potential issues with using L1 distance in image generation and suggest future work to address it.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Modest Performance Gains:** The performance improvements over MMD, while consistent, are not overwhelmingly large in the reported experiments.  Further experimentation on more complex datasets and architectures would strengthen the claims.
*   **Computational Cost of Matching:** While the authors discuss approximation techniques, the core of PMD relies on solving a matching problem. The exact Hungarian algorithm scales cubically with the population size, which could be a bottleneck for very large datasets. While the approximated algorithm helps, its performance is not thoroughly analyzed beyond mentioning its O(N^2) complexity.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus on relatively standard tasks and datasets. Expanding the empirical evaluation to more challenging problems (e.g., higher-resolution image generation, more complex domain adaptation scenarios) would increase confidence in the generalizability of the results.
* **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the approximation used to the Wasserstein metric. While theoretical consistency is shown, a practical understanding of how the error manifests in the applications would be valuable.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the experimental setup is appropriate. The authors are careful to present both the advantages and potential limitations of PMD.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical notation is clear and the explanations are concise.

**Originality:** PMD represents a novel approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric. The combination of optimal transport theory with deep learning training objectives is well-motivated and provides a fresh perspective.

**Significance:**  The potential impact of PMD is significant. The ability to train generative models and perform domain adaptation with stronger gradients and smaller mini-batches could lead to faster convergence and improved performance. The elimination of the bandwidth parameter is also a valuable contribution.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Solid theoretical foundation and justification.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over MMD in relevant tasks.
*   Practical advantages in terms of gradient behavior and mini-batch size.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses an important problem in deep learning: estimating distributional differences.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Performance gains are modest in the reported experiments.
*   Computational cost of the matching problem could be a bottleneck for large datasets.
*   Limited scope of empirical evaluation.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept.**

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning. The introduction of PMD offers a promising alternative to existing discrepancy measures, with potential benefits for training generative models and performing domain adaptation. While further research is needed to address the limitations identified above, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and offers a significant step forward in this area. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
