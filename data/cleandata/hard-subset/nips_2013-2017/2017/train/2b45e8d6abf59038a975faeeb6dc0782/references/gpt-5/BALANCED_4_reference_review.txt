Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets X and Y drawn from distributions pX and pY, with a given ground metric d. PMD is thus the empirical first Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between the uniform empirical measures on X and Y (assignment formulation). The authors prove “strong consistency” of PMD as an estimator of the population W1 by appealing to weak convergence of empirical measures and stability of optimal transport, and propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep models. Optimization proceeds by (i) computing a matching on a (large) “population” once, and (ii) performing several SGD steps while holding the matching fixed, optionally using smaller mini-batches that reference the fixed matching. The matching is solved either exactly (Hungarian, O(N^3)) or approximately (Drake–Hougardy, O(N^2)).

They compare PMD to MMD on two applications: unsupervised domain adaptation (Office: Amazon/Dslr/Webcam with VGG features) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), claiming PMD has three advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batches during SGD. Empirically, PMD performs comparably or better than MMD in domain adaptation on some transfer directions and yields visually less noisy samples than MMD in generative modeling. Timing studies indicate that approximate matching is comparable to backprop time and that PMD can converge faster than MMD due to smaller batches.

Strengths
- Clear goal and motivation: a sample-based, differentiable distributional discrepancy that is parameter-free and well-suited to deep learning.
- Correct connection to W1: the assignment-based PMD equals the 1-Wasserstein distance between empirical distributions; the consistency argument is standard and appropriate.
- Practical recipe: compute a matching, then update parameters for several steps without recomputing the matching; demonstrates that approximate matching works in practice.
- Empirical evidence that PMD gradients are more uniformly strong across samples than MMD with RBF kernels, aligning with the intuition for attraction/repulsion saturation in MMD.
- Applications to two relevant tasks with reasonable baselines (MMD) and analyses of sensitivity to batch/population sizes and matching algorithms.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: PMD is simply the Earth Mover’s (W1) distance between empirical measures with uniform weights; this formulation and its use as a loss are well known in OT and ML. The paper largely repackages standard OT results (e.g., assignment equivalence, consistency) under a new name.
- Missing and incomplete related work:
  - Entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) offer differentiable, fast O(N^2) computations with GPU-friendly implementations and have been widely used as losses in deep learning; not discussed or compared.
  - Sliced/Projected Wasserstein distances (Bonneel et al., Rabin et al.) provide scalable, differentiable approximations; not cited.
  - OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., Courty, Flamary, Tuia: OTDA/JDOT) directly aligns domains via OT; not cited/compared.
  - Wasserstein GANs (Arjovsky et al.) and improvements (e.g., gradient penalty) are mentioned but not empirically compared; the “unknown scale” argument is not compelling given established training protocols.
  - Cramér/energy distances and MMD variants (e.g., spectral mixtures) that alleviate gradient saturation are only lightly addressed.
- Theoretical depth: beyond consistency (which is inherited from standard OT), there is no finite-sample analysis, no discussion of the curse of dimensionality for W1 (slow N^{-1/d} convergence), and no optimization analysis for the “fixed matching for multiple steps” heuristic (Danskin’s theorem assumptions, subgradients, bias introduced by Eq. 4).
- Experimental breadth and rigor:
  - Generative modeling evaluation is mostly qualitative; no standard quantitative metrics (e.g., FID, Inception score) or diversity/overfitting checks beyond interpolation.
  - Baselines are narrow (MMD only). For domain adaptation, stronger baselines (DANN, CORAL, JAN, CMD) are common on Office; not reported.
  - Using L1 in pixel space yields blurry images; mitigation via code-space training is noted but not systematically analyzed (distance choice/feature space learning).
- Scalability and practicality: computing a global matching on N≈2000 every outer iteration is still nontrivial; GPU implementations are suggested but not provided. For larger-scale settings, Sinkhorn or sliced variants are likely preferable.

Quality
Technically sound within its scope; the equivalence to empirical W1 is correct. However, claiming an “unbiased stochastic gradient” (Eq. 3) deserves a formal justification via Danskin’s theorem (uniqueness/stability of the optimizer) and a clear acknowledgment that Eq. 4 introduces bias. The empirical studies are careful in parts (sensitivity, timing), but the experimental comparisons are too limited for strong claims.

Clarity
Overall clearly written and organized. Suggestions:
- Explicitly name PMD as empirical W1/EMD with uniform marginals early on to avoid confusion.
- Cite and discuss Danskin’s theorem for differentiating through argmin and clarify when the matching can change and how that affects gradients.
- Better separate “population” vs “mini-batch” terminology.

Originality
Incremental. The main contribution is an engineering recipe for using assignment-based empirical W1 as a deep learning loss, with some practical heuristics. The core idea and theory are established in OT.

Significance
Moderate at best in its current form. The idea is useful, but without comparisons to state-of-the-art OT approximations (Sinkhorn, sliced) and stronger baselines, the impact is limited.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple method that can be useful in practice.
- Empirical evidence that PMD can outperform/tune easier than MMD in some settings.
- Useful analysis of gradient magnitude and batch-size effects.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited novelty; largely rebranding empirical W1.
- Important related work and baselines missing (Sinkhorn OT, sliced OT, OT-based DA, WGAN-GP).
- Weak quantitative evaluation for generative modeling; modest gains in DA.
- Insufficient theory beyond standard consistency; no discussion of high-dimensional sample complexity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Position PMD explicitly as empirical W1/EMD; add citations to Cuturi (Sinkhorn), Peyré/Cuturi OT monograph, sliced Wasserstein, and OT-based domain adaptation (Courty et al.).
- Compare empirically to Sinkhorn OT (with different ε), sliced Wasserstein, and WGAN(-GP) on generative tasks; report FID/IS and training stability.
- Include stronger DA baselines (DANN, CORAL, JAN, CMD) and, if possible, an OT-based DA method.
- Provide a formal justification via Danskin’s theorem for Eq. (3), and discuss the bias/variance introduced by fixing the matching and sub-sampling (Eq. 4).
- Discuss and, if possible, mitigate the curse of dimensionality (e.g., feature-space costs, learned embeddings).
- Provide (or at least report) a GPU implementation or Sinkhorn alternative to demonstrate scalability.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The paper is clear and potentially useful, but the novelty is limited and the experimental/theoretical coverage relative to the OT literature is too narrow for NeurIPS acceptance.
