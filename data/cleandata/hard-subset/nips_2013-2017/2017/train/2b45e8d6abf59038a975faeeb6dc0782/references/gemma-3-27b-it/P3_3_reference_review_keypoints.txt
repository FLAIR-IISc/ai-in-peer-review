- Overall: PMD is a compelling, practical alternative to MMD with theoretical and empirical advantages; recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Summary & related work: Proposes PMD (Wasserstein-based) to estimate distribution distances, avoiding MMD’s bandwidth/gradient issues, with strong consistency and better empirical performance.  
- Strength — Theoretical soundness: Theorem 3.3 proves strong consistency and a clear connection to the Wasserstein metric.  
- Strength — Practical advantages: Parameter-free (no bandwidth), stronger gradients, and potential for smaller mini-batches.  
- Strength — Empirical validation: Outperforms MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling; faster convergence shown.  
- Strength — Presentation & analysis: Well-organized, includes helpful pseudocode and detailed ablation studies (population size, batch size, matching algorithm).  
- Weakness — Computational cost: Matching complexity may be a bottleneck for large sample sizes; need more discussion of efficient implementations.  
- Weakness — Distance metric choice: Exclusive use of L1 distance may limit applicability; deeper discussion of metric selection is needed.  
- Weakness — Experimental scope: Experiments are limited in scope; broader domains/models would strengthen claims.  
- Weakness — Approximation effects: More detail required on how approximations for large N impact results.  
- Detailed comment — Gradients (Sec 2.2): Justification that matching doesn’t change under infinitesimal θ changes feels hand-wavy; needs rigorous justification or citation.  
- Detailed comment — Intuition (Sec 3.1/3.2): Provide a more intuitive explanation of why PMD is a good practical estimator for readers unfamiliar with optimal transport.  
- Detailed comment — Time complexity (Sec 5.3): Include concrete runtime numbers (table) for different N and matching algorithms.  
- Detailed comment — Fig 2(a): Report standard deviation alongside relative approximation error.  
- Detailed comment — Fig 4: Provide quantitative image quality metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) in addition to qualitative images.  
- Pro acceptance arguments: Theoretically sound, parameter-free, better gradient behavior and convergence, strong empirical results, and clear writing.  
- Con acceptance arguments: Matching computational cost, limited discussion of alternative metrics, and narrower experimental evaluation.  
- Recommendation & required revisions: Accept with minor revisions — add runtime numbers, expand distance-metric discussion, broaden experiments, and provide a more rigorous justification for the gradient calculation.
