

# Peer Review: "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary and Context
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence defined as the cost of minimum weight matching between two samples from distributions. The authors establish PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, propose it as a training objective for deep learning, and demonstrate advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in domain adaptation and generative modeling. The work builds on recent advances in optimal transport for machine learning, with connections to Wasserstein GANs and MMD-based approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper correctly establishes the relationship between PMD and the Wasserstein metric through Propositions 3.1-3.3. The proof of strong consistency is sound and well-motivated, providing a solid theoretical basis for the proposed method.

2. **Practical Advantages**: The paper convincingly demonstrates three key advantages of PMD over MMD: (1) no bandwidth hyperparameter, (2) stronger gradients (supported by empirical gradient magnitude analysis in Fig. 2b), and (3) smaller mini-batch size requirements (|B|=100 vs. N=1000 for MMD). The gradient analysis is particularly valuable.

3. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) provide reasonable empirical support for the claims, with PMD showing clear advantages in convergence speed and performance on certain tasks.

## Weaknesses and Critical Issues

1. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper claims PMD is "a strongly consistent estimator" but fails to provide finite-sample error bounds. The empirical approximation error (Fig. 2a) decreases slowly (N^(-0.23)), which is much slower than the typical O(1/√N) rate. This could severely limit practical applicability, especially for large-scale problems.

2. **Computational Complexity**: While the paper mentions the O(N³) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, it doesn't adequately address the practical impact. For N=1000 (typical for MMD), the Hungarian algorithm would take seconds per iteration, making PMD significantly slower than MMD. The paper claims "the time complexity for computing matching is still acceptable," but provides no comparative timing analysis of the full training process.

3. **Inadequate Related Work**: The paper fails to properly contextualize with recent optimal transport methods. It mentions WGAN but doesn't address how PMD differs from or relates to the Sinkhorn approximation (a more efficient O(N²) method) or the recent work on differentiable optimal transport. The comparison with WGAN is superficial and misleading.

4. **Generative Modeling Limitations**: The claim that PMD "generates less noisy images" (Section 5.2) is questionable. The generated images (Fig. 4) for PMD appear blurry compared to MMD, which contradicts the claim. The paper attributes this to "pixel-level L1 distance not being good for natural images" but doesn't explore better distance metrics or demonstrate how PMD could be improved.

5. **Missing Comparisons**: The paper compares only to MMD, not to other state-of-the-art methods like WGAN, DCGAN, or the more recent MMD variants (e.g., GMMN). For generative modeling, the comparison should include a broader range of baselines.

## Specific Recommendations

1. **Add finite-sample error bounds**: The paper should provide theoretical error bounds for PMD to better justify its practical use.

2. **Include comprehensive timing analysis**: Compare the total training time per epoch for PMD (with both exact and approximate matching) against MMD, including the cost of the matching algorithm versus the cost of gradient computation.

3. **Expand related work**: Properly discuss recent advances in optimal transport for machine learning, particularly the Sinkhorn approximation and its relation to PMD. Address how PMD differs from WGAN and whether they could be combined.

4. **Clarify gradient discontinuity**: The paper notes "the gradient does not propagate through the matching" but doesn't discuss the implications of this discontinuity for optimization. This could explain some of the convergence issues observed in the experiments.

5. **Improve generative modeling analysis**: Provide a clearer explanation of why PMD appears to generate blurrier images and investigate alternative distance metrics that might address this issue.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents an interesting idea that connects minimum weight matching to statistical divergence estimation. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the experimental results support the claim that PMD has practical advantages over MMD in certain aspects. However, the theoretical limitations (slow convergence rate), computational concerns (high matching complexity), and inadequate comparison with recent methods significantly weaken the contribution.

The paper is technically sound but falls short of making a strong contribution to the field. While the method could be useful in specific scenarios (e.g., small-scale problems where gradient strength is critical), the limitations make it less generally applicable than the authors suggest.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept** (borderline)

The paper presents a novel application of minimum weight matching to statistical divergence estimation with some practical advantages over MMD. However, the theoretical limitations (slow approximation rate), computational concerns (high matching complexity), and inadequate comparison with recent advances in optimal transport prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS. With significant revisions addressing the theoretical gaps, computational analysis, and related work, this could be a strong paper. As is, it's a marginal contribution that would benefit from more rigorous analysis and comparison with the state-of-the-art.
