- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric using minimum-weight matching between samples.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance.  
- Proposes a stochastic gradient descent algorithm that uses PMD as a training objective for deep models.  
- Claims practical advantages over MMD: no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and compatibility with normal mini-batch sizes.  
- Empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling show improved performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.  
- Offers a novel alternative to existing approaches (MMD, WGAN) without requiring a critic network.  
- Paper is clearly written with well-explained mathematical definitions and algorithms.  
- Empirical analysis of gradient magnitudes (Fig. 2b) supports the stronger-gradient claim.  
- Computational cost of the matching algorithm is a potential bottleneck; GPU parallelization is suggested but not demonstrated.  
- Use of L1 distance within PMD may be restrictive; impact of other distance metrics is not systematically explored.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope (only two tasks) and results can be sensitive to architecture choices.  
- Connection to WGAN and explanation of why PMD avoids WGAN scaling issues are insufficiently articulated.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, original, and significant; strengths outweigh weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: accept.  
- Camera-ready suggestions: provide detailed computational-complexity analysis and GPU optimization results, explore alternative distance metrics, expand experiments to more datasets/tasks, and clarify the relation to WGAN.
