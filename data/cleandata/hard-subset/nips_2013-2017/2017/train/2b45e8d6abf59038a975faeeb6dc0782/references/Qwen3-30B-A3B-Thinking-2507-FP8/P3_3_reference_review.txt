

# Peer Review: "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary and Context

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sets of samples from two distributions, as a sample-based statistical divergence. The authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, and propose it as a training objective for deep learning tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling. They argue PMD has advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD): no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements. The paper makes a clear theoretical connection to Wasserstein metrics and provides empirical comparisons with MMD on standard benchmarks.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The paper correctly establishes PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), building on Varadarajan's Theorem and stability of optimal transport. This connection is well-motivated and technically sound.

2. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The paper precisely defines the problem of estimating statistical distances from samples, and the limitations of existing methods (MMD's bandwidth sensitivity, gradient vanishing, large batch requirements) are well-articulated.

3. **Well-Designed Experiments**: The empirical evaluation on standard benchmarks (Office dataset for domain adaptation, MNIST/SVHN/LFW for generative modeling) is thorough and properly designed. The comparison with MMD is fair, with appropriate hyperparameter tuning.

4. **Practical Insights**: The paper provides valuable empirical insights about gradient behavior (Figure 2b) and sensitivity to population size (Figure 5), which are important for practitioners.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Related Work**: The paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from recent work on Wasserstein approximation. The connection to WGAN [3] and Cramer GAN [Bellemare et al., 2017] is superficial. Unlike WGAN, PMD does not require a separate critic network, but the paper doesn't address why this is preferable to the WGAN approach (which has shown strong empirical performance). The paper also doesn't acknowledge that the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) used in some generative models is conceptually similar to PMD.

2. **Misleading Claims About "Stronger Gradients"**: The paper claims PMD has "stronger gradients" than MMD, but this is misleading. While Figure 2b shows more uniform gradient magnitudes for PMD, this doesn't necessarily translate to better convergence properties. The gradient magnitude for PMD (|sgn(x_i - y_{M_i})|) is constant in magnitude, but this doesn't mean it's "stronger" in terms of optimization dynamics. The paper doesn't provide evidence that this leads to faster convergence beyond the empirical results shown.

3. **Computational Complexity Concerns**: The paper's claim that "the time complexity for computing matching is still acceptable comparing with the cost of training neural networks" is unsubstantiated. While the Hungarian algorithm is O(N^3), the paper doesn't provide timing comparisons with MMD (O(N^2)) for the same hardware setup. Figure 5(c) shows that exact matching takes comparable time to SGD, but this is for small N (N=500). For larger N (common in deep learning), the cubic complexity would become prohibitive. The paper's claim about GPU acceleration is vague and lacks concrete timing data.

4. **Incomplete Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides a relative approximation error analysis (Figure 2a) but lacks finite-sample error bounds for PMD. The rate of convergence (N^(-0.23)) is not compared to MMD's convergence rate, which would be crucial for understanding the trade-offs.

5. **Limited Scope of Applications**: The paper only evaluates PMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling, but doesn't explore other relevant applications where statistical divergence estimation is critical (e.g., reinforcement learning, meta-learning).

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to statistical divergence estimation with potentially useful practical advantages over MMD. The core idea of using minimum weight matching as a discrepancy measure is intuitive and well-motivated. However, the paper's contribution is somewhat incremental given the recent advances in Wasserstein approximation (WGAN, Cramer GAN), and the claimed advantages are not sufficiently supported by theoretical analysis or comprehensive empirical comparison.

The most significant weakness is the paper's failure to adequately address the computational cost of the matching step, which is a major practical concern for large-scale deep learning applications. The paper also makes a misleading claim about "stronger gradients" without sufficient justification.

## Recommendation

I recommend *reject* for NIPS. While the paper is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem, the contribution is not sufficiently novel or impactful to justify acceptance at NIPS. The main novelty (applying minimum weight matching as a discrepancy measure) is incremental compared to recent work on Wasserstein approximation, and the paper's claims about advantages over MMD are not fully supported by the evidence provided.

The paper would need to substantially strengthen its theoretical analysis (including finite-sample error bounds), provide more comprehensive comparisons with WGAN/Cramer GAN, and address the computational complexity concerns to be competitive for NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Compare PMD more thoroughly with WGAN and Cramer GAN, not just MMD.
2. Provide concrete timing comparisons between PMD and MMD for larger population sizes (N > 1000).
3. Clarify the "stronger gradients" claim with a proper analysis of optimization dynamics.
4. Include finite-sample error bounds for PMD.
5. Discuss how to handle the computational bottleneck of the matching step for large-scale applications.

The paper has potential but requires significant strengthening to make a compelling contribution to the field.
