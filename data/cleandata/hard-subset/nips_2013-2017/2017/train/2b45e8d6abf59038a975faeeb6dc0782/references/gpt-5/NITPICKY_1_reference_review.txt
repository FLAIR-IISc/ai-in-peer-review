Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum average pairwise distance under a bipartite matching between two equal-sized samples from two distributions. With d as a metric (e.g., L1), DN(X, Y) equals the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (a.k.a. EMD) between the uniform empirical measures. The authors (i) restate that DN is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 via standard OT stability and weak convergence, (ii) propose to use PMD as a training objective for parametric generative models and for domain adaptation by computing matchings between minibatches, (iii) suggest practical approximations: approximate O(N^2) matching and reusing a fixed matching for several SGD steps, and (iv) empirically compare PMD to MMD on Office (domain adaptation) and MNIST/SVHN/LFW (generation), claiming better performance and faster convergence, plus qualitative advantages (stronger gradients, smaller effective minibatches).

Relation to prior work: The objective coincides with the classical OT/EMD objective on empirical distributions. There is significant related work on computational OT for ML (entropic regularization/Sinkhorn distances), OT-based generative modeling (WGAN, Sinkhorn variants), and sliced-Wasserstein distances, much of which is unaddressed. Known statistical rates for empirical W1 (e.g., N−1/d) are not discussed. Existing linear-time unbiased MMD estimators are also omitted.

Strengths
- Simple, clear objective; useful reminder that the assignment-based empirical W1 is directly usable as a loss.
- Empirical observation that gradients are more uniformly strong than with RBF-MMD (for the chosen configuration) is interesting and potentially practically relevant.
- The idea to decouple large population size N (for matching quality) and smaller |B| (for SGD updates) is pragmatic, and empirical results suggest it can work.
- Experiments show PMD matching-based training yields sensible generations on simple datasets and competitive performance on some Office tasks.
- The consistency discussion links PMD to W1 correctly at a high level.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical novelty:
- PMD is exactly the empirical W1/EMD on uniform samples with N=M (Villani 2009; Rubner et al.). The paper largely rebrands a standard quantity, then applies it as a loss. The theoretical result (Thm 3.3) follows directly from well-known results (Varadarajan’s theorem and OT stability). No new bounds, rates, or algorithmic theory are provided.
- Major related work is missing:
  - Entropic OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) and subsequent scalable/differentiable OT (e.g., Greenkhorn, stochastic OT, Sinkhorn divergences). These are the canonical ways to use W1-like objectives with automatic differentiation and GPU.
  - Sliced Wasserstein distances and their use in generative modeling and autoencoders (e.g., Rabin/Bonneel/Kolouri).
  - Statistical rates for empirical Wp, e.g., Fournier & Guillin (2015), Weed & Bach (2017), del Barrio et al., Ajtai-Komlós-Tusnády, etc. The empirical scaling in Fig. 2(a) is consistent with N−1/d but this connection is not made.
  - Linear-time/unbiased MMD estimators and multi-kernel MMD tuning strategies that mitigate gradient weakness.
  - Recent OT-based generative methods beyond WGAN (e.g., Sinkhorn GAN/Autoencoder variants).
Methodological rigor:
- Gradient computation: Eq. (3) claims an “unbiased stochastic gradient” and argues “the gradient does not propagate through the matching” because the optimal matching is locally constant under infinitesimal θ perturbations. This needs a rigorous justification (e.g., Danskin’s/Envelope theorem) with assumptions (uniqueness of minimizer, absence of ties, piecewise smoothness), and a discussion of subgradients at boundaries where M changes. As written, the unbiasedness claim is not substantiated. The inner-loop reuse of a fixed M and the subsampled approximation in Eq. (4) introduce additional bias; the paper provides no convergence analysis or conditions under which this bias is harmless.
- Objective non-smoothness: L1 with hard assignment creates kinked objectives. Practical stability when matchings change is not analyzed.
- Complexity and scalability: O(N^3) Hungarian is infeasible for N≈1000+ in the inner loop; authors switch to an O(N^2) approximate matching on CPU. However, comparisons to MMD do not control for optimized GPU implementations of both sides. Entropic OT (GPU) is a highly relevant baseline, both in speed and differentiation quality.
Experimental evaluation:
- Generative modeling is evaluated only qualitatively. No standard metrics (FID, Inception score, NDB, PRD), no precision/recall, no sample diversity tests, and no likelihood proxies. Without quantitative evaluation and stronger baselines (DCGAN, WGAN(-GP), Cramér GAN), the significance is unclear.
- MMD baselines: For domain adaptation, σ is validated on labeled target data (100 labels). This is semi-supervised model selection and weakens the unsupervised claim; at minimum it should be applied equally to PMD hyperparameters (and stated clearly). For generation, kernel choices are partially fixed from GMMN rather than tuned; linear-time MMD or multi-scale kernels could alter conclusions. The choice of N=2000 for MMD but |B|=100 for PMD also confounds compute fairness.
- Domain adaptation baselines: Only MMD is compared. Stronger baselines exist (DANN, JAN, CMD, DSN, etc.). Without these, improvements over the state of the art are not demonstrated.
- Fairness and stopping: PMD trained for 100 epochs vs MMD for 500; stopping criteria are not harmonized. Wall-clock comparisons should include optimized GPU code for all methods.
- Feature space distances: The paper notes pixel L1 causes blur, and moves to AE codes. Using perceptual features (e.g., convnet features) or learned critics is a standard remedy; comparison to such distances would be informative.
- Reproducibility: Key details are missing or scattered: frequency of recomputing M, inner-loop length, learning rates/optimizers per task, random seeds, exact architecture specs, and whether code will be released.
Presentation and minor issues:
- Numerous typos/grammar: e.g., “MMD have,” “st Conference…”, inconsistent math formatting (TXθX vs T X θX), missing spaces/superscripts, inconsistent notation for p̂, etc.
- References are incomplete or malformed (missing venues/years; several arXiv only; some key omissions noted above).
- The name “PMD” risks confusion with standard EMD/Wasserstein; the paper should clearly acknowledge it is the empirical W1 on equal-weight samples and justify the new terminology.
- Theoretical section should state the integrability conditions ensuring W1(pX, pY) < ∞ and specify the space (R^d vs general Polish) consistently.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple method likely useful in practice.
- Empirical evidence that hard matching can yield stronger gradients and allow smaller effective minibatches.
- Applicability to two tasks; some wins over a common baseline (MMD).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to well-known empirical OT; main theorem and algorithmic ideas are standard or require more rigor.
- Missing key related work (especially Sinkhorn/entropic OT, sliced Wasserstein, statistical rates).
- Methodological gaps (biased gradients, non-smoothness, lack of convergence analysis).
- Weak experimental design: limited baselines, no quantitative metrics for generation, fairness issues.

Recommendation: Reject
While the idea is sensible and potentially useful, the contribution is incremental over established OT/EMD, the theoretical claims are under-justified, important related work and baselines are missing, and the experimental evaluation is not strong enough for NeurIPS. Strengthening theory (rigorous gradient analysis; discussion of sample complexity), adding modern OT baselines (Sinkhorn, sliced), fair GPU-accelerated comparisons, and quantitative generative evaluations would substantially improve the paper.
