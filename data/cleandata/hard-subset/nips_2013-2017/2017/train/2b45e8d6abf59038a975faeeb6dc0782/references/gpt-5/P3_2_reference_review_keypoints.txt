- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) = minimum average pairwise distance under bipartite matching, equivalent to empirical W1 and shown strongly consistent.
- Suggests using PMD as a differentiable training objective with heuristics: reuse matchings across SGD steps and subsample matched pairs for efficiency.
- Strength: Correct connection to Wasserstein-1 and classical equivalence between empirical OT and assignment, with a simple, appealing training heuristic.
- Strength: Useful discussion comparing PMD vs MMD gradients; RBF saturation and outlier robustness intuition supported by gradient magnitude analysis.
- Strength: Parameter-light (no kernel bandwidth) and competitive empirical results on Office with timing and sensitivity analysis provided.
- Weakness: Novelty limited — PMD is empirical EMD/W1 and consistency/matching formulations are classical; the paper largely rebrands known concepts.
- Weakness: Missing key related work (entropic/Sinkhorn OT, OT for domain adaptation, sample complexity/rates, stochastic OT) that address differentiability and speed.
- Weakness: Weak baselines in generative modeling — lacks comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP and Cramér distance, limiting empirical significance.
- Weakness: Gradient treatment ignores dependence of optimal matching on parameters; reusing/subsampling matchings likely introduces bias that is unanalyzed.
- Weakness: Computational claims overlook that exact matching is O(N^3), pairwise distances are O(N^2D), and Sinkhorn offers fast, parallelizable alternatives not compared.
- Weakness: Evaluation lacks standard quantitative metrics for generative models (FID/IS), fair representation comparisons, and statistical significance across runs.
- Weakness: DA hyperparameter selection used 100 labeled target examples, deviating from strict unsupervised protocol and risking bias.
- Weakness: Some claims overstate MMD limitations — linear-time estimators and learned kernels mitigate issues raised.
- Quality: Theoretical results are correct but incremental; optimization heuristic is reasonable but lacks theoretical justification on bias/variance and stability.
- Quality: Experiments provide insight but miss strong baselines and standard metrics, limiting the strength of conclusions.
- Clarity: Paper is readable but needs expanded, precise relation to existing OT literature and acknowledgment of ground metric choice.
- Clarity: Must clarify rematching schedule, inner-loop steps, and provide pseudocode detailing training procedure.
- Originality: Using empirical W1 as a loss is not new; novelty mainly in heuristics (reusing/subsampling matchings) with moderate originality.
- Significance: Practical idea with potential — match-based couplings for stronger gradients and smaller mini-batches — but impact uncertain without broader comparisons.
- Suggestion: Add comparisons to Sinkhorn (entropic OT) and WGAN-GP/Cramér GAN with standard metrics (FID/IS).
- Suggestion: Analyze and ablate bias/variance from subsampling matched pairs and rematching frequency.
- Suggestion: Discuss or adopt GPU-accelerated matching or Sinkhorn for scalability.
- Suggestion: Evaluate PMD in learned/perceptual feature spaces for fair image comparisons and to avoid pixel-space blurriness.
- Suggestion: Report variance across runs, perform statistical tests, and clarify hyperparameter selection in DA.
- Suggestion: Cite and discuss missing related work (Cuturi 2013; Courty et al.; Fournier–Guillin; Genevay et al.; MMD-GAN; WGAN-GP).
- Pros: Clear formulation, correct W1 linkage, implementable training heuristic, parameter-light, yields stronger per-sample gradients.
- Cons: Limited novelty, missing key related work and baselines, unanalyzed bias from heuristics and potential scalability issues.
- Overall: Interesting practical approach with promise, but requires stronger positioning against OT/adversarial baselines and deeper empirical/theoretical support.
