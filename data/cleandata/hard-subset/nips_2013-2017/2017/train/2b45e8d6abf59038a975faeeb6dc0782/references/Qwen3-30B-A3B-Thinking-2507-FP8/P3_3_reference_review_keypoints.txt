- Paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): minimum weight matching between sample sets as a sample-based divergence and estimator of the 1-Wasserstein metric.  
- Claims PMD is strongly consistent and suitable as a training objective for domain adaptation and generative modeling.  

Strengths:
- Theoretical foundation: proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3).  
- Clear problem formulation and articulation of MMD limitations (bandwidth sensitivity, vanishing gradients, large batch needs).  
- Well-designed experiments on standard benchmarks with fair MMD comparisons and hyperparameter tuning.  
- Provides practical empirical insights on gradient behavior and sensitivity to population size.  

Weaknesses / Concerns:
- Insufficient differentiation from related Wasserstein-based work (WGAN, Cramer GAN, EMD-based models) and lack of discussion why PMD is preferable.  
- Misleading "stronger gradients" claim: uniform gradient magnitude for PMD does not necessarily imply better optimization or faster convergence.  
- Computational complexity concerns: matching (Hungarian) is O(N^3); paper lacks concrete timing comparisons with MMD and vague GPU acceleration claims.  
- Incomplete theoretical analysis: no finite-sample error bounds and no comparison of PMD convergence rate to MMD.  
- Limited application scope: evaluations restricted to domain adaptation and generative modeling, missing other relevant areas (e.g., RL, meta-learning).  

Overall assessment:
- Technically sound and intuitive idea but incremental relative to recent Wasserstein approximation advances and lacking in theoretical and empirical support for claimed advantages.  
- Major practical concern is the computational cost of matching for large-scale deep learning.  

Recommendation:
- Reviewer recommends reject for NIPS due to limited novelty, insufficient theoretical/empirical support, and computational concerns.  

Specific suggestions:
- Provide thorough comparisons with WGAN and Cramer GAN.  
- Add concrete timing comparisons with MMD for larger population sizes (N > 1000).  
- Analyze and clarify the optimization implications of PMD gradients.  
- Derive finite-sample error bounds and compare convergence rates to MMD.  
- Propose or evaluate methods to mitigate the matching computational bottleneck for large-scale use.
