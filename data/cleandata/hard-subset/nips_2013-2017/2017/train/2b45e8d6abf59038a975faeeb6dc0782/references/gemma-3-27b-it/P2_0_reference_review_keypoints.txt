- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric using minimum-cost matching between sample populations.  
- Claims PMD avoids MMD’s bandwidth tuning, yields stronger gradients, and allows smaller mini-batches for faster convergence.  
- Theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is clearly established, with proofs of strong consistency.  
- Empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling show consistent, though modest, improvements over MMD.  
- PMD is a novel and well-motivated approach that extends optimal transport ideas into deep learning training objectives.  
- Practical advantages include no bandwidth parameter, improved gradient behavior, and reduced mini-batch requirements.  
- Experiments provide convincing initial validation but are not exhaustive or on highly challenging datasets.  
- Core PMD relies on solving a matching problem; exact Hungarian algorithm scales cubically and may bottleneck large datasets.  
- Approximate matching algorithms are discussed but their empirical performance and error characteristics are not thoroughly analyzed.  
- Paper lacks a detailed practical error analysis of the approximation to the Wasserstein metric.  
- Writing, organization, and notation are clear and concise.  
- Overall contribution is significant and technically sound, warranting acceptance despite limitations.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, practical benefits, empirical improvements, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: modest gains, computational cost concerns, and limited empirical scope.  
- Overall recommendation: Accept.
