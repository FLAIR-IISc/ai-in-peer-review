

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based statistical divergence that estimates the first Wasserstein metric by computing the minimum weight matching between two sample populations. The authors establish PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, and propose its use as a training objective in deep learning tasks. The core novelty lies in leveraging combinatorial optimization (minimum weight matching) for statistical divergence estimation, contrasting with the popular MMD approach.

The paper's main contributions are threefold: (1) introducing PMD as a new statistical divergence with theoretical guarantees; (2) demonstrating its advantages over MMD (no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size); and (3) applying PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling with empirical evidence of superior performance.

This work stands out from existing literature on statistical divergences (like MMD [17], WGAN [3], and other integral probability metrics [17]) by offering a fundamentally different approach to measuring distributional distance. While the Wasserstein metric has been previously studied in machine learning [40, 49], PMD provides a computationally feasible approximation that avoids the limitations of both MMD and WGAN. The theoretical analysis is sound, with a clear proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3) that builds on established optimal transport theory. The authors effectively contrast PMD with MMD in terms of gradient properties (Fig. 2b) and computational requirements (Sec. 3.2), providing strong motivation for their approach.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates PMD's advantages across two challenging domains: domain adaptation on Office dataset (Table 1, Fig. 3) and generative modeling on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW (Fig. 4, Sec. 5.2). The results consistently show PMD outperforms MMD in both performance and convergence speed, with PMD requiring fewer hyperparameters (only λ vs. λ and σ for MMD). The ablation studies (Fig. 5) further validate the key claims about mini-batch size and population size.

While the paper has some minor weaknesses, they do not undermine its core contribution. The computational complexity of the Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)) is acknowledged, and the authors appropriately use approximated matching for larger N. The generative modeling results on SVHN/LFW show blurry images, which is a limitation but not a fundamental flaw—this could be addressed by using feature space matching rather than pixel space, as the authors suggest. The paper doesn't compare with WGAN in detail, but this is understandable given the focus on sample-based divergences.

What makes this paper stand out is its bold conceptual shift: rather than tweaking existing divergences, the authors propose a completely new approach grounded in combinatorial optimization. This is precisely the kind of novel direction NIPS values—moving beyond incremental improvements to establish a new conceptual framework. The paper's strength lies in its clean mathematical formulation, solid theoretical grounding, and convincing empirical validation of the key claims.

The paper successfully demonstrates that PMD offers a practical alternative to MMD with significant advantages. The claim that PMD "has no bandwidth hyper-parameter, has stronger gradient, and can use normal mini-batch size" is well-supported by both theory and experiments. This is not just another variant of MMD but a fundamentally different approach to statistical divergence estimation.

In my view, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of statistical learning and deep learning. The novelty is substantial, the theoretical foundation is strong, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate advantages over the current state-of-the-art (MMD). While there are minor limitations (computational complexity for large N, some image quality issues), these do not detract from the core novelty and significance of the work.

Given NIPS's emphasis on novel ideas over methodological perfection, this paper deserves strong consideration. The authors have identified a meaningful gap in the statistical divergence landscape and proposed a compelling new approach. The paper is well-written, clear, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The main arguments for acceptance:
- Introduces a genuinely novel statistical divergence (PMD) with strong theoretical guarantees
- Demonstrates clear advantages over the dominant MMD approach
- Provides compelling empirical evidence across multiple deep learning tasks
- Offers practical implementation with reasonable computational requirements
- Addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning (distributional distance estimation)

The main arguments against acceptance are minimal:
- Minor computational concerns with the Hungarian algorithm (addressed in the paper)
- Some generative modeling results show blurry images (a limitation, not a flaw in the core idea)

In conclusion, this paper presents a bold, novel approach to a fundamental problem in machine learning. The contribution is significant, the theoretical foundation is solid, and the empirical validation is convincing. I strongly recommend acceptance.
