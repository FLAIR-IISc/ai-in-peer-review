Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance constructed as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets from the distributions. The authors show that PMD coincides with the optimal transport (OT) distance (Earth Mover’s Distance) between empirical distributions and, leveraging weak convergence and stability results (Varadarajan; Villani), argue PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the population W1. They propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective by (i) computing the optimal matching on a population of samples, (ii) performing several SGD steps on parameters while holding the discrete matching fixed, and (iii) optionally sub-sampling matched pairs for minibatch updates. They compare PMD with MMD, arguing PMD has no kernel bandwidth, provides stronger gradients, and can use smaller minibatches. Experiments on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) suggest PMD converges faster and yields competitive or better performance than MMD.

This work relates to extensive literature on OT/Wasserstein distances for learning, including EMD for histograms (Rubner et al.), OT for domain adaptation, and recent Wasserstein GANs (Arjovsky et al.). The paper contrasts with MMD-based training (Li et al.; Dziugaite et al.) and mentions Cramér distance (Bellemare et al.). However, it appears to omit key developments on fast/differentiable OT via entropic regularization and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) and sliced-Wasserstein approaches, as well as linear-time unbiased MMD estimators (Gretton et al., 2012), which are directly relevant to claims about scalability and minibatching.

Quality
- Theory: The identification of PMD with empirical OT is standard; the consistency argument is sound at a high level. However, Theorem 3.3 is stated strongly (a.s. convergence of γN to a single γ). Stability usually guarantees convergence of subsequences to some optimal plan; uniqueness conditions are not discussed. No finite-sample error bounds are provided, despite well-known rates for empirical Wp (e.g., Fournier & Guillin; Weed & Bach), which would contextualize Fig. 2(a).
- Optimization: The training algorithm treats the matching as piecewise-constant w.r.t. parameters and ignores derivatives through the matching. This yields a biased/stale gradient when the permutation changes across updates. The paper assumes the matching “does not change much,” but provides no convergence analysis or sensitivity study on this assumption.
- Computational aspects: Exact matching is O(N^3), approximate matching O(N^2). Memory/time implications of storing O(N^2) pairwise distances are not fully analyzed; GPU acceleration is suggested but not implemented. Related, widely used alternatives (Sinkhorn OT with automatic differentiation) are not compared.
- Experiments: 
  - Domain adaptation: The setup uses precomputed VGG features and tunes λ and σ using 100 labeled target samples. While common, this weakens the “unsupervised” claim and complicates external validity. Comparisons are only to MMD; stronger baselines (e.g., CORAL, DANN, JAN, CMD, OT-based DA) are missing. Gains are modest on several shifts.
  - Generative modeling: Comparisons are limited to MMD; no GAN/WGAN/WGAN-GP baselines. Evaluation is primarily visual; no standard metrics (Inception Score, FID) are reported. Authors note blurriness with pixel-wise L1, and show improvements when training on AE codes. Claims about faster convergence are plausible but not supported by broader baselines.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with intuitive explanations and clear algorithms. Some claims about MMD (e.g., “mini-batch size must be large” or “must equal population size”) overstate the case; linear-time unbiased MMD estimators allow small batches, though variance can be high. A more nuanced discussion would improve clarity and fairness.

Originality
Using empirical OT (assignment/EMD) as a training objective is natural and has been explored in related forms. The paper’s contribution is an applied recipe (match-and-update with minibatch subsampling) and empirical demonstration versus MMD in two tasks. Given existing OT literature (EMD, Sinkhorn, OT for DA, WGAN), novelty is moderate and would benefit from positioning against entropic OT and sliced-Wasserstein approaches.

Significance
The idea is simple, implementable, and likely useful. Showing that matching-based OT can provide stronger gradients than MMD and work with smaller minibatches is of practical interest. However, the empirical scope is narrow, missing stronger baselines, modern evaluation, and scalability demonstrations. Without these, the potential impact is limited.

Pros
- Clear, intuitive formulation; consistent estimator of W1.
- Practical training recipe; empirical evidence of stronger gradients.
- Demonstrates faster convergence vs MMD on selected tasks.
- Ablations on matching algorithm and batch sizes are informative.

Cons
- Missing key related work and baselines (Sinkhorn OT; OT-based DA; WGAN/WGAN-GP).
- Optimization uses stale/biasing matchings; no analysis of induced bias or convergence.
- Claims about MMD minibatch requirements are overstated; no use of linear-time MMD.
- Limited evaluation: small datasets, visuals over standard metrics, modest gains in DA with target labels used for tuning.
- Computational scalability and memory not convincingly addressed.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
1) Include and compare against entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) with autodiff and GPU; discuss bias-variance tradeoffs and speed. Consider the Sinkhorn divergence that debiases entropic OT.
2) Provide finite-sample error rates or cite known bounds for empirical W1 to contextualize Fig. 2(a).
3) Analyze the effect of stale matchings: quantify how often permutations change, measure training bias/variance, and, if possible, provide convergence guarantees under reasonable assumptions.
4) Correct and nuance statements about MMD. Include linear-time MMD estimators and recent MMD-GAN variants in comparisons.
5) Expand baselines: domain adaptation (DANN, JAN, CORAL, CMD, OT-based DA), generative modeling (DCGAN/WGAN/WGAN-GP, Cramér distance).
6) Use standard generative metrics (FID/IS) and higher-resolution datasets; report training time vs quality tradeoffs and memory footprint.
7) Discuss unbalanced OT variants for differing sample sizes; explore learned feature spaces or perceptual distances to mitigate blurriness.
8) Clarify conditions for convergence of γN; discuss non-uniqueness and subgradient issues due to non-smooth L1.

Recommendation: Reject (weak)
The paper presents a clean and potentially useful application of empirical OT to training, with some encouraging results. However, key related work and baselines are missing, some claims (esp. vs MMD) are overstated, theoretical and computational aspects are underdeveloped, and evaluation is limited. Addressing the above points would substantially strengthen the contribution.
