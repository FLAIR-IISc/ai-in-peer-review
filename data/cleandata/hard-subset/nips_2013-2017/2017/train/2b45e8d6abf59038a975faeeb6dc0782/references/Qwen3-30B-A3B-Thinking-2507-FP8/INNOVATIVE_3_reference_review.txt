

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel approach for estimating statistical distance between two probability distributions based on samples. The core idea is to use the cost of minimum weight matching between two random populations as a discrepancy measure, which the authors prove is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes using PMD as a training objective in deep learning tasks, particularly domain adaptation and generative modeling, and demonstrates advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch size requirements.

The paper's main contribution is highly novel. While the Wasserstein metric has been used in machine learning, the authors' approach of directly using the minimum weight matching as the discrepancy measure represents a fresh perspective that differs fundamentally from existing approaches like WGAN (which approximates the dual) and MMD (which relies on kernel methods). The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is well-established with a clear proof of strong consistency, and the practical advantages over MMD are demonstrated through thorough experiments.

The novelty of PMD is particularly compelling because it addresses a fundamental limitation of MMD: the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter and gradient saturation issues. The paper's theoretical analysis of the gradient magnitude (Fig. 2b) provides a strong conceptual justification for why PMD might work better in practice, which is a significant contribution beyond just empirical results. The authors successfully bridge the gap between theory and practice by showing how PMD can be implemented efficiently in deep learning pipelines, with the key insight that the matching doesn't need to be recomputed at every gradient step.

The experimental results are convincing. In domain adaptation (Table 1), PMD outperforms MMD on multiple tasks (a→w and a→d) by a large margin while requiring fewer hyperparameters to tune. The convergence speed advantage is clearly demonstrated in Fig. 3a. For generative modeling, while the images are described as "blurry" on SVHN and LFW, the paper correctly identifies that this is likely due to using pixel-level L1 distance rather than a more appropriate metric, and PMD still generates less noisy images than MMD across the board. The ablation studies in Fig. 5 provide strong evidence for the claimed advantages of smaller mini-batch size and robustness to population size.

I particularly appreciate the authors' clear articulation of how PMD differs from related work. The comparison with MMD (Section 3.2) is thorough and well-justified, and the distinction from WGAN (which requires a separate critic network) is well-made. The paper doesn't claim to be a replacement for all Wasserstein-based methods, but rather presents a specific, novel approach that solves particular problems with existing methods.

The minor limitations I noted don't detract significantly from the paper's novelty and contribution. The blurry images in generative modeling could be addressed by using a different distance metric (e.g., learned features rather than pixel space), which the authors acknowledge in their future work plans. The computational complexity of the matching problem (O(N³) for Hungarian algorithm) is acknowledged as potentially problematic for very large N, but the authors demonstrate that the approach is practical for N in the hundreds to thousands, which is sufficient for reliable estimation and is acceptable given that neural network training is typically more expensive.

This paper stands out as a bold, novel approach that addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning with a fresh perspective. While the paper is not perfect (e.g., the generative modeling results on some datasets could be improved), the novelty of the core concept and the clear advantages demonstrated over a widely-used method (MMD) make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The authors have provided a clear, well-motivated new direction in statistical distance estimation that could have significant impact on multiple areas of deep learning.

Strengths:
- Highly novel concept that addresses a fundamental problem in statistical distance estimation
- Strong theoretical foundation connecting PMD to Wasserstein metric
- Clear practical advantages over MMD (no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size)
- Thorough empirical validation across multiple tasks and datasets
- Well-written with clear explanations and good illustrations

Weaknesses:
- Generative modeling results on some datasets are blurry, though the authors correctly identify this as a metric issue rather than a PMD flaw
- Limited comparison with other Wasserstein-based approaches (like WGAN)
- Computational complexity of matching could be a concern for very large datasets, though the authors demonstrate it's acceptable for practical use

Overall, this paper introduces a genuinely novel approach with clear theoretical and practical advantages over existing methods. The core idea of using minimum weight matching as a discrepancy measure is simple, elegant, and has significant potential to influence how we estimate statistical distances in deep learning. Given the reviewer's emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, I recommend acceptance with high confidence. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
