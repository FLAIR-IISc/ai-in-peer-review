- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric that avoids kernel bandwidth tuning.  
- PMD leverages optimal transport concepts and directly minimizes matching cost between samples as an alternative to MMD.  
- The paper provides strong theoretical grounding, proving PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric.  
- PMD offers practical advantages: no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and effective performance with standard mini-batch sizes.  
- Empirical results show faster convergence and superior performance of PMD over MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.  
- Presentation is clear, well-organized, and supported with helpful pseudocode and notation.  
- Authors acknowledge computational cost of matching and propose an approximate O(N^2) algorithm for larger samples.  
- Main weakness: computational complexity (Hungarian O(N^3)) remains a bottleneck for very large datasets.  
- Reliance on L1 distance may not be optimal for all data types; alternative distance metrics are not explored.  
- Comparisons are limited to MMD; the paper lacks evaluation against other Wasserstein estimators and additional baselines.  
- Missing ablation studies on mini-batch size and exact vs. approximate matching limits understanding of parameter impacts.  
- Generative modeling outputs (SVHN, LFW) are blurry and lack deeper analysis or solutions.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, practical improvements over MMD, and convincing empirical validation.  
- Against acceptance: scalability concerns, narrow comparative scope, and blurry image results for complex data.  
- Overall assessment: strong, valuable contribution with minor concerns; recommended for acceptance.  
- Review rating: 8/10 (Strong Accept).
