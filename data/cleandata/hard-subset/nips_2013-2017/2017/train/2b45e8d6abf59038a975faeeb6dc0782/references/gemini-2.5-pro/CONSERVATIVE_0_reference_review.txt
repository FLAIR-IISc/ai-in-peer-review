This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence measure, and proposes its use as a training objective in deep learning. The core idea is to define the distance between two distributions as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between samples drawn from them. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper positions PMD as an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD has advantages such as having no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, providing stronger gradients, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes during optimization. The method is evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, where it is compared primarily against MMD.

**Strengths**

*   The proposed method is grounded in the well-established theory of optimal transport. The connection between the minimum weight matching of empirical samples and the first Wasserstein metric provides a solid theoretical motivation for PMD as a discrepancy measure.
*   The paper correctly identifies a key practical challenge with kernel-based methods like MMD: the sensitivity to the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., the RBF kernel bandwidth). PMD, by being based on a ground distance (e.g., L1), elegantly sidesteps this issue, which is a notable practical advantage.
*   The analysis of the gradient properties is insightful. The argument that MMD gradients can vanish when samples are either too far apart or too close together is a known issue, and the constant-magnitude gradient of PMD (with L1 distance) presents a potentially compelling alternative for more stable optimization.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Scalability:** A major concern is the computational cost and the proposed optimization scheme. The core of the method relies on solving a minimum weight matching problem, which costs O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the proposed approximation. This matching must be computed in each "outer loop" of training. For population sizes N in the thousands, as used in the experiments, this is a significant computational burden that may not scale to larger problems.
    The proposed solution—fixing the matching M* for several "inner loop" SGD updates—is a heuristic that lacks rigorous justification. The gradient in Eq. (3) is only valid for an infinitesimal change in parameters. After one or more gradient steps, the optimal matching M* may change. Using a stale matching leads to an incorrect and potentially biased gradient estimate. The paper provides no analysis on how many updates can be safely performed before the gradient estimate becomes poor, which is a critical detail for the method's soundness.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The paper's empirical evaluation focuses almost exclusively on MMD as a baseline. While MMD is a classic and relevant method, the field has advanced considerably. In the context of generative modeling, the most prominent and powerful baselines are now derived from Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), particularly the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) [Arjovsky et al., 2017], which also aims to optimize the Wasserstein distance. The paper briefly mentions WGAN but dismisses it without a direct empirical comparison. WGAN and its successors represent the state of the art in this area, and failing to compare against them makes it impossible to judge the practical significance of PMD's performance. For a fair assessment, PMD should be benchmarked against these more established and powerful techniques.

3.  **Lack of Rigorous Quantitative Evaluation:** The evaluation of the generative modeling experiments relies on a qualitative visual assessment of generated samples. Claims such as PMD images being "less noisy" are subjective. Standard practice for evaluating generative models now requires quantitative metrics like the Frechet Inception Distance (FID) or Inception Score (IS). Without these standard, objective measures, the claims of improved generation quality are not well-supported. Furthermore, the domain adaptation results are not overwhelmingly positive; PMD outperforms MMD on two of six tasks and is comparable on the others. This does not present a compelling case for the general superiority of this new method.

4.  **Novelty in Context:** While the specific formulation and application as a direct training objective are presented, the core idea of using sample-based approximations of the Wasserstein distance is not new. The connection between empirical measures and optimal transport is classical. Given the intense recent interest in Wasserstein distances for machine learning, the contribution feels more incremental than foundational. The paper essentially proposes using the primal form of the W-1 distance estimator, in contrast to the dual form used by WGAN. However, it fails to demonstrate convincingly why this approach is superior to the existing, highly successful dual-based methods.

**Summary of pros and cons:**

**Pro:**
*   The method is theoretically well-motivated by its connection to the Wasserstein metric.
*   It eliminates the need for kernel hyperparameter tuning, a practical benefit over MMD.
*   The analysis of gradient properties suggests potential optimization benefits.

**Con:**
*   Significant computational concerns due to the expensive matching step in the training loop.
*   The proposed optimization heuristic of fixing the matching for multiple SGD steps is not well-justified.
*   The experimental evaluation is weak due to the omission of key state-of-the-art baselines (e.g., WGAN).
*   Evaluation of generative models lacks standard quantitative metrics, making the results difficult to interpret objectively.
*   The empirical evidence presented is not strong enough to demonstrate a clear and consistent advantage over established methods like MMD, let alone WGAN.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting idea with a sound theoretical basis, its contributions are undermined by significant concerns about computational scalability and, most importantly, a lack of rigorous and comprehensive empirical validation against established, state-of-the-art methods. The claims of superiority are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. Before this approach can be endorsed, a more thorough study is required, including a direct comparison to WGAN with standard quantitative metrics and a more rigorous analysis of the proposed optimization scheme.
