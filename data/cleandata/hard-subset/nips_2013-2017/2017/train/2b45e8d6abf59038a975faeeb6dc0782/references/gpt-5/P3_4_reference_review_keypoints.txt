- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): average cost of minimum-weight bipartite matching as a sample-based, differentiable surrogate for W1, used with a “frozen matching” training heuristic.  
- Theoretical claim: proves strong consistency to true W1 using standard empirical measure convergence and OT stability (results are classical).  
- Practical contribution: compute assignment for moderate populations (N≈1k–4k), perform several SGD steps keeping matching fixed, and subsample mini-batches from matched pairs.  
- Experiments: compare PMD to MMD on unsupervised domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW), reporting faster convergence and robustness to hyperparameters.  
- Relation to prior work: PMD is just empirical W1/EMD; theoretical results follow from classic OT literature.  
- Missing related work: no comparison/discussion of entropic OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi 2013) and stochastic Sinkhorn methods, which are fast and differentiable.  
- Missing related work: prior OT-based domain adaptation methods (e.g., Courty et al.) and stronger generative-model baselines (WGAN variants, sliced Wasserstein) are not adequately compared.  
- Strength: simple, intuitive, OT-grounded objective with an easy-to-integrate “frozen matching” trick.  
- Strength: useful empirical observations — more uniform gradients than RBF-MMD and effective training with small inner mini-batches.  
- Strength: parameter-light (no kernel bandwidth) and includes timing/ablation (exact vs approximate matching, N vs batch size).  
- Weakness (novelty): conceptually standard empirical W1; theoretical contributions are routine and the training heuristic is ad hoc without theory.  
- Weakness (gradients): claim of unbiased stochastic gradients ignores dependence of optimal matching on parameters; subgradient/unbiasedness issues and bias from reusing M* are not addressed.  
- Weakness (computational baselines): no comparison to Sinkhorn OT or linear-time MMD estimators, weakening runtime and scalability claims.  
- Weakness (experiments — DA): limited to Office with shallow heads over VGG features, missing stronger DA baselines (DANN, JAN, OTDA) and uses labeled target validation for tuning.  
- Weakness (experiments — generative): largely qualitative evaluation, no FID/Inception scores; pixel-space PMD yields blurry images and benefits may come from using perceptual feature spaces.  
- Weakness (claims): “parameter free” is overstated — PMD depends on ground metric, λ in DA, population size, inner steps; WGAN-related claims are not fully accurate.  
- Clarity: overall readable with helpful pseudocode, but notation and gradient discussion need precision; implementation details for matching refresh should be specified.  
- Suggestions: compare against entropic OT/Sinkhorn, analyze how often M* changes or warm-start solvers, include stronger DA baselines and quantitative generative metrics, explore learned/perceptual ground metrics, and consider linear-time MMD baselines.  
- Pros for acceptance: practical, easy-to-use objective with promising empirical behavior and clear mini-batch/gradient observations useful for practitioners.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited novelty, missing key related work (Sinkhorn, OT-DA), weak theoretical footing for gradient approximations, and incomplete experimental comparisons — reviewer leans toward rejection but sees a clear path to improvement.
