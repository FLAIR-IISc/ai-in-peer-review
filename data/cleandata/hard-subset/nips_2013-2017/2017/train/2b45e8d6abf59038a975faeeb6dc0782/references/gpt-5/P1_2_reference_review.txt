Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets from two distributions using a ground metric d (e.g., L1). PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between uniform empirical distributions. The authors (i) argue PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 via standard empirical measure convergence and stability of optimal transport; (ii) propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep models, with gradients flowing through matched pairs while treating the discrete matching as locally fixed; (iii) suggest reusing a matching over several SGD steps and sub-sampling matched pairs for cheaper gradients; and (iv) employ exact Hungarian or an O(N^2) approximate matching. Experiments compare PMD to MMD on unsupervised domain adaptation (Office) and deep generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), reporting better or comparable accuracy, faster convergence, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batches.

Relation to prior work
- PMD is the unregularized empirical OT (Wasserstein-1) distance, a well-studied object in OT and statistics (Villani, Cuturi, Genevay, Courty et al.). The consistency result is standard.
- The approach contrasts with MMD and with dual W1 estimation in WGAN.
- However, the paper omits key related work: entropic-regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) providing fast, differentiable approximations usable in deep learning; stochastic large-scale OT optimization (Genevay et al., 2016–2017); OT-based domain adaptation (Courty et al., 2014–2017). Comparisons to Sinkhorn-based methods and OT domain adaptation are missing.

Strengths
- Clear connection of PMD to W1 and a plausible SGD recipe that exploits pairwise matches.
- Empirical evidence that gradients from PMD are more uniform in magnitude than MMD, which may address vanishing gradients with RBF kernels.
- Practical notes on exact vs approximate matching and a simple sub-sampling scheme to reduce compute.
- Applications to domain adaptation and generative modeling, with some favorable results and convergence speedups.
- Theoretical consistency (albeit known) is stated correctly and cleanly.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty: PMD is simply the empirical W1 distance. Presenting it as a new discrepancy understates extensive prior OT literature and recent deep learning uses. The main technical proposal is to reuse matchings and sub-sample pairs for SGD; this is incremental and lacks theoretical analysis of the resulting bias/variance or convergence.
- Missing baselines and citations:
  - No comparison to Sinkhorn (entropic-regularized) OT, which is faster (O(N^2)) and differentiable, with GPU-friendly implementations, and widely used in deep learning. This is a critical omission for both tasks.
  - No comparison to OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al.), DANN (adversarial), CORAL/JAN/CMD/DSN, despite citing some of them.
  - For generative modeling, no comparison to WGAN/WGAN-GP or more recent energy/Cramér distances already cited.
- Claims about mini-batch advantages are somewhat overstated. MMD can be estimated stochastically (e.g., unbiased/low-bias U-statistic estimators or linear-time MMD) with mini-batches; the paper does not compare fairness of wall-clock training given similar compute budgets.
- Optimization and differentiability: treating the argmin matching as fixed yields a subgradient a.e., but the paper calls the gradient “unbiased” without proof (Eq. 3); reusing an old matching and sub-sampling (Eq. 4) introduces bias whose effect is not quantified. No stability analysis of match reassignments is provided.
- Computational practicality: exact matching is O(N^3), approximate O(N^2). For N=2000, this is substantial per iteration. The paper reports seconds for matching but does not provide end-to-end comparisons to MMD or Sinkhorn under equal hardware/epochs, and relies on single-thread CPU matching (no GPU), undercutting the speed claims.
- Experiments:
  - Domain adaptation: Only MMD is compared. Hyperparameters are selected using 100 labeled target samples (violates strict unsupervised DA; please clarify protocol and report results without target labels for model selection). Results improve on 2/6 shifts; no error bars/confidence intervals despite multiple runs. Using separate BN per domain should be justified relative to competing methods.
  - Generative models: Qualitative results only; images are blurred for SVHN/LFW; no standard metrics (FID/Inception). Claims of better quality over MMD are weak without quantitative evaluation. Pixel-space L1 is acknowledged as suboptimal; experiments in learned feature space are limited.
- Theoretical section: the consistency theorem is a direct corollary of standard results; the paper does not provide new finite-sample bounds or rates (the empirical N^-0.23 rate is anecdotal).

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized; the intuition and algorithm are easy to follow.
- Some claims need careful wording (e.g., “unbiased stochastic gradient”; “MMD must use population-size minibatches”; “parameter-free” despite dependence on ground metric and feature space).
- Missing essential references (Cuturi; Genevay; Courty et al.) should be added.

Quality
- Technically sound in its core (use of empirical W1 and matching-based gradients), but theoretical contributions are minimal and some optimization claims are not rigorously supported.
- Experimental methodology and baselines are insufficient to substantiate significance.

Originality
- Limited. The main idea is established in OT; the contributions are mainly empirical comparisons to MMD and a simple training heuristic.

Significance
- Potentially useful insight that empirical W1 (via matching) can outperform MMD in some settings and may yield stronger gradients. However, without comparisons to modern OT baselines (Sinkhorn, WGAN) and stronger experimental validation, the impact is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add and compare to Sinkhorn OT (with/without regularization strength tuning) and OT-based DA methods; include WGAN/WGAN-GP for generative modeling.
- Provide quantitative generative metrics (FID/Inception), wall-clock comparisons under matched compute, and ablations on matching refresh frequency and sub-sampling bias.
- Clarify the DA protocol regarding labeled target validation; consider reporting results with source-only validation or unsupervised model selection criteria.
- Discuss and evaluate different ground costs and learned feature spaces; consider perceptual distances.
- Include error bars and statistical significance in tables; broaden DA baselines.
- Refine theoretical claims (subgradient justification, potential bias from stale matchings) and, if possible, provide finite-sample error/rate guarantees.

Pros
- Clear exposition; practical algorithm; shows stronger gradients vs MMD.
- Some empirical wins and faster convergence.
- Brings attention to using primal OT directly with deep models.

Cons
- Novelty and related work gaps (especially Sinkhorn/OT DA).
- Limited baselines and metrics; mixed DA results; weak generative outcomes.
- Computational practicality not convincingly established; some claims overstated.

Overall, the paper contains interesting empirical insights but requires stronger positioning within the OT literature and more comprehensive experiments to meet the bar.
