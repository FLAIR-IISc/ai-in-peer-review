Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. sample sets, as a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance. The authors (i) show PMD converges almost surely to W1 by invoking classical results on weak convergence and stability of optimal transport, (ii) propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective by not backpropagating through the discrete matching, and (iii) demonstrate empirical benefits over MMD on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW). They argue PMD has stronger gradients, no bandwidth hyperparameter, and permits smaller minibatches than MMD.

Relation to prior work
Using the minimum-weight matching between empirical measures to estimate W1 is standard optimal transport (Villani, 2009). Using OT losses in learning is also not new (e.g., Frogner et al., ICML’15 “Learning with a Wasserstein Loss”). Fast differentiable OT with entropic regularization (Cuturi, NIPS’13; subsequent Sinkhorn autodiff and stochastic/batched OT works) provides scalable alternatives widely used for deep learning. Energy distance/Cramér distance (Bellemare et al., 2017) and MMD-GAN (Li et al., 2017) address gradient issues. The paper positions PMD primarily against MMD and WGAN but omits or underplays these critical alternatives (especially Sinkhorn OT).

Strengths
- Clear and useful reminder that empirical W1 is equivalent to a minimum-weight assignment when samples are equally weighted.
- Simple training recipe with a fixed matching over several SGD steps, and empirical evidence that small minibatches can still work.
- Some empirical advantages over a specific MMD baseline (e.g., convergence on Office tasks; visual quality vs. MMD on SVHN/LFW).
- Implementation details for distance computations and approximate matching, plus limited timing breakdowns.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Originality/significance
- The core estimator (assignment between empirical measures) is standard OT; renaming it PMD risks rebranding. The novelty is essentially a training heuristic (keep M fixed, subsample), which is not theoretically justified and is incremental relative to extensive prior work on differentiable OT with Sinkhorn and stochastic batching.
- Critical related work gaps: entropic OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi, 2013) and its differentiable variants, stochastic mini-batch OT, and OT losses in deep learning (e.g., Frogner et al., Genevay et al.). These methods are natural baselines and directly address scalability and differentiability concerns. Their omission weakens both positioning and evaluation.

Theory
- The strong consistency result is essentially known: empirical measures converge weakly (Varadarajan) and OT is stable. The theorem is stated for “real random variables,” while the rest of the paper (and OT stability) treats general Polish/Euclidean spaces; this mismatch should be fixed. No rates are provided; well-known rates exist (e.g., E[W1] ≍ N−1/d under moment conditions) and could contextualize the N−0.23 empirical slope in 5D.
- Claim of “unbiased stochastic gradient” (Eq. 3) is not justified. The function θ ↦ minM ∑ d(xθ, yθ; M) is nondifferentiable at assignment changes; the gradient they use is a valid subgradient away from ties, but unbiasedness of the estimator of ∇E[DN] requires interchange of expectation and subgradient and measure-zero tie events; none of this is formalized.
- The minibatch approximation (Eq. 4) with a stale M∗ computed on the full population is biased; the paper acknowledges it heuristically but provides no analysis of the bias/variance trade-off or conditions under which it helps.

Method/claims
- “PMD has no hyperparameters” is overstated. It depends on the choice of ground metric d (here L1), population size N, minibatch size |B|, and number of inner steps between rematchings. Those are impactful knobs.
- The claim that “MMD must use minibatch size equal to population size and cannot use small |B|” is inaccurate. Unbiased linear-time MMD estimators exist (Gretton et al.) and can be used with small batches; practitioners also use subsampling and kernel mixtures to stabilize gradients.
- Gradient “strength” comparisons are made only against RBF-kernel MMD; stronger/broader MMD variants (mixture bandwidths, learned kernels as in MMD-GAN, rational quadratic, energy distance) are not considered. Similarly, WGAN with gradient penalty addresses Lipschitz constant issues; the “unknown scale factor” criticism is oversimplified.
- Balanced matching assumes equal sample counts and uniform weights; the paper occasionally uses M and N in MMD and N-only in PMD, leading to notation confusion. Extending PMD to unequal sample sizes or non-uniform weights (standard in OT) is not discussed.

Experiments
- Baselines are too weak. In domain adaptation, comparing only to MMD ignores well-known baselines (DANN, CORAL/Deep CORAL, CMD, JAN, DSN). The results show modest gains on two shifts and parity elsewhere; no SOTA claims. Using a small labeled target set for hyperparameter tuning is standard but should be reported consistently for all baselines.
- Generative modeling evaluation lacks standard quantitative metrics (FID, Inception Score, precision/recall, 1-NN two-sample tests) and comparisons to WGAN/BEGAN/SWGAN or Sinkhorn-based methods. The authors themselves note blurriness with pixel L1; moving to feature space is an obvious fix but is not thoroughly explored.
- Fairness/timings: The claim of faster convergence (100 vs. 500 epochs) is not normalized by wall-clock time, and matching is done on CPU while MMD is fully GPU-amenable. No end-to-end time comparison vs. strong baselines.
- Many implementation details are missing for reproducibility: exact architectures, learning rates, optimizer settings, inner-loop steps per rematch, approximate matching parameters, and code availability.

Clarity/Presentation
- Numerous typos/formatting issues: stray line “st Conference…”, “∗Corresponding author.” in main text; inconsistent spacing/symbols (TXθX, T Y θY); inconsistent use of N vs. M; missing figure/table references in the text provided; several references lack years/venues or complete bibliographic data; some citations and equations have minor errors (e.g., δxN, δxi,yj notation). The manuscript needs substantial proofreading.
- The argument “the matching doesn’t change under infinitesimal θ, so we don’t backprop through M” should be reframed as using a subgradient and noting nondifferentiability on a set of measure zero, with a brief justification.

Pros
- Simple, intuitive use of empirical OT as a training objective.
- Empirical observation that small minibatches can suffice when keeping a matching fixed for multiple steps.
- Implementation shows that approximate matching can be competitive with exact matching in these settings.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond standard OT; key related work and baselines omitted.
- Theoretical claims are standard and lack rigor in key places (gradient unbiasedness, space assumptions), with no rates.
- Experimental evaluation is narrow and misses standard metrics and stronger baselines.
- Overstated/partially incorrect claims about MMD limitations; lack of comparison to Sinkhorn OT.
- Presentation/formatting issues throughout.

Questions for authors
1. How does PMD compare (accuracy, speed, stability) to entropic OT/Sinkhorn with autograd and ε-regularization as ε→0? Why was this baseline omitted?
2. Can you formalize conditions under which Eq. (3) yields an unbiased estimator of ∇E[DN]? How often do assignment changes occur in practice (measured)?
3. Can you report FID/IS and 1-NN two-sample tests, and add WGAN-GP and Sinkhorn baselines on SVHN/LFW?
4. How sensitive are results to the choice of ground metric (L1 vs. L2 vs. perceptual features)?
5. Can PMD handle unequal sample sizes or non-uniform weights in practice (common in domain adaptation)?

Recommendation: Reject
While the idea is sensible and the empirical results are encouraging against a specific MMD baseline, the contribution is incremental relative to well-established OT methodology (assignment-based empirical W1 and Sinkhorn OT), the theoretical development is mostly standard with gaps in optimization justification, the experimental evaluation is not competitive with current baselines, and the manuscript has numerous clarity/formatting issues. Strengthening related work, adding strong OT and GAN baselines with standard metrics, and providing rigorous analysis of the proposed SGD-with-stale-matching scheme would significantly improve the paper. Reviewer confidence: high.
