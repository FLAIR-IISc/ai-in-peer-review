## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric. PMD aims to address limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) – namely, the need for bandwidth tuning, gradient saturation, and large mini-batch sizes.  The authors demonstrate PMD's theoretical consistency and propose a stochastic gradient descent algorithm for its use as a training objective. They showcase PMD's effectiveness in domain adaptation and generative modeling, reporting performance and convergence speed improvements over MMD.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** While the Wasserstein metric isn’t new, framing it as a computationally tractable, sample-based discrepancy (PMD) for gradient-based learning *is* a novel contribution.  The explicit connection to the optimal transport problem and the highlighting of benefits over MMD provide a clear positioning.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical grounding by demonstrating the strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  The connection to existing optimal transport theory is clearly articulated.
* **Practical Advantages:**  The paper persuasively argues for the practical advantages of PMD: no bandwidth parameter, potentially stronger gradients (supported by empirical evidence in Fig. 2b), and the ability to use standard mini-batch sizes.  These are significant benefits for training deep learning models.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The results on domain adaptation and generative modeling are encouraging, with PMD outperforming MMD in most scenarios. The included convergence speed comparison (Fig. 3a) supports the claim of faster training.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear and the concepts are explained in a relatively accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the results are positive, the experiments are relatively limited in scope.  Only two applications (domain adaptation and generative modeling) are explored, and within those, the datasets are fairly standard. It's unclear how PMD will perform on more complex or high-dimensional datasets.
* **L1 Distance Choice:** The consistent use of the L1 distance could be limiting. The authors acknowledge this for generative modeling, suggesting it might be suboptimal for natural images.  Exploring different distance metrics within the PMD framework could reveal further improvements. The justification for consistently choosing L1 isn’t strong.
* **Matching Algorithm Complexity:** The discussion of the matching algorithm (Hungarian vs. approximated) is a bit cursory. While the authors claim the computational cost is acceptable, a more detailed benchmark of the runtime with varying dataset sizes would be helpful. The practical implications of choosing the approximate method aren't fully explored.
* **GPU Acceleration:** While mentioning potential GPU acceleration for the matching algorithm, the paper lacks concrete results demonstrating this speedup.  This is important given the computational cost.
* **Lack of Comparison to Wasserstein GAN:** A key omission is a comparison to Wasserstein GAN (WGAN). WGAN is a very popular method for leveraging the Wasserstein metric in generative modeling, and a direct comparison would better contextualize the contributions of PMD.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of sample-based statistical divergence estimation. The introduction of PMD, its theoretical justification, and the demonstrated practical advantages make it a worthwhile addition to the NIPS program.  The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.  However, the limited scope of experiments and the lack of comparison to related work (specifically WGAN) are weaknesses that should be addressed.  

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric with clear practical benefits.
* Strong theoretical foundation demonstrating consistency.
* Positive empirical results in two relevant applications.
* Addresses a known limitation of MMD.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited experimental evaluation.
* L1 distance choice is not sufficiently justified.
* Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods like WGAN.

**Rating:**

**Accept (Borderline)** - I recommend acceptance, but with the caveat that the authors should address the weaknesses identified above in a response to reviewers, and ideally, incorporate some of those changes into a final version of the paper.  Specifically, adding a comparison with WGAN and expanding the experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the submission.
