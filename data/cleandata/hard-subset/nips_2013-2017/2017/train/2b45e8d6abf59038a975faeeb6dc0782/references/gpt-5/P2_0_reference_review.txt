Summary
The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average distance between pairs in a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets. PMD is used as a sample-based, differentiable surrogate for the 1-Wasserstein distance to train deep models. The authors (i) formalize PMD and show it is exactly empirical OT on equally weighted samples and is a strongly consistent estimator of Wasserstein-1 (via Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of OT), (ii) propose a practical learning algorithm that alternates between computing a (approximate or exact) matching on a “population” and performing several SGD steps without backpropagating through the discrete assignment, and (iii) argue PMD has advantages over MMD (no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, smaller effective minibatches). Applications to domain adaptation (Office) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW) show PMD is competitive or better than MMD and often converges faster.

Quality
- Theory: The connection of PMD to Wasserstein-1 is correct and the strong consistency result follows directly from standard results (weak convergence + OT stability). This is sound but largely known; no finite-sample rates are provided. The gradient argument (no backprop through the assignment) is heuristically justified; it could be formalized via Danskin’s theorem/subdifferential calculus when the optimal matching is unique (almost everywhere), and acknowledging nondifferentiability where assignments switch. The paper would benefit from explicitly citing and applying Danskin/Clarke to justify Eq. (3).
- Algorithms and complexity: Using Hungarian O(N^3) or approximate O(N^2) matching with all-pairs distance O(N^2D) is feasible for N~10^3–10^4 and modest D, and the empirical timings suggest it can be practical. The alternating scheme (compute a large-population matching, then use smaller minibatches with fixed assignments for a few steps) is a reasonable engineering compromise, though it introduces staleness and the paper does not analyze the impact of assignment changes on convergence.
- Empirics: The Office experiments indicate PMD is competitive with MMD and sometimes better, with faster convergence. However, comparison is limited to MMD; modern domain-adaptation baselines (e.g., DANN/GRL, JAN, CORAL, CMD, and OT-based DA such as Courty et al.) are not included. For generative modeling, evaluation is almost entirely qualitative; no Inception Score or FID is reported, and no comparison to WGAN/GP, entropic OT (Sinkhorn), or sliced/energy distances. The observation that pixel L1 leads to blur is accurate; using feature-space distances helps, but the study remains narrow. Claims about MMD requiring large minibatches are overstated; unbiased linear-time MMD estimators exist and multi-kernel MMD reduces bandwidth sensitivity, so a stronger MMD baseline should be included.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Definitions and connections to OT are presented cleanly. Pseudocode and figures help. A few points could be clarified:
- State the precise conditions under which gradients ignore matching (Danskin) and discuss non-uniqueness.
- Detail the approximate matching algorithm used and its approximation guarantees.
- Clarify the schedule for recomputing matchings vs. inner-loop updates and sensitivity to this choice.
- Minor typographical issues (e.g., conference header artifact) should be fixed.

Originality
Using minimum-weight matching as a training loss is a natural instantiation of empirical OT. As such, the core theoretical content is not novel; the “strong consistency” result is standard. The main novelty is the practical training recipe (outer-loop matching + inner-loop minibatch updates without differentiating through assignments) and the empirical observation that gradients are stronger than with MMD, enabling smaller minibatches. Relative to prior work:
- Entropic regularized OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi, 2013) provides differentiable, scalable OT losses and is widely used; it is not discussed or compared, despite being the most relevant baseline.
- OT for domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al.) predates this and should be cited and compared.
- WGAN (and gradient-penalized variants) are relevant baselines for generative modeling; a discussion is present but empirical comparison is missing.

Significance
PMD is a simple, parameter-free (aside from choice of d) alternative to MMD that can yield stronger gradients and faster convergence in some settings. As a practical tool, it could see use when (i) one can afford O(N^2) costs intermittently and (ii) the chosen ground metric is meaningful (e.g., in learned feature space). However, without quantitative evaluations against stronger baselines (Sinkhorn OT, WGAN-GP, modern DA methods) and without finite-sample/statistical guarantees or convergence analysis for the alternating optimization, the paper’s impact is limited. The experimental evidence suggests PMD is a viable baseline, but it does not clearly advance the SotA.

Pros
- Clear formulation connecting PMD and empirical OT; correct consistency argument.
- Practical training recipe that often yields stronger, more uniform gradients than MMD.
- Demonstrated speed/convergence benefits and reduced hyperparameter tuning burden.
- Useful empirical analyses (gradient magnitude histograms, sensitivity to batch/population sizes).

Cons
- Limited novelty: theory is standard; algorithmic idea is incremental relative to OT literature.
- Missing key baselines (Sinkhorn OT; stronger DA and GAN baselines) and quantitative metrics (FID/IS).
- Some claims about MMD batch-size and bandwidth sensitivity are overstated; stronger MMD baselines exist.
- No analysis of the effect of stale matchings; ignoring assignment derivatives is only heuristically justified.
- Scalability to very high dimensions remains challenging; reliance on pixel L1 leads to blurry generations.

Suggestions
- Compare to entropic OT/Sinkhorn and OT-based domain adaptation methods; include WGAN-GP in generative experiments with FID/IS.
- Provide linear-time MMD baselines and multi-kernel bandwidth selection procedures.
- Add an ablation on matching recomputation frequency/inner-loop length and on approximate vs exact matching quality.
- Formalize the gradient argument via Danskin’s theorem and discuss subgradient behavior at assignment switches.
- Explore feature-space ground metrics (learned perceptual features) more systematically.

Overall evaluation
Quality: moderate; Clarity: good; Originality: limited; Significance: modest. I lean toward weak reject given missing baselines and limited novelty, but the idea is practical and could be strengthened with the above additions.
