

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein metric that avoids the hyperparameter tuning required by Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors define PMD as the minimum weight matching cost between two sets of samples from distributions, prove it's a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, and demonstrate its advantages over MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The work relates to NIPS papers on statistical divergences (e.g., [17, 31]) and Wasserstein metrics (e.g., [3, 40]), but proposes a novel estimator with practical advantages.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper provides clear theoretical justification for PMD, establishing its connection to the Wasserstein metric through a rigorous proof of strong consistency. The relationship with the optimal transport problem is well-explained, and the Varadarajan theorem is appropriately applied.

2. **Practical advantages**: The authors convincingly demonstrate PMD's advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients (with empirical evidence in Fig. 2b), and smaller mini-batch requirements. The empirical results in domain adaptation (Table 1) and generative modeling (Fig. 4) support these claims.

3. **Well-executed experiments**: The experimental design is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW), tasks (domain adaptation, generative modeling), and thorough analysis of convergence speed (Fig. 3), parameter sensitivity (Fig. 3b-3c), and time consumption (Fig. 5c).

4. **Clear presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear pseudocode (Fig. 1), good figures, and accessible explanations of the technical content. The connection between theory and practice is well-maintained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with other Wasserstein-based methods**: The paper focuses primarily on comparing PMD with MMD, but fails to compare with other Wasserstein-based approaches like WGAN [3] or its variants (e.g., WGAN-GP [53]). This is a significant omission given the popularity of Wasserstein-based methods in deep learning.

2. **Scalability concerns**: While the authors mention using approximate matching algorithms for larger N, they don't sufficiently analyze how approximation affects the quality of the gradient or final model performance. The time complexity analysis (O(N^3) for Hungarian, O(N^2) for approximation) doesn't address how this scales with high-dimensional data common in deep learning.

3. **Lack of distance metric analysis**: The paper uses L1 distance throughout but doesn't justify this choice or explore how other distance metrics (e.g., L2) might affect the results. This is particularly important as the gradient strength claim (Sec. 3.2) depends on the distance function.

4. **Underdeveloped limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss PMD's limitations. For high-dimensional data (common in deep learning), the curse of dimensionality might make matching less meaningful. The paper also doesn't address how PMD handles distributions with different supports.

5. **Limited statistical significance**: The claim that "PMD outperforms MMD" (Abstract) is supported by empirical results, but lacks statistical significance testing across multiple trials (the paper mentions repeating experiments 10 times, but doesn't present statistical analysis of the differences).

## Overall Assessment

**Quality**: The technical content is sound with strong theoretical foundations and well-executed experiments. The paper is careful in its theoretical claims and provides sufficient experimental validation.

**Clarity**: Excellent. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for replication.

**Originality**: The core idea of using minimum weight matching as a statistical divergence estimator is novel compared to existing MMD-based approaches. However, the paper could better distinguish itself from prior work on matching-based approaches.

**Significance**: The paper makes a modest but meaningful contribution to the field. While PMD offers practical advantages over MMD, it doesn't represent a major breakthrough. The improvements are incremental but useful for practitioners.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach that offers practical advantages over MMD in several deep learning applications. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the empirical results support the claims. However, the limited comparison with other Wasserstein-based methods and the underdeveloped analysis of scalability in high dimensions are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Add comparisons with WGAN and its variants to better position PMD within the current landscape of Wasserstein-based methods.

2. Include a thorough analysis of how the approximate matching algorithm affects model quality and convergence speed, especially for high-dimensional data.

3. Justify the choice of L1 distance and explore how other distance metrics might affect the results.

4. Provide statistical significance testing for the performance improvements over MMD.

5. Add a more detailed discussion of PMD's limitations, particularly for high-dimensional data.

With these improvements, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field and better justify its significance. As it stands, the paper is a solid technical contribution with practical value, but could be strengthened by addressing the gaps in comparison and scalability analysis.
