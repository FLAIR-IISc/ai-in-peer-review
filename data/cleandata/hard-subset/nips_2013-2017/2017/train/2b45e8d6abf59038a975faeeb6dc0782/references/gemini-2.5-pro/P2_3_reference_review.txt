### **Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). They propose a learning algorithm that uses PMD as a differentiable objective for training deep neural networks via stochastic gradient descent. The key insight enabling this is that for infinitesimal parameter updates, the optimal matching can be treated as fixed, allowing gradients to pass through the distance function to the sample generators.

The paper contrasts PMD with the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD has several advantages: it is hyperparameter-free (no kernel bandwidth to tune), provides stronger and more uniform gradients, and allows for smaller mini-batch sizes during optimization, leading to faster convergence. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD on two deep learning applications: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. Empirical results on standard benchmarks (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW) show that PMD is competitive with or outperforms MMD, particularly in terms of convergence speed and stability.

### **Evaluation**

This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel, theoretically-grounded, and practically useful tool for a fundamental problem in machine learning. The connection between the classic assignment problem and modern deep generative learning is elegant and compelling.

**Quality: (Strong)**

The paper is technically sound.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The core theoretical claim—that N-PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric—is well-supported by established results from optimal transport theory (Propositions 3.1 and 3.2). This provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors compare PMD against a strong and relevant baseline (MMD) on standard tasks and datasets. The inclusion of experiments analyzing convergence speed (Fig. 3a), hyperparameter sensitivity (Fig. 3b, 3c), and the impact of population/mini-batch sizes (Fig. 5) demonstrates a careful evaluation of the method's properties.
*   **Validation of Claims:** Crucially, the authors provide empirical evidence for their central claims. Figure 2(a) validates the convergence of PMD to the true Wasserstein distance, and Figure 2(b) provides a clear and compelling visualization of the "stronger gradient" property compared to MMD.
*   **Honesty about Limitations:** The authors are upfront about the computational cost of the matching algorithm (O(N^3) for exact, O(N^2) for approximate) and evaluate both, showing that the approximation works well in practice. This is a balanced presentation.

A minor technical point that could be clarified is the assumption in Eq. (3) that the optimal matching M* does not change for infinitesimal changes in parameters. While this is a reasonable and necessary simplification for differentiability, the objective function is technically non-smooth at points where the optimal matching changes. A brief mention of this, perhaps framing the computed gradient as a subgradient, would strengthen the technical discussion.

**Clarity: (Excellent)**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction.
*   The method is defined precisely in Section 2, and the learning algorithm is presented with clear pseudocode and an intuitive illustration (Fig. 1).
*   The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is easy to follow, building from known results to the main theorem.
*   The comparison with MMD is sharp and highlights the key differences and claimed advantages of PMD.
*   The experiments are well-described, and the results are presented clearly through tables and figures that directly support the paper's claims.

**Originality: (Strong)**

The paper presents a novel approach to a well-studied problem.
*   While the Wasserstein metric and MMD are not new, the use of minimum weight bipartite matching as a direct, sample-based, and differentiable estimator for training deep neural networks is a novel and significant contribution. This bridges a gap between combinatorial optimization and deep learning in a very practical way.
*   This work is distinct from concurrent work on Wasserstein GANs (WGAN) [3]. WGAN approximates the dual form of the Wasserstein distance using a learned critic network, which introduces its own set of parameters and training instabilities. PMD, in contrast, directly approximates the primal form, is parameter-free, and has a deterministic, polynomial-time computation. This offers a valuable and complementary perspective on using optimal transport metrics in deep learning.
*   The method is clearly distinguished from prior work on MMD-based generative models like GMMN [31], and the paper does an excellent job of citing and positioning itself within the relevant literature.

**Significance: (Strong)**

The results and ideas presented in this paper are likely to be influential.
*   **Practical Impact:** PMD provides practitioners with a strong alternative to MMD for tasks requiring distribution matching. The lack of a kernel bandwidth parameter removes a significant and often sensitive tuning step. The ability to use smaller mini-batches can dramatically speed up training, which is a major practical advantage.
*   **Research Impact:** The paper opens up several interesting research directions. The idea of using other combinatorial optimization solvers as differentiable components in deep learning is a promising one. Furthermore, exploring different ground metrics `d(x, y)` beyond L1/L2 distance for PMD, perhaps even learning the metric itself, could lead to further improvements, especially for perceptual tasks like image generation where pixel-wise distances are suboptimal.
*   **Advancing the State-of-the-Art:** The paper demonstrates a clear advance. On domain adaptation, it shows improved or comparable performance with faster convergence. In generative modeling, it produces competitive results without the architectural complexity of an adversary (like GANs) or the hyperparameter sensitivity of MMD.

### **Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Idea:** The core concept of using minimum weight matching as a differentiable loss is both original and theoretically well-motivated by its connection to the Wasserstein metric.
2.  **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The claims of superiority over MMD (no hyperparameters, stronger gradients, faster convergence) are convincingly supported by a thorough set of experiments on multiple standard benchmarks.
3.  **High Practical Value:** PMD is a practical, easy-to-implement tool that addresses known shortcomings of a widely used technique (MMD). It is likely to be adopted by researchers and practitioners in domain adaptation, generative modeling, and other areas.
4.  **Clarity and Quality of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a novel contribution accessible and easy to understand.

### **Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The primary weakness is the O(N^3) or O(N^2) complexity of the matching step, which could be a bottleneck for very large population sizes (N). While the paper addresses this and shows it is manageable for N in the thousands, it remains a scalability concern compared to the O(N^2) complexity of MMD.
2.  **Blurry Generative Results:** The generated images, while good, appear somewhat blurry, a common issue for models optimizing pixel-wise L1/L2 distances. The paper acknowledges this, but it highlights that PMD with a simple ground metric does not solve all problems of generative modeling.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear and significant contribution. It introduces a novel discrepancy measure, grounds it in solid theory, and demonstrates its practical advantages over a strong baseline through rigorous experiments. The weaknesses are minor compared to the strengths. I strongly recommend acceptance.
