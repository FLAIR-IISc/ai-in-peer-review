This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, proposing it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling. The core idea – finding the optimal matching between samples from two distributions – is intuitive and directly relates to the Wasserstein distance’s underlying concept of optimal transport. The authors correctly identify limitations of MMD, namely the kernel bandwidth parameter and potential gradient saturation, and position PMD as addressing these.

The paper is generally well-written and the motivation is clear. The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is established with appropriate references. However, my overall assessment is skeptical. While the idea is not *wrong*, the presentation feels incremental and lacks compelling evidence of significant advantage over established methods. The benefits claimed – no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and ability to use normal mini-batch sizes – feel more like engineering tweaks than fundamental methodological breakthroughs. The extensive reliance on claims of improvement without truly exhaustive comparison and analysis weakens the contribution.

A significant weakness is the lack of a thorough ablation study. While comparisons to MMD are provided, they are not particularly extensive. The choice of L1 distance for PMD feels somewhat arbitrary and its impact not adequately explored. The authors state the L1 distance is used, but don't justify *why* it's preferable over, say, Euclidean distance, especially given the common usage of Euclidean distance with MMD. The paper also lacks a comparison against other Wasserstein distance estimators, such as those based on duality, which could highlight the specific advantages (or disadvantages) of the proposed PMD approach.

The claim of “stronger gradient” is supported with a limited empirical analysis (Figure 2b), which shows a more uniform gradient magnitude. However, the implications of this are not fully explored. A more uniform gradient doesn't necessarily mean *better* optimization; it could simply indicate a different optimization trajectory. A more rigorous analysis of convergence speed and stability, potentially with different optimizers and learning rates, is needed. 

The experimental section is also concerning. The domain adaptation results show improvement on some tasks but not others, and the magnitude of improvement isn’t consistently dramatic. The generative modeling results, while showing qualitatively reasonable images, lack quantitative evaluation metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) to objectively assess the generated image quality compared to MMD-based GANs. The acknowledgement of blurry images generated by PMD highlights a limitation that should be discussed more thoroughly. 

The computational complexity analysis is reasonable, but the discussion of GPU acceleration feels like a future work afterthought rather than a core consideration. The efficiency gains, if achievable with GPU implementation, would strengthen the contribution but are not demonstrated in the current work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper proposes a valid estimator for the Wasserstein metric.
*   The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is sound.
*   The idea of using optimal matching is intuitive and potentially useful.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The claimed advantages over MMD are not convincingly demonstrated with extensive experiments and analysis.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited, lacking strong quantitative results and comparison to other relevant methods.
*   The paper feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
*   The ablation studies and sensitivity analysis are insufficient.
*   The choice of L1 distance isn't justified.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

While the paper presents a technically sound idea, it lacks the compelling evidence and thorough validation needed for acceptance to NIPS. The improvements over MMD are not substantial enough to warrant a shift in methodology, and the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of PMD. I recommend the authors conduct more extensive experiments, explore different distance metrics, compare against state-of-the-art Wasserstein estimators, and provide a more rigorous analysis of the gradient properties before resubmitting. A more substantial contribution, potentially with a focus on specific application areas where PMD demonstrably outperforms existing methods, would be required for reconsideration.
