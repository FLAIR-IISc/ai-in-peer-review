Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-cost bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets under a ground metric (e.g., L1). PMD is used as a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance and as a differentiable objective for training deep models. The authors: (i) connect PMD to empirical optimal transport (OT) and claim strong consistency; (ii) propose a practical SGD scheme that keeps the discrete matching fixed across several updates and allows mini-batches smaller than the “population” used to form the matching; (iii) compare PMD to MMD on domain adaptation (Office) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), claiming better stability, stronger gradients, fewer hyperparameters, and faster convergence.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: PMD is the empirical OT/EMD for equal-weight empirical distributions; the idea of using sample matching as a direct objective is simple and intuitive.
- Empirical gradient analysis (Fig. 2b) illustrates potential advantage over RBF-MMD in gradient magnitude uniformity.
- The convergence-rate experiment on a toy Gaussian example is in line with known OT rates (~N^{-1/d}).
- The implementation details (CUDA distance kernel, C++ matching, exact vs approximate matching) and timing breakdowns are useful.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and theory
- Consistency theorem (Theorem 3.3) is stated too strongly and lacks necessary assumptions. For W1, one typically requires finite first moments (E||X||+E||Y||<∞) to ensure finiteness; these are not stated. The convergence of optimal plans γN⇒γ requires conditions (e.g., tightness, lower semicontinuity) and usually guarantees subsequence convergence unless uniqueness holds; almost-sure convergence of the entire sequence needs additional assumptions (e.g., uniqueness of the optimal plan). The citation to Villani (Theorem 5.20) is not precise, and the proof is waved away.
- The gradient treatment through the argmin (Eq. 3) is informal. M* depends on θ; “the matching should not change for infinitesimal change” only holds almost everywhere. One should argue subgradients or Clarke differentials, and clarify that the resulting estimator is unbiased (the paper asserts this without proof). The minibatch subsampling with a fixed M* (Eq. 4) is in general biased; no analysis is provided.
- Claim “MMD mini-batch must be equal to population size” is incorrect. There are unbiased linear-time estimators of MMD (U-statistics) and practical mini-batch estimators (Gretton et al., 2012 JMLR), as well as random feature approximations. This mischaracterization affects fairness claims about convergence speed and batch size.
- Statement about WGAN “computes only a relative value up to an unknown scale factor” is misleading: when the critic is enforced to be 1-Lipschitz (e.g., via gradient penalty), the scale is fixed; the issue is approximation error, not an unknown multiplicative constant.

Related work and positioning
- Major omission: entropically regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) and their extensive use for differentiable, GPU-friendly OT in deep learning (including domain adaptation and generative modeling) prior to/around 2017. This is arguably the most relevant competing approach to PMD. No comparison or discussion is provided.
- Other OT solvers (e.g., auction algorithm (Bertsekas), ε-scaling) and modern approximate OT (Greenkhorn, stochastic Sinkhorn) are not discussed/cited, though they are directly pertinent to the computational claims.
- Claims of novelty (“parameter free”) are overstated. Choice of ground metric d and representation space (pixel vs feature) are hyperparameters; matching refresh frequency and population size are additional knobs.

Empirical evaluation
- Domain adaptation: Using 100 labeled target samples for hyperparameter validation departs from standard unsupervised DA protocols and can bias results. Comparison is limited to MMD with VGG features; no comparison to stronger baselines such as DANN, JAN, CMD, or OT-based DA methods (e.g., Courty et al., if contemporaneous) reduces significance.
- Generative modeling: Only qualitative samples and internal objectives are reported; no IS/FID or likelihood surrogates. Comparisons to GAN/WGAN/energy distance/Cramér distance are missing. Training budgets are unequal (PMD 100 epochs vs MMD 500), and kernel choices for MMD are constrained; this complicates fairness.
- Computational claims: PMD still requires O(N^2 D) distance computation and O(N^2) memory for the cost matrix, which is nontrivial at scale. The paper does not report peak memory for N=2000, nor behavior for larger N. Matching recomputation frequency (staleness) is not specified or ablated.

Clarity, rigor, and presentation
- Several citation mismatches and inaccuracies. E.g., “Proposition 5.4 in [6]” is not a canonical source for matching=OT equivalence; [10,34] are cited as GPU matching but refer to MMD UAI paper and a maximum matching paper (not min-cost bipartite assignment). Please fix and cite Hungarian (Kuhn), auction (Bertsekas), and Cuturi (2013).
- Grammar/typos: subject-verb agreement (“MMD have”), “it need to be differentiable,” “information retrival,” “st Conference…,” etc.
- Notation issues: D∞(X,Y) used as metric between distributions—unclear notation; need to define limit properly and conditions for metric property.
- Missing details for reproducibility: matching refresh schedule, exact optimizer settings per task, inner-loop steps, early stopping, random seeds, code availability.

Pros
- Simple, intuitive formulation tightly connected to empirical OT.
- Potentially stronger, more uniform gradients than RBF-MMD in practice.
- Some useful engineering notes (approximate matching vs exact; cost breakdown).

Cons
- Overstated theoretical claims and incomplete assumptions/proofs.
- Unfair/incorrect characterization of MMD efficiency; missing strong OT baselines (Sinkhorn).
- Experimental comparisons are limited and not standard (DA validation using target labels; no modern generative baselines; no standard metrics).
- Numerous citation errors and presentation issues.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide a rigorous proof (or corrected statement) of Theorem 3.3 with explicit assumptions (finite first moments, uniqueness or subsequence convergence).
2. Is the gradient estimator in Eq. (3) unbiased? Please formalize the argument using subgradients and quantify the bias introduced by Eq. (4).
3. How often is the matching recomputed? Ablate the number of inner SGD steps per matching and report performance vs runtime.
4. Compare against Sinkhorn (entropic OT) as a differentiable, GPU-accelerated baseline on both tasks.
5. Revisit the MMD comparison using the linear-time unbiased estimator and more robust kernel choices; equalize training budgets.
6. Report standard DA baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD) and generative metrics (FID/IS). Clarify DA validation protocol.

Minor comments/typos
- Multiple grammar errors (“MMD have,” “it need,” etc.) and typos (“retrival”). Please proofread.
- Correct citations for Hungarian, auction algorithm, Cuturi (2013), and ensure numbering matches text.
- Clarify memory usage for the N×N cost matrix and whether you stream blocks on GPU.
- PMD is not parameter-free: clarify dependence on ground metric and representation space.

Evaluation
- Quality: Weak (theory overstated; empirical validation limited; some misleading claims).
- Clarity: Fair-to-poor (citation errors, missing details).
- Originality: Modest (empirical OT as objective is known; novelty is in specific training heuristic).
- Significance: Limited without comparisons to Sinkhorn/modern baselines and standard metrics.

Recommendation: Reject. The idea is promising and potentially useful, but the paper requires substantial revisions in theory, related work, experimental rigor, and presentation.
