This paper proposes the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric, and advocates for its use as a training objective in deep learning applications like domain adaptation and generative modeling. The core idea is to compute the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from two distributions. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein-1 distance. The primary contribution is framing this as a practical, differentiable loss for training deep neural networks, and positioning it as a superior alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors claim PMD offers several advantages over MMD, including having no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, providing stronger gradients, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes during optimization, leading to faster convergence.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses the important problem of comparing probability distributions from samples, which is fundamental to many areas of modern machine learning.
*   The proposed PMD method is grounded in the well-established theory of optimal transport, and its connection to the first Wasserstein metric is clearly articulated. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed discrepancy measure.
*   The critique of MMD is valid. The need for kernel bandwidth tuning is a practical nuisance, and the potential for vanishing gradients, particularly when samples are far apart, is a recognized limitation. The paper provides a clear motivation for seeking alternatives.
*   The empirical analysis of the gradient magnitudes (Fig. 2(b)) is a nice contribution that provides concrete evidence for the claim that PMD provides more uniform and potentially stronger gradients than MMD with an RBF kernel.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Methodological Soundness of the Learning Algorithm:** The proposed learning algorithm rests on a critical and poorly justified approximation. The gradient in Eq. (3) is computed by assuming the optimal matching `M*` is fixed. While this holds almost everywhere, the potential for instability when the matching changes is not discussed. More problematically, the two-loop optimization scheme introduced in Sec 2.2 and Figure 1—computing a matching on a large population `N` and then performing multiple SGD steps on smaller mini-batches `|B|` using this fixed matching—is a heuristic. The paper claims this is acceptable because `M*` "does not change much," but this is an unsubstantiated assertion. This approximation fundamentally means that the inner loop is optimizing a stale objective. The paper lacks any analysis, theoretical or empirical, of the error introduced by this heuristic, which is central to making the method practical.

*   **Computational Cost:** The paper somewhat downplays the significant computational overhead of PMD. Even with the O(N^2) approximate matching algorithm, the cost is substantial for the population sizes (N=2000) used in the experiments. Figure 5(c) shows that the matching time is indeed a large component of the total epoch time. While MMD is also O(N^2), its implementation is typically simpler and more amenable to GPU acceleration. The authors mention that matching algorithms can be parallelized but do not provide such an implementation, making the practical comparison of wall-clock time less favorable for their proposed method. The aforementioned two-loop heuristic is a direct consequence of this high computational cost, but as noted, it compromises the methodological rigor.

*   **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while demonstrating that the method works, are not convincing enough to establish its superiority over established baselines.
    *   **Domain Adaptation:** PMD outperforms MMD on only two of the six tasks in the Office dataset, with comparable performance on the rest. This is not a compelling demonstration of superiority that would warrant adopting a more computationally complex method. Furthermore, the comparison is limited to MMD regularization, omitting stronger and highly relevant baselines from the domain adaptation literature, such as adversarial methods (e.g., DANN, Ganin et al., 2016).
    *   **Generative Modeling:** The comparison is made only against Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN), which is an MMD-based approach. By the time of this paper's submission, Generative Adversarial Networks, particularly Wasserstein GANs (WGAN), represented the state of the art. WGANs are highly relevant as they also optimize an approximation of the Wasserstein distance. A comparison against WGAN would have been necessary to properly assess the significance of this work. The qualitative results in Figure 4 are modest and do not clearly demonstrate a significant advance in generative quality.

*   **Limited Novelty:** The connection between minimum weight matching and the Wasserstein distance is a classical result in optimal transport theory. The main novelty here is the application of this primal formulation as a loss for deep networks. However, using optimal transport for machine learning tasks, including domain adaptation, was not a new idea at the time (e.g., Courty et al., PAMI 2017). The paper's contribution is more of an engineering one, but the methodological and computational concerns detract from its practical value.

**Summary of pros and cons:**
*   **Pro:** Provides a theoretically grounded alternative to MMD that avoids kernel bandwidth tuning.
*   **Pro:** The analysis of gradient behavior is insightful and motivates the method well.
*   **Con:** The proposed training algorithm relies on a questionable heuristic (the two-loop optimization with a fixed matching) that is not adequately justified.
*   **Con:** The method has a high computational cost that presents a practical barrier compared to MMD.
*   **Con:** The empirical results are not strong enough to demonstrate a clear and consistent advantage over MMD, and the paper fails to compare against more powerful, state-of-the-art baselines in both domain adaptation and generative modeling.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting idea with a solid theoretical basis, its practical realization for training deep models introduces methodological issues and computational burdens that are not adequately addressed. The experimental evidence is insufficient to support the claims of superiority over established methods. The work falls short of the rigorous validation required to endorse a new, unproven approach over existing, well-understood techniques.
