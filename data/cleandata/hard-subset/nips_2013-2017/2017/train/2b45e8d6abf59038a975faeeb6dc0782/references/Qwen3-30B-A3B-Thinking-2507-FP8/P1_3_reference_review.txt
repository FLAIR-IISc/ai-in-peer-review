

# NIPS 2017 Review: Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning

## Summary

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a new sample-based statistical divergence for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions, and the authors prove it's a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes an algorithm for using PMD as a training objective in deep learning, with applications to domain adaptation and generative modeling. The authors compare PMD to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a popular alternative, and demonstrate that PMD has several advantages: no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100 vs. 1000+ for MMD). The empirical results show PMD outperforms MMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution by establishing the connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric. The consistency proof (Theorem 3.3) is technically sound, building on existing results in optimal transport theory. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple domains (Office dataset for domain adaptation, MNIST/SVHN/LFW for generative modeling) and providing ablation studies on key parameters (population size, mini-batch size, matching algorithm). The authors carefully address both strengths (e.g., stronger gradients) and limitations (e.g., computational cost of matching).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement and motivation are well-articulated in Section 1, with a strong connection to the practical challenges of current methods. The theoretical analysis (Section 3) is accessible with sufficient background. The pseudocode in Section 2.2 effectively illustrates the learning algorithm, and the figures (e.g., Fig. 2, 3, 5) provide clear visual evidence of the advantages of PMD over MMD.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel statistical discrepancy measure (PMD) that differs fundamentally from MMD and other approaches. While the Wasserstein metric has been widely studied, the authors' specific formulation as a minimum weight matching problem between sample populations is new. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work (WGAN, MMD, etc.) and provides a thorough literature review.

**Significance:** The advantages of PMD over MMD are significant for deep learning practitioners. The ability to avoid bandwidth tuning, use smaller mini-batches, and obtain stronger gradients directly translates to faster convergence and easier hyperparameter tuning in practice. The empirical results across two important application domains (domain adaptation and generative modeling) demonstrate clear performance benefits. The paper addresses a real pain point in deep learning research and practice.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Considerations:** While the paper acknowledges the O(N³) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, it doesn't sufficiently address how this scales to very large datasets. The authors mention using approximate matching for generative modeling, but the computational trade-offs between exact vs. approximate matching for different dataset sizes could be more thoroughly analyzed. The paper states that approximate matching costs are comparable to SGD, but doesn't provide concrete timing comparisons for large-scale applications.

**Comparison with Alternative Wasserstein Approximations:** The paper focuses on comparing PMD with MMD, but doesn't adequately contrast it with other Wasserstein approximations like WGAN or Sinkhorn. The authors mention WGAN briefly (Section 3.1), but a more detailed comparison of computational complexity, gradient properties, and empirical performance would strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Generative Modeling Results:** The paper reports that PMD-generated images are "less noisy" than MMD, but the images on SVHN/LFW are described as "blurry" despite PMD's advantages. The authors attribute this to the L1 distance being "not good for natural images," but don't explore how PMD might perform with other distance metrics or in code spaces. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of why PMD struggles with natural images despite its theoretical advantages.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper. It makes a solid theoretical contribution with practical implications for deep learning. The paper addresses an important problem (statistical divergence estimation) with a novel approach that has clear advantages over the current state of the art (MMD). The empirical evaluation is thorough and supports the theoretical claims.

The weaknesses identified are relatively minor compared to the paper's strengths. The computational concerns could be addressed in future work, and the generative modeling results, while not perfect, still demonstrate PMD's advantages over MMD in the pixel space. The paper's contribution is significant enough to advance the state of the art in statistical divergence estimation for deep learning.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the comparison with other Wasserstein approximations (WGAN, Sinkhorn) in the theoretical and empirical sections.

2. Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational trade-offs between exact and approximate matching algorithms, especially for larger datasets.

3. Include a more detailed explanation of the generative modeling results, particularly why PMD struggles with natural images despite its theoretical advantages.

4. Consider adding a discussion of how PMD might extend to higher-order Wasserstein metrics or other distance metrics beyond L1.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The results are significant for both theoretical understanding and practical deep learning applications. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS 2017.

## Final Assessment

The paper successfully introduces PMD as a new statistical divergence with clear advantages over MMD. The theoretical foundation is strong, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the practical implications for deep learning are significant. While some computational considerations could be addressed more thoroughly, these do not undermine the paper's core contribution. The work advances the state of the art in statistical divergence estimation and provides a useful tool for deep learning practitioners.
