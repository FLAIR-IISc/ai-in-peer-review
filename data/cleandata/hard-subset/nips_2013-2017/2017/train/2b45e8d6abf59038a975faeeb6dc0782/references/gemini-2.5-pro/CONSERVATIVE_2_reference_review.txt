This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence measure based on the minimum weight matching between two sets of samples. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). The primary contribution is the application of PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep learning models, which they position as an alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The proposed method is evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, with experiments comparing its performance and convergence speed against MMD.

**Strengths:**

1.  The paper presents a clear and intuitive approach for approximating the Wasserstein distance and using it as a loss function for training neural networks. This provides an interesting alternative to other divergence measures like MMD or the dual-formulation-based approaches used in Wasserstein GANs.
2.  A significant practical advantage of PMD over MMD is that it is parameter-free. It does not require the tuning of a kernel or its bandwidth, which is often a non-trivial and sensitive procedure when using MMD.
3.  The paper is well-written and the proposed method is explained clearly. The connection between the minimum weight matching problem and the Wasserstein metric on empirical distributions is well-established and correctly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Questionable Methodological Soundness of the Gradient Calculation:** The derivation of the gradient in Equation (3) relies on the assumption that the optimal matching `M*` does not change for an infinitesimal change in the parameters `θ`. While this is true almost everywhere, the objective function is non-differentiable at points where the optimal matching switches. The paper completely overlooks this issue, which is a significant theoretical gap. This assumption may hold reasonably well in practice, but a rigorous treatment should at least acknowledge and discuss the implications of these points of non-differentiability. The proposed optimization procedure is therefore more of a heuristic than a principled gradient descent on the true expected PMD.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Baselines and Evaluation:** The paper's central claim is that PMD is a superior alternative to MMD. However, the experimental evaluation is not sufficiently thorough to substantiate this claim, especially in the context of the NIPS 2017 landscape.
    *   For generative modeling, the comparison is limited to Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN), which are based on MMD. The most relevant and powerful baseline at the time of this work would be the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN), which also directly optimizes an approximation of the Wasserstein distance. The absence of a comparison to WGAN is a major omission and makes it difficult to assess the significance of the proposed method in the broader field of generative modeling.
    *   The qualitative results for image generation (Fig. 4) are not compelling. The generated images are blurry, a fact the authors acknowledge. This suggests that using a fixed metric like L1 distance in pixel space is a significant limitation for complex, high-dimensional data. Adversarial methods like GANs have an advantage here as they effectively learn a metric through the discriminator.
    *   The quantitative improvements in the domain adaptation experiments (Table 1) are modest. PMD outperforms MMD on only two of the six tasks, with the gains being relatively small. On the other four tasks, the performance is comparable. This does not provide strong evidence of general superiority.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The core of the proposed method involves solving a minimum weight matching problem, which has a complexity of O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the proposed approximation. This is computationally much more demanding than the standard unbiased MMD estimator, which is O(N^2). The paper argues that this cost is acceptable compared to backpropagation, but this claim is not universally true and depends heavily on the model architecture and the population size `N`. The reliance on a CPU-based, single-threaded implementation for a core component of the algorithm also raises concerns about its practical scalability, even though GPU parallelization is mentioned as a future possibility.

4.  **Novelty:** While applying this estimator to deep learning is new, the idea of approximating the W1 distance using empirical matching is a well-known result in optimal transport theory. The theoretical contribution of the paper (Theorem 3.3) is presented as a main result but is a straightforward application of established theorems from the literature, as noted by the authors.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper proposes an interesting and parameter-free alternative to MMD for measuring divergence between distributions, based on a primal approximation of the Wasserstein distance. The idea is simple, intuitive, and shows some promising results in terms of convergence speed on certain tasks.

*   **Against Acceptance:** The work suffers from several key weaknesses. The theoretical justification for the gradient-based optimization is incomplete, as it ignores the non-differentiability of the matching operation. The experimental evaluation is insufficient; it lacks a crucial comparison to WGAN in the generative modeling task and the empirical gains over MMD in domain adaptation are marginal. Finally, the method introduces significant computational overhead and the quality of the generative modeling results is poor, questioning its utility for high-dimensional problems.

In conclusion, while the core idea of using a matching-based discrepancy is appealing due to its parameter-free nature, the paper fails to provide the rigorous theoretical analysis and extensive empirical validation required to demonstrate its value as a practical and superior alternative to established methods. The weaknesses in the methodology and, most importantly, the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art baselines, prevent a confident assessment of the work's significance.
