### Summary

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the statistical divergence between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions. The paper's central theoretical contribution is to show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors propose an algorithm to use PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep neural networks by treating the optimal matching as fixed during gradient computation.

The paper argues that PMD has several advantages over the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), namely: it has no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter to tune, it provides stronger and more uniform gradients that avoid saturation, and it allows for using smaller mini-batch sizes for SGD updates than the population size used for matching. These claims are evaluated on two deep learning applications: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. Experimental results suggest that PMD outperforms or matches MMD in terms of final performance and convergence speed.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Strengths:** The theoretical grounding is solid. The connection drawn between the minimum weight matching problem (a discrete optimization problem) and the linear programming formulation of the Wasserstein metric on empirical distributions is elegant and well-explained. The claim that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric is supported by referencing established results from optimal transport theory (Propositions 3.1 and 3.2). The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing PMD against a relevant and strong baseline (MMD) on standard tasks. The empirical analyses in sections 5.3 and 5.4, which investigate the impact of population/batch size, matching algorithms, and gradient distributions, add significant weight to the paper's claims.

*   **Weaknesses:** The primary weakness is the computational complexity. The exact Hungarian algorithm for matching is O(N^3), which is a significant bottleneck compared to the O(N^2) complexity of MMD. The paper acknowledges this and proposes using an O(N^2) approximate matching algorithm. However, even this is computationally intensive. The paper’s argument that this is acceptable compared to the cost of backpropagation is reasonable, but the scalability to very large population sizes (N) remains a concern. While GPU acceleration is mentioned as a future possibility, the current implementation relies on a single-threaded CPU version, which limits its practical appeal.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

*   **Strengths:** The motivation is well-articulated in the introduction. Section 2 clearly defines PMD and the proposed parameter learning algorithm, which is helpfully summarized in Figure 1. The theoretical connections in Section 3 are presented logically, building from the definition of the Wasserstein metric to the consistency proof for PMD. The distinction between the population size `N` (for matching) and the SGD mini-batch size `|B|` (for updates) is a crucial aspect of their method, and it is explained reasonably well.

*   **Weaknesses:** One point that could be slightly clearer is the justification for the approximation in Equation (4) and the overall training loop in Figure 1. The algorithm computes a matching `M*` on a large population `N` and then performs several SGD updates on smaller mini-batches `|B| < N`, assuming the matching remains fixed. The impact of this "stale" matching on the convergence properties is not deeply analyzed. While it appears to work well empirically, a more formal discussion of why this is a valid and stable approximation would strengthen the paper.

**Originality:** The paper presents a novel approach to a well-known problem.

*   **Strengths:** While the Wasserstein metric and MMD are not new, using the primal formulation of the empirical Wasserstein metric (i.e., minimum weight matching) directly as a differentiable loss for training deep generative models is a novel and clever idea. It provides a distinct alternative to both MMD and the dual-formulation approaches used in methods like Wasserstein GAN (WGAN). The proposed learning algorithm, which decouples the matching computation from the gradient updates via mini-batching, is also an original contribution that addresses the computational challenges of the method. The work is well-situated within existing literature, clearly differentiating itself from MMD, WGAN, and other estimators of integral probability metrics.

**Significance:** The results and ideas presented are likely to be influential.

*   **Strengths:** This paper introduces a valuable new tool for the deep learning community. The problem of comparing distributions from samples is fundamental to many areas, including generative modeling and domain adaptation. PMD offers a compelling alternative to MMD that alleviates several of its practical pain points, most notably the need for kernel selection and tuning. The demonstrated improvements in performance and convergence speed are significant. The idea of using optimal transport in its primal form opens up new avenues for research and is likely to be built upon by others.

*   **Weaknesses:** The practical significance is somewhat tempered by the computational overhead and the quality of the generative modeling results. The generated images, while demonstrating an advantage over MMD, are still quite blurry and not competitive with state-of-the-art GANs from the same era. This might limit its immediate adoption for high-fidelity image generation, although the authors correctly point out that this may be a limitation of the pixel-level L1 distance rather than PMD itself.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Novel and Sound Method:** Proposes a novel, theoretically-grounded method (PMD) for comparing distributions, directly linking it to the Wasserstein metric.
2.  **Solves Real Problems with a Popular Baseline:** PMD addresses key practical drawbacks of MMD, such as hyperparameter tuning and vanishing gradients, which are significant issues for practitioners.
3.  **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The paper provides compelling experimental results on two different tasks (domain adaptation, generative modeling), showing consistent improvements over MMD in both performance and convergence speed.
4.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, easy to follow, and presents a complete piece of work from theory to application and analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

1.  **Computational Cost:** The matching step is computationally expensive (O(N^3) or O(N^2)), which may limit its scalability and practical adoption compared to the conceptually simpler and often faster MMD.
2.  **Modest Generative Results:** The results on the generative modeling task, while showing an improvement over the baseline, are not state-of-the-art, which might understate the method's potential impact in that specific application area.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated, and effective method. The strengths, particularly the elegant theoretical foundation and the clear practical advantages over a widely-used baseline like MMD, far outweigh the weaknesses related to computational cost. The work is a significant contribution to the field of distribution comparison in deep learning and is very likely to inspire follow-up research. It meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS paper.
