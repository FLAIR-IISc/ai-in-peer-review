Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of the minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets from two distributions under a chosen ground metric d (e.g., L1). PMD is thus the empirical 1-Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance between uniform empirical measures with equal mass per sample. The authors show strong consistency of PMD for the 1-Wasserstein metric via standard results (Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport), and propose a training procedure that (i) treats the matching as fixed when differentiating (yielding a subgradient almost everywhere), and (ii) amortizes matching by reusing it for multiple SGD steps and subsampling matched pairs to allow small minibatches. They implement exact Hungarian and approximate O(N^2) matchers, and compare PMD mainly to MMD on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), reporting faster convergence, less sensitivity to hyperparameters, and better or comparable performance.

Relation to prior work
- PMD is precisely the empirical Wasserstein-1/EMD between point clouds with uniform weights. Its equivalence to assignment is classic (Rubner et al. 2000; Villani). The paper rebrands this but does not sufficiently acknowledge that the estimator and its consistency are well-known.
- There is substantial prior ML work on computational OT not cited/compared: Cuturi (2013) entropic regularization and Sinkhorn distances (GPU-friendly, differentiable), OT-based domain adaptation (Courty et al. 2014/2016), and recent Wasserstein training for generative models (WGAN, Cramer distance), as well as point set losses using EMD in deep learning. These omissions weaken the positioning.
- The MMD comparison overlooks linear-time unbiased MMD estimators that allow smaller minibatches and can mitigate some of the stated drawbacks; MMD-GAN and kernel mixtures are only partially addressed.

Quality
- Theory: The consistency result follows directly from standard OT theory; no finite-sample bounds or rates are provided. The key algorithmic step—ignoring the dependence of the discrete matching on parameters—yields a valid subgradient almost everywhere, but this should be explicitly justified (piecewise-constant matching with kinks on measure-zero boundaries). The claim of an “unbiased stochastic gradient” is too strong near switching regions and should be qualified.
- Algorithmics: Using assignment per population is computationally heavy relative to Sinkhorn; approximate matching is used, but no accuracy guarantees are discussed, and there is no comparison to entropic OT which is both fast and differentiable.
- Experiments: Comparisons are mostly against MMD only. For domain adaptation, stronger contemporary baselines (DANN, JAN, CORAL/CMD, OTDA) are not compared. For generative modeling, no quantitative image quality metrics (IS/FID) are reported; only qualitative samples and self-referential “final PMD/MMD” curves are shown. Claims of faster convergence should be supported by wall-clock time including matching (partial timing provided) and ideally by GPU-friendly implementations.
- Empirical observations (e.g., stronger per-sample gradients for PMD) are plausible and supported by histograms, but the evidence is limited to specific settings and does not explore alternative MMD settings (mixture bandwidths, MMD-GAN) or linear-time estimators.

Clarity
- Generally readable and organized. The connection to Wasserstein/EMD is clear, but the paper’s naming obscures that PMD is the empirical W1; this should be stated up front. Assumptions behind the gradient estimator (subgradient, reuse of matches, minibatch subsampling) should be made explicit, with caveats about when they break. More details on training protocol (population sizes, frequency of recomputing matches, cost per step) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- Conceptually limited. The estimator is standard (empirical W1/EMD); using it as a training loss via matched pairs is known in several contexts, and WGAN and Sinkhorn OT already provide alternative differentiable and scalable routes. The paper’s main novelty lies in the particular training heuristic (reuse of matchings and small minibatches) and empirical comparison to MMD.

Significance
- Potentially useful observation that assignment-based W1 can yield stronger gradients and permit smaller minibatches than quadratic-form MMD in some settings. However, without comparisons to state-of-the-art OT solvers (Sinkhorn) and to stronger baselines in DA and generative modeling, the impact is limited. Practical adoption is hampered by computational cost and by the lack of clear advantages over existing OT-based methods.

Pros
- Clear formulation and connection to W1; simple to implement with off-the-shelf matchers.
- Sensible training heuristic that can work well in practice; empirical evidence of stronger gradients than RBF-MMD.
- Competitive results vs MMD on Office and improved qualitative samples in some generative settings.

Cons
- Limited novelty: PMD = empirical W1/EMD; consistency is standard.
- Missing related work and comparisons (Sinkhorn OT, OT-based DA, WGAN/Cramer GAN, linear-time MMD).
- Questionable gradient claims (unbiasedness), no convergence analysis, and no finite-sample error bounds.
- Experimental evaluation lacks state-of-the-art baselines and quantitative metrics (IS/FID), and uses small-scale datasets.

Suggestions
- Reframe PMD explicitly as empirical W1/EMD; cite and compare to entropic OT (Sinkhorn) and OT-based DA.
- Provide theoretical clarifications on subgradients and matching changes; add finite-sample/statistical error analysis if possible.
- Strengthen experiments: add DANN/JAN/OTDA in DA; add WGAN/WAEs and Sinkhorn-based training in generative modeling; report IS/FID and wall-clock times; include linear-time MMD baselines.
- Explore feature-space ground metrics (perceptual) and Sinkhorn for scalability and better image fidelity.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The paper is clearly written and contains some useful empirical insights, but the core idea is a rebranding of a standard estimator with incomplete positioning relative to established OT methods and insufficient experimental comparisons to current baselines. With stronger related work treatment and more comprehensive experiments, this could be a solid workshop or revised submission. Confidence: High.
