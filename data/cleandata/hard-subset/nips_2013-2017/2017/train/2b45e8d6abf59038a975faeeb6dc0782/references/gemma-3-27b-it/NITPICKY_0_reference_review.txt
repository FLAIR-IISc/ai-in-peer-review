This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an estimator for the Wasserstein metric and proposes its use as a training objective for deep learning models. The core idea – framing the distributional distance as a minimum weight bipartite matching problem – is conceptually interesting and offers potential advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), particularly regarding gradient behavior and mini-batch size requirements. However, while the core ideas are promising, the paper suffers from a surprising number of small, but cumulatively significant, flaws in presentation, experimental detail, and theoretical justification.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of PMD as a minimum weight matching problem provides a fresh perspective on estimating Wasserstein distance.
*   **Potential Advantages:** The claimed benefits of PMD – no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes – are theoretically sound and could lead to faster and more stable training.
*   **Empirical Results:** The domain adaptation experiments show some improvement over MMD, although the margin isn’t overwhelming.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper is often difficult to follow. Several sentences are awkwardly worded, and the notation isn't always consistent (e.g., using both *N* and *M* for population sizes/permutations). The flow between theoretical explanations and practical implementation details is disjointed.
*   **Lack of Rigor in Theoretical Analysis:** While Theorem 3.3 states strong consistency, the proof relies heavily on citing existing theorems without substantial elaboration specific to the PMD context.  A more detailed explanation of *why* the conditions of Propositions 3.1 and 3.2 hold for PMD would significantly strengthen the argument.
*   **Inconsistent Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup is often vague.  For example, the exact architecture used for the neural networks in the generative modeling experiments is not sufficiently described.  The choice of the L1 distance for PMD is not adequately justified; other distance metrics should have been explored. The validation process for hyperparameter selection is described but the specific settings aren’t clearly presented.
*   **Minor Technical Flaws:**  The statement about the gradient not propagating through the matching in Eq. (3) feels too strong. While theoretically true for infinitesimal changes, SGD involves finite updates, and the matching *will* change as parameters are updated, potentially introducing errors.  The claim of GPU parallelization in Sec. 2.3 is mentioned but not elaborated upon.
*   **Unnecessary Claims/Overstatements:** The assertion that PMD does not require a separate critic network like WGAN is misleading. WGAN uses a critic to *approximate* the Wasserstein distance; PMD *estimates* it directly. This distinction is important and obscured by the phrasing.
*   **Formatting issues**: The superscript notation for set indexing (e.g. [N]) is unconventional.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Introduction:** The introduction is somewhat rambling and lacks focus. The motivation for using sample-based divergence estimation is well-established, but the connection to PMD isn't immediately clear.
*   **Section 2.1:** The definition of DN(X, Y) is clear, but the intuitive explanation could be expanded.  Why is this non-negative and symmetric?
*   **Section 2.2:** The assumption that transformations TX and TY are continuous is important but not fully justified. What if the transformation outputs a discrete value?
*   **Section 2.3:** While mentioning the time complexity is good, providing a concrete comparison (e.g., wall-clock time for different *N* values) would be more impactful. The remark about parallelizing on GPU is too brief.
*   **Section 3.1:** The connection to the Wasserstein metric is well-established, but the discussion feels rushed. A more intuitive explanation of the equivalence between the linear program and the minimum weight matching would be helpful.
*   **Section 3.2:** The claim about stronger gradients is supported by Fig. 2(b), but the explanation is not entirely convincing. Why does PMD produce more uniform gradients? A more detailed theoretical analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Section 4:** The application sections lack depth. The domain adaptation section simply states that PMD is used as a regularization term. How is it integrated into the overall training process?  The generative modeling section is similarly superficial.
*   **Section 5:** The experimental results are presented clearly, but the lack of detail about the network architectures and hyperparameter settings is a significant drawback. The choice of using the approximate matching for generative modeling and the exact Hungarian matching for other experiments is unexplained.  

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   The paper is plagued by numerous minor flaws in presentation, rigor, and experimental detail.
*   The experimental results, while positive, are not overwhelmingly convincing.
*   The theoretical justification is somewhat weak and relies heavily on existing results without sufficient elaboration.
*   The overall clarity of the paper is insufficient for easy understanding.

**For:**

*   The core idea of PMD is novel and potentially useful.
*   The claimed advantages of PMD are theoretically sound.
*   The empirical results suggest that PMD can outperform MMD in certain scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the core idea, the numerous flaws in the paper make it difficult to recommend acceptance. The lack of rigor, clarity, and experimental detail undermines the credibility of the results. While the ideas presented are valuable, the paper requires significant revision and polishing before it would be suitable for publication at NIPS. I recommend **rejection** with the suggestion that the authors address the concerns outlined above and resubmit a revised version. The potential of PMD is clear, but this version falls short of the high standards expected for NIPS publications.
