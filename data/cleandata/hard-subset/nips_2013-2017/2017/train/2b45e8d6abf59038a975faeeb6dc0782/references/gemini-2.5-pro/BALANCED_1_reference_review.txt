This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum cost of a bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions. The authors establish a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric (W1), proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1. The primary contribution is the application of PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep learning models. The paper positions PMD as a compelling alternative to the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD has fewer hyperparameters (no kernel bandwidth), provides stronger gradients, and allows for more efficient stochastic optimization by decoupling the population size from the SGD mini-batch size. The utility of PMD is demonstrated on two tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, where it is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD-based approaches.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper proposes a novel and elegant approach to approximate a true metric (Wasserstein-1) for training deep networks. While the Wasserstein distance has become popular through the dual formulation in WGAN, this work provides a practical algorithm based on the primal formulation. This offers a distinct, parameter-free alternative to both kernel-based methods like MMD and adversary-based methods like GANs. The idea is simple, well-motivated, and opens a new avenue for leveraging optimal transport theory in deep learning.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is clearly articulated and formally established. The proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3), while relying on existing results from optimal transport literature, provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. This grounding in established theory is a significant strength compared to more heuristic approaches.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the method is described precisely in Section 2, and the theoretical connections are detailed in Section 3. The comparisons to MMD and WGAN are explicit and insightful, helping the reader to situate the work within the current landscape. The algorithmic pseudocode and illustration in Figure 1 are also helpful.

4.  **Practical Advantages and Empirical Support:** The paper makes several compelling claims about the practical benefits of PMD over MMD, which are well-supported by experiments.
    *   **Fewer Hyperparameters:** The absence of a kernel and its associated bandwidth parameter is a clear practical advantage, as shown by the sensitivity analysis in Figure 3.
    *   **Stronger Gradients:** The analysis in Figure 2(b) empirically supports the claim that PMD provides more uniformly strong gradients across samples, which can prevent training from slowing down due to saturated kernels or outliers.
    *   **Efficient Optimization:** The proposed training scheme, which reuses a single matching solution for multiple smaller SGD updates, is a clever and practical trick. Figure 5(a) effectively demonstrates that this leads to better solutions than using a large batch size, validating a key claim of the paper.
    *   **Domain Adaptation Results:** The results on the Office benchmark are strong, showing that PMD can outperform MMD significantly on some adaptation tasks while being competitive on others (Table 1). The faster convergence shown in Figure 3(a) is also a valuable result.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The primary weakness of PMD is the computational cost of solving the minimum weight matching problem. The Hungarian algorithm has a complexity of O(N^3), and the approximation used is O(N^2). This is computationally more expensive than MMD, which is O(N^2 * D) for distance matrix computation and O(N^2) for the final loss. While the authors argue this cost is acceptable, it remains a significant bottleneck that may limit the "population size" N that can be used in practice, especially compared to the linear-time unbiased estimator of MMD that can also be used. The paper mentions the possibility of GPU parallelization as future work, but its absence here leaves a critical question about the method's scalability unanswered.

2.  **Quality of Generative Modeling Results:** While the generative modeling experiments demonstrate the feasibility of using PMD, the qualitative results (Figure 4) are not state-of-the-art. The generated images for SVHN and LFW are noticeably blurry and lack diversity compared to modern GANs (e.g., WGAN-GP, StyleGAN). The comparison is made only against GMMN (an MMD-based method), not against stronger adversarial baselines. While the authors correctly note that the pixel-wise L1 distance may be a cause, this limits the demonstrated impact of PMD in this widely studied application area.

3.  **Oversimplified Gradient Argument:** The claim that the constant-magnitude gradient of the L1 norm is strictly an advantage is an oversimplification. While it avoids the vanishing gradient problem of RBF kernels, a constant gradient magnitude can be suboptimal, potentially causing oscillations or overshooting when samples are already close to their targets. A more nuanced discussion acknowledging this trade-off would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Lack of Deeper Theoretical Analysis:** While the strong consistency result is important, the theoretical analysis could be deepened. The paper would benefit from a discussion of finite-sample error bounds for PMD as an estimator of W1. The convergence rate of W1 estimators is known to suffer from the curse of dimensionality, which is a critical point of comparison against MMD, whose sample complexity is dimension-independent. The empirical finding of an error rate of N⁻⁰.²³ (Section 5.4) hints at slow convergence, which warrants more discussion.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Computational Analysis:** It would be beneficial to provide a more direct wall-clock time comparison between PMD, MMD (quadratic and linear estimators), and one epoch of WGAN training for a fixed population/batch size. This would give readers a more concrete understanding of the practical computational trade-offs.

2.  **Strengthening Generative Modeling:** To better showcase PMD's potential, the authors could experiment with using PMD on features from a pre-trained network (as done in the "ae" experiments) or from an intermediate layer of a discriminator, similar to feature matching in GANs. This could potentially yield much better image quality by using a more perceptually relevant distance metric.

3.  **Ablation Study:** The idea of using a small SGD mini-batch |B| with a larger population N is a key algorithmic contribution. An ablation study analyzing the effect of the number of inner-loop updates per matching would be very valuable. How many updates can be performed before the matching becomes too stale? This would provide practical guidance for users.

4.  **Refining the Gradient Discussion:** The discussion in Section 3.2 could be revised to be more balanced, mentioning that while the L1 gradient avoids vanishing, it lacks the adaptive nature of the RBF kernel gradient, which naturally becomes smaller as points get closer.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, theoretically-grounded, and parameter-free method for comparing distributions in deep learning.
*   Establishes a clear connection to the Wasserstein metric via a primal formulation, offering a valuable alternative to the dominant dual/adversarial approaches.
*   Demonstrates clear practical advantages over MMD in terms of hyperparameter tuning and optimization efficiency.
*   Shows strong empirical performance on the challenging task of unsupervised domain adaptation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The computational cost of the matching algorithm is a significant drawback that may limit scalability.
*   The performance on generative modeling is underwhelming compared to state-of-the-art methods, and the baselines are not the strongest.
*   The theoretical analysis lacks finite-sample bounds, which are crucial for understanding the estimator's behavior.

**Decision:**
This paper presents a novel and valuable contribution to the field. The core idea of using minimum weight matching as a proxy for the Wasserstein distance is both elegant and effective. The strengths in originality, theoretical grounding, and strong performance on domain adaptation outweigh the weaknesses related to computational cost and the visual quality of generated samples. The work is likely to inspire further research into primal-form optimal transport methods in deep learning. I recommend acceptance.
