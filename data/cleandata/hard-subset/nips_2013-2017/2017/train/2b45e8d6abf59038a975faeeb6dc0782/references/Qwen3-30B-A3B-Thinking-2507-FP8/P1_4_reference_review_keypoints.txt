- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based distance defined via minimum weight matching between sample sets and shown to strongly consistently estimate the 1-Wasserstein metric.  
- Proposes using PMD as a training objective in deep learning with an algorithm and matching-based implementation.  
- Practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth tuning, empirically stronger gradients, and effective with smaller mini-batches.  
- Thorough empirical validation across domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW) showing consistent improvements and faster convergence versus MMD.  
- Clear, well-organized presentation with solid theoretical foundations and logical flow.  
- Provides useful implementation details and compares exact vs. approximate matching algorithms and their computational trade-offs.  
- Lacks finite-sample error bounds for PMD; only strong consistency is proven.  
- Insufficient comparison to recent distributional-distance methods (e.g., WGAN-GP, Cramer GAN, other OT-based approaches).  
- Claim of stronger gradients relative to MMD is supported empirically but lacks deeper theoretical justification.  
- Uses primarily L1 distance for PMD; more investigation of alternative metrics (L2, Mahalanobis) is needed, especially for images.  
- Computational complexity scaling with very large populations is not analyzed in sufficient detail.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel contribution with solid theory and empirical support; practical and relevant for deep learning.  
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, well-executed, and valuable to the community despite the noted theoretical and comparative gaps.  
- Suggested future work: develop scalable matching algorithms, explore different distance metrics, and derive finite-sample error bounds.
