Summary
This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. samples drawn from two distributions, as a sample-based, differentiable surrogate for the 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance. The authors (i) formalize PMD as the linear assignment problem with ground distance d (typically L1), (ii) prove strong consistency to the true W1 via standard empirical measure convergence and stability of optimal transport (OT), (iii) propose to use PMD as a training objective for deep models by computing a matching for moderately large “populations” (N ≈ 1000–4000), then performing several SGD steps while keeping the matching fixed, and even sub-sampling mini-batches from the matched pairs, and (iv) compare PMD experimentally to MMD in unsupervised domain adaptation (Office) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW). They report faster convergence, robustness to hyperparameters (no bandwidth), and better qualitative samples than MMD.

Relation to prior work
- PMD is the empirical OT distance between two uniform discrete measures, i.e., the Earth Mover’s distance (Rubner et al.; Villani). The theoretical consistency leverages classic results (Varadarajan; stability of OT), and is well known in OT literature.
- The central practical contribution is using assignment-based empirical OT as a loss for deep models with a “frozen matching” inner loop and mini-batch subsampling. Related, but missing from the discussion, is the extensive line on entropically-regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) that provide fast, differentiable, GPU-friendly approximations and have been used for learning; also stochastic Sinkhorn/OT methods (e.g., Genevay et al., NIPS’16/17).
- For domain adaptation, there is a directly relevant body of work on OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., Courty, Flamary, Tuia, 2016/2017) that matches source/target samples with class regularization; these should be discussed and compared.
- For generative modeling, comparisons to WGAN (and variants such as gradient penalty) and sliced Wasserstein methods (e.g., Kolouri et al., 2017) would better position the contribution. The paper discusses WGAN briefly but does not empirically compare.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive objective grounded in OT, with a clear algorithmic recipe. The “frozen matching” trick is practical and easy to integrate into deep learning pipelines.
- Useful empirical observations: PMD’s gradients are more uniform across samples than RBF-MMD, and training can proceed with smaller inner-loop mini-batches. The convergence plots and gradient magnitude histograms are informative.
- The method is parameter-light (no kernel bandwidth), which is appealing in practice.
- Experiments cover two application domains and include some timing/ablation analysis (exact vs approximate matching, N vs batch size).

Weaknesses / Concerns
- Novelty: Conceptually, PMD is the standard empirical W1/EMD with uniform weights and assignment. The theoretical result (strong consistency) follows directly from classical theorems; there is no new analysis (e.g., finite-sample rates, variance, or sample complexity). The new part is the training heuristic (reuse match, subsample), which is useful but ad hoc and not theoretically justified.
- Gradients and unbiasedness: Equation (3) claims an unbiased stochastic gradient by ignoring the dependence of the optimal permutation M* on θ. This requires care: the objective is piecewise smooth with kinks when the active matching changes; the gradient is a subgradient of a pointwise min, but unbiasedness is not established, and uniqueness/regularity assumptions are not stated. The further approximation in Eq. (4), reusing M* for sub-batches, introduces additional bias. Some discussion of subgradient calculus and convergence (even informal) would strengthen the paper.
- Computational trade-offs and baselines: While assignment can be acceptable for N in the low thousands, the paper does not compare to entropic OT/Sinkhorn, which is the de facto scalable and differentiable alternative and likely more GPU-friendly than Hungarian/approximate matching. This is a major omission given the stated goal of deep learning applications. Linear-time unbiased MMD estimators (e.g., linear-time MMD, Gretton et al.) are not considered in the runtime/batch-size comparisons, weakening the argument that MMD necessarily requires large batches.
- Experimental scope: 
  - Domain adaptation: Results are limited to Office with shallow heads over VGG features. Comparisons are only to MMD-based DA; stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, OTDA) are absent. Also, hyperparameter tuning uses labeled target validation, which departs from strict unsupervised DA and may bias the comparison.
  - Generative modeling: Evaluation is mostly qualitative; no standard metrics (Inception score, FID) are reported. PMD images in pixel space are blurry; using a perceptual/code-space ground metric alleviates this, but this raises the question of whether the core benefit is the choice of feature space rather than the discrepancy.
- Claims: “Parameter free” is overstated; PMD depends on the ground metric and λ in DA, and the algorithm introduces knobs (population size N, number of inner steps). The statement that WGAN only provides a value up to a scale is less relevant for training; and WGAN variants address gradient issues not discussed here.

Clarity and reproducibility
- Overall clear and readable; algorithmic description and pseudocode are helpful. Some notation is slightly overloaded; the discussion around gradients would benefit from a precise statement about differentiability and using subgradients.
- Implementation details for matching (frequency of recomputation, number of inner SGD steps, and criteria for refreshing M*) should be specified more concretely to aid reproduction.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare against entropic OT/Sinkhorn (with/without regularization annealing) as a fast, differentiable alternative. This could also mitigate non-differentiability and allow backprop through a soft transport plan γ.
- Provide a brief theoretical discussion (or empirical ablation) on how often M* changes and how this affects convergence; consider using a warm-started assignment solver or incremental matching updates.
- Include stronger baselines in DA (DANN, JAN, OTDA) and quantitative metrics in generative modeling (FID/Inception) for a more convincing case.
- Explore learned or perceptual ground metrics (feature extractors) to improve sample quality, and report the effect systematically.
- Discuss and/or adopt linear-time MMD estimators as a fair runtime/batch-size baseline.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, simple-to-use objective with promising empirical behavior and useful training heuristics.
- Clear articulation of gradient properties and mini-batch behavior relative to MMD.
- Potentially impactful for practitioners frustrated by MMD hyperparameters.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond applying empirical OT/assignment as a loss; missing key related work (Sinkhorn, OT-based DA).
- Weak theoretical footing for the proposed gradient approximations; no finite-sample/statistical analysis.
- Experimental comparisons and metrics are limited; absence of strong contemporary baselines.

Overall evaluation
The paper addresses a relevant problem and presents a practical approach with some insightful empirical observations. However, the contribution is incremental relative to existing OT literature, misses important related methods (notably Sinkhorn OT), and lacks rigorous analysis of the proposed training heuristics. Strengthening baselines and situating PMD against modern OT approximations would substantially improve the significance. As it stands, I lean toward rejection, but I see a clear path to a strong submission with the above additions.
