This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an estimator for the Wasserstein metric and proposes its use as a training objective for deep learning models. While the idea of leveraging the Wasserstein metric is not new (as evidenced by WGAN and related work), the authors argue that PMD offers advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) – namely, no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use normal mini-batch sizes. 

The core concept of PMD – minimizing the cost of matching samples between distributions – is relatively straightforward and builds upon established optimal transport theory. The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is clearly stated and supported by appropriate citations. However, the claims of significant advantages over MMD require more robust justification. The argument about stronger gradients, while intuitively appealing and supported by a limited empirical demonstration (Fig. 2b), feels somewhat hand-wavy and doesn’t fully explain *why* PMD consistently provides better gradient flow, especially considering the potential for gradient vanishing even with the L1 distance. 

The experimental evaluation, encompassing domain adaptation and generative modeling, is a good start, but not entirely convincing. The performance gains on the Office dataset are present, but not dramatic, and the improvements appear task-dependent. Critically, the comparison lacks thorough hyperparameter tuning for both methods – the authors mention validating parameters but the details are limited. A more rigorous comparison would necessitate a grid search or automated optimization of both PMD and MMD hyperparameters, ensuring a fair assessment. The generative modeling experiments, while showing visually improved results with PMD on SVHN and LFW, lack quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) for a more objective evaluation. Relying solely on visual inspection is insufficient. The observation that PMD struggles with pixel-level L1 distance highlights a limitation and the need to explore more appropriate distance metrics.

The discussion of computational complexity is appreciated, and the acknowledgement that both exact and approximate matching can be accelerated with GPU is important. However, a more detailed timing analysis, including the overhead of the matching algorithm itself (especially for larger population sizes), would strengthen the argument for PMD’s practical efficiency.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear motivation and connection to established concepts in optimal transport.
*   Well-defined PMD formulation and theoretical justification.
*   Empirical demonstration of potential benefits in domain adaptation and generative modeling.
*   Acknowledges limitations and potential areas for improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Claims of significant advantages over MMD lack conclusive evidence.
*   Limited hyperparameter tuning and fair comparison with MMD.
*   Insufficient quantitative evaluation in generative modeling.
*   Intuitive argument for stronger gradients isn't fully substantiated.
*   Reliance on L1 distance may limit applicability to complex data.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric and leverages it for deep learning tasks.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for PMD.
*   The experimental results, while not overwhelming, suggest potential benefits over MMD.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous to definitively establish the superiority of PMD.
*   The claims of significant advantages are not fully supported by evidence.
*   The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to other Wasserstein metric estimation methods (e.g., WGAN).
*   The presentation could benefit from more detailed discussion of computational costs and practical considerations.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am currently leaning towards **reject**. While the paper presents an interesting idea with a solid theoretical foundation, the lack of a convincing empirical evaluation and the somewhat overstated claims of advantages over MMD prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by conducting more thorough experiments with rigorous hyperparameter tuning and quantitative evaluation, and by providing a more compelling justification for the claimed benefits of PMD. This work has potential, but requires further refinement and validation before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field.
