

# Review for "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein metric that uses minimum weight matching between samples from two distributions. The authors prove PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and propose a training algorithm for learning parameters of distributions using PMD. They compare PMD with MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, claiming advantages in no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch size requirements.

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, establishing PMD as a strongly consistent estimator through rigorous mathematical arguments (Theorem 3.3, Propositions 3.1-3.2).

2. **Clear comparison with MMD**: The paper effectively contrasts PMD with MMD, highlighting specific advantages including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients (Fig. 2b), and smaller mini-batch requirements (Sec. 3.2). The empirical validation of gradient strength is particularly convincing.

3. **Comprehensive experiments**: The authors conduct thorough experiments on both domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), showing PMD outperforms MMD in convergence speed and sometimes performance.

4. **Practical implementation**: The paper provides detailed implementation details (Sec. 5.3, Fig. 5) showing that PMD's computational cost is acceptable compared to neural network training, with both exact and approximate matching algorithms working well.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited comparison with recent Wasserstein approximations**: The paper mentions WGAN [3] but doesn't thoroughly contrast PMD with other recent Wasserstein approximation methods like WGAN-GP, Sliced Wasserstein, or the Cramer distance [Bellemare et al., 2017]. This weakens the claim of PMD's novelty in the context of current literature.

2. **Generative modeling results are suboptimal**: The paper acknowledges that PMD-generated images on SVHN and LFW are "blurry" (Sec. 5.2). This is a significant drawback for generative modeling applications, yet the paper doesn't adequately address why this happens or propose solutions. The authors suggest using code space, but this isn't tested.

3. **Misleading claim about no hyperparameters**: While PMD avoids the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter of MMD, the choice of distance metric (L1 in their experiments) is still a hyperparameter. The paper should clarify this nuance.

4. **Limited theoretical analysis**: The paper focuses only on the first Wasserstein metric, but many applications (e.g., generative modeling) often use higher-order metrics. The paper doesn't discuss how PMD would generalize to these cases.

5. **Computational complexity**: While the paper mentions O(N^3) complexity for Hungarian algorithm, it doesn't provide a clear comparison of actual runtime versus the cost of training neural networks. The claim that "O(N^3) time complexity is acceptable" needs stronger empirical support.

## Constructive Suggestions
1. **Expand comparison with state-of-the-art**: Include a more thorough comparison with recent Wasserstein approximation methods, particularly those designed for deep learning applications.

2. **Address generative modeling limitations**: Investigate why PMD generates blurry images on natural datasets and propose solutions (e.g., using perceptual losses, different distance metrics, or code space approaches).

3. **Clarify hyperparameter discussion**: Acknowledge that the choice of distance metric is a hyperparameter and discuss how it affects performance.

4. **Add theoretical analysis for high-dimensional spaces**: Discuss the potential limitations of PMD in high-dimensional settings, which is crucial for many deep learning applications.

5. **Provide more detailed runtime analysis**: Include a clear comparison of PMD's computational cost versus MMD's across different dataset sizes and model architectures.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a theoretically grounded and practically useful approach to estimating statistical divergence between distributions. The connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is well-established, and the empirical results are generally compelling. The main weaknesses lie in the incomplete comparison with recent literature and the suboptimal generative modeling results. With the suggested improvements, particularly addressing the generative modeling limitations and expanding the comparison with state-of-the-art methods, this paper would make a significant contribution to the field.

The paper's strengths in theoretical foundation and clear comparison with MMD outweigh its weaknesses, though the generative modeling results require further investigation. The work is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning with both theoretical and practical significance.

**Decision**: Accept (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above)
