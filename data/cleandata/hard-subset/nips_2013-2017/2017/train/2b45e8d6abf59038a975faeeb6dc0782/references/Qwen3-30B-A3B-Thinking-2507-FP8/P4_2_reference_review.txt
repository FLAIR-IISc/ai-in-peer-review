

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. The authors propose PMD as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric with several practical advantages. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching between two samples from the distributions, which the authors show can be used as a training objective in deep learning tasks. The paper demonstrates PMD's application in domain adaptation and generative modeling, comparing it to MMD across multiple experiments.

The main contribution of this paper is the novel PMD formulation and its practical advantages over MMD. The authors establish theoretical connections between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, proving PMD is a strongly consistent estimator. They also provide empirical evidence that PMD has stronger gradients, requires no bandwidth hyperparameter, and can use smaller mini-batch sizes than MMD. The paper compares PMD with MMD on the Office dataset for domain adaptation and on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW for generative modeling, with results showing PMD generally outperforms MMD in both tasks.

**Quality**: The paper presents technically sound work with solid theoretical foundations. The proof of PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric is clear and well-supported by Propositions 3.1-3.3. The experimental design is comprehensive, with multiple datasets, architectures, and ablation studies. The authors carefully evaluate both the advantages and limitations of PMD (e.g., acknowledging the blurry images in generative modeling due to L1 distance). The implementation details are sufficient for reproducibility, though some GPU acceleration details could be expanded.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The main concepts are explained with appropriate mathematical formalism, and the pseudocode (Fig. 1) effectively illustrates the parameter learning algorithm. The theoretical analysis is presented in a logical sequence, and the empirical results are clearly presented with informative figures (Figs. 2-5). The writing is precise, and the paper adequately informs the reader about both the theoretical and practical aspects of PMD.

**Originality**: PMD represents a novel approach to estimating statistical divergences. While the Wasserstein metric has been previously used in machine learning, the specific formulation as a minimum weight matching between samples is new. The paper clearly differentiates PMD from MMD (by eliminating the bandwidth parameter) and from WGAN (by not requiring a critic network). The authors cite relevant prior work on MMD, Wasserstein metrics, and related applications, and their comparison with these approaches demonstrates the novelty of their contribution.

**Significance**: The paper addresses an important practical problem in deep learning - estimating statistical divergences between distributions when only samples are available. Many deep learning tasks (domain adaptation, generative modeling) rely on such divergences as training objectives. The demonstrated advantages of PMD (stronger gradients, no bandwidth tuning, faster convergence) suggest it could be widely adopted in practice. The paper shows PMD outperforms MMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, advancing the state of the art in divergence estimation for deep learning.

**Strengths**:
1. Clear theoretical justification for PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric
2. Well-designed experiments showing consistent advantages of PMD over MMD across multiple tasks
3. Practical demonstration of PMD in two important deep learning applications
4. Detailed analysis of gradient properties and parameter sensitivity
5. Clear articulation of PMD's advantages over MMD (no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size)

**Weaknesses**:
1. Limited theoretical analysis beyond consistency (e.g., no finite-sample error bounds)
2. The generative modeling results show blurry images for natural images, suggesting limitations of the L1 distance approach
3. The paper does not fully explore how PMD might be adapted for better performance in image generation (e.g., using feature space distances)
4. Limited comparison with other recent Wasserstein approximations beyond WGAN

**Overall assessment**: This paper presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful approach to statistical divergence estimation in deep learning. The main contribution is well-motivated, well-executed, and demonstrates clear advantages over the widely used MMD. The theoretical analysis is strong, and the empirical results consistently support the claims. While the generative modeling results for natural images could be improved with additional modifications, this does not undermine the core contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in statistical divergence estimation for deep learning applications.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a novel, theoretically grounded, and practically valuable approach to statistical divergence estimation. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the paper is well-written and clearly organized. The work is highly relevant to the NIPS community, addressing a key challenge in deep learning applications.
