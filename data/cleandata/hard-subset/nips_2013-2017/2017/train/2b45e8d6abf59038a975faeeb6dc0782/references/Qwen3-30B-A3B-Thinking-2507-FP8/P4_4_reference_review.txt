

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric that is defined as the minimum weight matching between two random samples from two distributions. The authors establish theoretical connections between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. They propose using PMD as a training objective for deep learning tasks, particularly domain adaptation and generative modeling, and demonstrate its advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in terms of hyperparameter sensitivity, gradient strength, and mini-batch size requirements.

The paper makes a clear contribution by directly approximating the primal form of the Wasserstein metric using minimum weight matching, rather than the dual form used in WGAN or other approaches. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 establishes the connection to optimal transport theory, showing that PMD converges to the Wasserstein metric as the population size increases. The authors provide a rigorous proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3), building on Varadarajan's theorem and the stability of optimal transport.

The paper's experimental evaluation is thorough, with comparisons on standard benchmarks including the Office dataset for domain adaptation and MNIST, SVHN, and LFW for generative modeling. The results consistently show PMD outperforms MMD in both performance and convergence speed. The ablation studies (Section 5.3) effectively demonstrate the advantages of PMD's smaller mini-batch size and insensitivity to population size.

Strengths:
1. Strong theoretical foundation with clear proofs of consistency and relationship to Wasserstein metric.
2. Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple tasks and datasets.
3. Clear demonstration of practical advantages over MMD (no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size).
4. Well-organized presentation with clear figures illustrating key results.
5. Appropriate comparison with related work (MMD, WGAN).

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not directly compare PMD with WGAN on the same tasks, which would strengthen the position that PMD is a better alternative to existing approaches.
2. The computational complexity of the matching algorithm (O(N^3) for exact Hungarian) might be a limitation for large-scale applications, though the authors acknowledge this and propose approximate matching.
3. The paper doesn't explore the use of more sophisticated distance metrics than L1 for natural images, which could address the blurry generation issue noted in Section 5.2.
4. The paper lacks theoretical analysis of convergence rates for PMD versus MMD, relying solely on empirical evidence.

Novelty: The paper presents a novel approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric directly from samples using minimum weight matching. While the Wasserstein metric has been used in machine learning, this specific approximation method appears to be new to the literature. The authors correctly position their work as distinct from both MMD (which uses kernel-based distance) and WGAN (which approximates the dual form), making a clear contribution to the field of statistical distance estimation for deep learning.

Significance: The advantages of PMD (no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size) are practically significant for deep learning practitioners. The paper demonstrates these advantages across multiple important applications (domain adaptation and generative modeling), showing that PMD can improve both performance and training efficiency. The approach could be particularly valuable for practitioners who find tuning MMD's bandwidth parameter challenging.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are thorough and well-documented. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the L1 distance being suboptimal for natural images.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written with a logical structure. The main ideas are well-explained, and the figures effectively illustrate key concepts. The algorithm pseudocode (Fig. 1) provides useful implementation details.

Overall, this paper makes a solid contribution to the field of statistical distance estimation for deep learning. The theoretical foundation is strong, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, and the practical advantages of PMD over MMD are clearly demonstrated. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding computational complexity and distance metric choice for generative modeling), these do not undermine the main contribution. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it addresses a significant problem in machine learning with a novel, theoretically grounded approach that has practical implications.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, clear demonstration of advantages over MMD, comprehensive experimental evaluation, well-written and well-organized.
Con: Limited comparison with WGAN, computational complexity concerns for large-scale applications, doesn't address distance metric for natural images.
