Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance that computes the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets. PMD is used as a differentiable training objective by assuming the optimal matching is locally stable, so gradients do not flow through the discrete matching. The authors show strong consistency of PMD to W1 via standard results (Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport), argue PMD has stronger gradients and fewer hyperparameters than MMD, and present applications to unsupervised domain adaptation and implicit generative modeling. They provide an approximate O(N^2) matching option, empirical analysis of batch-size sensitivity, and limited comparisons to MMD, reporting better performance and faster convergence on several tasks.

Relation to prior work
PMD corresponds to the empirical OT/EMD between empirical measures. There is substantial related OT literature absent from the discussion that is directly relevant: entropic-regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, NIPS’13), differentiable Sinkhorn losses now widely used for generative modeling and domain adaptation; stochastic/mini-batch OT and sample complexity results (e.g., Genevay et al.; Fournier & Guillin); OT-based domain adaptation (Courty, Flamary, Tuia et al.). The dual approach (WGAN) is correctly discussed, and MMD baselines (GMMN, MMD-GAN) are included.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity: training by directly minimizing empirical W1 via matching is intuitive and parameter-free (no kernel bandwidth).
- The consistency statement is correct and appropriate; the connection to the OT linear program is clean.
- Practical heuristics: using approximate matching and decoupling population size N from SGD mini-batch size |B| is a useful engineering observation and seems to work empirically.
- Informative gradient discussion: the “stronger gradient” argument vs RBF-MMD is plausible and supported by an empirical gradient magnitude analysis.
- Applications: PMD improves over MMD on several Office DA tasks and yields less noisy generations than MMD in pixel space; convergence speed and sensitivity studies are useful.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited. Using empirical OT/EMD as a training loss is well known; strong consistency is classical. The main novelty is the particular training heuristic (fix matching for multiple SGD steps and sub-sample matched pairs), and the emphasis on batch-size advantages vs MMD. This reads as an incremental alternative to MMD rather than a fundamentally new concept.
- Missing key related work. Entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) provides a scalable, differentiable surrogate with GPU-friendly implementations, and is now standard for learning with OT; OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al.) is directly relevant; sliced Wasserstein distances are another important, scalable W1 surrogate. The paper should compare and position PMD against these.
- Empirical scope. Comparisons are only to MMD variants. For DA, stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, DSN, CMD) are missing; for generative modeling, WGAN/DCGAN/Sinkhorn-based losses are missing. Reported generations are blurry and not competitive with contemporary GANs; FID/IS metrics are not reported.
- Scalability. O(N^3) exact matching and O(N^2) approximate matching remain expensive. While the authors argue the cost is acceptable relative to backprop, in modern settings with large models and datasets, the extra O(N^2) per population is significant. The lack of GPU/parallel implementation in experiments weakens the practicality claim, and no wall-clock comparisons vs robust baselines are provided.
- Optimization details. Treating the matching as fixed for several steps and subsampling matched pairs introduces bias; there is no analysis of how often re-matching is needed or how this affects convergence. The non-smoothness and potential discontinuities when matchings change are not discussed beyond an informal “almost everywhere” argument.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; the consistency result is correct. The gradient-through-matching argument is acceptable almost everywhere, but the training heuristic (stale matchings, biased mini-batch estimates) lacks theoretical justification. Experimental methodology is careful where it compares to MMD, but the baseline coverage is insufficient to establish state of the art.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The presentation of PMD and its relation to OT and MMD is easy to follow. The paper would benefit from:
- Explicit discussion of non-differentiability/subgradients when matchings change.
- More details on the approximate matching algorithm used and its quality guarantees.
- A clearer computational budget comparison (matching vs forward/backward passes) with wall-clock numbers.

Originality
Moderate. The paper repackages empirical OT/EMD with a training heuristic and focuses on its advantages over MMD. Given the breadth of existing OT-based learning, the contribution feels incremental without comparisons to Sinkhorn/sliced OT and recent OT DA methods.

Significance
Potentially useful as a simple, parameter-free alternative to MMD in some regimes; the “small |B| vs large N” observation is practically interesting. However, without broader comparisons and stronger results, it is hard to see PMD displacing established OT-based losses in practice.

Questions for the authors
- How often do you recompute the matching in practice, and how sensitive is performance to this frequency?
- Can you report wall-clock training time vs MMD, WGAN, and a Sinkhorn loss on the same hardware?
- Why not use a perceptual or feature-space cost for PMD in pixel-space generation to mitigate blurriness?
- Can you compare PMD against Sinkhorn-based domain adaptation and WGAN on the same tasks?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, bold idea applied across two areas; easy to implement.
- Clear empirical advantage over MMD in several settings; useful insights about gradient behavior and batch sizing.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to established OT literature; missing key related work and baselines.
- Modest empirical results for generative modeling; scalability concerns not convincingly addressed.
- Heuristic optimization (stale matching) lacks analysis.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is clean and potentially useful, but the contribution is incremental given existing OT-based methods, the experimental comparisons are too narrow, and important related work is missing. With stronger baselines (WGAN, Sinkhorn/sliced OT), clearer scalability evidence, and a tighter connection to modern OT literature, this could become a solid contribution.

Confidence
High.
