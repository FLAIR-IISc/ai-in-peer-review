

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper proposes "Population Matching Discrepancy" (PMD) as a new method for estimating statistical divergence between two distributions, claiming advantages over the established Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors define PMD as the minimum weight matching between two sample populations, which they claim is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. They argue PMD has no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and can use smaller mini-batch sizes than MMD.

The theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is not novel - the paper essentially rederives a known relationship between optimal transport and minimum weight matching (see Villani's "Optimal Transport: Old and New" and the classic Hungarian algorithm literature). The "strong consistency" claim is based on straightforward applications of Varadarajan's theorem and stability of optimal transport, which are well-established results. The paper fails to present any new theoretical contributions beyond this rederivation.

The empirical evaluation is limited and raises significant concerns. The authors compare PMD only against MMD on a small number of tasks (Office dataset for domain adaptation, MNIST/SVHN/LFW for generative modeling). They don't compare against the Wasserstein metric itself (which is the target they claim to approximate), nor against other established methods like the recently popularized WGAN. Their claim about "stronger gradients" is supported by a single figure (Fig. 2b) showing normalized gradient magnitudes without demonstrating how this translates to actual performance improvements beyond the claimed convergence speed.

The computational complexity analysis is misleading. The paper claims PMD can use smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs MMD's 1000), but this overlooks the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm. For N=100, the Hungarian algorithm requires approximately 1,000,000 operations, while MMD with N=1000 requires only O(N^2)=1,000,000 operations for the kernel matrix. The paper's Fig. 5(c) shows the Hungarian algorithm takes longer than SGD, and they acknowledge their implementation is single-threaded (not GPU-accelerated as they claim is possible).

The generative modeling results are particularly concerning. The authors claim PMD generates "less noisy" images than MMD, but their Fig. 4 shows blurry images for SVHN and LFW, with no comparison to state-of-the-art generative models (e.g., GANs, VAEs). Their explanation that "pixel-level L1 distance is not good for natural images" undermines their core claim about PMD's advantages.

The paper makes several unsupported claims: that PMD "has no bandwidth hyper-parameter" (it depends on the distance metric, which may require tuning), and that it "can use normal mini-batch size" (the paper itself uses N=2000 for population size, which is larger than typical mini-batch sizes).

The most significant weakness is the lack of thorough validation. The paper doesn't compare against the Wasserstein metric itself (the target metric they claim to approximate), doesn't provide error bounds or convergence rates, and doesn't demonstrate PMD's advantages in large-scale settings. The theoretical claims are minimal, and the empirical evidence is insufficient to support the paper's claims of superiority over MMD.

Strengths: Clear presentation of the matching problem, reasonable theoretical foundation for the connection to Wasserstein metric, and some practical implementation details.

Weaknesses: Overstated claims, limited empirical validation, misleading complexity analysis, lack of comparison against the target metric (Wasserstein), and insufficient theoretical contribution.

In terms of significance, the paper does not advance the state of the art demonstrably. The proposed method is a simple recombination of known techniques (minimum weight matching + Wasserstein metric) without providing compelling evidence that it offers substantial advantages over existing methods in practical settings. The paper would have been significantly stronger with comparisons against the Wasserstein metric itself, a thorough analysis of computational trade-offs, and more extensive experiments on larger datasets.

Overall, this paper presents a method that is not sufficiently novel, not rigorously validated, and lacks compelling evidence of its advantages over established approaches. The claims about PMD's advantages over MMD are not adequately supported by the presented evidence. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with the Wasserstein metric itself (not just MMD), a more realistic analysis of computational costs, and additional empirical evidence demonstrating PMD's superiority in practical deep learning applications.

I recommend rejection on grounds of insufficient novelty, inadequate validation, and overstated claims. The paper would require substantially more evidence to support its claims before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
