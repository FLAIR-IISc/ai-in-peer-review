### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence measure for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the respective distributions. The authors establish a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator.

The primary contribution is positioning PMD as a practical alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The paper argues that PMD has several advantages over MMD for training deep neural networks: (1) it is hyperparameter-free (specifically, no kernel bandwidth to tune), (2) it provides stronger, non-saturating gradients, and (3) it allows for efficient stochastic optimization using smaller mini-batch sizes than the full population size. The paper proposes a training algorithm where the optimal matching is computed periodically, and then multiple SGD steps are taken on mini-batches of the matched pairs.

The efficacy of PMD is demonstrated on two common deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. In both applications, empirical results show that PMD achieves performance comparable to or better than MMD, often with faster convergence.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The idea of using minimum weight bipartite matching as a differentiable loss for training deep models is simple, elegant, and well-motivated. It provides a direct, non-parametric method for approximating the Wasserstein-1 distance, which is a powerful and increasingly popular metric in machine learning.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper does an excellent job of grounding PMD in optimal transport theory. Section 3.1 clearly shows how PMD on empirical distributions is an exact solution to the Wasserstein-1 problem between those same empirical distributions. The argument for its strong consistency as an estimator for the true Wasserstein metric is sound and relies on established theoretical results.

3.  **Well-Articulated Advantages over MMD:** The paper makes a compelling case against MMD in the context of deep learning and highlights how PMD addresses its key weaknesses.
    *   The removal of the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter is a significant practical advantage, as MMD's performance can be very sensitive to this choice.
    *   The analysis of the gradient properties (Section 3.2, Figure 2b) is insightful. The argument that PMD's L1-based gradient does not saturate, unlike the RBF kernel gradient in MMD, provides a strong theoretical reason for its faster and more stable training dynamics.
    *   The proposed SGD scheme, which decouples the population size `N` from the SGD mini-batch size `|B|`, is a crucial practical contribution that makes the method computationally efficient and leverages the benefits of small-batch SGD.

4.  **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-chosen and effectively support the paper's claims.
    *   In domain adaptation, PMD shows competitive results and faster convergence (Figure 3a). The sensitivity analysis (Figure 3b, 3c) clearly illustrates the practical benefit of having fewer hyperparameters.
    *   In generative modeling, the qualitative results (Figure 4) suggest PMD can produce higher-quality samples than MMD-based GMMNs, particularly when operating directly on pixel space.
    *   The ablation studies in Section 5.3 are particularly strong, convincingly demonstrating the robustness of PMD to population size and the significant benefit of using a small SGD mini-batch size.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The primary weakness of PMD is the computational cost of solving the matching problem, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. While the authors correctly argue that this can be acceptable compared to the cost of backpropagation, this may not hold for architectures with fewer parameters or for very large population sizes `N`. The reliance on a single-threaded C++ implementation is a current limitation. Mentioning the specific values of `N` where the matching time starts to dominate the backpropagation time would provide readers with a better sense of the method's practical scalability limits.

2.  **Clarity on Training Heuristic:** The algorithm relies on the assumption that the optimal matching `M*` remains relatively stable across several SGD updates. While this is an intuitive and practical heuristic, its validity is not discussed. More importantly, the paper is not specific about how frequently the matching is recomputed in the experiments (e.g., once per epoch, every `k` iterations). This is a critical detail for reproducibility and understanding the trade-off between matching accuracy and computational cost. Please clarify this in the experimental setup description.

3.  **Limited Comparison in Generative Modeling:** The comparison in the generative modeling section is primarily against GMMN (an MMD-based model). While this is the most direct baseline, the field of generative modeling has largely moved towards Wasserstein GANs (WGAN, WGAN-GP) for training with Wasserstein-like distances. A discussion of the conceptual differences and trade-offs with WGANs would be valuable. WGANs require a critic network with its own optimization challenges (e.g., enforcing the Lipschitz constraint), while PMD is parameter-free but has a higher computational cost per step. An empirical comparison, even on a smaller scale, would have significantly strengthened the paper's impact.

4.  **Choice of Ground Metric:** The paper defaults to the L1 distance `d(·, ·)`. This choice replaces MMD's kernel and bandwidth selection with the selection of a ground metric. While L1 is simple and has good gradient properties, it may not be ideal for all data types (e.g., the authors note it can lead to blurry images). A brief discussion on the influence of other metrics (like L2) and whether this introduces a new form of "hyperparameter tuning" would make the analysis more complete.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated discrepancy measure (PMD) that serves as a strong alternative to MMD.
2.  It addresses several well-known practical issues with using MMD for deep learning, including hyperparameter sensitivity and vanishing gradients.
3.  The proposed method is backed by both solid theoretical analysis connecting it to the Wasserstein metric and strong, comprehensive empirical results across two distinct applications.
4.  The work is a valuable contribution that provides the community with a new, practical tool for comparing distributions.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

1.  The computational cost of the matching step could limit scalability to very large population sizes.
2.  The comparison to other Wasserstein-based methods in generative modeling is limited, omitting a discussion or comparison with the popular WGAN family.

### Final Recommendation

The strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a novel, theoretically sound, and practically useful method for training deep models. The clear exposition and thorough experiments provide convincing evidence of its advantages over MMD. I recommend **Accept**. The suggested improvements, particularly regarding clarity on the training procedure and a broader discussion of related work like WGANs, would further enhance the quality of the final manuscript.
