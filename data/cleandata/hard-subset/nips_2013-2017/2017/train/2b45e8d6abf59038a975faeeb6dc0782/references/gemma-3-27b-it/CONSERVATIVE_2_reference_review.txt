This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating statistical divergence between distributions, particularly in the context of deep learning. The core idea—using a minimum weight bipartite matching to approximate the Wasserstein metric—is presented with theoretical justification (strong consistency) and empirical validation on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

While the motivation for seeking alternatives to MMD (bandwidth tuning, gradient saturation, large mini-batch requirements) is valid, and the proposed PMD offers potential improvements on these fronts, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a substantial advance warranting acceptance to NIPS. My primary concern is a lack of thorough and comparative evaluation against well-established baselines. The experimental section feels somewhat superficial. For example, the domain adaptation experiments, while showing some improvement, lack a detailed ablation study comparing the impact of PMD *specifically* to that of carefully tuned MMD. Simply stating PMD outperforms MMD doesn't suffice; demonstrating *why* (e.g., faster convergence with smaller batch sizes, robustness to hyperparameter choices) is crucial.

The clarity is adequate, though the presentation could be more concise. The notation is generally well-defined, but the flow between theoretical sections and experimental setup feels disjointed. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is stated but not deeply explored – a more detailed discussion of the assumptions and limitations of approximating the metric in this way would be helpful. The discussion of gradient behavior (Fig. 2b) is interesting, but its practical impact isn't convincingly linked to the observed performance gains.

The originality lies primarily in applying the minimum weight matching concept to this specific divergence estimation problem, and framing it as a consistent estimator for the Wasserstein metric. However, the underlying concepts (Wasserstein metric, bipartite matching) are well-established. The novelty isn't groundbreaking, and its significance is still unclear given the limited experimental scope.  There's a significant body of work on optimal transport and Wasserstein GANs; the paper needs to more clearly differentiate itself within this landscape. The claim of not requiring a critic network is a point of distinction, but the advantages over methods building on WGAN should be explicitly articulated.

The significance of the work is questionable at this stage. While the presented results are positive, they don’t demonstrate a paradigm shift in domain adaptation or generative modeling. The improvements are incremental, and the lack of a thorough comparison against strong baselines makes it difficult to assess the practical impact of PMD. The computational complexity analysis is appreciated, but the emphasis on GPU acceleration hints at a potential performance bottleneck not fully addressed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a valid problem: mitigating the limitations of MMD.
*   Provides a theoretically sound alternative with strong consistency guarantees.
*   Demonstrates initial empirical success in two relevant application areas.
*   Offers potential for faster training and reduced hyperparameter tuning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficiently rigorous comparison to well-tuned MMD and other relevant baselines.
*   Limited experimental scope and lack of ablation studies.
*   The significance of the improvements is not convincingly demonstrated.
*   The connection to existing work on Wasserstein metrics and GANs is not adequately discussed.
*   The reliance on a serial CPU implementation for matching is a limitation.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution, but requires significant revisions. The authors need to conduct a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, provide a more in-depth analysis of the theoretical properties of PMD, and clearly articulate its advantages over existing methods. A stronger focus on the practical implications and limitations of the proposed approach is also necessary. As it stands, the paper doesn't meet the NIPS standards for originality and significance.
