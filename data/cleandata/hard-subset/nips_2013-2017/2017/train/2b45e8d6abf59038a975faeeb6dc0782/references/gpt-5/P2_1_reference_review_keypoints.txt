- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between equal-size samples, used as a sample-based estimator of 1-Wasserstein distance and a training objective.  
- Formalizes PMD as empirical optimal transport (OT) on equal-mass empirical measures and proves strong consistency via standard OT stability.  
- Uses PMD as a differentiable training loss by freezing discrete matchings (Danskin-like argument) and reusing matchings with sub-sampling in an inner-loop SGD.  
- Reports experiments on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), claiming PMD needs no bandwidth tuning, gives stronger gradients, smaller minibatch use, and faster convergence than MMD.  
- PMD is essentially empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD for equal-size samples; the linear assignment equivalence and consistency are classical results.  
- Paper omits key related work: Cuturi’s Sinkhorn distances (entropic OT/backprop) and OT-based domain adaptation methods, leaving positioning and novelty unclear.  
- Claims about differentiability versus dual OT/WGAN are misleading since WGAN and entropic OT/Sinkhorn are differentiable and standard for training.  
- The consistency proof restates known facts; no new convergence rates or bounds are provided.  
- Gradient analysis relies on local constancy/ Danskin’s theorem but lacks formal assumptions (uniqueness, measure-zero nondifferentiabilities) and citations.  
- The sub-sampling/frozen-plan gradient estimator is introduced without formal bias/variance analysis; only empirical study is provided.  
- Scalability: Hungarian O(N^3) and approximate O(N^2) matchings plus O(N^2D) distances are costly; no comparison to GPU-friendly Sinkhorn is given.  
- The claim that smaller batch size helps PMD (due to frozen plans) is intriguing but unexplained theoretically (bias/variance trade-off).  
- Domain adaptation experiments compare only to MMD, not to stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, DSN, CMD, OTDA); gains are modest and inconsistent.  
- Generative modeling results are qualitative with no standard metrics (FID/IS) and no comparisons to WGAN, Cramér GAN, MMD-GAN, or Sinkhorn-based methods; sample quality is blurry.  
- Analysis plots (gradient magnitudes, sample-size sensitivity) are useful but limited; observed sample-rate N^{-0.23} matches known slow Wasserstein behavior without discussion of curse of dimensionality.  
- Reproducibility: implementation details are adequate but code availability is not mentioned.  
- Clarity: paper is generally clear, but the “unbiased gradient” claim, positioning against dual OT, and missing citations reduce precision.  
- Originality is limited: PMD renames empirical OT/EMD and the training recipe is a pragmatic variant of prior OT-based learning ideas.  
- Significance is incremental; lacking new algorithmic advances or stronger empirical evidence limits impact.  
- Pros: clear exposition, correct consistency statement, practical tricks (freezing plans, sub-batches), and useful empirical analyses.  
- Cons: limited novelty, missing key OT literature, informal theoretical gradient claims, incomplete scalability discussion, and limited experimental baselines/metrics.  
- Suggested improvements: compare and cite Sinkhorn/backprop OT and OTDA work; give precise Danskin/subgradient justification; analyze bias/variance of frozen-plan estimator; add stronger baselines and standard generative metrics; explore feature-space/learned costs and sample-complexity effects.  
- Overall: technically sound on known foundations but incremental and lacking breadth against established OT methods, so it does not meet the bar for NeurIPS as presented.
