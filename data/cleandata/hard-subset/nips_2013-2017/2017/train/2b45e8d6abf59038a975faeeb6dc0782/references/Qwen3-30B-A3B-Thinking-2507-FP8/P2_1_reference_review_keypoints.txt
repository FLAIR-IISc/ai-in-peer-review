- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the minimum weight matching cost between sample sets and proves it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- PMD requires no bandwidth hyper-parameter, provides stronger gradients, and allows smaller mini-batch sizes compared to MMD.  
- Comprehensive empirical validation on domain adaptation and generative modeling (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW) shows PMD outperforms MMD and converges faster.  
- Theoretical contribution is well-supported using Varadarajan’s Theorem and stability of optimal transport to link PMD to the Wasserstein metric.  
- Ablation studies demonstrate PMD’s insensitivity to population size and advantages of smaller mini-batches; pseudocode and implementation details aid reproducibility.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized, with effective figures and algorithm illustrations.  
- Computational efficiency concern: Hungarian algorithm’s O(N^3) complexity raises scalability questions for very large datasets; approximate O(N^2) matching is mentioned but not thoroughly evaluated.  
- Limited distance-metric exploration: only L1 distance used, which may cause blurry generated images; alternatives (L2, perceptual) are not studied.  
- Comparison with WGAN is brief and insufficient given WGAN’s relevance to generative modeling.  
- Authors acknowledge weaknesses and suggest approximate matching algorithms as a path forward.  
- Overall assessment: strong theoretical and empirical contribution that advances distribution-matching for deep learning.  
- Recommendation: accept.  
- Specific suggestions: discuss integrating PMD with perceptual distance metrics, provide a deeper comparison with WGAN, and consider extensions to higher-order Wasserstein metrics.
