**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the respective distributions. The authors establish a clear theoretical link between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of it. The main contribution is the application of this discrepancy as a differentiable loss function for training deep neural networks. By assuming the optimal matching is locally constant, gradients can be passed through the distance function to the network parameters. The authors contrast PMD with the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), highlighting advantages such as being hyperparameter-free (no kernel bandwidth), providing stronger gradients, and allowing for faster optimization through smaller SGD mini-batches. The effectiveness of PMD is demonstrated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, where it is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD-based methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The central idea of using the primal form of the Wasserstein metric, approximated via minimum weight matching, as a direct loss for deep learning is highly novel and compelling. In a landscape dominated by adversarial (dual-form) approaches like WGAN or kernel-based methods like MMD, this work presents a fundamentally different and elegant alternative. It forges a direct and practical link between classical combinatorial optimization and the training of deep generative models, which is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **A Bold, Parameter-Free Approach:** The proposed PMD metric is parameter-free, directly addressing a major practical challenge of MMD—the selection of kernel type and bandwidth, which often requires careful tuning. This simplicity makes the method more robust and easier to apply. This is a bold departure from methods that add complexity (e.g., critic networks in GANs) and instead relies on a well-established, non-parametric optimization primitive.

3.  **Strong Practical Advantages:** The paper makes a convincing case for the practical benefits of PMD over MMD. The analysis and empirical evidence (Fig 2b) suggesting that PMD provides stronger, more uniform gradients is a key insight. This helps mitigate the issue of vanishing gradients that can plague saturated RBF kernels in MMD. Furthermore, the ability to decouple the SGD mini-batch size from the population size is a clever optimization that demonstrably speeds up convergence (Fig 5a), a significant practical advantage.

4.  **Solid Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-conducted and provide strong evidence for the method's efficacy. PMD shows competitive or superior performance against MMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling. The fact that PMD can generate reasonable images directly in pixel space, a setting where MMD often struggles, further highlights its potential.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The most significant weakness of PMD is its computational complexity. Solving the minimum weight matching problem takes O(N^3) time for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. This scales much more poorly than MMD and may become a prohibitive bottleneck for the very large population sizes (N) required for accurate estimation in high dimensions. While the authors rightly point out that this can be parallelized, it remains a serious concern for scalability compared to alternatives.

2.  **Theoretical Gaps:** While the paper establishes the strong consistency of PMD, the theoretical analysis could be deepened. A crucial missing piece is a finite-sample analysis providing error bounds on the estimation of the true Wasserstein metric. This would allow for a principled understanding of how the population size N should be chosen relative to the data dimensionality and complexity. The empirical convergence rate of the estimator (N^-0.23) appears quite slow, which warrants further theoretical investigation.

3.  **Dependence on Ground Metric:** The performance of PMD is fundamentally tied to the choice of the ground distance `d(x,y)` in the sample space (e.g., L1 distance). As the authors note, the blurriness of generated images is likely due to the perceptual inadequacy of pixel-wise L1 distance. This is an inherent property of the Wasserstein metric, but it means that applying PMD successfully to complex data types like images may require engineering sophisticated, learned, or domain-specific distance metrics, which re-introduces complexity.

4.  **Optimization Heuristic:** The training algorithm relies on the approximation that the optimal matching M* remains fixed across several SGD updates. While this seems reasonable and works well in practice, the paper does not analyze the potential impact of this heuristic. It would be valuable to understand if and when this assumption might break down and how it affects the optimization dynamics.

**Recommendation:**

Accept.

**Justification:**

This paper introduces a genuinely novel and thought-provoking idea. The connection it draws between optimal transport, combinatorial matching, and deep learning is elegant and opens up a new avenue for research in distribution matching. While I acknowledge the weaknesses, particularly the computational scaling and the lack of finite-sample bounds, I believe the novelty and significance of the core concept substantially outweigh these limitations. My reviewing philosophy prioritizes bold new directions over methodologically flawless but incremental work. This paper falls squarely in the former category. It presents a clear and compelling alternative to established methods like MMD and GANs, with a distinct set of trade-offs. The demonstrated practical advantages—no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and faster convergence—are significant. This work is highly likely to inspire follow-up research on topics such as faster matching solvers for deep learning, principled choices of ground metrics, and deeper theoretical analysis of this class of estimators. It is an excellent contribution to the NIPS community.
