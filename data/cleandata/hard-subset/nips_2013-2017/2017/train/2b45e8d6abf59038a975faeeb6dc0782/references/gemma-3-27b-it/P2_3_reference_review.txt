## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric. PMD minimizes the cost of a bipartite matching between samples from two distributions. The authors argue that PMD offers advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), specifically the absence of a bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use standard mini-batch sizes. They demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  PMD provides a fresh perspective on estimating Wasserstein distance from samples, differing from both primal and dual approaches like WGAN. It's a legitimate contribution to the landscape of statistical divergence measures.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a theoretical grounding for PMD, demonstrating its strong consistency as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. This is crucial for establishing the validity of the proposed method.
* **Practical Advantages:** The authors convincingly argue for the practical benefits of PMD – no bandwidth tuning, potentially stronger gradients (supported by empirical evidence in Fig. 2b), and the ability to use standard mini-batch sizes. These are important considerations for real-world application and scalability.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling provide strong evidence of PMD’s effectiveness. The improvement in convergence speed (Fig. 3a) is particularly noteworthy. The ablation study in Fig. 5(a) provides further insights.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the authors' ideas. The pseudocode (Fig. 1) is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, they don't fully explore the scalability implications for very large N. The discussion of GPU acceleration is brief.  Although the approximated matching algorithm is introduced, more details on its performance trade-offs compared to the exact method would be valuable.
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The authors consistently use the L1 distance. While justified, the paper doesn't explore the impact of different distance metrics within the PMD framework.  The statement that L1 is generally good seems a bit strong. The blurry generated images (SVHN & LFW) could be related to this choice, hinting at a potential limitation.
* **Limited Comparison Scope:** While PMD is compared to MMD, a more comprehensive comparison to other Wasserstein distance estimators (like those based on optimal transport approximation, or WGAN) would strengthen the paper.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (MMD):** While the paper correctly points out MMD's sensitivity to hyperparameters, it leans heavily on this as a negative.  It's common for kernel methods to require tuning, and a well-tuned MMD could potentially provide a stronger baseline in some cases.
* **Theoretical Depth:** While the consistency result is good, a deeper theoretical analysis regarding convergence rates or error bounds with respect to sample size could further strengthen the paper.



**Quality:**  The paper is technically sound. The theoretical results are correctly stated and supported.  The experiments are well-designed, and the results are compelling. It’s a complete piece of work, though some further investigation into scalability and distance metric choices would elevate it further.  The authors are reasonably careful in their evaluation.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. The explanations of PMD and its advantages are easy to understand.

**Originality:** The idea of using minimum weight matching to estimate Wasserstein distance is novel, and the authors clearly position their work within the existing literature.

**Significance:** PMD has the potential to be a valuable tool for researchers working on generative modeling, domain adaptation, and other tasks requiring statistical divergence estimation. The advantages in terms of parameter tuning and gradient stability could make it a preferred alternative to MMD in certain scenarios. The practical benefits warrant consideration.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to Wasserstein distance estimation.
* Strong theoretical foundation.
* Clear practical advantages over MMD.
* Compelling empirical results.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Scalability concerns related to the Hungarian algorithm.
* Limited exploration of alternative distance metrics.
* Narrow comparison scope (primarily MMD).




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, combined with the strong theoretical and empirical support, makes it a worthy addition to NIPS. While there are some areas for improvement (particularly regarding scalability and comparison scope), the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.

Specifically, I recommend the authors:

* Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of PMD, particularly for very large datasets.
* Explore the impact of different distance metrics on the performance of PMD.
* Include a comparison to more recent Wasserstein distance estimation techniques, such as WGAN.
* Briefly discuss potential limitations associated with the choice of the L1 distance.
