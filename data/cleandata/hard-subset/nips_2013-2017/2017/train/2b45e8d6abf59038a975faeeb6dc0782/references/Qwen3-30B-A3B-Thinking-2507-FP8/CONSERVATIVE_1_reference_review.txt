

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations, as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for measuring statistical divergence between distributions. The authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, has no bandwidth hyperparameter, provides stronger gradients, and can use smaller mini-batch sizes than MMD.

**Strengths:**
The paper is well-written with clear mathematical definitions. The theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is soundly established in Section 3.1, showing PMD is a consistent estimator. The experimental results on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) demonstrate marginal improvements over MMD in some settings, particularly in convergence speed. The authors provide a reasonable empirical analysis of gradient magnitudes (Figure 2b) supporting their claim about stronger gradients.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is its insufficient validation of the claimed advantages. While the theoretical justification for consistency is acceptable, the paper lacks rigorous evidence for the practical benefits of PMD over MMD. The computational complexity argument is flawed: the Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)) becomes prohibitively expensive for reasonable sample sizes (N=1000 would require ~1e9 operations), and the paper doesn't properly address how this impacts training speed in practice. The claim that "PMD can use normal mini-batch size, such as 100" is misleading—the paper still requires matching between two populations of size N (which they call the "population size," not mini-batch size), and they use N=2000 for generative modeling, contradicting their claim about small batch sizes.

The experimental evaluation is limited and unconvincing. The domain adaptation results (Table 1) show only marginal improvements on two out of six tasks, with PMD being comparable on the other four. For generative modeling, PMD only produces acceptable results on the autoencoder code space (ae architecture), not on pixel space, which is a significant limitation for practical applications. The paper fails to compare with recent state-of-the-art methods beyond MMD, such as WGAN-GP, SNGAN, or the more recent Cramer GAN.

The theoretical foundation is incomplete. The authors acknowledge in Section 6 that they "plan to derive finite-sample error bounds for PMD" as future work, but these are essential for evaluating the practical utility of PMD. Without these bounds, it's unclear how large N must be to achieve reliable estimates, and the empirical results in Figure 2a show the approximation error decays very slowly (proportional to N^(-0.23)).

Most critically, the paper doesn't properly contextualize its contribution. The connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is straightforward, but the paper fails to distinguish itself from other approaches to Wasserstein distance estimation (like WGAN) or other bandwidth-free distance measures (like energy distance). The claim that PMD "differs from WGAN by not requiring a separate critic network" is misleading—WGAN uses a critic network to approximate the dual Wasserstein distance, while PMD approximates the primal problem, but neither is bandwidth-free in the same sense.

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution that fails to provide sufficient evidence for its claimed advantages over established methods. While the theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is valid, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate practical benefits over MMD or other state-of-the-art methods. The computational complexity concerns and limited experimental validation make the claims about "stronger gradients" and "smaller mini-batch size" unsubstantiated in practice.

The paper would benefit significantly from:
1) A more thorough comparison with recent distribution distance estimators (beyond MMD)
2) Rigorous finite-sample error bounds
3) Comprehensive analysis of computational costs at scale
4) Demonstration of PMD's utility on larger datasets and higher-dimensional problems

As it stands, the paper lacks the empirical and theoretical rigor to justify its claims about PMD's superiority over established methods. While the idea is not entirely novel, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support its significance beyond minor improvements in specific settings. I recommend rejection.
