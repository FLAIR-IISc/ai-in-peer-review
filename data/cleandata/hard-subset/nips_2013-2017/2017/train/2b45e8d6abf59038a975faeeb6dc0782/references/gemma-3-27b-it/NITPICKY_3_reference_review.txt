This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating distances between probability distributions. The core idea – minimizing the cost of matching samples from two distributions – is intuitively appealing and builds upon the Wasserstein metric. While the paper identifies legitimate shortcomings of MMD (bandwidth tuning, gradient saturation, large batch size requirements) and positions PMD as a solution, the presentation suffers from a relentless focus on minor details and a somewhat superficial engagement with existing literature, ultimately undermining the perceived significance of the contribution.

The paper's strengths lie in the clear definition of PMD and the theoretical connection established to the Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3). The authors correctly identify potential advantages of PMD – the lack of a bandwidth parameter, potentially stronger gradients, and the possibility of using smaller mini-batch sizes. However, these advantages are presented as breakthroughs rather than incremental improvements. For example, the claim of a "stronger gradient" is supported by a rather limited empirical analysis (Fig. 2b) that doesn't fully contextualize the observed differences. A more rigorous investigation comparing the gradient behavior under different scenarios would be necessary.

The weaknesses, however, are numerous and, critically, many are presented with an unnecessarily critical tone. The paper repeatedly emphasizes minor stylistic issues and formatting inconsistencies. While attention to detail is appreciated, dwelling on aspects like the single-threaded implementation of matching algorithms (Sec. 5.3) detracts from the core message. The argument that the O(N^3) complexity of Hungarian matching is "acceptable" for N in the hundreds feels oddly dismissive of computational efficiency, especially given the existence of highly optimized implementations.

Furthermore, the literature review is selective and, in some cases, inaccurate. The claim that other Wasserstein metric estimators (e.g., those based on the dual problem) lack differentiability regarding distribution parameters is demonstrably false.  The connection to WGAN is dismissed too quickly; WGAN’s limitations are acknowledged, but the authors fail to recognize the nuances of the "critic" network’s role and its relationship to Lipschitz continuity.  The paper also makes claims regarding the advantages of PMD’s gradients without thoroughly comparing them to other gradient-based divergence estimators beyond MMD.

The experimental evaluation, while presented clearly, lacks depth. The domain adaptation results (Table 1) show mixed performance, with PMD outperforming MMD on some tasks but not others.  The explanation for the blurry images generated by PMD in the generative modeling experiments (“the pixel-level L1 distance is not good for natural images”) feels like an afterthought rather than a fundamental limitation acknowledged earlier in the paper.  The sensitivity analysis (Fig. 5) is helpful but doesn’t explore the interplay between different hyperparameters. 

Several minor issues further detract from the paper's quality:

*   The inclusion of author affiliations and conference information within the body of the text is unconventional and distracting.
*   The numerous cross-references to figure numbers (e.g., “Fig. 1 is the pseudocode…”) create a repetitive and slightly awkward reading experience.
*   The consistent use of “Sec.” for section references is fine, but it feels overly formal and rigid.
*   The concluding remarks regarding future work are generic and lack specific research directions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   PMD offers a theoretically sound alternative to MMD for estimating distances between distributions.
*   The paper identifies genuine limitations of MMD and proposes a viable solution.
*   The experimental results, while not overwhelming, demonstrate the potential of PMD.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The paper is overly critical and focuses excessively on minor details.
*   The literature review is selective and, in places, inaccurate.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks depth and fails to fully explore the limitations of PMD.
*   The presentation is often rigid and lacks a broader perspective on the related work.
*   The claimed advantages of PMD are incremental rather than groundbreaking.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea behind PMD is reasonable, the paper’s execution is flawed. The relentless focus on minor details and the somewhat dismissive tone towards related work overshadow the potential significance of the contribution. The paper reads less like a compelling research paper and more like a meticulous (and somewhat pedantic) technical report.  Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The paper needs significant revision to address the aforementioned weaknesses and present a more balanced and nuanced assessment of its contributions. A less critical tone, a more comprehensive literature review, and a more in-depth experimental evaluation are essential for this paper to be considered for NIPS.
