This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples, one from each distribution. The central theoretical contribution is to frame PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). Building on this, the authors propose an algorithm to use PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep neural networks, where parameters are optimized via stochastic gradient descent.

The paper positions PMD as an alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). It argues that PMD offers several advantages: it is parameter-free (avoiding MMD's kernel bandwidth selection), provides stronger and more uniform gradients, and allows for a smaller mini-batch size during SGD updates than the population size used for matching, potentially leading to faster convergence. These claims are evaluated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. The work relates to a growing body of literature on using optimal transport distances in machine learning, offering a primal, parameter-free approach in contrast to dual-formulation methods like the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN).

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of using the minimum weight matching cost directly as a loss for training deep networks is simple, elegant, and novel. While the connection between W1 and minimum weight matching on empirical samples is known in optimal transport theory, its application as a practical, differentiable objective for large-scale deep learning is a significant contribution. It provides a valuable new tool for the many tasks that require matching distributions, presenting a compelling alternative to MMD and adversarial methods.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the definition of PMD is precise, and the connection to the Wasserstein metric is logically derived through the linear programming formulation of optimal transport. The proposed learning algorithm, including the practical trick of decoupling the matching population size `N` from the SGD mini-batch size `|B|`, is explained clearly with pseudocode and a helpful illustration.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical claims are well-supported. The strong consistency of PMD is established by correctly applying standard results from optimal transport theory (Varadarajan's theorem and the stability of optimal transport). The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides convincing evidence for the paper's claims. The experiments cover two distinct and relevant applications, include a major baseline (MMD), and are supported by insightful ablation studies on the impact of population/mini-batch size (Fig. 5) and an empirical validation of the "stronger gradient" hypothesis (Fig. 2b).

4.  **Practical Advantages:** The paper makes a strong case for the practical utility of PMD over MMD. Eliminating the need to tune a kernel bandwidth is a clear advantage. The analysis and empirical results suggesting that PMD provides more robust gradients and converges faster are compelling. The ability to use a large population `N` for a stable distance estimate while using a small mini-batch `|B|` for efficient SGD updates is a clever and impactful algorithmic choice.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The primary weakness of PMD is the computational complexity of the matching algorithm, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. This is computationally demanding compared to the O(N^2) cost of MMD (which can be reduced to O(N) with linear-time estimators). While the authors argue the cost is acceptable, this bottleneck fundamentally limits the population size `N` that can be used in practice, which in turn affects the quality of the W1 estimate, especially in high dimensions.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this limitation. It would also be valuable to include a more direct wall-clock time comparison against MMD for a full training run in the main results tables. Furthermore, a discussion of alternative, more scalable optimal transport solvers, such as those based on entropic regularization (e.g., the Sinkhorn algorithm), would place the work in a broader context and highlight avenues for future improvements in scalability.

2.  **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper's discussion of Wasserstein GANs (WGAN) is brief. Both PMD and WGAN aim to optimize the W1 distance. A more detailed comparative discussion would be beneficial. PMD uses a direct primal estimator, which is parameter-free but computationally expensive. WGAN approximates the dual form, which is more scalable but introduces a critic network that must be carefully trained and constrained (e.g., via weight clipping or a gradient penalty). Exploring these complementary trade-offs more deeply would better contextualize the contribution.

3.  **Dependence on the Ground Metric:** The quality of generative modeling results seems highly dependent on the choice of the ground metric `d(·, ·)`, which was L1 distance on pixels in the experiments. The authors rightly note that this may be the cause of blurry samples on complex image datasets like SVHN and LFW. This highlights a potential limitation compared to methods like MMD (where the kernel can capture more complex relationships) or GANs (where the discriminator learns a suitable metric).
    *   **Suggestion:** To better demonstrate the potential of PMD, it would be interesting to see results where the L1/L2 distance is applied not to raw pixels, but to a feature space from a pre-trained network (akin to a perceptual loss). This could mitigate the issue of blurry samples and show the method's effectiveness with more perceptually relevant metrics.

4.  **Rigor of Gradient Calculation:** The argument that the gradient can be computed by treating the optimal matching `M*` as a constant relies on the intuition that it does not change for an infinitesimal parameter update. While plausible, this step could be made more rigorous by referencing relevant mathematical principles, such as Danskin's theorem or the envelope theorem, which formalize the differentiation of `min` operators.

### Summary of Arguments

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Novel and significant contribution: a new, parameter-free, and effective discrepancy measure for deep learning.
    *   Well-written paper with strong theoretical grounding and clear presentation.
    *   Thorough empirical evaluation demonstrating clear advantages (performance, convergence speed, no hyperparameter tuning) over a strong baseline (MMD) in two important applications.
    *   The algorithmic insight to decouple population and mini-batch sizes is a valuable practical trick.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   Scalability is a major concern due to the O(N^3) or O(N^2) cost of matching.
    *   The discussion of related optimal transport methods (like WGAN and Sinkhorn-based losses) could be more comprehensive.
    *   The performance is sensitive to the choice of the ground metric, as shown by the blurry generative modeling results.

### Decision

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated, and useful method. The strengths, particularly the originality of the idea and the quality of the empirical comparison to MMD, outweigh the weaknesses. The scalability issue is a real limitation but does not invalidate the contribution, and the authors are transparent about it. I recommend acceptance. The suggestions above are intended to further strengthen the paper for the final version.
