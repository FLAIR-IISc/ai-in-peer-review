- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): cost of minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. samples as a sample-based estimator of 1‑Wasserstein.
- Shows PMD equals empirical OT for balanced, equally weighted samples and argues strong consistency via Varadarajan’s theorem and OT stability.
- Uses PMD as a differentiable training objective by computing a matching, treating it as fixed for gradient steps, and optionally reusing matchings with sub-sampling for cheaper updates.
- Empirical comparisons limited to MMD in domain adaptation (Office) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW), claiming faster convergence, better gradients, no bandwidth tuning, and acceptable cost with an approximate O(N^2) matcher.
- Correctly notes PMD is the empirical 1‑Wasserstein on equally weighted samples.
- Fails to cite or compare to entropic/Sinkhorn OT and Sinkhorn divergences (highly relevant, GPU-friendly O(N^2) alternatives).
- Domain adaptation related work using OT and other divergences (e.g., Courty OTDA, CMD, DANN, JAN) is not compared against.
- Does not discuss known rates for empirical OT convergence (e.g., Fournier & Guillin) despite observed dimension-dependent scaling.
- Consistency result is essentially known and lacks novel finite-sample bounds.
- Gradient treatment ignores derivatives through discrete matching; justification via Danskin’s theorem/subgradient calculus is not provided.
- Claim of “unbiased stochastic gradient” is unsubstantiated; interchanging min/gradient/expectation needs conditions.
- Reusing fixed matchings and sub-sampling introduces bias unless assignments remain optimal; no convergence guarantees given.
- Algorithmic discussion ignores scalable differentiable Sinkhorn approaches despite their suitability for GPUs and large N.
- Claim of PMD being “parameter free” is misleading — ground metric, population size, mini-batch size, and inner-loop length are hyperparameters.
- Domain adaptation experiments use standard setup but compare only to MMD; tuning uses 100 labeled target samples, violating pure unsupervised validation and possibly inflating results.
- Improvements on Office are modest and inconsistent; no statistical tests reported.
- Generative modeling results are qualitative and blurry on SVHN/LFW with no quantitative metrics (FID, Inception, bits/dim); comparisons exclude GAN/WGAN and Sinkhorn-based methods.
- Evidence for “stronger gradients” is anecdotal (histogram of gradient magnitudes) and depends on chosen metric and feature space.
- Training schedule fairness is unclear (100 epochs PMD vs 500 epochs MMD).
- Timing shows matching not a bottleneck for small N, but no end-to-end runtime comparisons to strong alternatives (WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn).
- Paper is readable but overstates/imprecise claims (unbiased gradients, MMD mini-batch constraint, parameter-free).
- Should explicitly state PMD=empirical 1‑Wasserstein and discuss uniqueness/non-uniqueness of matchings and differentiability implications.
- Originality limited: using empirical OT/assignment as loss is not new; main contribution is an engineering recipe and empirical observations.
- Significance limited by missing comparisons to modern OT-based training and incomplete theoretical guarantees for optimization heuristics.
- Pros: clear Wasserstein connection, easy-to-implement objective, practical heuristics (reuse/sub-sample), observed more uniform per-sample gradients, some improvement on Office and qualitative gains over MMD.
- Cons: missing key related work (Sinkhorn, OTDA, GAN/WGAN), theoretical claims not rigorous, limited/weak experimental evaluation, overstated claims.
- Suggestions: compare to entropic OT/Sinkhorn and Sinkhorn divergences; justify gradient handling with Danskin/subgradient analysis; analyze bias from reusing matchings; strengthen experiments (DANN/JAN/CMD/OTDA baselines, target‑label‑free validation, quantitative generative metrics, comparisons to WGAN(-GP)); test sensitivity to N, |B|, and matching approximation quality with significance.
- Recommendation: Weak reject (high reviewer confidence).
