### Summary

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the statistical divergence between two probability distributions. The method is proposed as an alternative to other estimators like the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), particularly for training deep neural networks. The PMD between two sets of N samples is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between the two sets, where the cost is a simple distance metric like L1.

The authors establish a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric (W1), showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 as the number of samples N goes to infinity. This positions the work within the broader context of optimal transport methods, which have recently gained popularity in machine learning (e.g., Wasserstein GANs).

The paper proposes a training algorithm that uses PMD as a differentiable objective for stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key insight is that the optimal matching can be treated as fixed during infinitesimal parameter updates, allowing gradients to be computed easily. To improve efficiency, the algorithm uses an outer loop to compute the matching on a "population" of samples and an inner loop to perform multiple SGD updates on smaller "mini-batches" sub-sampled from the matched pairs.

The authors compare PMD against MMD, arguing that PMD has several advantages: it is free of hyper-parameters (like the kernel bandwidth in MMD), it provides stronger and more uniform gradients during training, and it allows for smaller effective mini-batch sizes for SGD updates, leading to faster convergence. These claims are evaluated empirically on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation on the Office dataset and deep generative modeling on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW. The results suggest that PMD can outperform or match MMD in terms of final performance and convergence speed.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Conceptual Simplicity:** The paper proposes a novel and elegant approach for estimating a statistical divergence directly from samples. While the connection between minimum weight matching and the discrete Wasserstein distance is known, its application as a direct, differentiable training objective for deep generative models and domain adaptation is a new contribution. It provides a parameter-free alternative to both kernel-based methods like MMD and adversary-based methods like WGAN.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation of the paper is solid. The authors correctly identify PMD as a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and provide the relevant theoretical results (Theorem 3.3) connecting their method to established literature on optimal transport and weak convergence of measures. The derivation of the stochastic gradient is also sound, based on the valid assumption that the discrete matching is locally constant with respect to infinitesimal changes in the model parameters.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The definition of PMD, the parameter learning algorithm, and the connections to the Wasserstein metric and MMD are explained logically and concisely. Figure 1 provides a helpful illustration of the training procedure. The advantages over MMD are articulated with clear arguments (hyper-parameters, gradient strength, mini-batch size) and supported by empirical analysis (Figure 2).

4.  **Significance:** The proposed method has the potential to be a useful tool for researchers and practitioners. The removal of the sensitive kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter from MMD is a significant practical advantage. The experiments, particularly for domain adaptation, show promising results where PMD achieves superior performance on several tasks. The method represents a valuable contribution to the growing body of work on using optimal transport metrics in deep learning.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The primary weakness of PMD is its computational cost. The minimum weight matching step has a complexity of O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. This is a significant bottleneck compared to MMD's O(N^2) evaluation, which is often highly optimized on GPUs. While the authors argue the cost is acceptable compared to backpropagation, this may not hold for very deep networks or large population sizes (N). The experiments are limited to N up to a few thousand, and the scalability of the method to larger N or higher-dimensional data remains a concern. The reliance on single-threaded CPU implementations for matching further hinders its practical speed in a typical deep learning workflow.

2.  **Heuristics in Training Algorithm:** The proposed training algorithm involves fixing the matching `M*` for several inner-loop SGD updates based on the assumption that it "does not change much." This is a heuristic without theoretical justification regarding its effect on convergence or stability. An analysis of how many inner-loop steps are "safe" before the matching becomes stale, or an empirical study of how frequently the matching changes, would strengthen the paper's claims about the algorithm's effectiveness.

3.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating advantages over MMD on the chosen tasks, is somewhat limited. The generative modeling results on 32x32 images (SVHN, LFW) produce rather blurry samples, falling short of the state of the art. The authors rightly note this could be due to the pixel-wise L1 distance, but this highlights a limitation of using a fixed, simple ground metric `d(·, ·)` for complex data. It is unclear how the method would perform on more complex, high-dimensional datasets where both the choice of `d` and the computational cost would be more challenging.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   Proposes a novel, parameter-free, and theoretically grounded divergence estimator for deep learning.
*   Provides a clear and well-reasoned comparison to MMD, highlighting several practical advantages.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance against MMD on standard domain adaptation benchmarks.
*   Contributes a new perspective to the application of optimal transport metrics in machine learning.

### Arguments for Rejection

*   The computational cost of the matching step poses a significant scalability challenge.
*   The training algorithm relies on a heuristic (fixed matching for inner-loop SGD) that lacks theoretical analysis.
*   The empirical validation on generative modeling does not demonstrate state-of-the-art performance, limiting the assessment of its impact in that area.

### Final Assessment

This paper presents a solid and interesting contribution. The proposed Population Matching Discrepancy is a conceptually simple yet powerful idea with a sound theoretical basis. The primary strengths are its originality as a training objective and its practical advantages over MMD, which are convincingly demonstrated. The main weaknesses relate to computational scalability and the heuristic nature of the training algorithm. Despite these limitations, the novelty of the approach and its demonstrated effectiveness in domain adaptation make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. The work is likely to inspire further research into direct optimization of optimal transport costs for deep learning.
