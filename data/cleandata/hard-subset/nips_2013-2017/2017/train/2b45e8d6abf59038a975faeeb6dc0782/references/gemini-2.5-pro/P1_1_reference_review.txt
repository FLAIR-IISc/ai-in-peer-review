### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel method for estimating the statistical distance between two probability distributions from their samples. The core idea is to define this distance as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples (populations) drawn from the respective distributions. The authors establish a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of it.

The paper proposes an algorithm to use PMD as a differentiable objective for training deep neural networks via stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A key insight is that the gradient of the PMD objective can be computed by treating the optimal matching as fixed, which simplifies the optimization. The authors draw a detailed comparison with the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD offers several key advantages: it has no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, it provides stronger and more uniform gradients, and it allows for using smaller mini-batch sizes for SGD updates, which can lead to faster convergence.

These claims are empirically validated on two significant deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. In both applications, PMD is shown to be competitive with or outperform MMD-based methods, both in terms of final performance and convergence speed.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The central idea of using minimum weight bipartite matching to define a discrepancy for training deep models is simple, intuitive, and novel. It provides a direct, constructive approximation of the Wasserstein-1 distance (the primal form), distinguishing it from other recent works like WGAN that approximate the dual form using a critic network.

2.  **Solid Theoretical Grounding:** The paper clearly establishes the connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator (Theorem 3.3). This provides a firm theoretical foundation for the proposed method, framing it within the established theory of optimal transport.

3.  **Significant Practical Advantages over MMD:** The paper makes a compelling case for PMD as a practical alternative to MMD. The advantages are not merely incremental but address fundamental limitations of MMD:
    *   **No Kernel Bandwidth:** Eliminating the need to tune the kernel bandwidth σ is a major practical benefit, as MMD's performance is often highly sensitive to this choice (as shown in Fig 3b).
    *   **Stronger Gradients:** The analysis and empirical results (Fig 2b) convincingly argue that PMD's gradients do not suffer from the saturation issues of RBF-kernel MMD, where samples that are either too close or too far contribute little to the gradient. This likely contributes to more stable and faster training.
    *   **Efficient Mini-batching:** The ability to decouple the population size `N` (for a stable distance estimate) from the SGD mini-batch size `|B|` (for efficient updates) is a key algorithmic contribution. The experiments (Fig 5a) effectively demonstrate that smaller `|B|` leads to better results, supporting this claim and explaining the observed faster convergence.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed. The authors test PMD on two distinct and relevant problems. The experiments are well-designed not only to show superior performance but also to specifically validate the claimed advantages over MMD, including faster convergence (Fig 3a), reduced hyperparameter sensitivity (Fig 3), and analysis of computational trade-offs (Fig 5). The generative modeling results are qualitatively impressive, particularly the ability to generate reasonable images directly in pixel space.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The primary drawback of PMD is the computational cost of solving the matching problem, which is O(N³) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N²) for the approximation used. This is significantly higher than MMD's O(N²) complexity for computing the objective. While the paper argues this cost is acceptable compared to backpropagation and can be amortized (Fig 1), it remains a potential barrier to scaling `N` to very large population sizes (e.g., tens of thousands) that might be needed for high-dimensional or highly complex distributions. The suggestion of GPU acceleration is noted, but its practical feasibility and performance gain are not demonstrated.

2.  **Gradient Assumption:** The derivation of the stochastic gradient in Eq. (3) relies on the assumption that the optimal matching `M*` is locally constant with respect to infinitesimal changes in the parameters `θ`. While this is a common technique (related to the envelope theorem) and intuitively plausible, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of this point. Are there scenarios (e.g., near-degenerate solutions where multiple matchings have similar costs) where this assumption might be violated, and what would the consequences be? This could add further rigor to the proposed learning algorithm.

3.  **Choice of Ground Metric `d(·, ·)`:** The paper replaces MMD's kernel choice with the choice of a ground metric `d(·, ·)`. While the L1 distance used is parameter-free, the choice of metric is itself a form of hyperparameter. As the authors acknowledge, the pixel-wise L1 distance might be responsible for the blurriness in generated images. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to this choice (e.g., L1 vs. L2) or the potential for using other metrics would be valuable.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and effective method for comparing distributions. The work is technically sound, with strong theoretical underpinnings and a thorough empirical evaluation that clearly demonstrates its advantages over a strong baseline (MMD). The originality lies in the clever application of minimum weight matching as a trainable loss for deep learning, and the significance is high, as PMD presents a compelling alternative for practitioners working on domain adaptation, generative modeling, and other tasks requiring a sample-based distance measure. The identified weaknesses, primarily concerning computational scalability and a key assumption in the gradient derivation, are valid but do not undermine the core contribution. They represent interesting avenues for future work. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the claims are well-supported. It is a solid contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept.
