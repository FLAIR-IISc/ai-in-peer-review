### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based method for estimating the statistical divergence between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions. The authors establish a strong connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein (W-1) metric, showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W-1. The paper proposes using PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep learning models.

The primary contribution is positioning PMD as a practical and effective alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors argue and demonstrate that PMD has several advantages over MMD: 1) it is hyperparameter-free (no kernel bandwidth to tune), 2) it provides stronger and more stable gradients for optimization, and 3) it allows for a decoupled population size and SGD mini-batch size, enabling faster training. These claims are supported by theoretical connections to optimal transport, analysis of the gradient properties, and extensive experiments on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling. In both applications, PMD is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD, often with faster convergence.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Approach:** The core idea of using the cost of bipartite matching directly as a loss function for training deep neural networks is simple, elegant, and well-motivated. While the connection between optimal transport and bipartite matching on empirical distributions is known, its application as a practical, end-to-end differentiable (piecewise) training objective in this context is a novel contribution. It provides a compelling alternative to dual-form approximations of the Wasserstein distance (like in WGAN) and kernel-based methods (like MMD).

2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The paper firmly grounds PMD in the theory of optimal transport, proving that it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3). This provides significant theoretical justification for the method and explains why it should be a valid statistical distance for comparing distributions.

3.  **Clear Practical Advantages over MMD:** The paper makes a very convincing case for PMD's practical benefits over MMD.
    *   **No Hyperparameters:** The removal of the sensitive kernel bandwidth parameter (`σ` in the RBF kernel) is a major practical advantage, reducing the burden of model tuning.
    *   **Stronger Gradients:** The analysis in Section 3.2, supported by the empirical results in Figure 2(b), provides a compelling explanation for PMD's superior performance. The insight that MMD's RBF kernel can lead to vanishing gradients for samples that are either too close or too far apart, while PMD's L1-based gradient remains strong, is a key strength.
    *   **Efficient Training Scheme:** The proposed training algorithm, which decouples the population size `N` (for matching) from the SGD mini-batch size `|B|` (for updates), is a clever and effective strategy. It allows the model to leverage a reliable distance estimate from a larger population while benefiting from the efficiency of small-batch SGD. This is a significant advantage over MMD, which requires computation over the entire population for each gradient step.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is strong. The paper tests PMD on two distinct and important problems (domain adaptation and generative modeling) and compares it directly against its main competitor, MMD. The results consistently demonstrate PMD's advantages in both final performance and convergence speed (Fig. 3(a)). The ablation studies on the effects of population size, mini-batch size, and the matching algorithm (Fig. 5) are particularly valuable and strongly support the paper's claims.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The primary drawback of PMD is the computational cost of solving the minimum weight matching problem. Using the Hungarian algorithm is `O(N^3)`, and the approximation is `O(N^2)`. This is computationally more expensive than the `O(N^2)` evaluation of MMD (though the constants may differ). While the authors show that the approximate solver works well and its runtime is comparable to the SGD updates for the tested `N`, this cost could become a significant bottleneck for larger population sizes, potentially limiting the method's scalability. The paper mentions the possibility of GPU acceleration but does not provide an implementation or results, leaving this as an open question.

2.  **Treatment of Differentiability:** The argument in Equation (3) for computing the gradient—that the optimal matching `M*` is treated as a constant—is a key simplification. While this is valid almost everywhere, the objective is not truly differentiable at points where the optimal matching changes. A more formal discussion regarding subgradients or citing relevant results from optimization theory (e.g., Danskin's theorem) would strengthen the technical quality of this section.

3.  **The Role of the Ground Metric `d(·, ·)`:** The paper uses the L1 distance as the ground metric `d` in all experiments. The authors astutely note in Section 5.2 that the blurriness of generated images might be due to the pixel-level L1 distance. This hints that the choice of `d` is effectively a new, important hyperparameter. While PMD avoids the kernel bandwidth, it introduces sensitivity to the ground metric, which can have a major impact on performance, especially for perceptual tasks. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of this trade-off.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   The distinction between the population size `N` and the SGD mini-batch size `|B|` is a key algorithmic contribution. This could be emphasized even more clearly in Section 2.2, as it's a non-obvious and powerful idea.
*   In Figure 2(a), the empirical error rate is shown to be proportional to `N^-0.23`. It would be useful to compare this to known theoretical rates for Wasserstein metric estimation to put this result in context.
*   The comparison with WGAN is insightful. A key advantage of PMD not explicitly stated is that it computes an absolute value for the W-1 distance, whereas WGAN's critic only provides a value up to an unknown scaling factor related to its Lipschitz constant. This could be a valuable property for model criticism and evaluation.

### **Final Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and practical method for comparing distributions in deep learning. It addresses clear limitations of MMD, a widely used technique, and provides strong theoretical and empirical evidence for its proposed alternative, PMD. The work is original, technically sound, and the results are significant. The demonstrated improvements in convergence speed and performance, combined with the practical benefit of having fewer hyperparameters, make PMD a valuable contribution that researchers and practitioners are likely to adopt and build upon. The weaknesses, primarily related to computational cost and a more formal treatment of differentiability, are present but do not detract from the overall strength and impact of the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a simple, elegant, and novel method (PMD) for training deep models.
2.  Strong theoretical foundation connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric.
3.  Clearly demonstrates significant practical advantages over MMD (no kernel tuning, stronger gradients, faster training).
4.  Excellent and thorough experimental validation across multiple tasks and with insightful ablation studies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The computational complexity of the matching step may limit scalability to very large population sizes.
2.  The discussion on the differentiability of the matching-based objective could be more rigorous.
3.  The choice of the ground distance `d(·, ·)` is an important design choice that is not fully explored.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This is a clear accept.
