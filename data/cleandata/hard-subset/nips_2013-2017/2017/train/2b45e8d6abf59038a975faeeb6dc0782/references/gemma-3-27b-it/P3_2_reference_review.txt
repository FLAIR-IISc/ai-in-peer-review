## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, termed Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD). It's a conceptually simple and theoretically grounded approach with promising empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling. While the core idea isn't groundbreaking in its reliance on the Wasserstein metric, the practical advantages claimed – particularly gradient behavior and mini-batch size independence – are significant and well-supported by the experiments. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary:**

The paper tackles the problem of estimating statistical divergence between distributions when only samples are available, a common scenario in deep learning.  Existing methods like MMD suffer from issues like hyperparameter sensitivity (bandwidth) and vanishing gradients. The authors propose PMD, which is based on finding the optimal matching between samples from the two distributions using a bipartite matching algorithm. They prove strong consistency, demonstrate a more favorable gradient profile compared to MMD, and show improved performance in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.  

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper clearly establishes the connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, proving strong consistency. This theoretical grounding is a major strength.
* **Practical Advantages:** The claimed advantages – elimination of bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch size – are compelling. These directly address limitations of MMD and potentially contribute to faster and more stable training. The empirical results (Figures 2 & 3, Table 1) support these claims.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The pseudocode (Fig. 1) is helpful for understanding the algorithm.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling provide convincing evidence of PMD's effectiveness.  The comparison with MMD is fair and highlights the benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the computational cost of the matching algorithm and discuss potential speedups (GPU parallelization).  They also recognize the limitations of using L1 distance for image generation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Contribution:** While the proposed PMD is a valuable addition, it builds upon existing concepts (Wasserstein metric, optimal transport). The novelty lies in the specific formulation and the focus on its advantages for deep learning, rather than a fundamentally new theoretical breakthrough.  This isn't necessarily a negative, but it's important to acknowledge.
* **Computational Cost:** Although the authors discuss potential GPU acceleration, the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm (for the exact matching) is a potential bottleneck for very large datasets.  The impact of using the approximated matching algorithm on the accuracy of the distance estimation needs further clarification. A more detailed discussion of the practical scalability limits would be helpful.
* **Limited Ablation Study:** The experiments largely compare PMD to MMD.  A more detailed ablation study, for example, exploring different distance metrics *within* the PMD framework (beyond L1) or comparing with other Wasserstein distance estimators, would strengthen the results.
* **Overstated Claims?** The claim that PMD "does not require a separate critic network with additional parameters" compared to WGAN feels slightly misleading. While PMD doesn't explicitly have a critic *network*, the matching algorithm itself acts as a form of implicit critic. This is a subtle point, but it should be clarified.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant problem in deep learning (statistical divergence estimation).
* The proposed method (PMD) is theoretically sound and offers practical advantages over existing approaches (MMD).
* The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in two challenging tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The contribution is incremental, building on established concepts.
* The computational cost of the exact matching algorithm may limit scalability. 




**Specific Comments and Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Section 3.1:**  While the theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is clear, briefly discussing the advantages of the Wasserstein metric *itself* over other divergence measures (e.g., KL divergence) would provide more context.
* **Section 3.2:**  Expand on the intuition behind why PMD gradients are "stronger." Provide a more illustrative example of how the gradient behavior differs in a simplified scenario.
* **Section 5.3:**  Provide more details on the computational time for the exact vs. approximated matching algorithms for different population sizes. Quantify the trade-off between accuracy and speed.
* **Section 5.4:** Mention whether the 5-dimensional Gaussian example used to validate the theoretical error bound has the same dimensionality as the application problems in section 5.1 and 5.2.
* **Minor:** Throughout the paper, "N -PMD" notation feels clunky.  Consider simply using "PMD" with N defined in the context.




**Overall, I believe this is a strong paper with the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed PMD offers a practical and theoretically grounded alternative to existing methods for estimating statistical divergence, and the empirical results are encouraging. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.**
