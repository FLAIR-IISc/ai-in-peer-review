

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes "Population Matching Discrepancy" (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions by finding the minimum weight matching between samples from each distribution. The authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and demonstrate advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements. The paper applies PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, reporting improved performance and convergence speed over MMD.

## Strengths
The paper introduces a novel approach to statistical divergence estimation that is conceptually simple and has potential theoretical grounding. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is theoretically interesting, and the empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling provide some support for the claims.

## Weaknesses

### Technical and Theoretical Flaws

1. **Incorrect Claim About Gradient Propagation**: The paper states in Equation (3) that "the discrete matching M* should not change for infinitesimal change of θ" and that "the gradient does not propagate through the matching." This is a critical but unsupported claim. The optimal matching is a discrete optimization problem whose solution can change discontinuously with small changes to the input data. The authors provide no theoretical justification or empirical evidence for this assertion, which fundamentally undermines their proposed optimization method.

2. **Mischaracterization of MMD Gradient**: The paper claims "the L2 norm of the term between two samples x and y is k(x, y) ‖x−y‖^2 / σ^2" (Section 3.2), which is incorrect. The gradient magnitude should be proportional to k(x, y)‖x−y‖, not k(x, y)‖x−y‖^2. This error weakens their argument about PMD having "stronger gradients."

3. **Inconsistent Population Size Terminology**: The paper uses "N" for population size throughout, but in Section 5.3, they state "the mini-batch size for PMD can be smaller than the population size," which is confusing because in standard machine learning terminology, the mini-batch size *is* the population size for the current training iteration. This inconsistency creates significant confusion.

4. **Missing Consistency Condition**: In Theorem 3.3, the authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric but fail to establish the crucial condition from Proposition 3.2: "lim inf_N ∫ d(x, y)γ_N(x, y)dxdy < +∞." Without this condition, the consistency claim is unproven.

### Experimental and Implementation Issues

1. **Lack of Quantitative Results**: The paper claims "PMD outperforms MMD on the a→w and a→d tasks by a large margin" (Section 5.1), but Table 1 is not provided in the submitted text. Without the actual results, it's impossible to evaluate this claim.

2. **Insufficient Comparison Metrics**: For generative modeling, the paper claims "images generated by PMD are less noisy than that generated by MMD" (Section 5.2), but provides no quantitative metrics (e.g., FID score, Inception score) to support this qualitative observation.

3. **Inadequate Timing Analysis**: The paper states "the cost of approximated matching is comparable with the cost of SGD" (Section 5.3), but provides no timing data to substantiate this claim. Figure 5(c) shows "Time (seconds)" but doesn't provide actual numbers.

4. **Implementation Ambiguities**: The paper references "Drake and Hougardy's approximated matching algorithm" (Section 2.3) but doesn't provide a specific reference or details about the algorithm, making it impossible to verify their implementation.

### Formatting and Presentation Issues

1. **Incorrect Conference Reference**: The paper states "st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NIPS 2017)" instead of "The 31st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NeurIPS 2017)."

2. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for the transformation function (e.g., "T^X_θX" vs. "T X θX"), which is confusing for readers.

3. **Missing Figure References**: The paper references "Fig. 1" and "Fig. 2" but doesn't include these figures in the provided text.

4. **Typographical Errors**: The reference for "The cramer distance as a solution to biased wasserstein gradients" contains extra commas: "Marc G Bellemare, Ivo Danihelka, Will Dabney, Shakir Mohamed, Balaji Lakshminarayanan, Stephan Hoyer, Rémi Munos, arXiv preprint arXiv:1705.10743,, , ,"

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting idea for a new statistical divergence, the numerous technical errors, unsupported claims, and presentation issues significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The theoretical claims about consistency and gradient properties are either incorrect or insufficiently justified, and the experimental results are not properly documented or quantified. The paper's claims about PMD having "stronger gradients" and "smaller mini-batch size" are particularly problematic due to the theoretical errors in their justification.

The paper's most significant flaw is the unsupported assertion that the matching does not change with small parameter updates, which is central to the proposed optimization method. Without a rigorous theoretical foundation for this claim, the entire parameter learning approach is questionable.

While the idea has potential, the current version of the paper is not ready for publication. The authors would need to substantially revise the theoretical foundations, correct the mathematical errors, provide complete experimental results with quantitative metrics, and fix the numerous formatting inconsistencies before the paper could be considered for publication.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper contains significant technical errors and unsupported claims that undermine its core contributions, despite the potentially interesting idea. The issues are fundamental to the paper's claims and cannot be addressed with minor revisions.
