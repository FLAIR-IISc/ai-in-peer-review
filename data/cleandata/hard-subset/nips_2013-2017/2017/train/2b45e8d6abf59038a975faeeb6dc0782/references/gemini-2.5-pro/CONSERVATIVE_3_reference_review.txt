### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based method for estimating the statistical divergence between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of a minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the respective distributions. The authors establish that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The main contribution is the proposal of PMD as a practical alternative to the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for training deep neural networks. The authors claim several advantages for PMD over MMD, including having no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, providing stronger gradients for optimization, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes during stochastic gradient descent. The method is empirically evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling tasks, where it is reported to outperform MMD in terms of both final performance and convergence speed.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Connection:** The paper provides a clear and sound connection between the proposed PMD and the well-established first Wasserstein metric. Showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method. This grounding in optimal transport theory is a significant strength.

2.  **Motivation:** The motivation for the work is strong. The authors correctly identify several well-known practical limitations of MMD, such as the need for careful kernel and bandwidth selection and the potential for vanishing gradients, which can hinder training in deep learning settings. Proposing an alternative that aims to address these issues is a valuable direction of research.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The core concept of using bipartite matching is intuitive, and the proposed learning algorithm is described clearly.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Methodological Soundness of the Optimization Procedure:** The central weakness of the paper lies in the proposed optimization scheme. The gradient calculation in Equation (3) relies on the assumption that the optimal matching M* is fixed with respect to infinitesimal changes in the parameters θ. While this is true for a single gradient step, the proposed algorithm (Figure 1) uses the same matching M* for multiple SGD updates in an "inner loop." This is a significant heuristic that lacks justification. The optimal matching is a function of the sample locations, which in turn depend on θ. By fixing M* over several updates, the algorithm is no longer optimizing the true PMD objective but rather a sequence of surrogate objectives defined by fixed (and potentially stale) matchings. This simplification may introduce biases and could lead to suboptimal solutions. The potential impact of this approximation is not analyzed or discussed.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Relevant Baselines:** The experimental comparison is almost exclusively focused on MMD. While this is a reasonable baseline, the paper largely ignores more recent and highly relevant work, especially concerning the Wasserstein metric. In the context of generative modeling, the development of Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) and its various improvements (e.g., WGAN-GP) represents the state-of-the-art for using Wasserstein distances. Dismissing WGAN because it requires a critic network is not a sufficient justification for its exclusion. A rigorous comparison against such methods is necessary to properly evaluate the significance and practical utility of PMD. Without this comparison, the claims about advancing the state of the art are not well-supported.

3.  **Scalability and Computational Concerns:** The computational complexity of the core matching step is a significant practical limitation. The Hungarian algorithm runs in O(N^3), and the proposed approximation in O(N^2). While the authors argue this is acceptable compared to the cost of backpropagation, this may not hold true for very deep models or large population sizes (N). In their own experiments, N is as large as 4000, where an O(N^2) or O(N^3) step per outer-loop iteration can be a substantial bottleneck. In contrast, the O(N^2) computation for MMD is highly parallelizable on modern hardware. The paper downplays this scalability challenge, which could be a major barrier to the adoption of PMD in larger-scale problems.

4.  **Empirical Evidence and Claims:**
    *   The claim of "stronger gradients" is supported only by an empirical histogram (Figure 2b). This is illustrative but not conclusive. It is not self-evident that uniformly large gradients are always superior; they can lead to optimization instability. The observed behavior for MMD (some small gradients) might be a desirable property, allowing the model to focus on more informative pairs of samples. A more rigorous analysis is needed.
    *   The qualitative results for generative modeling are not convincing. The generated images (Figure 4) are blurry and lack the quality achieved by established generative models. The authors themselves note this, attributing it to the L1 pixel-wise distance, which further questions the suitability of the proposed approach for complex, high-dimensional data like natural images without more sophisticated distance metrics.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces an intuitive and theoretically grounded estimator for the first Wasserstein metric.
*   It identifies and attempts to solve known practical issues with the popular MMD method.
*   The experimental results show some promise, particularly in terms of convergence speed and performance on certain domain adaptation tasks against the MMD baseline.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The proposed optimization algorithm relies on a key heuristic (fixing the matching over multiple updates) that is not theoretically justified or empirically analyzed, calling the methodological soundness into question.
*   The paper fails to compare against the most relevant and powerful baselines for its chosen applications, particularly WGAN and its variants in the generative modeling context.
*   The computational complexity of the matching step poses a significant scalability challenge that is not adequately addressed.
*   The empirical evidence for the method's superiority is limited. The generative modeling results are weak, and claims about gradient properties lack rigorous support.

### Conclusion

While the paper presents an interesting idea with a sound theoretical connection to optimal transport, its contributions are undermined by significant methodological weaknesses in the optimization procedure and an inadequate experimental evaluation. The failure to compare against state-of-the-art Wasserstein-based methods and the unaddressed concerns about computational scalability severely limit the paper's impact. The approach, in its current form, does not demonstrate a clear and convincing advantage over established techniques. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance.
