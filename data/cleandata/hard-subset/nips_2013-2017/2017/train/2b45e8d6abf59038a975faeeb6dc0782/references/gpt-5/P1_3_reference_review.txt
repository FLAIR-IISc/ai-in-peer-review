Summary
This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two i.i.d. samples from distributions pX and pY using a ground distance d (typically L1). PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance for equally weighted samples. The authors prove strong consistency by appealing to Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport, and propose a practical training scheme: compute the optimal matching once per population (optionally approximate), keep the matching fixed for several SGD steps, and optionally compute gradients on a smaller minibatch than the population. They argue PMD has advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients that do not saturate, and the ability to decouple population size from SGD minibatch size. They evaluate PMD against MMD on Office domain adaptation and on implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), showing faster convergence and better or comparable performance.

Relation to prior work
- PMD coincides with the empirical W1/EMD with uniform masses; the consistency result is a direct corollary of standard OT results (Villani). This connection is clearly stated but the novelty of the theory is limited.
- Recent, highly relevant work on scalable, differentiable OT via entropic regularization (Sinkhorn distances; Cuturi 2013) is not cited or compared. Nor are strong baselines in generative modeling (WGAN/GP) beyond a brief mention of WGAN, or other distances such as energy/Cramér distance, or sliced Wasserstein variants.
- In domain adaptation, comparisons are limited to MMD; more competitive baselines (DANN, JAN, CORAL, CMD) are referenced but not included.

Quality
Strengths:
- The training recipe (freeze-and-update matching; decouple population/minibatch) is simple and well-motivated. The envelope/Danskin intuition that the matching does not change under infinitesimal parameter updates is reasonable, and the gradient expression with fixed matching is correct almost everywhere.
- Empirical analyses illustrate the claimed gradient-strength advantage and the benefit of smaller minibatches. The approximation error study vs. N is informative.
- Experiments on Office show PMD improves over MMD on some shifts substantially and converges faster at similar accuracy; in generation, PMD yields cleaner samples than MMD in pixel space and performs competitively in AE code space.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty: PMD is a rebranding of empirical W1 with uniform weights; the “strong consistency” theorem is standard. The main contribution is the training heuristic and empirical comparison to MMD, not a new discrepancy or new theory.
- Missing baselines and citations: no comparison to Sinkhorn OT (which is GPU-friendly and differentiable), WGAN-GP, Cramér/energy distances, or sliced Wasserstein; in DA, no comparison to DANN, JAN, CORAL, CMD (despite citing some).
- Evaluation for generative modeling is largely qualitative. No Inception Score/FID or likelihood proxies; training loss curves are not compelling evidence of sample quality. Datasets are small; the SVHN/LFW samples are blurry, and the feature/metric choice (L1 in pixel space) is acknowledged as suboptimal.
- Computational claims: while matching on N≈2000 is feasible, Hungarian O(N^3) is slow and even O(N^2) approximate matching on CPU is nontrivial. The paper does not compare to Sinkhorn both in speed and accuracy, despite strong claims about practicality. GPU acceleration is suggested but not implemented.
- Optimization details: The paper claims “unbiased stochastic gradient” (Eq. 3) but does not justify via Danskin’s theorem; the inner-loop “fixed matching + minibatch subsampling” creates biased updates. There is no convergence analysis of this scheme.
- “Parameter-free” is somewhat overstated: the ground metric and representation space are crucial hyperparameters; performance depends on choosing L1 and often a learned feature space.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. The OT connection is explained; the training algorithm is described with pseudocode and figures. However:
  - Please cite and use Danskin’s theorem when arguing gradients don’t propagate through the matching.
  - Clarify the bias introduced by subsampling a minibatch after computing a population-level matching and by reusing matchings across updates.
  - Some notational inconsistencies (population vs minibatch, N vs |B|) and minor grammatical issues can be cleaned up.
  - Include full architectural and hyperparameter details for reproducibility.

Originality
- The idea to directly use empirical W1/EMD (via minimum matching) as a training objective for deep models is not new in itself; what is new here is a specific SGD scheme and the empirical study vs. MMD. The theoretical result is known. The originality is therefore moderate.

Significance
- Practically, having a simple alternative to MMD that yields stronger gradients is useful. However, without comparisons to modern OT solvers (Sinkhorn) and strong generative/DA baselines, it is hard to judge impact. The scope of experiments (datasets and metrics) limits the demonstrated significance.

Pros
- Simple, intuitive objective coinciding with empirical Wasserstein-1.
- Clear training recipe; empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence than MMD.
- Solid, if standard, theoretical grounding (consistency).

Cons
- Limited novelty; theory is known; PMD is empirical W1/EMD.
- Missing key related work and baselines (Sinkhorn OT, WGAN-GP, energy/Cramér, sliced Wasserstein; DANN/JAN/CMD/CORAL).
- Generative evaluation lacks quantitative metrics; DA comparisons limited.
- No convergence analysis for the biased training heuristic; computational claims not benchmarked against strong OT baselines.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Compare against entropic OT (Sinkhorn) in both speed (GPU) and training quality; discuss stability/regularization trade-offs.
- Add WGAN-GP and energy/Cramér distance baselines for generation; report FID/Inception Score.
- In DA, include DANN, JAN, CMD, CORAL; avoid (or justify) using labeled target validation for hyperparameters.
- Provide a formal justification via Danskin/envelope theorem for ignoring ∂M*/∂θ; analyze the bias from subsampling/frozen matchings and give convergence guarantees if possible.
- Explore alternative ground metrics and learned feature spaces (e.g., perceptual features) to mitigate blurriness; discuss sensitivity.
- Situate the sample complexity more formally (known W1 convergence rates) and discuss dimension dependence.

Recommendation: Borderline reject. The paper presents a clean and practical use of empirical Wasserstein-1 with some nice empirical properties vs. MMD, but the conceptual novelty is limited and the experimental evaluation omits critical baselines and quantitative metrics needed for a NIPS-level contribution.

Confidence: 0.7
