This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, intended as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for training deep learning models. The core idea—matching samples from two distributions to estimate their distance—is intuitive and well-motivated, particularly given the limitations of MMD (bandwidth tuning, gradient saturation, large batch size requirements). The authors provide a theoretical justification for PMD’s consistency and demonstrate its advantages through experiments in domain adaptation and generative modeling.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PMD offers a fresh perspective on estimating distributional divergence, moving away from kernel-based approaches like MMD. The direct sample matching approach is conceptually simple and potentially more robust to certain issues affecting MMD.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper establishes a clear connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, proving its strong consistency. This provides a solid theoretical backing for the proposed method.
* **Practical Advantages:** The authors convincingly argue for PMD’s practical benefits—no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use standard mini-batch sizes—over MMD.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate PMD’s effectiveness, showing improvements in both performance and convergence speed compared to MMD. The inclusion of convergence speed comparison and analysis of gradient magnitudes are particularly valuable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical derivations are clear and the explanations are accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, a more detailed discussion of the practical implications for large-scale datasets would be beneficial. The transition to the approximated matching algorithm and its impact on accuracy requires further elaboration.  Specifically, how does the approximation affect the theoretical consistency guarantees?
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The paper exclusively uses the L1 distance for PMD. While justified, exploring the sensitivity of PMD to other distance metrics (e.g., Euclidean) and their potential impact on performance could strengthen the analysis.  Is L1 optimal for the presented applications?
* **Generative Modeling Limitations:** The observation that PMD generates blurry images on SVHN and LFW suggests a limitation of using L1 distance in pixel space. A more thorough investigation into alternative distance metrics or applying PMD in a latent space would be valuable.
* **Limited Baselines:** While MMD is a strong baseline, comparing PMD to more recent Wasserstein distance estimators (e.g., WGAN, sliced Wasserstein distance) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of its performance.
* **GPU Acceleration:** The paper mentions the possibility of GPU acceleration for the matching algorithms but does not present any empirical results on its effectiveness. Providing speed-up numbers would strengthen the practicality argument.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to estimating distributional divergence.
* The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over MMD in practical applications.
* The paper addresses an important problem in deep learning—efficiently estimating statistical distances.
* The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the matching algorithm could limit scalability to very large datasets.
* The reliance on the L1 distance may restrict performance in certain applications.
* The lack of comparison to more recent Wasserstein distance estimators weakens the assessment of novelty and significance.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of deep learning. The proposed PMD method offers a promising alternative to MMD, with clear theoretical advantages and compelling empirical results.  While the computational complexity and choice of distance metric warrant further investigation, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance.

**Recommendations:**

* **Expand the discussion on computational complexity:**  Provide more detailed analysis of the impact of the matching algorithm’s complexity on scalability and explore potential optimizations.
* **Investigate alternative distance metrics:**  Evaluate the sensitivity of PMD to different distance metrics and their impact on performance.
* **Compare with state-of-the-art Wasserstein estimators:**  Include a comparison to more recent methods like WGAN and sliced Wasserstein distance.
* **Explore GPU acceleration:**  Present empirical results on the speed-up achieved by GPU acceleration of the matching algorithms.
* **Address the limitations of L1 distance in generative modeling:** Investigate using PMD in a latent space or with alternative distance functions for improved image generation quality.



**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
