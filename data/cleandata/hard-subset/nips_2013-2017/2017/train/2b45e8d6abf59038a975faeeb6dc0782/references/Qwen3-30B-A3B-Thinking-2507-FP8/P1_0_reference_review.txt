

# Peer Review: "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical distance between two probability distributions defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two samples from the distributions. The authors establish that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and propose its use as a training objective in deep learning. The paper demonstrates PMD's advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes. The authors evaluate PMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing it outperforms MMD in both performance and convergence speed.

The paper connects to prior work on optimal transport (Wasserstein metric) and MMD, positioning PMD as a different approach from both. It builds on theoretical foundations of optimal transport and provides empirical validation of the claimed advantages over MMD.

## Quality
The theoretical analysis is sound, establishing PMD's connection to the Wasserstein metric through a series of well-supported propositions. The authors correctly cite Varadarajan's theorem and the stability of optimal transport to prove PMD's strong consistency. The experimental design is appropriate, with careful comparisons to MMD on standard benchmarks (Office dataset for domain adaptation and MNIST/SVHN/LFW for generative modeling).

The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the computational complexity of the matching problem and the potential suboptimality of L1 distance for natural image generation. The empirical analysis of matching algorithm performance (exact vs. approximate) and sensitivity to population size is thorough.

However, the paper lacks finite-sample error bounds for PMD, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. While the authors mention this as future work, it would have been valuable to include at least preliminary bounds in the current paper.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The definitions, algorithms, and theoretical results are presented logically. The pseudocode for parameter learning (Fig. 1) and the experimental results (Figs. 2-5) are particularly helpful for understanding the method.

The paper could improve clarity by:
1. Providing more intuitive explanations of why PMD gradients are stronger than MMD's (Fig. 2b is helpful but could be better explained in text)
2. Clarifying the relationship between the population size N and mini-batch size |B| (the paper uses N as the "population size" but in MMD literature, this is often called the mini-batch size)

## Originality
The core idea of using minimum weight matching as a statistical discrepancy is novel in the context of deep learning applications. While the Hungarian algorithm for matching has been used before, the application to statistical distance estimation in deep learning is original. The paper clearly differentiates PMD from related work like MMD and Wasserstein GAN.

The authors properly cite relevant work, including the seminal optimal transport papers and MMD literature. However, the paper could better address recent work on approximating Wasserstein distances (e.g., the Cramer distance [Bellemare et al., 2017] and the work by Li et al. on MMD GANs), which also aim to improve upon MMD for generative modeling.

## Significance
The potential significance of PMD is substantial if the claimed advantages (no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch) hold in practice. For practitioners, the ability to use smaller batch sizes could significantly speed up training, especially for large-scale applications. The empirical results across multiple domains (domain adaptation, generative modeling) demonstrate that PMD can be a viable alternative to MMD.

The paper addresses a real problem in deep learning: the limitations of MMD in generative modeling (where it struggles with pixel-space generation) and the need for efficient distribution matching in domain adaptation.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Strong theoretical foundation connecting PMD to Wasserstein metric
2. Clear demonstration of practical advantages over MMD (no bandwidth, stronger gradients, smaller batch size)
3. Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple domains and datasets
4. Good discussion of implementation considerations (matching algorithms, computational complexity)
5. Clear presentation of results with informative figures

**Weaknesses:**
1. Computational complexity of matching (O(N^3)) could be a bottleneck for large-scale applications, and the paper's claim that it's "acceptable" compared to backpropagation needs stronger justification
2. Limited comparison with other distribution matching methods beyond MMD (e.g., WGAN, Cramer distance)
3. Lacks finite-sample error bounds for PMD
4. Uses L1 distance for image generation, which is suboptimal for natural images (acknowledged but not addressed with a better solution)
5. The connection to deep learning is somewhat limited to specific architectures (fc, conv, ae), without broader exploration

## Recommendation
The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to statistical distance estimation with clear practical advantages over MMD. While the computational complexity of the matching algorithm is a concern, the authors provide thorough empirical analysis showing that approximate matching can be used effectively. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the experimental results are convincing.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of distribution matching in deep learning. It addresses a practical problem with a theoretically grounded solution that has demonstrable benefits in real applications. While there are some limitations to be addressed (particularly regarding computational complexity at scale), these do not undermine the core contribution.

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel approach to statistical distance estimation
- Strong theoretical foundation
- Clear practical advantages over MMD
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple domains
- Well-written and well-organized

**Con Acceptance:**
- Computational complexity of exact matching might limit scalability
- Limited comparison with other recent methods beyond MMD
- Lacks finite-sample error bounds

I recommend acceptance to NIPS. This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field with clear theoretical and empirical advantages over existing methods, and the authors have provided a thorough and well-executed study. The work could have immediate impact on practitioners working with distribution matching in deep learning applications.
