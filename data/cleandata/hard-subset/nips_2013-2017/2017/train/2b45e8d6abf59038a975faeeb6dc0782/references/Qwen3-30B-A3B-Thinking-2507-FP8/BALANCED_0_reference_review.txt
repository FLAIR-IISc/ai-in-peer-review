

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based statistical divergence for comparing probability distributions. The authors define PMD as the minimum weight matching cost between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions, and establish its theoretical connection to the first Wasserstein metric. They propose an algorithm to use PMD as a training objective in deep learning, claiming advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch size requirements. The authors validate their approach on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, reporting consistent improvements over MMD.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, establishing PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric. The connection between PMD and optimal transport theory is well-motivated and rigorously proven through Propositions 3.1-3.3. This theoretical grounding is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Clear Advantages Over MMD**: The authors effectively articulate PMD's advantages over MMD, particularly regarding gradient strength (Fig. 2b) and mini-batch size requirements. The gradient analysis is compelling and addresses a genuine limitation of MMD that affects training dynamics.

3. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The empirical evaluation covers multiple datasets (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW) and tasks (domain adaptation, generative modeling). The ablation studies (Fig. 5) thoroughly examine the impact of population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithm choice.

4. **Practical Implementation**: The paper addresses practical concerns by proposing a stochastic optimization approach that avoids computing the full matching at every step, and by discussing both exact and approximate matching algorithms.

## Weaknesses

1. **Computational Cost**: While the paper acknowledges the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, the practical impact on large-scale applications is not sufficiently addressed. The authors note that "when N is larger, e.g., a few thousands, the Hungarian algorithm takes seconds to run," but for modern deep learning applications with N=1000-10,000, this could become a bottleneck. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of computational trade-offs compared to other methods.

2. **Limited Comparison with Other Wasserstein Approaches**: The paper focuses primarily on comparing PMD with MMD, but doesn't thoroughly contrast it with other Wasserstein-based methods like WGAN, WGAN-GP, or the Cramer distance. This is particularly relevant given that the paper's theoretical foundation is the Wasserstein metric, but the experimental comparisons are limited to MMD.

3. **Generative Modeling Results**: The blurry images generated by PMD on SVHN and LFW (Fig. 4) suggest a limitation of using L1 distance at the pixel level. The authors acknowledge this but don't explore alternatives (e.g., using feature space distances) that might improve image quality while maintaining PMD's advantages.

4. **Approximation Error Analysis**: The approximation error analysis (Fig. 2a) shows a relatively slow convergence rate (N^{-0.23}), which might be concerning for applications requiring high accuracy. The paper doesn't discuss how this affects practical applications or how it compares to other estimators' error rates.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to statistical divergence estimation with clear practical advantages over MMD. The theoretical contributions are strong, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the paper addresses important limitations of existing methods. However, the computational considerations and limited comparison with other Wasserstein-based approaches represent significant gaps.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical contribution connecting minimum weight matching to the Wasserstein metric
- Clear, well-supported advantages over MMD with strong empirical evidence
- Comprehensive experimental validation across multiple tasks and datasets
- Practical implementation details addressing real-world training concerns

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Insufficient discussion of computational trade-offs for large-scale applications
- Limited comparison with other Wasserstein-based methods (WGAN, Cramer distance)
- Unresolved limitations in generative modeling results
- Relatively slow convergence rate of the estimator (N^{-0.23})

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a more detailed computational analysis comparing PMD with WGAN and other Wasserstein-based methods, including wall-clock time and memory requirements.

2. Extend the generative modeling experiments to explore feature space distances instead of pixel-level L1, which might improve image quality while maintaining PMD's advantages.

3. Conduct a more thorough comparison with the Cramer distance [Bellemare et al., 2017] and other recent Wasserstein-based approaches.

4. Provide a more detailed analysis of the approximation error rate and its implications for practical applications.

5. Discuss the limitations of PMD in scenarios where matching might not be meaningful (e.g., high-dimensional data with complex structures).

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of statistical divergence estimation for deep learning. With the suggested improvements, particularly regarding computational analysis and broader methodological comparisons, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. As it stands, the paper is strong but would benefit from addressing the computational and comparative aspects more thoroughly. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
