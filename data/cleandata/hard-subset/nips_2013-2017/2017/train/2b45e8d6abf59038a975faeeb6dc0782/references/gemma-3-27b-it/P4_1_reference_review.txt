This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, proposing it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for various deep learning tasks. PMD minimizes the cost of matching samples between two distributions. The authors demonstrate theoretical consistency and empirical improvements on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.  Prior work in sample-based divergence estimation includes MMD [Gretton et al., 2012], which is a kernel-based approach, and Wasserstein GANs [Arjovsky et al., 2017], which approximates the dual problem. This work distinguishes itself by providing a differentiable estimator of the Wasserstein metric that doesn’t rely on kernel bandwidth tuning or a critic network, and claims to perform well with standard mini-batch sizes.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper establishes a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, proving its strong consistency. This provides a solid mathematical grounding for the proposed method.
*   **Clear Motivation:**  The authors clearly articulate the limitations of MMD (bandwidth tuning, gradient saturation, large batch size requirement) and position PMD as a solution to these issues.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of both performance and convergence speed.  The ablation studies regarding population size and mini-batch size provide useful insights.
*   **Gradient Analysis:** The analysis of gradient behavior (Fig. 2b) is insightful and supports the claim that PMD provides stronger, more consistent gradients compared to MMD.
*   **Computational Considerations:** The discussion of computational complexity and potential GPU acceleration of the matching algorithm is practical and relevant.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Distance Metric Choice:**  The paper consistently uses the L1 distance for PMD. While justified, the impact of other distance metrics on performance isn't explored. The authors acknowledge that L1 distance isn’t optimal for natural images, but don't investigate alternatives.
*   **Limited Generative Modeling Results:** The generative modeling results, while positive, are somewhat limited. The blurry images generated on SVHN and LFW suggest further investigation is needed, especially with more sophisticated architectures or loss functions. The claim that PMD *does not trivially reproduce the training dataset* feels somewhat hand-wavy without more rigorous assessment.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity in Experiments:** While PMD is presented as having fewer hyperparameters, the domain adaptation experiments still require tuning of the regularization parameter λ.  A more thorough comparison of hyperparameter sensitivity between PMD and MMD would strengthen the argument.
*   **Matching Algorithm Scalability:** While the paper acknowledges the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm and suggests approximations, the scalability to very large datasets remains a concern. Further analysis of the computational bottlenecks and potential optimization strategies is warranted.
* **Lack of Comparison to WGAN:** While acknowledging WGAN, the paper doesn’t include a direct empirical comparison. Given WGAN’s success in generative modeling, a comparison would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound method for estimating the Wasserstein metric.
*   The empirical results demonstrate a clear advantage of PMD over MMD in the tested scenarios.
*   The analysis of gradient behavior provides valuable insights into the benefits of PMD.
*   The work addresses a relevant and challenging problem in deep learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on the L1 distance may limit the applicability of PMD to certain data types.
*   The generative modeling results are not entirely convincing and require further investigation.
*   The scalability of the matching algorithm to very large datasets is a potential concern.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of sample-based divergence estimation. The theoretical foundation is solid, the empirical results are compelling, and the analysis is insightful.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should consider exploring alternative distance metrics, providing more comprehensive generative modeling results, and discussing the scalability of the matching algorithm in more detail.
