- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based distance via minimum weight matching and positions it as an alternative to MMD with no bandwidth hyperparameter and purportedly stronger gradients and smaller mini-batch needs.
- Theoretical result: PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (proof uses Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport).
- Empirical claim: PMD shows advantages over MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling—faster convergence, less parameter sensitivity, and steadier gradient magnitudes (supported by Figure 2(b)).
- Main theoretical weakness: paper omits finite-sample error bounds, leaving practical performance guarantees unclear.
- Computational concern: matching requires O(N^3) time (Hungarian algorithm); authors mention approximations but do not analyze their impact on estimate quality or provide justification for normal mini-batch use.
- Comparison weakness: limited empirical comparison—does not evaluate against other Wasserstein approximations/approaches such as WGAN or EMD.
- Experimental issues: training-duration inconsistency (PMD 100 epochs vs MMD 500) undermines convergence claims; L1 distance choice not justified and likely explains blurry generated images.
- Originality/significance: reasonable but incremental—conceptually related to existing optimal transport/EMD work and improvements over MMD are modest and potentially offset by computational costs.
- Recommendations: provide finite-sample bounds, analyze exact vs approximate matching trade-offs, compare with other Wasserstein methods (e.g., WGAN), and fix experimental inconsistencies.
- Final recommendation: weak accept—technically sound with promising results but needs stronger theory and more comprehensive experimental comparisons before being a major contribution.
