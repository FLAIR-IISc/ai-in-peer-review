This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the Wasserstein metric between distributions. The core idea – finding a minimum-weight matching between samples – is intuitively appealing and theoretically grounded in its consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein metric. While the authors identify several potential advantages of PMD (no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch sizes), the empirical validation provided feels insufficient to strongly support these claims and justify a significant departure from the widely adopted MMD.

The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the connection to the Wasserstein metric is well-explained. However, the discussion often feels somewhat repetitive, reiterating the advantages of PMD without providing deeper insights. Reproducibility is hampered by the lack of detailed implementation specifics (beyond mentioning TensorFlow and C++), and the reliance on a single-threaded C++ matching implementation seems unnecessarily limiting given the potential for GPU acceleration.

The originality of the work is moderate. Estimating the Wasserstein distance with sample matching is not entirely novel, and the core contribution lies in framing it as PMD and providing a specific stochastic gradient descent algorithm. The comparison to MMD, while useful, is fairly standard; the authors don’t deeply investigate *why* PMD performs better when it does, beyond suggesting stronger gradients. A more thorough literature review exploring existing sample-based Wasserstein estimators would strengthen this aspect.

The significance of the work is questionable at this stage. While the reported improvements on the domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks are encouraging, they are not dramatic, and the experiments are limited in scope. The authors acknowledge blurriness in the generated images and attribute it to the L1 distance, but this suggests a fundamental limitation of using PMD directly on pixel space, without proposing a solution. The claim of faster convergence is supported by a single figure, and the impact of different mini-batch sizes isn't fully explored. The theoretical analysis establishing consistency is valuable, but doesn't automatically translate to practical superiority. 

**Strengths:**

*   Clear mathematical formulation of PMD and its connection to the Wasserstein metric.
*   Theoretical justification for PMD as a consistent estimator.
*   Identification of potential advantages over MMD (bandwidth-free, gradient properties, mini-batch size).
*   Demonstrated improvement on some experimental tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited empirical validation – experiments are relatively small-scale and focus on a limited number of datasets and architectures.
*   Lack of detailed implementation specifics and GPU acceleration.
*   Limited analysis of *why* PMD performs better when it does.
*   The choice of L1 distance seems suboptimal for image generation.
*   Comparison to other Wasserstein distance estimators is missing.
*   Repetitive presentation of advantages without deeper exploration.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretically sound alternative to MMD for estimating Wasserstein distance.
*   Demonstrates promising results on domain adaptation and generative modeling.
*   The smaller mini-batch size capability is a potential advantage for scalability.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Empirical results are not compelling enough to outweigh the established benefits of MMD.
*   Limited scope of experiments and lack of ablation studies.
*   Missing comparison to other relevant Wasserstein distance estimation methods.
*   The dependence on the L1 distance may limit the applicability of PMD in some domains.
*   The practical benefits (faster convergence, stronger gradients) are not convincingly demonstrated across a wider range of settings.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the ideas presented are sound and the theoretical analysis is solid, the empirical validation is insufficient to convince me that PMD represents a significant advance over existing methods like MMD. The advantages claimed are not consistently demonstrated, and the limitations (particularly the use of L1 distance) are not adequately addressed. More extensive experimentation, a broader comparison to related work, and a deeper analysis of the results are needed before PMD can be considered a viable alternative to established techniques. The paper feels more like a promising initial exploration than a fully developed contribution.
