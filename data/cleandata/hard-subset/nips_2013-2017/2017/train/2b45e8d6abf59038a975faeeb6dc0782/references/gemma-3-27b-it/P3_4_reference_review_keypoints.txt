- Paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric using minimum weight bipartite matching.  
- PMD is theoretically consistent and connects clearly to the Wasserstein distance.  
- PMD avoids kernel bandwidth tuning, can yield stronger gradients, and supports smaller mini-batches compared to MMD.  
- Authors provide clear presentation of PMD formulation, algorithm, and theoretical properties.  
- Empirical evaluation includes domain adaptation and generative modeling with ablation studies and comparisons to MMD/WGAN.  
- Experiments on MNIST/SVHN/LFW show compelling initial performance of PMD.  
- Computational cost is a major concern due to O(N^3) Hungarian algorithm complexity and potential matching bottlenecks.  
- Runtime comparisons to MMD are insufficiently detailed and need benchmarking on comparable hardware.  
- Exclusive use of L1 distance is limiting; other distance metrics should be explored, especially for image quality.  
- Experimental scope is limited to two applications; broader evaluations (e.g., RL, adversarial training) would strengthen claims.  
- Domain adaptation results lack statistical significance testing to confirm improvements.  
- Subsampling-based gradient approximation (Eq. 4) is intuitively reasonable but lacks rigorous justification or empirical validation.  
- PMD’s originality lies in a solvable, differentiable optimal-transport-based objective distinct from dual formulations like WGAN.  
- If computational bottlenecks (e.g., GPU-accelerated matching) are addressed, PMD could be widely useful as an alternative to MMD.  
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theory, practical advantages over MMD, compelling empirical results, and clear writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: matching computational cost, limited distance metrics explored, missing significance testing, and narrow application scope.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance with minor revisions: add detailed runtime benchmarks, statistical tests, discussion of alternative metrics, and expand analysis of subsampling impact.
