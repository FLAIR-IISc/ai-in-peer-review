Summary
This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets. PMD coincides with the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance under uniform weights and is shown (via Varadarajan + OT stability) to be a strongly consistent estimator of W1. The authors use PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep models by alternating between computing a matching over a “population” of samples and performing several SGD steps while keeping the matching fixed; they also propose sub-batching to reduce compute. Experiments in domain adaptation (Office) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) compare PMD primarily to MMD, claiming fewer hyperparameters, stronger gradients, and faster convergence.

Strengths
- Clear connection to W1 and a straightforward consistency argument. The estimator is intuitive and well known in OT as empirical EMD.
- Practical algorithmic recipe and empirical observation that per-sample gradients are more uniformly strong than with RBF-MMD.
- Some engineering considerations (approximate matching, GPU kernel for pairwise distances) and sensitivity analyses (N vs |B|).
- In domain adaptation, PMD appears competitive or better than MMD on several Office transfer tasks and converges faster on one setting.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited novelty. PMD is exactly the empirical Earth Mover’s Distance (W1) with equal masses. Using OT as a training loss for deep models predates this work (e.g., Cuturi 2013 Sinkhorn distances for scalable, differentiable OT; Frogner et al., NIPS 2015 “Learning with a Wasserstein Loss”). These works offer smooth, GPU-friendly, differentiable surrogates and have been used to train neural networks. The paper does not cite or compare to these canonical baselines, which significantly weakens the contribution.
- Incomplete and partly incorrect discussion of alternatives. The paper claims dual approximations are not differentiable w.r.t. distribution parameters because parameters appear in constraints, which is misleading; WGAN provides gradients via the critic, and entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) is differentiable end-to-end. Energy/Cramér distances and MMD-GAN are also relevant and not adequately compared.
- Theoretical depth is modest. Consistency follows directly from standard results; there are no finite-sample bounds, convergence guarantees for the proposed stochastic procedure, or analysis of the well-known N−1/d sample complexity barrier of W1 in d dimensions (the toy 5D regression hints at this but is not analyzed). The claim of “unbiased stochastic gradient” is questionable: (i) the objective is non-smooth because the optimal matching is piecewise constant in parameters (Danskin’s theorem/subgradients should be invoked); (ii) reusing a fixed matching for several SGD steps and subsampling B from the N matched pairs introduces bias. None of this is addressed rigorously.
- Scalability and practicality. Exact matching is O(N^3) and approximate matching O(N^2). In modern deep learning regimes, N in the thousands per step is expensive. The paper suggests GPU parallelization but does not implement it; comparisons to Sinkhorn (O(N^2) matrix scaling on GPU) are absent. The use of approximate matching for generative modeling lacks guarantees or ablations on approximation quality.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow with weak baselines. For domain adaptation, the paper only contrasts with MMD. It omits strong, established baselines such as DANN/GRL, DAN, JAN, CORAL, CMD, and more recent OT-based DA. Without these comparisons, it is unclear that PMD is competitive beyond beating plain MMD. For generative modeling, evaluation is qualitative; no standard metrics (IS, FID) or likelihood surrogates are reported, and datasets are small. The authors note the generated images are blurry with pixel L1 and switch to code space, where MMD is known to work well; yet even there, the comparisons remain limited. The fairness of hyperparameter tuning (e.g., kernel mixtures, bandwidth selection strategies) for MMD could be improved; linear-time/unbiased MMD estimators are not considered.
- Clarity and presentation issues. Some claims (unbiased gradients, mini-batch vs population distinction) are confusing or not carefully qualified. Important details (e.g., Table 1 results) are missing from the excerpt, and there are formatting glitches. The relationship to prior OT-in-learning literature needs substantial expansion and correction.

Questions for the authors
1. How does PMD compare, both in accuracy and wall-clock, to entropic OT (Sinkhorn) and Sinkhorn divergences when used as training losses on the same tasks? Please include GPU implementations.
2. Can you formalize the gradient claims using Danskin’s theorem and clarify when your estimator is unbiased? What is the impact of fixing the matching for multiple steps and sub-batching on convergence?
3. Can you report quantitative generative metrics (IS/FID) and include stronger baselines (WGAN/WGAN-GP, Cramér, MMD-GAN)?
4. For domain adaptation, please compare to DANN/GRL, DAN/JAN, CORAL, and CMD on Office with the same features and report standard protocols.
5. What is the effect of dimensionality on sample complexity for PMD in practice? Any mitigation beyond code-space training?

Pros
- Simple, parameter-free objective directly tied to W1.
- Empirical evidence of stronger, more uniform per-sample gradients than RBF-MMD.
- Works reasonably with approximate matching and smaller sub-batches in some settings.

Cons
- Overlaps heavily with known OT/EMD; missing key related work and comparisons (notably Sinkhorn and Wasserstein-based training).
- Theoretical contributions are minimal; important claims about gradients and bias are not rigorously supported.
- Scalability and practicality relative to established OT alternatives are not convincingly demonstrated.
- Empirical evaluation lacks strong baselines and standard quantitative metrics.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is intuitive and potentially useful, it largely revisits established ground (empirical W1/EMD as a loss) without engaging with the strongest existing methods, and the evidence provided is insufficient to recommend acceptance over well-validated alternatives. Strengthening the related work, adding rigorous gradient analysis, and providing comprehensive comparisons (especially to Sinkhorn-based methods and state-of-the-art baselines) with quantitative metrics would significantly improve the paper.

Confidence
High.
