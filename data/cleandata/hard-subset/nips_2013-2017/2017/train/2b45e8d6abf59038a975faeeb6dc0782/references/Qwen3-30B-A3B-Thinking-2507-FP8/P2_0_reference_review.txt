

# Review: Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein metric that avoids the bandwidth parameter required by Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors propose using PMD as a training objective in deep learning tasks, arguing it offers advantages including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements. The paper demonstrates PMD's effectiveness in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

## Quality

The paper provides a theoretically grounded foundation for PMD as a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Theorem 3.3), which is a significant contribution. The authors effectively connect PMD to optimal transport theory and establish its relationship to the Wasserstein metric through the minimum weight matching formulation. The empirical evaluation is reasonably thorough, with experiments on standard benchmarks (Office dataset for domain adaptation, MNIST/SVHN/LFW for generative modeling).

However, the theoretical analysis could be strengthened. While the consistency proof is mentioned, the paper lacks detailed error bounds for finite N (which would be important for practical applications). The claim about "stronger gradients" is supported by empirical gradient magnitude distributions (Fig. 2b), but lacks a rigorous theoretical justification. The paper also doesn't address the computational complexity of the matching step for large N (O(N³) for Hungarian algorithm) in the context of large-scale deep learning applications.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical definitions are precise, and the connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is well-explained. Figures 2(a) and 2(b) effectively illustrate key empirical findings about approximation error and gradient distributions.

The paper could improve clarity by:
1. More explicitly distinguishing between "population size" N (used in PMD definition) and "mini-batch size" |B| (used in training), as these terms are sometimes conflated.
2. Providing a more detailed explanation of why the gradient doesn't propagate through the matching (Eq. 3), as this is a critical technical point.
3. Clarifying the relationship between PMD and Earth Mover's Distance (EMD), which is conceptually similar but has been used in previous deep learning work.

## Originality

The core idea of using minimum weight matching as a distributional distance measure is not entirely novel (Earth Mover's Distance has been used before), but the specific formulation as "Population Matching Discrepancy" and its application as a training objective in deep learning appears original. The paper effectively contrasts PMD with MMD and identifies concrete advantages (no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch requirements) that distinguish it from existing approaches.

The paper could strengthen its originality claim by more thoroughly comparing with related work on matching-based distances in deep learning. While the authors cite some relevant papers (e.g., WGAN), they don't engage deeply with existing applications of matching-based metrics in the literature.

## Significance

The paper addresses an important practical challenge in deep learning: measuring the distance between complex distributions. The claims that PMD avoids bandwidth tuning (a known pain point in MMD), provides stronger gradients, and allows smaller mini-batch sizes could be significant for practitioners. The empirical results showing PMD outperforming MMD on domain adaptation tasks (Table 1) and generating less noisy images (Fig. 4) are promising.

However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the computational cost of the matching step. The paper acknowledges the O(N³) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm but doesn't adequately address how this scales to large-scale deep learning applications. For example, the paper uses N=2000 for generative modeling experiments, but in practice, larger N would be needed for complex distributions, potentially making the approach prohibitively expensive.

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid contribution that makes a meaningful technical advance in the area of distributional distance metrics for deep learning. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides valuable empirical evidence supporting its claims. The main limitations are the lack of detailed computational complexity analysis for large-scale applications and insufficient theoretical justification for the "stronger gradients" claim.

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical foundation connecting PMD to Wasserstein metric
- Clear empirical evidence of advantages over MMD in specific tasks
- Practical implementation details for using PMD in deep learning
- Well-organized and clearly written

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of gradient strength
- Insufficient discussion of computational complexity for large-scale applications
- Some conceptual overlap with Earth Mover's Distance that could be better addressed
- Limited exploration of the trade-offs between matching algorithm choices

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. While the paper has some limitations in theoretical depth and computational analysis, it makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distributional distance metrics in deep learning. The advantages of PMD over MMD are compelling, and the empirical results are convincing. With minor revisions addressing the computational complexity discussion and strengthening the theoretical justification for gradient strength, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NeurIPS.

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way by providing a practical, parameter-free alternative to MMD with clear advantages in specific applications. This work is likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on generative models, domain adaptation, and distributional metrics.
