This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, positioning it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling. The core idea – minimizing the cost of matching samples between distributions – is conceptually straightforward and builds upon existing optimal transport theory. The authors claim advantages over MMD regarding hyperparameter sensitivity, gradient behavior, and mini-batch size requirements.

While the idea of employing a minimum cost matching for distribution comparison is reasonable, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to potentially impactful methodological simplifications and a lack of thorough experimental validation. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Conceptually Sound:** The core idea of PMD is well-rooted in optimal transport and provides a potentially viable alternative to MMD.
*   **Theoretical Connection:** The paper correctly establishes the connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, demonstrating strong consistency.
*   **Potential Advantages:** The claims regarding reduced hyperparameter tuning and potential for smaller mini-batch sizes are promising.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** The notation is inconsistent throughout. For instance, ‘N’ is used to denote both the population size *and* the number of samples in a batch (denoted ‘|B|’). Using distinct symbols would improve clarity.
*   **Gradient Approximation Justification:** The justification for the subsampling gradient approximation in Eq. (4) is weak. While stated to be for efficiency, the authors do not explore the impact of this approximation on gradient accuracy or convergence in a quantitative manner. It's simply *assumed* that the matching (M*) doesn't change much, which is not always true.
*   **Matching Algorithm Choice:** The paper discusses both the exact Hungarian algorithm and the approximated Drake & Hougardy algorithm, but the selection criteria are vague. The computational cost analysis lacks granularity – simply stating O(N3) vs. O(N2) is insufficient. The authors claim the approximate matching "works well," but don't provide detailed empirical comparisons demonstrating negligible performance degradation.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup is concerning. The domain adaptation experiments validate hyperparameters on a separate 100-sample dataset, which is arguably too small for reliable validation. Furthermore, only reporting results for a few specific tasks and dataset combinations limits the generalizability of the conclusions. The ablation study is non-existent.
*   **L1 Distance Limitation:** The exclusive use of the L1 distance is a significant limitation. The authors acknowledge it might not be ideal for natural images, yet proceed with it regardless. Exploring other distance metrics (e.g., Euclidean, cosine) could yield substantially better results. The choice feels arbitrary.
*   **Missing Baseline:** The paper lacks a comparison to Wasserstein GAN (WGAN), a highly relevant and successful application of the Wasserstein metric in generative modeling. This omission is a major oversight.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim that PMD "outperforms MMD" is too strong given the limited experimental evidence. While some improvements are observed, they aren’t universally consistent across all tasks.
*   **Minor Issues:** Numerous minor grammatical errors and awkward phrasing detract from the paper's readability. Equation (2) contains a poorly formatted expectation. Figure 1 is difficult to interpret and lacks clear labeling.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The theoretical analysis is sound and the connection to the Wasserstein metric is well-established.
*   The idea is novel and potentially useful.

**Con:**

*   The experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims.
*   The methodological simplifications (gradient approximation, L1 distance) raise concerns.
*   The lack of a comparison to WGAN is a significant omission.
*   The paper is riddled with minor flaws that detract from its overall quality.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a potentially interesting idea, the numerous methodological shortcomings, limited experimental validation, and overall lack of rigor prevent it from meeting the standards of a NIPS publication. The authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above, provide a more thorough experimental evaluation (including comparisons to state-of-the-art methods like WGAN), and significantly improve the clarity and presentation of the paper. Even with the potential benefits, the accumulation of flaws outweighs the novelty of the approach. It reads more like a preliminary study than a complete and polished research contribution.
