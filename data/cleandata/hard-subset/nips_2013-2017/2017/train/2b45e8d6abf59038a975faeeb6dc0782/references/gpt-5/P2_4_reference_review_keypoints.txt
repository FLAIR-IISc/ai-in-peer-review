Proposes PMD: a sample-based estimator of 1-Wasserstein using minimum-weight bipartite matching and average pairwise cost.

Formalizes PMD as empirical OT with uniform masses and shows consistency via Varadarajan’s theorem and OT stability.

No new theoretical contributions beyond known OT results; lacks finite-sample bounds or dimensional-rate analysis.

Differentiation/optimization argument relies on permutation being locally constant but lacks a formal Danskin-style statement and proof of unbiased gradients; tie/uniqueness issues unaddressed.

Algorithmic and scalability concerns: exact Hungarian O(N^3), approximate O(N^2), and pairwise distance storage/computation O(N^2D); only CPU implementations shown.

GPU acceleration is claimed possible but not demonstrated; practical scaling strategies and approximation guarantees are not provided.

Domain adaptation experiments show improvements over MMD on some Office tasks but compare to a weak set of baselines (missing DANN, CMD, OT-based DA).

Generative modeling shows qualitatively less noisy but blurry images; no standard quantitative metrics (FID/Inception) are reported, so competitiveness vs GAN/WGAN is unclear.

Gradient-magnitude histograms suggest stronger gradients than MMD but provide indirect evidence without analysis of optimization stability or convergence.

The claim that MMD “must” use minibatches equal to population size ignores linear-time unbiased MMD estimators and misrepresents MMD minibatch behavior.

Paper is generally clear but needs formal references/lemma for stopping gradients, more detail on approximate matching algorithms, exact training hyperparameters, and code for reproducibility.

Originality is moderate and somewhat incremental; key related work (Sinkhorn/entropic OT, OT-based DA) is missing and not compared against.

Significance is uncertain: PMD is simple and parameter-free but limited baselines, missing OT comparisons, and scalability costs reduce practical impact.

Recommendation: borderline reject; strengthen related work, add comparisons to entropic OT and modern DA/GAN baselines, include quantitative generative metrics, and clarify theoretical/differentiation and scalability issues.
