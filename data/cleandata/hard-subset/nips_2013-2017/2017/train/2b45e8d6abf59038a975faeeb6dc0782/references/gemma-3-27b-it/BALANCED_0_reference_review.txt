This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, proposing it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for training deep learning models. The core idea is to minimize the cost of optimally matching samples from two distributions. The authors demonstrate theoretical consistency and empirical benefits in domain adaptation and generative modeling, highlighting PMD’s advantages: no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and ability to use standard mini-batch sizes.

The paper builds upon existing work in statistical divergence estimation, specifically MMD [Gretton et al., 2012] and Wasserstein distance [Villani, 2008]. It also connects to recent advancements in generative modeling like WGAN [Arjovsky et al., 2017] and moment matching networks [Li et al., 2017]. The authors correctly identify limitations of MMD (bandwidth tuning, gradient saturation) and position PMD as a potential solution.  The focus on differentiable divergence estimation is relevant to the NIPS audience, as it directly impacts gradient-based training of deep models.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PMD offers a fresh perspective on estimating the Wasserstein metric and presents a viable alternative to MMD.  The theoretical analysis demonstrating consistency is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The connection to the first Wasserstein metric is rigorously established, providing a strong mathematical basis for PMD.
* **Empirical Results:**  The experiments in domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate PMD’s effectiveness and highlight its advantages over MMD, particularly in convergence speed. The ablation studies examining population size and matching algorithms are valuable.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts and methodology. The pseudocode aids understanding.
* **Practical Considerations:** Addressing the computational cost of the matching problem and proposing both exact and approximate solutions shows awareness of practical implementation challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the results are promising, the experiments are limited to two tasks (domain adaptation and generative modeling).  Exploring other applications (e.g., metric learning, adversarial training) would strengthen the paper.
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The paper exclusively uses the L1 distance for PMD. While this choice enables stronger gradients, it might not be optimal for all data modalities (e.g., images).  Investigating other distance metrics (e.g., Euclidean distance) could be beneficial.
* **Comparison with WGAN:**  The paper mentions WGAN but doesn’t provide a direct comparison, especially regarding performance and computational cost. A comparative evaluation would be insightful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Although PMD has fewer hyperparameters than MMD, the performance can still be sensitive to population size and mini-batch size, as shown in Fig. 5(a). A more thorough sensitivity analysis and guidelines for choosing these parameters would be helpful.
* **GPU Acceleration:** While the authors mention GPU acceleration for the matching algorithms, the current implementation is CPU-based. Providing GPU-accelerated code or at least demonstrating significant speedups with GPU acceleration would enhance the practical impact of the work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant advantages over MMD in specific applications.
* The clear writing and well-organized structure make the paper accessible to a broad audience.
* The authors address practical considerations such as computational cost and provide both exact and approximate solutions.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The exclusive use of the L1 distance may not be optimal for all applications.
* The lack of a direct comparison with WGAN leaves a gap in the analysis.
* The sensitivity to hyperparameters requires further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel contribution and promising results. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and a comparison with WGAN would be valuable, the theoretical foundations and empirical evidence presented are convincing.  I believe the paper merits acceptance, with the caveat that the authors should consider addressing the weaknesses mentioned above in future work.

**Recommendation:**  Accept.

**Specific Suggestions:**

* **Expand Experiments:**  Include experiments on other tasks beyond domain adaptation and generative modeling.
* **Investigate Distance Metrics:** Explore the impact of using different distance metrics with PMD.
* **Compare to WGAN:**  Provide a direct comparison with WGAN in terms of performance and computational cost.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning Guidelines:**  Provide more detailed guidelines for choosing population size and mini-batch size.
* **GPU Implementation:**  Develop a GPU-accelerated implementation of the matching algorithms and report the speedups.
* **Clarify Notation:**  Ensure consistent use of notation throughout the paper (e.g., clarify the difference between N and mini-batch size |B|).
