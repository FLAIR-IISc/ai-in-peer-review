

# Review

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel method for estimating statistical divergence between two probability distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sets of samples from the distributions, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes an algorithm to use PMD as a training objective for learning distribution parameters, and compares it with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

The paper makes several significant technical contributions. The theoretical analysis in Section 3 establishes PMD's relationship with the Wasserstein metric and demonstrates its strong consistency through appropriate applications of Varadarajan's theorem and stability of optimal transport. The authors provide a clear comparison of PMD with MMD, highlighting advantages such as no kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs. 1000 for MMD). The experimental results in Section 5 generally support these claims, showing PMD outperforms MMD on Office domain adaptation tasks (a→w and a→d by large margins) and generates less noisy images in generative modeling tasks.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The theoretical foundations are solid, with appropriate references to optimal transport theory and related work (e.g., Villani [49], Gretton et al. [17]). The experimental design is sound, with appropriate baselines and evaluation metrics on standard benchmarks. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the computational cost of matching and the suboptimality of L1 distance for natural images.

**Strengths:**
- Strong theoretical foundation establishing PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric.
- Clear demonstration of PMD's practical advantages over MMD (no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size).
- Comprehensive experimental validation across two important deep learning tasks.
- Well-written with good organization and sufficient detail for reproduction.
- Theoretical analysis properly contextualizes PMD within existing work on MMD and Wasserstein distance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The computational complexity of the matching problem (O(N³) for Hungarian algorithm) may become prohibitive for large N, though the authors note this is acceptable compared to training costs. The paper could provide more concrete guidance on when PMD becomes computationally infeasible.
- Limited comparison with other distance metrics beyond MMD (e.g., WGAN variants, other approximate Wasserstein distance methods).
- The claim about "stronger gradients" is supported by empirical evidence and analysis, but the theoretical comparison of gradient magnitudes could be more rigorous.
- The use of L1 distance for image generation is noted to be suboptimal, but the paper doesn't explore alternative distance metrics that might yield better results.

The significance of this work is moderate. While the paper demonstrates clear improvements over MMD in specific scenarios, it doesn't introduce a fundamentally new theoretical framework. However, the practical advantages in terms of hyper-parameter sensitivity and convergence speed could be valuable to practitioners in domain adaptation and generative modeling.

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is correct and properly referenced, and the experimental results generally support the theoretical claims. The authors have adequately referenced relevant work, including recent NIPS papers on MMD (Gretton et al. [17]) and Wasserstein distance (Arjovsky et al. [3]).

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a practical alternative to MMD for statistical divergence estimation that addresses specific limitations of MMD. The incremental novelty is well-justified by the practical advantages demonstrated in experiments, and the work is well-executed with appropriate theoretical grounding.

In summary, this is a high-quality technical contribution that advances the state of the art in statistical divergence estimation for deep learning. The paper is clearly written, technically sound, and provides convincing evidence for the claimed advantages of PMD over MMD. While the novelty is incremental rather than revolutionary, the practical benefits are significant for practitioners. The work is relevant to NIPS and would be of interest to researchers working on generative models, domain adaptation, and statistical learning.
