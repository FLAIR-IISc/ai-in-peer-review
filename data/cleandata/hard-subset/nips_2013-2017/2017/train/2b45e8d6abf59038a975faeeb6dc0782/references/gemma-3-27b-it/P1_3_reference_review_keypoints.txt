- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric with advantages over MMD.  
- Claims PMD removes kernel bandwidth tuning, improves gradient behavior, and works with standard mini-batch sizes.  
- Provides theoretical justification: PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Empirically validates PMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing better performance and faster convergence than MMD.  
- Positions work within optimal transport and prior divergence estimation literature, differentiating PMD from dual-form Wasserstein estimators.  
- Paper is well-written with clear definitions and helpful pseudocode for parameter learning.  
- Includes insightful empirical analysis of approximation error and gradient behavior supporting the claims.  
- Computational cost of the matching step (Hungarian O(N^3), O(N^2) approximation) is a potential bottleneck for large populations.  
- Uses primarily L1 distance for PMD, which may limit effectiveness for some data types (e.g., images).  
- Experimental scope is limited to standard benchmarks; more diverse or challenging datasets/architectures would strengthen claims.  
- Lacks a direct, detailed comparison with WGANs, including discussion of scale-free vs. scale-dependent properties.  
- Missing ablation study to isolate the individual contributions of bandwidth-free operation, gradient strength, and mini-batch behavior.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretically sound estimator, clear empirical benefits over MMD, practical advantages for practitioners, and strong presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: matching computational cost, limited experimental breadth, and potential suboptimality of L1 distance choice.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept (8.5/10) with high reviewer confidence (9/10).
