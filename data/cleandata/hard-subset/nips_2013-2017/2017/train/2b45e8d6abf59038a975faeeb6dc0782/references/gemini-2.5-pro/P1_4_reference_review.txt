### Summary

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the respective distributions. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. They propose an algorithm to use PMD as a differentiable objective for training deep neural networks, for example in domain adaptation and generative modeling. The key claimed advantages of PMD over the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) are: 1) it is hyperparameter-free (no kernel bandwidth), 2) it provides stronger, more uniform gradients, and 3) it allows for smaller mini-batch sizes during SGD, leading to faster convergence. The authors validate these claims through experiments on domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), showing that PMD outperforms or is competitive with MMD in both performance and convergence speed.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Simplicity:** The central idea of using the cost of a minimum weight bipartite matching as a differentiable loss for deep learning is novel and elegant. It provides a direct, parameter-free method for approximating the Wasserstein-1 distance from samples, contrasting with other approaches like MMD (which requires kernel selection) or Wasserstein GANs (which require training an auxiliary critic network).

2.  **Theoretical Grounding:** The paper clearly establishes the connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric by showing that PMD is the solution to the linear programming formulation of the empirical Wasserstein distance. The argument for strong consistency (Theorem 3.3), though brief, is well-founded on established results in probability theory (Varadarajan's theorem, stability of optimal transport), lending theoretical credibility to the proposed measure.

3.  **Well-Argued Practical Advantages:** The paper makes a compelling case for PMD's advantages over MMD. The "stronger gradient" argument (Section 3.2) is particularly insightful; the saturation of RBF kernels in MMD is a known practical issue, and PMD's use of L1 distance provides non-saturating gradients, which can prevent training from stalling. The proposed optimization scheme, which decouples the population size `N` (for matching) from the SGD mini-batch size `|B|` (for updates), is a clever practical heuristic that directly addresses the slow convergence of MMD when large batch sizes are required.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-chosen and effectively support the paper's claims.
    *   The domain adaptation experiments show clear performance gains or competitive results, and critically, demonstrate PMD's faster convergence and reduced hyperparameter sensitivity (Figure 3).
    *   The generative modeling results are convincing, showing that PMD can produce higher-quality samples than MMD-based GMMNs, particularly when generating directly in pixel space.
    *   The ablation studies in Section 5.3 are excellent, providing valuable insights into the effects of population size, mini-batch size, and the choice of matching algorithm. This analysis strengthens the paper's practical recommendations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scalability of the Matching Algorithm:** The primary weakness is the computational cost of solving the minimum weight matching problem. The paper acknowledges the O(N^3) complexity for the exact Hungarian algorithm and O(N^2) for the approximation, but perhaps understates this as a limitation. While the cost may be acceptable compared to backpropagation for the tested values of `N` (up to 4000), it remains a significant bottleneck that scales poorly compared to linear-time MMD estimators. The suggestion of GPU acceleration is noted, but since it is not implemented, this remains a promise of future work. This computational aspect could limit PMD's applicability in settings requiring very large populations.

2.  **Subtleties of Differentiation:** The paper's argument for differentiability relies on the assumption that the optimal matching `M*` does not change for an infinitesimal change in the parameters `θ`. While this is true almost everywhere, the gradient is technically undefined at points where the optimal matching switches. This is a common issue in objectives involving an `argmin` operator. A more complete treatment would acknowledge this and perhaps frame the gradient as a subgradient. While the practical approach works, the theoretical discussion of the gradient is slightly simplified.

3.  **Choice of Ground Metric `d(·, ·)`:** The paper touts PMD as being "hyperparameter-free," which is a key advantage over MMD's kernel bandwidth. However, this shifts the burden to selecting an appropriate ground metric `d(·, ·)`. The paper uses L1 distance throughout. As acknowledged in Section 5.2, pixel-wise L1 distance is often a poor perceptual metric for images, potentially explaining the blurriness of generated samples. This suggests that the performance of PMD is highly dependent on the choice of `d`, which could be considered a new, important hyperparameter. A more explicit discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Comparison to WGAN:** The paper compares PMD to MMD but provides only a brief discussion of Wasserstein GANs (WGANs). Both PMD and WGAN approximate the Wasserstein-1 distance. A more detailed comparison would be beneficial. PMD approximates the primal form and is parameter-free but computationally expensive in `N`. WGAN approximates the dual form, introduces a parametric critic network, but may be more scalable per iteration. Discussing these complementary approaches would better position PMD in the landscape of Wasserstein-based learning.

### Detailed Comments & Questions

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the method is explained well, and the connection to theory is presented logically. Figure 1 provides an excellent illustration of the learning algorithm.
*   **Quality:** The work is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by both theory and extensive experiments. The ablation studies in particular show a high degree of care in the evaluation.
*   **Originality & Significance:** This work makes a significant contribution by introducing a practical and effective new tool for distribution matching in deep learning. Given the widespread use of MMD and the recent interest in Wasserstein distances, PMD is likely to be adopted and built upon by other researchers. It offers a valuable new point in the trade-off space of divergence estimators.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated, and empirically successful method for comparing distributions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Introduces PMD, a novel and practical method for leveraging the Wasserstein-1 distance in deep learning.
2.  Provides sound theoretical justification, connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric via a consistency proof.
3.  Demonstrates clear and compelling advantages over MMD, a widely used baseline, in terms of hyperparameter tuning, gradient properties, and convergence speed.
4.  Supports claims with a thorough and convincing set of experiments across multiple relevant tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  The computational complexity of the matching algorithm is a potential scalability concern that is not fully resolved.
2.  The paper simplifies the discussion around the differentiability of the objective.
3.  The "hyperparameter-free" claim is debatable, as the choice of the ground metric `d` is crucial and problem-dependent.

Overall, the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. The paper presents a complete and valuable piece of research that advances the state of the art in sample-based distribution comparison for deep learning. It is well-suited for the NIPS audience and is likely to inspire follow-up work.
