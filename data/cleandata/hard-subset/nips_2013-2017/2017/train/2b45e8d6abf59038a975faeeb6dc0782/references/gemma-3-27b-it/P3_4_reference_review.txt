## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric. PMD leverages the minimum weight bipartite matching between samples from two distributions, offering an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors demonstrate theoretical consistency, highlight advantages like the absence of bandwidth tuning, potentially stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batches, and validate these claims through experiments in domain adaptation and generative modeling.  The core idea is compelling – moving away from kernel-based methods to a more direct distance measure based on sample matching.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper establishes a clear connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, demonstrating strong consistency. This theoretical grounding is a major strength.
* **Practical Advantages over MMD:** The identified advantages – no bandwidth parameter, potentially stronger gradients (backed by empirical evidence in Fig. 2b), and the possibility of using smaller mini-batches – address well-known limitations of MMD, making PMD a potentially attractive alternative in practical applications. The empirical results on the MNIST/SVHN/LFW datasets are compelling.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the PMD formulation, algorithm, and theoretical properties.  The mathematical notation is relatively accessible.
* **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments in both domain adaptation and generative modeling provide a good initial assessment of PMD’s performance. The ablation studies (Fig. 5) are also valuable for understanding the impact of key parameters.
* **Insightful Comparison to Related Work:** The paper offers a reasonable comparison to MMD and other relevant approaches (e.g., WGAN), clearly articulating the differences and potential benefits of PMD.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, they downplay its practical impact. Even with approximations, the matching step remains a significant computational bottleneck, especially for larger datasets. The comparisons to MMD in terms of runtime, while acknowledging that MMD’s backpropagation is expensive, could be more detailed and benchmarked on comparable hardware. Specifically, how much *faster* is PMD in practice, particularly considering the cost of the matching algorithm? The claim of "acceptable" runtime for N up to thousands needs more justification.
* **Distance Metric Choice:**  The exclusive use of the L1 distance for PMD is a limitation. While justified in the context of stronger gradients, the L1 distance may not be optimal for all applications, especially image generation (as the authors themselves note with the blurry results). Exploring other distance metrics within the PMD framework could lead to improved performance.
* **Limited Scope of Applications:** The paper focuses on two specific applications. Expanding the experimental evaluation to other areas where sample-based divergence estimation is crucial (e.g., reinforcement learning, adversarial training) would strengthen the claims of PMD’s broader applicability.
* **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The experimental results in the domain adaptation section (Table 1) would be much more convincing with statistical significance testing to confirm that the observed improvements are not due to random chance.
* **Subsampling Gradient Approximation (Eq. 4):** While the idea of using a subsampled mini-batch for gradient estimation is intuitive, the justification for its validity is somewhat weak. More rigorous analysis or empirical validation of the impact of subsampling on gradient accuracy would be beneficial.



**Originality:**

The idea of directly applying optimal transport to sample-based divergence estimation is not entirely novel (as acknowledged with reference to Wasserstein GANs). However, the formulation of PMD as a specifically *solvable* and *differentiable* objective, avoiding the limitations of dual formulations, is a valuable contribution. The analysis of its theoretical properties and comparison to MMD further enhance the originality of the work.

**Significance:**

PMD offers a promising alternative to MMD for sample-based divergence estimation. If the computational bottlenecks can be addressed (perhaps through GPU acceleration of the matching algorithms), PMD has the potential to become a widely adopted technique in various machine learning applications. The insights into gradient behavior and mini-batch size optimization are also significant. 



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and proven consistency.
* Clear advantages over MMD in terms of hyperparameter tuning and gradient behavior.
* Compelling experimental results demonstrating performance gains in domain adaptation and generative modeling.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost of the matching algorithm remains a significant concern.
* Limited exploration of alternative distance metrics.
* Lack of statistical significance testing in some experiments.
* Limited scope of application.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the computational cost is a concern, the theoretical strengths and practical advantages of PMD warrant acceptance. The authors have identified a valuable alternative to existing methods and provided a strong initial evaluation. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed benchmark comparing the runtime of PMD and MMD on comparable hardware.
2.  Add statistical significance testing to the domain adaptation results.
3.  Discuss the potential for exploring other distance metrics within the PMD framework.
4. Expand the discussion of the potential impact of subsampling (Eq. 4)
