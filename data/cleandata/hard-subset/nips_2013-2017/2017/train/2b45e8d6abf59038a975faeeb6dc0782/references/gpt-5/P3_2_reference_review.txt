Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum average pairwise distance under a bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets from distributions pX and pY. PMD equals the 1-Wasserstein distance between the corresponding empirical measures (equal weights) and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the population W1 by invoking Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport. The authors propose using PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep models, arguing that gradients are simple to compute if one treats the optimal matching as fixed almost everywhere, and that one can reuse a matching for multiple SGD steps and even subsample matched pairs (|B| ≪ N) for efficiency. They compare PMD to MMD on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), reporting faster convergence and better or comparable performance, with qualitative improvements in sample quality for PMD.

Strengths
- Clear connection to Wasserstein-1 and a correct equivalence between empirical OT and minimum-cost matching. The consistency statement is correct (albeit known), and the practical training heuristic (outer loop rematching, inner loop SGD with subsampled pairs) is simple and appealing.
- Helpful discussion contrasting gradients of PMD vs MMD; the intuition about RBF kernel saturation and outliers is plausible and supported by a small empirical gradient magnitude analysis.
- The approach is parameter-light (no kernel bandwidth), and the empirical results on Office show competitiveness.
- The experimental analysis includes timing breakdowns and sensitivity to population size and mini-batch for PMD.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty: PMD is exactly the empirical Earth Mover’s Distance (EMD)/Wasserstein-1 on empirical measures. The strong consistency result and the matching formulation are classical. The paper mostly rebrands known concepts and applies them as a loss, which has substantial prior art in OT and deep learning. Important related work is missing, notably: entropic regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) and their widespread use for differentiable, GPU-accelerated OT; OT for domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al., 2014/2017); OT sample complexity/rates (Fournier & Guillin, 2015); stochastic/mini-batch OT (Genevay et al., 2016/2017). These methods directly address speed, differentiability, and stability, and should be discussed and compared.
- Baselines: In generative modeling, comparing only to MMD/GMMN misses the most relevant contemporary baselines (WGAN and its gradient penalty variant, Cramer distance). The claim that WGAN “only computes a relative value up to an unknown scale” is misleading; with proper Lipschitz enforcement, the critic approximates the Kantorovich potential with scale near 1. Without these comparisons, the empirical significance is limited.
- Optimization details: The gradient treatment ignores the dependence of the optimal assignment M* on parameters. While the objective is piecewise smooth and this is acceptable almost everywhere, reusing M* for multiple steps and subsampling matched pairs (Eq. 4) introduces bias that is neither analyzed nor quantified (how often is rematching required? what is the variance/bias trade-off?). A formal subgradient argument or empirical ablation on rematching frequency would strengthen the claims.
- Computational claims: Exact matching is O(N^3) and the approximate O(N^2); computing pairwise distances is O(N^2D). In contrast, Sinkhorn-based OT yields fast, parallelizable computation and differentiable couplings; no comparison is provided. The claim that PMD allows “normal mini-batches” hinges on computing a large-population matching in an outer loop, which can dominate wall-clock for realistic N unless accelerated.
- Evaluation: For generative models, no standard quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID) are reported; qualitative samples are blurry for pixel-space L1 (as acknowledged). If PMD is computed in code space, both PMD and MMD should be compared fairly in the same representation with quantitative metrics. For Office, using 100 labeled target examples for hyperparameter selection deviates from strictly unsupervised DA; although applied equally, it should be explicitly justified and potential biases discussed. Statistical significance (variance across runs) is not presented.
- Some claims overstate differences: MMD can use linear-time unbiased estimators and mixtures/learned kernels (e.g., MMD-GAN) to mitigate saturation; the assertion that mini-batch must equal population is not generally true in practice.

Quality
- The theoretical part is correct but incremental (well-known results combined).
- The optimization heuristic is reasonable but lacks theoretical justification regarding bias/variance and stability when matchings change.
- Experiments provide some insight but lack strong baselines and standard metrics, limiting conclusions.

Clarity
- Generally readable and organized. However:
  - The relationship to existing OT literature should be expanded and more precise (especially Cuturi’s Sinkhorn distances, OT in domain adaptation).
  - The “parameter-free” claim should acknowledge the choice of ground metric d (and data scaling).
  - Clarify the schedule for rematching, the number of inner-loop steps, and the effect on convergence; provide pseudo-code with these details.

Originality
- Using empirical W1 (assignment) as a loss is not new; the main novelty lies in the specific training heuristics (reusing and subsampling matchings) and the empirical comparison to MMD. Given the missing comparisons to entropic OT/WGAN, the novelty is moderate.

Significance
- The idea of leveraging match-based couplings for stronger gradients and smaller inner mini-batches is practically interesting. However, without broader comparisons and stronger evaluations, the impact is uncertain.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include comparisons to Sinkhorn (entropic OT) and WGAN-GP/Cramér GAN on the same datasets with standard metrics (FID/IS).
- Analyze the bias/variance introduced by subsampling matched pairs and by reusing matchings; ablate rematching frequency and |B|.
- Discuss and, if possible, adopt GPU-accelerated matching or Sinkhorn to improve scalability.
- Evaluate PMD in learned feature spaces (e.g., pretrained perceptual features) to address image blurriness and provide fair comparisons with MMD using the same representation.
- Add statistical tests and report variance over multiple runs; clarify the hyperparameter selection protocol in unsupervised DA.
- Cite and discuss missing related work (Cuturi 2013; Courty et al. for DA; Fournier–Guillin rates; Genevay et al.; MMD-GAN, WGAN-GP).

Pros
- Clear formulation; correct link to W1 and consistency.
- Simple, implementable training heuristic; promising empirical behavior vs MMD.
- Parameter-light and yields stronger per-sample gradients.

Cons
- Limited novelty; rebranding of empirical OT.
- Missing key related work and baselines; weak quantitative evaluation.
- Unanalyzed bias from heuristic training and potential scalability issues.

Overall: Interesting practical idea with some promise, but requires stronger positioning relative to OT-based and adversarial baselines and deeper empirical/theoretical support to meet the bar.
