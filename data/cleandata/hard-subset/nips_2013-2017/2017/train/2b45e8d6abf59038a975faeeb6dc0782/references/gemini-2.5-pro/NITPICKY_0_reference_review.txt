**Summary**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric. PMD is calculated as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from two distributions. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the W-1 distance. A learning algorithm is proposed to minimize PMD via stochastic gradient descent (SGD), where the gradient is computed by treating the optimal matching as fixed and can be approximated using a smaller mini-batch of matched pairs. The paper contrasts PMD with the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD has advantages such as being hyperparameter-free (no kernel bandwidth), providing stronger gradients, and allowing for faster convergence with smaller SGD mini-batches. The method is evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, where it is shown to be competitive with or outperform MMD-based approaches.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Intuition:** The core idea of using the minimum weight bipartite matching cost as a differentiable training objective for deep generative models is novel and elegant. It provides a direct, constructive way to approximate the primal form of the Wasserstein-1 distance, contrasting with recent popular methods that approximate its dual form.
2.  **Theoretical Grounding:** The connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is clearly established. Proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method.
3.  **Practical Advantages:** The method obviates the need for kernel selection and bandwidth tuning, which is a notorious practical difficulty with MMD. The proposed scheme of using a large population `N` for matching and a smaller mini-batch `|B|` for SGD updates is a clever practical heuristic that appears effective in experiments.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents a compelling idea, it is undermined by a significant number of technical gaps, methodological oversights, and a lack of polish.

1.  **Insufficient Justification of Gradient Computation:**
    *   The central assumption for differentiability, stated in Section 2.2, is that "the discrete matching M* should not change for infinitesimal change of θ." This statement glosses over the fact that the objective function `min_M ...` is non-differentiable at points where the optimal matching changes. While the set of such points may be of measure zero, this is a critical detail that warrants a more rigorous discussion, perhaps invoking subgradient theory or Danskin's theorem. Without this, the validity of the gradient in Eq. (3) is not fully established.
    *   The approximation in Eq. (4) introduces a biased gradient estimator. The matching `M*` is computed over the entire population `N`, but the gradient is estimated on a small, random subset `B`. The paper provides only a weak heuristic justification that `M*` does not change much. A proper analysis of the bias and variance of this estimator is missing, which is a significant methodological gap. How does the size of `|B|` relative to `N` affect training?

2.  **Inadequate Experimental Evaluation and Baselines:**
    *   The most significant omission is the lack of comparison to Wasserstein GANs (WGAN/WGAN-GP) in the generative modeling experiments. Since PMD directly estimates the W-1 metric, WGANs are the most relevant and powerful state-of-the-art baseline. Comparing only against GMMN (an MMD-based method) is insufficient to demonstrate the significance of the contribution in the context of modern generative models.
    *   The paper claims PMD has "fewer hyper-parameters" than MMD. However, it trades the kernel bandwidth `σ` for the population size `N` and the SGD mini-batch size `|B|`. Figure 5(a) clearly shows that performance is sensitive to `|B|`. The tuning process for `N` and `|B|` is not described for the domain adaptation experiments, making it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison with the carefully tuned MMD.
    *   The generative modeling results are mixed. The authors note that the generated images are "blurry," a common issue with methods that average over modes. This critical trade-off (sharpness vs. mode collapse) is not discussed in sufficient depth.

3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   The theoretical analysis is presented without sufficient detail. The proof of Theorem 3.3 is dismissed as "straightforward" and completely omitted. For a NIPS paper, key theoretical claims should be substantiated with at least a proof sketch or a reference to a full proof in an appendix.
    *   The paper suffers from numerous formatting and grammatical errors that detract from its quality. For example: "MMD have been..." (should be "has"), "Fewer hyper-parameter" (plural), "upon to" (should be "up to"), inconsistent use of "approximated" vs. "approximate," and multiple incomplete references in the bibliography (e.g., [22], [25]). The footer "st Conference..." appears to contain a typo. These issues suggest a lack of careful preparation.
    *   Experimental details, such as the specific network architectures for the generative models, are too sparse for the work to be easily reproduced.

**Decision**

I recommend **rejection**.

The paper introduces a genuinely interesting and promising approach for training deep models by minimizing an estimate of the Wasserstein distance. However, the current manuscript falls short of the standards expected for NIPS. The theoretical underpinnings of the learning algorithm are not rigorously justified, particularly concerning the differentiability and the biased nature of the stochastic gradients. The experimental evaluation is critically incomplete due to the absence of a comparison with WGANs. Finally, the paper is marred by a general lack of polish, including numerous typos, formatting errors, and incomplete references. While the core idea is valuable, the work requires a major revision to address these substantial flaws before it can be considered for publication.
