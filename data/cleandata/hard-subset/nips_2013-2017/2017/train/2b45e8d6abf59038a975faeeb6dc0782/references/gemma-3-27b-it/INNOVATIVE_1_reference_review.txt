## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, and proposes its use as a training objective for deep learning models. The core idea – framing distribution comparison as a minimum weight matching problem – is quite novel and offers a compelling alternative to commonly used metrics like MMD. The authors demonstrate theoretical consistency and empirically validate PMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showcasing performance gains and faster convergence compared to MMD.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The PMD formulation is a genuinely fresh perspective on estimating Wasserstein distance from samples.  It shifts the paradigm away from kernel-based methods and towards a combinatorial optimization approach. This is a strong point, as it addresses several limitations inherent in MMD (bandwidth tuning, gradient saturation).
* **Theoretical Grounding:**  Establishing the strong consistency of PMD with the Wasserstein metric provides a solid theoretical foundation. Demonstrating its connection to optimal transport theory strengthens the paper’s contribution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on domain adaptation and generative modeling are promising. Outperforming MMD in both performance and convergence speed is a significant empirical finding.
* **Practical Advantages:** The claimed advantages of PMD – no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use normal mini-batch sizes – are all highly desirable in practice. The analysis supporting these claims (Fig. 2b) is useful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations of the algorithms are accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the O(N^3) complexity of the exact Hungarian algorithm, the practical implications aren’t fully explored.  The approximation used in the generative modeling experiment seems to mitigate this, but a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost would be beneficial.  The single-threaded C++ implementation is a limitation; GPU acceleration is mentioned but not demonstrated.
* **L1 Distance Choice:**  The exclusive use of the L1 distance feels somewhat arbitrary. While the paper argues for its gradient properties, exploring other distance metrics within the PMD framework could reveal further improvements or limitations. A sensitivity analysis concerning the choice of distance metric would strengthen the paper.
* **Generative Modeling Results:** The images generated by PMD, while less noisy than MMD’s, are still described as blurry. This suggests the L1 distance might not be ideal for image generation.  A more in-depth comparison with state-of-the-art generative models (e.g., WGAN-GP) would provide a better benchmark for PMD’s performance.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks are reasonable starting points, extending the evaluation to other areas where distributional divergence estimation is crucial (e.g., reinforcement learning, anomaly detection) would broaden the impact of the work.
* **Missing Related Work:** The paper mentions WGAN but doesn't fully contextualize PMD within the broader landscape of Wasserstein-based GANs and other recent advances in optimal transport for machine learning.  A more comprehensive review of related work would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the PMD formulation is a significant strength. It offers a potentially valuable alternative to existing methods for estimating distributional divergence.
* The theoretical guarantees and empirical validation provide confidence in the approach.
* The practical advantages (no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients) are compelling.
* The paper is well-written and clear.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational cost of the matching algorithm remains a concern, especially for large-scale applications.
* The limited scope of experiments restricts the generalizability of the findings.
* The choice of the L1 distance might be suboptimal for certain applications.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach. While there are some weaknesses, the originality and potential impact of the work outweigh them. The authors have identified a genuine problem and proposed a creative solution.  I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance, despite the areas for potential improvement. I would lean towards acceptance, even with the current limitations, given the boldness of the idea and the initial positive results. The potential for future work to address the identified weaknesses is significant.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
