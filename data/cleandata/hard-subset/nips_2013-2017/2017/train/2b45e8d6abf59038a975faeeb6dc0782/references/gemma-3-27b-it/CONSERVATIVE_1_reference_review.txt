This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating statistical divergence between distributions, particularly in the context of deep learning. The core idea – minimizing the cost of matching samples between distributions – is intuitively appealing and builds upon the well-established Wasserstein metric. However, while the idea is not *wrong*, the paper falls short of presenting a truly compelling contribution to warrant acceptance at NIPS.

The primary weakness lies in the incremental nature of the work. The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is established, which is good, but the Wasserstein metric itself isn’t new. The claim of advantages over MMD (no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size) are presented, but lack substantial backing. The argument for stronger gradients feels hand-wavy and is only superficially supported by Figure 2(b), which shows *normalized* gradient magnitudes – a potentially misleading visualization. The claimed ability to use smaller mini-batches is a practical benefit, but it’s not a fundamentally novel idea; adaptive batch sizes are common practice. 

The experimental evaluation is also insufficient. The domain adaptation results (Table 1) show improvement on *some* tasks, but not consistently. The claim of faster convergence (Fig. 3(a)) isn’t dramatic, and the parameter sensitivity analysis (Figs. 3(b) & 3(c)) reinforces the point that MMD also requires careful tuning. The generative modeling experiments, while showing some qualitative improvements, are not rigorously quantified and rely on visual inspection. The authors acknowledge the blurry image generation on SVHN and LFW, and attribute it to the L1 distance, but don't explore alternative distance metrics within PMD. The inclusion of an ablation study concerning distance metric would be beneficial. The fact that PMD only excels on the autoencoder space, while MMD struggles to perform is not well explored. Furthermore, the implementation details (serial CPU matching algorithm despite claims of GPU parallelizability) raise concerns about the scalability of this approach. While the mention of GPU acceleration is good, it's currently just a statement without evidence.

The paper also suffers from a lack of comparison to more recent and established methods. The choice to compare only with MMD, a relatively older technique, feels deliberate. A comparison with Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) or other modern distance estimation techniques would have provided a much stronger evaluation of the proposed PMD. The authors attempt to differentiate from WGAN by highlighting PMD’s parameter-free nature, however, that feels like a simplification as WGAN's critic network, while requiring training, allows for gradient estimation without requiring exact matching.

**Strengths:**

*   The idea is conceptually sound and builds on established theoretical foundations (Wasserstein metric).
*   The theoretical analysis demonstrates the consistency of PMD as an estimator.
*   The potential for smaller mini-batch sizes is a practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental novelty – the contribution doesn't represent a significant leap forward.
*   Insufficient experimental validation, lacking quantitative comparison with modern methods (e.g., WGAN).
*   Weak support for the claim of stronger gradients.
*   Limited exploration of alternative distance metrics within the PMD framework.
*   Scalability concerns due to the reliance on a serial CPU-based matching algorithm.
*   Lack of in-depth discussion of limitations and potential failure modes.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper is theoretically sound and clearly presented.
*   It proposes a viable alternative to MMD in certain scenarios.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental and lacks substantial empirical validation.
*   The comparison with existing methods is limited.
*   The scalability concerns are significant.
*   The benefits over MMD aren’t convincingly demonstrated.

**Overall:**

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While technically sound, the paper lacks the novelty and empirical validation necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to provide a more compelling demonstration of the benefits of PMD over existing methods, address the scalability concerns, and conduct a more thorough experimental evaluation. The paper feels more suitable for a workshop or a more specialized conference focused on metric learning.
