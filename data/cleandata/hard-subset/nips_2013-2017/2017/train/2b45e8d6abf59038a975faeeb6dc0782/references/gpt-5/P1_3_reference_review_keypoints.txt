- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. samples; equals empirical W1 for uniform weights and uses a freeze-and-update matching SGD scheme.  
- The consistency theorem is proven but is effectively a standard corollary of optimal transport results (Villani/Varadarajan); theoretical novelty is limited.  
- Fails to cite or compare to entropic OT / Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi 2013) and other scalable differentiable OT methods.  
- Omits strong generative baselines (WGAN-GP), other distances (energy/Cramér, sliced Wasserstein), and quantitative generative metrics (FID/Inception/likelihood).  
- Domain adaptation evaluation compares only to MMD and not to stronger DA baselines (DANN, JAN, CORAL, CMD) despite citing them.  
- Strength: simple, well-motivated training recipe (freeze-and-update matching; decouple population/minibatch) with correct gradient expression almost everywhere.  
- Empirical results show PMD yields stronger gradients, benefits from smaller minibatches, informative approximation-error vs N study, and faster convergence than MMD on some tasks.  
- In generative modeling PMD produces qualitatively cleaner pixel-space samples than MMD and is competitive in AE code space, but samples can be blurry.  
- Key weakness: PMD is essentially empirical W1/EMD—main contribution is a training heuristic and empirical comparisons, not a new discrepancy or deep theory.  
- Missing baselines and citations undermine claims of practicality and competitiveness, especially no Sinkhorn speed/accuracy comparison.  
- Generative evaluation is largely qualitative with no FID/Inception/likelihood; datasets are small and chosen metric (L1 pixel space) is suboptimal.  
- Computational claims are questionable: exact Hungarian is O(N^3) and slow, approximate O(N^2) is still costly, and GPU-accelerated comparisons to Sinkhorn are not performed.  
- Optimization concerns: claim of “unbiased stochastic gradient” is not justified; subsampling after computing a population matching and reusing matchings introduces bias and lacks convergence analysis.  
- “Parameter-free” claim is overstated because ground metric and representation space are crucial hyperparameters affecting performance.  
- Clarity: generally clear with pseudocode and figures, but needs Danskin/envelope theorem citation, clarification of subsampling/frozen-matching bias, notational fixes, and full reproducibility details.  
- Originality: moderate — using empirical W1 as objective is not new; novelty lies primarily in the SGD scheme and empirical MMD comparison.  
- Significance: potentially useful as a simple alternative to MMD, but impact is limited without comparisons to modern OT solvers and stronger baselines and without quantitative metrics.  
- Pros: simple intuitive objective matching empirical Wasserstein-1, clear training recipe, empirical gradient and convergence advantages, and standard consistency results.  
- Cons: limited novelty, missing key related work and baselines, lack of quantitative generative evaluation, no convergence analysis for biased training heuristic, and unbenchmarked computational claims.  
- Recommendations for authors: compare to Sinkhorn (speed/quality), add WGAN-GP/energy baselines and report FID/Inception, include DA baselines (DANN/JAN/CMD/CORAL), justify ignoring ∂M*/∂θ via Danskin/envelope theorem, analyze subsampling/freeze bias and convergence, explore learned/perceptual metrics, and discuss sample-complexity/dimension dependence.  
- Overall recommendation: Borderline reject — clean and practical empirical W1 usage with nice properties vs MMD, but limited conceptual novelty and incomplete experimental evaluation for a top-tier venue.
