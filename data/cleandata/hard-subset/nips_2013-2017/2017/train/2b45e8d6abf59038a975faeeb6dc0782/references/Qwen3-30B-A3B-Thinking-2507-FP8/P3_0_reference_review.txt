

# Peer Review: "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence defined as the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sample populations. The authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and demonstrate advantages over MMD including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements. The work builds on established connections between optimal transport and statistical divergences (e.g., Wasserstein metric) and addresses limitations of MMD identified in prior work (e.g., [3, 17, 31]).

While the theoretical connection to Wasserstein distance is correct (Theorem 3.3), this relationship is not novel—optimal transport theory has long established the connection between empirical Wasserstein distance and minimum weight matching (see [49, 50]). The paper's main contribution is the practical application of this concept to deep learning tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical foundation**: The authors correctly establish PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric through a clear application of Varadarajan's theorem and stability results for optimal transport.

2. **Practical advantages**: The empirical demonstration that PMD can use smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs. MMD's typical 1000) is valuable and addresses a real pain point in deep learning. The gradient analysis (Fig. 2b) convincingly shows PMD's gradients are more uniformly distributed than MMD's.

3. **Clear experimental comparisons**: The domain adaptation experiments (Table 1, Fig. 3) demonstrate clear performance advantages for PMD on specific tasks (a→w, a→d), with faster convergence (Fig. 3a).

## Weaknesses and Limitations

1. **Theoretical gap**: The paper claims PMD is a "strongly consistent estimator" but fails to establish the rate of convergence. The empirical analysis in Fig. 2a shows a slow convergence rate (N^{-0.23}), much slower than the typical N^{-1/2} rate for many statistical estimators. This weakens the theoretical contribution.

2. **Unfair MMD comparison**: The generative modeling experiments use a fixed bandwidth for MMD (σ=1) for the autoencoder architecture, while the paper acknowledges MMD's performance is highly sensitive to bandwidth (Fig. 3b). The authors do not demonstrate PMD's superiority when MMD is optimally tuned across bandwidths.

3. **Generative modeling results**: The PMD-generated images on SVHN and LFW (Fig. 4) are blurry and inferior to MMD's results, contradicting the paper's claim of "better performance." The authors acknowledge this but don't adequately address why PMD fails on natural images—especially since pixel-level L1 distance is known to be suboptimal for image generation.

4. **Computational complexity**: The claim that "O(N³) time complexity is acceptable" is misleading. Figure 5c shows the Hungarian algorithm takes comparable time to SGD, making PMD computationally expensive for large N. The paper doesn't compare with more efficient Wasserstein approximations like Sinkhorn [14] or WGAN-GP [3].

5. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper fails to engage with recent advances in Wasserstein-based methods (WGAN-GP, [3, 14, 45]), which have become standard for generative modeling. The comparison is limited to MMD, ignoring more relevant baselines.

## Significance and Originality

The paper's core idea—using minimum weight matching as a distance estimator—is not novel (it's a standard approach in optimal transport). While the application to deep learning is interesting, the work doesn't significantly advance the state-of-the-art. The advantages claimed over MMD are real but modest, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how PMD would be superior to established Wasserstein approximations in more challenging scenarios.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a modest contribution to the literature on statistical divergences for deep learning. While the theoretical connection is sound, the practical advantages are limited to specific tasks (domain adaptation) and are not sufficiently supported by comprehensive experiments. The generative modeling results are underwhelming, and the computational costs are not adequately addressed.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject**

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Comparing with WGAN-GP and other modern Wasserstein approximations
2. Providing a thorough analysis of PMD's performance when MMD is optimally tuned
3. Addressing the blurry image generation issue with better distance metrics or architectures
4. Establishing a convergence rate for PMD

Without these improvements, the paper's contribution is incremental and doesn't advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS. The current version is better suited for a more application-focused venue rather than a top-tier theoretical machine learning conference.
