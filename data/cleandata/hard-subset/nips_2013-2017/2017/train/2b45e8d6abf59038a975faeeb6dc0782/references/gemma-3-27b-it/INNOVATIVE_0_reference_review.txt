This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, positioning it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The core idea – framing the distance estimation as a minimum weight bipartite matching problem – is surprisingly simple yet potentially impactful. The authors argue PMD offers advantages over MMD including the absence of a bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use standard mini-batch sizes. The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is established, and empirical evaluation on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks demonstrates promising results.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The PMD approach is a fresh perspective on sample-based Wasserstein estimation. While the Wasserstein metric itself isn't new, framing it as a matching problem and demonstrating its advantages over established methods like MMD is a significant contribution. The elimination of the bandwidth parameter is a key benefit, reducing hyperparameter tuning complexity.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper clearly establishes the theoretical consistency of PMD as an estimator for the Wasserstein metric, grounding the empirical observations in solid mathematical principles.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling, while not extensive, provide initial evidence supporting the claims of improved performance and convergence speed. The ablation study regarding population size and mini-batch size (Fig 5) is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and the rationale behind the proposed approach. The pseudocode and graphical illustration are helpful.
* **Gradient Analysis:** The analysis of gradient magnitudes (Fig. 2b) provides a compelling argument for PMD’s improved training dynamics.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation, while promising, is somewhat limited. The comparison with MMD is conducted on only two tasks. Expanding the evaluation to more diverse datasets and tasks would strengthen the claims.
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The use of L1 distance (Manhattan distance) in the PMD formulation might be a limiting factor. While it contributes to stronger gradients, it may not be optimal for all data modalities (e.g., images where Euclidean distance might be more appropriate).  The discussion on this trade-off is brief.
* **Matching Algorithm Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, a more detailed discussion of the practical implications for large-scale datasets would be helpful. The reliance on the approximation algorithm, while necessary, introduces potential inaccuracies.
* **Lack of Comparison with other Wasserstein estimators:** The paper primarily focuses on comparing PMD with MMD. A comparison with other existing sample-based Wasserstein estimators (e.g. those based on the dual problem) would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.
* **Generative Modeling Limitations:** The generated images on SVHN and LFW are described as blurry. While the authors acknowledge this, a deeper analysis of the causes and potential solutions would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is novel and addresses a significant challenge in sample-based divergence estimation.
* The theoretical analysis provides a strong foundation for the proposed approach.
* The empirical results, though limited, are encouraging and suggest PMD has the potential to outperform existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the methodology.
*  The identified advantages – no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients – are important practical benefits.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to fully validate the claims.
* The choice of L1 distance may limit the applicability of PMD to certain data modalities.
*  The potential computational cost of the matching algorithm for large datasets needs further consideration.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and promising approach to sample-based Wasserstein estimation. While the experimental evaluation could be more extensive and the limitations of the L1 distance and matching algorithm complexity should be addressed, the core idea is strong and the theoretical analysis is solid. The potential benefits of PMD – particularly the elimination of the bandwidth parameter and improved gradient behavior – are significant. I believe the novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh the current limitations. 

**Recommendation:** Accept. I would encourage the authors to expand the experimental evaluation and address the identified weaknesses in a future version of the work.
