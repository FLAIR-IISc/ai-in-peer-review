Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets X and Y of equal size N, with cost d(·,·). PMD is exactly the empirical optimal transport (OT) problem for uniform empirical measures, i.e., the linear assignment formulation of the first Wasserstein distance (W1). The authors propose to use PMD as a training objective for deep generative modeling and domain adaptation, argue that it has advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) (no bandwidth, stronger per-sample gradients, smaller mini-batches for updates), and present experiments on Office (domain adaptation) and image generation (MNIST, SVHN, LFW). They show: (1) a standard consistency result stating that the empirical OT plans and costs converge to the population W1; (2) a practical stochastic optimization scheme where matchings are recomputed intermittently and gradients do not propagate through the discrete matching; and (3) runtime measurements using exact (Hungarian, O(N^3)) and approximate (O(N^2)) matching.

Relation to prior work: PMD is the empirical W1/EMD between samples (Rubner et al. 2000; Villani 2009). Learning with OT-type losses in deep models has notable precedents: entropic regularization and Sinkhorn distances for fast, differentiable OT (Cuturi 2013; Genevay et al. 2016), and “Learning with a Wasserstein Loss” (Frogner et al., NIPS 2015), which backpropagate through a smoothed transport plan. For implicit generative modeling, WGAN (Arjovsky et al. 2017) and follow-ups (e.g., gradient penalty) approximate W1 via the dual. For domain adaptation, strong baselines include DANN (Ganin et al.), CORAL, CMD, and more recent Wasserstein/domain-discrepancy methods. The paper positions PMD mainly against MMD and WGAN, with limited engagement with Sinkhorn-based OT or prior deep learning with OT losses.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity: PMD is a straightforward, parameter-free empirical estimator of W1 for equal-size sample sets and can be optimized with standard SGD without an auxiliary critic.
- Theoretical grounding: the connection to OT is correct; empirical OT is a well-defined metric and converges to population W1 under standard conditions. The equivalence to the assignment problem is accurate for uniform empirical measures.
- Practical insights: a simple training loop that freezes the matching for multiple SGD steps; empirical observation that small update batches suffice once a matching is computed; and measurements comparing exact vs approximate matchings.
- Empirical indications that PMD can yield competitive or better performance than MMD on Office in some transfer directions and produces cleaner samples than MMD in some settings, with faster convergence.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: PMD is the empirical W1/EMD. Using empirical OT as a deep learning loss has close precedents (Sinkhorn OT; Frogner et al. 2015). The consistency result and the assignment formulation are standard; presenting them as a main theoretical contribution without new analysis (e.g., finite-sample rates, variance reduction, or convergence of the proposed stochastic scheme) weakens originality.
- Missing related work: no discussion or comparison to entropic-regularized OT (Cuturi 2013) and differentiable Sinkhorn approximations widely used to scale OT and enable backpropagation, nor to stochastic OT for large-scale problems. These are highly relevant for both computational and methodological positioning.
- Theoretical gaps: The “strong consistency” theorem omits standard moment/tightness assumptions required for W1 (finite first moments) and plan convergence. The statement that gradients “do not propagate through the matching” is informal; the objective is nonsmooth and piecewise constant in the matching, so the method uses a subgradient-type estimator without analysis. The mini-batch subsampling after fixing a matching yields a biased gradient for the original objective; no convergence guarantees are provided.
- Experimental scope and baselines: Comparisons are limited to MMD. For domain adaptation, stronger baselines (DANN, CMD, CORAL) are absent. For generative modeling, comparisons to WGAN(-GP), Sinkhorn-based OT, and standard GANs are missing. Quantitative metrics (FID, IS) are not reported; evaluation relies mostly on qualitative inspection and internal losses (PMD/MMD), which are not comparable across methods.
- Computational considerations: OT by matching scales as O(N^2)–O(N^3) per population; while small N is feasible, scalability remains a concern. The paper mentions GPU-parallel matching but does not implement or compare to Sinkhorn, which is the established approach for scalable, differentiable OT.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Some notation/typos and incomplete references; key assumptions and algorithmic details (e.g., approximate matching algorithm parameters) are sparse. The fairness of using different spaces (pixel vs AE code) across datasets is not fully controlled.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Core definitions and the linkage to OT are correct. However, claims about unbiased gradients and stability of matchings across updates lack formal justification. The consistency theorem needs proper assumptions. No analysis of the bias introduced by fixed-match subsampling or of convergence properties of the proposed training scheme is provided.
- Empirical support: Evidence is indicative but incomplete due to limited baselines and lack of standard quantitative metrics.

Clarity
- Generally readable and organized. Some sections would benefit from explicit assumptions, more precise statements, and a clearer description of the optimization procedure and its limitations. Figures referenced but not fully visible here limit assessment of presentation quality.

Originality
- Low to moderate. The paper repackages empirical W1/EMD as PMD and applies it to deep training with a pragmatic optimization loop. Similar ideas exist in the literature; missing discussion of closely related OT-based deep methods reduces the novelty claim.

Significance
- Moderate potential if positioned and evaluated against the right baselines. As written, the impact is constrained by missing comparisons and theoretical analysis. The practical takeaways (no kernel bandwidth, workable gradients, small update batches) are useful but incremental.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, well-motivated objective grounded in OT.
- Practical algorithm and engineering demonstrating feasibility.
- Some empirical gains and faster convergence versus MMD.
- Clear articulation of advantages over kernel MMD.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; core idea is standard empirical OT/EMD.
- Missing key related work (Sinkhorn OT) and baselines (DANN, WGAN-GP).
- Theoretical statements incomplete; no analysis of the proposed stochastic optimization’s convergence/bias.
- Evaluation lacks standard quantitative metrics and larger-scale benchmarks.
- Scalability concerns not convincingly addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate PMD within the OT literature: discuss and compare to entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn and stochastic OT; consider using Sinkhorn for better scalability and differentiability.
- Add formal assumptions to the consistency theorem; provide subgradient analysis for the “no backprop through matching” choice; analyze the bias and convergence of the fixed-matching mini-batch updates.
- Expand baselines: DANN/CMD/CORAL for domain adaptation; WGAN(-GP), Sinkhorn-based GANs/WAEs for generation. Report FID/IS or task-appropriate quantitative metrics.
- Evaluate scalability on larger datasets and batch sizes; include GPU implementations or Sinkhorn comparisons.
- Study sensitivity to the ground cost (L1 vs perceptual/feature costs) and consider unbalanced OT for unequal batch sizes.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is sensible and practically useful, but the novelty over known empirical OT is limited, theoretical claims are incomplete, and experimental comparisons miss key baselines and metrics. Strengthening positioning, theory, and evaluation would substantially improve the contribution.
