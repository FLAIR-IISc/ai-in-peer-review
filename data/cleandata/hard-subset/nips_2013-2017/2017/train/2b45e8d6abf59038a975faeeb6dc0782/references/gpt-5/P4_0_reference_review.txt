Summary
The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching (assignment) between two i.i.d. sample sets drawn from distributions pX and pY using a ground metric d (e.g., L1). PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (Earth Mover’s Distance) between the corresponding empirical measures with uniform weights. The authors (i) propose to use PMD as a training objective for parameterized distributions, treating the matching as fixed during small parameter updates so gradients do not backpropagate through the discrete matching, and optionally reusing a matching while taking several SGD steps with smaller mini-batches; (ii) show PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the true W1 via Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport; (iii) compare PMD with MMD, arguing PMD has no bandwidth hyperparameter, provides stronger gradients, and permits smaller mini-batches than the population size; and (iv) apply PMD to unsupervised domain adaptation (as a feature-alignment regularizer) and sample-based generative modeling (training implicit models) on Office, MNIST, SVHN, and LFW. Empirically, PMD reportedly outperforms or matches MMD and converges faster; approximation error decreases with sample size; and approximate O(N^2) matching is competitive with exact Hungarian matching.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The connection between PMD and the empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD is correct, and strong consistency follows from standard OT results. However, details are light:
  - The non-differentiability issue is treated informally (“matching does not change for infinitesimal change”). A formal justification via Danskin’s theorem/subgradient calculus and a statement that the objective is differentiable almost everywhere (plus how gradients are handled at non-differentiable points) would strengthen the method section.
  - The proposed “fixed matching over several updates” and “subsampling matched pairs |B| < N for gradients” are heuristic. This induces a biased gradient unless the subset is sampled uniformly over the matched pairs, and even then the bias due to a stale matching is unquantified. No analysis of stability or bias-variance is provided.
  - Assumptions for consistency (e.g., finite first moment of pX, pY) are not explicitly stated; some statements restrict to “real random variables” but earlier propositions are phrased for Polish spaces.
- Experimental evaluation: Results suggest PMD works well compared to MMD on selected tasks; convergence and gradient magnitude analyses are informative. However:
  - Comparisons are limited. For generative modeling, stronger baselines (WGAN/WGAN-GP, Cramér distance, sliced Wasserstein, Sinkhorn divergence) are absent. For domain adaptation, methods like DANN or CMD are not compared. The claim that MMD requires large mini-batches is overstated; unbiased/low-variance linear-time MMD estimators and block estimators exist.
  - Quantitative metrics for generative modeling (e.g., FID/IS) are missing; only visual inspection is provided, and generated images are noted as blurry in pixel space.
  - Statistical significance, multiple seeds, and standard deviations are largely absent.
  - Computational cost: Matching is O(N^3) exact or O(N^2) approximate with O(N^2D) distance computation; while acceptable for the presented scales, an apples-to-apples runtime comparison vs modern alternatives (e.g., Sinkhorn with GPU) is missing.

Clarity
- Generally well-written with clear intuition and algorithmic description. Pseudocode and figures aid understanding.
- Some notational typos (indices in empirical measures), and a reference mismatch (“Proposition 5.4 in [6]” appears not to correspond to the listed [6]). Please check all citation indices.
- The “gradient does not propagate through matching” argument should cite Danskin and clarify subgradient usage.
- Explicitly state assumptions (metric continuity, lower bound, finite first moment) for Theorem 3.3.
- Be precise about when mini-batch |B| can be smaller than N and whether the resulting gradient is unbiased.

Originality
- The core idea—using the empirical 1-Wasserstein (assignment cost) as a sample-based divergence—is well-known in OT and has seen prior use in ML (EMD in vision; empirical OT distances; Cuturi’s Sinkhorn distances for fast, differentiable OT). The theoretical result (consistency) is standard. The contribution is mainly an engineering/application package: positioning EMD as “PMD,” advocating fixed-matching SGD and subsampled updates, and empirically contrasting with MMD. Novelty is therefore moderate.
- Important related work is missing or under-discussed: entropic regularization and Sinkhorn divergence (Cuturi 2013 and subsequent works), convergence rates for empirical OT (e.g., Fournier & Guillin 2015), sliced Wasserstein distances, WGAN-GP, and MMD-GAN. Some are cited (WGAN, Cramér) but not compared.

Significance
- As a practical alternative to MMD with favorable gradients and no kernel tuning, PMD may be useful, and the empirical results on Office and small-scale generation are encouraging. However, without stronger baselines and broader evaluation, it is difficult to conclude clear state-of-the-art impact.
- Computational scalability remains a concern for larger N or higher-dimensional data unless GPU-accelerated matching or Sinkhorn-like methods are used.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, conceptually clear objective tightly connected to Wasserstein distance.
- Solid (albeit standard) consistency justification.
- Practical training routine; empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence than MMD on selected tasks.
- Applicable to multiple problems (domain adaptation, generative modeling).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to established empirical OT/EMD; renaming to PMD may obscure prior art.
- Missing comparisons to key OT-based and adversarial baselines (Sinkhorn divergence, WGAN-GP, sliced Wasserstein, CMD/DANN).
- Methodological gaps: lack of formal treatment of non-differentiability; potential bias from fixed matching and subsampling; overstatements about MMD mini-batch requirements.
- Evaluation scope and metrics are limited; no quantitative generative metrics; small-scale experiments.
- Citation and notation issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add formal gradient justification (Danskin/subgradients) and state assumptions for Theorem 3.3; discuss bias/variance of the subsampled, stale-matching gradient.
- Compare against Sinkhorn-based OT (and/or Sinkhorn divergence) with GPU acceleration, WGAN-GP, Cramér distance, sliced Wasserstein, and MMD-GAN.
- Provide quantitative generative metrics (FID/IS), more datasets, and robustness across seeds.
- Discuss and, if possible, provide convergence rates or high-probability error bounds; at least cite known results.
- Fix references/notation and moderate claims about MMD mini-batch size.

Overall assessment
A clear and useful empirical application of the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance as a training objective, with promising results versus MMD. However, the contribution is incremental relative to known OT machinery, and the experimental and theoretical gaps reduce confidence. With stronger comparisons and a more rigorous treatment of optimization details, the work could be impactful.
