- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein metric via minimum-weight matching between sample sets.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric using Varadarajan’s theorem and optimal transport stability.  
- PMD has practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and effective use of much smaller mini-batches (100 vs. 1000).  
- Empirical results show faster convergence, reduced hyperparameter sensitivity, and improved domain adaptation performance compared to MMD.  
- Applied PMD to generative modeling and domain adaptation, demonstrating consistent performance improvements in tasks evaluated.  
- Theoretical comparison to MMD highlights PMD’s stronger gradients and lower sensitivity to outliers.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized with effective figures and helpful pseudocode.  
- Lacks finite-sample error bounds; only asymptotic strong consistency is provided.  
- Generative modeling produces blurry images on natural datasets (SVHN, LFW) when using L1 distance, and alternative metrics or code-space learning are not explored.  
- Computational complexity concerns: exact Hungarian algorithm is O(N^3), approximate methods (O(N^2)) are mentioned but large-scale trade-offs aren’t thoroughly analyzed.  
- Experiments use relatively small N (e.g., 2000), raising questions about scalability to modern, million-sample datasets.  
- Overall judgment: strong, novel, and practically useful contribution with acknowledged minor theoretical and empirical limitations.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
