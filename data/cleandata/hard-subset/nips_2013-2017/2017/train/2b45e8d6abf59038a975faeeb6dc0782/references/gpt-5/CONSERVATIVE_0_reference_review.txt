Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two equal-size samples drawn from two distributions, using a chosen ground metric (e.g., L1). PMD is thus the empirical first Wasserstein distance (a.k.a. Earth Mover’s Distance) between the empirical measures. The authors (i) restate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 by invoking weak convergence of empirical measures and stability of optimal transport; (ii) propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep models, arguing the matching is piecewise constant and gradients need not backpropagate through the combinatorial assignment; and (iii) empirically compare PMD to MMD on Office domain adaptation and on implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), claiming PMD has stronger gradients, fewer hyperparameters, works with smaller mini-batches, and converges faster.

Relation to prior work
- PMD is the standard empirical Wasserstein-1/EMD with equal weights. Its consistency is well-known from classic OT theory.
- For generative modeling with Wasserstein distances, WGAN (2017) uses the dual form with a 1-Lipschitz critic; there are also alternatives such as Cramér distance. The paper cites WGAN but does not compare empirically.
- For domain adaptation, optimal transport–based methods (e.g., Courty et al., 2016/2017; JDOT) explicitly align source and target via OT/EMD; these closely relate to PMD but are not cited or compared.
- MMD-based training (GMMN) is an appropriate baseline; however, other competitive baselines (GANs, VAEs, DANN, JAN, CORAL/CMD) are absent.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive objective grounded in optimal transport; no kernel bandwidth selection as in RBF-MMD.
- Clear exposition connecting PMD to W1 via the empirical OT program; the consistency argument is correct (though standard).
- Practical training recipe (compute matching on larger “population,” update parameters with smaller mini-batches, optionally reuse the matching for several steps) is easy to implement.
- Some empirical indications that PMD provides less vanishing gradients for outliers than RBF-MMD and can train with smaller mini-batches.
- Experiments on Office and small-scale generative modeling show PMD is at least competitive with, sometimes better than, MMD and converges faster in their setup.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Novelty is limited: PMD is exactly empirical W1/EMD with equal weights; the “strong consistency” theorem rephrases standard results (Villani; Varadarajan). No new theory (e.g., finite-sample rates, generalization bounds, stability of the stale-matching SGD) is provided.
- The proposed gradient estimator ignores the dependence of the matching on parameters and reuses stale matchings across steps. This can introduce bias; no analysis or convergence guarantees are given. The “does not change for infinitesimal change” argument is insufficient for a multi-step SGD scheme.
- The claim that PMD is “parameter-free” is overstated: the ground cost (L1 vs L2, feature space choice, scaling) is a consequential hyperparameter. In practice, results suggest pixel-space L1 yields blurry images.
- Comparisons are narrow. For domain adaptation, only MMD is considered, despite strong established baselines (DANN, JAN, CORAL/CMD, OT-based DA). For generative modeling, results are compared only to MMD/GMMN; no comparison to WGAN/DCGAN/VAEs or quantitative metrics (IS/FID) is provided.
- Computational overhead is significant: O(N^2) distance plus O(N^2–N^3) matching per population. The authors rely on CPU matching and argue potential GPU acceleration but provide no large-scale evidence. This is a critical practical limitation relative to critic-based methods.
- Some claims about MMD seem misleading: one can train with small mini-batches using unbiased or linear-time MMD estimators; MMD training does not intrinsically require N=population size.
- The domain adaptation protocol uses labeled target samples for validation, which is common but should be discussed; absolute performance vs state-of-the-art is unclear.

Clarity
- Overall writing is understandable, but several points need precision: the status of gradient unbiasedness; when and how often to recompute matchings; sensitivity to the ground metric; complexity in high dimensions.
- Important related work on OT for domain adaptation is missing and should be cited and discussed.
- Figures and tables referenced (e.g., Table 1, Fig. 4) are not fully included here; quantitative margins and standard deviations are not visible.

Originality
- The core idea (use empirical Wasserstein/EMD as a loss) and its consistency are well-established. The main novelty is an engineering choice to reuse matchings and sub-sample matched pairs within SGD; this is incremental.

Significance
- As presented, the contribution is unlikely to shift practice: the method underperforms or is incomparable to established adversarial/OT-based baselines on generative modeling and domain adaptation, and the computational cost inhibits scaling. Without stronger empirical evidence against established methods and/or new theory (rates, guarantees for the SGD-with-stale-matchings scheme), impact is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide finite-sample error rates and/or convergence analysis for the proposed training scheme (including the effect of stale matchings and sub-sampling).
- Compare against strong baselines: WGAN/LSGAN/DCGAN/VAEs for generation (with IS/FID), and DANN, JAN, CORAL/CMD, and OT-based DA (Courty et al.) for domain adaptation.
- Explore and report sensitivity to the ground metric and feature space (e.g., learned feature embeddings vs pixel space).
- Clarify MMD baseline: use linear-time/unbiased estimators and tuned multi-kernel bandwidths; ensure fair computation budgets.
- Address computational scalability with actual GPU-accelerated matching and larger datasets.
- Correct and expand related work to include OT in DA and rates for empirical Wasserstein distances (e.g., Fournier & Guillin, 2015).

Pros
- Simple, theoretically grounded objective; easy to implement.
- Avoids kernel bandwidth tuning; sometimes faster convergence than MMD.
- Clear connection to OT; consistent estimator.

Cons
- Largely rebranding of empirical Wasserstein/EMD; limited novelty.
- No theoretical guarantees for the proposed SGD scheme; potential bias.
- Narrow and incomplete empirical comparisons; missing strong baselines and quantitative metrics.
- Computationally heavy; scalability not demonstrated.

Recommendation: Reject. The idea is intuitive but not sufficiently novel or validated against established methods, and key theoretical and empirical gaps remain.
