Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two sets of i.i.d. samples, as a sample-based estimate of the Wasserstein-1 distance. PMD is exactly the empirical optimal transport (OT) distance between two uniform empirical measures; the paper shows (via Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of OT) that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1. The authors present a practical SGD recipe that (i) computes matchings on moderate “population” batches, (ii) treats the assignment as fixed when differentiating (not backpropagating through the discrete argmin), and (iii) allows using a smaller SGD mini-batch than the population by subsampling matched pairs. They advocate PMD over MMD, arguing it has no kernel bandwidth, yields stronger gradients, and supports smaller mini-batches. Applications to unsupervised domain adaptation (Office dataset) and implicit deep generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW) show PMD is competitive or better than MMD and converges faster. Additional analyses include approximation error vs population size, gradient magnitude distributions, and timing comparisons between exact Hungarian and approximate matching.

Quality
- Theory: The consistency of empirical OT/W1 is correct, but essentially standard OT theory (e.g., Villani). The equivalence between problem (6) and minimum matching with uniform marginals is well-known. The claim that the resulting gradient is “unbiased” when not differentiating through the assignment is not justified; at best, one obtains a valid subgradient almost everywhere, and the subsampled variant is a biased estimator of the full objective. No convergence guarantees are given for the alternating scheme that reuses assignments across several SGD steps.
- Algorithms: Using approximate O(N^2) matching plus reuse of assignments is a reasonable heuristic and is simple to implement. However, the computational tradeoffs vs modern differentiable OT approximations (e.g., entropic regularization/Sinkhorn) are not evaluated.
- Empirics: The domain adaptation experiments suggest gains on some Office shifts; convergence plots and parameter sensitivity are useful. The generative modeling results are suggestive but limited (small datasets; no quantitative metrics like FID/Inception score; pixel-space L1 leading to blur). Comparisons are restricted to MMD; stronger baselines (WGAN, Sinkhorn-based losses, sliced Wasserstein) are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well structured. The training algorithm and approximations are explained, and the figures/plots help. Some claims need more careful wording (e.g., “unbiased gradient” in Eq. (3); “MMD must use mini-batch size equal to the population” is incorrect—linear-time/unbiased estimators for MMD exist). Several important related works are not cited (see below), making it harder for readers to place the contribution.

Originality
The core objective (empirical W1 over equal-weight samples, i.e., Earth Mover’s Distance via matching) is known. The consistency result is standard. The paper’s novelty lies in the specific training heuristic (reuse of assignments; subsampling matched pairs) and the empirical observation that this leads to stronger per-sample gradients and allows smaller SGD batches. This is incremental relative to the OT literature in ML.

Relation to prior work
- Differentiable OT/fast computation: Entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn distances; Cuturi 2013) and subsequent stochastic/automatic differentiation work (e.g., Genevay et al.) provide scalable, differentiable approximations of W1 and have been used in deep learning. Not cited or compared.
- Sliced Wasserstein distances (Kolouri et al., 2017) give cheap and differentiable W1 approximations and have been used for generative modeling. Not cited.
- WGAN (cited) is a core baseline; no experimental comparison is provided.
- Domain adaptation with OT (e.g., Courty et al., 2016) is highly relevant; not cited.
- MMD linear/unbiased estimators enable smaller batches; the claim that MMD requires population-size batches is inaccurate.

Significance
The paper promotes a simple, direct, primal W1 loss via matching for deep learning applications, with empirical evidence of advantages over MMD in certain settings. However, given existing OT-based alternatives that are scalable, differentiable, and widely used, the incremental nature and incomplete comparison reduce the overall significance. Still, the finding that reusing assignments enables effective training with smaller mini-batches is practically interesting.

Strengths
- Clear, simple objective grounded in OT; consistency stated explicitly.
- Practical training heuristic; empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence than MMD.
- Useful analyses (error vs N, gradient magnitude histograms, timing; exact vs approximate matching).
- Competitive domain adaptation results on Office; clear hyperparameter sensitivity study.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: PMD is empirical W1/EMD; theory is standard.
- Incomplete and sometimes inaccurate related work and comparisons (no Sinkhorn/sliced-W/WGAN/OT-DA; mischaracterization of MMD minibatching).
- The “unbiased gradient” claim is questionable; no convergence theory for the alternating scheme; no discussion of nondifferentiability issues at assignment changes.
- Known sample complexity issues for W1 in high dimensions are not discussed; only small datasets considered.
- Generative modeling evaluation lacks quantitative metrics and stronger baselines; images are blurry in pixel space, undermining claims unless using code space.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare against Sinkhorn-regularized OT (with backprop), sliced Wasserstein, WGAN/Cramér distance, and OT-based domain adaptation methods; include quantitative generative metrics (FID/IS).
- Clarify the gradient story: discuss subgradients, points of nondifferentiability, and the bias introduced by assignment reuse and subsampling; provide any empirical ablation on how often to recompute assignments and its effect.
- Acknowledge and correct the statement about MMD mini-batches; include linear-time MMD baselines for speed/fairness.
- Discuss sample complexity of W1 in high dimensions; consider feature-space distances or learned metrics to mitigate blur.
- Broaden DA baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD) and report results with end-to-end fine-tuning, not only frozen VGG features.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, practical approach that outperforms/accelerates MMD in two applications.
- Clear exposition and useful empirical analyses.
- Potentially useful heuristic (assignment reuse with small mini-batches) for practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance
- Core idea is a rebranding of empirical OT/EMD; theory is not novel.
- Missing key related work and baselines; some claims are inaccurate.
- Limited empirical scope and metrics; no comparison to dominant OT-based deep methods.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper has practical merit and could be impactful with stronger baselines, corrected claims, and a fuller positioning within the OT literature.
