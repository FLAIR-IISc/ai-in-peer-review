Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two equal-sized sample sets from two distributions, with a chosen ground metric (typically L1). The authors show PMD is the empirical first Wasserstein distance (aka Earth Mover’s Distance) between the two empirical measures, and prove strong consistency by invoking Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport. They propose using PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep models by (i) computing a matching once per population, (ii) taking gradients only through the sample distances (not through the discrete matching), and (iii) optionally subsampling mini-batches while holding the matching fixed for several SGD steps. They discuss exact (Hungarian, O(N^3)) and approximate (O(N^2)) matchers. Empirically, they compare PMD to MMD on Office domain adaptation and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), claiming PMD has stronger gradients, fewer hyperparameters, allows smaller mini-batches, converges faster, and outperforms MMD on several tasks.

Relation to prior work
- Conceptually, PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance/EMD. The equivalence to assignment is standard, and strong consistency of empirical OT is well-known in the OT literature (e.g., Villani). The paper rebrands this as “PMD.”
- A major omission is Cuturi (2013), Sinkhorn distances, and the extensive follow-up on regularized OT that provides fast, differentiable approximations widely used in ML, including for deep learning, generative modeling, and domain adaptation. Also missing are optimal transport for domain adaptation works (e.g., Courty et al., 2014/2016) that explicitly leverage OT maps/couplings for adaptation.
- For generative modeling, comparisons to WGANs (cited), Cramér distance (cited), and Sinkhorn-based OT losses (not cited) are important but not performed.
- For domain adaptation, comparisons to DANN, JAN, CMD, DSN (some cited) should be included as baselines, not only MMD.

Strengths
- Clear, simple objective: empirical Wasserstein with a practical recipe to use it in SGD without backpropagating through the matching.
- Reasonable argument and empirical evidence that PMD provides stronger, more uniformly distributed gradients than MMD with RBF kernels, and avoids bandwidth tuning.
- Useful practical insights: approximate matching works well; using small mini-batches while holding a matching fixed for a few steps can accelerate training.
- Empirical results show improvements over MMD on some domain adaptation tasks and cleaner samples than MMD-based GMMN in pixel space; convergence curves and sensitivity analyses are informative.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited. The core object (empirical W1/EMD) and its properties are established; the theory section mostly restates known results. The main contribution is an application recipe rather than a new discrepancy or theory.
- Missing key related work, especially Sinkhorn OT and OT-based domain adaptation, diminishes the contribution and makes the framing (introducing PMD as new) misleading.
- Scalability remains a concern. Even O(N^2) matching per population is expensive compared to critic-based training (WGAN) or Sinkhorn losses; the paper uses single-thread CPU matchers and suggests GPU, but does not demonstrate scalability on modern settings (e.g., ImageNet-scale, large batches).
- The “no gradient through matching” assumption (matching stable under small perturbations) is heuristic; no analysis of how often matchings change or of the effect on convergence is provided.
- Experimental scope is narrow. For domain adaptation, only MMD is compared and target labels are used for hyperparameter selection. For generative modeling, no quantitative metrics (e.g., FID/Inception) or comparisons to WGAN/Sinkhorn are provided; samples on SVHN/LFW are blurry, and pixel-space L1 is likely suboptimal.
- The paper introduces a new name (PMD) for a well-known quantity (empirical Wasserstein/EMD), which can confuse readers and overstates novelty.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Please position PMD explicitly as empirical Wasserstein and cite Cuturi (2013) and OT-for-DA works (e.g., Courty et al.). How does your approach compare empirically to Sinkhorn divergences in both speed and accuracy, and to WGAN with gradient penalty?
- Provide quantitative generative metrics (FID/Inception) and add strong baselines (WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn loss).
- For domain adaptation, include DANN/JAN/CMD/DSN baselines and discuss hyperparameter selection without target labels.
- Analyze how often the matching changes during training and how this impacts the gradient estimator; is there a way to amortize or warm-start the matching?
- Explore feature-space ground metrics (e.g., learned perceptual features) to mitigate pixel-space blur and report impact.
- Discuss and, if possible, demonstrate GPU-accelerated matching to address scalability.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, parameter-free loss with practical training heuristics that yield stronger gradients than MMD.
- Empirical evidence of faster convergence and some performance gains over a popular baseline.
- Potentially useful in settings where critic training is unstable or undesirable.

Cons for acceptance
- Core idea is not novel; largely a rebranding and application of empirical Wasserstein with limited new theory.
- Missing critical related work and baselines; experimental evaluation is too narrow for NeurIPS standards.
- Scalability and convergence concerns not fully addressed; generative results not competitive with contemporary alternatives.

Recommendation
Reject. While the paper is clear and the idea is practically appealing, the novelty over established empirical OT is limited, key related work and baselines are missing, and the experimental evidence is not yet sufficient to establish significance relative to existing OT-based methods. Strengthening the positioning, comparisons (especially to Sinkhorn/WGAN/OTDA), and scalability would make this a much more compelling contribution.
