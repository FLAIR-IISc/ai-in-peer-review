

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces "Population Matching Discrepancy" (PMD), defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations from distributions, and claims it serves as a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein metric. The authors position PMD as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), highlighting advantages including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100 instead of 1000).

The theoretical section establishes that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, which is a valid statistical distance. The paper presents a stochastic gradient descent algorithm to optimize parameters using PMD as the training objective, and evaluates PMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, reporting improved performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.

## Strengths

1. **Clear presentation**: The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the core idea of PMD, the theoretical connection to Wasserstein metrics, and the practical implementation details.

2. **Empirical validation**: The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling provide concrete evidence of PMD's advantages over MMD in specific settings. The paper demonstrates PMD's ability to converge faster with smaller batch sizes, which is a practical advantage.

3. **Theoretical foundation**: The paper provides solid theoretical grounding for PMD as a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, including a proof of strong consistency.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty**: The core concept of PMD is essentially the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD), a well-established concept in computer vision and optimal transport theory. The seminal paper by Rubner et al. (1998) introduced EMD for image retrieval, and it has been extensively used since then. The paper cites this work but fails to properly connect PMD to EMD or acknowledge that the authors are rediscovering a standard concept. The paper's claim of novelty is therefore significantly overstated.

2. **Misleading comparison**: The paper frames PMD as being compared to MMD, but PMD is fundamentally related to EMD (a Wasserstein metric approximation), not MMD. The comparison should be between EMD and MMD, not PMD and MMD. The paper's claim that PMD "has no bandwidth hyper-parameter" is misleading since EMD also lacks such a parameter, making this not a distinctive advantage over the established EMD approach.

3. **Inadequate literature review**: The paper fails to properly contextualize its contribution within the broader literature on optimal transport and distribution comparison. It cites [3] (WGAN) but doesn't properly connect to the extensive literature on Wasserstein metrics and EMD.

4. **Theoretical claims without context**: The paper claims PMD "has stronger gradients" than MMD, but this is presented as a novel observation. However, the gradient behavior of EMD versus MMD has been discussed in the literature (e.g., in the context of WGAN), and the paper doesn't acknowledge this.

## Recommendation

This paper should be rejected due to its lack of novelty. The core concept is not new but rather a rediscovery of the Earth Mover's Distance without proper attribution to the prior work. While the paper presents a clear implementation and some empirical results, the fundamental contribution is not novel, which is a critical criterion for NIPS.

The authors would need to significantly revise the paper to properly contextualize their work within the existing literature on EMD and optimal transport, clearly distinguishing their specific contributions (if any) from the established concept. Without this crucial revision, the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for novelty.

## Pro and Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Well-written and clear presentation
- Solid empirical validation on domain adaptation and generative modeling
- Good theoretical foundation for PMD as a consistent estimator
- Practical advantages in terms of batch size and convergence speed

**Con:**
- Core idea is not novel (essentially rediscovering EMD)
- Fails to properly cite and contextualize prior work on EMD
- Misleading comparison between PMD and MMD (should be EMD vs MMD)
- Overstated novelty claims without proper justification

This paper does not present a significant new concept or direction as required for NIPS acceptance. The contribution is incremental at best when properly contextualized within the existing literature.
