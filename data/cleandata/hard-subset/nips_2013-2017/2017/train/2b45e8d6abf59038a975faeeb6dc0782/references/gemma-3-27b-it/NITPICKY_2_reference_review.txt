This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an estimator for the Wasserstein metric, proposing it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling. The core idea – minimizing the cost of matching samples between distributions – is intuitively appealing and aims to address limitations of MMD regarding hyperparameter tuning, gradient behavior, and mini-batch size requirements.

While the central concept is potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a multitude of issues ranging from superficial analyses to formatting inconsistencies and a lack of rigorous justification for certain claims. The theoretical connections to the Wasserstein metric are stated (and rely on established results), but the practical advantages of PMD over MMD are not convincingly demonstrated, and the presentation is riddled with minor, yet cumulatively significant, flaws.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** The use of optimal transport/matching as a discrepancy measure is a reasonable direction, though not entirely groundbreaking given existing work on Wasserstein GANs and related methods.
*   **Potential for Gradient Improvement:** The authors correctly identify a potential issue with MMD’s gradient saturation and suggest PMD alleviates this.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The motivation for seeking alternatives to MMD is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Superficial Comparison:** The comparison to MMD feels shallow. While the authors *claim* PMD has stronger gradients and allows for smaller mini-batches, the evidence presented (Fig. 2b) is limited. The distribution of gradient magnitudes, while interesting, doesn’t conclusively demonstrate a significant advantage. The experiment in Fig. 3a shows faster convergence, but lacks sufficient detail on hyperparameter settings and statistical significance.
*   **Lack of Rigorous Ablation:** The experiments largely present PMD *alongside* MMD without systematically ablating specific components or hyperparameters to isolate the impact of PMD’s characteristics. For example, a well-tuned MMD with a carefully selected kernel bandwidth might perform comparably or even better.
*   **Inconsistent Notation & Formatting:** The paper suffers from numerous inconsistencies. Equation (2) uses θX, θY but later sections sometimes refer to TXθX without definition. The notation for sets (e.g., “[N]”) is unusual and unnecessary. The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., using “Sec. X” sometimes, and “Section X” other times). The figure captions are often incomplete or lack sufficient detail. Figure 1 is overly simplistic and doesn't fully capture the described learning process.
*   **Missing Details:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. What are the specific network architectures used? What optimization parameters (learning rate, momentum) are employed? How are the mini-batch sizes chosen? Without this information, reproducing the results is difficult.
*   **Weak Theoretical Justification for Practical Choices:** The claim that a small mini-batch size is sufficient for PMD relies on the argument that the matching doesn't change much within a small number of updates. This feels like a heuristic rather than a rigorously justified property.
*   **Overstatement of Advantages:** The claim that PMD is “parameter-free” is misleading. While it doesn't have a kernel bandwidth, the choice of distance function *d(·, ·)* effectively introduces a parameter (or parameter space).
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to a handful of datasets and architectures. The results are not generalizable enough to warrant strong claims.
*   **Language & Presentation:** The writing is often awkward and contains grammatical errors. For example, “While the Wasserstein metric itself…is tractable” is phrased poorly.

**Specific Concerns:**

*   **Equation (3):** The statement that the discrete matching M\* does not change for infinitesimal changes in θ is overly strong and requires more justification. It's not guaranteed, particularly when the distance function is not strictly convex.
*   **Section 3.1:** The transition from the empirical distributions to the Wasserstein metric feels abrupt. While the connection is known, a more detailed explanation would be beneficial.
*   **Section 5.3:** The discussion of time complexity is confusing. The mention of “O(N × BackProp)” is vague. A more precise comparison of computational costs would be valuable.
*   **Figure 2(a):** The y-axis label "Relative approximation error" is uninformative without specifying what it's relative *to*.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   Numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies detract from the paper’s credibility.
*   The experimental validation is insufficient to support the claims.
*   The paper lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS submission.
*   The novelty is incremental and the presented advantages are not compelling.

**For:**

*   The core idea has potential, and the authors identify a relevant problem (limitations of MMD).
*   The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is established.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting idea, the paper falls short of NIPS standards. The numerous minor flaws, superficial analysis, and lack of rigorous validation outweigh the strengths. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the polish expected of a top-tier conference publication. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the identified weaknesses, conduct more thorough experiments, and improve the clarity and rigor of their presentation before resubmitting to a venue more appropriate for the current state of the work.
