

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

## Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein metric defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two samples from distributions. The authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, has no bandwidth hyperparameter, provides stronger gradients, and allows smaller mini-batch sizes compared to MMD. They apply PMD to domain adaptation and generative modeling, reporting improved performance and faster convergence.

## Strengths
The paper presents an interesting approach to estimating distributional distances without relying on kernel bandwidths. The theoretical connection to Wasserstein metric is well-motivated, and the experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling show promising improvements over MMD in some settings. The idea of using minimum weight matching for distributional distance is novel in the context of deep learning applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Inconsistency in Gradient Calculation**: The paper claims "the discrete matching M* should not change for infinitesimal change of θ" (Section 2.2), which is critical to their argument that gradients don't propagate through the matching. However, this is not justified and is likely incorrect. The matching can change with θ, and the gradient should account for this change (via the implicit function theorem). This undermines the entire theoretical foundation of their method. The authors should provide a rigorous justification or revise their claim.

2. **Incomplete Theoretical Justification**: Theorem 3.3 claims strong consistency of PMD, but the proof relies on Proposition 3.2 which requires "lim inf N∈N ∫ d(x, y)γN(x, y)dxdy < +∞". This condition is not established in the paper, making the proof incomplete. The authors should explicitly verify this condition or provide a corrected proof.

3. **Confusing Terminology**: The paper uses inconsistent terminology throughout, particularly regarding "population size" (N) and "mini-batch size" (|B|). In Section 2.2, they state "the mini-batch size for PMD can be smaller than the population size", but in standard ML terminology, the "mini-batch size" is the batch size used for SGD, while "population size" is the sample size used to compute the distance. This confusion makes it difficult to understand their claims about mini-batch size advantages.

4. **Inconsistent Experimental Setup**: In Section 5.2, the authors train PMD for 100 epochs and MMD for 500 epochs. This is an unfair comparison as MMD is trained for 5 times as many epochs, making the reported convergence speed difference misleading. The paper should either train both methods for the same number of epochs or normalize the comparison by training time.

5. **Missing Justification for L1 Distance**: The paper claims "the pixel-level L1 distance is not good for natural images" (Section 5.2) but doesn't provide any justification or comparison with other distances (e.g., L2). They should investigate why L1 is better for their application or provide a theoretical rationale.

6. **Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies**: 
   - Equation (1) has "N N∑ i=1" which should be "1/N ∑_{i=1}^N"
   - Inconsistent notation: "pX" vs "p_X"
   - Figure 5(a) caption states "Exact N=500" but then refers to "N=500" for other curves, creating confusion
   - The abstract contains a typo: "st Conference" should be "The Conference"

7. **Misleading Claim about MMD**: The paper claims MMD "must have mini-batch size equal to the population size," but this is only true for the standard MMD estimator that uses all pairs. MMD can be approximated with mini-batches by computing a subset of the pairwise distances, which is commonly done in practice. The authors should clarify this point to avoid misleading readers.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting approach to distributional distance estimation, the technical flaws in the gradient calculation and theoretical justification are substantial. The inconsistent terminology and experimental setup further undermine the paper's claims. The minor formatting inconsistencies, while not critical, add to the impression of a hastily written manuscript.

The paper has potential value, but the technical flaws are significant enough to warrant rejection. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for publication.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments
**Pro (acceptance)**: Novel approach to distributional distance estimation with theoretical connections to Wasserstein metric, promising empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling.

**Con (rejection)**: Critical theoretical flaw in gradient calculation claim, incomplete proof of strong consistency, confusing terminology, unfair experimental comparison, and multiple minor formatting inconsistencies.
