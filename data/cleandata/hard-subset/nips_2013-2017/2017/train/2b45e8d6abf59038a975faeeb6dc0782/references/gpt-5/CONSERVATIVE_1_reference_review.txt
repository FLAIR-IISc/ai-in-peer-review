Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets, as a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance. The authors (i) show PMD equals the empirical OT objective and is “strongly consistent” via weak convergence plus stability of optimal transport, (ii) propose to use PMD as a training objective for deep models, taking gradients only through the cost and not through the discrete matching (and reusing a fixed matching for several SGD steps), and (iii) present experiments in unsupervised domain adaptation (Office) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), comparing primarily against MMD. They claim PMD has no bandwidth hyperparameter, yields stronger gradients than RBF-MMD, and allows smaller mini-batches.

Relation to prior work
The core object is the empirical 1-Wasserstein/earth mover’s distance computed via assignment—classical in OT and computer vision. Using OT distances in ML is well-established. Crucially, the paper omits key OT-based machine learning references, notably Cuturi (2013) on Sinkhorn distances and a large body of work on differentiable, GPU-friendly OT and stochastic/mini-batch OT (e.g., Genevay et al.). The comparison to WGAN focuses on the dual view but ignores that entropic-regularized primal OT provides a differentiable, scalable alternative with tunable bias/variance. In domain adaptation, stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD, DSN, adversarial methods) are not evaluated.

Strengths
- Simple and intuitive objective; direct connection to empirical OT is correct.
- The use of PMD as a loss is straightforward to implement and parameter-free (beyond choice of ground cost).
- Empirical studies suggest PMD can yield stronger per-sample gradients than RBF-MMD and tolerate smaller mini-batches.
- Experiments include two application areas and some ablations (batch size, population size, approximate vs exact matching).
- The observation that pixel-space L1 cost is poor and that feature/code-space distances help is sensible.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: PMD is essentially the standard empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD with assignment; rebranding as a new discrepancy is misleading. Using empirical OT as a training loss has prior art (primal OT with Sinkhorn regularization and its variants) not cited or compared.
- Missing key related work: No discussion of Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013), Sinkhorn divergence, stochastic/mini-batch OT, or recent OT-based adaptation/generative modeling. This omission undermines positioning and significance.
- Theory is minimal and incomplete: “Strong consistency” follows directly from standard OT stability under weak convergence but omits necessary moment/integrability conditions for W1 finiteness. The gradient argument should be stated via Danskin’s theorem/subdifferentials; as written, it is informal. Reusing a fixed matching across inner-loop SGD steps introduces bias with no analysis. No finite-sample/statistical rates; in high dimensions W1 has poor sample complexity, which is not discussed.
- Computational/scalability concerns: Matching is O(N^3) (Hungarian) or O(N^2) (approximate) plus O(N^2D) to form the cost matrix. Even with approximate matching, the approach scales poorly compared to Sinkhorn (vectorized, GPU-friendly) or dual approaches; experiments are limited to small images and modest N. No large-scale benchmarks, memory analysis, or strong wall-clock comparisons.
- Experimental evaluation is narrow: In domain adaptation, only MMD is compared, on fixed VGG features, with target-label-based hyperparameter tuning (violates the spirit of unsupervised DA, even if applied equally). Stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD, DSN, recent OT-based methods) are missing. In generative modeling, comparisons exclude GAN/WGAN/VAEs; evaluation is largely qualitative; no standard quantitative metrics (Inception Score, FID, NDB/coverage). Reported gains over MMD are modest/inconsistent.
- Claims on “stronger gradients” are specific to RBF-MMD with single bandwidth; multi-scale kernels, learned kernels, energy/Cramér distances, or Sinkhorn divergences address many of these issues. The paper doesn’t compare to these variants.

Quality
Technically, the main pieces are sound but largely known. The training heuristic (fixing matchings for multiple steps) is not theoretically justified. The empirical support is insufficient to conclude PMD is generally preferable to established alternatives.

Clarity
Generally readable, but several formal arguments are hand-wavy (gradient through min, conditions for consistency). Important implementation details (e.g., frequency of rematching, effect of step size on matching stability) are not carefully specified. The paper would benefit from clearer notation and an explicit statement of assumptions (cost continuity, moment bounds, uniqueness/regularity).

Originality
Low. The core discrepancy is standard empirical OT; the idea to use it as a loss is not new. The specific training heuristic and the “mini-batch smaller than population” trick are incremental.

Significance
Limited in current form due to missing comparisons to the most relevant OT baselines and weak scalability evidence. Potentially useful as a simple alternative to MMD in some settings, but the paper does not convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art performance or practical superiority.

Suggestions for improvement
- Situate the work within the OT literature: compare to Sinkhorn distances/divergences and stochastic OT; discuss bias-variance trade-offs and computational scaling; cite and compare to WGAN, energy/Cramér distances, and OT-based domain adaptation methods.
- Provide a proper gradient justification via Danskin’s theorem and discuss subgradient issues; analyze the bias introduced by reusing matchings; at least provide empirical sensitivity to inner-loop length/step size.
- Address sample complexity: discuss high-dimensional convergence rates for W1 and implications; consider feature/perceptual costs or learned embeddings (and compare).
- Strengthen experiments: add stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD, DSN) for DA; avoid using labeled target validation or report results without it; for generative modeling, include FID/IS and comparisons to GAN/WGAN; test on more challenging datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10) and provide rigorous wall-clock and memory scaling.
- Consider Sinkhorn regularization as a baseline/variant; it offers differentiability and GPU efficiency.
- Release code to support reproducibility.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is intuitive and the empirical results are somewhat positive, novelty over established OT-based methods is limited, related work is incomplete, theoretical support is minimal, and the experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous against strong baselines.
