## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating distances between probability distributions, particularly in the context of deep learning. The proposed Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) offers several theoretical and practical advantages, including the absence of a bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and potential for smaller mini-batch sizes. The empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate its effectiveness. While not a groundbreaking paradigm shift, the paper offers a valuable contribution with clear potential for wider adoption. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in deep learning: estimating the distance between probability distributions when only samples are available. Existing methods like MMD are popular but suffer from limitations related to bandwidth selection, gradient issues, and computational cost. The authors propose PMD, based on the Wasserstein metric, which minimizes the cost of matching samples from two distributions. They establish theoretical connections to the Wasserstein metric, demonstrating strong consistency, and show empirically that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

The authors correctly position their work within the context of related research, acknowledging key papers on MMD [17], Wasserstein distance [49], GANs [3], and domain adaptation [5, 47]. They effectively highlight the shortcomings of MMD and the challenges associated with estimating Wasserstein distance, justifying the need for PMD.  The comparison to WGAN [3] is particularly insightful, pointing out PMD’s parameter-free nature and direct distance estimation.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3) provides a solid theoretical foundation for PMD.  Connecting it directly to the Wasserstein metric is a strong point.
* **Practical Advantages:** Eliminating the bandwidth parameter simplifies usage and removes a source of potential error.  The argument for stronger gradients and smaller mini-batch sizes is persuasive and supported by the empirical results.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling clearly demonstrate that PMD can outperform MMD. The convergence speed comparison (Fig. 3a) is particularly convincing.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is well-organized and the concepts are explained clearly. The inclusion of pseudocode (Fig. 1) is helpful.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The ablation studies examining the impact of population size, mini-batch size and matching algorithm provides valuable insights.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors address the computational complexity of the matching problem, it remains a potential bottleneck, especially for very large sample sizes.  Further discussion of efficient implementations (beyond the mention of GPU parallelization) could be beneficial.
* **Distance Metric:** The exclusive use of L1 distance may limit PMD's applicability in scenarios where other distance metrics are more appropriate (e.g., images where Euclidean or cosine distance might be more meaningful). While the authors mention this briefly in relation to the blurry images, a more in-depth discussion of distance metric selection would be useful.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the chosen tasks are representative, expanding the experimental evaluation to other domains or generative models could further strengthen the paper.
* **Discussion of Matching Algorithm Limitations:** The authors mention the approximation used for larger N, but a more detailed discussion of the potential impact of this approximation on the results would be beneficial.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Sec 2.2:** The justification for the gradient calculation in Eq. (3) relying on the matching *not* changing for infinitesimal changes in θ feels a bit hand-wavy. While intuitively plausible, a more rigorous justification or a citation would be helpful.
* **Sec 3.1/3.2:**  The connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is well-established, but a more intuitive explanation of *why* PMD is a good estimator in practical scenarios would be useful for readers less familiar with optimal transport theory.
* **Sec 5.3:** The discussion of time complexity is good, but it doesn't provide concrete runtime numbers. Including a table showing the actual runtime for different N and matching algorithms would be very helpful.
* **Fig 2(a):** It would be useful to include the standard deviation along with the relative approximation error.
* **Fig 4:** The generated images are qualitative. Quantifying the quality (e.g., using Inception Score or FID) would provide a more objective comparison.


**5. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Provides a theoretically sound and practically effective alternative to MMD.
* Offers clear advantages in terms of parameter tuning, gradient behavior, and potential for faster convergence.
* Empirical results demonstrate superior performance on relevant deep learning tasks.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Con:**

*  Computational cost of the matching problem might be a limitation for very large datasets.
*  Limited discussion of distance metric selection.
*  Experimental evaluation could be broadened.




**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and deserves to be published in NIPS. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by:

* Providing more concrete runtime numbers for the matching algorithm.
*  Expanding the discussion of distance metric selection.
* Considering additional experiments to broaden the evaluation.
*  Adding a more rigorous justification for the gradient calculation in Section 2.2.
