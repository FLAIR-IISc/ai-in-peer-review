This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an estimator for the Wasserstein metric, positioning it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling. The core idea – framing the distance estimation as a minimum weight bipartite matching problem – is genuinely novel and a potentially interesting direction. While the theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is clearly established, and the advantages over MMD (no bandwidth parameter, potentially stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes) are compellingly argued, the presentation feels somewhat preliminary and lacks a depth of empirical validation that would fully justify a strong recommendation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The PMD approach is a fresh perspective on sample-based Wasserstein distance estimation. The connection to optimal transport and bipartite matching is well articulated.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The proof of strong consistency is a valuable contribution, bolstering the theoretical appeal of PMD.
*   **Potential advantages:** The claimed benefits over MMD—gradient behavior and mini-batch size—are potentially significant, especially for large-scale deep learning.
*   **Clear problem statement:** The paper clearly motivates the need for improved sample-based divergence estimation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While domain adaptation and generative modeling are used, the experiments feel somewhat cursory. The results aren’t overwhelmingly convincing, and the ablation studies are minimal.  The convergence speed comparison (Fig 3a) is helpful but lacks quantitative detail.
*   **L1 Distance Choice:** The exclusive use of the L1 distance is a limitation. While justified in the paper, it’s unclear how PMD performs with other distance metrics (e.g., Euclidean). The justification relating to the gradient strength seems post-hoc, and a comparative analysis would be valuable.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The discussion of computational complexity, particularly regarding the matching algorithm, is adequate but could be more detailed.  While the approximated matching is mentioned, a more thorough analysis of its impact on accuracy would be beneficial.
*   **Generative Modeling Results:** The statement that PMD generates "less noisy" images is subjective.  A quantitative evaluation of image quality (e.g., Inception Score, FID) is missing.  The blurry results on SVHN/LFW require further investigation.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** The writing is generally clear, but there are instances where the explanations could be more concise and accessible.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The core idea is genuinely novel and has the potential to impact areas reliant on sample-based divergence estimation.
*   The theoretical results provide a strong foundation for the proposed method.
*   The initial empirical results are promising, demonstrating performance comparable to or better than MMD in specific settings.
*   The paper tackles a significant problem in deep learning: robust and efficient divergence estimation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical validation is limited and doesn't convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over MMD across a broader range of tasks and datasets.
*   The exclusive reliance on L1 distance could limit the applicability of PMD.
*   The scalability concerns, while addressed, require further investigation.
*   The paper feels somewhat incomplete; more thorough experimentation and analysis are needed to solidify the claims.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards accepting this paper, but with reservations. The novelty and theoretical soundness are strong points that outweigh the weaknesses. This is a bold idea that presents a fresh perspective on a challenging problem. However, the paper needs further refinement and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation to fully realize its potential.  The authors should be encouraged to address the weaknesses identified in this review in future work.  Given the potential impact of a successful PMD implementation, I believe that the risk of accepting a slightly preliminary paper is justified.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with encouragement for future work addressing the noted weaknesses).
