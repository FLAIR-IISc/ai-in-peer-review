- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between i.i.d. samples to estimate Wasserstein-1 (empirical OT/EMD).  
- Shows consistency of PMD as an estimator of W1 using standard OT theory (Varadarajan’s theorem and OT stability).  
- Presents a practical SGD recipe: compute matchings on moderate “population” batches, treat assignments as fixed for differentiation, and subsample matched pairs for smaller mini-batches.  
- Claims PMD is preferable to MMD due to no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, and support for smaller mini-batches; empirics show faster or competitive convergence.  
- Theory is correct but largely standard OT results; equivalence to minimum matching with uniform marginals is well-known.  
- The claim of an “unbiased” gradient without differentiating through assignment is unjustified; at best one gets a valid subgradient almost everywhere and subsampling induces bias.  
- No convergence guarantees are provided for the alternating scheme that reuses assignments across SGD steps.  
- The approximate O(N^2) matching plus assignment reuse is a reasonable heuristic but tradeoffs versus differentiable OT approximations (e.g., Sinkhorn) are not evaluated.  
- Empirics: domain adaptation shows gains on some Office shifts with useful plots; generative results are limited (small datasets, no FID/IS, pixel L1 causes blur).  
- Comparisons are restricted to MMD; missing stronger baselines like WGAN, Sinkhorn-based losses, and sliced Wasserstein.  
- Paper is generally clear and well structured, with helpful figures and algorithm explanations.  
- Some claims need rewording (e.g., “unbiased gradient” and incorrect statement that MMD requires population-sized batches).  
- Core objective is known (empirical W1/EMD); novelty is incremental, mainly the training heuristic (assignment reuse and subsampling).  
- Important related work on entropic-regularized OT, sliced Wasserstein, OT-based domain adaptation, and linear-time MMD estimators is missing.  
- Significance is limited by incremental contribution and incomplete comparisons, though the assignment-reuse finding is practically interesting.  
- Strengths: simple OT-grounded objective, practical heuristic with empirical evidence of stronger gradients and faster convergence, and useful analyses (error vs N, timings, exact vs approximate).  
- Weaknesses: limited novelty, missing/incorrect related work, questionable unbiased gradient claim, no convergence theory for alternating scheme, no discussion of W1 sample complexity in high dimensions.  
- Generative modeling evaluation lacks quantitative metrics and stronger baselines; pixel-space results are blurry and may not support claims.  
- Suggestions: compare to Sinkhorn, sliced Wasserstein, WGAN/Cramér, and OT-DA methods; report FID/IS for generative tasks and include linear-time MMD baselines.  
- Suggestions: clarify gradient/subgradient behavior and bias from assignment reuse/subsampling; ablate recompute frequency and effect.  
- Suggestions: correct MMD mini-batch claims, discuss high-dimensional sample complexity, and consider feature- or learned-metric spaces to reduce blur.  
- Suggestions: broaden DA baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD) and test end-to-end fine-tuning beyond frozen features.  
- Arguments for acceptance: simple, practical approach with clear exposition and empirical analyses; useful heuristic for practitioners.  
- Arguments against acceptance: rebranding of empirical OT with standard theory, missing key baselines and citations, limited empirical scope and metrics.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject — paper has practical merit but needs stronger baselines, corrected claims, and fuller positioning within OT literature.
