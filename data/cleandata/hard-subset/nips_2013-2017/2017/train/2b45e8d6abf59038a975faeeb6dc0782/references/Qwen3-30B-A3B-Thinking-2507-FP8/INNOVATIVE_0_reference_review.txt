

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based statistical divergence that estimates the first Wasserstein metric between two distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two populations of samples, and the authors prove it's a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes using PMD as a training objective in deep learning, demonstrating advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch requirements.

The core novelty of this work lies in the conceptual shift from kernel-based methods (like MMD) to combinatorial optimization for measuring distributional differences. While the Wasserstein metric has been used in machine learning, its direct application has been limited by computational complexity. PMD provides a practical, polynomial-time approximation that can be incorporated into deep learning pipelines without additional networks or hyperparameters. This represents a bold, novel direction that addresses a fundamental limitation of current distributional distance metrics.

The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear connections between PMD and the Wasserstein metric established through the Varadarajan theorem and stability of optimal transport. The authors effectively demonstrate that PMD avoids key MMD limitations: the gradient saturation problem, bandwidth tuning, and large mini-batch requirements. The empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling are compelling, showing PMD outperforms MMD on several benchmarks while converging faster.

What makes this paper particularly strong is its practical focus - the authors don't just present a theoretical construct but demonstrate how PMD can be integrated into existing deep learning frameworks with minimal modification. The implementation details (including the use of approximate matching algorithms) show practical awareness, and the ablation studies on population size and mini-batch size provide useful guidance for practitioners.

Minor weaknesses include the focus on L1 distance (which performs poorly on natural images, as acknowledged in Section 5.2), and the lack of comparison with other Wasserstein approximations (like those in [3, 45]). The theoretical analysis is limited to the first Wasserstein metric, and the paper doesn't explore how PMD might generalize to higher-order metrics or different distance functions.

However, these limitations don't undermine the paper's core contribution, which is the conceptual novelty of PMD as a distributional distance metric. The authors have made a significant step toward making Wasserstein-like metrics more practical for deep learning, addressing a real pain point in the field. The paper's claim that "PMD has no bandwidth hyper-parameter" is particularly valuable, as bandwidth tuning has been a persistent source of frustration in MMD applications.

Compared to other NIPS papers on distributional distances (like WGAN [3] or MMD-based methods [17, 31]), this work offers a fundamentally different approach that sidesteps many of the limitations of existing methods. The connection to optimal transport theory is clear, but the practical implementation is refreshingly simple - no separate critic network is needed, and the method can be integrated directly into standard deep learning pipelines.

The experiments are well-designed and sufficiently comprehensive for the claims being made, with results that consistently support the theoretical advantages of PMD. The convergence speed comparison (Fig. 3) is particularly convincing for domain adaptation, and the gradient analysis (Fig. 2b) provides strong theoretical justification for the empirical observations.

This paper's greatest contribution is the paradigm shift it proposes - moving from kernel-based to combinatorial approaches for measuring distributional differences. While the computational complexity of matching (O(N^3) for Hungarian) might be a concern, the authors wisely note that both exact and approximate matching algorithms work well in practice, and the matching cost is comparable to SGD in modern implementations (Fig. 5c).

In summary, this paper presents a novel, conceptually clean, and practically useful approach to measuring distributional differences in deep learning. It addresses a real problem with current methods (MMD's bandwidth hyperparameter and weak gradients) in a way that's both theoretically grounded and empirically validated. The work is significant for advancing the practical application of optimal transport in deep learning, and it opens up new research directions for combinatorial approaches to statistical distances.

Given the high value placed on novelty and bold approaches at NIPS, and the paper's clear demonstration of a new direction in distributional distance metrics, I strongly recommend acceptance. The minor methodological limitations do not detract from the paper's significant contribution to the field.
