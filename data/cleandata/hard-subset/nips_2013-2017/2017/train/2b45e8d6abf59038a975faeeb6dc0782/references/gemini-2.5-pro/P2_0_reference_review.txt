### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based metric for comparing two probability distributions, designed for use as a loss function in deep learning. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples (populations) drawn from the respective distributions. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1, or Earth Mover's Distance).

The core of the paper is a comparison between PMD and the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors argue that PMD offers several key advantages: it is hyperparameter-free (unlike MMD, which requires tuning a kernel bandwidth), it provides stronger and more stable gradients that do not saturate, and it allows for more efficient stochastic optimization by using small update mini-batches decoupled from the larger population size used for matching.

The paper proposes an algorithm for learning the parameters of a generative model by minimizing PMD via stochastic gradient descent. The key insight enabling this is that the gradient does not need to propagate through the discrete matching operation. The authors evaluate PMD on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. In both applications, empirical results show that PMD achieves competitive or superior performance compared to MMD, often with faster convergence.

### Review

This is a high-quality paper that presents a simple, elegant, and effective method for a fundamental problem in deep learning: comparing implicit probability distributions. The proposed PMD is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.

**Quality: (Excellent)**

The paper is technically sound.
-   **Theoretical Grounding:** The connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is clearly and correctly established by framing the problem on empirical distributions as a linear program, whose solution is known to be a permutation matrix (i.e., a matching). The claim that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the W1 metric is supported by citing established results in optimal transport and measure theory (Propositions 3.1 and 3.2). This provides a firm theoretical foundation for the method.
-   **Experimental Rigor:** The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The authors compare PMD against its most direct competitor, MMD, on standard benchmarks (Office dataset for domain adaptation, MNIST/SVHN/LFW for generative modeling). The inclusion of ablation studies is a significant strength. Figure 5, which analyzes the sensitivity to population size (N) and SGD mini-batch size (|B|), provides strong evidence for the claim that PMD benefits from a small update batch, a key practical advantage. The empirical analysis of the gradient distribution (Figure 2b) also effectively supports the theoretical argument about MMD's vanishing gradients.
-   **Honesty:** The authors are upfront about the computational cost of the matching step (O(N^3) or O(N^2)), which is a primary weakness. They correctly argue that for moderate N, this can be acceptable relative to the cost of backpropagation, and they evaluate both exact and approximate matching algorithms.

**Clarity: (Excellent)**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
-   The motivation in the introduction is compelling, clearly outlining the drawbacks of MMD that PMD aims to solve.
-   Section 2 explains the method and the learning algorithm with admirable clarity, aided by a helpful figure and pseudocode. The crucial distinction between the population size `N` (for matching) and the SGD mini-batch size `|B|` (for updates) is well-explained.
-   The theoretical connections in Section 3 are presented logically and build upon each other, making the formal justification accessible.
-   The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, with informative tables and figures.

**Originality: (Very Good)**

While the Wasserstein metric and bipartite matching are not new concepts, their application in this specific manner as a direct, differentiable-through-the-samples loss for deep learning is novel and insightful.
-   **Relation to Prior Work:** The paper does a good job of situating PMD relative to MMD. The connection to the Wasserstein metric also naturally brings WGAN to mind. The authors briefly but effectively distinguish PMD from WGAN by noting that PMD is a parameter-free, non-adversarial approach that computes a direct estimate of the metric, whereas WGAN learns a critic network to approximate the dual formulation. This work can be seen as a strong, non-adversarial alternative in the family of Wasserstein-based losses. It provides a distinct approach compared to both kernel-based methods (MMD) and adversarial methods (WGAN).
-   The core original contribution is the formulation of a practical learning algorithm around the bipartite matching of samples and the empirical demonstration of its advantages.

**Significance: (Very Good)**

The results presented are important and likely to have an impact on the field.
-   **Practical Utility:** MMD is a workhorse for many tasks, but its sensitivity to kernel bandwidth is a well-known practical pain point. By providing a competitive, hyperparameter-free alternative, PMD is a valuable contribution that practitioners are likely to adopt. The faster convergence and ability to use smaller update batches are also significant practical benefits.
-   **Broader Impact:** This work provides a simple and direct way to incorporate optimal transport ideas into deep learning frameworks without the complexity and potential instability of adversarial training (as in WGAN). It could inspire further research into primal-form, sample-based estimators for various statistical divergences.
-   **Advancement of State-of-the-Art:** In the domain adaptation experiments, PMD demonstrates a clear improvement on several standard tasks. In generative modeling, the qualitative results appear to be at least as good as, and in some cases better than, the MMD-based GMMN, especially when generating directly in pixel space.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Elegant and Simple Idea:** The core idea of using minimum weight matching as a loss is intuitive and powerful.
2.  **Strong Practical Advantages:** The method eliminates the need for kernel hyperparameter tuning, a significant practical hurdle for MMD. The stronger gradient signal and faster convergence are also compelling benefits.
3.  **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The method is well-grounded as a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.
4.  **Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The paper includes a thorough experimental comparison against a strong baseline (MMD) on multiple tasks, along with insightful ablation studies that support the paper's central claims.
5.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a valuable contribution highly accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance (Weaknesses to address):**
1.  **Computational Scalability:** The primary weakness is the computational complexity of the matching algorithm (O(N^3) or O(N^2)). While the paper shows this is manageable for N up to a few thousand, it remains a significant bottleneck compared to the O(N^2) complexity of MMD. This may limit its applicability in settings requiring very large population sizes for accurate discrepancy estimation. The authors mention GPU acceleration as future work, but it is a current limitation.
2.  **Limited Theoretical Novelty:** The theoretical analysis primarily consists of applying existing results to the proposed estimator. While sound, the paper would be even stronger with novel theoretical results, such as finite-sample error bounds, which are mentioned as future work.

### Final Recommendation

This is a strong paper presenting a valuable and practical contribution. The proposed PMD method is well-motivated, well-executed, and offers clear advantages over the widely-used MMD. The weaknesses, primarily related to computational scaling, are acknowledged and do not undermine the core contribution for the problem scales addressed. The paper is a clear advancement and is likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
