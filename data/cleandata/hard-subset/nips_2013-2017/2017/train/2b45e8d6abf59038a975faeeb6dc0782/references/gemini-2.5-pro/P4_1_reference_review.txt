This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples, one from each distribution. The paper's main theoretical contribution is to show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. This insight is leveraged to propose an algorithm for learning the parameters of distributions (e.g., deep generative models) by minimizing PMD using stochastic gradient descent. The authors argue that PMD offers several advantages over the widely-used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), including having no kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, providing stronger gradients during training, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes in optimization. The utility of PMD is demonstrated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling. This work relates to a growing body of literature that uses optimal transport metrics in machine learning, most notably the recent Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) [3], although it approaches the estimation of the Wasserstein metric from the primal formulation, unlike the dual approach of WGAN. It also directly builds on and contrasts with the line of work using MMD for similar tasks, such as Generative Moment Matching Networks (GMMN) [31].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of using the exact or approximate solution to the minimum weight matching problem directly as a differentiable loss for training deep neural networks is novel. While the Wasserstein metric is not new to the community, this parameter-free, primal-based approach to optimizing it is a fresh and valuable contribution. It provides a compelling alternative to MMD, directly addressing some of its well-known practical shortcomings like the sensitivity to kernel bandwidth selection and the issue of vanishing gradients, which can slow down training. The potential for a more robust and easier-to-tune divergence measure is significant for many applications.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The connection between the minimum weight matching problem and the first Wasserstein metric on empirical distributions is correctly established by relating it to the linear programming formulation of optimal transport. The main theoretical result—that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric—is well-supported by established theorems from probability theory (Varadarajan's theorem) and optimal transport (stability of optimal transport), making the claim credible. The proposed algorithm for parameter learning via SGD is straightforward and well-justified.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-articulated, the definition of PMD is precise, and the derivation of its properties is easy to follow. The algorithm is presented with a clear pseudocode block and a helpful illustration. The experimental sections are well-structured, clearly outlining the setups and results for each application.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides good support for the paper's claims.
    *   The domain adaptation experiments on the Office dataset show that PMD can outperform MMD and is less sensitive to hyper-parameter choices (Fig. 3), which is a strong practical advantage.
    *   The generative modeling results demonstrate that PMD can produce reasonable samples and compares favorably to MMD-based GMMN, particularly in the challenging pixel space.
    *   The ablation studies on the population size (N) and SGD mini-batch size (|B|) in Figure 5 effectively demonstrate the claimed benefits of PMD's optimization scheme, showing its robustness to N and its ability to benefit from small |B|.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Scalability:** The primary concern with the proposed method is its computational complexity. The minimum weight matching step has a complexity of O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm and O(N^2) for the approximation used. While the authors argue this is acceptable for moderate population sizes (N) compared to the cost of backpropagation, this can become a significant bottleneck as N grows, which may be necessary for accurately representing complex distributions in high dimensions. The timing results in Figure 5(c) show that the matching cost is already comparable to the SGD updates for N in the thousands. This scalability issue may limit the method's applicability in settings that demand very large batch sizes.

2.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** In the generative modeling application, the empirical comparison is made against MMD (GMMN). However, a more direct and highly relevant baseline would have been the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) [3], which also seeks to optimize the Wasserstein distance. The comparison to WGAN is confined to a brief theoretical discussion. An empirical comparison would have been crucial for positioning PMD within the current state-of-the-art of generative models, as WGAN and its successors represent the most prominent use of the Wasserstein metric in this area.

3.  **Impact of the Ground Distance `d(·, ·)`:** The paper uses the L1 distance as the ground metric in all experiments. The performance of PMD is inherently tied to this choice. For image generation tasks, it is well-known that pixel-wise L1 or L2 distances do not align well with perceptual similarity, which could be a contributing factor to the blurriness observed in the generated samples (Fig. 4). A discussion on the sensitivity to this choice and an exploration of alternative distances (e.g., a distance in a pretrained feature space) would have strengthened the paper.

4.  **Nature of the Gradient:** The claim of "stronger gradients" is supported by showing that the L1-based PMD gradient (a sign function) has a constant magnitude, avoiding the decay of MMD's RBF kernel-based gradient. While this can prevent vanishing gradients, a constant-magnitude gradient can also introduce optimization challenges, such as oscillations around a minimum, and may require careful tuning of the learning rate. A more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs of this gradient behavior would be beneficial.

In summary, this paper presents a novel, theoretically-grounded, and promising method for estimating and optimizing a statistical divergence between distributions. It successfully demonstrates its advantages over MMD in two relevant deep learning applications. The primary weaknesses relate to the computational scalability of the matching algorithm and a lack of empirical comparison with the highly relevant WGAN framework. Despite these points, the originality of the approach and the strength of the presented results make this a solid contribution to the field.
