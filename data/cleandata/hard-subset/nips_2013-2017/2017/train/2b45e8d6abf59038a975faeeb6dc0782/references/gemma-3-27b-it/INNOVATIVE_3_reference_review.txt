This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for estimating distances between probability distributions, particularly in the context of deep learning. The core idea – using a minimum weight bipartite matching between samples – is novel and addresses some known drawbacks of MMD, namely the bandwidth parameter and potential gradient issues. The theoretical grounding, demonstrating PMD’s consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein metric, is a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The PMD formulation is a fresh perspective on sample-based divergence estimation.  It's not merely a tuning of MMD, but a fundamentally different approach based on optimal transport principles.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Establishing the strong consistency of PMD with the Wasserstein metric provides a solid theoretical foundation. This is a significant strength.
*   **Potential Practical Advantages:** The claim of eliminating the bandwidth parameter, having stronger gradients, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes are highly appealing, particularly for deep learning applications. The empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling suggest these advantages hold in practice.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concept of PMD and its relationship to existing methods. The pseudocode helps understanding the implementation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of PMD compared to MMD on two relevant tasks, providing initial evidence of its potential.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors address this, the computational complexity of the matching problem (O(N<sup>3</sup>) or O(N<sup>2</sup>) with approximation) remains a concern, especially as N grows. The impact of this on very large datasets isn't fully explored. The scaling discussion in 5.3 is useful, but could be more extensive.
*   **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are limited to two tasks and a few datasets. A more thorough evaluation across a wider range of problems would strengthen the claims.
*   **Distance Metric Choice:**  The use of L1 distance is justified, but a brief discussion of the potential impact of different distance metrics *within* the PMD framework would be helpful. It’s implied the choice influences performance (mentioning blurry images for SVHN/LFW), but isn’t explored.
*   **Practical Gradient Issues:** While the paper argues for stronger gradients, a more detailed analysis of the gradient behavior during training (e.g., gradient norms, variance) compared to MMD would be beneficial. The distribution plot in Fig 2(b) is a good start, but more quantitative analysis is needed.
*   **Approximation Algorithm Details:**  The description of the approximation algorithm for the matching problem could be more detailed, providing some insight into its accuracy and potential biases.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to divergence estimation.
*   The potential practical benefits (bandwidth-free, better gradients, smaller batches) are significant for deep learning practitioners.
*   The initial empirical results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD.
*   The paper addresses a challenging problem in a creative and well-motivated way.  The work opens up new avenues for research on sample-based divergence estimation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity remains a concern, potentially limiting scalability to very large datasets.
*   The empirical evaluation is relatively limited and could be strengthened with more extensive experimentation.
*   The theoretical analysis focuses primarily on consistency; further analysis of finite-sample error bounds could be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. While there are minor weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of PMD are substantial.  The paper presents a significant contribution to the field, offering a promising alternative to existing divergence estimation methods.  The authors have identified a real problem and proposed a creative and well-justified solution.  I believe this work is likely to stimulate further research and potentially lead to improvements in various deep learning applications.  The weaknesses are not critical enough to outweigh the strengths, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated the value of their approach.
