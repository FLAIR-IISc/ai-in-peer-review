Summary
This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. samples from two distributions. PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance (Earth Mover’s Distance) between the two empirical measures with equal weights. The authors show PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance (via Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport) and propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep models by not backpropagating through the discrete matching. They argue PMD has advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients (RBF kernels can saturate), and the ability to use smaller minibatches than the “population” used to compute matching. Applications are demonstrated on unsupervised domain adaptation (Office dataset) and deep generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW). Empirically, PMD is often faster/more stable than MMD and yields better or comparable results.

Relation to prior work: PMD is equivalent to empirical Wasserstein-1/EMD (Rubner et al. 2000; Villani 2008), optimized by assignment (Hungarian) or approximations. Wasserstein distances have been used in deep learning recently (e.g., WGAN; Cramér distance), and there is a substantial literature on scalable/differentiable OT (Sinkhorn distances/entropic regularization; Cuturi, NIPS 2013; subsequent stochastic/automatic differentiation works). The paper positions itself mainly against MMD and WGAN, with less discussion of Sinkhorn-based alternatives.

Quality
- Theory: The consistency statement follows directly from classic results; however, the assumptions for finiteness of the objective (e.g., finite first moments for unbounded d) should be stated. The claim that gradients do not propagate through the matching is intuitive for piecewise-constant matchings but lacks a rigorous subgradient justification or discussion of nondifferentiability at boundaries; nevertheless, using such “frozen-assignment” gradients is a common heuristic in structured losses and tends to work empirically.
- Algorithms: Matching is solved exactly (Hungarian, O(N^3)) or via an O(N^2) approximation (Drake-Hougardy). The proposed “outer-loop recompute matching, inner-loop SGD with fixed matching and possibly subsampling” is practical. The idea of using |B| < N induces biased gradients; the paper observes it works well but does not analyze this bias.
- Experiments: Results show PMD beats or matches MMD on Office (with VGG features, simple classifier) on some transfers and converges faster. In generative modeling, PMD produces less noisy samples than MMD in pixel space, with better stability; using AE codes helps. However, evaluations are largely qualitative (no inception/FID/likelihood proxies), and comparisons are only against MMD, not against WGAN or Sinkhorn-based methods. Domain adaptation comparisons omit stronger baselines like DANN, CORAL, CMD, JAN.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with useful figures and pseudocode. The connection to OT is correctly explained. Some claims (e.g., “not differentiable” for dual-based approaches) are too strong given the existence of differentiable entropic OT. Notation is mostly consistent, with minor typos. It would help to clearly acknowledge PMD = empirical W1 upfront rather than introducing a new name that may obscure this equivalence.

Originality
The core discrepancy (PMD) is not new; it is the standard empirical W1/EMD. The theoretical result is standard. The main contributions are:
- Advocating the use of empirical W1 via minimum matching as a training loss for deep models without backprop through the matching.
- A practical SGD scheme with periodic matching and subsampled updates.
- Empirical observations about gradient strength vs MMD and minibatch effects.
While the combination and empirical study are useful, the methodological novelty is limited relative to existing OT-based approaches in ML.

Significance
The idea of using Wasserstein-type losses in deep learning is important and active. Showing that straight empirical W1 can work competitively with MMD in domain adaptation and generative modeling is useful. However, without comparisons to contemporary Wasserstein approaches (WGAN, Sinkhorn/entropic OT, sliced Wasserstein), and without stronger quantitative evaluations, it is hard to assess impact. The computational story would be stronger with GPU Sinkhorn or GPU matching results and larger-scale experiments.

Strengths
- Clear connection to OT; simple, parameter-free objective.
- Practical training procedure; stronger, non-saturating gradients than RBF-MMD in practice.
- Empirical evidence of faster convergence and reduced sensitivity to hyperparameters vs MMD.
- Useful sensitivity and timing analyses; nice gradient magnitude study.
- The observed N−0.23 empirical error rate in 5D aligns with known W1 rates (≈ N−1/d).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: PMD is empirical W1/EMD; the consistency theorem is standard.
- Missing or underplayed related work: notably entropic regularization (Sinkhorn distances; Cuturi 2013) and differentiable OT solvers that are GPU-friendly and scalable.
- Incomplete experimental comparisons: no WGAN (or WGAN-GP) or Sinkhorn-based baselines; qualitative generative results without standard metrics.
- The gradient-through-matching argument is heuristic; lack of discussion on subgradients and the potential bias from fixed-match subsampling.
- Claims about dual approximations being non-differentiable are inaccurate given existing methods.
- Domain adaptation baselines are limited and the tuning procedure leverages labeled target validation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explicitly acknowledge PMD ≡ empirical Wasserstein-1/EMD and position the contribution as a practical training recipe and empirical study.
- Compare against WGAN/WGAN-GP and Sinkhorn OT (with automatic differentiation) in both tasks; report standard generative metrics (e.g., FID/Inception on SVHN; NDB/Coverage).
- Include references and experiments with entropic OT (Sinkhorn) and possibly sliced Wasserstein, discussing trade-offs vs matching-based PMD.
- Provide a short discussion on subgradients for piecewise-constant matchings and conditions for the “no backprop through matching” trick.
- Clarify assumptions for Theorem 3.3 (finite first moments).
- Strengthen domain adaptation comparisons with DANN, CORAL, CMD, JAN and avoid using labeled target validation if aiming for purely unsupervised DA.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of ground metric (L1 vs L2 vs feature-space distances)?
- How often does the matching change in practice during the inner-loop SGD? Can you quantify stability?
- What is the computational trade-off vs Sinkhorn OT on GPU at similar N?
- Can you report quantitative generative metrics and add WGAN/Sinkhorn baselines?

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, simple method; consistent with OT theory.
- Empirical benefits over MMD in convergence and stability.
- Clear exposition and useful empirical analyses.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; rebranding empirical W1 as PMD.
- Missing key related work and stronger baselines (Sinkhorn, WGAN).
- Weak quantitative evaluation in generative modeling.
- Heuristic gradient treatment without theoretical support.

Recommendation: Weak reject
Confidence: High
