- Paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) = average cost of minimum bipartite matching between samples, equal to empirical 1-Wasserstein (W1).
- Authors prove strong consistency of PMD to true W1 using standard results and match known empirical OT convergence rates.
- Proposes a practical training recipe: compute a batch matching once per population and take gradients without backpropagating through the discrete matching.
- Empirical claim: PMD yields stronger gradients and can use smaller minibatches than RBF-MMD, supported by gradient histograms and toy studies.
- Applications demonstrated in domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) with timing and convergence analyses.
- Strength: conceptually simple and well-grounded—PMD is just empirical EMD/W1 with correct consistency statements.
- Strength: practical and implementable training heuristic; approximate O(N^2) matching makes it feasible at moderate N.
- Strength: clear empirical message about reduced vanishing gradients versus RBF-MMD and informative toy experiments.
- Strength: useful sensitivity and timing studies; Office domain adaptation results competitive with MMD.
- Weakness: limited novelty—PMD is classical empirical OT and matching-based computation is standard; paper misses key related work.
- Missing related work: no discussion/comparison to entropic OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi 2013) and stochastic/mini-batch OT (Genevay et al.), or Sliced Wasserstein variants.
- Weakness: differentiability assumptions ignore gradients through combinatorial argmin; freezing matchings and subsampling likely introduce bias and non-smoothness without theoretical justification.
- Weakness: incorrect/incomplete claim that MMD minibatch size must equal population size—linear-time unbiased/low-variance MMD estimators exist for SGD.
- Weakness: experimental baselines are narrow—domain adaptation comparisons limited to MMD; stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD, DSN) not compared.
- Weakness: generative modeling evaluation is qualitative and lacks standard quantitative metrics (Inception score, FID, Parzen); pixel-space L1 cost is suboptimal.
- Weakness: computational scalability concerns remain—pairwise distance and matching costs are O(N^2) and GPU acceleration/Sinkhorn comparisons are missing.
- Quality: theoretical contributions are incremental (standard stability + Varadarajan); no finite-sample bounds or generalization analysis.
- Quality: algorithmic training heuristic is reasonable but lacks convergence guarantees; claims of unbiased gradients are unproven.
- Quality: empirical reporting is careful with timing/optimization details, but comparisons and quantitative evaluations are limited.
- Clarity: paper is generally clear and organized but has notational clutter and should clarify MMD minibatch claims and inner-loop update effects.
- Originality: low-to-moderate—application of empirical W1 as a loss is useful but not a novel algorithmic contribution.
- Significance: moderate—practical observations may interest practitioners but impact limited by missing baselines and related work.
- Suggestions: add and compare entropic/Sinkhorn and stochastic OT methods, include stronger baselines (DANN, WGAN-GP, MMD-GAN), report FID/IS, analyze bias from frozen matchings/subsampling, and discuss GPU scalability.
- Recommendation: weak reject (borderline) pending stronger baselines, related-work integration, theoretical/experimental rigor improvements.
