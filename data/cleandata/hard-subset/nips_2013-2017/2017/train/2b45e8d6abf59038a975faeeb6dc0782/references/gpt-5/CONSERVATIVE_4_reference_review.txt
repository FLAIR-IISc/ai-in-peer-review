Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-cost bipartite matching between two sample sets under a base metric (e.g., L1). PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between uniform empirical measures, expressed via an assignment. The authors (i) show strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of the population Wasserstein-1 distance using standard optimal transport results, (ii) propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective by ignoring the dependence of the discrete matching on parameters (arguing it is locally constant), and (iii) introduce a practical training loop that reuses a fixed matching across several SGD steps and subsamples matched pairs to enable smaller mini-batches. They compare PMD to MMD in domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), reporting faster convergence and, in some cases, better performance than MMD.

Relation to prior work
PMD is the classical empirical Wasserstein-1/EMD between equal-weight empirical distributions solved via assignment (Rubner et al., 2000; Villani, 2008). The paper contrasts with MMD and WGAN-style dual approximations. However, important related literature is missing, notably Sinkhorn/entropic OT for fast, differentiable approximations (Cuturi, NIPS 2013) and subsequent stochastic/mini-batch OT methods (e.g., Genevay et al.), sliced Wasserstein distances, and more recent Wasserstein GAN variants (e.g., gradient penalty). Positioning relative to this line is crucial.

Strengths
- Simple, clear formulation connecting a well-known OT objective to a training procedure that leverages match reuse and subsampling.
- Empirical demonstrations that PMD can train with smaller mini-batches than MMD and may provide more uniform per-sample gradients.
- Implementation details and a comparison of exact vs approximate matching are useful; showing matching cost comparable to SGD for moderate N is informative.
- The consistency statement is correct and cleanly presented (albeit standard).

Weaknesses
Quality/rigor:
- The key learning claim relies on treating the matching M* as locally constant and ignoring its dependence on parameters. No formal justification (e.g., subgradient/directional derivative characterization, conditions for almost-everywhere differentiability, or convergence guarantees with stale/approximate matchings) is provided. The claim that Eq. (3) yields an “unbiased stochastic gradient” is not established; with changing matchings this is, at best, a heuristic subgradient method.
- No finite-sample statistical guarantees or known convergence rates for empirical W1 (which depend strongly on dimension) are discussed. The empirical N−0.23 observation is not placed in context of known OT rates.
- Computational scalability is a concern: computing and storing O(N2) pairwise distances and solving O(N3) (or O(N2) approx.) matchings each outer loop is limiting for modern deep learning scales; the paper stops short of proposing scalable remedies beyond citing GPU potential.

Originality:
- The core discrepancy is standard (empirical W1/EMD with assignment). Using PMD as a loss with backprop through only the cost and not the matching is a straightforward application; the novelty rests on the training heuristic (match-then-reuse with subsampling). The theoretical contribution (strong consistency) largely repackages well-known OT results.

Significance/empirics:
- Comparisons are limited. For domain adaptation, only MMD is considered; stronger baselines such as DANN, JAN, CORAL/Deep CORAL, CMD, DSN are absent, and the setup uses frozen VGG features with a shallow classifier rather than the end-to-end settings common in the literature. The reported gains over MMD on Office are modest/inconsistent.
- For generative modeling, there are no standard quantitative metrics (FID/IS/precision-recall) and no comparisons to GAN/WGAN/WGAN-GP; samples are often blurry. As such, claims of superiority over established approaches are not substantiated.
- The “no hyper-parameter” claim is overstated: the base ground cost (L1 vs L2), population size N, inner-loop steps, and subsample size |B| materially affect behavior.

Clarity:
- Generally readable, but some statements are too strong (e.g., “unbiased stochastic gradient”), and the training protocol (frequency of re-matching, sensitivity to stale matchings) could be described more precisely.

Questions for the authors
- Can you formalize the gradient/subgradient you use? Under what regularity/uniqueness conditions is ignoring dM*/dθ justified, and what is the effect when M* changes?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of base distance (L1 vs L2 or feature-space distances)? Could learning a feature metric improve the generative image quality?
- How often do you recompute matchings in practice, and how does performance degrade with staler matchings?
- How does PMD compare to Sinkhorn-regularized OT (Cuturi, 2013) in speed, stability, and accuracy, especially when using GPU and automatic differentiation?
- Can you provide quantitative generative metrics and compare with WGAN(-GP) and MMD-GAN?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to established baselines in domain adaptation (DANN, JAN, CORAL/Deep CORAL, CMD, DSN) and to GAN/WGAN variants in generation with standard metrics (FID/IS).
- Discuss and/or leverage entropic regularization (Sinkhorn) or sliced OT for scalability; cite relevant works and situate PMD’s niche.
- Provide a theoretical treatment of differentiability/subgradients of the assignment objective and convergence under approximate/stale matchings.
- Either derive or cite finite-sample rates for empirical Wasserstein to contextualize the N dependence.
- Clarify hyper-parameter sensitivity beyond λ; include ablations over N, |B|, matching frequency, and base metric.

Pros
- Clear exposition; simple idea with intuitive training heuristic.
- Demonstrates practical gains over MMD in some settings and smaller-batch training.
- Bridges OT-based discrepancy with deep learning applications.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to established OT literature; missing key citations.
- Theoretical claims about gradients/optimization are heuristic, not rigorous.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow (mostly vs MMD), with modest improvements and blurry samples.
- Scalability concerns not fully addressed.

Overall assessment
While the paper is well written and the idea is reasonable, the contribution is incremental relative to known empirical OT, and the empirical validation against established baselines is insufficient. The optimization treatment lacks rigor. Given NeurIPS standards and the availability of stronger, well-tested OT-based alternatives, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
