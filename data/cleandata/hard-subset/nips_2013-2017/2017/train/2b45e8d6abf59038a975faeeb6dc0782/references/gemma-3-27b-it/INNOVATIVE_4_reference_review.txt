This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, positioning it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling. The core idea – formulating the distance as a minimum weight bipartite matching problem – is novel and conceptually elegant. It directly addresses some known drawbacks of MMD, such as the need for bandwidth tuning and potential gradient saturation. The theoretical grounding, establishing PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, is a significant strength.

The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear, and the motivations are well-explained. The inclusion of pseudocode and a graphical illustration of the learning process enhances clarity. The empirical evaluation, encompassing domain adaptation and generative modeling, provides a reasonable starting point for assessing the practical benefits of PMD.

However, despite the novelty and theoretical appeal, certain aspects leave room for improvement. While the authors demonstrate improvements over MMD in their experiments, the gains aren’t overwhelmingly substantial. The primary advantage highlighted – the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes – is valuable but perhaps not revolutionary. The discussion around the L1 distance's appropriateness for pixel-level image generation is a crucial self-criticism, acknowledging a limitation of their current approach. A deeper investigation into alternative distance metrics within the PMD framework would strengthen the work. Furthermore, the GPU acceleration aspect is mentioned but not implemented or thoroughly evaluated; demonstrating actual speedups on a GPU would be compelling. The paper also lacks a more in-depth comparison to other Wasserstein distance estimators, like those based on dual formulations, as mentioned in the related work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The PMD formulation offers a fresh perspective on estimating Wasserstein distance, moving beyond kernel-based methods.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The proof of strong consistency provides a solid foundation for the method.
*   **Addresses MMD Limitations:** PMD directly tackles the bandwidth tuning and gradient issues associated with MMD.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrated improvements in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** The performance gains over MMD, while present, aren't dramatic.
*   **Distance Metric Limitation:**  The use of L1 distance for image generation is acknowledged as suboptimal.
*   **Lack of GPU Implementation:** The potential for GPU acceleration is mentioned but not demonstrated.
*   **Limited Comparison to Alternatives:** A more thorough comparison to other Wasserstein estimators is missing.
*   **Experiment Scope:** Experiments are somewhat limited in scope; exploring other applications could strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The core idea is genuinely novel and has the potential to influence future research on sample-based distance estimation.
*   The theoretical analysis provides a valuable guarantee on the method’s convergence.
*   Even modest performance improvements, coupled with the advantages of bandwidth-free operation and stronger gradients, represent a worthwhile contribution.
*   The paper opens up a new research direction with potential for further development (e.g., exploring different distance metrics, GPU acceleration).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The performance gains are not substantial enough to represent a major breakthrough.
*   The reliance on L1 distance limits the applicability to certain domains.
*   The lack of a GPU implementation and comparison to other Wasserstein estimators weakens the empirical evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **acceptance** despite the identified weaknesses. The novelty of the PMD formulation and the associated theoretical guarantees are significant. While the empirical results aren’t earth-shattering, they demonstrate the potential of the method. The identified limitations represent opportunities for future research, rather than fundamental flaws. I believe this paper introduces a promising new direction and warrants inclusion in NIPS, even with some room for refinement. I would encourage the authors to address the weaknesses in future work, particularly exploring more appropriate distance metrics and implementing GPU acceleration.
