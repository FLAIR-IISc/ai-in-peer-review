- Paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based divergence equal to the minimum weight matching cost between populations and a consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein metric.  
- PMD is proposed as a training objective for deep learning with claimed advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients, and smaller usable mini-batches.  
- Theoretical strength: proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Practical strength: argues and demonstrates PMD yields non-saturating gradients, aiding training stability.  
- Empirical strength: experiments on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW) show better performance and faster convergence than MMD; ablations on population and batch size support claims.  
- Presentation strength: paper is well-written, with clear definitions, logical flow, and helpful figures.  
- Theoretical weakness: lacks finite-sample error bounds or convergence rates for PMD, limiting practical theoretical guidance.  
- Missing comparison: fails to compare PMD with WGAN despite discussing it, leaving out a key baseline for Wasserstein-based generative modeling.  
- Evaluation weakness: generative modeling results are qualitative only, missing standard quantitative metrics like FID or Inception Score.  
- Computational concern: mentions O(N^3) Hungarian algorithm without sufficient empirical evidence on runtime scaling for large N (e.g., 1000–2000).  
- Narrow baselines: compares only to MMD and omits other divergences (e.g., Cramer GAN, Energy Distance), limiting contextual evaluation.  
- Critical assessment: core idea of avoiding MMD bandwidth is compelling, but lack of finite-sample analysis and omitted comparisons weaken the theoretical and empirical case.  
- Experimental gap: claims about PMD generating better images need quantitative validation and direct comparison to WGAN; supplementary visualizations are insufficient.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions, contingent on addressing finite-sample bounds/discussion, adding WGAN comparison, including quantitative generative metrics, and expanding empirical complexity analysis.  
- Final verdict: technically sound and valuable contribution that should be accepted after the suggested minor revisions.
