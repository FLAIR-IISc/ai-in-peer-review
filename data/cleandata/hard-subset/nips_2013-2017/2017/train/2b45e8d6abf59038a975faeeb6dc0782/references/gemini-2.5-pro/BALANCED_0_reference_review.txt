**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based statistical divergence for comparing probability distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two sets of samples (populations) drawn from the respective distributions. The authors establish a strong theoretical connection, showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). A key contribution is a practical algorithm for using PMD as a differentiable training objective in deep learning models, leveraging the insight that the optimal matching can be treated as fixed during infinitesimal parameter updates. This allows for an efficient stochastic optimization scheme where a matching is computed on a large population, followed by several SGD updates on smaller mini-batches sampled from that population. The paper positions PMD as an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD has fewer hyperparameters (no kernel bandwidth), provides stronger and more stable gradients, and allows for more efficient training with smaller mini-batch sizes. The effectiveness of PMD is demonstrated on two challenging deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, where it is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD in terms of both final performance and convergence speed.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposal to use the minimum weight matching cost directly as a differentiable loss for training deep neural networks is both elegant and novel. While optimal transport and the Wasserstein distance have seen increasing use in machine learning (e.g., WGAN), this paper presents a distinct, parameter-free, and direct approach to optimizing a primal formulation of the W1 distance on empirical samples. This provides a valuable new tool for the many deep learning tasks that rely on comparing distributions.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness:** The work is well-grounded in theory. The connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is clearly established through classical results in optimal transport and probability theory. Proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 lends significant credibility to the proposed metric, distinguishing it from more heuristic-based divergence measures.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the PMD method and its associated learning algorithm are explained precisely with helpful pseudocode and illustrations, and the theoretical analysis is presented logically. The empirical results are well-organized and support the paper's main claims effectively.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive and convincing. The authors evaluate PMD on two distinct and relevant applications, providing a strong head-to-head comparison against MMD, a very relevant and widely used baseline. The inclusion of insightful ablation studies on the impact of population size, mini-batch size, and the choice of matching algorithm (exact vs. approximate) strengthens the paper's practical claims. Furthermore, the empirical validation of the estimator's consistency and the analysis of gradient magnitudes provide concrete evidence for the theoretical advantages discussed.

5.  **Practical Advantages:** The paper convincingly demonstrates several practical benefits over MMD. The elimination of the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter is a significant advantage, simplifying model tuning. The analysis suggesting that PMD yields stronger, non-saturating gradients is compelling and is supported by empirical evidence, offering a potential solution to training difficulties observed with MMD.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The primary weakness of PMD is the computational cost of the matching algorithm, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm and O(N^2) for the approximation used. This is substantially more expensive than MMD, which is O(N^2) but with a smaller constant factor and simpler implementation. While the authors rightly argue that this cost can be amortized over several SGD updates, it still presents a scalability bottleneck as the required population size N grows. The paper mentions the possibility of GPU parallelization for matching algorithms but provides no experiments or concrete analysis, leaving the true scalability of the method as an open question. A more in-depth discussion of this limitation and potential paths to mitigate it (e.g., via modern, faster optimal transport solvers) would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Lack of Finite-Sample Analysis:** While the asymptotic consistency (as N → ∞) is a valuable theoretical result, the paper lacks a non-asymptotic analysis. A finite-sample error bound quantifying the deviation between PMD and the true W1 distance for a given population size N would provide much stronger theoretical guarantees, akin to those available for MMD. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but its absence is a notable limitation.

3.  **Comparison with Other Wasserstein-based Methods:** The discussion comparing PMD to Wasserstein GANs (WGAN) is brief. Given that WGAN is a highly influential method also based on the Wasserstein distance, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs would be beneficial. For instance, comparing the computational cost and stability of PMD against the need for a critic network and the associated training challenges in WGANs (e.g., enforcing the Lipschitz constraint) would better contextualize the contribution. Citing and discussing other primal-form estimators, such as the one proposed in Bellemare et al. (2017) for the Cramér distance (related to W1), would also be appropriate.

4.  **Clarification on Experimental Protocol:** In the generative modeling experiments (Sec 5.2), PMD models were trained for 100 epochs while MMD models were trained for 500 epochs. This discrepancy makes a direct comparison of the final generated image quality difficult. To demonstrate faster convergence, it would be more convincing to show results for both methods at various checkpoints (e.g., after 100 epochs, 200 epochs, etc.) or to compare results after a fixed wall-clock training time. Please clarify the reasoning behind this experimental choice.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel, theoretically-grounded, and parameter-free metric for comparing distributions.
*   Provides a practical and effective algorithm for its use as a loss function in deep learning.
*   Demonstrates clear advantages over a strong and popular baseline (MMD) across multiple dimensions (hyperparameter tuning, gradient properties, convergence speed).
*   The contribution is significant and likely to be impactful for researchers and practitioners in areas like generative modeling and domain adaptation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational cost of the matching step is a significant concern for scalability.
*   The theoretical analysis, while sound, could be strengthened with finite-sample bounds.
*   The experimental comparison in the generative modeling section could be made more direct and fair.

**Decision:**
Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and well-motivated contribution. The strengths in originality, theoretical grounding, and empirical validation substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The proposed PMD is a valuable addition to the machine learning toolbox. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors address the points raised above—particularly clarifying the experimental protocol and expanding the discussion on computational scalability and related work—in the final version of the paper.
