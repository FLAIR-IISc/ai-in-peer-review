Summary
The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-cost one-to-one matching between two i.i.d. sample sets from distributions pX and pY under a ground metric d. PMD is exactly the empirical optimal transport (OT) cost (Earth Mover’s Distance) for uniform empirical measures and thus a finite-sample estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance. The authors show PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1 by invoking Varadarajan’s theorem (weak convergence of empirical measures) and stability of optimal transport. They propose a training procedure that periodically computes a matching on a “population” of size N, and performs several SGD updates while holding the matching fixed; for efficiency, they use an O(N^2) approximate matching algorithm or O(N^3) Hungarian matching, and allow smaller SGD minibatches |B| < N by sampling matched pairs. They argue PMD offers practical advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients (especially for outliers), and the ability to use smaller minibatches in the inner loop. Applications are shown in unsupervised domain adaptation (Office dataset with VGG features) and implicit generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW), where PMD is compared primarily to MMD-based methods.

Relation to prior work
- Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) is a widely used IPM; the paper positions PMD as an alternative IPM with different computational/statistical tradeoffs.
- Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) approximates the dual form with a learned critic; the paper contrasts PMD as primal, parameter-free, and with a computable scale.
- Empirical OT as a distance between discrete empirical measures is classical; strong consistency of empirical OT estimators is well established.
- A major omission is entropic-regularized OT and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013), which offer O(N^2) iterative algorithms with GPU acceleration and differentiability through the transport plan, and have been used in ML for domain adaptation and generative modeling. This is highly relevant for both algorithmic efficiency and differentiability.
- For MMD, linear-time unbiased estimators exist, enabling smaller minibatches; this is not discussed.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: PMD is a direct, simple objective with a clean link to W1. The theoretical consistency result follows standard OT results and is correctly framed.
- Practical training recipe: decoupling matching (outer loop) and SGD updates (inner loop) is sensible and easy to implement; subsampling matched pairs gives an unbiased gradient estimator of the fixed-matching objective.
- Empirical gradients: the analysis and histogram suggest PMD yields more uniformly strong per-sample gradients than RBF-MMD in the tested setting.
- Experiments: PMD matches or surpasses MMD on Office domain adaptation and qualitatively improves GMMN-like generation, with convergence speed analyses and timing breakdowns. The observation that smaller |B| improves PMD is useful.

Weaknesses
- Novelty: PMD is essentially the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance/EMD between two equal-size uniform empirical measures. Using empirical OT as a training loss has prior art across ML (e.g., OT for domain adaptation, deep learning with EMD-like losses). The main algorithmic idea—holding the matching fixed across several SGD steps—is practical but incremental.
- Missing related work: The absence of entropic OT/Sinkhorn (and subsequent differentiable OT literature) is a significant gap. These methods directly address the computational bottleneck, allow full-batch or minibatch differentiable approximations, and are widely adopted. Comparisons would be appropriate and likely stronger than MMD on both tasks.
- Claims about minibatches for MMD: The statement that MMD requires large batches because it compares all pairs is incomplete. Linear-time unbiased MMD estimators and random feature approximations enable smaller batches and faster training; this should be acknowledged and compared.
- Evaluation breadth: In domain adaptation, only MMD is considered; methods like DANN, JAN, CMD, and OT-based domain adaptation are relevant baselines. Improvements are modest (2/6 transfers) and sometimes comparable to MMD. For generative modeling, no quantitative metrics (FID, Inception score) are provided and comparisons to WGAN/WGAN-GP, DCGAN, or Sinkhorn-based OT are absent. Qualitative results show blurriness with pixel L1, as noted by the authors.
- Theoretical scope: The “strong consistency” result adds little beyond known OT convergence. No finite-sample rates or high-dimensional sample complexity discussion (where W1 convergence is slow) are provided beyond a toy empirical rate (≈N^-0.23 consistent with N^-1/d scaling).
- Differentiability/optimization: Gradients ignore the dependence of the optimal matching on parameters; while envelope theorem implies correctness almost everywhere, this non-smoothness and the “stale” matching in the inner loop are heuristics without convergence guarantees. This is fine in practice but should be discussed more carefully.

Quality
- Technical soundness: Core definitions and the consistency argument are correct. The training algorithm is reasonable, but formal guarantees are limited. Some claims (minibatch requirement for MMD) are overstated given known estimators.
- Empirical evidence: Adequate for showing PMD vs MMD, but limited baselines and metrics restrict conclusions about broader utility.
- Reproducibility: Implementation details are given, but no code link is provided.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. Minor typos remain. The relationship to classical empirical OT could be emphasized earlier, and the limitations and missing baselines could be discussed.

Originality
- Low-to-moderate. The method is a straightforward application of empirical OT/EMD as a training loss with a practical SGD schedule.

Significance
- Moderate within the niche of sample-based divergence objectives; practical insights could be useful to practitioners. Without broader comparisons, impact is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include and compare to entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) as a differentiable, GPU-friendly alternative; discuss its regularization bias and tradeoffs.
- Compare to linear-time MMD estimators and random-feature MMD.
- For domain adaptation, add DANN/JAN/CMD and OT-based baselines; for generation, report FID/IS and include WGAN/WGAN-GP and Sinkhorn OT baselines.
- Discuss sample complexity in high dimensions and the impact on required N.
- Provide a more formal justification of the fixed-matching inner-loop updates or empirical ablations on matching refresh frequency.
- Release code.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, principled objective directly tied to W1; practical algorithm; useful empirical observations about gradients and minibatch usage; improvements over MMD in tested settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to known empirical OT; missing crucial related work and strong baselines; evaluation lacks standard quantitative metrics; some claims about MMD are incomplete.
