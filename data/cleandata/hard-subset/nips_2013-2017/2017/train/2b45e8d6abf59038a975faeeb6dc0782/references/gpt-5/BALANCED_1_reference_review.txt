Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the minimum-cost bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets under a ground metric (e.g., L1), averaged over pairs. The authors show that PMD coincides with the empirical 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between the empirical measures and is a strongly consistent estimator of the true W1 distance, via Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport. They propose to use PMD as a training loss for deep models by (i) computing a matching M* on a population of N samples from each distribution; (ii) updating parameters by backpropagating through the distances but not through the discrete matching; and (iii) optionally using smaller mini-batches drawn from the matched pairs over several SGD steps before recomputing M*. They argue PMD has advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients (no saturation), and ability to use smaller mini-batches than the “population size”. They evaluate on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW), showing faster convergence and some performance gains over MMD.

This work sits at the intersection of kernel two-sample testing (MMD) and optimal transport (OT). The core construction—empirical W1 via minimum-cost matching between two equally sized samples—is standard in OT (e.g., Rubner et al., Villani). The idea of using OT distances as learning objectives is well-studied: WGAN uses the dual with a learned 1-Lipschitz critic; entropic OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) provide fast, differentiable approximations to OT and have been applied in deep learning; OT-based domain adaptation methods (e.g., Courty et al., NIPS 2014; JDOT) align distributions via OT. The paper compares primarily with MMD and mentions WGAN but does not situate PMD against entropic OT/Sinkhorn or OT-based domain adaptation, which are highly relevant.

Strengths
- Clear, simple objective: PMD is a familiar, interpretable quantity (empirical W1) with straightforward gradients when the matching is fixed.
- Consistency: The proof that empirical PMD converges to true W1 follows standard results; the empirical rate experiment is reasonable and matches intuition.
- Practical training heuristic: Reusing the matching for several SGD steps and subsampling matched pairs is simple to implement and empirically effective.
- Empirical evidence: PMD shows competitive or better results than MMD on Office in two transfers and converges faster; qualitative samples on SVHN/LFW appear less noisy than MMD.
- Useful analysis: The gradient magnitude comparison between PMD and MMD highlights a practical optimization issue with RBF MMD.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: PMD is essentially the empirical W1 with assignment. The theoretical result is known in OT; no new bounds or analysis (e.g., finite-sample rates) are provided.
- Missing and insufficient related work: No discussion or comparison with entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances (fast, differentiable, GPU-friendly), sliced Wasserstein methods, or OT-based domain adaptation (e.g., Courty et al.). This omission weakens both positioning and claims about computational advantages and applicability.
- Optimization claims need tightening: The paper states an “unbiased stochastic gradient” while ignoring the dependence of M* on parameters. A more careful statement via Danskin’s theorem/subgradients is needed, plus a discussion of bias introduced by reusing M* and subsampling. No convergence guarantees are provided for the “inner-loop” heuristic.
- Computational scalability: Matching is O(N^3) (Hungarian) or O(N^2) approximate per population; pairwise distance computation is O(N^2D). For modern batch sizes and high-D features, this can dominate training unless GPU-accelerated. The paper’s CPU implementation and small N limit the generality of the speed claims; memory requirements for O(N^2) distances are not discussed.
- Experimental scope and baselines: 
  - Domain adaptation compares only to MMD; stronger baselines like DANN, CORAL/Deep CORAL, CMD, JAN (cited), and OT-based methods are missing. Validation uses 100 labeled target samples; while common, this should be clearly discussed to ensure fair comparison.
  - Generative modeling lacks quantitative metrics (e.g., FID/IS, two-sample tests) and comparisons to WGAN or energy/Cramér distances. Images are blurry in pixel space, suggesting sensitivity to ground metric and representation.
- Claims about “no hyper-parameter”: Choice of ground metric and representation space are important hyper-parameters; sensitivity is only partially explored.
- Clarity/precision: A few typos and notational inconsistencies; the distinction between “population size” and “mini-batch size” is confusing at first read.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound insofar as PMD equals empirical W1 and the training heuristic is reasonable. However, theoretical contributions are minimal; key optimization claims are imprecise; and computational trade-offs are under-examined.
- Clarity: Generally readable with helpful figures, but needs more precise statements (Danskin/subgradients), clearer algorithmic description (complexity, memory, GPU), and better positioning.
- Originality: Modest. The main idea is known; the contribution is to promote it as a practical deep learning loss with a simple training heuristic.
- Significance: Potentially useful as a practical alternative to MMD in some regimes, but without comparisons to modern OT approximations and stronger baselines, it is hard to assess broader impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, interpretable objective; straightforward to implement.
- Empirical improvements over MMD and faster convergence in studied tasks.
- Insightful gradient analysis illustrating an advantage over RBF MMD.

Cons for acceptance
- Conceptually incremental; theoretical results are standard.
- Missing key related work and baselines (Sinkhorn OT, OT-based DA, WGAN), undermining significance claims.
- Optimization theory and computational scalability insufficiently addressed.
- Generative results lack quantitative evaluation; domain adaptation comparisons are limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Position PMD against entropic OT/Sinkhorn (Cuturi 2013) and sliced Wasserstein; include comparisons and discuss differentiability, speed, and stability. Consider Sinkhorn or unbalanced OT to reduce cost and enable larger batches.
- Tighten optimization claims using Danskin’s theorem; clarify that gradients are subgradients; analyze bias from reusing/subsampling matches; provide empirical ablations on how often to refresh M*.
- Expand baselines: DANN, Deep CORAL, CMD, JAN, OT-based DA (e.g., Courty et al.) for domain adaptation; WGAN/Cramér/energy distance for generative modeling.
- Report quantitative generative metrics (FID/IS), sample diversity, and robustness to ground metric/feature space; include CIFAR-10 or higher-res benchmarks.
- Discuss and, if possible, implement GPU-accelerated matching and distance computation; report wall-clock comparisons at realistic batch sizes and dimensions.
- Add references on finite-sample rates for empirical Wasserstein (e.g., Fournier & Guillin) and OT for DA.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The paper is clear and practical, but the novelty over known empirical OT is limited, key related work is missing, and the experimental comparisons and theoretical precision are not yet at the NeurIPS bar. With the suggested additions and stronger positioning, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
