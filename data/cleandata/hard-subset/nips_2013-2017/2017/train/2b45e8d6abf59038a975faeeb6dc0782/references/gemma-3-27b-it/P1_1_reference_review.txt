## Peer Review: Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, termed Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD). The authors convincingly argue for its advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), specifically highlighting the lack of bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and the ability to utilize smaller mini-batch sizes. The empirical evaluation on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks demonstrates the potential of PMD. While the theoretical results are relatively straightforward, the practical advantages and experimental validation are compelling. I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance, with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of estimating statistical divergence between probability distributions given only samples, a common scenario in deep learning applications like generative modeling and domain adaptation. Existing methods, such as MMD, suffer from limitations like bandwidth parameter tuning and gradient issues. PMD offers an alternative by directly estimating the Wasserstein metric through a minimum weight matching problem between samples.  The work builds on the optimal transport theory (Wasserstein metric) and connects it to the practical implementation of estimating the distance with samples. The authors rightly position PMD as an alternative to MMD, specifically referencing its limitations. They also acknowledge related work on estimating Wasserstein distances directly from samples, differentiating PMD by focusing on a primal problem approach without requiring a separate critic network as in WGANs.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PMD introduces a conceptually simple yet effective approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric using a min-cost matching formulation.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear and concise proof establishing the strong consistency of PMD as an estimator for the first Wasserstein metric.
* **Practical Advantages:** The arguments for PMD’s advantages—no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch size—are well-motivated and intuitively appealing.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate PMD's superior performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.  The results on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW provide a good range of tests.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms.
* **Code availability:** (Not explicitly stated, but it would significantly strengthen the paper if code were made available).

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Depth:** While the consistency proof is solid, a deeper theoretical analysis exploring the convergence rate or providing more nuanced bounds on the approximation error would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors acknowledge the O(N^3) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm, a more detailed discussion of the scalability of PMD to very high-dimensional datasets or extremely large sample sizes would be helpful.  The mention of GPU parallelization is good, but details on potential speedups would be beneficial.
* **L1 Distance Choice:**  The exclusive use of the L1 distance is somewhat limiting.  While justified by the gradient properties, exploring other distance metrics and their impact on performance would add to the robustness of the work.
* **Generative Modeling Results:** The generated images from PMD on SVHN and LFW are described as blurry. The authors briefly mention that the L1 distance may not be ideal for natural images but don’t explore potential solutions (e.g., using a learned distance metric or working in a different feature space).
* **Limited Ablation Studies:**  More comprehensive ablation studies, for example, varying the mini-batch size more systematically, would further solidify the claims about PMD’s robustness.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper is technically sound, with a clear theoretical justification and strong empirical results. The connection to Wasserstein distance provides a solid foundation.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The explanations of the key concepts are clear and concise.
* **Originality (7/10):** While the idea of using matching to estimate distance isn’t entirely novel, the specific application to the Wasserstein metric and the focus on the advantages over MMD are original contributions.
* **Significance (8/10):** PMD offers a practical alternative to MMD with several advantages that could make it a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners in various deep learning applications. The potential for faster convergence and improved performance is significant.


**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Provides a novel and theoretically grounded approach to estimating Wasserstein distance.
* Demonstrates practical advantages over MMD in terms of tuning, gradients, and mini-batch size.
* Empirically validates the effectiveness of PMD on relevant tasks.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Theoretical analysis could be more in-depth.
* Scalability concerns with large datasets need further discussion.
* Limited exploration of alternative distance metrics.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS 2017.  The work is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing a more detailed discussion of scalability and exploring alternative distance metrics, would further strengthen the paper.  I also strongly encourage the authors to release their code.
