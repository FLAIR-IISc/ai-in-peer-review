- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): empirical 1-Wasserstein via minimum-weight bipartite matching between two sample populations.  
- Proves strong consistency using standard weak convergence and OT stability results.  
- Claims PMD is a differentiable training objective by treating the discrete matching as locally constant almost everywhere.  
- Introduces a practical training recipe: compute matching occasionally on large populations and “freeze” it while using smaller mini-batches for inner SGD.  
- Empirically compares PMD to MMD on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW), claiming fewer hyperparameters and stronger/non-vanishing gradients.  
- Omits key related work: entropic regularized OT/Sinkhorn, stochastic/mini-batch OT, OT for domain adaptation, sliced Wasserstein, and WGAN-GP.  
- Theoretical contribution is limited: consistency is straightforward and no finite-sample bounds or new analyses are provided.  
- The claim of an “unbiased” gradient ignoring M*’s dependence on parameters is not rigorously justified; it is effectively a subgradient almost everywhere.  
- The inner-loop frozen-matching scheme introduces bias and lacks convergence guarantees.  
- Exact matching is computationally expensive (O(N^3)); approximate matching reduces cost but still requires O(N^2) distance computation and memory.  
- Scalability and GPU practicality for large N and high-dimensional data are not thoroughly evaluated.  
- No empirical comparison to Sinkhorn OT, which is typically faster, differentiable, and GPU-accelerated.  
- Domain adaptation experiments are limited: only compared to MMD, use fixed ImageNet features, show modest gains, and use 100 labeled target samples for validation (departing from unsupervised DA).  
- Generative modeling evaluation is mostly qualitative, lacks FID/IS metrics, and omits WGAN-GP and Sinkhorn baselines; training budgets differ between methods.  
- Ablation studies are interesting but incomplete; more rigorous tests on ground metric, dimension, population size, and runtime are needed.  
- Paper is generally readable and well organized, but key implementation details (non-differentiability treatment, bias from frozen matching, complexity/GPU details) are insufficiently explained.  
- Novelty is limited: PMD is the classical empirical Wasserstein-1; contributions are empirical insights and a practical training heuristic.  
- Significance is constrained: PMD may be useful in small-to-medium batch regimes, but impact is limited without stronger baselines, theoretical guarantees, and broader experiments.  
- Pros: clear formulation, simple implementation, useful empirical observation about stronger gradients vs RBF-MMD, and standard consistency result.  
- Cons: limited novelty, missing core OT literature and baselines, unsupported claims about unbiased gradients and frozen-matching, limited experimental scope, and scalability concerns.  
- Suggestions: compare to Sinkhorn/sliced OT and WGAN-GP, clarify subgradient justification and frozen-matching convergence, avoid labeled-target validation for DA, report FID/IS for generative models, and discuss GPU/memory implementation.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject — well written with useful insights but lacks theoretical novelty, important baselines, and convincing experiments for acceptance.
