This paper proposes the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric, for use as a training objective in deep learning. The PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from two distributions. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein-1 distance. A stochastic gradient-based learning algorithm is proposed, and PMD is contrasted with the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors claim PMD has advantages over MMD, including having no kernel hyperparameter, providing stronger gradients, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes during optimization. The method is evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, where it is shown to be competitive with or outperform MMD-based methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Simplicity:** The core idea of using the minimum weight matching cost as a discrepancy measure for training deep networks is simple, intuitive, and, to my knowledge, novel in this context. It provides a direct, parameter-free method for approximating the Wasserstein-1 distance, contrasting with other approaches like WGAN that require a separate, parameterized critic network.
*   **Practical Advantages over MMD:** The paper makes a compelling case for some practical benefits of PMD over MMD. The elimination of the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter is a significant advantage, as tuning this parameter can be difficult and crucial for MMD's performance. The argument that PMD (with L1 distance) provides non-saturating gradients is well-articulated and supported by empirical evidence (Fig. 2(b)), which could lead to more stable and faster training.
*   **Novel Training Scheme:** The proposed optimization strategy—computing the matching on a large "population" (N) and then performing multiple SGD updates on smaller mini-batches (|B|)—is an interesting heuristic that leverages the structure of the PMD objective. The empirical results in Fig. 5(a) suggest this is an effective strategy.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Technical Rigor:**
    1.  The derivation of the gradient in Equation (3) hinges on the critical assumption that the optimal matching M* does not change for an infinitesimal change in the parameters θ. While the paper claims this holds due to continuity, this is a non-trivial statement that lacks formal justification. The gradient of a minimum of functions is generally a subgradient, and the conditions under which the interchange of gradient and `min` is valid should be formally stated and discussed.
    2.  The proof of the main theoretical result, Theorem 3.3 (Strong Consistency), is entirely omitted and described as "straightforward". This is insufficient for a rigorous scientific paper. A proof sketch, at minimum, should be provided in the main text or an appendix to substantiate this central claim.
    3.  The empirical analysis in Section 5.4 shows the approximation error scales with N⁻⁰.²³, which is considerably slower than the standard Monte Carlo rate of N⁻⁰.⁵. This slow convergence of the estimator is a significant limitation but is not discussed or analyzed by the authors.

*   **Clarity and Framing:** The argument in Section 3.2 regarding "Smaller mini-batch size" is presented in a confusing manner. The paper compares its specific training algorithm for PMD (large population N, small update batch |B|) to a standard MMD algorithm where the batch size for updates is the same as the population size. This is a comparison of two different optimization schemes, not an inherent property of the metrics themselves. The terminology is also inconsistent with MMD literature, where "mini-batch size" is the term for what this paper calls "population size" (N). This framing could be misleading.

*   **Underwhelming Experimental Results:**
    1.  In the domain adaptation experiments (Table 1), the performance improvements over MMD are marginal. Describing gains of 2-4% as outperforming MMD "by a large margin" is an overstatement. On four of the six tasks, the methods perform comparably.
    2.  In the generative modeling task, the images produced by PMD (Fig. 4) are noticeably blurry. The authors acknowledge this, attributing it to the use of pixel-wise L1 distance. While a plausible explanation, it represents a significant qualitative failure for this application and suggests that the proposed PMD setup is not well-suited for generating sharp natural images, a key goal in this area.

*   **Poor Presentation and Attention to Detail:** The manuscript suffers from a notable lack of polish, which detracts from the overall quality and confidence in the work.
    1.  **Formatting:** The conference identifier on the first page is incorrect ("st Conference" instead of "31st Conference"). The asterisk for the corresponding author is misplaced.
    2.  **Bibliography:** The reference list is in an unacceptable state for a NIPS submission. Numerous entries are incomplete, missing critical information such as the publication venue, year, or page numbers (e.g., [1], [22], [46], [51]). For example, the seminal Adam paper [22] is cited as `Diederik Kingma, Jimmy Ba, , ,`. This level of carelessness is concerning.

**Summary of pros and cons for acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, parameter-free, and intuitive discrepancy measure for training deep networks. The analysis of its gradient properties compared to MMD is insightful.
*   **Con:** The work suffers from a lack of theoretical rigor in key areas (gradient derivation, consistency proof). The experimental validation is not compelling enough to overlook these theoretical gaps, with marginal improvements in one task and poor qualitative results in another. Finally, the paper is marred by significant and numerous presentation and formatting errors.

**Recommendation:**

While the core idea of PMD is appealing and possesses some clear practical merits, the paper in its current form does not meet the high standards of NIPS. The combination of unaddressed technical assumptions, omitted proofs, mixed experimental results, and a strikingly careless presentation leads me to recommend **rejection**. The authors have a promising direction, but the work requires substantial revision to strengthen the theoretical foundations, improve the experimental validation (perhaps by exploring different ground distances `d` for generative modeling), and meticulously correct the numerous presentation flaws.
