Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the average cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. samples from distributions pX and pY with ground cost d. This is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance between the two empirical measures with uniform weights. The authors use PMD as a training objective for deep models, arguing advantages over MMD: no bandwidth, stronger gradients, and the ability to use small minibatches by freezing the matching across several parameter updates. They show strong consistency of PMD for W1 via Varadarajan’s theorem and stability of optimal transport, provide an approximate matching algorithm for scalability, and present empirical results on domain adaptation (Office) and deep generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW).

This connects to a large literature on optimal transport (Villani, 2009), empirical OT/EMD in vision and IR, and more recent deep learning with Wasserstein distances (e.g., WGAN). Crucially, related work on entropic regularization and Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013), which enables differentiable, GPU-friendly approximations to OT in O(N^2 log N) and has been widely used in learning, is not discussed. The relationship to energy distance/Cramér distance (Bellemare et al., 2017) and unbiased/linear-time MMD estimators is also relevant to the claimed MMD disadvantages.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of using empirical W1 (via minimum matching) as a sample-based, parameter-free alternative to MMD, with intuitive gradient properties (L1 ground cost).
- Theoretical consistency follows cleanly from standard OT results; the empirical rate observed (~N^-0.23 in 5D) aligns with known N^-1/d rates.
- Practical SGD procedure that reuses a fixed matching for several steps, enabling smaller inner minibatches and reducing compute.
- Empirical comparisons indicate faster convergence and sometimes better target accuracy on Office, and qualitatively less noisy samples than MMD on generative tasks.
- Useful empirical observations: MMD sensitivity to σ and batch size; PMD’s robustness to N and benefit from smaller |B|.

Weaknesses and concerns
Novelty/positioning
- PMD is simply the empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD between uniform empirical measures. Rebranding it as a new “discrepancy” obscures this and underplays substantial prior art (EMD in vision/IR; OT in ML). The core theoretical statement (consistency) is classical; no new analysis (e.g., sample complexity rates, convergence of the proposed SGD scheme) is provided.

Computation and differentiability
- Computing exact/approximate matchings plus an O(N^2) distance matrix is expensive. The paper argues it is acceptable relative to backprop, but scalability beyond N≈1–4k is unclear. There is no comparison to entropic regularized OT (Sinkhorn), which is differentiable, GPU-accelerated, and has become standard for learning with W1-like objectives. This omission weakens both methodological and empirical contributions.
- The gradient treatment ignores the dependence of the argmin matching on parameters (“does not propagate through the matching”). This can be justified by Danskin’s theorem/envelope theorem under uniqueness/nonsingularity, but no formal statement or conditions are provided. In practice, matchings can change abruptly, creating non-smooth objectives; the “frozen matching for K steps” heuristic introduces bias with no convergence analysis or ablation on K.

Claims about MMD
- The claim that MMD requires large minibatches and that the minibatch size must equal the “population size” is overstated. Unbiased, linear-time MMD/energy distance estimators (e.g., U-statistics, block estimates, Random Fourier Features) allow small minibatches and stable gradients. Multi-scale kernels mitigate bandwidth sensitivity and gradient saturation. The paper uses multi-kernel MMD in some settings but still frames the comparison as categorical.
- A more appropriate baseline for “stronger gradients” is the energy/Cramér distance (cited), which was proposed to fix biased/vanishing gradient issues of Wasserstein GAN and provide unbiased, well-conditioned gradients; it is not evaluated here.

Experimental evaluation
- Domain adaptation: Only MMD is compared; stronger baselines (DANN, JAN, CMD) are missing, and the setting uses fixed VGG features rather than end-to-end convolutional encoders, which limits significance. It’s unclear how λ and σ were tuned relative to PMD’s implicit choices (e.g., ground distance scaling), and whether numerical gains are statistically significant across all shifts.
- Generative modeling: Evaluation is qualitative; no standard metrics (Inception score, FID) are reported. Training schedules differ (100 epochs for PMD vs 500 for MMD), which confounds fairness. Pixel-space L1 is known to produce blur, acknowledged by the authors; using feature-space distances (“perceptual” features) would be more informative. Comparisons to GAN/WGAN are absent.
- Efficiency: Matching is implemented on CPU; wall-clock comparisons to MMD are not standardized, and the per-epoch breakdown shows matching dominates in some regimes. No experiments with larger batch sizes or higher-dimensional data to stress scalability.

Clarity and related work
- The paper is generally clear but misses key related work: Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013), differentiability of OT plans, sliced Wasserstein distances, and linear-time MMD/energy distance estimators. A brief discussion would help position PMD and guide practitioners.
- The “parameter-free” claim is too strong: choice of ground metric (L1 vs L2), its scaling, and feature space are impactful hyperparameters.

Questions for the authors
- Please compare to entropic regularized OT (Sinkhorn) as a training loss: speed, stability, and accuracy, and discuss epsilon’s effect vs your “parameter-free” stance.
- Can you justify the “no gradient through matching” with a formal statement (e.g., Danskin) and report sensitivity to the number of inner steps with a frozen matching?
- Can you provide results for domain adaptation against DANN/JAN/CMD and for generation against GAN/WGAN, with standard metrics and matched wall-clock budgets?
- How does PMD behave with higher-dimensional data and larger N? Any GPU matching implementation?

Minor comments
- Be precise that PMD equals empirical W1/EMD with uniform weights; Eq. (6) is a standard transportation LP.
- The observed error rate in Fig. 2(a) matches known N^-1/d rates; citing known rate results would strengthen Section 3 (e.g., Fournier–Guillin).
- Typo/formatting issues throughout (e.g., subscripts/superscripts and spaces).

Assessment
- Quality: Mixed. Theoretical part is correct but standard; empirical results are promising but limited and missing baselines/metrics; some claims about MMD are overstated.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but related work coverage is incomplete.
- Originality: Limited; the main idea is a straightforward application of empirical W1/EMD. The training heuristic is practical but not deeply analyzed.
- Significance: Moderate if positioned as a practical recipe; currently limited by missing comparisons to established OT-based and adversarial methods.

Recommendation: Borderline reject. With stronger positioning as “using empirical W1 with a practical frozen-matching SGD,” inclusion of key OT baselines (Sinkhorn, energy distance), fairer and more comprehensive experiments, and a short analysis of the optimization heuristic, this could become a solid contribution.
