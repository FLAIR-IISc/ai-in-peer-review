- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator approximating the first Wasserstein metric via optimal matching between samples.
- Claims PMD is strongly consistent with solid theoretical grounding and clear connection to optimal transport theory.
- Argues PMD has advantages over MMD: no bandwidth tuning, stronger/non-saturating gradients, and suitability for smaller mini-batches.
- Empirical results show improved performance and faster convergence in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
- Paper is well-written, well-structured, and includes helpful pseudocode and illustrations.
- Uses L1 distance as the underlying metric, which limits performance on high-dimensional data (e.g., causes blurry image generation).
- Computational advantage is not convincingly demonstrated; matching remains a bottleneck and relies on a C++ implementation without GPU optimization.
- Experiments are limited in scope; broader datasets and tasks are needed to establish generalizability.
- Comparison to MMD highlights MMD sensitivity (bandwidth, regularization) but lacks a comparable sensitivity analysis for PMD’s own parameters.
- Recommends exploring alternative distance metrics (Euclidean or learned metrics) to improve image results.
- Suggests prioritizing GPU acceleration and providing detailed timing comparisons for the matching step.
- Calls for wider evaluation across datasets and tasks to strengthen empirical claims.
- Advises conducting a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for PMD on complex tasks.
- Suggests discussing challenges of PMD in very high-dimensional spaces (curse of dimensionality) and potential mitigations.
- Overall assessment: theoretically solid, empirically promising, with practical limitations; score 8/10 and recommendation to accept.
