This paper proposes the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric, for use in deep learning applications. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from two distributions. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein-1 metric. They propose an optimization procedure using stochastic gradient descent where a large population matching is computed, and then smaller mini-batches of matched pairs are used for gradient updates. The paper argues that PMD has several advantages over the commonly used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), namely having no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, providing stronger gradients, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes for optimization. The utility of PMD is demonstrated on unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, where it is shown to be competitive with or outperform MMD-based methods.

The core idea of using a direct, consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric as a differentiable loss is compelling. It provides a principled alternative to MMD and avoids the complexities of adversarial training required by methods like WGAN. However, the paper in its current form is marred by a significant number of flaws ranging from critical omissions in related work to a lack of technical rigor and unclear presentation of experimental results.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The formulation of a tractable, differentiable loss based on minimum weight matching for estimating the Wasserstein distance is a novel contribution in the context of training deep neural networks.
2.  **Reduced Hyperparameters:** The method successfully removes the need to tune a kernel bandwidth parameter, which is a notorious practical difficulty when using MMD. This is a significant practical advantage.
3.  **Gradient Properties:** The analysis of the non-saturating nature of the gradients when using an L1 ground metric is insightful. This potentially explains the observed faster convergence compared to MMD with an RBF kernel.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Critical Omission of Related Work:** The paper discusses Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) in Section 3.1 but provides an incorrect citation. The citation given, "[3]", points to a 2010 paper by Ben-David et al. on domain adaptation theory. The seminal WGAN paper (Arjovsky et al., 2017) is not cited anywhere in the manuscript. This is a major scholarly lapse that fundamentally undermines the paper's positioning within the current literature. A proper comparison and discussion of WGAN is essential for a paper on this topic.

2.  **Lack of Theoretical Rigor:**
    *   The justification for the gradient computation in Equation (3) is insufficient. The claim that the discrete matching M* remains constant for an infinitesimal change in θ relies on the envelope theorem (or Danskin's theorem), which is not mentioned, cited, or justified. For a paper presented at a top-tier conference, such a key step requires a more formal argument.
    *   The proof for the main theoretical result, Theorem 3.3 (Strong Consistency of PMD), is entirely omitted. The authors dismiss it as "straightforward by applying Proposition 3.1 and 3.2." This is unacceptable. The proof, or at least a detailed sketch, must be included in the paper or an appendix to allow for proper verification.
    *   The empirical analysis of the approximation error in Section 5.4 finds an error rate proportional to N⁻⁰.²³. The authors fail to connect this to the known theoretical literature on the convergence rate of the empirical Wasserstein distance, which is known to be slow and dimension-dependent (e.g., N⁻¹/d for dimension d ≥ 2). Contextualizing their empirical finding with existing theory would strengthen the analysis.

3.  **Clarity and Methodological Concerns:**
    *   The discussion of "population size N" versus "mini-batch size |B|" is confusing. In standard MMD literature (e.g., GMMN), the entire mini-batch (of size N) is used to compute the discrepancy. The authors’ claim that PMD allows for a smaller mini-batch size |B| < N is based on their specific optimization strategy of sub-sampling from a pre-computed matching. While this is a valid heuristic, the comparison to MMD is presented in a muddled way, making it difficult to assess the true computational trade-offs.
    *   The experimental result in Figure 5(b) is highly suspect. It claims that the final MMD value *worsens* as the population size N increases from 1000 to 6000. This contradicts the statistical properties of MMD, where a larger sample size should yield a more accurate estimate and, presumably, lead to a better-optimized model. The authors' explanation ("the SGD optimization converges slowly") does not adequately explain why the final performance is worse. This result suggests a potential flaw in the experimental protocol or a misunderstanding of the method's behavior.
    *   The generative modeling experiments (Section 5.2) use 100 epochs for PMD but 500 for MMD. This unequal footing complicates the comparison of the final generated image quality, making it difficult to conclude if one is definitively better than the other, or simply trained for a more appropriate duration.

4.  **Minor Issues:**
    *   The paper contains formatting inconsistencies. The conference information ("st Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems...") is unedited boilerplate text. The "Corresponding author" footnote is placed unusually.
    *   In Equation (3), the second equality relies on continuity of the transformations and the distance function, but the non-differentiability of the L1 norm at zero (which would provide the strong, non-saturating gradient) is not discussed.
    *   The claim that PMD is "parameter free" (Section 3.1) is an overstatement, as the choice of the ground distance d(·, ·) (e.g., L1 vs. L2) is itself a model choice that can impact performance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel and intuitive idea for a sample-based divergence metric.
*   The method has clear practical benefits, most notably the elimination of the MMD kernel bandwidth hyperparameter.
*   Empirical results show promise in terms of convergence speed.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   There is a critical failure to cite and properly discuss the highly relevant WGAN paper.
*   The paper lacks the technical rigor expected at NIPS, with key proofs and justifications being omitted or hand-waved.
*   Several experimental results and their interpretations are confusing or questionable (e.g., Figure 5(b)).
*   The clarity of the methodological comparison between PMD and MMD is poor.
*   The manuscript suffers from numerous minor formatting and presentation issues.

**Decision:**

While the core idea of this paper is interesting and potentially significant, the execution is deeply flawed. The combination of a critical citation error, a severe lack of theoretical rigor, and questionable experimental analysis leads me to recommend **rejection**. The paper is not ready for publication in its current state and requires a major revision to address these fundamental issues.
