### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions. The authors establish a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of it.

The primary contribution is a method for using this differentiable discrepancy as an objective for training deep neural networks. The proposed algorithm computes the optimal matching in an outer loop and then performs several stochastic gradient descent (SGD) updates in an inner loop using smaller mini-batches, assuming the matching remains locally constant. This approach is contrasted with the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors argue that PMD offers several advantages over MMD: it has no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter to tune, it provides stronger and more uniform gradients, and it allows for the use of smaller mini-batches for SGD updates, leading to faster convergence.

The effectiveness of PMD is demonstrated on two deep learning applications: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling. In both settings, empirical results suggest that PMD outperforms MMD in terms of final performance and convergence speed.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The idea of using the primal form of the Wasserstein-1 distance, instantiated as a minimum weight bipartite matching problem, as a direct, differentiable loss for training deep models is both simple and elegant. It provides a compelling alternative to kernel-based methods like MMD and critic-based methods like Wasserstein GAN (WGAN).

2.  **Practical Training Scheme:** The proposed training algorithm, which decouples the expensive matching computation from the frequent gradient updates, is a clever and practical solution to make an otherwise computationally intensive method viable. The ability to use small mini-batches (|B|) for SGD updates after a single large population (N) matching is a significant practical advantage.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides convincing experimental results on standard benchmarks for both domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW). The comparisons against MMD are direct and well-executed, clearly demonstrating PMD's advantages in convergence speed and performance. The ablation studies in Section 5.3 effectively support the claims about the benefits of smaller mini-batch sizes and the robustness to the choice of matching algorithm.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the definition of PMD is precise, and the connection to the Wasserstein metric is explained well. The algorithm is presented with a clear diagram and pseudocode, aiding reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scalability of the Matching Algorithm:** The primary bottleneck of PMD is the computational cost of solving the minimum weight matching problem, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm and O(N^2) for the approximation used. While the authors argue this is acceptable compared to backpropagation costs, this may not hold for very deep models or very large population sizes (N). MMD is O(N^2) and critic-based methods like WGAN do not have this explicit dependency on the square or cube of the batch size. This concern limits the immediate applicability of PMD to problems requiring very large batches. While GPU acceleration is mentioned as future work, its feasibility and performance gains are not demonstrated.

2.  **Justification of the Gradient Approximation:** A crucial step in the method is the assumption in Eq. (3) that the optimal matching M* does not change for infinitesimal changes in the parameters θ. This allows the gradient to pass "through" the matching operator. While this is a common technique in similar non-smooth optimization settings, the paper does not provide sufficient justification or analysis of when this assumption might be violated and what the practical consequences would be. The objective function is piecewise smooth, and the gradient is undefined at points where the optimal matching changes. A discussion of these discontinuities would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation.

3.  **Limited Experimental Baselines:** The experiments focus almost exclusively on comparing PMD to MMD. While this is a very relevant baseline, the paper would be significantly stronger with comparisons to other state-of-the-art divergence measures, particularly for the generative modeling task. Given the explicit connection to the Wasserstein metric, a comparison with Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) [3] or its variants would have been highly informative. The authors mention WGAN but do not include it in the empirical evaluation, which is a missed opportunity to position PMD within the broader landscape of optimal transport-based deep learning.

4.  **Sensitivity to the Ground Metric `d(·, ·)`:** The paper uses the L1 distance as the ground metric `d(·, ·)`. The performance of PMD is likely dependent on this choice. For image generation, pixel-wise L1 distance is known to be a poor measure of perceptual similarity, which could explain the blurry results seen in Figure 4. A discussion on the impact of this choice and potential alternatives (e.g., using distances in a pretrained feature space) would be a valuable addition.

### Detailed Comments and Questions

-   In Section 3.3, the proof for Theorem 3.3 is stated to be "straightforward" and omitted. While plausible, including at least a proof sketch in an appendix would be standard practice and necessary for full technical verification.
-   The empirical study of the approximation error in Section 5.4 finds an error rate proportional to N^-0.23. How does this compare to known theoretical rates for Wasserstein estimation? Some discussion here would provide useful context.
-   Could the authors comment on the memory complexity? Storing the N x N distance matrix for the matching algorithm requires O(N^2) memory, which could also be a limiting factor for large N.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper introducing a novel, interesting, and well-motivated method. The core idea is elegant, the theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is sound, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its advantages over MMD. The paper is very well-written and easy to understand.

The main weaknesses relate to the scalability of the matching algorithm and the limited scope of the experimental baselines (notably the absence of WGAN). The assumption regarding the gradient computation could also benefit from a more rigorous discussion.

Despite these weaknesses, the strengths of the paper are significant. PMD is a valuable contribution to the toolbox of divergence measures for deep learning. The work is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research on primal-based optimal transport methods and efficient large-scale matching algorithms.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant formulation of a sample-based Wasserstein-1 estimator.
*   Clever and practical training scheme that makes the method feasible.
*   Strong empirical evidence of superiority over MMD in terms of performance, convergence, and hyperparameter tuning.
*   High clarity in presentation and writing.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Scalability concerns due to the O(N^3) or O(N^2) matching step.
*   Lack of comparison to key baselines like WGAN.
*   The key assumption for gradient computation is not fully justified.

On balance, the contributions are significant and the paper is well-executed. I recommend acceptance.
