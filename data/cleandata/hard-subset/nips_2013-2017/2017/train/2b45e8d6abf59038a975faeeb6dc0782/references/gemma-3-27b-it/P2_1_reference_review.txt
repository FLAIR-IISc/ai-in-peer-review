## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two sample populations. The authors demonstrate theoretical consistency and propose a stochastic gradient descent algorithm for learning parameters using PMD as a training objective. They highlight advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) – no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use normal mini-batch sizes.  The method is evaluated on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, showing performance improvements and faster convergence compared to MMD.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PMD offers a different approach to estimating Wasserstein distance from samples, potentially circumventing some drawbacks of existing methods like MMD and WGAN (which requires a critic network).
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a theoretical justification for PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric, grounding the method in established mathematical principles.
* **Practical Advantages:** The claimed advantages of PMD - eliminating bandwidth tuning, enabling stronger gradients, and allowing for smaller mini-batches - are significant practical improvements for training deep learning models.  The empirical results appear to support these claims.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The mathematical definitions are clear, and the algorithm is well-explained.
* **Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD on two standard deep learning tasks (domain adaptation and generative modeling). The comparison with MMD is well-conducted and shows promising results.
* **Analysis of Gradient Behavior:** The empirical study analyzing gradient magnitudes (Fig 2(b)) provides valuable insight into the potential benefits of PMD.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors address the computational complexity of the matching problem, it remains a potential bottleneck, especially for large datasets.  The paper acknowledges that GPU parallelization is possible but doesn't present results with such optimization.  The comparison to MMD's computational cost could be more detailed and nuanced.
* **Distance Metric Limitation:** The choice of L1 distance within PMD might be restrictive.  The authors acknowledge this limitation in the generative modeling section but don't explore the impact of different distance metrics systematically. The impact of different distance metrics could be worth exploring.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are solid, the evaluation is limited to two tasks. Expanding the evaluation to other domains and datasets would strengthen the claims.  The results are also somewhat sensitive to architecture choices.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:**  The connection to WGAN isn’t fully articulated. While the authors mention differences, a clearer explanation of why PMD doesn’t suffer from the scaling issues of WGAN would be beneficial.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the experimental results are well-presented and support the claims. The completeness of the work is good for a conference paper, although further exploration of computational costs and distance metrics would be beneficial.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized. However, the explanation of the advantage over WGAN could be more detailed.
* **Originality:** PMD represents a novel approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric and leveraging it for deep learning tasks. The combination of theoretical analysis and empirical validation adds to its originality.
* **Significance:** The potential for improved training dynamics (stronger gradients, smaller mini-batches) and the elimination of a hyperparameter (bandwidth) are significant advantages.  If these benefits translate to broader applicability, PMD could become a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners.  The demonstrated improvements on domain adaptation and generative modeling are promising.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to estimating Wasserstein distance.
* Demonstrable practical advantages over MMD in terms of performance and convergence speed.
* Clear presentation and well-conducted experiments.
* Addresses a relevant problem in deep learning – estimating distribution divergence.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational cost of the matching algorithm could be a limiting factor.
* Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
* Restricted choice of distance metric.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field.  The introduction of PMD as a novel and theoretically grounded method for estimating Wasserstein distance, combined with its demonstrated practical advantages, makes it a valuable addition to the NIPS program.  While there are some weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend **acceptance**.  



I suggest the authors consider the following for a camera-ready version:

* Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and potential optimizations (especially GPU parallelization).
* Explore the impact of different distance metrics within PMD.
* Expand the experimental evaluation to additional datasets and tasks.
* Elaborate on the connection to WGAN and clarify why PMD avoids the scaling issues.
