## Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, termed Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), and demonstrates its effectiveness in domain adaptation and generative modeling. The paper is generally well-written and the technical contributions appear sound. While the ideas aren’t groundbreaking in the sense of introducing entirely new theoretical concepts, the practical advantages of PMD – particularly its gradient behavior and reduced sensitivity to hyperparameters – are compelling and address known limitations of MMD. I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance, albeit with minor revisions.

**Summary:**

The authors address the problem of estimating statistical divergence between distributions when only samples are available. They propose PMD as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), highlighting that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric. The core idea is to minimize the cost of optimally matching samples from the two distributions. They show that PMD avoids the bandwidth tuning issues of MMD, exhibits stronger gradients, and can work effectively with standard mini-batch sizes.  The authors demonstrate the benefits of PMD through experiments in domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing competitive or improved performance and faster convergence compared to MMD.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges with existing sample-based divergence estimators and motivates the development of PMD.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The connection to the Wasserstein metric is well-established, and the proof of strong consistency provides a strong theoretical basis for the proposed method.
* **Practical Advantages:** The elimination of bandwidth tuning, improved gradient behavior, and ability to use smaller mini-batches are significant practical advantages over MMD. These improvements are well-explained and supported by experimental results (Fig. 2b).
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD. The comparison with MMD is fair and provides convincing evidence of PMD's benefits. The ablation study (Fig. 5) further clarifies the impact of population size and batch size.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure and logical flow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** The concept of using optimal transport/matching for comparing distributions is not entirely new. However, the authors' contribution lies in framing it as a practical and effective estimator (PMD) specifically tailored for deep learning applications.
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The exclusive use of L1 distance within PMD might be a limitation. While justified in the paper, the authors do not explore the impact of other distance metrics (e.g., Euclidean, Cosine) on performance, especially for generative modeling with complex data like images. The blurry results in the LFW experiment (Section 5.2) suggest L1 might not be ideal for all data types.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors address the computational cost of the matching algorithm, a more detailed discussion of scalability to very large datasets (beyond the sizes used in the experiments) would be beneficial.  The claim of parallelization on GPUs is mentioned but not elaborated upon.
* **Lack of Ablation on Matching Algorithm:** The paper compares exact and approximate matching algorithms, but doesn’t provide a detailed ablation study of the impact of approximation quality on overall performance.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on successes. A discussion of potential scenarios where PMD might *not* outperform MMD would strengthen the analysis.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a well-motivated and theoretically sound alternative to MMD for estimating distributional divergence.
* The practical advantages of PMD (no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batches) are significant and address limitations of existing methods.
* The experimental results demonstrate that PMD performs competitively or outperforms MMD in relevant applications.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and its benefits.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The core idea builds upon existing concepts in optimal transport.
* The reliance on L1 distance may limit the applicability of PMD to certain data types.
*  More in-depth analysis of scalability and the impact of matching algorithm approximation would be beneficial.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* **Explore alternative distance metrics:** Investigate the performance of PMD with different distance metrics (e.g., Euclidean, Cosine) and discuss their suitability for different types of data.
* **Scalability Analysis:**  Provide a more detailed discussion of the scalability of PMD to larger datasets, including potential optimizations and parallelization strategies.
* **Matching Algorithm Ablation:** Conduct a more thorough ablation study examining the sensitivity of PMD performance to the quality of the approximate matching algorithm.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** Include a section discussing potential failure cases or scenarios where PMD might not be the best choice.



**Overall Score:** 8/10

**Confidence:** 8/10

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above. The paper offers a valuable contribution to the field by providing a practical and effective alternative to MMD for estimating distributional divergence in deep learning.
