

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the cost of minimum weight matching between two sample populations from two distributions, as a new approach to estimating statistical divergence. The authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and offers advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), including no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes.

## Strengths
The paper provides a clear theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, with Proposition 3.3 establishing PMD as a strongly consistent estimator. The authors correctly identify a limitation of MMD (bandwidth tuning) and provide empirical evidence of stronger gradients in PMD (Figure 2b). The implementation details and pseudocode for the learning algorithm are well-presented, and the paper demonstrates reasonable empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.

## Weaknesses
Despite the promising theoretical foundation, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings that undermine its contribution:

1. **Theoretical gaps**: The authors claim PMD is a "strongly consistent estimator" but fail to provide finite-sample error bounds (which they note they plan to derive in future work). The theoretical justification relies on Proposition 3.2, which is a special case of a more general theorem, and lacks depth. The connection to Wasserstein distance is well-established, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how PMD compares to other Wasserstein approximations like WGAN.

2. **Computational concerns**: The paper's core method relies on the Hungarian algorithm (O(N³) time complexity), which is computationally prohibitive for large N. While they mention an approximate O(N²) algorithm, they don't provide a thorough analysis of how approximation quality affects model performance. The claimed advantage of "smaller mini-batch size" (Section 3.2) is questionable since the matching must be computed for the entire population (N), not just the mini-batch. The paper's Figure 5(c) shows matching time is comparable to SGD, suggesting the approach may not actually accelerate training as claimed.

3. **Experimental limitations**: The empirical validation is insufficiently rigorous. The domain adaptation experiments (Table 1) show PMD outperforming MMD only on two out of six tasks, which is a weak claim for "outperforming" MMD. The generative modeling results (Figure 4) show PMD generating "less noisy" images but also "blurry" images, with no quantitative evaluation of image quality (e.g., FID scores). The paper fails to compare with WGAN or other Wasserstein-based methods, which are the standard for generative modeling.

4. **Lack of comparison with relevant work**: The paper references WGAN but doesn't adequately compare PMD to it. WGAN has been extensively studied and implemented in deep learning, with known advantages and limitations. The paper doesn't explain why PMD would be preferable to WGAN, which also uses a differentiable approximation of Wasserstein distance without needing a separate critic network.

5. **Unsubstantiated claims**: The claim that PMD "has stronger gradient" (Section 3.2) is based on a theoretical argument about gradient magnitude, but the empirical evidence (Figure 2b) is limited to a single dataset and doesn't correlate with performance gains. The paper also claims PMD "can use normal mini-batch size, such as 100," but the experiments use N=2000 for PMD (Section 5.2), contradicting this claim.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting idea with some theoretical grounding, but the claims are not sufficiently validated. The paper is primarily a methodological proposal without strong empirical evidence to support its advantages over existing methods. The computational cost of the matching algorithm, particularly for large-scale applications, is a major concern that isn't adequately addressed. The experiments don't provide convincing evidence that PMD is superior to established methods like MMD or WGAN, and the paper fails to engage deeply with the relevant literature.

While the paper correctly identifies limitations of MMD, it doesn't provide a compelling case for why PMD is a better solution. The claimed advantages (no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch) are either not substantiated or outweighed by computational concerns. The paper would benefit from more thorough experimental validation, comparison with WGAN, and analysis of the trade-offs between exact and approximate matching.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper lacks the rigorous validation required for NIPS. While the idea has potential, the authors haven't provided sufficient evidence to support their claims. The computational cost of the proposed method and the limited empirical validation make it difficult to assess whether PMD offers a meaningful advantage over existing methods. The paper would need substantially more thorough experiments, including comparisons with WGAN and other state-of-the-art methods, to be considered for publication at NIPS.
