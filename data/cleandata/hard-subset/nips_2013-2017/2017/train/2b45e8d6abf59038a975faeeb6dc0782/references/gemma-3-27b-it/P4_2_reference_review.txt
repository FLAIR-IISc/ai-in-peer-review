This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, positioning it as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling. The core idea is to minimize the cost of optimally matching samples from two distributions, offering advantages such as the absence of a bandwidth parameter, potentially stronger gradients, and the ability to utilize smaller mini-batch sizes compared to MMD. The authors provide theoretical justification for PMD's consistency and present empirical results demonstrating its effectiveness in two application areas.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PMD presents a novel approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric using a matching-based formulation. While the Wasserstein metric and its use in GANs are well-established, framing it as a minimum-weight matching problem and directly employing it as a differentiable loss function is a fresh perspective.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, demonstrating its strong consistency. This grounding in statistical theory adds credibility to the proposed method.
* **Potential Advantages:** The identified advantages over MMD – no bandwidth tuning, stronger gradients, and smaller mini-batch sizes – are potentially significant, addressing common limitations of MMD in deep learning applications. The empirical results suggest these benefits materialize in practice.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the concepts are explained in a reasonably accessible manner.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling provide a reasonable demonstration of PMD's performance. The comparisons with MMD are insightful, showing performance gains and faster convergence.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** While the results are encouraging, the experiments are limited to two tasks and a relatively small set of architectures. Further evaluation on a wider range of datasets and model types would strengthen the claims.
* **Computational Cost Discussion:**  While the paper acknowledges the O(N^3) complexity of the exact matching algorithm, the discussion of computational costs and scalability could be more thorough.  A more detailed analysis of the runtime breakdown, particularly highlighting the GPU acceleration potential, would be helpful. The statement “the time complexity is acceptable comparing with the O(N × BackProp) time complexity of computing the gradient…” feels somewhat imprecise, as the backpropagation cost also scales with network size.
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The exclusive use of the L1 distance in PMD is a potential limitation. The paper briefly acknowledges this in the generative modeling section, but a more comprehensive investigation of different distance metrics and their impact on performance would be valuable. The choice of L1 might be suitable for some scenarios but may not generalize well to all types of data.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments show PMD is less sensitive to hyperparameters than MMD. However, demonstrating the robustness of PMD's performance across a wider range of hyperparameter settings would further solidify this advantage.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The relation to existing work on optimal transport approximation (cited as [45]) could be expanded.  Specifically, how PMD differs in its differentiability properties compared to the dual formulation explored in that work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric.
* The proposed PMD method offers potential advantages over existing techniques like MMD, addressing practical limitations in deep learning applications.
* The empirical results provide evidence of PMD's effectiveness in domain adaptation and generative modeling.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts and methodology.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The empirical evaluation is limited in scope, and more extensive validation is needed.
* The discussion of computational costs and scalability could be more thorough.
* The choice of L1 distance may limit the generalizability of the method.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The introduction of PMD is a novel and potentially valuable addition to the toolkit for statistical divergence estimation in deep learning. The theoretical justification and initial empirical results are promising. While the empirical evaluation could be more extensive and the discussion of computational aspects more detailed, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically suggesting a more comprehensive computational analysis and exploration of alternative distance metrics.
