

# Review: Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the cost of minimum weight matching between two sample populations from two distributions. The authors establish PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and propose its use as a training objective in deep learning, particularly for domain adaptation and generative modeling. The paper claims PMD has advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), including no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes.

## Strengths

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a theoretically grounded approach to estimating statistical distances between distributions from samples. The connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is well-established through rigorous theoretical analysis (Section 3.1), with Proposition 3.3 demonstrating strong consistency. The authors effectively compare PMD with MMD across multiple dimensions (Section 3.2), highlighting practical advantages that could benefit practitioners.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering two important applications (domain adaptation and generative modeling) with appropriate datasets. The empirical results (Section 5) support the theoretical claims, showing PMD outperforms MMD in domain adaptation tasks (Table 1) and generates less noisy samples in generative modeling (Fig. 4). The ablation studies (Fig. 5) effectively demonstrate PMD's insensitivity to population size and mini-batch size, validating the claim about smaller batch requirements.

## Weaknesses

The paper's most significant limitation is its narrow focus on the first Wasserstein metric. Many applications, particularly in generative modeling (like WGANs), rely on the 2nd Wasserstein metric, which the authors do not address. The theoretical analysis is limited to the 1st Wasserstein metric, leaving the applicability to broader settings unclear.

The computational complexity claim requires more careful justification. While the paper mentions that Hungarian algorithm is O(N³) and approximate matching is O(N²), it doesn't properly contextualize this with the cost of neural network training. For large batch sizes (e.g., 1000), the matching step could become a significant bottleneck, especially compared to MMD's O(N²) complexity (though the paper claims MMD requires population size N for mini-batch, which is not standard practice).

The "stronger gradient" claim (Section 3.2) lacks rigorous justification. The gradient analysis is based on simple L1 distance, but the authors don't demonstrate this holds for more complex distance metrics or in high-dimensional spaces typical of deep learning. The empirical comparison in Fig. 2(b) shows PMD gradients have more consistent magnitudes, but this doesn't necessarily translate to better optimization performance.

The generative modeling results on natural images (SVHN, LFW) are described as "blurry" without clear explanation. The paper should provide more insight into why PMD performs worse on pixel-space generation than on code-space (ae architecture), especially since the authors claim PMD has advantages over MMD.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Extend the theoretical analysis to cover the 2nd Wasserstein metric, which is more commonly used in deep learning applications.

2. Provide a more detailed computational complexity analysis comparing PMD and MMD, including actual runtime measurements for different batch sizes in practical deep learning settings.

3. Conduct additional experiments to validate the "stronger gradient" claim beyond simple L1 distance, using more complex distance metrics and high-dimensional data.

4. Investigate the reasons for blurry images in PMD's pixel-space generation and provide potential solutions, such as using perceptual losses or architectural modifications.

5. Include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art methods beyond MMD, such as WGAN-GP or CramerGAN, to better establish the significance of the proposed approach.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically sound and practically useful approach to estimating statistical distances between distributions from samples. The connection to Wasserstein metric is well-established, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over MMD in certain scenarios. However, the narrow focus on the first Wasserstein metric and insufficient justification for computational claims limit the paper's broader impact.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, but would benefit from addressing the limitations identified above. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the practical advantages are well-demonstrated in specific applications. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the toolkit for distribution comparison in deep learning, though the scope could be expanded to better address the needs of the broader machine learning community.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, with strong theoretical grounding and practical benefits. The weaknesses identified are important but not fundamental to the core contribution, and the paper would benefit from the suggested improvements in a revised version. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses an important problem in deep learning with potential for significant impact.
