- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), the minimum-weight bipartite matching cost between two sample sets, equivalent to empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD.  
- Authors derive PMD as optimal transport on empirical distributions and prove almost-sure consistency.  
- Introduce a training loop that alternates computing a matching on a population of size N and performing several SGD steps on mini-batches using the fixed matching.  
- Empirical results show PMD matches or improves over MMD on Office domain adaptation and yields cleaner, faster-converging samples than MMD on MNIST/SVHN/LFW.  

- Clear motivation: sample-based, differentiable distributional distances can avoid MMD kernel tuning and gradient saturation.  
- Correct grounding in OT: PMD is exactly empirical 1-Wasserstein and the consistency claim is standard but valid.  
- Practical training recipe: fixing matching for several SGD steps with subsampled pairs is simple and empirically effective; L1 costs keep gradients strong for outliers.  
- Empirical evidence: improvements in some domain shifts, cleaner generative samples, and faster convergence vs. MMD.  
- Useful discussion of computational choices (Hungarian vs approximate matching), timings, and insensitivity to N/matching algorithm.  

- Novelty is limited: PMD is standard empirical 1-Wasserstein and main novelty is the fixed-assignment training scheme.  
- Major missing related work: entropic-regularized OT / Sinkhorn (Cuturi 2013) and GPU-scalable OT baselines.  
- Missing comparisons to sliced Wasserstein methods and projection-based OT approaches.  
- Omitted sample-complexity literature for empirical OT (e.g., Fournier & Guillin 2015; Weed & Bach 2017) to contextualize observed rates.  
- Missing OT-based domain adaptation literature (e.g., Courty et al.).  

- Gradient treatment is informal and needs Danskin/Envelope theorem: differentiability holds only when argmin is unique and subgradients arise at assignment changes.  
- Claim that gradients do not propagate through matching and are “unbiased” is unjustified; fixing matchings for inner-loop updates introduces bias with no analysis.  
- Computational claims overlook that O(N^2) matching and O(N^2 D) pairwise distances are expensive; Sinkhorn offers GPU-parallel, tunable tradeoffs and should be compared.  

- Experimental scope is limited: domain adaptation baselines only include MMD and omit stronger methods (DANN/GRL, CORAL, JAN, CMD, DSN).  
- Generative experiments lack standard quantitative metrics (FID/IS) and comparisons to WGAN-GP; only qualitative pixel-space results shown.  
- Claims require nuance: PMD is not strictly “parameter free” (ground metric and scaling matter) and still needs a large population N, unlike some linear-time MMD estimators.  

- Technical quality: mostly sound but needs proper citation/analysis of gradient/Danskin and bias from fixed matches; theoretical contribution is incremental.  
- Clarity: well written, but naming PMD anew may confuse—should explicitly state equivalence to EMD and position contribution as a training strategy.  
- Originality: moderate — known distance reused with a specific SGD scheme; empirical gradient-strength observation is useful but not fundamental.  
- Significance: potentially useful in practice but limited without Sinkhorn and stronger baselines; impact unclear.  

- Pros for acceptance: simple, implementable method; clear OT connection; practical training recipe and useful empirical observations about gradients and batch sizes.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited novelty, missing key related work/baselines (especially Sinkhorn), incomplete experiments (no FID/IS or WGAN-GP), and insufficient theoretical optimization treatment.  

- Questions for authors: formalize gradient claims via Danskin and discuss subgradients at assignment changes?  
- Questions for authors: compare PMD to entropic OT (Sinkhorn) in speed and accuracy on presented tasks?  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to ground metric and scaling; have learned cost feature spaces been tried?  
- Questions for authors: how does PMD compare to DANN/CORAL/JAN/CMD on Office domain adaptation?  
- Questions for authors: can you report FID/IS and include WGAN-GP as a baseline for generative experiments?  

- Recommendation: Weak reject — clear and practical but needs stronger baselines, fuller related work, and more rigorous optimization analysis to be a solid contribution.
