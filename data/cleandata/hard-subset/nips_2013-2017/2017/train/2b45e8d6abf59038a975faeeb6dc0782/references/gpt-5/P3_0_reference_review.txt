Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two i.i.d. sample sets, as a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance. The authors show PMD equals the empirical OT distance for balanced, equally weighted empirical measures and argue strong consistency using Varadarajan’s theorem plus stability of OT. They propose to use PMD as a differentiable training objective in deep models: compute a matching for two populations, take gradients with respect to parameters of the generators/feature extractors while treating the matching as fixed (no gradient through the argmin), and optionally reuse the matching across several SGD steps while sub-sampling matched pairs for cheaper updates. They compare PMD to MMD in domain adaptation (Office) and in implicit generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW), reporting faster convergence, better gradients, no bandwidth tuning, and acceptable computational cost using an approximate O(N^2) matcher.

Relation to prior work
- PMD is exactly the empirical 1-Wasserstein/EMD on equally weighted samples; that connection is properly made.
- Dual formulations (WGAN) are discussed. However, the entropic regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013) and more recent “Sinkhorn divergences” (e.g., Genevay et al., 2017) are not cited or compared; these provide fast, differentiable approximations with GPU-friendly O(N^2) time and are highly relevant alternatives to discrete matching.
- For domain adaptation, there is substantial related work using OT (e.g., Courty et al., OTDA, 2017), and other divergences (CMD, DANN, JAN). The experimental comparison is limited to MMD.
- Rates for empirical OT convergence (e.g., Fournier & Guillin, 2015) are not discussed; the empirical N−0.23 scaling observed in 5D is consistent with known dimension-dependent rates.

Quality
Theory:
- The consistency argument is essentially known: empirical OT converges to population OT under mild conditions. It is correct but not novel; no finite-sample bounds are provided.
- The gradient treatment relies on ignoring derivatives through the discrete matching. This can be justified via Danskin’s theorem/subgradient calculus if the active assignment is unique and the cost is smooth; the paper does not cite or formalize this. The claim of “unbiased stochastic gradient” is not substantiated; interchanging min and gradient, and expectation and gradient, needs conditions.
- The proposed inner-loop scheme that reuses a fixed matching across multiple SGD steps and sub-samples matched pairs introduces bias unless the assignment remains optimal; no convergence guarantees are given.

Algorithmic/computational aspects:
- Exact Hungarian O(N^3) is standard; the approximate O(N^2) algorithm is reasonable. However, the more scalable and differentiable Sinkhorn approach is not considered, despite being well-suited to GPUs and large N.
- The assertion that PMD is “parameter free” overlooks choices of ground metric d(·,·), population size N, mini-batch size |B|, and inner-loop length; these are important hyperparameters.

Experiments:
- Domain adaptation: Using Office with fixed VGG16 features is standard, but comparisons are only to MMD (not to DANN, JAN, CMD, or OT-based methods). Validation uses 100 labeled target samples to tune λ and σ, which is not purely unsupervised and can inflate performance. Improvements are modest and not uniform across tasks; no statistical tests are provided.
- Generative modeling: Results are qualitative/blurry on SVHN/LFW; no quantitative metrics (e.g., FID, Inception score or bits/dim for tractable models). Comparisons are again only to MMD; no comparisons to GAN/WGAN or to Sinkhorn-based training. The claim of “stronger gradients” is supported by a histogram of per-sample gradient magnitudes, but this is anecdotal and depends on choice of d and feature space. The fairness of training schedules (100 epochs PMD vs 500 epochs MMD) is unclear.
- Efficiency: Timings suggest matching is not the bottleneck for N up to a few thousand, but end-to-end runtime comparisons against strong alternatives (e.g., WGAN-GP, Sinkhorn) are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. Some statements are overstated or imprecise:
- “Unbiased stochastic gradient” (Sec. 2.2) needs justification (Danskin’s theorem, conditions for exchanging min/grad/expectation).
- “MMD must use mini-batch size equal to population size” is not accurate; linear-time unbiased MMD estimators and random Fourier features allow mini-batch training.
- “Parameter-free” should be softened.
- It would help to explicitly state that PMD is the empirical 1-Wasserstein distance and to discuss uniqueness/non-uniqueness of matchings and implications for differentiability.

Originality
Using empirical OT/assignment as a loss is not new; the theoretical result (consistency) is known. The paper’s contributions are primarily an engineering recipe for deep learning with empirical OT via minimum matching and some empirical evidence about gradient behavior and mini-batch reuse. The omission of strong contemporary alternatives (Sinkhorn) weakens the novelty and positioning.

Significance
The idea of using OT as a training signal is important. However, the current submission falls short of demonstrating clear advantages over existing OT-based training (Sinkhorn/WGAN) or over MMD across a broad set of tasks with strong baselines and quantitative metrics. Practical impact is limited by missing comparisons and incomplete theoretical guarantees for the proposed optimization scheme.

Pros
- Clear connection to Wasserstein; easy-to-implement objective.
- Practical heuristics (reuse matching, sub-sampling) that appear to work.
- Empirical observation of more uniform per-sample gradients vs MMD.
- Some improvements on Office and better qualitative samples than MMD in pixel space.

Cons
- Missing key related work and comparisons (Sinkhorn OT; OT domain adaptation; GAN/WGAN).
- Theoretical contribution is largely known; no finite-sample rates; gradient claims not rigorously justified.
- Experimental evaluation limited: weak baselines, mixed results, qualitative metrics only for generation, non-pure UDA validation.
- Overstated claims (unbiased gradients; parameter-free; MMD mini-batch constraint).

Suggestions for improvement
- Include and compare against entropic OT (Sinkhorn) and Sinkhorn divergences; discuss trade-offs vs discrete matching.
- Provide rigorous justification via Danskin’s theorem and discuss subgradient handling at assignment changes; analyze bias from reusing matchings and sub-batching; give convergence guarantees or empirical ablations.
- Strengthen experiments: add DANN/JAN/CMD/OTDA baselines; use target-label-free validation; report quantitative gen metrics (FID/Inception); compare to WGAN(-GP).
- Explore learned feature spaces for d(·,·) to mitigate blurriness; or use perceptual costs.
- Report sensitivity to N, |B|, and matching approximation quality with statistical significance.
