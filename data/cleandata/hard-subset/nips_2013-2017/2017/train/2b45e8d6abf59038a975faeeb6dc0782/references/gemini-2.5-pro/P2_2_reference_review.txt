### NIPS Review: Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning

**Summary:**
This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions. The authors establish a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric (W1), showing that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1.

The main contribution is a method for using PMD as a differentiable objective function for training deep neural networks. The proposed algorithm computes the optimal matching between two "populations" (large mini-batches) of samples and then uses this fixed matching to perform several smaller mini-batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) updates. This work positions PMD as a direct competitor to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The paper argues that PMD offers several advantages over MMD, namely: it has no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, it provides stronger (non-saturating) gradients, and it allows for faster convergence by using smaller SGD mini-batches. These claims are evaluated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling, where PMD is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea of using the solution to the assignment problem as a direct, parameter-free, and differentiable loss for training deep generative models and for domain adaptation is both novel and elegant. While the connection between optimal transport and bipartite matching on empirical distributions is known, its application as a practical training objective in this manner is a significant contribution. It provides a concrete, primal-form alternative to the dual-form, critic-based approximation of the Wasserstein distance used in Wasserstein GANs (WGANs), and a compelling alternative to kernel-based methods like MMD. The potential impact is high, as it offers practitioners a new tool for distribution matching with clear advantages in certain scenarios.

2.  **Quality (Theoretical Soundness):** The paper provides a solid theoretical motivation for PMD. By framing the N-PMD as the solution to a linear program that discretizes the optimal transport problem, the authors correctly identify it as the empirical first Wasserstein metric. The claim of strong consistency is well-supported by citing established results in probability theory (Varadarajan's Theorem, Stability of Optimal Transport), lending theoretical credibility to the proposed metric.

3.  **Quality (Experimental Evaluation):** The empirical validation is thorough and convincing.
    *   The paper compares PMD against its most relevant baseline, MMD, on two distinct and important applications (domain adaptation and generative modeling).
    *   The results demonstrate tangible benefits: PMD achieves better or comparable performance on the Office dataset for domain adaptation and generates visually less noisy images in the generative modeling task.
    *   Crucially, the paper includes insightful ablation studies (Sec 5.3) that support its central claims. Figure 5(a) effectively shows that performance improves with smaller SGD mini-batch sizes `|B|`, a key advantage of the proposed method. Figure 2(b) provides compelling empirical evidence for the "stronger gradient" claim.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated in the introduction. Section 2 explains the PMD and the learning algorithm with helpful pseudocode and a diagram. The connections to the Wasserstein metric and MMD are laid out logically in Section 3.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Quality (Technical Soundness Concern):** The justification for the gradient computation in Equation (3) is based on the argument that "the discrete matching M* should not change for infinitesimal change of θ". This is a strong assumption that is not rigorously justified. While it holds true *almost* everywhere, the cost surface is non-convex and the matching M* can change abruptly. The behavior of the gradient at these transition points is not discussed. This simplification is key to the method's tractability, but the lack of a more formal analysis is a weakness. The paper would be stronger with a discussion of the implications of this approximation, for instance, whether it introduces any bias.

2.  **Significance (Scalability):** The primary practical limitation of PMD is the computational cost of solving the minimum weight matching problem, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm and O(N^2) for the approximation used. The authors argue this is acceptable compared to the backpropagation cost, but this may not hold for very deep or complex models. This complexity limits the "population size" `N` that can be used in practice (the paper uses N up to 4000). While MMD also has costs scaling with N, its O(N^2) complexity is for a simpler, highly parallelizable kernel computation. The paper mentions that matching algorithms can be parallelized on GPU but does not provide such an implementation, leaving a significant engineering hurdle for future work.

3.  **Originality (Context):** The paper was written in the context of a surge of interest in using the Wasserstein distance for deep learning, most notably spurred by WGAN. While the paper does a good job differentiating its primal approach from WGAN's dual approach, the broader idea of leveraging the Wasserstein metric was already gaining significant traction. This slightly tempers the originality, though the specific mechanism (PMD) remains novel.

4.  **Clarity (Minor):** The notation can be slightly confusing. `N` is referred to as the "population size," which corresponds to what is typically called the "mini-batch size" in the MMD/GMMN literature. The paper then introduces `|B|` as a *sub-sampled* mini-batch for the SGD update step. This distinction is crucial but could be made more explicit early on to avoid confusion for readers familiar with related work.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend **acceptance**.

The strengths of the paper, particularly its novel formulation of a differentiable Wasserstein-based loss and its strong empirical results, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. PMD is an elegant and principled method that offers a valuable new approach to distribution matching in deep learning. The practical benefits over MMD (no kernel tuning, stronger gradients, faster convergence via mini-batching) are well-demonstrated and significant. While the concerns about the gradient approximation and computational scalability are valid, they represent reasonable trade-offs and interesting directions for future research rather than fatal flaws. This work is a solid contribution to the NIPS community and is likely to inspire follow-up research.
