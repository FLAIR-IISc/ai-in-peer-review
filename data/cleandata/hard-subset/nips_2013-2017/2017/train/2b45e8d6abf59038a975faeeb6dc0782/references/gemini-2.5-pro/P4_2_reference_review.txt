### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence measure designed for training deep learning models. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples, each drawn from a different distribution. The authors establish a direct theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric, proving that N-PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of this metric as the sample size N approaches infinity.

The primary contribution is a practical algorithm for using PMD as a differentiable objective function in gradient-based optimization. The gradient is computed with respect to the sample locations, holding the optimal matching fixed, an assumption valid for infinitesimal parameter changes. To improve efficiency, the proposed algorithm reuses a single matching solution for several mini-batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) updates.

The paper positions PMD as an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). It argues that PMD offers several advantages: it is free of the kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, it provides stronger and more uniform gradients during training, and it allows for using smaller SGD mini-batches than the population size, potentially leading to faster convergence. The authors differentiate PMD from other Wasserstein-based methods like Wasserstein GANs (WGAN) by noting that PMD is a parameter-free estimator of the primal form of the Wasserstein metric, whereas WGAN approximates the dual form using an auxiliary critic network.

The utility of PMD is demonstrated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation on the Office dataset and deep generative modeling on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW datasets. In both applications, PMD is shown to achieve performance that is competitive with or superior to MMD-based approaches.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric is well-founded, relying on established results from optimal transport theory (e.g., Varadarajan's theorem, stability of optimal transport). The derivation of the stochastic gradient is straightforward and appears correct under the assumption that the discrete matching does not change. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments, including applications on standard benchmarks and empirical studies analyzing approximation error, gradient distributions, and the impact of hyperparameters like population and mini-batch size. The authors are also transparent about the computational cost of the matching step.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The concept of PMD is introduced intuitively and its mathematical formulation is precise. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is explained logically in Section 3.1. The proposed learning algorithm is presented with a helpful diagram and pseudocode, making it easy to understand. The experiments are described in sufficient detail, and the results are presented clearly through tables and figures. The paper provides enough information for an expert to grasp the method and its implementation details.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel approach for leveraging the Wasserstein metric in deep learning. While using optimal transport and the Wasserstein metric is not new to the field (e.g., Rubner et al., 2000; WGAN), the specific formulation of PMD as a direct, sample-based, and parameter-free estimator for the primal problem is a novel contribution. This stands in contrast to the more common critic-based methods for approximating the dual form. The idea of using minimum weight matching as a differentiable loss for large-scale deep learning models is a creative and original combination of classical algorithms and modern network optimization.

**Significance:** This work introduces a potentially impactful tool for researchers and practitioners. PMD offers a compelling alternative to MMD, and the empirical evidence suggests it can lead to faster convergence and better performance, while also simplifying the hyperparameter tuning process by removing the need for kernel selection and bandwidth tuning. If the computational overhead can be managed, PMD could be widely adopted for tasks involving distribution matching, such as domain adaptation, generative modeling, and model criticism. The paper effectively demonstrates that a direct primal-form estimator of the Wasserstein metric can be a practical and powerful objective for deep learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Computational Complexity:** The primary drawback of PMD is the computational cost of solving the minimum weight matching problem, which is O(N^3) for the exact Hungarian algorithm or O(N^2) for the approximation used. This is computationally more intensive than MMD's O(N^2) evaluation cost. While the authors argue this is acceptable relative to the cost of backpropagation, this may not hold for simpler models or when very large population sizes (N) are required for accurate estimation. The reliance on single-threaded CPU implementations for matching in the experiments highlights this as a potential bottleneck.

2.  **Gradient Approximation Scheme:** The proposed training algorithm reuses the same matching M* for multiple inner-loop SGD updates. This is an essential heuristic for making the method practical. However, the paper does not provide a theoretical or empirical analysis of the potential bias introduced by this approximation. It is unclear how many SGD steps can be taken before the matching becomes stale and negatively impacts training dynamics. A more detailed study on the trade-off between the number of inner-loop updates and model performance would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Missing Comparison with WGAN:** Given that PMD is presented as an estimator of the Wasserstein metric, an empirical comparison with WGAN, a highly influential and successful method also based on this metric, is a notable omission. Such a comparison would be crucial for contextualizing the performance of PMD within the broader landscape of generative models, especially concerning sample quality and training stability.

4.  **Choice of Ground Metric:** The paper highlights that PMD has fewer hyperparameters than MMD because it lacks a kernel bandwidth. However, PMD still depends on the choice of the ground metric `d(·, ·)`, for which the L1 distance was used in the experiments. This choice is analogous to the choice of kernel in MMD and can significantly impact performance. The authors acknowledge that the pixel-wise L1 distance may contribute to blurry generated images, but a deeper discussion of the sensitivity to this choice and potential alternatives (e.g., learned metrics) would be beneficial.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and elegant method (PMD) for estimating a statistical divergence based on a clear connection to the first Wasserstein metric.
*   The proposed method is technically sound and offers practical advantages over MMD, such as eliminating the kernel bandwidth parameter and providing stronger gradients.
*   The empirical results are strong, demonstrating improved performance and faster convergence compared to MMD on standard domain adaptation and generative modeling benchmarks.
*   The work is of high significance, providing a new and practical tool for a broad class of problems in machine learning that require comparing probability distributions.

**Arguments against:**
*   The computational cost of the matching algorithm poses a potential scalability issue, which may limit its applicability for very large population sizes.
*   The paper lacks an empirical comparison to WGAN, a critical baseline in the context of Wasserstein-based generative modeling.
*   The impact of key design choices, such as the gradient approximation scheme (reusing matchings) and the choice of the ground distance metric, are not fully analyzed.
