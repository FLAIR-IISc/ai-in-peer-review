### Summary

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing probability distributions. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (W1). They propose using PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep learning models, highlighting several advantages over the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Specifically, PMD has no kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, provides stronger and more stable gradients, and allows for more efficient stochastic optimization by decoupling the matching population size from the SGD mini-batch size. The effectiveness of PMD is demonstrated on two key applications: unsupervised domain adaptation and deep generative modeling, where it generally outperforms MMD in both final performance and convergence speed.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation and Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper does an excellent job of grounding PMD in the well-established theory of optimal transport. By showing that PMD on empirical distributions is equivalent to a linear programming formulation of the W1 distance, the authors provide a solid theoretical justification for their method. The proof sketch for strong consistency (Theorem 3.3) is clear and relies on standard results, lending significant credibility. The paper also clearly situates PMD relative to other important divergences like MMD and the dual-form Wasserstein distance used in WGAN, highlighting its unique position as a parameter-free, primal-form estimator.

2.  **Compelling Practical Advantages over MMD:** The arguments against MMD and in favor of PMD are practical, well-articulated, and empirically supported.
    *   **No Hyper-parameters:** Eliminating the need to tune kernel bandwidth(s) is a major practical advantage, as shown in the sensitivity analysis (Fig 3b).
    *   **Stronger Gradients:** The analysis of vanishing gradients in MMD (due to the RBF kernel) versus the constant-magnitude gradient of PMD (with L1 distance) is insightful. The empirical validation in Figure 2(b) provides compelling evidence for this claim, suggesting that PMD can avoid learning slowdowns caused by outliers or overly clustered samples.
    *   **Efficient Mini-batching:** The proposed optimization scheme—computing the matching on a larger population `N` and then performing SGD updates on smaller mini-batches `|B|`—is a clever and effective way to amortize the computational cost of matching. This is a significant practical contribution that allows for faster convergence, as demonstrated in Figure 5(a).

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and cover relevant applications.
    *   The domain adaptation results on the Office dataset show that PMD is highly competitive, outperforming MMD by a clear margin in some cases. The faster convergence shown in Figure 3(a) is a strong result.
    *   In generative modeling, PMD demonstrates qualitatively superior sample generation compared to the MMD-based GMMN, especially directly in pixel space. This suggests PMD provides a more robust learning signal.
    *   The ablation studies on population size, mini-batch size, and the choice of matching algorithm are excellent, providing valuable insights into the method's behavior and practical trade-offs.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The primary drawback of PMD is the computational complexity of the matching algorithm (O(N^3) for exact, O(N^2) for approximate). While the authors are upfront about this and show it is manageable for N in the low thousands, this is a significant bottleneck compared to MMD's O(N^2) complexity for calculating the kernel matrix. For problems requiring very large population sizes `N` for accurate distance estimation, PMD could become prohibitively slow. The paper notes that GPU acceleration is possible, but this remains future work.

2.  **Choice of Ground Metric d(·, ·):** The paper uses the L1 distance as the ground metric `d`. While simple and effective in the demonstrated experiments, the performance of PMD is fundamentally tied to this choice. In very high-dimensional or structured spaces (e.g., high-resolution images, text), a simple pixel/feature-wise L1 or L2 distance may not be a perceptually or semantically meaningful measure of difference, potentially limiting PMD's effectiveness. This is a point of contrast with WGAN, where the "critic" learns a suitable distance function, or MMD, where the kernel can capture more complex relationships. Some discussion of this limitation and potential avenues (e.g., using distances in a learned feature space) would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Discontinuity of the Gradient:** The paper states that the gradient does not propagate through the matching M* because it should not change for infinitesimal changes in parameters. While this is a reasonable and widely used practical assumption (e.g., in k-means), it's a slight theoretical simplification. The objective function is not continuously differentiable; the gradient is undefined at points where the optimal matching changes. While this doesn't appear to be a problem in practice, a brief acknowledgment of this nuance would be appropriate.

### Detailed Comments

*   The distinction between population size `N` (for matching) and mini-batch size `|B|` (for SGD) is a key contribution and is explained very clearly in Section 2.2 and Figure 1.
*   The empirical study of the approximation error in Figure 2(a) is a nice addition, showing convergence to the true W1 distance. The observed convergence rate of N^-0.23 is somewhat slow, but consistent with rates for Wasserstein estimation in moderate dimensions.
*   The comparison with WGAN is fair and accurate. Highlighting that PMD is parameter-free and computes an absolute distance (unlike WGAN's critic-dependent value) is an important distinction.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel and practical method for comparing distributions. It is well-written, theoretically well-founded, and supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that clearly demonstrate its advantages over MMD, a strong and popular baseline. The work bridges theory from optimal transport with the practical needs of deep learning in a compelling way. While the computational cost is a notable limitation, the authors address it pragmatically and the proposed benefits—especially the removal of the kernel hyper-parameter and the improved optimization dynamics—are significant enough to make this a valuable contribution to the community.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and its claims are well-supported by both theoretical arguments and strong empirical results. The work is complete and carefully evaluated.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The core ideas are presented with precision, and the experimental sections are easy to follow.

**Originality:** The idea of using minimum weight matching as a direct, differentiable objective for deep generative models and domain adaptation is novel. While the connection between matching and Wasserstein distance is known, its specific application, optimization strategy, and thorough comparison against deep learning standards like MMD constitute a significant original contribution.

**Significance:** This work is likely to be influential. PMD provides a strong, easy-to-implement alternative to MMD that addresses several of its key practical pain points. Researchers working on generative modeling, domain adaptation, and other areas requiring sample-based divergence estimation are likely to adopt and build upon these ideas.

**Recommendation: Accept**
