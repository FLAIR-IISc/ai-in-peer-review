### Paper Summary

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based statistical divergence for comparing two probability distributions. Given two sets of N samples, PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between them, where the cost is a ground distance like L1 or L2. The authors establish a strong theoretical connection between PMD and the first Wasserstein metric (W1), showing that N-PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the W1 distance as N approaches infinity.

The core contribution is a method for using PMD as a differentiable training objective for deep neural networks. By assuming the optimal matching is locally constant, gradients can be computed through the matched pairs. The authors highlight several advantages of PMD over the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD): 1) it is hyperparameter-free (no kernel bandwidth to tune), 2) it provides stronger, non-saturating gradients, and 3) it allows for the use of smaller mini-batches for the stochastic gradient updates, decoupling the optimization batch size from the population size used for distance estimation.

The effectiveness of PMD is demonstrated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation on the Office dataset and deep generative modeling on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW. In both applications, PMD is shown to achieve competitive or superior performance compared to MMD, often with faster convergence.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Elegance:** The idea of using minimum weight matching as a direct, sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein distance to train deep models is simple, elegant, and novel. It provides a compelling alternative to both kernel-based methods like MMD and adversary-based methods like Wasserstein GANs (WGANs).

2.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper grounds PMD in the well-established theory of optimal transport, proving that it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. This provides a solid theoretical justification for the proposed method, which is often lacking in more heuristic approaches.

3.  **Clear Practical Advantages over MMD:** The paper makes a convincing case for the practical benefits of PMD over MMD. The elimination of the sensitive kernel bandwidth hyperparameter is a significant practical advantage. The analysis and empirical validation of PMD's stronger gradient signal (Fig. 2b) and its ability to use smaller mini-batches for optimization (Fig. 5a) are well-executed and highlight key improvements for training deep models.

4.  **Thorough and Well-Presented Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering two distinct and important application areas. The comparisons to MMD are direct and fair. The inclusion of ablation studies on the population size, mini-batch size, and the choice of exact vs. approximate matching solvers adds significant depth to the empirical analysis and strengthens the paper's conclusions. The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The primary weakness of PMD is its computational complexity. The Hungarian algorithm for exact matching is O(N^3), and the proposed approximation is O(N^2). This is significantly more expensive than the O(N) complexity of linear-time MMD estimators and can be a bottleneck for large population sizes (N). While the authors acknowledge this and show that the cost of an O(N^2) solver is comparable to the SGD updates for N=4000 (Fig. 5c), this can still limit the scalability to larger problems or higher-dimensional data where larger populations might be needed for accurate distance estimation.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors could strengthen the paper by discussing this limitation more directly in the context of other optimal transport solvers. For example, a discussion on why they chose bipartite matching over entropic-regularized approaches (like the Sinkhorn algorithm), which are often more scalable and amenable to GPU acceleration, would be valuable. This would help position PMD more clearly within the broader literature on computational optimal transport.

2.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** For generative modeling, the comparison is limited to MMD-based methods (GMMN). At the time of this work, Wasserstein GANs (WGAN) represented the state-of-the-art for training generative models using the Wasserstein distance. The paper mentions WGAN briefly but dismisses it for requiring a critic network. However, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs (e.g., PMD's parameter-free nature vs. WGAN's scalability and potentially better performance on high-dimensional image data) and an empirical comparison, even on a smaller scale, would have been highly informative and would have more convincingly demonstrated the significance of PMD.

3.  **Gradient Discontinuity:** The derivation of the gradient in Eq. (3) relies on the assumption that the optimal matching M* is locally constant. While this is a reasonable simplification that holds almost everywhere, the objective function is technically non-smooth and the gradient is discontinuous at points where the optimal matching changes. This could potentially lead to optimization instability.
    *   **Suggestion:** It would be beneficial to briefly acknowledge this theoretical point. The authors could add a sentence noting that while the objective is not continuously differentiable, the stochastic nature of the training process allows SGD to effectively optimize it, as demonstrated by the empirical results.

4.  **Choice of Ground Metric:** PMD replaces MMD's kernel choice with the choice of a ground metric `d(·, ·)`. The experiments exclusively use the L1 distance, which, as the authors note, can lead to blurry results for image generation. This implies that the performance of PMD may be quite sensitive to this choice.
    *   **Suggestion:** To better demonstrate the flexibility and power of the framework, it would be interesting to see results using a different ground metric, particularly for the image generation task. For instance, using a perceptual distance based on features from a pre-trained CNN (as is common in style transfer) instead of a pixel-wise L1 distance might produce visually superior samples and would showcase an important advantage of the optimal transport framework.

### Decision

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and theoretically sound method for comparing distributions in deep learning. The connection to the Wasserstein metric is clear, and the demonstrated advantages over MMD are significant and well-supported by empirical evidence. The main weaknesses concern computational scalability and a limited comparison to WGANs. However, the core contribution is strong and opens up a new and interesting direction for research. The paper meets the high standards for originality, quality, and significance expected at NeurIPS. I recommend acceptance.
