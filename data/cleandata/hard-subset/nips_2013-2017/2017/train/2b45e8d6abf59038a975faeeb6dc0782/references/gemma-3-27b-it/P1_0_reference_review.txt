## Peer Review for "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric, offering a compelling alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors demonstrate its theoretical consistency and empirical benefits in domain adaptation and generative modeling. While the core idea is well-motivated and the experimental results promising, the presentation could be more polished, and a more thorough discussion of limitations is needed. I lean towards **Accept** with minor revisions, as the novelty and potential impact warrant inclusion in NIPS, but improvements are crucial.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of estimating statistical divergence between distributions when only samples are available. It proposes PMD, which minimizes the cost of matching samples from two distributions. This is framed as an estimator for the Wasserstein metric. The authors convincingly position PMD within the landscape of sample-based divergences, highlighting the limitations of MMD (bandwidth tuning, gradient saturation, large batch size requirements) and contrasting them with PMD’s advantages. The connection to optimal transport and Wasserstein metric is clearly established. The paper correctly cites relevant work on MMD, Wasserstein GANs, and domain adaptation/generative modeling.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** PMD offers a fresh perspective on estimating Wasserstein distance from samples, sidestepping the need for kernel bandwidth selection inherent in MMD.  The focus on a matching-based approach is a valuable contribution.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The proof of strong consistency for PMD is a significant strength, solidifying the theoretical underpinning of the proposed metric.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate PMD's practical benefits. The faster convergence and comparable/improved performance are noteworthy.  The inclusion of convergence speed comparisons (Fig. 3a) is particularly valuable.
* **Clear Advantages over MMD:** The authors articulate a strong case for PMD's advantages: no bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients (supported by Fig. 2b), and smaller mini-batch size requirements.
* **Well-structured:** The paper follows a logical flow, clearly defining PMD, explaining its theoretical properties, and presenting experimental validation.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions the complexity of the matching algorithm (O(N<sup>3</sup>) for exact, O(N<sup>2</sup>) for approximate), it doesn’t sufficiently address the practical implications for very large N.  How does the computational bottleneck of the matching step scale with problem size, and what strategies (beyond GPU parallelization briefly mentioned) can be employed to mitigate it?
* **Distance Metric Choice:** The paper exclusively uses the L1 distance in their experiments. While justified, it would be valuable to briefly explore the sensitivity of PMD to different distance metrics (e.g., Euclidean) and discuss any potential trade-offs.  The statement that L1 is better for natural images needs more support - why?
* **Generative Modeling Results - Blurriness:** The authors acknowledge blurry results in generative modeling. This is a significant weakness.  More investigation is needed to understand why PMD struggles with generating sharp images, and potential solutions should be discussed. Is it the L1 distance, or a limitation of PMD itself?
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** More comprehensive ablation studies are needed. For example, how does the performance of PMD change with varying mini-batch sizes, especially in the generative modeling experiments?
* **Clarity of Gradient Explanation:** The explanation of the stronger gradient property of PMD could be more intuitive. While the statement about outlier samples is helpful, a more visual illustration or simpler explanation would be beneficial.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide empirical error analysis (Fig. 2a) for the approximation of the Wasserstein metric, it would be beneficial to analyze the *types* of errors PMD makes compared to MMD in the application tasks.

**4. Originality & Significance:**

The paper presents a novel approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric, making it a worthwhile contribution. The significance lies in its potential to improve the performance and efficiency of various deep learning tasks that rely on distributional divergence estimation, particularly those where MMD suffers from limitations. If the practical scalability concerns can be addressed, PMD could become a widely adopted alternative to MMD.

**5. Quality & Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental setup is clearly described. However, as mentioned above, some sections could benefit from more detailed explanations and discussions. The figures are clear and informative. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to Wasserstein distance estimation.
* Strong theoretical foundation (consistency proof).
* Promising empirical results in two important applications.
* Addresses limitations of MMD.
* Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Computational cost of the matching algorithm may be a limiting factor.
* Generative modeling results need improvement.
* Limited exploration of different distance metrics.
* Lack of comprehensive ablation studies.



**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

The authors should:

1. **Expand the discussion of computational complexity:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the scalability of the matching algorithm and potential mitigation strategies.
2. **Investigate and address the blurry image generation issue:** Conduct more experiments and analyze the limitations of PMD in this context.
3. **Consider exploring alternative distance metrics:** Briefly assess the impact of different distance metrics on performance.
4. **Include additional ablation studies:** Analyze the sensitivity of PMD to mini-batch size and other hyperparameters.
5. **Clarify the gradient explanation:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of why PMD exhibits stronger gradients.
6. **Add error analysis:** Compare the types of errors made by PMD and MMD in the application tasks.
