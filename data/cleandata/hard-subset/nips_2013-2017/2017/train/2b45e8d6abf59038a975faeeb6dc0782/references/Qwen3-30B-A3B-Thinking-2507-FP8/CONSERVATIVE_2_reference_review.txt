

# Review of "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the cost of minimum weight matching between samples from two distributions, as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for measuring statistical divergence. The authors claim PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric and offers advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyper-parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes.

## Summary and Context

The paper builds on established work in optimal transport and statistical divergences. The theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is correctly established (Theorem 3.3), though the proof relies on standard results from optimal transport theory (Varadarajan's theorem and stability of optimal transport). The authors compare PMD with MMD, which is a well-established method in the field, but fail to adequately compare with more recent Wasserstein-based approaches like WGAN or WGAN-GP, which have become standard baselines in generative modeling.

## Strengths

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The theoretical analysis connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric is technically sound. The empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling show PMD outperforming MMD on some tasks, which is a non-trivial finding.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comprehensive validation against established baselines:** The paper compares PMD only with MMD, ignoring more recent and relevant baselines like WGAN and WGAN-GP, which have become standard in generative modeling. This is a significant omission, as MMD is already considered outdated in many generative modeling contexts. The authors should have compared against these more modern approaches to demonstrate the true value of their method.

2. **Insufficient evidence for claimed advantages:** The paper claims PMD has "stronger gradients" than MMD (Section 3.2), but the evidence is limited to a single figure (Fig. 2b) showing normalized gradient magnitudes. The claim lacks quantitative justification and doesn't address how these gradient properties translate to actual performance gains across different architectures and datasets. The authors also don't provide any theoretical justification for why the L1 distance used in PMD would yield stronger gradients than the RBF kernel used in MMD.

3. **Understated computational costs:** The paper acknowledges the O(N³) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm (Section 2.3) but fails to thoroughly discuss how this impacts scalability for large datasets. The authors claim PMD can use mini-batch sizes of 100, but don't provide evidence that the matching quality remains sufficient at this scale. For large-scale applications, the computational overhead of the matching step may outweigh any potential benefits.

4. **Limited scope of empirical validation:** The experiments are limited to two tasks (domain adaptation and generative modeling) and a small set of datasets (Office, MNIST, SVHN, LFW). The paper doesn't demonstrate PMD's effectiveness on more challenging benchmarks or in settings where MMD has been particularly successful.

5. **Inadequate comparison with related work:** The paper cites prior work on matching-based approaches but doesn't sufficiently differentiate PMD from existing methods. For instance, the connection to the Earth Mover's Distance (EMD) is mentioned but not properly contextualized with prior work that has used similar matching concepts.

## Methodological Concerns

The most concerning aspect is the paper's failure to provide sufficient evidence for its core claims. The authors state that PMD "has stronger gradient" than MMD, but this claim is not rigorously validated. The gradient analysis in Section 3.2 is largely theoretical and lacks empirical support beyond a single figure. The paper also makes the claim that "PMD can use normal mini-batch size, such as 100," but doesn't provide evidence that this leads to better matching quality or convergence properties compared to using larger batch sizes.

The paper's computational cost analysis (Section 5.3) is superficial. While the authors note that the matching step is "comparable with the cost of SGD," they don't provide a detailed breakdown of how this translates to wall-clock time for large-scale training, which is crucial for practical adoption.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents an interesting idea that has some theoretical grounding, it falls short of the rigorous validation expected at NIPS. The claims about PMD's advantages over MMD are not sufficiently supported by comprehensive experiments against relevant baselines. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with modern Wasserstein-based methods and a deeper analysis of the computational trade-offs involved.

The paper does not demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods that would justify acceptance at NIPS. The authors should provide more extensive validation against established baselines and address the computational concerns before the paper can be considered for publication.

## Pro/Con Acceptance

**Pros:**
- The theoretical connection to Wasserstein metric is sound
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized
- The empirical results on two tasks show some promising results

**Cons:**
- Limited comparison with relevant modern baselines (WGAN, WGAN-GP)
- Insufficient evidence for key claims (stronger gradients, smaller batch size benefits)
- Understated computational costs of the matching algorithm
- Limited scope of empirical validation

I recommend rejection. While the idea has potential, the paper does not provide the level of rigorous validation required to demonstrate a meaningful advance over existing methods. The authors should conduct more comprehensive experiments and address the computational concerns before resubmitting.
