## Peer Review for "Population Matching Discrepancy and Applications in Deep Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a sample-based estimator for the Wasserstein metric (Earth Mover’s Distance). The authors argue that PMD offers advantages over the commonly used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), specifically in terms of hyperparameter sensitivity (no kernel bandwidth tuning), gradient behavior, and the ability to use standard mini-batch sizes during training. They provide theoretical justification for PMD being a strongly consistent estimator and demonstrate its effectiveness in two deep learning applications: domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing improved performance and convergence speed compared to MMD.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work on statistical divergence estimation, notably MMD [Gretton et al., 2006].  It correctly positions itself within the context of recent advancements in generative modeling (GANs, VAEs) and domain adaptation, highlighting the need for robust and efficient divergence measures. The connection to optimal transport theory and the Wasserstein metric is well-established [Villani, 2008; Rubner et al., 2000], and the paper appropriately cites existing work utilizing the Wasserstein metric in deep learning, such as WGAN [Arjovsky et al., 2017].  The authors acknowledge and differentiate PMD from existing dual-form Wasserstein metric estimators.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for PMD as a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, grounding the proposed method in solid mathematical principles.
*   **Practical Advantages:** The claims regarding reduced hyperparameter tuning (eliminating the kernel bandwidth) and improved gradient behavior are compelling.  The ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes for SGD is a significant practical benefit, particularly for large-scale deep learning.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments in both domain adaptation and generative modeling demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD. The performance improvements over MMD are notable, and the convergence speed results provide further support for the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and explanations of the key concepts. The inclusion of pseudocode for parameter learning (Fig. 1) is helpful.
* **Detailed Analysis**: The empirical analysis regarding the approximation error and gradient behavior (Fig 2) are insightful and support the claims made in the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Cost of Matching:** While the authors acknowledge the O(N<sup>3</sup>) complexity of the Hungarian algorithm and offer an O(N<sup>2</sup>) approximation, the matching step remains a potential bottleneck, particularly for very large population sizes.  A more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost in the matching algorithm would be beneficial.
*   **Distance Metric Limitation:** The paper primarily uses the L1 distance for PMD. While justifiable, this choice might limit the method's effectiveness for certain types of data (e.g., images).  Exploring other distance metrics and their impact on performance could be valuable.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are focused on relatively standard benchmarks.  Exploring more challenging or diverse datasets and architectures could further strengthen the claims.
*   **WGAN Comparison**: The paper positions PMD as an alternative to WGAN, but a direct comparison with WGAN, including a discussion of the scale-free property of PMD versus the scale-dependence of WGAN, would be stronger.
* **Lack of Ablation Study**: While the paper compares PMD and MMD, it lacks an ablation study to understand the impact of each advantage (bandwidth-free, stronger gradient, smaller mini-batch) independently.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric from samples.
*   The empirical results demonstrate clear benefits over a widely used alternative (MMD).
*   The practical advantages of PMD (reduced hyperparameter tuning, improved gradient behavior, smaller mini-batch sizes) make it a potentially valuable tool for deep learning practitioners.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the matching step could be a limiting factor for very large datasets.
*   The experimental evaluation, while positive, could be broadened to include more diverse datasets and architectures.
*   The choice of L1 distance may be suboptimal for certain applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** for NIPS 2017.  The proposed method (PMD) is a valuable contribution to the field of statistical divergence estimation and has the potential to significantly improve the performance and efficiency of deep learning models. While the computational cost of the matching step and the limited scope of experiments are minor concerns, the theoretical justification, practical advantages, and empirical results outweigh these drawbacks. The clarity and quality of the writing are also commendable.



**Rating:** 8.5/10

**Confidence:** 9/10
