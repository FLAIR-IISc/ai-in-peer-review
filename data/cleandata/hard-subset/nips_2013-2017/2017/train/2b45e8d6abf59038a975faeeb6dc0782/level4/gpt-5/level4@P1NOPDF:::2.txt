This paper proposes the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a stronger alternative to MMD for distribution matching. PMD is presented as a sampled version of the Wasserstein metric (Earth Mover’s Distance) and is claimed to have several advantages over MMD, most notably stronger gradients, the ability to use smaller mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters.

For training generative models, MMD indeed suffers from weak gradients and typically requires large mini-batches, so the proposals in this paper offer a compelling solution to both issues. The small mini-batch claim is supported convincingly by the empirical results. However, the evidence for the “stronger gradients” claim is less satisfactory. Because MMD depends critically on the kernel bandwidth σ, it is essential to evaluate performance over an appropriate range of σ values—or at least report results for a well-chosen σ—before concluding that PMD provides stronger gradients.

The claim of having fewer hyperparameters is also not fully supported. PMD depends on a distance metric, which itself may introduce additional hyperparameters, much like the kernel choice and bandwidth in MMD. Moreover, constructing a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces is challenging; PMD may therefore inherit similar vulnerabilities to those of MMD due to its reliance on distance computations. On the other hand, there are well-known heuristics for selecting the MMD bandwidth (e.g., median heuristic), which can render MMD effectively hyperparameter-free in practice. It would be useful to compare against such heuristics and frame the comparison accordingly.

Overall, the method’s ability to use small mini-batches for fast training is a clear strength, and it appears to be a practical improvement over large-batch MMD approaches. Nevertheless, its dependence on a distance metric may limit its effectiveness on higher-dimensional data.
