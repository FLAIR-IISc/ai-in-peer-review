- Paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the Wasserstein distance between empirical distributions of two minibatches, computed via exact O(N³) or approximate O(N²) algorithms.  
- Strength: Novel, well-motivated experimental exploration of PMD as a training objective with reasonable image synthesis results.  
- Major concern 1: PMD requires large minibatch size N to estimate Wasserstein distances for diverse, multimodal distributions, failing to capture variance (e.g., Gaussian mixtures).  
- Evidence: Experiments (and SVHN samples) suggest low diversity/entropy (overrepresentation of digit '8'), indicating insufficient N.  
- Major concern 2: With small N, PMD optimization can converge to suboptimal minima because minibatch-based Wasserstein fails to capture global structure, undermining practical use when large batches are infeasible.  
- Comparison to MMD: MMD avoids PMD’s limitations—MMD has an unbiased estimator enabling convergence with batch_size=2 (Gretton et al., 2012), while PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator.  
- Rebuttal note: Authors addressed Gaussian mixture experiments, confirming PMD’s failure on multimodal variance but did not clarify PMD’s dependence on large N for reliable gradient estimation.  
- Minor issues: Typo ("usally" → "usually") and need to clarify whether SVHN low entropy is due to PMD or other factors (e.g., architecture).  
- Required revisions: Explicitly state PMD’s N-dependent bias in abstract/conclusion, contrast PMD’s gradient limitations with MMD’s unbiased estimator, and include Gaussian mixture experiments.  
- Overall assessment: Core idea interesting but fundamentally limited by scalability; current version overstates PMD’s advantages relative to MMD.  
- Final verdict: Weak Reject — revisions required to clarify limitations and provide empirical justification for PMD’s practicality.
