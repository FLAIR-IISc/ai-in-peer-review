

This paper introduces population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a superior alternative to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. The authors demonstrate that PMD corresponds to a sampled variant of the Wasserstein metric (earth mover's distance) and highlight key advantages over MMD, particularly stronger gradients during training and the ability to operate effectively with smaller mini-batch sizes, alongside a reduced hyperparameter count.  

For generative model training, MMD indeed suffers from weak gradients and necessitates large mini-batches—issues that PMD appears to address compellingly. The empirical validation of PMD’s efficacy with small mini-batches is well-supported. However, the claim regarding stronger gradients requires more rigorous justification. Since MMD’s performance is highly sensitive to the bandwidth parameter σ, any comparison must account for optimal σ selection or a comprehensive range of σ values. The paper’s current presentation does not adequately address this dependency, weakening the gradient-strength argument.  

The assertion that PMD involves "fewer hyperparameters" is also not well-substantiated. PMD relies on a distance metric (e.g., Euclidean, Mahalanobis), which may introduce additional hyperparameters—potentially negating the claimed advantage. Furthermore, reliably estimating a meaningful distance metric in high-dimensional spaces remains challenging, implying PMD faces the same fundamental limitation as MMD: dependence on a distance metric. In contrast, MMD benefits from established heuristics (e.g., the median heuristic for σ selection), which can render it effectively hyperparameter-free in practice. A fair comparison should evaluate MMD using such heuristics rather than treating it as inherently hyperparameter-heavy.  

Overall, PMD offers a valuable practical improvement by enabling fast training through small mini-batch usage, representing a meaningful advance over large-batch MMD approaches. However, it retains the core limitation of relying on a distance metric, which may hinder its effectiveness for high-dimensional data modeling. The method shows promise but requires further validation on complex, high-dimensional distributions to fully establish its broader applicability. The empirical results are promising yet insufficient to fully resolve the concerns about gradient strength and hyperparameter transparency.
