The authors present Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a population-based divergence between probability distributions, and demonstrate its consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein distance. The proposed estimator is conceptually simple and differentiable, making it suitable for training neural network-based models.

The authors provide a thorough comparison between PMD and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), the most prominent population-based divergence in machine learning. They highlight the drawbacks of MMD, including its sensitive kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, weak gradients, and the need for a large mini-batch size. In contrast, PMD has no kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter, produces stronger gradients, and can operate with a smaller mini-batch size.

The paper is well-structured and written, with proper references to previous work. The theoretical results appear to be correct, and the experimental analysis is adequate. The authors compare PMD to MMD and other methods for domain adaptation and generative modeling, demonstrating the effectiveness of PMD in these tasks.

One potential limitation of the paper is the lack of experimentation with generative modeling in domains with multiple modes. It is unclear whether PMD would perform well in such scenarios, particularly when the number of modes is larger than the sample size (N). Further investigation into this aspect would strengthen the paper.

Overall, this is a good paper that presents a potentially useful method for comparing distributions. The authors have demonstrated the effectiveness of PMD in various tasks and have provided a thorough analysis of its strengths and weaknesses. With some additional experimentation, this work could have a significant impact on the field.

Strengths:
- Conceptually simple and differentiable estimator
- Thorough comparison with MMD
- Effective in domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks

Weaknesses:
- Exact calculation has cubic complexity
- Limited experimentation with generative modeling in multi-modal domains

Acceptance arguments:
- PMD is a novel and effective method for comparing distributions
- The paper provides a thorough analysis of PMD's strengths and weaknesses
- The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in various tasks

Rejection arguments:
- Limited experimentation with generative modeling in multi-modal domains
- The cubic complexity of exact calculation may be a limitation in certain scenarios
