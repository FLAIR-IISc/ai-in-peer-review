This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a metric for comparing two probability distributions, defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches sampled from these distributions. The authors propose computing this distance for generative modeling using either an exact O(N^3) algorithm or a faster O(N^2) approximation.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work is its novel application of a direct, non-adversarial computation of the Wasserstein distance for training generative models. The experiments demonstrating this approach are interesting and provide a valuable data point in the space of distribution-matching techniques for deep learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on Large Minibatch Sizes:** A significant concern with the proposed PMD is its reliance on a large minibatch size (N) to obtain a reliable estimate of the true Wasserstein distance between the underlying distributions. This is particularly problematic for complex and multimodal target distributions. If N is not sufficiently large, the optimization landscape may be distorted, potentially causing the model to converge to a global minimum that does not correspond to the true data distribution.

2.  **Mode Collapse and Low Sample Diversity:** A potential consequence of using an insufficient minibatch size is that the learned distribution may exhibit lower entropy or diversity than the true distribution. This issue appears to be evident in the qualitative results. For instance, the SVHN samples presented in Figure 4 seem to have low diversity, with a noticeable overrepresentation of certain digits, like the digit "8". I suspect that these problems would be readily apparent even in simpler synthetic settings, such as trying to match a mixture of Gaussians (especially when learning the component variances).

3.  **Biased Gradient Estimation:** This weakness becomes particularly clear when comparing PMD to alternative metrics like the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). MMD, when using the unbiased estimator from its original formulation ("A Kernel Two-Sample Test"), can be optimized effectively with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) even with very small minibatches (e.g., batch size of 2), and is guaranteed to converge to the correct distribution. In contrast, the gradient of the PMD, as estimated on a minibatch, is a biased estimator of the true gradient of the Wasserstein distance. The paper does not provide or reference a known unbiased estimator for the PMD gradient, which raises fundamental questions about its suitability for SGD-based training in the practical, small-batch regime common to deep learning.

4.  **Empirical Performance:** While the approach is interesting, the empirical quality of the generated samples does not appear to reach the level of state-of-the-art methods like the Wasserstein GAN, which this work aims to provide an alternative to.

**Post-Rebuttal Comments:**

I thank the authors for their response and the additional experiments provided in the rebuttal. My central concern regarding the bias of the minibatch-based PMD estimator remains. I strongly recommend that the authors explicitly and clearly discuss the limitations of PMD in the final manuscript, particularly in contrast to metrics like MMD.

A key distinction that must be highlighted is that MMD possesses an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator, allowing it to converge correctly even with small minibatches. PMD, as presented, appears to lack this crucial property. This makes its convergence guarantees in the practical, small-batch training regime questionable and is a critical point for accurately positioning the work within the existing literature.
