This paper presents the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a viable alternative to the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching applications. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein metric, or earth mover's distance, offering several advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients and the ability to accommodate smaller mini-batch sizes, as well as fewer hyperparameters.

In the context of training generative models, the MMD metric is known to suffer from weak gradients and the requirement of large mini-batches, making the proposals in this paper a valuable solution to these issues. The empirical results provide a satisfactory verification of the small mini-batch claim. However, the verification of the stronger gradients claim is less convincing, as the MMD metric's performance depends on the scale parameter sigma. To make a more robust claim, it would be essential to consider either the optimal sigma or a range of sigmas.

The claim that PMD has fewer hyperparameters is not entirely supported, as PMD relies on a distance metric that may itself contain additional hyperparameters, similar to the MMD case. Furthermore, obtaining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces can be challenging, which may lead to PMD facing similar issues as MMD. Notably, there are established heuristics for choosing the bandwidth parameter in MMD, and it would be beneficial to compare PMD against these heuristics, effectively treating MMD as a hyperparameter-free metric.

Overall, the proposed method has the desirable property of allowing small mini-batch sizes, facilitating faster training. This appears to be a valid improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, the reliance on a distance metric remains a potential limitation, which may hinder the success of PMD in modeling high-dimensional data.
