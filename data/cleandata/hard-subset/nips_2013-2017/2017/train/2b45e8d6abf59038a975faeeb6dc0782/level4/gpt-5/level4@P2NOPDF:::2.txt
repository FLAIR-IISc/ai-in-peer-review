This paper introduces the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to MMD for distribution matching. PMD is presented as an empirical approximation to the Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance and is argued to offer several advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients, effective training with smaller minibatches, and fewer hyperparameters.

For training generative models, MMD is known to yield weak gradients and to require large minibatches. In this respect, the paper’s proposals are appealing and provide a plausible remedy to both issues. The empirical validation of the small-minibatch claim is convincing. However, the evidence for stronger gradients is less compelling. Because the quality of MMD gradients depends critically on the kernel bandwidth σ, any comparison should consider either the best σ or a range of σ values; otherwise, the conclusion may be confounded by suboptimal bandwidth selection.

The claim of having fewer hyperparameters also seems under-supported. PMD depends on a choice of distance metric, which can itself introduce hyperparameters, just as MMD does. Moreover, defining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces is challenging, so PMD may inherit the same dependence on metric choice that affects MMD. In contrast, MMD benefits from common heuristics for bandwidth selection; it would be fair to compare against these heuristics and, in that setting, consider MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free as well.

Overall, PMD’s ability to work with small minibatches is a practical and attractive property that can accelerate training. The approach appears to offer a meaningful improvement over large-batch MMD methods. Nonetheless, its reliance on a distance metric may limit performance on high-dimensional data, where metric selection and reliability remain difficult.
