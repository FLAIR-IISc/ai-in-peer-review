

Review:  
The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches sampled from the respective distributions. This distance is computed via an exact \(O(N^3)\) or approximate \(O(N^2)\) algorithm.  

**Strengths:**  
The empirical validation of PMD represents a valuable contribution, particularly in demonstrating its behavior through experiments.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Scalability to multimodal distributions:** PMD requires large minibatch sizes \(N\) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between diverse multimodal distributions. This limitation is likely to manifest even in simple cases, such as matching a mixture of Gaussians (including learning variances), where insufficient \(N\) would prevent capturing the full multimodal structure.  
2. **Optimization pitfalls:** With small \(N\), the optimization may converge to a global minimum that does not align with the true distribution. For instance, the learned distribution may exhibit reduced entropy.  
3. **Empirical evidence:** Figure 4 shows limited diversity in SVHN samples (e.g., digit '8' appears disproportionately often), suggesting PMD may fail to capture distributional richness at practical minibatch sizes.  

**Minor issues:**  
- Line 141: "s/usally/usually" → "usually" (typo).  

**Rebuttal update:**  
The authors’ additional experiments are appreciated. However, the rebuttal must clarify PMD’s limitations relative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD):  
- MMD is viable with batch size \(=2\) using the unbiased estimator from Gretton et al. (2012), enabling SGD-based training and convergence to the true distribution with small minibatches.  
- In contrast, PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator, preventing similar convergence guarantees. The authors should explicitly state this fundamental distinction in the revised manuscript.
