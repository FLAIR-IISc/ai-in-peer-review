This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a metric for comparing two distributions defined as the Wasserstein distance between minibatches sampled from them. The Wasserstein distance is calculated using either an exact O(N^3) algorithm or an O(N^2) approximation.

**Strengths:**

*   The empirical evaluation of this method for computing the Wasserstein distance is an interesting contribution. However, it is worth noting that the quality of the generated images does not yet match that of state-of-the-art methods like Wasserstein GANs.

**Weaknesses:**

*   A significant limitation of the proposed distance is that a large minibatch size (N) appears necessary to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between diverse, multimodal distributions. I suspect this issue would become apparent even in simpler settings, such as attempting to match a mixture of Gaussians (including learning the variances).
*   When the minibatch size N is insufficient, the optimization objective may possess a global minimum that does not correspond to the true data distribution. This could lead, for example, to the learned distribution having lower entropy than the target. This concern seems to be supported by the experimental results; the SVHN samples in Figure 4 exhibit low diversity, with a noticeable overrepresentation of the digit '8'.

**Update After Rebuttal:**

I have read the authors' rebuttal and thank them for the additional experiments.

Following the rebuttal, I believe the authors should more clearly delineate the limitations of PMD, particularly in contrast to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). MMD can perform well even with very small batch sizes (e.g., N=2). This is because MMD can be optimized effectively with SGD using the unbiased estimator proposed in the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper. Consequently, MMD-based models can converge to the target distribution even when trained with small minibatches. PMD, in contrast, does not appear to have a known unbiased estimator for its gradient, which is a critical distinction that should be discussed.
