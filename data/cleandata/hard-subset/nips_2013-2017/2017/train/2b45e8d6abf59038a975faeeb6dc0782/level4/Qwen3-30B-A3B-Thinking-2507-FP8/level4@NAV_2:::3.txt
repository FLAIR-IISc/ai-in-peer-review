

The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable distribution distance estimator based on minimum weight matching between sample populations, and establishes its consistency with the first Wasserstein metric. This work directly extends NIPS 2017 contributions like WGAN (Arjovsky et al.) and GMMN (Li et al.), addressing key limitations of MMD—the most prevalent sample-based divergence in prior NIPS work—by eliminating kernel bandwidth hyperparameters, providing stronger gradients, and enabling smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs. 1000). The authors thoroughly compare PMD to MMD across domain adaptation (Office benchmark) and generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW), demonstrating superior convergence speed and performance.  

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* Theoretical analysis (Theorem 3.3) rigorously proves PMD’s strong consistency with Wasserstein distance, and empirical validation (Fig. 2b, 5a) robustly supports claims about gradient strength and mini-batch efficiency. The implementation details (e.g., exact/approximate matching trade-offs) are technically sound.  
*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-structured, with clear definitions (Sec. 2.1), intuitive graphical explanations (Fig. 1), and precise experimental design (Sec. 5). The comparison to MMD (Sec. 3.2) is particularly instructive.  
*Originality:* PMD offers a novel combinatorial approach distinct from kernel-based (MMD) or dual-approximation methods (WGAN), with clear differentiation from NIPS 2016-2017 proceedings (e.g., GMMN, WGAN).  
*Significance:* The method directly addresses a critical bottleneck in deep learning—distribution distance estimation—enabling faster, more stable training for domain adaptation and generative modeling, with immediate applicability to practitioners.  

**Weaknesses:**  
The theoretical analysis does not discuss finite-sample error bounds for PMD (a limitation noted in the conclusion), and the generative modeling results on SVHN/LFW show blurry samples (Fig. 4), suggesting PMD’s L1 distance may not suit natural images. While the paper acknowledges the cubic complexity of exact matching (Sec. 2.3), the empirical validation of the approximated matching’s statistical impact (e.g., Fig. 5a) could be more thorough for large-N scenarios.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel, theoretically grounded, empirically superior to MMD in key metrics, solves practical training bottlenecks.  
*Con:* Limited exploration of PMD’s behavior when N < number of modes (a potential constraint in complex generative tasks), and the generative modeling results for natural images require further validation.  

The paper aligns with NIPS scope (distribution estimation for deep learning) and makes a clear, impactful contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses do not undermine the overall scientific quality.
