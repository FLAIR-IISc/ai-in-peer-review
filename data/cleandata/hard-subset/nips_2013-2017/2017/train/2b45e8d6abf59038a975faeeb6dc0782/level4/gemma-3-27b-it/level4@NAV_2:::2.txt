This paper presents the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a compelling alternative to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for estimating distributional distance and its use as a training objective. The core idea – framing PMD as a sample-based approximation of the Wasserstein metric – is well-motivated. A key strength lies in addressing known drawbacks of MMD, specifically its sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, potential for weak gradients, and the need for large mini-batches. The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric, demonstrating PMD as a strongly consistent estimator, provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.

The paper is well-structured, clearly explaining the definition of PMD, the parameter learning algorithm, and the theoretical underpinnings. The empirical validation on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks provides strong evidence of PMD's advantages, particularly its faster convergence and comparable or superior performance to MMD. The analysis of gradient behavior (Fig. 2(b)) effectively illustrates the more consistent gradient magnitudes achieved with PMD compared to MMD.

However, the claim of having “fewer hyper-parameters” warrants further scrutiny. While PMD avoids the kernel bandwidth parameter of MMD, it still relies on a distance metric (e.g., Euclidean distance) which, in high-dimensional spaces, can be sensitive to the choice of metric and potential scaling issues. A discussion of this trade-off, and a comparison to established heuristics for bandwidth selection in MMD (treating MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free in such cases), would strengthen this argument. Additionally, while the paper shows improved gradient behavior, a more comprehensive comparison accounting for optimal or well-tuned MMD bandwidths would provide a more robust evaluation.

Regarding the experimental setup, the use of a single thread for the matching algorithms is a limitation, though the authors acknowledge potential GPU acceleration. Furthermore, the empirical analysis of approximation error (Fig. 2(a)) provides valuable insight, but could be complemented with an analysis of how the choice of distance metric influences the approximation quality.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to distributional distance estimation based on a solid theoretical foundation.
*   Addresses key limitations of MMD (gradient issues, mini-batch size).
*   Strong empirical results on two relevant deep learning tasks.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The claim of fewer hyperparameters needs further qualification.
*   Potential sensitivity to the choice of distance metric in high dimensions is not fully addressed.
*   Limited scalability of the matching algorithm without GPU acceleration.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed PMD offers a promising alternative to MMD, particularly in scenarios where efficient gradient-based optimization with smaller mini-batches is crucial. Despite the minor concerns outlined above, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
