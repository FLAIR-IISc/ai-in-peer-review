

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a superior alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching applications. It demonstrates that PMD constitutes a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein metric (earth mover's distance) and offers several advantages over MMD, particularly stronger gradients, compatibility with smaller mini-batch sizes, and reduced hyperparameter requirements.  

For generative model training, MMD indeed suffers from weak gradients and necessitates large mini-batches, making the proposed PMD a compelling solution to both issues. The claim regarding smaller mini-batch efficacy is well-supported by the empirical results. However, the evidence for stronger gradients is less conclusive, as MMD's performance critically depends on the scale parameter σ; the authors should evaluate against either the optimal σ or a comprehensive range of σ values to substantiate this claim.  

The assertion of "fewer hyperparameters" is less well-justified. PMD relies on a distance metric, which may introduce additional hyperparameters—similar to MMD's σ—thereby undermining the claim. Furthermore, reliably estimating a distance metric in high-dimensional spaces remains challenging, meaning PMD may inherit MMD's dependency on distance metrics. In contrast, established heuristics exist for selecting MMD's bandwidth parameter; the authors should compare against these heuristics to position MMD as a hyperparameter-free baseline, rather than implying PMD inherently avoids this issue.  

Overall, PMD's ability to operate with small mini-batch sizes enables faster training, representing a valid improvement over large-batch MMD methods. Nevertheless, its continued reliance on a distance metric may constrain its applicability for high-dimensional data modeling.
