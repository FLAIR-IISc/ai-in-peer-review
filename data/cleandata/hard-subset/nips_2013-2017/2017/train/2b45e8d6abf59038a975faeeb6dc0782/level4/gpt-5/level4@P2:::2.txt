Summary
The paper introduces population matching discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based objective for distribution matching that computes a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two finite populations and shows strong consistency to the 1-Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance. The authors propose using PMD as a differentiable objective for learning distribution parameters and argue that, relative to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), PMD yields stronger gradients, works with smaller mini-batches, and eliminates kernel bandwidth tuning. They demonstrate benefits on domain adaptation and generative modeling, reporting faster convergence and better performance than MMD.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: MMD is known to suffer from weak gradients and large required mini-batch sizes in deep generative modeling; PMD targets both issues.
- Solid theoretical grounding: PMD is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance via empirical optimal transport.
- Practical algorithm: The paper details how to use PMD within SGD, including reusing matchings across several updates and employing exact or approximate matching.
- Empirical evidence: The small mini-batch claim is convincingly supported, and runtime/accuracy trade-offs with exact vs approximate matching are discussed.
- Applicability: Demonstrations on domain adaptation and generative modeling are relevant to the NIPS community.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Stronger gradients claim: The evidence is not fully persuasive. Because MMD’s gradients depend critically on the kernel bandwidth σ, any comparison should sweep σ (or use multi-kernel MMD) and report best-case or robust performance. As presented, the comparison could be confounded by suboptimal σ.
- “Fewer hyperparameters” claim: PMD removes kernel bandwidths but still depends on the choice of ground metric d(·,·), which itself may have hyperparameters (e.g., feature-space choices, scaling). In high-dimensional spaces, Euclidean L1/L2 distances can be ill-behaved; thus PMD may inherit the same sensitivity to metric choice that challenges MMD.
- High-dimensional distance reliability: The paper acknowledges blur in image generation with pixel-space L1, suggesting that metric choice is crucial. A more thorough study using learned or perceptual feature metrics (or comparing to MMD with standard bandwidth heuristics and multi-kernel setups) would clarify relative robustness.
- Baselines: Since MMD is often used with median-heuristic bandwidths and multi-kernel schemes to reduce tuning, treating MMD as effectively hyperparameter-light would be a fairer baseline. Comparisons to WGAN or Cramér distance variants would further contextualize PMD’s strengths.

Quality
- Theoretical results are correct and appropriately tied to optimal transport. The SGD treatment (not backpropagating through the discrete matching) is reasonable, though additional discussion of potential instability when the matching changes would help.
- Experiments are generally sound. The small-batch advantage is well supported; the gradient-strength finding needs a more rigorous bandwidth treatment for MMD.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well organized. A concise discussion on when approximate matching suffices, and guidelines for choosing d(·,·) in high dimensions (e.g., feature-space distances), would improve clarity and practicality.

Originality
- Bridging empirical optimal transport with deep learning objectives in this specific PMD form and training regime is a useful contribution, distinct from MMD-based and critic-based (WGAN) approaches.

Significance
- Allowing small mini-batches for distribution matching is practically valuable, especially for deep generative modeling and domain adaptation. However, sensitivity to the distance metric in high-dimensional spaces may limit impact unless metric selection is addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare MMD using:
  - Median heuristic for σ and multi-kernel MMD (as in GMMN).
  - Bandwidth sweeps to report best and robust performance.
- Evaluate PMD with alternative metrics:
  - Feature-space L2/L1 (e.g., VGG features) to mitigate pixel-space shortcomings.
  - Ablations showing sensitivity to metric scaling.
- Provide stability analysis for matching reuse across SGD steps (e.g., how often the matching changes, and its effect on convergence).
- Include comparisons to WGAN or Cramér distance for broader context.
- Report wall-clock time including matching vs. forward/backward passes under GPU acceleration assumptions.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong theoretical foundation; practical algorithm.
- Clear training efficiency gains via small mini-batches.
- Competitive or superior empirical results on two relevant tasks.

Cons for acceptance
- Claims about gradient strength and hyperparameter reduction are not fully substantiated against well-tuned or multi-kernel MMD.
- Sensitivity to ground-metric choice in high dimensions may limit generality.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated and theoretically grounded contribution that offers a practical alternative to MMD with real training benefits. The work would be strengthened by fairer MMD baselines and a deeper exploration of metric choice. I view PMD as a meaningful improvement for scenarios constrained by batch size, with caveats about distance metrics in high-dimensional settings.
