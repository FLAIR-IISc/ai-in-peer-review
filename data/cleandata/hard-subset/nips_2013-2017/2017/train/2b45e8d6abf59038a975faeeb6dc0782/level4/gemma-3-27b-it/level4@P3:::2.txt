This paper presents the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a compelling alternative to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for estimating distances between probability distributions, particularly within the context of deep learning. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (Earth Mover’s Distance) and highlight advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients, applicability with smaller mini-batch sizes, and a reduced need for hyperparameter tuning.

The motivation is well-established. MMD, while popular, can suffer from vanishing gradients and requires large mini-batches for reliable estimation, hindering efficient training of deep generative models and other applications. PMD directly addresses these limitations, offering a potentially significant improvement in training speed and stability. The theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric provides a solid foundation for the proposed method.

The strengths of this work are considerable. The core idea of formulating distribution distance as a minimum weight matching problem is insightful. The analysis demonstrating PMD’s consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein metric is a valuable contribution. The empirical results, particularly the demonstration of faster convergence with smaller mini-batches, are convincing and directly support the claims. The comparison to MMD on both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks provides a good assessment of PMD’s practical utility. The authors also acknowledge and address potential computational costs associated with solving the matching problem.

However, there are areas for improvement. The claim of “fewer hyperparameters” requires nuance. While PMD eliminates the kernel bandwidth parameter present in MMD, it introduces a dependency on the chosen distance metric `d(·, ·)`, which *can* itself have hyperparameters (e.g., parameters within a learned metric). A more precise discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper. The assertion of stronger gradients is not fully substantiated; the evaluation should ideally include a comparison against MMD with optimized bandwidth selection (e.g., using heuristics or a validation set), framing MMD as a similarly tuned metric.  The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the potential challenges of applying PMD to high-dimensional data, where the choice of distance metric becomes critical and may limit performance.  The limitations section could address this point explicitly. The effect of the choice of matching algorithm on performance could be explored in greater detail. While the authors mention using both exact and approximate methods, further analysis of the trade-offs between speed and accuracy would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical problem in deep learning (training stability and speed in distribution matching).
*   Offers a theoretically sound alternative to MMD with desirable properties (stronger gradients, smaller mini-batches).
*   Provides convincing empirical evidence demonstrating improved performance on relevant tasks.
*   The work is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The claim of "fewer hyperparameters" is somewhat misleading without acknowledging the potential hyperparameters of the distance metric.
*   The comparison to MMD could be made more rigorous by including optimized MMD configurations.
*   The limitations related to high-dimensional data and distance metric selection are not thoroughly discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed PMD metric offers a promising improvement over existing methods for estimating distribution distances, particularly in the context of deep learning. While some minor clarifications and a more nuanced discussion of hyperparameters and high-dimensional data would strengthen the paper, the core ideas are sound, the results are compelling, and the writing is clear. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
