

The paper introduces PMD (Population-based Metric Divergence), a novel approach for estimating the Wasserstein distance between probability distributions via population-based divergence. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance, providing a theoretically grounded alternative to existing methods. A key strength lies in its conceptual simplicity and differentiability, enabling seamless integration into neural network training pipelines—a significant advantage over non-differentiable alternatives. The method is thoroughly compared against MMD (Maximum Mean Discrepancy), the dominant population-based divergence in machine learning, with the authors clearly articulating PMD’s distinct theoretical and practical merits.

The paper addresses a critical limitation of exact PMD computation (cubic complexity in sample size) by proposing a quadratic-time approximation. Empirical results demonstrate that this approximation maintains competitive statistical performance across multiple benchmarks, mitigating the primary scalability concern. The theoretical analysis is sound, with proofs supporting consistency and convergence properties, and the experimental section is comprehensive. The authors validate PMD against MMD and other baselines on standard domain adaptation tasks (e.g., Office-Home, VisDA) and generative modeling (e.g., CIFAR-10, CelebA), showing consistent improvements in both settings. The discussion of MMD’s limitations (e.g., sensitivity to kernel choice, weaker theoretical guarantees for Wasserstein approximation) is particularly valuable.

The presentation is clear and well-structured, with appropriate references to foundational work (e.g., MMD [Gretton et al., 2012], optimal transport [Peyré & Cuturi, 2019]). Minor improvements could be made to the experimental setup descriptions, but these do not undermine the paper’s contributions. The method’s differentiability and scalability via the quadratic approximation are compelling for practitioners, especially in applications like GAN training where Wasserstein metrics are preferred but computationally prohibitive.

A notable gap concerns the evaluation of PMD in high-mode generative modeling scenarios. The authors demonstrate success on standard datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10 with ~10 modes), but they do not test the method when the sample size \(N\) is smaller than the number of modes—a common challenge in complex distributions (e.g., multi-modal image synthesis). For instance, on datasets like MNIST with 10 distinct digit classes or synthetic mixtures with 20+ modes, PMD’s performance could degrade if \(N < K\) (where \(K\) is the number of modes). The authors might consider adding experiments on such benchmarks (e.g., using a 100-mode mixture or a high-resolution multi-digit dataset) to assess robustness under these conditions. This omission is understandable given the scope, but it leaves an important question unanswered: *Does PMD reliably capture multi-modal structure when sample efficiency is critical?*

The paper makes a strong contribution to distribution comparison, offering a practical, theoretically sound alternative to MMD. The quadratic-time approximation effectively balances accuracy and scalability, and the empirical results support its utility across diverse tasks. While the generative modeling evaluation on high-mode data is missing, the existing experiments are thorough and convincing for standard settings. The authors have clearly articulated both the advantages and limitations of PMD, and the proposed approximation adequately addresses the main scalability hurdle.

In summary, this is a well-executed, impactful paper that advances the toolkit for distributional comparison. The method’s simplicity, differentiability, and competitive performance make it immediately applicable to a wide range of machine learning problems. The minor critique regarding multi-modal generative modeling is a valid extension but does not detract from the paper’s core strengths. The work is technically rigorous, clearly presented, and represents a meaningful step toward more efficient and effective distribution divergence estimation. I recommend acceptance.
