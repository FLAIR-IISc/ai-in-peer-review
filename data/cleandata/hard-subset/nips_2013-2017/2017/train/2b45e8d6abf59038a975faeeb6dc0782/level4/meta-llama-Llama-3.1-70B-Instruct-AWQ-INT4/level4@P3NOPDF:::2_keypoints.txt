- Proposes PMD as a superior alternative to MMD and shows PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance.  
- Claims advantages of PMD: stronger gradients, ability to use smaller mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters.  
- Empirical results convincingly show PMD works with smaller mini-batches and can speed up training.  
- The stronger-gradients claim is unconvincing because MMD’s gradients depend on the kernel bandwidth (sigma); comparison should include optimal or a range of sigmas.  
- The fewer-hyperparameters claim is weak since PMD depends on a distance metric, which can itself have hyperparameters.  
- Reliable distance metrics are hard to obtain in high-dimensional spaces, which may limit PMD’s effectiveness.  
- MMD has standard bandwidth heuristics that could make it effectively hyperparameter-free; a comparison to those heuristics is needed.  
- Overall: small-batch performance is a valid improvement, but reliance on a distance metric raises concerns for high-dimensional modeling.
