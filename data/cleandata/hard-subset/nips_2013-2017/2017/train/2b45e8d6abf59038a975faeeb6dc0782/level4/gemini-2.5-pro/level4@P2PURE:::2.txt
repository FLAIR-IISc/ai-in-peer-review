This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching applications. The authors show that PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein metric (or Earth Mover's Distance) and argue that it offers several advantages over MMD, including stronger training gradients, applicability with smaller mini-batch sizes, and fewer hyperparameters.

The MMD metric is known to suffer from weak gradients and require large mini-batches when training generative models, and the proposed method offers a potential solution to these issues. The claim of effectiveness with small mini-batches is well-supported by the empirical results. However, the verification of the "stronger gradients" claim is less convincing. Since the performance of MMD is highly dependent on its kernel bandwidth parameter (sigma), a fair comparison would require evaluating MMD with an optimal sigma or across a range of sigmas.

The claim of having fewer hyperparameters is also not fully substantiated. PMD's formulation relies on a distance metric, which may itself have hyperparameters, similar to the kernel in MMD. Furthermore, the difficulty of defining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces suggests that PMD may face challenges similar to those of MMD. It is also worth noting that standard heuristics exist for setting the MMD bandwidth parameter. For a more compelling comparison, the authors should consider these heuristics, which effectively render MMD a hyperparameter-free baseline.

Overall, the proposed method's primary advantage is its ability to operate with small mini-batches, potentially enabling faster training. As such, it appears to be a valuable improvement over MMD-based methods that require large batches. Nevertheless, its reliance on a user-defined distance metric remains a significant concern, which could limit its practical success, particularly for modeling high-dimensional data.
