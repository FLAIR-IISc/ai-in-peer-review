This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based divergence measure between probability distributions. The core idea is to define the distance as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two populations of samples drawn from the respective distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. A key practical advantage is that the estimator is differentiable, allowing it to be used as an objective function for training deep neural network models via gradient descent.

The paper provides a thorough comparison of PMD to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), which is arguably the most prominent sample-based divergence used in machine learning. The authors highlight several advantages of PMD over MMD, including the absence of a sensitive kernel bandwidth hyperparameter, the presence of stronger training gradients, and the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes for optimization. The computational complexity of the exact matching algorithm is acknowledged as a potential drawback, and a faster quadratic-time approximation is proposed and evaluated. The utility of PMD is demonstrated on two significant deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Simplicity:** The proposed estimator is conceptually simple and elegant, yet it provides a novel and practical approach to estimating the Wasserstein distance for training deep models. The connection to the classic minimum weight matching problem is clear and well-motivated.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical results appear correct and provide a solid foundation for the method. Proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric rigorously connects the practical algorithm to established theory in optimal transport.
*   **Practical Advantages:** The paper clearly articulates the practical benefits of PMD over MMD. The discussion on hyperparameter tuning, gradient saturation, and mini-batch size efficiency is convincing and addresses well-known challenges when using MMD in practice.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental analysis is comprehensive and well-executed. The authors compare PMD against MMD and other relevant methods on a standard domain adaptation benchmark and demonstrate its effectiveness for generative modeling on several image datasets. The results generally support the claims made in the paper.
*   **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and properly contextualizes the work by referencing prior art. The authors are commendably upfront about the computational cost of their method and empirically show that a proposed approximation works well without significantly degrading performance.

**Weaknesses / Questions for the Authors:**

*   The primary question concerns the behavior of PMD in domains with a large number of modes. I would have liked to see an investigation of how the method performs when the population size, N, is smaller than the number of modes in the underlying distributions. In such a scenario, can PMD still capture the global structure of the distributions, or does it risk focusing only on a subset of the modes, potentially leading to issues like mode collapse in generative modeling? Further insight into this aspect would strengthen the paper.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that introduces a promising and potentially very useful method for comparing probability distributions. The proposed PMD is well-grounded in theory, offers clear practical advantages over existing techniques like MMD, and is supported by strong empirical results. It represents a valuable contribution to the toolbox for training deep generative models and other architectures that rely on distributional matching.
