

The paper defines Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) between two distributions as the Wasserstein distance computed between two minibatches drawn from those distributions. This distance is evaluated using either an exact O(N³) algorithm or an approximate O(N²) method.  
Pros:  
- The experimental evaluation of this Wasserstein distance computation approach is noteworthy.  
Cons:  
- The proposed PMD metric likely requires large minibatch sizes N to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between diverse multimodal distributions. This suggests potential limitations when matching complex distributions, such as a mixture of Gaussians (including variance learning).  
- Insufficient N may cause optimization to converge to a suboptimal global minimum, yielding distributions with reduced entropy rather than matching the true target. For instance, the SVHN samples in Figure 4 exhibit low diversity, with digit 8 appearing disproportionately.  
Minor typos:  
- Line 141: "s/usally/usually" → "usually"  
Update:  
Following the rebuttal, the authors should clarify PMD's limitations relative to MMD. MMD remains effective at batch_size=2 and can be trained via SGD using the unbiased estimator from Gretton et al.’s "A Kernel Two-Sample Test." Thus, MMD converges to the correct distribution with small minibatches. Conversely, PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator, precluding similar convergence guarantees.
