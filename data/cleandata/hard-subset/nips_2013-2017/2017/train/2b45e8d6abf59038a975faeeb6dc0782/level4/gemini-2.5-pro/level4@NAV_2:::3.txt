This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions. The core idea is to define this distance as the cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between samples drawn from the two distributions. The authors provide a key theoretical contribution by proving that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The work is well-situated within the context of previous research, particularly positioning PMD as a direct alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD).

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper proposes a conceptually simple yet powerful alternative to MMD for comparing distributions in deep learning contexts. Its main advantages—having no kernel bandwidth hyperparameter to tune, exhibiting stronger gradient signals, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes during optimization—are significant practical contributions that could make it a highly useful tool for researchers and practitioners in areas like generative modeling and domain adaptation.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical claims, particularly the proof of strong consistency with the first Wasserstein metric, appear sound and provide a solid foundation for the method. The authors are also commendably transparent about the computational complexity (O(N³)), and they proactively address this by employing and evaluating a well-justified O(N²) approximation, demonstrating that the performance does not significantly degrade.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is clearly written and well-structured. The motivation is well-established, the methodology is explained logically, and the connections to both the Wasserstein metric and MMD are thoroughly elucidated.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental analysis is comprehensive and provides convincing support for the paper's claims. By evaluating PMD on two distinct and important tasks (domain adaptation and generative modeling), the authors demonstrate its effectiveness and empirically validate its advantages over MMD in terms of both final performance and convergence speed.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The primary weakness is the computational cost of the matching algorithm, which scales at least quadratically with the population size N. While the authors address this with an approximation and argue it is acceptable compared to backpropagation costs, this could still be a limiting factor for applications requiring very large population sizes, where MMD might be more scalable.
*   The experimental results on generative modeling are promising, but it would be beneficial to see an analysis of PMD's behavior in settings with a high number of distinct modes. A potential question for future work is how effectively PMD captures multi-modal distributions, especially when the population size N is smaller than the number of modes.

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable and practical method for comparing distributions. The theoretical grounding is solid, the proposed benefits are significant, and the empirical results are compelling. It successfully identifies and addresses several practical shortcomings of MMD, presenting a compelling alternative for the community.
