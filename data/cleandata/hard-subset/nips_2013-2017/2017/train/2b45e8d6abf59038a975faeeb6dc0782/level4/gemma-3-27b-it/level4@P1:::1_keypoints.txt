- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based minimum weight matching estimator of distribution distance.  
- PMD is theoretically shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Authors propose an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD as the objective.  
- Empirically, PMD shows faster convergence than MMD in a domain adaptation task.  
- PMD advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and ability to use normal mini-batch sizes.  
- Framing distance estimation as a minimum weight matching problem is an interesting and well-motivated idea.  
- Gradient property analysis for PMD is a valuable contribution.  
- Major weakness: computational cost of solving the matching problem limits scalability to large sample sizes.  
- Scalability concern persists despite discussion of approximate matching algorithms compared to MMD’s computational efficiency.  
- Simplifying assumption that PMD’s gradient does not propagate through matching requires further investigation.  
- Generated samples show qualitative improvement over some MMD baselines but lack diversity, questioning multimodal capture.  
- Comparison to MMD is incomplete—authors overlooked unbiased MMD estimators that allow small-batch training.  
- Empirical validation should be expanded to more challenging datasets and architectures.  
- Rebuttal: authors clarified small-batch MMD but did not address existence of an unbiased gradient estimator for PMD.  
- Suggestion: discuss how lack of unbiased PMD gradient estimators affects PMD’s theoretical properties and practical use.
