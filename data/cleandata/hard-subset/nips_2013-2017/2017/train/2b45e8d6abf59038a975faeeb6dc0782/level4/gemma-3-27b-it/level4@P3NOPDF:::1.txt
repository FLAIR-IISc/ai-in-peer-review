## Review of Population Matching Discrepancy for Distribution Matching

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance between minibatches sampled from two distributions. The authors explore the use of PMD as a loss function for distribution matching, employing both exact (O(N^3)) and approximate (O(N^2)) algorithms to compute the Wasserstein distance. While the experimental evaluation is interesting, the paper highlights significant limitations of PMD, particularly concerning its sensitivity to minibatch size and potential for converging to suboptimal solutions.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its exploration of a novel approach to estimating Wasserstein distance and applying it to distribution matching. The experimental results, despite not achieving state-of-the-art image generation quality compared to Wasserstein GANs, provide valuable insight into the behavior of PMD. Observing the performance with different settings and datasets is a constructive contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

The core weakness of PMD, as demonstrated by the authors themselves, is its dependence on sufficiently large minibatch sizes (N) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance, especially when dealing with complex, multimodal distributions. The authors correctly anticipate that challenges will arise when attempting to match distributions with varying variances, such as mixtures of Gaussians. This limitation is critical. If N is insufficiently large, the optimization process is prone to converging to a local minimum that does not represent the true underlying distribution. This can manifest as a learned distribution with reduced entropy.

The visual evidence in Figure 4, displaying SVHN samples, lends credence to this concern. The noticeable lack of diversity, and the over-representation of the digit '8', suggest that the learned distribution may be collapsing, possibly due to limitations in the minibatch size employed during training. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear and thorough comparison of PMD's limitations to established methods like Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). While the rebuttal addresses this partially, the original manuscript does not adequately contextualize PMD within the existing landscape of distribution matching techniques.

**Discussion of Rebuttal:**

The authors' response to the review and the included additional experiments were helpful in clarifying some aspects of PMD’s performance. Specifically, the point raised about MMD’s ability to be effectively trained with small batch sizes (batch_size=2) using an unbiased estimator (as described in the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper) is important. This highlights a significant advantage of MMD – the availability of unbiased gradient estimates – which is currently lacking for PMD. The authors correctly acknowledge that MMD can converge to the true distribution under these conditions.  The rebuttal successfully demonstrates that MMD is less sensitive to batch size than PMD. 

However, this reinforces the core issue with PMD: the absence of a known unbiased estimator for its gradient. Without such an estimator, the optimization is inherently susceptible to the problems described above, particularly when dealing with limited computational resources and the practical constraints of minibatch size.  More emphasis should be placed on this fundamental difference in the final manuscript.

**Typos & Clarity:**

The manuscript contained a minor typographical error on line 141 ("s/usally/usually/").

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting, though ultimately limited, approach to distribution matching. The concept of using the Wasserstein distance between minibatches is intriguing, but the practical limitations stemming from the requirement for large minibatches and the absence of an unbiased gradient estimator significantly hamper its applicability. While the experimental results and the rebuttal provide valuable insights, the paper, even with the updated information, doesn't present a compelling case for PMD’s superiority over existing methods like MMD, particularly given the computational costs associated with Wasserstein distance calculations. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend rejection. The limitations of PMD are significant and outweigh its potential benefits in its current form. Further research investigating methods to mitigate the sensitivity to minibatch size or derive an unbiased gradient estimator for PMD might yield more promising results. The authors should clearly articulate these limitations in the final manuscript and contrast PMD's strengths and weaknesses more directly with alternative approaches like MMD.
