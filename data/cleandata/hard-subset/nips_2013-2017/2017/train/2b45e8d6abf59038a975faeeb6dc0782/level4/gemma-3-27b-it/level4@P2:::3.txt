This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel and conceptually simple divergence measure for comparing probability distributions. The core idea – finding the minimum weight matching between samples from two distributions – is intuitively appealing and, crucially, differentiable, enabling its use as a training objective for neural networks. A key theoretical contribution is demonstrating that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance. 

The authors position their work effectively within the existing literature, particularly in relation to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a widely used population-based divergence. They provide a thorough comparison of PMD and MMD, highlighting advantages such as the elimination of kernel bandwidth tuning and the potential for faster convergence due to stronger gradients and the ability to use standard mini-batch sizes. They acknowledge the computational cost of exact matching (cubic complexity) and propose a quadratic-complexity approximation, demonstrating empirically that this approximation does not significantly compromise performance. 

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and appropriately references related work. The theoretical results appear sound, and the experimental evaluation is adequate, encompassing both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. The authors demonstrate improvements over MMD in both areas.

One potential area for further investigation, not addressed in the current work, is the performance of PMD in generative modeling scenarios with complex, multi-modal distributions. It would be valuable to understand how PMD performs when the number of modes exceeds the population size (N), as this could potentially impact the quality of the matching and, consequently, the generated samples.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a potentially valuable method for distribution comparison. The clarity of the presentation, the theoretical justification, and the empirical validation all contribute to a compelling contribution. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and conceptually simple approach to distribution divergence estimation.
*   Strong theoretical foundation (consistency guarantee for Wasserstein distance).
*   Demonstrated advantages over MMD in terms of hyperparameter sensitivity, gradient behavior, and convergence speed.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Solid experimental validation on standard benchmarks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited investigation of performance in high-dimensional, multi-modal generative modeling scenarios.
*   Computational complexity of exact matching may be a limitation for very large datasets (though the approximation addresses this).
