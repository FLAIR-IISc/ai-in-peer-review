This paper introduces the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a superior alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching applications. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein metric, also known as earth mover's distance, and offers several advantages over MMD. Notably, PMD provides stronger gradients and accommodates smaller mini-batch sizes, while requiring fewer hyperparameters.

For training generative models, at least, MMD is known to suffer from weak gradients and the necessity of large mini-batches. The proposals outlined in this paper offer a viable solution to both issues. The empirical results convincingly support the claim of smaller mini-batch sizes. However, the verification of stronger gradients is less satisfactory, as the comparison with MMD is sensitive to the choice of the scale parameter sigma. To strengthen this claim, it would be essential to consider either the optimal sigma or a range of sigmas.

Regarding the claim of having fewer hyperparameters, I believe it is not well-supported. Although PMD has fewer hyperparameters than MMD, it relies on a distance metric, which may introduce additional hyperparameters. Moreover, estimating a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces can be challenging, and PMD may face similar issues as MMD in this regard. It would be beneficial to compare PMD against standard heuristics for choosing the bandwidth parameter in MMD, effectively treating MMD as a hyperparameter-free metric.

Overall, I think the proposed method has the desirable property of allowing small mini-batch sizes, enabling fast training. This appears to be a valid improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, the reliance on a distance metric may limit the success of PMD in modeling high-dimensional data.
