- Tackles a practical problem: PMD addresses MMD’s need for large batch sizes with an intuitive, theoretically grounded solution.  
- Enables faster training: PMD decouples mini-batch size from population size, allowing small batches, more frequent updates, and faster convergence (empirical support in Fig. 5a).  
- Theoretical grounding: PMD is proven to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, linking it to optimal transport.  
- Promising empirical results: PMD outperforms MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling, producing less noisy images and faster convergence in experiments.  
- “Fewer hyperparameters” claim overstated: PMD requires choosing a distance metric d(·,·) (e.g., L1 vs L2), which is analogous to kernel choice and can strongly affect performance.  
- Incomplete comparison with MMD heuristics: Paper does not compare against common robust MMD practices (median heuristic or kernel mixtures) that reduce sensitivity to bandwidth σ.  
- “Stronger gradients” claim needs nuance: Gradient comparisons use a single σ; MMD gradients vary with σ and could be stronger under other reasonable settings.  
- High-dimensional challenges: Distance metrics become less meaningful in high dimensions, likely causing blurrier generated images and better results in lower-dimensional latent spaces—a limitation shared with MMD.  
- Overall: PMD is a valuable, well-motivated method that improves training efficiency, but some superiority claims (hyperparameters, gradients) are not fully substantiated.
