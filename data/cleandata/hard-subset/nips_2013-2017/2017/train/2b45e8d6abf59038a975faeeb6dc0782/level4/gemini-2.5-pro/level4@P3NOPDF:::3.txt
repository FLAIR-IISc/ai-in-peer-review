This paper introduces the Population-based Matched Divergence (PMD), a novel, sample-based method for estimating the divergence between probability distributions. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance. Key advantages of the proposed estimator are its conceptual simplicity and its differentiability, making it well-suited for training neural network-based models. The paper provides a thorough comparison to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), one of the most prominent sample-based divergences used in machine learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides proper context by referencing relevant prior work. The motivation for developing an alternative to MMD is well-articulated.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical results, particularly the consistency of the PMD estimator for the Wasserstein distance, appear correct and represent a significant contribution.
*   **Practicality and Utility:** A major strength of PMD is its differentiability, which directly enables its use as a loss function for training deep generative models and other neural network architectures. The authors are transparent about the method's computational complexity. They acknowledge that the exact calculation has cubic complexity but propose a practical quadratic-time approximation, showing empirically that this does not significantly degrade statistical performance.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental analysis is adequate and provides a solid comparison of PMD against MMD and other relevant methods in the context of domain adaptation. The inclusion of experiments on generative modeling further demonstrates the method's utility.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Generative Modeling for Complex Distributions:** The experiments on generative modeling serve as a good proof of concept. However, the true test for many divergence measures is their ability to handle highly multi-modal target distributions. I would have liked to see the method applied in such a setting. This would provide valuable insight into the estimator's behavior in more complex scenarios.
*   A key question that arises from this is how PMD performs when the sample size, N, is smaller than the number of modes in the target distribution. Does it suffer from mode collapse, or does its connection to the Wasserstein distance help it capture the overall geometry of the distribution even with a limited number of samples per mode? Further investigation into this aspect would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about its applicability to generative modeling.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a promising and potentially very useful method for comparing probability distributions. The proposed PMD is theoretically grounded, practical for modern machine learning applications, and empirically validated against a key baseline. While the experimental section could be extended to more challenging scenarios, the work as it stands represents a valuable contribution to the field.
