

The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein distance between two distributions. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between two samples drawn from the distributions, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes an optimization algorithm using PMD as a training objective, and evaluates its performance on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, claiming advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in terms of not requiring bandwidth hyperparameters, providing stronger gradients, and allowing smaller mini-batch sizes.

The work builds on recent advances in using distribution distances for deep learning tasks, particularly extending the line of work on Wasserstein-based metrics for generative modeling. The authors appropriately cite foundational work on optimal transport theory, MMD, and related domain adaptation methods, though they could more thoroughly contextualize their contribution relative to other Wasserstein approximation methods like WGAN and its variants.

Strengths:
The theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is well-established with rigorous proofs. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages of PMD over MMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling, particularly in terms of convergence speed. The authors provide convincing evidence for their claims through detailed ablation studies on population size, mini-batch size, and matching algorithm choice. The implementation details are thorough, including a careful comparison of exact and approximate matching algorithms.

Weaknesses:
The primary limitation of PMD is its sensitivity to population size N when estimating distances between complex, multimodal distributions. The authors acknowledge this in their experiments, but the paper could more explicitly address how this limitation impacts practical applications. For instance, when matching distributions with multiple modes (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians), PMD would require large N to capture all modes adequately, potentially leading to suboptimal solutions with insufficient samples. The SVHN samples in Figure 4 exhibit low diversity (digit 8 appears frequently), which suggests the model may not fully capture the distribution's complexity with the chosen N.

The rebuttal clarifies that MMD can be trained with small batch sizes using the unbiased estimator from Gretton et al. (2012), which is a valid point. However, this doesn't fully address the key distinction between PMD and MMD: PMD provides stronger gradients per sample, enabling faster convergence with smaller batch sizes. The authors should clarify this distinction more precisely in the revision.

The claim that PMD has "no bandwidth hyper-parameter" is accurate, but the paper could better acknowledge that while PMD avoids the kernel bandwidth choice, it introduces the choice of distance metric (e.g., L1 vs. L2), which may affect performance. The paper uses L1 by default, but this choice isn't thoroughly justified.

The generated images, while less noisy than MMD's, show blurriness on SVHN and LFW datasets, suggesting PMD may not be optimal for pixel-level generation. The authors note this limitation in the supplementary material but should discuss it more prominently in the main text.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated approach with clear empirical advantages over MMD in specific settings. The main weakness is the sensitivity to population size for complex distributions, which limits its applicability without careful parameter selection. The authors could strengthen their contribution by more thoroughly addressing this limitation and providing clearer guidance on when PMD would be preferable to MMD.

Pro:
- Strong theoretical foundation connecting PMD to the Wasserstein metric
- Clear empirical advantages over MMD in convergence speed and performance
- Thorough experimental validation across multiple settings
- Practical implementation details and ablation studies

Con:
- Sensitivity to population size N for complex multimodal distributions
- Limited discussion of the distance metric choice (L1 vs. L2)
- Generated images show blurriness on some datasets
- Insufficient discussion of when PMD would be preferable to MMD

The paper makes a solid contribution to the literature on distribution distance estimation for deep learning, with clear empirical advantages over MMD in several key aspects. While the population size limitation is a genuine concern, the authors provide sufficient evidence that PMD is effective for many practical applications. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the paper should more clearly articulate the conditions under which PMD provides advantages over MMD.
