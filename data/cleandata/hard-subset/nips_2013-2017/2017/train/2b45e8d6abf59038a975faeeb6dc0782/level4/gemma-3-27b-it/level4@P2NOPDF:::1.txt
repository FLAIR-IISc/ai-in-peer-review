This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance between minibatches sampled from two distributions. The Wasserstein distance is calculated using either exact (O(N^3)) or approximate (O(N^2)) algorithms.

**Summary of Assessment:**

I find the exploration of this Wasserstein distance computation method interesting, and the experimental results are a valuable contribution. However, the method appears sensitive to minibatch size and may struggle with complex, multimodal distributions. While the rebuttal clarifies some aspects, significant limitations regarding convergence and gradient estimation remain.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work lies in the practical investigation of Wasserstein distance computation via minibatch comparison. The empirical evaluation provides insights into the behavior of PMD, and the generated samples, while not reaching the quality of those produced by Wasserstein GANs, demonstrate the potential of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

A key concern is the requirement for a large minibatch size (N) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance, particularly when comparing distributions with significant diversity or multimodality. I anticipate that difficulties would arise even when attempting to match a mixture of Gaussians, especially if the variances of the components are also learned. Specifically, with insufficient N, the optimization process risks converging to a suboptimal solution – a distribution that matches PMD criteria but doesn't fully represent the true underlying distribution. This could manifest as a learned distribution with lower entropy than the target. The SVHN samples presented in Figure 4 offer supporting evidence, exhibiting limited diversity and a noticeable over-representation of the digit '8'.

Furthermore, the paper doesn’t adequately address the practical implications of the computational cost associated with calculating the Wasserstein distance, even with the approximate algorithm, for very large N.

**Further Considerations Raised by the Rebuttal:**

The authors’ rebuttal and additional experiments are appreciated. However, they highlight an important distinction between PMD and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD).  While MMD can be effectively used with very small batch sizes (e.g., batch_size=2), and can be trained with standard SGD using the unbiased gradient estimator presented in the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper, PMD currently lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator. This is a critical limitation; MMD demonstrably *can* converge to the correct distribution even with small minibatches, whereas the absence of an unbiased gradient for PMD raises serious questions about its convergence properties when N is constrained. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this fundamental difference.

**Overall Recommendation:**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but the limitations related to minibatch size and the lack of an unbiased gradient estimator raise concerns about its practical applicability and theoretical grounding. The clarification in the rebuttal regarding the comparison with MMD further emphasizes these limitations. While the experimental results provide some initial validation, additional work is needed to address these issues before I can recommend a strong acceptance. I suggest the authors clearly delineate the limitations of PMD, particularly in comparison to alternative methods like MMD, and investigate potential strategies for mitigating the sensitivity to minibatch size or developing an unbiased gradient estimator.
