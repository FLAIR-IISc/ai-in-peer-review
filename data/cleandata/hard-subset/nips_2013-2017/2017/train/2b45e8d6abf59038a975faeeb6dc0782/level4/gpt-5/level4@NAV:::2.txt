This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a preferable alternative to MMD for distribution-matching tasks. The authors argue that PMD can be viewed as a sampled version of the Wasserstein metric (Earth Mover’s Distance), and highlight several benefits over MMD, most notably stronger gradients, the ability to use small mini-batches, and fewer hyperparameters.

In the context of training generative models, MMD indeed tends to yield weak gradients and often requires large mini-batches, so the proposed approach offers a practical remedy to both issues. The small mini-batch claim is demonstrated convincingly in the experiments. The evidence for stronger gradients is less compelling, however. Because MMD depends on the scale (bandwidth) parameter sigma, any comparison should either tune sigma optimally or evaluate across a range of sigma values before drawing conclusions.

Regarding the claim of fewer hyperparameters, the support is weaker. PMD depends on a chosen distance metric, which can itself introduce additional hyperparameters, analogous to MMD. Moreover, obtaining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces is challenging, so PMD may inherit the same vulnerability of relying on a distance metric as MMD. There are also standard heuristics for selecting the MMD bandwidth; comparing against such heuristics and treating MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free would make the comparison fairer.

Overall, the method’s ability to use small mini-batches is attractive and can lead to faster training, representing a real improvement over large-batch MMD approaches. Nevertheless, its dependence on a distance metric remains, which may limit performance on high-dimensional data.
