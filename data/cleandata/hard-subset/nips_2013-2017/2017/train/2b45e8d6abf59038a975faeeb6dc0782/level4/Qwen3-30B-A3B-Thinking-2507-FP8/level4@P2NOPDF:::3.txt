

The authors introduce PMD, a population-based divergence measure between probability distributions that consistently estimates the Wasserstein distance. The estimator is conceptually simple and differentiable, enabling seamless integration into neural network training pipelines. The paper provides a thorough comparison of PMD against MMD, the dominant population-based divergence in machine learning, highlighting key distinctions. The authors acknowledge PMD's computational drawback: exact calculation requires cubic time complexity. They mitigate this by proposing a quadratic-time approximation, demonstrating empirically that statistical performance remains largely unaffected. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and appropriately contextualizes contributions within existing literature. The theoretical claims appear sound and correctly supported. The experimental evaluation is adequate, comparing PMD to MMD and other methods on domain adaptation tasks, and validating MMD's performance in generative modeling. However, the authors do not explore PMD's application in generative modeling for high-mode scenarios, a significant gap. It would be valuable to assess PMD's efficacy when the sample size N is smaller than the number of modes in the target distribution, as this is a common challenge in complex generative tasks. While the current experiments are relevant, this omission limits the method's perceived applicability to challenging multimodal settings. Despite this, the paper presents a strong contribution: a theoretically grounded, computationally feasible divergence measure that offers a practical alternative to MMD for distribution comparison. The method's simplicity, differentiability, and demonstrated empirical robustness make it a potentially valuable tool for a range of machine learning applications involving distributional comparisons. The work is well-executed and represents a solid advance in the field.
