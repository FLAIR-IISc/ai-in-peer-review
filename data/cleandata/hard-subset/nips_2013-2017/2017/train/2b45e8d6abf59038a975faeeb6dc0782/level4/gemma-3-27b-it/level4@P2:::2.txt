This paper introduces the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for estimating the distance between probability distributions. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (earth mover’s distance) and offers several advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients, the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes, and a reduced need for hyperparameter tuning.

The motivation for this work is strong. MMD, while popular, suffers from sensitivity to its kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and the requirement of large mini-batches, especially in the context of training generative models. PMD directly addresses these issues, and the potential for faster training with smaller mini-batches is a significant benefit. The empirical results support this claim, demonstrating faster convergence compared to MMD.

The claim of fewer hyperparameters requires further scrutiny. While PMD avoids the kernel bandwidth parameter of MMD, it relies on a distance metric *d(x, y)*, which itself may introduce hyperparameters (e.g., in more complex distance functions). The authors acknowledge this implicitly, but a more thorough discussion comparing the parameter tuning complexity of PMD with established MMD heuristics (for bandwidth selection) would strengthen the argument. Additionally, the reliance on a distance metric, especially in high-dimensional spaces, is a potential limitation that the paper acknowledges. The performance of PMD will be sensitive to the choice of distance metric, mirroring a similar challenge faced by MMD with kernel selection.

The verification of the stronger gradient claim could be strengthened. The authors appropriately note the dependence of MMD’s gradients on the kernel bandwidth *σ*. To make a compelling comparison, it would be beneficial to evaluate MMD using optimized bandwidth settings (e.g., through validation) rather than arbitrary choices, effectively treating MMD as a “tuned” metric.

Overall, PMD appears to be a valuable contribution with the potential to improve the efficiency of training deep learning models relying on distribution matching. The proposed method offers a practical advantage by enabling the use of smaller mini-batches, leading to faster training. While the reliance on a suitable distance metric remains a potential limitation, the benefits of PMD, particularly its gradient properties and speed, are compelling.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a known weakness of MMD (vanishing gradients, large mini-batch requirement).
*   Provides a theoretically sound alternative grounded in Wasserstein metric theory.
*   Demonstrates improved convergence speed in empirical evaluations.
*   Offers a potentially more stable training process due to stronger gradients.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is not fully substantiated and needs qualification.
*   The choice of distance metric in PMD introduces its own challenges, potentially limiting performance in high dimensions.
*   The comparison of gradient strength could be more rigorous by considering optimized MMD parameters.
