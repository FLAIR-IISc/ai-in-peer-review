The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), which measures the discrepancy between two distributions by computing the Wasserstein distance between two sampled mini-batches. The distance is obtained via either an exact O(N^3) Hungarian matching solver or an O(N^2) approximate matching algorithm.

Pros:
- The empirical evaluation using this Wasserstein-based objective is interesting.
- The image generation results, while informative, are not on par with those from Wasserstein GAN.

Cons:
- Accurately estimating the Wasserstein distance for complex, multimodal distributions likely requires large N. Challenges may already appear on mixtures of Gaussians, particularly when variances are learned.
- With small N, optimization may converge to a global minimum that deviates from the true distribution (e.g., overly low-entropy solutions). The SVHN samples in Figure 4 appear to have limited diversity; for instance, the digit 8 occurs frequently.

Update:
I have read the rebuttal—thank you for the additional experiments. The paper should clearly articulate the limitations of both PMD and MMD. Notably, MMD can operate with batch_size=2 and can be trained via SGD using the unbiased estimator from “A Kernel Two-Sample Test,” allowing convergence to the correct distribution with small mini-batches. By contrast, PMD currently lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator.
