Summary
This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of distributional distance designed as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for deep learning objectives such as domain adaptation and generative modeling. PMD equals the cost of a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two sample sets under a ground metric d(·,·), and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance (Earth Mover’s Distance) by appealing to standard results on weak convergence and stability of optimal transport (Villani, 2009). The authors propose a practical training procedure that alternates between computing matchings on “populations” and performing several SGD steps assuming the matching remains stable; they also use an approximate O(N^2) matching (Drake & Hougardy) for efficiency. Empirically, they demonstrate that PMD avoids kernel bandwidth tuning, provides stronger and more uniformly distributed gradients than MMD, and enables smaller mini-batches, leading to faster convergence. Experiments cover Office domain adaptation with VGG features and implicit generative models on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW.

Relation to prior work: PMD is essentially empirical 1-Wasserstein with uniform masses; its equivalence to minimum-weight matching is classical. The paper contrasts PMD with MMD [Gretton et al., 2012], and with Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) [Arjovsky et al., 2017], arguing that PMD is parameter-free and avoids a critic network. It would be useful to also position this method against entropic-regularized OT/Sinkhorn distances (Cuturi, 2013), sliced Wasserstein distances, and the Cramér distance/energy distance for generative modeling [Bellemare et al., 2017], which offer alternative ways to obtain stronger gradients and GPU-friendly computation.

Quality
- Theory: The consistency argument is sound and relies on standard OT results. However, the gradient estimator in Eq. (3) deserves a more careful treatment. The matching M is piecewise constant in parameters and can change at boundaries; the claim of an unbiased gradient by ignoring ∂M/∂θ is plausible almost everywhere, but a formal statement (e.g., subgradient justification or measure-zero argument) and discussion of potential instability near matching changes would strengthen the paper. Likewise, reusing a fixed matching for multiple inner-loop updates (Eq. 4) introduces bias; empirical evidence is promising, but a short analysis or ablation would help.
- Algorithms: The use of approximate matching is reasonable and well-motivated; timing breakdowns are useful. A discussion of memory footprint and batching strategies for large N, and a comparison to GPU-friendly Sinkhorn approximations, would broaden applicability.
- Experiments: The domain adaptation results show PMD outperforming or matching MMD on several Office transfers; convergence speed improvements are clear. For generative modeling, PMD improves sample quality over MMD in pixel space, but images remain blurry, and performance is best in learned code spaces. Comparisons to WGAN/Cramér distance or sliced/entropic OT would provide a stronger case. Quantitative metrics (e.g., Inception Score, FID for SVHN/LFW, or 1-NN two-sample tests) would complement visual inspection.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Two suggestions:
- Terminology: the distinction between “population size N” and “mini-batch size |B|” is easy to confuse since MMD papers often call N the mini-batch. Consolidate terminology early and maintain consistency throughout.
- “Stronger gradients”: formalize what is meant (e.g., bounds on gradient magnitudes under L1 ground metric vs RBF kernels), and when this advantage holds. The gradient comparison for MMD should include standard bandwidth choices (median heuristic or multi-kernel MMD) and report performance across a range of σ values.

Originality
The core idea (empirical Wasserstein via matching) is well known; the contributions are in adapting it as a practical deep learning objective, highlighting its optimization behavior (small mini-batches, stronger gradients), and demonstrating competitive results. The “fixed matching inner loop” is a simple, pragmatic tweak. The level of novelty is moderate but useful.

Significance
For practitioners who have encountered MMD’s weak gradients and large-batch requirements, PMD is an appealing alternative with a solid theoretical footing. The improvements in convergence speed and the ability to use normal mini-batch sizes are practically meaningful. Broader significance would be increased by stronger baselines (WGAN/Cramér/Sinkhorn) and quantitative metrics, and by demonstrating scalability with GPU-accelerated matching or entropic OT.

Strengths
- Clear connection to OT; proof of strong consistency.
- Practical training scheme; approximate matching works well.
- Empirical evidence that PMD tolerates smaller mini-batches and converges faster than MMD.
- Fewer tuning knobs than vanilla MMD with a single RBF kernel; competitive to superior performance in domain adaptation and better image quality than MMD in pixel space.

Weaknesses
- Dependence on the choice of ground metric: in high dimensions, Euclidean/L1 distances can be uninformative; PMD is not truly “hyperparameter-free” if the metric or feature space needs tuning. The fairness claim vs MMD should consider multi-kernel MMD and the median heuristic [Gretton et al., 2012], which largely removes manual σ tuning.
- Limited baselines: no comparison to WGAN, Cramér/energy distance, sliced or entropic OT.
- Gradient estimator lacks a formal treatment around matching changes; inner-loop fixed-matching bias not analyzed.
- Generative samples are still blurry; quantitative evaluation is missing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare against multi-kernel MMD and MMD with the median heuristic; report gradient-strength comparisons across σ.
- Include baselines using WGAN/Cramér distance and, if possible, Sinkhorn OT for speed and differentiability.
- Provide a brief theoretical note on gradients/subgradients through the matching and a robustness check when matchings change.
- Explore learned or perceptual ground metrics (e.g., feature-space distances) and report sensitivity.
- Add quantitative metrics for generation.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Solid formulation with clear theoretical grounding.
- Practical, simple algorithm that improves training efficiency.
- Positive results on two tasks, with clear convergence benefits.

Cons:
- Moderate novelty relative to known empirical OT.
- Missing strong baselines and quantitative metrics.
- Claims about “fewer hyperparameters” and “stronger gradients” need fairer comparisons and more formalism.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper addresses a real pain point of MMD objectives and provides a well-motivated, empirically validated alternative with rigorous connections to OT. Strengthening comparisons and clarifying the gradient analysis would make it a solid contribution.
