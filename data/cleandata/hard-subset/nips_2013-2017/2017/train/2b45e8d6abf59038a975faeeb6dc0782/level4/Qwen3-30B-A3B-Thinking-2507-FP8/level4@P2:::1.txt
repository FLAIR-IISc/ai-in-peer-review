

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable estimator of the Wasserstein distance between two distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching of sample populations from each distribution, and the authors prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. They propose an algorithm to learn distribution parameters using PMD as a training objective and evaluate it on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, claiming PMD outperforms MMD in both performance and convergence speed.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature on distribution distance estimation for deep learning. It addresses an important practical limitation of MMD (kernel bandwidth sensitivity, weak gradients, and large mini-batch requirements) and proposes a theoretically grounded alternative. The connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric is well-established through careful theoretical analysis, which is a significant strength. The empirical results demonstrate that PMD achieves better performance than MMD on domain adaptation tasks (Office dataset) and generates less noisy images in generative modeling compared to MMD.

The main strengths of the paper include:
- Theoretical foundation showing PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric
- Practical advantages over MMD: no bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients (as demonstrated in Figure 2b), and ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes (100 vs. 1000 for MMD)
- Clear experimental validation across two important deep learning applications
- Well-organized presentation of the mathematical concepts and experimental results

However, the paper has several important limitations that require more explicit discussion:

1. The paper does not sufficiently address the critical dependency on population size N for accurate estimation. The authors acknowledge in the rebuttal that PMD requires large N to estimate Wasserstein distances accurately for diverse multimodal distributions. For example, when matching mixture of Gaussians (including learning variances), the current approach would likely fail with small N. The experiments in Section 5.4 show the approximation error decreases as N increases, but the paper should clearly state the minimum N required for reliable performance.

2. The generative modeling results (Figure 4) show blurry images on SVHN and LFW, suggesting PMD may not be optimal for pixel-level generation. The authors note that pixel-level L1 distance is not ideal for natural images, but this limitation should be more prominently discussed as a key constraint of their approach.

3. The paper does not adequately compare PMD with other Wasserstein distance estimators (e.g., WGAN, WGAN-GP). While the authors mention WGAN in the introduction, they don't clearly explain how PMD differs from these approaches in terms of computational cost, implementation complexity, or performance.

4. The authors claim MMD requires large mini-batch sizes, but as noted in the rebuttal, MMD can be trained with small batch sizes using the unbiased estimator from "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" (Gretton et al., 2012). The paper should clarify this distinction and explain why PMD's small mini-batch advantage is significant compared to MMD's unbiased estimator.

The paper is well-written with clear organization, though the connection between PMD and practical deep learning applications could be strengthened. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results are convincing for the tasks tested. However, the paper would benefit from a more balanced discussion of PMD's limitations, particularly regarding the population size N requirement and its applicability to different types of generative tasks.

In terms of significance, PMD addresses a genuine practical problem in deep learning (the limitations of MMD as a training objective) and provides a theoretically grounded alternative with clear advantages. The method could be valuable for practitioners working with domain adaptation and generative models. However, the paper's claim that PMD "outperforms MMD on both tasks" should be tempered with the acknowledgment that PMD may not be suitable for all generative modeling scenarios (as evidenced by the blurry images in Figure 4).

The authors' rebuttal addressed some concerns, but the paper still needs to more explicitly acknowledge the limitations of PMD, particularly regarding the minimum population size required for reliable performance with diverse distributions. The claim that PMD has "no bandwidth hyper-parameter" is valid, but the paper should clarify that PMD introduces a new hyperparameter (population size N) that requires careful tuning.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with significant practical implications for distribution distance estimation in deep learning. While the current version has some limitations in its discussion of PMD's constraints, the core contribution is valuable and well-supported by the experimental results. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should more clearly articulate PMD's limitations in the final version.
