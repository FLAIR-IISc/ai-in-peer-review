This paper introduces the concept of Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) between two distributions, defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches drawn from these distributions. The Wasserstein distance is computed using either an exact algorithm with a time complexity of O(N^3) or an approximate algorithm with a time complexity of O(N^2).

The paper presents an interesting application of the Wasserstein distance computation in the context of generative modeling. The experimental results, including the generated images, are a valuable contribution to the field.

However, there are several limitations and concerns regarding the proposed approach. Firstly, accurately estimating the Wasserstein distance between two diverse, multimodal distributions would likely require a large minibatch size (N). This could be problematic, as even matching a mixture of Gaussians (including learning the variances) might reveal issues with the proposed method. Furthermore, if N is not sufficiently large, the optimization process may converge to a global minimum that is far from the true distribution. This could result in a learned distribution with lower entropy than the true distribution.

For instance, the SVHN samples presented in Figure 4 appear to have low diversity, with digit 8 being overrepresented. This suggests that the proposed method may struggle to capture the full range of variability in the data.

In addition to these concerns, there are a few minor typos that need to be corrected, such as the typo in Line 141 ("usally" instead of "usually").

After reading the authors' rebuttal and the additional experiments, it is clear that further clarification is needed regarding the limitations of both PMD and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Specifically, the authors should highlight that MMD can be successfully trained with small minibatches and stochastic gradient descent (SGD) using the unbiased estimator of MMD from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper. This allows MMD to converge to the correct distribution even with small minibatches. In contrast, PMD does not have a known unbiased estimator of the gradient, which may limit its performance in certain scenarios.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting idea and some valuable experimental results, it is essential to address the limitations and concerns outlined above to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach.
