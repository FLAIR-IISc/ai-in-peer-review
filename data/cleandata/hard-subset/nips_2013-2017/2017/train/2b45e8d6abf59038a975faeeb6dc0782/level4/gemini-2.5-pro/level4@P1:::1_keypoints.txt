- Proposal: Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator using minimum-weight matching between mini-batches to approximate distributional distance.
- Theoretical result: Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein-1 metric.
- Motivation: Positioned as an alternative to MMD to avoid kernel hyperparameter sensitivity, weak gradients, and large mini-batch requirements.
- Optimization: PMD optimized with SGD using exact O(N^3) or approximate O(N^2) matching solvers per step.
- Empirical validation: Demonstrates PMD on unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling, comparing directly against MMD.
- Strength: Novel and interesting direct primal-form Wasserstein approximation as a training objective.
- Strength: Solid theoretical foundation via the strong consistency proof.
- Strength: Practical applicability shown through experiments on two deep learning tasks.
- Weakness: Sample complexity — reliable Wasserstein estimation likely requires very large N, failing on multimodal or moderately complex distributions.
- Weakness: Finite/small N introduces systematic bias in the objective, risking low-entropy solutions and mode collapse (e.g., low-diversity SVHN samples).
- Weakness: Comparison to MMD is debatable — MMD has unbiased estimators usable with very small mini-batches, contrary to the paper's claims.
- Weakness: PMD gradient estimator is biased because the matched pairs from the mini-batch are treated as fixed, and no unbiased gradient estimator is provided.
- Weakness: Generated image quality appears worse than other Wasserstein-based methods (e.g., WGAN), questioning practical benefits for high-fidelity generation.
- Post-rebuttal: Reviewer remains unconvinced; requests clearer clarification of the biased PMD gradient vs. unbiased MMD estimator and implications for optimization and convergence.
