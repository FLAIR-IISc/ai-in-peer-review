

The paper defines Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) between two distributions as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches drawn from those distributions. This distance is computed either via an exact O(N³) algorithm or an approximate O(N²) method.  

Pros:  
- The experiments utilizing this Wasserstein distance computation are interesting.  

Cons:  
- The proposed PMD metric would require large minibatch sizes N to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between diverse multimodal distributions. This limitation would likely manifest when attempting to match a mixture of Gaussians (including learning their variances).  
- If N is insufficient, the optimization may converge to a global minimum distinct from the true distribution. For instance, the learned distribution may exhibit lower entropy. The SVHN samples in Figure 4 display low diversity, with digit 8 appearing frequently.

Update:  
I have read the rebuttal. The authors should clarify the limitations of PMD relative to MMD. MMD is valid with batch_size=2 and can be trained via SGD using the unbiased estimator from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper, enabling convergence to the correct distribution with small minibatches. Conversely, PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator.
