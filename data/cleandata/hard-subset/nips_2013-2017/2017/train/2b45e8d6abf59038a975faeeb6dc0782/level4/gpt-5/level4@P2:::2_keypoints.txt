- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based objective matching two finite populations via minimum-weight bipartite matching and consistent with the 1-Wasserstein distance.
- Proposes using PMD as a differentiable objective for learning distribution parameters, claiming stronger gradients, smaller mini-batch viability, and no kernel bandwidth tuning compared to MMD.
- Demonstrates empirical benefits on domain adaptation and generative modeling, reporting faster convergence and better performance than MMD.

- Clear motivation: targets known MMD issues of weak gradients and large required mini-batches.
- Solid theoretical grounding: PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance via empirical optimal transport.
- Practical algorithm: details for SGD use, including reuse of matchings and options for exact or approximate matching.
- Empirical evidence: convincingly supports small mini-batch claims and discusses runtime/accuracy trade-offs for matching methods.
- Applicability: relevant demonstrations on domain adaptation and generative modeling for the community.

- Stronger gradients claim is not fully persuasive without sweeping MMD kernel bandwidth σ or using multi-kernel MMD for fair comparison.
- “Fewer hyperparameters” is overstated: PMD depends on the ground metric d(·,·), which may have its own hyperparameters and sensitivities.
- High-dimensional distance reliability concern: Euclidean distances in pixel space can be ill-behaved, and metric choice (e.g., learned perceptual features) is crucial.
- Baselines: comparisons should include MMD with median-heuristic and multi-kernel setups, and contextualize against WGAN or Cramér distance variants.

- Theoretical results are correct and well tied to optimal transport; SGD treatment (not backpropagating through discrete matching) is reasonable but needs more on matching-change stability.
- Experiments are generally sound; small-batch advantages are supported but gradient-strength findings need more rigorous bandwidth treatment for MMD.

- Paper is mostly clear and well organized; would benefit from concise guidance on when approximate matching suffices and how to choose d(·,·) in high dimensions.

- Originality: useful and distinct contribution bridging empirical optimal transport with deep learning objectives in the PMD form and training regime.

- Significance: practically valuable for enabling small mini-batches, but impact may be limited by sensitivity to distance metric choice in high-dimensional settings.

- Suggested comparisons: MMD with median heuristic, multi-kernel MMD, and bandwidth sweeps to report best/robust performance.
- Suggested evaluations: PMD using feature-space metrics (e.g., VGG), ablations on metric scaling, and analysis of matching reuse stability across SGD.
- Suggested baselines and metrics: include WGAN and Cramér distance comparisons and report wall-clock times including matching under GPU assumptions.

- Pros for acceptance: strong theoretical foundation, practical algorithm, clear small-batch training efficiency gains, and competitive empirical results on relevant tasks.
- Cons for acceptance: gradient-strength and hyperparameter-reduction claims not fully substantiated versus well-tuned MMD; sensitivity to ground-metric choice may limit generality.

- Overall: a well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and practically useful alternative to MMD that would be strengthened by fairer MMD baselines and deeper exploration of metric choice; valuable for small-batch scenarios with caveats about distance metrics.
