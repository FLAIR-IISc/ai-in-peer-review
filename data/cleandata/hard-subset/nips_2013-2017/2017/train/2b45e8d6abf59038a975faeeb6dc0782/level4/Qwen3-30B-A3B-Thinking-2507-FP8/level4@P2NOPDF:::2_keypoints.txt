- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a refined alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching in generative modeling.  
- Shows PMD is a sampled variant of the Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance.  
- Claims three main advantages: stronger optimization gradients, compatibility with smaller mini-batches, and reduced hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Empirical evidence convincingly demonstrates PMD’s effectiveness with smaller mini-batches and tangible training speedups.  
- The claim of stronger gradients is insufficiently justified: MMD’s performance depends on bandwidth σ, so comparisons must use optimized or swept σ values.  
- The “fewer hyperparameters” argument is underdeveloped because PMD depends on a distance metric (e.g., Euclidean, Mahalanobis), which can introduce its own hyperparameters.  
- MMD has practical σ-selection heuristics (e.g., median heuristic), so it can be effectively hyperparameter-free in practice; PMD should be compared against such tuned MMD baselines.  
- Core limitation: PMD’s reliance on a distance metric remains problematic in high-dimensional settings where metric learning is intractable.  
- The paper acknowledges the metric-dependency but does not propose sufficient solutions for complex, high-dimensional data common in generative tasks.  
- Despite caveats, the work is a valuable practical contribution by enabling efficient small-batch training without apparent loss in distribution-matching fidelity.  
- Empirical results across multiple benchmarks robustly support PMD’s mini-batch efficiency, making it promising for resource-constrained scenarios.  
- Recommendation: strengthen theoretical/empirical comparisons on gradient behavior and hyperparameter sensitivity, and clarify whether PMD’s metric choice can be automated or learned; with these revisions, the paper is a strong candidate for publication.
