The authors introduce PMD, a population-based discrepancy between probability distributions, and prove it is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance.

The estimator is conceptually straightforward and differentiable, which enables training neural network–based models.

The paper provides a thorough comparison to MMD, the most prominent population-based discrepancy in machine learning.

The authors note computational drawbacks—exact computation is cubic time—but propose a quadratic-time approximation and show empirically that it does not substantially harm statistical performance.

The manuscript is clearly organized and well written, with appropriate references to prior work.

The theoretical results appear correct.

The empirical evaluation is adequate: PMD is compared to MMD and other baselines for domain adaptation, and to MMD for generative modeling.

I would have liked to see generative modeling experiments on datasets with many modes; it is unclear how PMD behaves when N is smaller than the number of modes.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions.
