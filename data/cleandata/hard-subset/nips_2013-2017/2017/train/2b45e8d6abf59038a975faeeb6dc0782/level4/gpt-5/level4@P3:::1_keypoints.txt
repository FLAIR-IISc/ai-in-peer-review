- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): average cost of minimum bipartite matching between equal-size sample sets as a sample-based discrepancy.
- PMD with fixed ground metric equals empirical optimal transport (earth mover’s distance) under uniform weights.
- Authors prove strong consistency of PMD as an estimator of the 1-Wasserstein distance.
- Training approach: compute matchings for sampled “populations” and perform several SGD updates with matching fixed (inner-loop).
- Claimed benefits vs MMD: fewer hyperparameters, stronger gradients, and ability to use smaller mini-batches.
- Missing comparison to entropically regularized OT (Sinkhorn), which is fast, differentiable, and widely used.
- Strength: clear, parameter-free empirical OT objective with principled consistency guarantee.
- Strength: practical algorithm—decoupling matching from gradient steps is simple; approximate matching reduces cost from O(N^3) to O(N^2).
- Strength: empirical results show faster convergence on Office domain adaptation and competitive, stable generative samples without adversarial training.
- Strength: insightful gradient analysis showing more uniform sample-wise gradients than RBF-MMD.
- Weakness: lacks finite-sample error rates or guidance on required population size N for multimodal/high-variance distributions.
- Weakness: limited stress tests—needs multimodal toy experiments to evaluate mode coverage and variance estimation with small N.
- Weakness: SVHN samples show low diversity, suggesting possible mode under-coverage.
- Weakness: missing key baselines—no Sinkhorn OT comparison, limited WGAN baselines (no WGAN-GP), and omission of unbiased/linear-time MMD estimators.
- Weakness: gradient/differentiability concerns—gradient does not propagate through discrete matching; unbiasedness of stochastic gradient estimator not fully justified.
- Weakness: generative results largely visual; lacks quantitative diversity/quality metrics (e.g., class-conditional diversity, precision/recall).
- Theory: consistency argument is correct and clear but known in OT literature; no finite-sample guarantees or complexity/error trade-offs.
- Algorithms: matching methods are standard; inner-loop SGD with fixed matching is reasonable and empirically effective at modest N.
- Experiments: mixed—convergence speed convincing, sample quality acceptable but not state-of-the-art; gradient-strength claims supported by diagnostics.
- Clarity: generally well written; suggest distinguishing “population size” vs “mini-batch size” earlier and providing wall-clock/GPU cost comparisons; minor typo noted.
- Originality: using empirical OT as training objective is natural; novelty mainly in “population matching” framing and inner-loop strategy; missing Sinkhorn discussion weakens novelty claim.
- Significance: moderate—PMD is a useful alternative with practical benefits but limited by scalability and sample-complexity concerns and missing comparisons.
- Pros for acceptance: simple principled objective, practical training recipe, parameter-free stable gradients, faster convergence than MMD in reported tasks.
- Cons for acceptance: incomplete evaluation and missing baselines, limited finite-sample analysis and mode-collapse concerns, generative results not state-of-the-art and lacking quantitative metrics.
- Suggested improvements: add controlled multimodal experiments, compare to Sinkhorn OT and unbiased/linear-time MMD with small batches, include quantitative diversity metrics, and clarify stochastic gradient properties.
- Rebuttal update: authors added experiments; reviewer requests explicit clarification of PMD vs MMD limitations and whether PMD admits an unbiased stochastic gradient estimator under specified assumptions.
