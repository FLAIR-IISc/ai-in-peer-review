This paper introduces the population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a viable alternative to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. Notably, PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein metric or earth mover's distance, offering several advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients, suitability for smaller mini-batch sizes, and a reduced number of hyperparameters.

The proposed method addresses two significant limitations of the MMD metric in training generative models, namely weak gradients and the necessity for large mini-batches. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the feasibility of using small mini-batches with PMD. However, the verification of stronger gradients in PMD is less conclusive, as the comparison with MMD should consider the optimal or a range of scale parameters (sigma) to substantiate this claim.

Regarding the reduction in hyperparameters, this advantage is not entirely convincing, as PMD relies on a distance metric, which may itself introduce additional hyperparameters. Moreover, estimating a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces can be challenging, and PMD may face similar issues as MMD in this regard. It would be beneficial to compare PMD with MMD using established heuristics for choosing the bandwidth parameter, effectively treating MMD as a hyperparameter-free metric.

In summary, the proposed PMD method has the desirable property of enabling fast training with small mini-batch sizes, presenting a valid improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, its reliance on a distance metric may limit its effectiveness in modeling high-dimensional data.
