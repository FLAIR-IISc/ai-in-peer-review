This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an improved alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein metric (earth mover’s distance) and offers several benefits over MMD, particularly stronger gradients and the ability to utilize smaller mini-batch sizes, alongside a reduction in the number of hyperparameters.

For training generative models, MMD is known to suffer from weak gradients and a requirement for large mini-batches; therefore, the proposed PMD offers a compelling solution to these limitations. The empirical results effectively validate the claim regarding smaller mini-batch sizes. However, the justification for stronger gradients is less convincing. Given that MMD performance is sensitive to the scale parameter σ, a robust comparison requires evaluating either the optimal σ or a range of σ values. 

Regarding the claim of fewer hyperparameters, I find the support to be somewhat weak. PMD relies on a chosen distance metric, which itself may introduce hyperparameters comparable to those in MMD. Furthermore, obtaining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces remains challenging, potentially subjecting PMD to similar limitations as MMD. Conversely, established heuristics exist for selecting the bandwidth parameter in MMD, allowing it to be treated as a hyperparameter-free metric; a comparison against these heuristics would strengthen the evaluation. 

Overall, the proposed method’s ability to enable small mini-batch sizes and, consequently, faster training is a significant advantage. It appears to be a valuable improvement over large-batch MMD approaches. However, its dependence on a distance metric raises concerns about performance when modeling high-dimensional data.
