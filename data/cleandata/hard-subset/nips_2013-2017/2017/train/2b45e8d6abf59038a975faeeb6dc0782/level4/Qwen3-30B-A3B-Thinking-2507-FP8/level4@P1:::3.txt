

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel differentiable estimator for measuring the distance between probability distributions based on samples. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, making it theoretically well-founded for distribution comparison. The method is conceptually straightforward: PMD computes the minimum weight matching between two sets of samples from the distributions, which provides a natural and intuitive measure of distributional distance.

The paper makes a strong case for PMD's advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), the most widely used sample-based distribution distance in machine learning. The authors thoroughly analyze and empirically validate that PMD eliminates the problematic kernel bandwidth hyperparameter required by MMD, provides stronger gradients (which are less likely to vanish during training), and allows for smaller mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100 instead of 1000). These properties are particularly valuable for deep learning applications where training efficiency is critical.

The theoretical contribution is significant: the authors establish PMD's connection to the Wasserstein metric through a clear proof that demonstrates PMD is a strongly consistent estimator. This theoretical foundation is robust and well-supported by their empirical validation of the approximation error with respect to population size. The paper also addresses practical concerns by proposing an approximate matching algorithm with O(N²) complexity that maintains statistical performance while significantly reducing computational requirements.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare PMD to MMD across two important deep learning tasks: domain adaptation (on the Office benchmark) and generative modeling (on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW datasets). Their results consistently show PMD outperforms MMD in terms of both final performance and convergence speed. The ablation studies on mini-batch size and population size further validate the theoretical claims about PMD's advantages. The authors also provide insightful visualizations of gradient magnitudes, demonstrating PMD's more uniform gradient distribution compared to MMD's sparse gradient behavior.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with appropriate citations to related work. The authors thoughtfully address potential limitations, including the cubic complexity of exact matching, and propose a practical approximation algorithm that maintains performance while significantly reducing computational cost. The experimental setup is rigorous, with careful parameter tuning and multiple independent runs to ensure reliability.

Regarding the four key evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims and comprehensive experimental validation. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach.
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with machine learning. The pseudocode and figures effectively illustrate the method.
- Originality: While the paper builds on optimal transport theory, PMD represents a novel approach to estimating the Wasserstein metric that differs substantially from existing methods like WGAN and MMD. The paper thoroughly contextualizes its contribution within the literature.
- Significance: The practical advantages of PMD for deep learning applications are compelling. The method's ability to work with smaller mini-batch sizes and avoid hyperparameter tuning could significantly improve training efficiency across many applications, making it a potentially valuable tool for practitioners.

The only minor limitation is that the generative modeling experiments do not test PMD on highly multimodal distributions where the population size N might be smaller than the number of modes. This could be an interesting direction for future work, as the paper's theoretical foundation suggests PMD should still be applicable, but empirical validation in such challenging settings would strengthen the claims.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distribution distance estimation. The proposed PMD method offers practical advantages over the widely used MMD with strong theoretical backing and compelling experimental results. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and represents a significant advancement in the tools available for deep learning applications requiring distribution comparison. I strongly recommend acceptance.
