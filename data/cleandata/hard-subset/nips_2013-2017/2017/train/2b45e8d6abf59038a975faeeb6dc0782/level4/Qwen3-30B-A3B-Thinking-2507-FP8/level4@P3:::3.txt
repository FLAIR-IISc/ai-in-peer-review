

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel sample-based estimator of distribution distance that addresses key limitations of Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). The authors define PMD as the minimum weight matching cost between two sample populations from the respective distributions, proving it to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The paper presents a practical algorithm for using PMD as a training objective in deep learning, demonstrating its advantages over MMD in both theoretical properties and empirical performance.

The work is well-positioned within the NIPS literature, building upon the growing interest in distribution distance estimation for deep learning applications. While MMD has been widely adopted for tasks like domain adaptation and generative modeling (e.g., GMMN [31]), it suffers from three critical drawbacks: sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients in deep networks, and the need for large mini-batch sizes. The authors convincingly demonstrate that PMD overcomes these limitations: it has no bandwidth hyper-parameter, provides stronger gradients (as shown in Figure 2b), and can use standard mini-batch sizes (e.g., 100) instead of requiring large populations (e.g., 1000).

The theoretical contributions are strong and well-supported. The authors establish PMD's relationship to the Wasserstein metric through a series of clear propositions (Section 3.1), showing how PMD approximates the optimal transport problem. This is particularly valuable as the Wasserstein metric has gained traction in machine learning applications (e.g., WGAN [3]), but its direct computation remains challenging. The proof of strong consistency (Theorem 3.3) is straightforward yet rigorous, and the empirical approximation error study (Figure 2a) provides useful practical insights.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare PMD with MMD across two major deep learning tasks: domain adaptation on Office datasets (Section 5.1) and generative modeling on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW (Section 5.2). In domain adaptation, PMD significantly outperforms MMD on a→w and a→d tasks while matching performance on others. For generative modeling, PMD produces less noisy samples than MMD, particularly in pixel space (Figure 4). The ablation studies (Figures 5a-c) effectively demonstrate PMD's advantages regarding mini-batch size, population size, and computational efficiency.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The theoretical development is logically presented, with clear explanations of the matching problem and parameter learning algorithm (Section 2). The empirical results are well-organized, with detailed experimental settings and appropriate baselines. The discussion of computational considerations (Section 2.3) is particularly valuable, acknowledging the cubic complexity of exact matching but demonstrating that approximated matching (O(N²)) suffices for practical applications.

The main limitation of the paper relates to the generative modeling results. While PMD produces less noisy samples than MMD, the generated images on SVHN and LFW appear blurry (Figure 4), suggesting potential limitations in natural image generation. The authors acknowledge that the L1 distance may not be ideal for natural images, but it would strengthen the paper to explore alternative distance metrics or demonstrate PMD's performance on more challenging multi-modal datasets where MMD has been known to struggle (e.g., CelebA with many distinct modes).

The paper's significance is substantial. By providing a practical, differentiable distribution distance estimator with clear advantages over MMD, PMD addresses a fundamental challenge in deep learning applications requiring distribution alignment. The method's simplicity (no bandwidth tuning) and efficiency (smaller mini-batch requirements) make it immediately applicable to a wide range of tasks. The authors' claim that PMD "overcomes the aforementioned drawbacks of MMD" is well-supported by their experiments.

Strengths:
- Novel approach to distribution distance estimation that directly addresses MMD's limitations
- Strong theoretical foundation with clear proof of consistency
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple tasks
- Practical implementation with computational considerations
- Clear writing and well-organized presentation

Weaknesses:
- Limited demonstration of PMD's performance on highly multi-modal generative tasks
- Blurry generated images on some datasets suggest potential limitations for natural image generation
- The paper could more explicitly discuss when PMD might be less suitable than other metrics

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in distribution distance estimation. The authors have made a compelling case for PMD as a practical alternative to MMD with clear advantages for deep learning applications. The paper is well-written, theoretically sound, and empirically validated, making it a strong candidate for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
