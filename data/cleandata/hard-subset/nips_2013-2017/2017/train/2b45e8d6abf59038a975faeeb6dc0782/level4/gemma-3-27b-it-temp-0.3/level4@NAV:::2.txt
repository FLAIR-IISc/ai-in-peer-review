This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a promising alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein distance (Earth Mover’s Distance), offering benefits over MMD, specifically stronger gradients and the ability to utilize smaller mini-batch sizes, alongside a reduction in the number of hyperparameters.

Given the known challenges of MMD – namely weak gradients and the need for large mini-batches – in the context of training generative models, this work offers a valuable solution to both issues. The empirical results effectively validate the claim regarding smaller mini-batch requirements. However, the evidence supporting the claim of stronger gradients is less convincing. As MMD’s performance is sensitive to the bandwidth parameter σ, a thorough comparison should include results across a range of σ values, or at least the optimal σ, to substantiate this assertion. 

The claim of fewer hyperparameters is also not fully supported. PMD’s reliance on a distance metric introduces potential hyperparameters of its own, comparable to those in MMD. Furthermore, obtaining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces remains a challenge, potentially limiting PMD’s effectiveness in similar ways to MMD.  It would strengthen the paper to compare PMD against established heuristics for selecting the bandwidth parameter in MMD, effectively treating MMD as a hyperparameter-tuned metric as well.

In summary, the proposed PMD method offers a significant advantage in enabling small mini-batch sizes and, consequently, faster training. It represents a valid improvement over MMD when large batches are impractical. However, its dependence on a distance metric introduces a potential limitation, particularly when applied to modeling high-dimensional data.
