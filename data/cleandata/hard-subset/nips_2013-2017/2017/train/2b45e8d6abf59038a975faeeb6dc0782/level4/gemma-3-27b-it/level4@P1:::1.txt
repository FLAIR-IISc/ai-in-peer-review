This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching between samples from each distribution and is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. The authors propose an algorithm to learn the parameters of distributions using PMD as an objective and demonstrate its advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) – namely, the absence of a bandwidth hyperparameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to use normal mini-batch sizes – through experiments on domain adaptation and generative modeling.

The core idea of framing the distributional distance estimation as a minimum weight matching problem is interesting, and the theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric is well-established. The experimental results demonstrate PMD's potential, particularly its faster convergence speed compared to MMD in the domain adaptation task. The analysis highlighting PMD's gradient properties is also valuable.

However, there are some weaknesses. The practical utility of PMD is somewhat limited by the computational cost of solving the matching problem, particularly for larger sample sizes. While the authors discuss approximate matching algorithms, the scalability remains a concern, especially when compared to the computational efficiency of MMD. The assumption that PMD’s gradient doesn’t propagate through the matching is a simplification that could be further investigated. The generated images in the generative modeling experiments, while qualitatively improved over some MMD baselines, still exhibit limitations in diversity, raising questions about the method’s ability to capture complex multimodal distributions. The authors acknowledge this, but a deeper analysis of why this occurs would be beneficial.

Furthermore, the comparison to MMD lacks nuance. The authors state MMD requires large mini-batches, but fail to mention the existence of unbiased estimators for MMD that allow for training with smaller batches, effectively addressing that concern. A more thorough discussion of these alternative MMD formulations would strengthen the comparison. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to estimating distributional distance based on optimal transport.
*   Theoretical guarantees demonstrating consistency with the Wasserstein metric.
*   Empirical evidence of faster convergence in domain adaptation compared to MMD.
*   Clear identification of advantages over MMD regarding hyperparameter sensitivity and gradient behavior.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational cost of the matching problem may limit scalability.
*   Limited diversity in generated samples suggests challenges with complex distributions.
*   Incomplete comparison with existing MMD techniques, particularly those employing unbiased estimators.
*   The empirical validation could be expanded to include more challenging datasets and model architectures.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributional distance estimation. While the computational cost and limitations in sample diversity are concerns, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical grounding, and the promising empirical results warrant consideration. The authors should clarify the limitations of PMD relative to state-of-the-art MMD techniques in the final version.
