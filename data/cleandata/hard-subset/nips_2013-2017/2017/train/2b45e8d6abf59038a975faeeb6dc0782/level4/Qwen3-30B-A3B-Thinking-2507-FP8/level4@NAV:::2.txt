

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a superior alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. It establishes PMD as a sampled formulation of the Wasserstein metric (Earth Mover's Distance), highlighting key advantages over MMD, particularly stronger gradients, compatibility with smaller mini-batch sizes, and reduced hyperparameter requirements.

For generative model training, MMD indeed exhibits weak gradients and necessitates large mini-batches; the proposed PMD effectively addresses both limitations. The claim regarding smaller mini-batch viability is well-supported by the empirical results. However, the assertion of stronger gradients is less compelling, as MMD's performance critically depends on the scale parameter σ. A fair comparison must evaluate MMD using either the optimal σ or a range of σ values, which the paper does not adequately address.

Regarding fewer hyperparameters, this claim is less compelling. PMD relies on a distance metric, which may itself introduce additional hyperparameters—similar to MMD's bandwidth dependency. Furthermore, reliably obtaining an effective distance metric in high-dimensional spaces remains challenging, suggesting PMD faces the same fundamental limitation as MMD in this regard. While established heuristics exist for selecting MMD's bandwidth, the paper should compare against these to position MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free.

Overall, the method's ability to enable small mini-batch training represents a valid and practical improvement over large-batch MMD approaches. Nevertheless, PMD retains the critical dependency on a distance metric, which may constrain its effectiveness for modeling high-dimensional data.
