This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching, particularly in the context of training generative models. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance, and highlight advantages over MMD including improved gradient behavior and the ability to utilize smaller mini-batch sizes, potentially reducing computational cost.

MMD is known to suffer from vanishing gradients and a requirement for large mini-batches, particularly when used for training generative models. This work offers a compelling solution to these issues, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the benefit of smaller mini-batch sizes with PMD. However, the claim of stronger gradients requires further scrutiny. As MMD performance is highly sensitive to the bandwidth parameter σ, a thorough comparison should include either the optimal σ for a given task, or a range of σ values. Without this, it is difficult to definitively assess PMD’s gradient superiority.

The assertion that PMD has fewer hyperparameters is less persuasive. While PMD may reduce the need to tune the MMD bandwidth, it introduces a dependency on a distance metric, which itself may contain hyperparameters. Furthermore, reliably estimating distances in high-dimensional spaces remains a significant challenge. Consequently, PMD may be susceptible to the same issues of distance metric selection that plague MMD. It would strengthen the paper to compare PMD against established heuristics for MMD bandwidth selection, effectively treating MMD as a potentially hyperparameter-free baseline. Such a comparison would provide a more nuanced evaluation of the hyperparameter burden of each approach. 

Overall, this paper presents a promising method with the significant advantage of enabling smaller mini-batch sizes and thus potentially faster training. The proposed PMD appears to be a valuable improvement over large-batch MMD implementations. However, the reliance on a distance metric remains a potential limitation, especially when applied to modeling complex, high-dimensional data. 

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a known limitation of MMD (vanishing gradients, large batch size requirements).
*   Provides a theoretically sound connection to the Wasserstein distance.
*   Strong empirical validation of the smaller mini-batch benefit.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The stronger gradients claim lacks a robust comparative analysis with appropriately tuned MMD.
*   The claim of fewer hyperparameters is not fully justified, given the potential hyperparameters of the chosen distance metric.
*   Discussion of the challenges in high-dimensional distance estimation is crucial but limited.



**Suggestions for improvement:**

*   Expand the gradient comparison to include MMD using optimized bandwidth selection strategies.
*   Provide a clearer discussion of the hyperparameters associated with the chosen distance metric and their impact on PMD performance.
*   Acknowledge and discuss the potential difficulties of applying PMD to very high-dimensional data and suggest potential mitigation strategies.
