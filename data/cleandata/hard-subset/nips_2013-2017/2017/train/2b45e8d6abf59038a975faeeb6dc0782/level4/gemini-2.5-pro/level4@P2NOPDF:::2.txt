This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for matching distributions, particularly in the context of training generative models. The authors establish that PMD is a sample-based approximation of the Wasserstein metric (or Earth Mover's Distance). They claim several advantages over MMD, most notably the ability to use smaller mini-batch sizes, the presence of stronger training gradients, and a reduction in the number of hyperparameters.

The motivation for this work is well-founded. MMD-based training of generative models is known to suffer from weak gradients and often requires large mini-batches to obtain reliable estimates, which can be computationally expensive. The proposed PMD method offers a promising solution to these challenges. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness with small mini-batch sizes, which is a significant practical advantage.

However, the empirical validation for some of the other claims is less satisfactory. The claim of stronger gradients, for instance, requires a more rigorous comparison. The performance of MMD is highly sensitive to the choice of the kernel bandwidth (sigma). A fair and comprehensive comparison would necessitate evaluating MMD performance across a range of well-chosen sigma values, or by using a value optimized for the task. Without such a comparison, the claim that PMD provides stronger gradients than a properly tuned MMD baseline is not fully substantiated.

Similarly, the claim of having fewer hyperparameters is questionable. While PMD removes the kernel bandwidth parameter, it introduces a dependency on a chosen distance metric (e.g., Euclidean or cosine distance). The choice of this metric is itself a critical design decision, which may involve its own hyperparameters and significantly impact performance. Furthermore, the reliance on standard distance metrics can be problematic in high-dimensional spaces due to the curse of dimensionality. In this sense, PMD faces a challenge analogous to that of selecting an appropriate kernel for MMD. To strengthen this claim, the authors should compare PMD against MMD variants that employ standard heuristics for automatic bandwidth selection (e.g., the median heuristic). Such approaches effectively render MMD hyperparameter-free in practice and would provide a more direct and equitable comparison.

In summary, the proposed method's primary strength lies in its ability to operate effectively with small mini-batch sizes, which can significantly accelerate the training of generative models. It represents a valuable improvement over MMD-based methods that are constrained to large batches. However, its core reliance on an underlying distance metric remains a significant concern. This dependency may limit its scalability and performance on high-dimensional, complex data distributions—a challenge it shares, in principle, with kernel-based methods like MMD.
