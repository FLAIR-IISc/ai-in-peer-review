- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based approximation of the Wasserstein metric as an alternative to MMD for distribution matching.  
- Claims PMD provides stronger training gradients, enables smaller mini-batches, and requires fewer hyperparameters than MMD.  
- Addresses known MMD limitations: weak gradients and need for large mini-batches in generative model training.  
- Empirically validates that PMD works effectively with much smaller batch sizes, offering practical speed and memory benefits.  
- The “stronger gradients” claim is not fully convincing because MMD gradient strength depends strongly on kernel bandwidth choice.  
- A fair comparison requires evaluating MMD with properly tuned bandwidths or across a range of bandwidths.  
- The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is debatable: PMD replaces kernel bandwidth with a choice of distance metric (e.g., L1/L2), which is itself a critical hyperparameter.  
- High-dimensional data may challenge PMD due to distance-metric issues and the curse of dimensionality, similar to MMD.  
- Established heuristics (e.g., median heuristic) can make MMD effectively parameter-free; comparisons should include such baselines.  
- Overall: PMD is a promising contribution for small-batch training, but claims about superior gradients and reduced hyperparameters need stronger empirical and conceptual support.
