

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a superior alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. It establishes that PMD constitutes a sampled variant of the Wasserstein distance (earth mover's distance) and demonstrates several advantages over MMD, including stronger gradients, compatibility with smaller mini-batch sizes, and reduced hyperparameter requirements.  

For generative model training, MMD indeed suffers from weak gradients and necessitates large mini-batches, making the proposed PMD a viable solution to both limitations. The claim regarding smaller mini-batch efficacy is well-supported by the empirical results. However, the evidence for stronger gradients is less compelling: since MMD's performance critically depends on the bandwidth parameter σ, any comparison must account for either the optimal σ or a range of σ values, which the paper does not adequately address.  

The assertion that PMD has fewer hyperparameters is also under-supported. PMD relies on a distance metric, which may itself introduce additional hyperparameters—similar to MMD's σ dependency. Furthermore, reliably defining a distance metric in high-dimensional spaces remains challenging, implying PMD may inherit MMD's fundamental reliance on distance metrics. In contrast, MMD benefits from established heuristics for bandwidth selection; treating MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free (via these heuristics) would provide a more equitable comparison.  

Overall, PMD offers a meaningful advantage through its compatibility with small mini-batch training, enabling faster convergence. It represents a valid improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, its continued dependence on a distance metric—potentially limiting scalability to high-dimensional data—remains a significant caveat.
