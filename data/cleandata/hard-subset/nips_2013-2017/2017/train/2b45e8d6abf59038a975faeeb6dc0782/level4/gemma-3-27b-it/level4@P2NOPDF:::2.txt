This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an improved alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein distance (or Earth Mover’s Distance), and highlight several benefits over MMD, primarily stronger gradients and the ability to effectively utilize smaller mini-batch sizes, alongside a reduction in the number of hyperparameters.

For training generative models, MMD is known to suffer from weak gradients and a requirement for large mini-batches. This work offers a compelling solution to both of these limitations. The empirical results convincingly validate the claim regarding smaller mini-batch sizes. However, the evidence supporting the claim of stronger gradients is less conclusive. Because the performance of MMD is sensitive to the choice of the bandwidth parameter, σ, a rigorous comparison should consider either the optimal σ value or a range of σ values when asserting superior gradient properties.

The assertion that PMD has fewer hyperparameters is also not fully substantiated. PMD relies on a distance metric, which itself may introduce hyperparameters comparable to those found in MMD. Furthermore, defining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces is a known challenge; therefore, PMD could potentially encounter similar difficulties to MMD in this regard. Conversely, established heuristics exist for selecting the bandwidth parameter in MMD. A fairer comparison would involve evaluating PMD against these established heuristics, effectively treating MMD as a method with minimal hyperparameter tuning.

Overall, the proposed PMD method possesses the valuable characteristic of enabling small mini-batch sizes, which facilitates faster training. It appears to be a valid advancement over large-batch MMD approaches. However, the continued reliance on a distance metric remains a potential limitation, particularly when applied to modeling high-dimensional data.
