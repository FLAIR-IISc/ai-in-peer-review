This paper proposes population matching discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to MMD for matching distributions from samples. PMD is framed as a sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance, and the authors argue it offers several advantages over MMD, notably stronger gradients, the ability to use smaller mini-batches during training, and fewer hyperparameters.

For training generative models in particular, MMD is known to yield weak gradients and to require large mini-batches; the approach here addresses both issues. The empirical support for the small mini-batch claim is compelling. Evidence for the “stronger gradients” claim is less conclusive: because MMD’s behavior depends on the bandwidth σ, it is important to either optimize σ or evaluate across a range of σ values when making such comparisons.

The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is also less persuasive, since PMD relies on a choice of distance function, which itself can introduce hyperparameters similar to those in MMD. Moreover, robust distance measurement in high-dimensional spaces is challenging, so PMD may inherit the same sensitivity to distance choice as MMD. Conversely, there are standard heuristics for selecting MMD bandwidths; it would be useful to compare against those and, in that light, treat MMD as effectively hyperparameter-free as well.

Overall, the method’s support for small mini-batches and consequently faster training is attractive. It appears to be a practical improvement over large-batch MMD approaches. However, its reliance on a distance metric may still constrain performance on higher-dimensional data.
