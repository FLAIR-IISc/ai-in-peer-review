Summary
The paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), which defines a discrepancy between two distributions as the Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance computed on two minibatches sampled from the distributions. The Wasserstein distance is computed either exactly in O(N^3) time or approximately in O(N^2). The paper presents empirical results using this objective.

Strengths
- The idea of leveraging minibatch-level optimal transport as a training signal is interesting and timely, given recent interest in OT-based learning.
- The paper includes experiments that help assess the practical behavior of minibatch Wasserstein training.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Sample complexity and bias: Using a minibatch Wasserstein distance to approximate the true distributional distance is likely to require large batch sizes N, especially for diverse, multimodal distributions. With small N, one should expect a biased objective whose global minimizer need not coincide with the true data distribution. This bias can encourage lower-entropy solutions and mode dropping. The SVHN samples in Figure 4 appear to show reduced diversity (e.g., digit “8” appears frequently), consistent with this concern.
- Sensitivity on multimodal mixtures: I suspect PMD will struggle to match even simple multimodal targets (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians, including learning variances) unless N is large. A toy study here would be illuminating and might already reveal the failure mode.
- Computational cost: An exact O(N^3) optimal transport per minibatch limits scalability. While the O(N^2) approximation is more tractable, the paper should more clearly discuss the trade-offs, accuracy loss, and practical batch sizes that make training feasible.
- Comparisons and baselines: The generated images are not as good as those from Wasserstein GAN. It would help to include stronger quantitative comparisons (e.g., FID/Inception scores), ablations over N, and training curves to isolate the effect of the PMD objective from architectural choices.

Rebuttal notes and requests for clarification
- Please clearly articulate the limitations of PMD versus MMD in the small-batch regime. In particular:
  - MMD: With the unbiased estimator from “A Kernel Two-Sample Test,” MMD can be optimized with SGD and remains valid with very small batch sizes (even batch_size = 2). This means MMD’s stochastic gradients are unbiased (or have known unbiased estimators), enabling convergence to the right distribution in principle under standard conditions.
  - PMD: To my knowledge, there is no known unbiased estimator of the gradient of the proposed PMD objective when it is defined via minibatch Wasserstein distances. This distinction is important: without an unbiased (or well-characterized) gradient estimator, small-batch training may systematically deviate from the true objective, exacerbating mode dropping and entropy reduction.
- Please add a discussion of the sample complexity required for PMD to approximate the population Wasserstein distance well. What are the implications for the necessary N as the data complexity (number of modes, variance heterogeneity, dimensionality) increases?
- Clarify the practical compute/memory costs for the exact and approximate solvers at the batch sizes used in the experiments, and report wall-clock times to contextualize feasibility.
- If possible, provide experiments on:
  - Synthetic mixtures of Gaussians to test recovery of means and variances across modes as N varies.
  - Sensitivity to N on real datasets, tracking diversity metrics and mode coverage.
  - Training stability and variance of gradients versus MMD and WGAN.

Quality of experiments
- The current qualitative results suggest lower sample diversity relative to WGAN. Including quantitative metrics, multiple random seeds, and ablations over batch size and solver choice (exact vs. approximate) would strengthen the claims.
- A fairness note: Ensure comparable architectures, training budgets, and hyperparameter tuning across baselines, since OT solvers add overhead that may effectively reduce the number of parameter updates.

Clarity and presentation
- The core idea is straightforward, but the distinction between population Wasserstein distance and its minibatch approximation should be emphasized to avoid over-claiming. Please state explicitly that PMD as used in training is a minibatch-based surrogate, discuss its bias, and provide guidance on when it is reliable.
- It would help to define the exact objective optimized during training (expectation over minibatches or single-sample minibatch draws per step), and whether any variance-reduction techniques are applied.

Minor issues
- Line 141: s/usally/usually/

Overall assessment
The paper explores an appealing direction—using minibatch optimal transport as a learning signal—but the current formulation appears to require large batch sizes to avoid biased learning that can reduce diversity. The lack of an unbiased gradient estimator for PMD (as commonly instantiated via minibatch Wasserstein) puts it at a disadvantage relative to MMD in the small-batch regime. The empirical results, especially on SVHN, show signs of these issues. With additional analysis of bias and sample complexity, stronger and fairer baselines, and experiments that probe multimodality and batch-size sensitivity, the work could become a more solid contribution. As it stands, I am not convinced that PMD, in its current form, offers practical advantages over established alternatives such as MMD and WGAN.
