The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a measure of the distance between two distributions, defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches drawn from these distributions. The Wasserstein distance is computed using either an exact O(N^3) algorithm or an approximate O(N^2) algorithm.

The paper has several positive aspects:
- The experimental results using this computation of the Wasserstein distance are intriguing and worth exploring. However, the quality of the generated images is not on par with those produced by Wasserstein GAN.

However, there are several concerns with the proposed method:
- To accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between two diverse multimodal distributions, a large minibatch size (N) is likely required. I suspect that even matching a mixture of Gaussians (including learning the variances) would reveal issues with the approach. 
- If N is not sufficiently large, the optimization process may converge to a global minimum that does not correspond to the true distribution. For instance, the learned distribution may have lower entropy than expected. This concern is echoed in the limited diversity of the SVHN samples in Figure 4, where digit 8 appears with high frequency.

Minor corrections:
- Line 141: "usually" should be used instead of "usally".

Update:
After reading the authors' rebuttal, I appreciate the additional experiments. However, I still believe that the authors should clarify the limitations of both PMD and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Specifically, MMD can be effective even with small batch sizes (e.g., batch_size=2) and can be trained using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) when employing the unbiased estimator of MMD from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper. This implies that MMD can converge to the correct distribution even with small minibatches. In contrast, PMD lacks a known unbiased estimator of the gradient, which may hinder its performance in such scenarios.
