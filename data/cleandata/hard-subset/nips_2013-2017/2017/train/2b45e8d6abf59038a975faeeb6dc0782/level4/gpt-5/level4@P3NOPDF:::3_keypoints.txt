- Introduces PMD, a population-based divergence that is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance and is fully differentiable for neural training.  
- Provides a thorough empirical comparison to MMD, the common population-based divergence in ML.  
- Exact PMD computation is cubic; authors propose a quadratic-time approximation that empirically retains strong statistical performance.  
- Clear, conceptually simple contribution grounded by consistency with the Wasserstein distance.  
- Theoretical results appear correct and are clearly presented.  
- Differentiability makes PMD practically relevant for end-to-end neural network training.  
- Empirical evaluation is rigorous, covering domain adaptation and generative modeling.  
- Thoughtful treatment of computational complexity and a practical quadratic approximation.  
- Good presentation quality: well structured, well written, and properly referenced.  
- Concern about computational cost at large scale; need clearer wall-clock time and memory comparisons to MMD.  
- Lacks evaluation on highly multimodal data to assess mode coverage vs MMD and adversarial methods.  
- Unclear how PMD behaves when sample size N is smaller than the number of target modes; needs evidence of robustness or degradation.  
- Suggest adding multimodal generative experiments with mode-coverage diagnostics (recovered modes, precision/recall).  
- Suggest detailed runtime and scalability analyses comparing exact PMD, the quadratic approximation, and MMD across dataset sizes/dimensions and batch training costs.  
- Suggest practitioner guidance on when to use the approximation, parameter selection, and effects on gradient accuracy and training stability.  
- Overall assessment: solid and promising work likely to be impactful; recommended for acceptance despite remaining scalability and multimodality questions.
