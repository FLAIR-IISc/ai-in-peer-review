This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator for the first Wasserstein metric between two probability distributions. The core idea is to compute the cost of a minimum weight bipartite matching between two finite sets of samples (or mini-batches) drawn from the distributions. This matching problem can be solved using exact algorithms like the Hungarian method or faster approximations. The resulting discrepancy is then used as a differentiable objective for training deep generative models or for domain adaptation, positioning PMD as an alternative to other distribution matching techniques like Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) or the dual-formulation-based Wasserstein GAN (WGAN).

**Strengths:**
*   The paper proposes a direct and intuitive method for optimizing a sample-based approximation of the primal form of the Wasserstein metric. This approach avoids the need for a separate, parameterized critic network and the associated training stability issues often found in adversarial methods like WGAN.
*   The connection between minimum weight matching and the Wasserstein metric is clearly established, and the proof that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the metric provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method.
*   Conceptually, PMD offers an advantage over kernel-based methods like MMD by eliminating the need to tune a sensitive kernel bandwidth hyper-parameter.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary concern is that the quality of the PMD estimate is highly dependent on the population size `N`. Accurately estimating the Wasserstein distance between complex, high-dimensional, or multimodal distributions would likely require a very large `N`. With an insufficient `N`, the optimization objective may be a poor proxy for the true distance, and the global minimum of the PMD objective might not correspond to the true data distribution. This could lead to biased models that, for example, exhibit lower entropy or mode collapse.
*   This concern about diversity seems to be reflected in the experimental results. The generated SVHN samples shown in Figure 4 appear to have low diversity, with a notable over-representation of the digit '8'. It would be insightful to see experiments on simpler, controlled problems (e.g., learning a mixture of Gaussians, including their variances) where this potential failure mode could be more clearly demonstrated.
*   The claims regarding training with small mini-batches are potentially misleading. The gradient of PMD, as formulated in Eq. (3) and (4), is computed with respect to a fixed matching `M*` derived from a specific population of size `N`. This gradient is not an unbiased estimator of the gradient of the true expected distance. In contrast, MMD has a well-established unbiased U-statistic estimator (from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper by Gretton et al.) which allows for stochastic gradient descent with very small mini-batches while still converging to the correct objective. Therefore, the argument that PMD holds a distinct advantage over MMD in terms of mini-batch size is not fully substantiated.
*   While interesting, the quality of the generated images does not appear to reach the standard set by other contemporary methods like WGAN.
