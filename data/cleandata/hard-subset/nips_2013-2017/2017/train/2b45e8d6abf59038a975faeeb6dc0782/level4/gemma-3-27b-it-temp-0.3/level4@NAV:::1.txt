The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance between minibatches sampled from two distributions. The Wasserstein distance is calculated using either exact (O(N^3)) or approximate (O(N^2)) algorithms.

**Pros:**
- The experimental evaluation of this Wasserstein distance computation is noteworthy.
- While the generated images demonstrate the method, they do not achieve the same quality as those produced by Wasserstein GANs.

**Cons:**
- The proposed PMD distance likely requires substantial minibatch sizes (N) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance, particularly when comparing diverse, multimodal distributions. I anticipate challenges even when attempting to match mixtures of Gaussians, including learning their variances.
- Insufficiently large N may lead optimization to converge to a suboptimal solution, distinct from the true underlying distribution. For instance, the learned distribution could exhibit reduced entropy.
- The SVHN samples presented in Figure 4 appear to lack diversity, with a noticeable over-representation of the digit '8'.

**Minor Issues:**
- Line 141: Correct "usally" to "usually".

**Update (Post-Rebuttal):**
I have reviewed the rebuttal and appreciate the additional experiments provided. 
The authors should more clearly articulate the limitations of PMD in comparison to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). 
Specifically, MMD can function effectively even with small batch sizes (e.g., batch_size=2), and can be trained using standard SGD when employing the unbiased estimator for MMD presented in the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper, allowing it to converge to the correct distribution with small minibatches. 
In contrast, PMD currently lacks a known unbiased estimator for its gradient.
