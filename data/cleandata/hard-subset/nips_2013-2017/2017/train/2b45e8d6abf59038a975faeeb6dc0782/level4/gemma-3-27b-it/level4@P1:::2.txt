This paper presents population matching discrepancy (PMD) as a compelling alternative to maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sample-based approximation of the Wasserstein metric (or Earth Mover's Distance) and possesses several advantages over MMD, most notably stronger gradients and the ability to utilize smaller mini-batch sizes, along with a reduced need for hyperparameter tuning.

The challenges associated with weak gradients and the requirement for large mini-batches when using MMD for training generative models are well-known, and the proposed PMD offers a promising solution to both issues. The empirical results effectively validate the claim regarding smaller mini-batch sizes. The verification of stronger gradients, however, could be strengthened. Given that MMD's performance is sensitive to the kernel bandwidth parameter σ, a more robust comparison would involve evaluating MMD with either the optimal σ or a range of σ values.

Regarding the claim of fewer hyperparameters, this appears less conclusive. PMD relies on a distance metric, which may itself introduce hyperparameters, similar to the kernel bandwidth in MMD. Furthermore, obtaining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces remains a challenge, and PMD may therefore face similar limitations as MMD in this regard. Conversely, established heuristics exist for choosing the bandwidth parameter in MMD, and it would be beneficial to compare PMD against these heuristics, effectively treating MMD as a potentially hyperparameter-free metric for a fairer comparison.

Overall, the proposed method's ability to enable small mini-batch sizes, leading to faster training, is a significant strength. It represents a valid improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, its reliance on a distance metric could potentially limit its performance when modeling high-dimensional data.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical limitation of MMD (large mini-batch size) with a theoretically sound alternative.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.
*   Provides a clear theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric.
*   Offers a potentially more stable training process due to stronger gradients.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The advantage of fewer hyperparameters is not fully convincing, as the chosen distance metric may introduce its own parameters.
*   The reliance on a distance metric could pose challenges in high-dimensional spaces.
*   The analysis of gradient strength could be strengthened with a more comprehensive comparison against optimized MMD configurations.
