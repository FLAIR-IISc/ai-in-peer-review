This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a compelling alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching tasks. The authors demonstrate that PMD can be interpreted as a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein distance (or Earth Mover’s Distance), offering benefits over MMD, specifically improved gradient behavior and the ability to utilize smaller mini-batch sizes with fewer hyperparameters.

Given the known challenges of MMD – namely weak gradients and the need for large mini-batches – in the context of training generative models, the proposed PMD offers a valuable solution to these issues. The empirical results effectively validate the claim regarding smaller mini-batch requirements. However, the evidence supporting stronger gradients is less conclusive. As MMD's performance is sensitive to the scale parameter σ, a comprehensive comparison should ideally consider either the optimal σ or a spectrum of σ values. 

The assertion of having fewer hyperparameters requires further substantiation. PMD’s dependence on a distance metric introduces the potential for its own hyperparameters, mirroring the situation with MMD. Furthermore, obtaining a robust distance metric in high-dimensional spaces remains a challenge, potentially subjecting PMD to similar limitations as MMD.  A useful addition would be a comparison to standard heuristics used for selecting the bandwidth parameter in MMD, potentially framing MMD as a hyperparameter-free alternative for evaluation.

In summary, the proposed PMD method offers a clear advantage by enabling small mini-batch sizes and, consequently, faster training. It represents a worthwhile improvement over large-batch MMD approaches. However, its reliance on a distance metric remains a potential limitation, particularly when applied to modeling high-dimensional data.
