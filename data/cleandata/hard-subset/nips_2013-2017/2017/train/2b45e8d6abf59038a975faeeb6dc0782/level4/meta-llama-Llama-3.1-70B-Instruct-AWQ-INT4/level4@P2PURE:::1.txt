The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a measure of the distance between two distributions, defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches drawn from these distributions. This distance is computed using either an exact algorithm with a time complexity of O(N^3) or an approximate algorithm with a time complexity of O(N^2).

The paper presents some interesting experiments utilizing this computation of the Wasserstein distance, which is a notable aspect of the work. However, the quality of the generated images is not comparable to those produced by Wasserstein GAN.

A significant drawback of the proposed PMD is that it likely requires large minibatch sizes (N) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between two diverse, multimodal distributions. This limitation may already be apparent when attempting to match a mixture of Gaussians, including learning the variances. Furthermore, if N is insufficiently large, the optimization process may converge to a global minimum that does not correspond to the true distribution, potentially resulting in a learned distribution with lower entropy. After considering the authors' rebuttal and the additional experiments provided, I appreciate the effort to address some of the concerns. However, I believe it is essential for the authors to clarify the limitations of both PMD and Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Notably, MMD can be effectively used with a batch size as small as 2 and can be trained using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) when employing the unbiased estimator of MMD from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper. This implies that MMD can converge to the correct distribution even with small minibatches. In contrast, PMD lacks a known unbiased estimator of the gradient, which is a significant limitation.
