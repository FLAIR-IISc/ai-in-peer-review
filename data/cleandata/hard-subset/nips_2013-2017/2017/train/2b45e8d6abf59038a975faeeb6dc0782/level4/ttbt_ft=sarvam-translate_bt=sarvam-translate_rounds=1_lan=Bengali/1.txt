This paper defines the difference in population matching between two distributions.
The Wasserstein distance between two minibatches from the distribution is considered as the difference in population matching.
The Wasserstein distance is computed by an exact O(N^3) or approximate O(N^2) algorithm.
Advantages:
- Experimenting with this calculation of the Wasserstein distance is quite interesting.
The resulting figures are not as good as those obtained from the Wasserstein GAN.
Disadvantages:
- Estimating the Wasserstein distance between two different multimodal distributions will require a large N for the proposed distance.
I suspect that if Gaussian mixtures (including variance learning) are attempted to be matched, the problems will already be apparent.
- If N is not large enough, the global minimum of optimization may lie at a point different from the actual distribution.
For example, the learned distribution may have less entropy.

The authors should clarify the limitations of PMD and MMD.
MMD is fine with batch_size=2.
MMD can be trained with SGD, provided the unbiased estimator of MMD is used from the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper.
Therefore, using smaller minibatches can allow MMD to converge to the correct distribution.
On the other hand, there is no known unbiased estimator of the gradient of PMD.
