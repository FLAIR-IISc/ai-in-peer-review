- Introduces PMD as a superior alternative to MMD for distribution matching tasks.  
- Shows PMD corresponds to a sampled variant of the Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance.  
- Claims PMD provides stronger gradients, works with smaller mini-batches, and has fewer hyperparameters.  
- Notes MMD suffers from weak gradients and requires large mini-batches in generative model training.  
- Empirical results support PMD’s efficacy with small mini-batches.  
- The stronger-gradient claim lacks rigorous justification and requires more analysis.  
- MMD comparisons must account for bandwidth σ selection; current experiments don’t adequately sweep or optimize σ.  
- The paper underestimates dependency on σ, weakening its gradient-strength argument.  
- The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is not well-substantiated since PMD depends on a chosen distance metric (e.g., Euclidean, Mahalanobis).  
- Choosing or estimating a meaningful distance metric in high dimensions is difficult, so PMD shares MMD’s core limitation.  
- MMD benefits from practical heuristics (e.g., median heuristic) that can make it effectively hyperparameter-free; comparisons should use them.  
- PMD is a promising practical advance for fast, small-batch training but needs further validation on complex, high-dimensional distributions; current empirical results are promising but insufficient.
