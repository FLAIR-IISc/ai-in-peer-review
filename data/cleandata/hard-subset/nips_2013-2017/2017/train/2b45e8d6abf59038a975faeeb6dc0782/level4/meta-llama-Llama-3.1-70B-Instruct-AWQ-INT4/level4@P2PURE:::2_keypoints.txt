- Proposes PMD as an alternative to MMD and shows PMD is a sampled version of the Wasserstein (earth mover’s) distance.  
- Claims PMD offers stronger gradients, supports smaller mini-batches, and has fewer hyperparameters.  
- Empirical results convincingly verify the small mini-batch advantage.  
- Evidence for stronger gradients is unconvincing because MMD’s performance depends on the kernel bandwidth (sigma); need comparisons to optimal or a range of sigmas.  
- The “fewer hyperparameters” claim is weak: PMD depends on a distance metric that may introduce its own hyperparameters.  
- Obtaining reliable distance metrics in high-dimensional spaces is challenging, so PMD may face similar problems as MMD.  
- There are established heuristics for MMD bandwidth selection; PMD should be compared to MMD using those heuristics.  
- Bottom line: PMD enables faster training via small batches but its reliance on a distance metric limits its applicability for high-dimensional data.
