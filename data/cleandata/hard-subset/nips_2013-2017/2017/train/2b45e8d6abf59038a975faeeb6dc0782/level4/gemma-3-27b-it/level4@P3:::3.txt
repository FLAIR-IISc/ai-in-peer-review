This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel and conceptually simple differentiable estimator for the distance between probability distributions. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric, addressing limitations of existing methods like Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), specifically its sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, vanishing gradients, and large batch size requirements. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides a thorough comparison of PMD to MMD across two deep learning tasks: domain adaptation and generative modeling. 

The core idea of PMD – minimizing the cost of matching samples between populations – is intuitive and elegantly connects to optimal transport theory. The theoretical analysis establishing the consistency of PMD with respect to the Wasserstein metric is a valuable contribution. The authors acknowledge and address the computational complexity of exact matching (cubic in the number of samples) by proposing and evaluating an efficient approximation with quadratic complexity, mitigating a potential practical concern. The paper is well-grounded in related work, appropriately citing key publications in the field of distribution divergence estimation and generative modeling.

The strengths of this work lie in its theoretical soundness, practical differentiability, and empirical validation. The experiments demonstrate that PMD overcomes the drawbacks of MMD, achieving improved performance and faster convergence in both domain adaptation and generative modeling scenarios. The ablation study on the impact of population size and matching algorithm provides useful insights into the method’s behavior.  The analysis of gradient magnitudes (Fig. 2b) effectively illustrates the advantage of PMD over MMD in avoiding gradient saturation.

However, the experimental evaluation could be further strengthened. While the results on MNIST, SVHN, and LFW are promising, the generative modeling experiments might benefit from evaluation on datasets with more complex, multi-modal distributions. It would be insightful to investigate the performance of PMD in such scenarios, particularly when the population size *N* is smaller than the number of modes in the underlying distribution – as the reviewer correctly notes.  Further exploration of the sensitivity of PMD to the choice of distance metric *d(·, ·)* beyond the L1 distance would also be valuable. While the authors mention the L1 distance, exploring alternatives and their impact on performance would be worthwhile. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:**  The paper rigorously establishes the theoretical properties of PMD, demonstrating its consistency as a Wasserstein metric estimator.
*   **Practical Advantages:** PMD offers a differentiable and parameter-free alternative to MMD, addressing key limitations such as bandwidth selection and gradient issues.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of PMD in both domain adaptation and generative modeling, showing improvements over MMD.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.
*   **Novel Contribution:** PMD represents a novel and potentially impactful contribution to the field of distribution divergence estimation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Exploration of Complex Generative Models:** The generative modeling experiments could be expanded to include more challenging datasets with multi-modal distributions.
*   **Distance Metric Sensitivity:** A more comprehensive exploration of the impact of different distance metrics *d(·, ·)* on PMD’s performance would strengthen the analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed PMD estimator is theoretically sound, practically appealing, and empirically validated. While the experimental evaluation could be further expanded, the existing results are convincing. I recommend **acceptance** of this paper to NIPS 2017.
