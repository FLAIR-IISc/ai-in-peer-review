This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based discrepancy defined via minimum-weight bipartite matching between two populations drawn from the respective distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance and propose using it as a differentiable training objective for distributional learning. In practice, they compute matchings either exactly with the Hungarian algorithm (O(N^3)) or approximately in O(N^2), and show empirically that the approximation maintains good performance. They also describe an SGD-based procedure where the matching is treated as fixed over several parameter updates, enabling efficient training.

The paper positions PMD relative to two prominent lines of work: (1) optimal transport/Wasserstein distances, where PMD corresponds to the empirical OT solution between equal-weight populations, and (2) maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), a widely used kernel-based discrepancy. The authors argue PMD has practical advantages over MMD as a training objective: it avoids kernel bandwidth tuning, provides stronger gradients (mitigating saturation issues with RBF kernels), and allows smaller SGD mini-batches than the population size because only matched pairs matter. They evaluate PMD on domain adaptation (using PMD as a feature-alignment regularizer) and on implicit deep generative modeling (both in pixel space and in autoencoder code space), comparing to MMD and to competitive baselines in domain adaptation.

Quality: The theoretical component appears sound. The strong consistency result follows standard tools (weak convergence of empirical measures and stability of optimal transport). The learning algorithm is clearly described, including the non-differentiation through the discrete matching. The computational trade-offs are acknowledged, and the approximate matching is a reasonable and well-motivated choice. The experimental evaluation is adequate: PMD is compared against MMD on both domain adaptation and generative modeling, with analyses of convergence speed, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and matching approximations. I particularly appreciate the fair protocol in domain adaptation (validation for both PMD and MMD).

Clarity: The paper is well organized and clearly written, with appropriate references to prior work in OT, MMD, GANs/WGANs, and domain adaptation. Claims are easy to follow, and empirical setups are described with sufficient detail.

Originality: Conceptually, PMD instantiates the empirical Wasserstein distance via equal-weight minimum matching; this is a known construction in OT. The contribution here is to expose it as a practical, differentiable objective for deep learning, analyze its gradient properties relative to MMD, and demonstrate effective training with approximate matching and subsampling. The thorough comparative study against MMD in both theory and practice is valuable.

Significance: The method is simple, practical, and potentially broadly useful for tasks that need sample-based distribution matching. Avoiding kernel bandwidth tuning and obtaining stronger, more uniform gradients are meaningful advantages in deep learning. The demonstrated gains in convergence speed and accuracy on domain adaptation, and improved sample quality over MMD in pixel space for generative modeling, suggest impact.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Computational cost remains a concern: exact matching is O(N^3) and even O(N^2) approximate matching can be nontrivial at scale, though the authors’ timing and approximation analyses are reassuring.
- The generative modeling results would be stronger on datasets with many distinct modes. I would like to see how PMD behaves when the population size N is smaller than the number of modes—does matching induce mode dropping, or can training still adequately cover the distribution?

Pros:
- Conceptually simple, differentiable estimator of distributional distance with a clear connection to Wasserstein.
- Strong consistency result.
- Practical training recipe (non-backprop through matching, subsampling, approximate matching).
- Addresses known MMD issues (bandwidth tuning, weak/saturated gradients) and supports smaller mini-batches.
- Empirically competitive or superior to MMD on domain adaptation; better convergence speed; improved robustness in pixel-space generation.

Cons:
- Matching cost can be high; scalability to very large N relies on approximations and potential GPU implementations.
- Generative experiments do not stress highly multimodal distributions; unclear behavior when N < number of modes.

Overall assessment: This is a solid and well-executed paper. It presents a useful and practical discrepancy for deep learning with convincing theoretical grounding and empirical validation. I recommend acceptance.
