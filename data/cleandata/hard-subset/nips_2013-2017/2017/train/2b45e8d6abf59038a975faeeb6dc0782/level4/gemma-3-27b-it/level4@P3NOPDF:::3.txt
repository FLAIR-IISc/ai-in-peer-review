## Review of PMD: Population-Based Divergence for Distribution Comparison

This paper introduces PMD, a novel population-based divergence measure between probability distributions, and demonstrates its consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein distance. The key strength of PMD lies in its conceptual simplicity and, crucially, its differentiability, enabling its use within neural network-based models – a significant advantage over many existing divergence measures. 

The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of PMD with MMD, the current leading population-based divergence in machine learning. This comparison is well-executed, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. Importantly, the paper acknowledges and addresses the computational cost associated with PMD; while exact calculation exhibits cubic complexity, the authors propose a computationally efficient quadratic approximation and convincingly demonstrate – through empirical results – that this approximation does not substantially impact statistical performance. 

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and appropriately cites relevant prior work. The theoretical results appear sound and the proofs are presented with sufficient detail. The experimental evaluation is adequate in scope, showcasing PMD’s performance in both domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks. Comparisons to MMD and other established methods are insightful and provide a strong baseline for assessing PMD’s effectiveness.

However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened. While the generative modeling results are promising, I would have been interested to see PMD applied to generative tasks involving distributions with a large number of modes. Specifically, I wonder about PMD’s behavior when the sample size (N) is smaller than the number of underlying modes in the true distribution. Investigating this scenario would provide a more complete understanding of the method’s limitations and potential areas for future improvement. Does the method struggle to accurately capture the full complexity of multimodal distributions with limited samples? 

Despite this minor concern, I believe this is a strong paper that presents a potentially valuable new method for comparing probability distributions. PMD’s differentiability, combined with its theoretical guarantees and competitive empirical performance, makes it a promising tool for a wide range of machine learning applications. The authors have identified a practical challenge (computational cost) and provided a reasonable solution, further demonstrating the robustness of their approach. I recommend acceptance.
