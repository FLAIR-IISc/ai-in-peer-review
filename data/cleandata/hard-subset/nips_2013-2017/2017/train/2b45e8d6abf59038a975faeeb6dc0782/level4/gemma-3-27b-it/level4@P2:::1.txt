This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a novel method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions based on samples, framing it as an estimator of the first Wasserstein metric. PMD is defined as the minimum weight matching cost between samples drawn from each distribution. The authors propose an algorithm for learning distribution parameters using PMD as an objective and demonstrate its application to domain adaptation and generative modeling. They highlight advantages over Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), including the absence of a bandwidth parameter, stronger gradients, and the ability to utilize standard mini-batch sizes.

The core idea of using optimal transport (and specifically, approximating the Wasserstein distance via sample matching) is well-motivated, building on the increasing interest in Wasserstein-based methods in deep learning, such as Wasserstein GANs [3]. The paper clearly establishes the theoretical connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric, demonstrating that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator. The experimental evaluation on domain adaptation and generative modeling provides a practical assessment of PMD’s performance. The observation that PMD can function effectively with smaller mini-batch sizes than MMD is a potentially valuable advantage for training efficiency.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. A primary concern is the potential for PMD to require large sample sizes (N) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance, particularly when dealing with complex, multimodal distributions. The authors acknowledge this implicitly, but a more thorough investigation of this limitation – perhaps with experiments demonstrating performance degradation as distribution complexity increases – would strengthen the work. The observation of low diversity in the generated SVHN samples (Figure 4) raises concerns about potential mode collapse or limitations in representing complex data distributions. While the authors address this briefly, a deeper analysis is needed.  Furthermore, while the paper claims stronger gradients with PMD, it doesn’t fully quantify this advantage beyond the distribution of gradient magnitudes shown in Figure 2(b).  A more rigorous comparison of gradient properties (e.g., variance, condition number) could offer further insight.

The rebuttal addresses concerns about MMD’s ability to converge with small mini-batches when using an unbiased estimator [17], which is a valid point and highlights a key difference in the estimation approach. However, the authors do not discuss whether a similar unbiased estimator could be developed for PMD, potentially mitigating some of its limitations. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to estimating Wasserstein distance with potentially beneficial properties (gradient behavior, mini-batch size).
*   Solid theoretical foundation establishing consistency with the Wasserstein metric.
*   Empirical results demonstrate competitive performance on benchmark tasks.
*   Clear presentation of the method and its advantages.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Potential sensitivity to sample size (N) and limitations with complex distributions.
*   Lack of in-depth analysis of limitations and comparison to alternative unbiased estimators for MMD.
*   Limited investigation of gradient properties beyond magnitude distribution.
*   The practical gains demonstrated are not overwhelmingly significant.

**Overall:**

The paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field of sample-based distribution distance estimation. While some limitations exist, the novelty of the approach, theoretical grounding, and promising empirical results justify consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the concerns regarding sample size sensitivity and clarifying the potential for unbiased gradient estimation in PMD.
