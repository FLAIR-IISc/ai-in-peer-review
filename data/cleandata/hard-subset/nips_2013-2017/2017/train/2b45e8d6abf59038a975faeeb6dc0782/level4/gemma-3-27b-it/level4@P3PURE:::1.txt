The paper defines the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) between two distributions as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches sampled from those distributions. The Wasserstein distance is computed using either an exact O(N^3) algorithm or an approximate O(N^2) algorithm.

**Pros:**

- The experimental evaluation of this Wasserstein distance computation is interesting. However, the generated images do not achieve the same quality as those produced by Wasserstein GANs.

**Cons:**

- The proposed distance likely requires large minibatch sizes (N) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between two sufficiently diverse, multimodal distributions. I anticipate that challenges would emerge even when attempting to match a mixture of Gaussians, particularly when learning the variances of those Gaussians.
- If N is insufficient, the optimization process may converge to a suboptimal solution, differing from the true underlying distribution. For instance, the learned distribution could exhibit lower entropy. The SVHN samples presented in Figure 4 appear to demonstrate limited diversity, with the digit '8' appearing disproportionately frequently.

**Update:**

I have reviewed the rebuttal and appreciate the additional experiments provided. The authors should more clearly delineate the limitations of PMD compared to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Specifically, MMD performs adequately even with a small batch size (e.g., batch_size=2), and can be effectively trained using Stochastic Gradient Descent when utilizing the unbiased estimator of MMD presented in the original “A Kernel Two-Sample Test” paper. This allows MMD to converge to the correct distribution even with small minibatches. Conversely, PMD lacks a known unbiased estimator for its gradient, which could hinder convergence.
