- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable sample-based estimator of the Wasserstein distance, and proves strong consistency with the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Proposes an algorithm using PMD as a training objective and evaluates it on domain adaptation and generative modeling, claiming better performance and convergence than MMD.  
- Addresses practical MMD limitations (kernel bandwidth sensitivity, weak gradients, large mini-batch needs) and offers PMD as a theoretically grounded alternative without a bandwidth hyperparameter.  
- Theoretical contribution: well-established connection between PMD and the Wasserstein metric via careful analysis.  
- Empirical results: PMD shows improved domain adaptation performance (Office dataset) and produces less noisy images than MMD in generative experiments.  
- Strength: PMD provides stronger gradients and can operate with smaller mini-batches (e.g., 100 vs. 1000 for MMD in the paper).  
- Major limitation: PMD critically depends on population size N for accurate estimation, especially for diverse multimodal distributions; minimum reliable N is not specified.  
- Generative limitation: produced blurry images on SVHN and LFW, indicating PMD/pixel-level L1 may be unsuitable for natural-image generation; this should be emphasized.  
- Comparative gap: insufficient comparison with other Wasserstein estimators (e.g., WGAN, WGAN-GP) in terms of computational cost, implementation complexity, and performance.  
- Claim about MMD requiring large batches is misleading; unbiased MMD estimators (Gretton et al., 2012) work with small batches and the paper should clarify PMD’s relative small-batch advantage.  
- Presentation is clear and well-organized, with sound theory and convincing empirical results, but the paper must more explicitly discuss PMD’s limitations (notably N as a new hyperparameter).  
- Recommendation: technically strong and practically relevant; accept pending clearer articulation and discussion of PMD’s constraints (population size and generative applicability).
