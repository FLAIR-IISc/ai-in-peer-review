Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based discrepancy that estimates the first Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) distance by computing a minimum-weight bipartite matching between two sample populations. The authors show that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of W1, and present a practical algorithm for learning distribution parameters by minimizing an expected PMD objective. Gradients are computed by holding the discrete matching fixed (no gradient is propagated through the matching), which makes the objective tractable for stochastic gradient descent.

PMD is compared primarily to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), a widely used sample-based discrepancy in machine learning for two-sample testing and learning (e.g., kernel two-sample tests, domain adaptation, and GMMN). The paper argues PMD has several advantages over MMD when used as a training loss: (i) no kernel bandwidth to tune; (ii) stronger gradients that avoid saturation; and (iii) the ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches than the population size. Experiments show improvements over MMD in domain adaptation (Office dataset) and generative modeling (MNIST, SVHN, LFW). Theoretical connections to optimal transport (OT) are developed via the empirical OT linear program, and consistency is established by appealing to classical results (Varadarajan theorem and stability of OT).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The connection between PMD and the empirical OT problem is correct, and the strong consistency result follows from standard OT stability under weak convergence. The learning algorithm is sensible, and the empirical evaluation is thorough within the chosen baselines.
- Algorithmic details: Using exact Hungarian matching (O(N^3)) or a known O(N^2) approximation is clearly described. The practical choice to ignore gradients through the discrete matching is justified informally (piecewise-constant matching under infinitesimal parameter changes), but a more formal treatment (e.g., subgradient arguments, measure-zero boundary changes) would strengthen the claims about unbiasedness.
- Empirical support: The domain adaptation experiments show competitive or better accuracy than MMD on Office, with faster convergence and reduced sensitivity to hyperparameters. In generative modeling, PMD yields cleaner samples than MMD in pixel space and works well in code space, though some generated images are blurry, likely reflecting the limitations of L1 at the pixel level. The timing analysis is useful.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The motivation, method, and theoretical analysis are clearly presented. The algorithmic choices (population size versus mini-batch size, exact vs. approximate matching) and their impact on speed/quality are explained with informative plots.

A few points could be clarified:
- Provide a more precise justification for treating the gradient as not propagating through the matching (e.g., subgradient interpretation or almost-everywhere differentiability). Discuss potential effects when the matching changes during learning.
- Explicitly discuss memory and computational costs of storing/processing O(N^2) pairwise distances for large N.
- In domain adaptation, clarify the protocol for hyperparameter tuning on labeled target data and relate it to standard unsupervised adaptation practice.

Originality
The core idea—using minimum-cost matching of empirical samples as a learning loss—is well grounded in OT and is not new per se (empirical OT). The contribution here is to package this as PMD, provide a clean consistency argument, and show that it can serve as a practical, differentiable training objective that compares favorably to MMD in deep learning. This is a useful and timely angle given the popularity of OT/Wasserstein-based learning.

That said, the paper should acknowledge and position itself relative to entropic-regularized OT (“Sinkhorn distances,” Cuturi 2013) and subsequent automatic differentiation of entropic OT/regularized OT in learning (e.g., Genevay et al., 2016/2017), which also provide scalable, differentiable approximations to W1 with favorable complexity and GPU implementations. Similarly, there exist linear-time unbiased MMD estimators that reduce the quadratic cost and can be used with small batches; a comparison would make the MMD baseline stronger.

Significance
PMD is a promising alternative to MMD as a training objective in scenarios where gradient strength, parameter tuning, and batch size are limiting factors. The empirical gains in domain adaptation and generative modeling, combined with conceptual simplicity and solid theory, make this a potentially impactful contribution. However, broader significance would be clearer with:
- Comparisons to more recent Wasserstein-based methods used for learning (e.g., Sinkhorn-regularized OT, WGAN and its variants, and Cramér distance) using standard quantitative metrics (Inception Score, FID) on SVHN/LFW, not only qualitative visuals or PMD/MMD values.
- Analysis of sample complexity in high dimensions: empirical OT is known to converge slowly with dimension (rates ~ N^−1/d). The authors’ empirical rate (~N^−0.23) is informative; discussing implications and mitigations (e.g., learned feature spaces, regularization) would be valuable.

Pros
- Simple, well-motivated objective with a clear connection to W1 and a strong consistency guarantee.
- Practical learning algorithm; informative analysis of matching complexity and approximations.
- Empirical advantages over MMD: stronger gradients, reduced hyperparameter sensitivity, faster convergence, and ability to use smaller mini-batches.
- Clear presentation and thorough comparisons within the chosen scope.

Cons
- Missing comparisons and discussion of closely related, scalable OT methods (Sinkhorn/entropic OT) and stronger MMD baselines (linear-time estimators).
- Limited quantitative evaluation for generative modeling; results are on low-resolution datasets and mostly qualitative.
- The gradient treatment through the discrete matching is informal; more rigorous justification would help.
- Potential scalability concerns (O(N^2) memory/time for distances) not fully explored; GPU implementations are suggested but not demonstrated.
- Domain adaptation tuning uses labeled target validation, which weakens claims about unsupervised adaptation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to entropic-regularized OT (Sinkhorn) and modern Wasserstein-based training methods; report standard generative metrics.
- Evaluate on multi-modal synthetic distributions and real datasets where the number of modes exceeds the population size, to probe behavior when N < number of modes.
- Include a discussion or appendix with a formal subgradient view of the objective and conditions under which the “frozen matching” gradient is valid.
- Consider stronger MMD baselines (e.g., linear-time MMD, multi-kernel strategies, bandwidth selection) to ensure fairness.
- Report memory/runtime scaling with N and D, and, if possible, provide a GPU implementation for matching.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and well-presented paper. PMD is a practical and theoretically grounded discrepancy that addresses several shortcomings of MMD in learning. With improved positioning relative to entropic OT and stronger quantitative evaluations, it would make a strong NeurIPS contribution. I lean toward acceptance.
