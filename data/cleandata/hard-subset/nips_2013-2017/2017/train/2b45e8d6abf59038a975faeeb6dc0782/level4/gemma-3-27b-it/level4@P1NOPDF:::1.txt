The paper defines Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) between two distributions as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches sampled from those distributions. The Wasserstein distance is computed using either an exact O(N<sup>3</sup>) algorithm or an approximate O(N<sup>2</sup>) algorithm.

**Pros:**

- The experiments demonstrating this computation of the Wasserstein distance are interesting. However, the generated images are not as visually compelling as those produced by Wasserstein GANs.

**Cons:**

- The proposed distance likely requires large minibatch sizes (N) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between two significantly different, multimodal distributions. I anticipate potential issues even when attempting to match a mixture of Gaussians, particularly if the variances are also being learned.
- If N is insufficient, the optimization process may converge to a suboptimal solution, differing from the true underlying distribution. For instance, the learned distribution could exhibit lower entropy. The SVHN samples shown in Figure 4 appear to lack diversity; the digit 8 is noticeably overrepresented.

**Update:**

I have reviewed the rebuttal and appreciate the additional experiments provided. The authors should more clearly articulate the limitations of PMD in comparison to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). 

MMD performs adequately even with a batch size of 2. Furthermore, MMD can be successfully trained using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) when employing the unbiased estimator of MMD presented in the original “A Kernel Two-Sample Test” paper. This allows MMD to converge to the correct distribution even with small minibatches. Conversely, PMD currently lacks a known unbiased estimator for its gradient.
