This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a compelling alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching. The authors demonstrate that PMD is a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein metric (or Earth Mover’s Distance) and possesses several advantages over MMD, most notably stronger gradients and the ability to utilize smaller mini-batch sizes, along with a reduction in the number of hyperparameters.

For training generative models, MMD often suffers from weak gradients and a requirement for large mini-batches. Therefore, the proposals in this paper offer a valuable solution to both of these challenges. The claim regarding the utility of small mini-batch sizes is well-supported by the empirical results. However, the evidence supporting the stronger gradients claim is less conclusive. Because the performance of MMD is highly dependent on the scale parameter σ, a fair comparison requires considering either the optimal σ or a range of values when evaluating gradient strength.

Regarding the claim of fewer hyperparameters, I find it less convincing. PMD relies on a distance metric, which may itself contain additional hyperparameters comparable to those in MMD. Furthermore, obtaining a reliable distance metric in high-dimensional spaces is challenging, and PMD may therefore encounter similar issues to MMD in this regard. Conversely, established heuristics exist for selecting the bandwidth parameter in MMD; it would strengthen the paper to compare against these heuristics and treat MMD as a relatively hyperparameter-free metric as well.

Overall, the proposed method offers the attractive benefit of enabling small mini-batch sizes, thereby facilitating faster training. It appears to be a worthwhile improvement over large-batch MMD methods. However, the reliance on a distance metric remains a potential limitation, particularly when modeling high-dimensional data.
