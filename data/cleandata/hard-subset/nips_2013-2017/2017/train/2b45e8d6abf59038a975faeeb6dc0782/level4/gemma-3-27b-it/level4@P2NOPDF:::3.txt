This paper introduces PMD, a novel population-based divergence measure between probability distributions, and demonstrates its consistency as an estimator of the Wasserstein distance. A key strength of PMD lies in its conceptual simplicity and differentiability, enabling its application within neural network-based models – a significant advantage for practical implementation.

The authors provide a comprehensive comparative analysis of PMD against MMD, the currently dominant population-based divergence used in machine learning. This comparison effectively highlights the benefits and trade-offs of the proposed method. They acknowledge a computational limitation – the cubic complexity of exact PMD calculation – and proactively address this with a quadratic-complexity approximation. Importantly, their empirical results indicate that this approximation introduces minimal degradation in statistical performance.

The paper is exceptionally well-structured and clearly written, demonstrating a thorough engagement with relevant prior work through appropriate citations. The theoretical foundations appear sound and the mathematical arguments are presented convincingly. 

The experimental evaluation is adequate, showcasing PMD's performance in both domain adaptation tasks (compared to MMD and other relevant methods) and generative modeling applications (again, primarily compared to MMD). While the results are promising, I would have been interested to see PMD applied to generative modeling problems characterized by high-modality data distributions. Specifically, it remains an open question whether PMD performs effectively when the sample size (*N*) is smaller than the number of modes in the underlying distribution. Exploring this scenario would provide a more complete understanding of the method’s limitations and potential vulnerabilities. 

Overall, this is a strong contribution that introduces a potentially valuable tool for comparing probability distributions. The method's differentiability and theoretical guarantees, combined with its competitive empirical performance, suggest it has the potential to be widely adopted within the machine learning community.
