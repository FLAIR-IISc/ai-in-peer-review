- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the Wasserstein distance between target and generated minibatches, computed with O(N^3) exact or O(N^2) approximate algorithms.  
- Strength: Novel use of PMD as a training objective with useful ablation studies showing minibatch-size vs. sample-quality trade-offs.  
- Major limitation: PMD needs large minibatch sizes N to estimate Wasserstein for diverse/multimodal distributions; small N fails to capture distributional structure.  
- Empirical consequence: Small-N training leads to reduced diversity/low-entropy solutions (e.g., SVHN mode collapse, overrepresentation of digit “8”).  
- Major limitation: No known unbiased gradient estimator for PMD, so SGD with small minibatches is not justified.  
- Contrast to MMD: MMD has an unbiased estimator (Gretton et al.) enabling SGD and theoretical convergence with small N; PMD lacks similar optimization guarantees.  
- Authors’ rebuttal insufficiently frames the gradient/minibatch issue as a fundamental constraint rather than a technicality.  
- Minor: Typo “s/usally/usually.”  
- Minor: The claim “MMD can be trained with SGD” should explicitly cite the unbiased MMD estimator from the kernel two-sample test.  
- Conclusion: PMD is theoretically interesting but practically constrained by the N bottleneck and absence of scalable gradient estimation; experiments don’t overcome these limits.  
- Recommendation: Reject — PMD is impractical for standard deep-learning pipelines; authors should explicitly state PMD is viable only for very large N (e.g., >1000) and explain why small-batch optimization isn’t possible.
