- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based estimator of the 1-Wasserstein metric using minimum-weight bipartite matching.  
- Proves PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the Wasserstein metric.  
- Claims advantages over MMD: no kernel bandwidth, stronger gradients during optimization, and ability to use smaller SGD mini-batches.  
- Demonstrates PMD on unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling.  

Strengths:
- Addresses practical MMD issues: kernel bandwidth sensitivity, vanishing gradients, and need for large mini-batches.  
- Theoretically well-founded by linking sample matching directly to the Wasserstein metric.  
- Empirically shows compatibility with small mini-batches, yielding faster convergence and better final performance (Fig. 5a).  
- Offers an alternative to WGAN-style methods by approximating the primal Wasserstein metric without a learned critic.  

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evidence for “stronger gradients” is limited; comparisons should include optimally tuned or mixture-kernel MMD baselines.  
- Claim of fewer hyperparameters is weak because PMD requires choosing a distance metric d(·,·), a nontrivial hyperparameter.  
- Using pixel-wise L1/L2 as the distance may be inappropriate for perceptual similarity and could explain blurry generations; sensitivity to metric choice is not analyzed.  
- MMD practical heuristics (e.g., median heuristic, kernel mixtures) can mitigate bandwidth issues, and comparisons against those are missing.  
- Computational cost of matching (even with O(N^2) approximation) may be a bottleneck relative to closed-form O(N^2) MMD.  

Overall:
- PMD is a valuable, well-motivated estimator that enables efficient small-batch training, but its gradient/hyperparameter claims need stronger validation and its effectiveness depends on choosing a suitable distance metric for high-dimensional data.
