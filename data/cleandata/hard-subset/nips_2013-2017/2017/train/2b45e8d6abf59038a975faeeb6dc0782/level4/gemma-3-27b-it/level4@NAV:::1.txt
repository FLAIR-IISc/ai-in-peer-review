This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance calculated between minibatches sampled from two distributions. The Wasserstein distance is computed using either exact (O(N^3)) or approximate (O(N^2)) algorithms. 

**Strengths:**
- The experimental evaluation of this Wasserstein distance computation is a valuable contribution.
- While the generated images demonstrate the method’s application, their quality currently does not reach the level achieved by Wasserstein GANs.

**Weaknesses:**
- A significant limitation of the proposed PMD lies in its requirement for large sample sizes (N) to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance, particularly when comparing diverse and multimodal distributions. I anticipate challenges even in scenarios like matching mixtures of Gaussians, including accurate variance estimation.
- Insufficiently large N may lead to optimization converging to suboptimal solutions distinct from the true underlying distribution; for instance, the learned distribution could exhibit reduced entropy.
- The SVHN samples presented in Figure 4 appear to lack diversity, with a noticeable over-representation of the digit '8'.

**Post-Rebuttal Update:**
I appreciate the additional experiments provided in the rebuttal. 

However, the authors should further clarify the limitations of PMD in comparison to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD). Specifically, MMD can be effectively used with small batch sizes (e.g., batch_size=2), and can be trained using SGD with the unbiased estimator presented in the original "A Kernel Two-Sample Test" paper, allowing convergence to the true distribution even with small minibatches. In contrast, PMD currently lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator.
