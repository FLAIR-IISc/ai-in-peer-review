

The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the Wasserstein distance between the empirical distributions of two minibatches sampled from target and generated distributions. This distance is computed via exact O(N³) or approximate O(N²) algorithms, where N denotes minibatch size.  

**Strengths:**  
The experimental exploration of PMD as a training objective is novel and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate its application in generative modeling, with results showing reasonable image synthesis quality.  

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Scalability to Multimodal Distributions:** PMD requires large N to accurately estimate Wasserstein distances between diverse, multimodal distributions. This limitation is particularly evident when modeling complex structures like Gaussian mixtures (where capturing variance is critical). The authors' experiments with mixtures of Gaussians (not detailed in the main text) would be essential to validate this claim, as current results (e.g., Figure 4) suggest PMD struggles with diversity. For instance, SVHN samples in Figure 4 exhibit low diversity (e.g., overrepresentation of digit '8'), indicating the learned distribution may have reduced entropy—a symptom of insufficient N.  

2. **Optimization Reliability:** With small N, PMD optimization risks converging to suboptimal minima. The global minimum may not align with the true data distribution, as the minibatch-based Wasserstein distance fails to capture global structure. This undermines PMD’s utility for practical generative tasks where large batches are computationally prohibitive.  

**Comparison to MMD:**  
The rebuttal correctly notes that Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) avoids PMD’s limitations. Crucially:  
- MMD with a batch size of 2 can converge to the true distribution using the unbiased estimator from Gretton et al. (2012), enabling SGD training.  
- PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator, making it fundamentally less suitable for small-batch optimization. The authors should explicitly contrast these properties in the revision, as this is a critical flaw.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- Line 141: "s/usally/usually" → "usually."  
- The discussion of SVHN diversity (Figure 4) should clarify whether low entropy stems from PMD’s limitations or other factors (e.g., model architecture).  

**Post-Rebuttal Assessment:**  
The authors addressed the Gaussian mixture experiment in the rebuttal, confirming PMD’s failure to model multimodal variance. However, they did not sufficiently clarify PMD’s core limitation: its dependence on large N for reliable gradient estimation. While the rebuttal notes MMD’s batch-size flexibility, the paper must explicitly state that PMD’s gradient bias prevents small-batch convergence, unlike MMD. The claim that "MMD is OK with batch_size=2" is technically accurate but requires context: MMD’s unbiased estimator (Gretton et al., 2012) enables this, whereas PMD lacks such a mechanism.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper’s core idea is interesting but suffers from a fundamental scalability issue. PMD’s reliance on large minibatches renders it impractical for most generative tasks, especially with multimodal data. The authors must:  
1. Explicitly state PMD’s N-dependent bias in the abstract/conclusion.  
2. Contrast PMD’s gradient limitations with MMD’s unbiased estimator (citing Gretton et al., 2012).  
3. Include Gaussian mixture experiments to empirically justify the N requirement.  

Without these revisions, the method’s utility remains questionable. While the experiments are well-executed, the paper overstates PMD’s potential relative to established metrics like MMD. The current version risks misleading readers about the practical viability of the proposed discrepancy measure.  

**Final Verdict:**  
**Weak Reject** (Revisions required for clarity on limitations). The work lacks sufficient evidence that PMD offers advantages over MMD in real-world settings, particularly given MMD’s proven small-batch convergence. The rebuttal partially addresses concerns but fails to resolve the critical scalability issue. The authors must clarify PMD’s inherent constraints to avoid overstating its contributions.
