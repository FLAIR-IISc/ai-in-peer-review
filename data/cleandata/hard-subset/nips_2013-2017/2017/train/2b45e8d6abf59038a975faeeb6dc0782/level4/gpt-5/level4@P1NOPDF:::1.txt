This paper defines the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) between two distributions as the Wasserstein distance computed between two minibatches drawn from those distributions. The Wasserstein distance is evaluated either exactly in O(N^3) time or approximately in O(N^2), where N is the minibatch size.

Pros:
- It is interesting to see experiments based on this formulation of the Wasserstein distance.
- That said, the generated images are not as good as those produced by the Wasserstein GAN.

Cons:
- Estimating the Wasserstein distance between diverse, multimodal distributions appears to require large N. I suspect that issues would already be evident when trying to match a mixture of Gaussians (including learning the variances).
- If N is not sufficiently large, the optimization may have its global minimum at a distribution different from the true one. In particular, the learned distribution may have lower entropy. The SVHN samples in Figure 4 seem to exhibit low diversity; for example, the digit 8 appears frequently.

Update:
I have read the rebuttal; thank you for the additional experiments. The paper should clarify the limitations of PMD versus MMD. MMD is fine with batch_size = 2. It can be trained with SGD when using the unbiased estimator from the original “A Kernel Two-Sample Test” paper, and thus can converge to the correct distribution even with small minibatches. By contrast, PMD does not have a known unbiased gradient estimator.
