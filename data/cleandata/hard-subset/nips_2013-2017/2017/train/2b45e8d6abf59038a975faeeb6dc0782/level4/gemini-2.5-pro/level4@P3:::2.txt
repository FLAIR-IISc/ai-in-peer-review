**Summary**

This paper introduces the Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a novel, sample-based estimator for the distance between two probability distributions. PMD is defined as the cost of the minimum weight matching between two sets of samples drawn from the distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein metric (or Earth Mover's Distance). A key contribution is an algorithm for using PMD as a differentiable objective for training deep neural networks via stochastic gradient descent. The paper positions PMD as an alternative to the widely used Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), arguing that PMD overcomes several of MMD's practical drawbacks: it has fewer hyperparameters, provides stronger gradients, and, crucially, allows for the use of small mini-batches during optimization, leading to faster convergence. These claims are evaluated on two deep learning tasks: unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling, where PMD is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD-based methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled Approach:** The paper establishes a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed metric by connecting it to the first Wasserstein distance. The proof that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of this metric lends significant credibility to the approach. This provides a well-grounded alternative to other distribution matching techniques in deep learning.

2.  **Addresses a Practical Problem:** The method directly targets known limitations of training with MMD, particularly the requirement for large batch sizes to get a stable estimate, which can slow down training considerably. The proposed algorithm, which computes the matching on a larger "population" and then performs SGD updates on smaller mini-batches, is an elegant and effective solution to this problem. The empirical results in Figure 5(a) convincingly demonstrate the advantage of using smaller mini-batches for faster and better convergence.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is thorough, covering two distinct and relevant applications. In both domain adaptation and generative modeling, PMD demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to MMD. The faster convergence shown in Figure 3(a) is a particularly strong practical advantage.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-articulated, the proposed method is described in detail, and the connection to prior work is well-established.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **The "Fewer Hyperparameters" Claim:** This claim is not fully substantiated. While PMD eliminates the kernel bandwidth parameter (`sigma`) required by RBF-kernel MMD, it introduces a dependency on the choice of the underlying distance metric `d(·, ·)`. The choice of metric (e.g., L1 vs. L2) is a critical hyperparameter, especially in high-dimensional spaces where distances can be ill-defined or non-perceptual. The paper defaults to L1 distance but does not investigate the sensitivity to this choice. To make the comparison fairer, the MMD baseline could be evaluated using standard heuristics for bandwidth selection (e.g., the median heuristic), which effectively makes MMD a "hyperparameter-free" method in practice.

2.  **Validation of the "Stronger Gradients" Claim:** The empirical analysis supporting the claim of stronger gradients (Figure 2b) could be strengthened. The gradient of MMD is highly sensitive to the kernel bandwidth. The paper does not specify how the bandwidth was chosen for this particular analysis. A more convincing comparison would involve showing that PMD's gradients are stronger than MMD's across a reasonable range of bandwidths, or at least when compared against an MMD model with an optimally tuned bandwidth. Without this, it is difficult to conclude that the observed effect is not simply due to a suboptimal choice of `sigma` for MMD.

3.  **Challenges in High-Dimensional Spaces:** The reliance on a standard distance metric like L1 in the sample space may limit PMD's effectiveness on complex, high-dimensional data like natural images. The authors acknowledge that the generated images can be blurry and attribute this to the pixel-level L1 distance. This highlights a fundamental challenge: the performance of PMD is directly tied to how well the chosen metric captures the semantic or perceptual distance between samples. While moving to a feature space (e.g., from a pre-trained autoencoder) is a valid and effective workaround, it introduces additional modeling complexity and is a limitation shared by MMD (which relies on a good kernel).

4.  **Computational Cost:** Although addressed by using an approximate matching algorithm, the O(N^2) or O(N^3) complexity of the matching step is a notable computational overhead compared to the O(N^2) complexity of MMD. While the experiments show this is manageable for the population sizes used, this trade-off between computational cost and estimation accuracy could become a bottleneck for applications requiring very large populations.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper presents a valuable and well-motivated contribution to the problem of sample-based distribution matching. The proposed PMD method is theoretically sound and offers a compelling practical advantage over MMD by enabling efficient small-batch training. The empirical results are strong and support the primary claims of the paper. While the arguments regarding hyperparameter reduction and gradient strength could be more robustly supported, the method stands as a significant and useful improvement for practitioners training deep models with distribution matching objectives. The work is a solid piece of research that is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.
