The authors present PMD, a population-based divergence between probability distributions, and show that it is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance. The proposed estimator is conceptually simple and differentiable, a key property that allows for the training of neural network-based models. The authors provide a thorough comparison of PMD to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), one of the most prominent population-based divergences in machine learning.

The authors acknowledge the primary drawback of their method—the cubic complexity of its exact calculation—but address this by proposing a quadratic-time approximation. They demonstrate through empirical results that this approximation does not significantly degrade statistical performance.

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and includes appropriate references to prior work. The theoretical results appear to be sound, and the experimental analysis is adequate. The authors compare PMD against MMD and other methods for domain adaptation and also evaluate it against MMD for generative modeling.

I would have liked to see the method applied to generative modeling in domains with many modes. It would be interesting to understand how PMD performs when the sample size is smaller than the number of modes in the distribution.

All things considered, this is a good paper that presents a promising and potentially very useful method for comparing distributions.
