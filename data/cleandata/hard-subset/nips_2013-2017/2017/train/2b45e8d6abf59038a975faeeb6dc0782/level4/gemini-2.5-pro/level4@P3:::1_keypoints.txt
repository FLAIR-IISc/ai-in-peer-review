- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD): a sample-based, min-weight bipartite matching estimator for the 1-Wasserstein distance used as an SGD training objective.  
- The authors prove PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein metric.  

- Strength — Novelty & OT connection: a direct, parameter-free, and intuitive optimal-transport-based alternative to MMD and adversarial methods.  
- Strength — Empirical demonstration: shows competitive or better results than MMD on domain adaptation and generative modeling, with informative ablations on population and mini-batch sizes.  
- Strength — Clarity: paper is well-written and the method and algorithm are clearly explained.  

- Weakness — High sample complexity: PMD may require large population sizes to reliably approximate Wasserstein in high-dimensional or multi-modal settings.  
- Weakness — Risk of biased optimization and mode collapse: small N can bias the objective toward low-entropy/few-mode solutions, with empirical signs of low diversity (e.g., SVHN).  
- Weakness — Potentially unfair MMD comparison: authors compare against a possibly biased MMD estimator instead of standard unbiased small-batch SGD MMD, weakening claims about PMD’s advantages.  
- Weakness — Unanalyzed gradient estimator: SGD updates rely on approximations (stable matching, subsampling) whose bias/variance and theoretical properties are not analyzed; no known unbiased PMD gradient estimator.  
- Weakness — Lower generative quality: generated images are visibly inferior to contemporary methods like WGANs, limiting practical impact for generation tasks.  

- Acceptance argument: novel, parameter-free 1-Wasserstein approximation with solid OT theory and promising domain-adaptation results.  
- Rejection argument: sample-complexity and mode-collapse risks, unfair MMD baseline, and missing theory on the PMD stochastic gradient estimator.  

- Minor: Typo at Line 141: "usally" → "usually".  

- Post-rebuttal: Additional experiments appreciated but core concerns persist; authors should explicitly discuss PMD’s limitations (small-N bias) and the lack of an unbiased gradient estimator versus MMD.
