

This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as a refined alternative to Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) for distribution matching, particularly in generative modeling. The authors establish that PMD constitutes a sampled variant of the Wasserstein metric (Earth Mover's Distance), offering three key advantages: stronger gradients during optimization, compatibility with smaller mini-batches, and reduced hyperparameter sensitivity. The empirical validation of smaller mini-batch efficacy is compelling, demonstrating tangible speed improvements in training. However, the claim regarding stronger gradients requires careful scrutiny. Since MMD's performance critically depends on the bandwidth parameter σ, a fair comparison must evaluate PMD against MMD using optimized σ or a comprehensive range of σ values—not merely the authors' default choice. Without this, the gradient strength assertion lacks sufficient rigor.  

Regarding the "fewer hyperparameters" claim, the justification is underdeveloped. PMD relies on an underlying distance metric (e.g., Euclidean, Mahalanobis), which may itself introduce hyperparameters—potentially negating the purported advantage over MMD. In contrast, MMD benefits from well-established heuristics for σ selection (e.g., median heuristic), which could position it as effectively hyperparameter-free in practice. The authors should explicitly compare PMD against such MMD tuning strategies to substantiate their hyperparameter claim.  

The core limitation of PMD remains its dependency on a distance metric, a challenge that persists in high-dimensional spaces where reliable metric learning becomes intractable. This issue parallels MMD’s own reliance on σ, though PMD’s metric choice adds an additional layer of complexity. While the paper acknowledges this challenge, it does not sufficiently address how PMD might overcome it—particularly for complex, high-dimensional data common in modern generative tasks.  

Despite these caveats, the paper delivers a valuable contribution: PMD enables efficient training via small mini-batches without sacrificing distribution-matching fidelity, directly addressing a well-documented bottleneck in MMD-based methods. The empirical results robustly support this advantage across multiple benchmarks, making PMD a promising practical alternative for resource-constrained settings.  

In summary, the work presents a technically sound and empirically validated improvement over large-batch MMD approaches, with clear benefits for training speed. However, the theoretical claims about gradient strength and hyperparameter reduction require stronger justification through rigorous comparison protocols. The unresolved dependency on a distance metric represents a significant practical constraint, especially for high-dimensional applications where metric learning remains challenging. The authors should clarify whether PMD’s metric choice can be automated or generalized (e.g., via learned metrics) to mitigate this limitation.  

Overall, PMD is a valid and useful advancement for specific use cases where small-batch training is prioritized, but its broader applicability hinges on addressing the distance metric dependency. The paper’s strengths lie in its clear empirical demonstration of mini-batch efficiency, while its weaknesses stem from insufficiently defended theoretical claims. With minor revisions to strengthen the hyperparameter and gradient arguments, this work would be a strong candidate for publication. As presented, it offers a pragmatic solution to a real problem but warrants cautious interpretation regarding its theoretical advantages over MMD.
