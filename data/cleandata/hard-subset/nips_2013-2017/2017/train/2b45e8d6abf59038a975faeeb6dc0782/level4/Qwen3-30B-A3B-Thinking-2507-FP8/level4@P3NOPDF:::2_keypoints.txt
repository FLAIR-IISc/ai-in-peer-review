- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to MMD, claiming stronger gradients, small-minibatch compatibility, and reduced hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Shows PMD can be viewed as a sampled approximation of the Wasserstein distance, providing theoretical support.  
- Empirical results convincingly demonstrate PMD enables effective small-minibatch training, a practical advantage over MMD.  
- Claim of “stronger gradients” for PMD is under-supported empirically.  
- MMD comparisons appear unfair because MMD’s bandwidth σ was fixed rather than optimized (median heuristic or grid search).  
- A rigorous comparison should tune both PMD and MMD to their optimal configurations before claiming gradient superiority.  
- Claim that PMD reduces hyperparameters is questionable because PMD depends on a distance metric (e.g., Euclidean, Mahalanobis) that may require tuning.  
- MMD has well-established heuristics (e.g., median heuristic) that make it effectively hyperparameter-free in practice; PMD’s advantage is not demonstrated.  
- PMD’s reliance on a distance metric raises fundamental concerns in high-dimensional settings due to the curse of dimensionality.  
- Paper lacks analysis or experiments on high-dimensional scalability (e.g., ImageNet or high-dim embeddings), limiting claims of broad applicability.  
- Overall: PMD is a valuable, context-limited advance for small-batch scenarios, but theoretical and empirical claims overreach without further validation.  
- Recommendation: authors should tune MMD fairly, analyze/mitigate distance-metric issues in high dimensions, add high-dimensional experiments or theory, and temper broad claims.
