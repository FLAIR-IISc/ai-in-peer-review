The authors present PMD as a population-based deviation among probability distributions and show that it is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance.

The presented estimator is conceptually simple and differentiable, which clearly allows for training an NN-based model.

The authors compare PMD to MMD, which is the most prominent population-based deviation in machine learning.

The authors comment on the shortcomings of their method: the exact calculation has cubic complexity, but propose using an approximation with quadratic complexity, and show in their empirical results that this does not significantly degrade statistical performance.

This paper is well-structured and written, and includes references to previous work where necessary.

The theoretical results seem correct.

The experimental analysis is sufficient. They compare PMD to MMD and other methods for domain optimization, and to MMD for generative modeling.

I would have liked to see the method being used for generative modeling in a multi-mode domain. I wonder if PMD works when the number of modes is smaller than the number of modes.

Overall, I think this is a good paper, which presents a potentially very useful method for comparing distributions.
