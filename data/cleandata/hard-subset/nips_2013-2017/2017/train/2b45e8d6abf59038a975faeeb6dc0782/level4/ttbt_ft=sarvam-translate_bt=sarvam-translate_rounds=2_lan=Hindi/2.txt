This paper presents Population Matching Discreteness (PMD) as a better alternative to MMD for distribution matching applications. It is shown that PMD is a sample version of the Wasserstein metric or Earth mover distance, and has some advantages over MMD, particularly strong gradient claims and the applicability of small mini-batches, and lower hyperparameters.

To train a least-squares model, the MMD metric suffers from weak gradients and the need for large mini-batches, so the proposals in this paper provide a good solution to both of these problems. The claim of small mini-batches has been quite well verified in empirical results. Verification of the strong gradient claim is less satisfactory, as the MMD metric depends on the parameter sigma, so it is necessary to consider either the best sigma or a range of sigmas when making such a claim.

In terms of having lower hyperparameters, I think this claim is less well-supported, as PMD depends on a distance metric, and this distance metric may also have additional hyperparameters in the case of MMD. Furthermore, it is difficult to obtain a reliable distance metric in a high-dimensional space, so PMD may have the problem of depending on a distance metric like MMD. On the other hand, there are some standard heuristics for choosing bandwidth parameters for MMD, it would be good to compare against such heuristics and consider MMD as a hyperparameter-free metric.

Overall, I think the proposed method has good quality in allowing small minibus batch sizes, so training is fast. This seems like a legitimate improvement over large batch MMD methods. But it still has the problem of depending on a distance metric, which may limit its success in modeling high-dimensional data.
