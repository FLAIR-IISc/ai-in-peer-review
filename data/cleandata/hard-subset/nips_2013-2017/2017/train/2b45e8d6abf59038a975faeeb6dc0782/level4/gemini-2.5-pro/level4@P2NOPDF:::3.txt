This paper introduces the Population-based Matched Divergence (PMD), a novel sample-based divergence for comparing probability distributions. A key theoretical contribution is the proof that PMD is a consistent estimator of the Wasserstein distance.

A significant practical advantage of the proposed PMD estimator is its conceptual simplicity and differentiability. These properties make it directly applicable as a loss function for training deep neural network-based models, a clear benefit for applications in generative modeling and other areas. The paper provides a thorough comparison of PMD to the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), one of the most prominent sample-based divergences in machine learning. This comparison effectively situates PMD within the existing literature and highlights its relative strengths.

The authors are transparent about the limitations of their method, noting that the exact calculation of PMD has cubic complexity. To address this computational bottleneck, they propose a practical approximation with quadratic complexity. Crucially, their empirical results demonstrate that this approximation does not significantly degrade statistical performance, making the method viable for larger-scale problems.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a comprehensive review of related work and appropriate citations. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the provided proofs. The experimental evaluation is solid, providing convincing evidence for the utility of PMD. The authors benchmark their method against MMD and other relevant baselines on a domain adaptation task and further demonstrate its applicability by comparing it to MMD in a generative modeling setting.

One area for potential improvement in the experimental section would be an investigation into generative modeling for more complex, multi-modal distributions. It would be particularly interesting to understand the behavior of PMD in challenging scenarios where the sample size, N, is smaller than the number of modes in the target distribution. It is unclear if the matching-based nature of PMD offers an advantage in capturing such distributions or if it struggles when samples fail to cover all modes.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and promising method for comparing probability distributions. The proposed Population-based Matched Divergence is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. It represents a valuable contribution to the field and is likely to be of significant interest to researchers working in generative modeling and domain adaptation.
