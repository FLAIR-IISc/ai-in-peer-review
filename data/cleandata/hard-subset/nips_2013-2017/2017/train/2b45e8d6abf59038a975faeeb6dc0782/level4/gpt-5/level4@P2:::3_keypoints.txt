- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based, minimum-weight bipartite matching measure that is differentiable.
- PMD is shown to be a strongly consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein distance.
- Differentiability enables gradient-based neural network training without an auxiliary critic.
- Authors provide a learning algorithm using PMD and apply it to domain adaptation and deep generative modeling.
- Empirically, PMD compares favorably to MMD in both performance and convergence speed.
- PMD avoids MMD’s kernel bandwidth tuning and adversarial critics’ training requirements.
- Paper gives direct, appropriate comparisons to MMD across tasks.
- Strength: Conceptually simple and practical to integrate into deep learning pipelines.
- Strength: Theoretical support with a proven strong consistency result.
- Strength: Careful computational trade-off discussion; approximate quadratic matching works well empirically.
- Strength: Solid empirical evaluation on domain adaptation and generative modeling.
- Strength: Clear, well-organized presentation with relevant references.
- Weakness: Lacks experiments on highly multimodal datasets to assess behavior when modes exceed sample size N.
- Weakness: Needs deeper analysis of approximation error and impact of approximate matching on gradients/optimization dynamics.
- Weakness: Missing sensitivity analyses for base distance choices (L1 vs L2 or learned features) in image tasks.
- Qualitative: Methods and claims are well supported by theory and experiments; methodology appears sound.
- Qualitative: Manuscript is clear and reproducible.
- Originality: Builds on optimal transport but offers a useful, practical differentiable formulation.
- Significance: Addresses key MMD limitations and provides a practical alternative with meaningful impact potential.
- Pros for acceptance: Simple principled estimator, strong theory, empirical gains, fewer hyperparameters, practical training recipe.
- Cons for acceptance: Limited multimodal evaluation and limited analysis of approximate matching’s effect on optimization.
- Overall: Well-executed, theoretically grounded, and practical alternative to MMD with compelling results; broader multimodal evaluation would strengthen it.
