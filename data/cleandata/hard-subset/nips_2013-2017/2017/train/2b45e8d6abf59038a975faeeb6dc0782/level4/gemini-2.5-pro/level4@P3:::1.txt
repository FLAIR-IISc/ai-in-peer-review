This paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a sample-based method for estimating the distance between two probability distributions, designed for use as a training objective in deep learning. PMD is defined as the average cost of the minimum weight bipartite matching between two sets of samples (or mini-batches) drawn from the distributions. The authors prove that PMD is a strongly consistent estimator of the 1-Wasserstein metric. The paper proposes an algorithm for learning model parameters by minimizing PMD via stochastic gradient descent and highlights several advantages over the popular Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), such as having no kernel hyper-parameters, providing stronger gradients, and allowing for smaller mini-batch sizes during optimization. The method is evaluated on unsupervised domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks, where it is shown to outperform or be competitive with MMD.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Connection to Optimal Transport:** The paper proposes a direct and parameter-free method for approximating the 1-Wasserstein metric for training deep models. By framing the discrepancy as a minimum weight matching problem, the work provides a clear and intuitive connection to optimal transport theory. This approach is a valuable alternative to other distribution matching techniques like MMD or adversarial methods (e.g., Wasserstein GANs), which rely on kernel choices or auxiliary critic networks, respectively.

2.  **Empirical Demonstration:** The authors provide experiments on two relevant deep learning tasks—domain adaptation and generative modeling. The results suggest that PMD can be an effective regularizer and training objective, demonstrating faster convergence and, in some domain adaptation settings, better final performance than MMD. The ablation studies on the impact of population size and mini-batch size are informative.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definition of PMD and its connection to the 1-Wasserstein metric are explained clearly. The proposed parameter learning algorithm is straightforward to understand.

### Weaknesses

1.  **High Sample Complexity for Reliable Estimation:** A major concern is the sample complexity required for PMD to be a reliable estimator of the true Wasserstein distance, particularly for high-dimensional and complex multi-modal distributions. While the paper shows empirical convergence for a simple case (two displaced Gaussians), this may not generalize. With an insufficient population size `N`, the matching cost could be a poor approximation of the true distance between the underlying continuous distributions.

2.  **Risk of Biased Optimization and Mode Collapse:** Related to the sample complexity issue, using a small population size `N` could introduce significant bias into the optimization landscape. The PMD objective for small `N` might have a global minimum that does not correspond to the target distribution, potentially leading the model to learn a distribution with lower entropy or fewer modes. This concern appears to be empirically validated by the generative modeling results in Figure 4. For instance, the generated SVHN samples exhibit low diversity, with certain digits (like '8') appearing with disproportionate frequency. A more controlled experiment, such as attempting to learn a mixture of Gaussians, would be valuable to assess whether PMD can capture all modes or is prone to mode collapse.

3.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison to MMD:** The paper's central claims about MMD's weaknesses, particularly the need for a large mini-batch size, are contestable. While the authors' implementation of MMD may require large batches, the original work on MMD ("A Kernel Two-Sample Test," Gretton et al., 2012) presents an unbiased estimator (based on a V-statistic) that can be computed efficiently with small mini-batches (as small as two samples). Training with this unbiased estimator and SGD allows MMD to converge correctly even with small batches. The paper's comparison seems to be against a biased, full-batch U-statistic estimator, which weakens the claims about PMD's superior convergence properties. The authors should clarify this and ideally compare against a more standard, unbiased SGD implementation for MMD.

4.  **Properties of the Gradient Estimator:** The proposed SGD update for PMD relies on two key approximations. First, it assumes the optimal matching `M*` is stable across several gradient steps. Second, the subsampling approach in Eq. (4) is an approximation of the full population gradient. The theoretical properties of this gradient estimator (e.g., its bias and variance) are not analyzed. Unlike MMD, for which unbiased stochastic gradient estimators are known, it is not clear if one exists for PMD, which is a notable theoretical limitation.

5.  **Quality of Generative Models:** While the experiments demonstrate the feasibility of using PMD for generative modeling, the quality of the generated images is visibly lower than that of contemporary methods like Wasserstein GANs. This may limit the practical significance of PMD for this specific application, though its value for tasks like domain adaptation remains plausible.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, interesting, and parameter-free method for approximating the 1-Wasserstein distance.
*   The theoretical connection to optimal transport is well-established and elegant.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results, especially for domain adaptation, where it can outperform MMD.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Serious concerns about the sample complexity of PMD for complex distributions.
*   Evidence of mode collapse in generative modeling experiments suggests the method may be biased with practical population sizes.
*   The comparison against MMD is potentially weakened by not using a standard unbiased MMD estimator suitable for small-batch SGD.
*   The theoretical properties of the proposed stochastic gradient estimator are not analyzed.
