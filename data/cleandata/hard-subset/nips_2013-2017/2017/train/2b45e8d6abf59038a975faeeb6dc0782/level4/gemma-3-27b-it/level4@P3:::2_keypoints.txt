- Proposes Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as an alternative to MMD for measuring distribution distance in deep learning.  
- Shows PMD is a consistent estimator of the first Wasserstein (Earth Mover’s) metric.  
- Claims advantages over MMD: stronger gradients, effective with smaller mini-batches, and reduced hyperparameter tuning.  
- Motivation: MMD can suffer vanishing gradients and needs large mini-batches, hindering efficient training of generative models.  
- Theoretical connection to the Wasserstein metric provides a solid foundation.  
- Key insight: formulate distribution distance as a minimum weight matching problem.  
- Empirical strengths: faster convergence with smaller mini-batches and strong results on domain adaptation and generative modeling tasks.  
- Authors acknowledge and address computational costs of solving the matching problem.  
- Caveat: “fewer hyperparameters” is misleading because the chosen distance metric d(·,·) can introduce its own hyperparameters.  
- Claim of stronger gradients is not fully substantiated; needs comparison to MMD with optimized bandwidth selection.  
- Needs more discussion of PMD’s applicability and challenges in high-dimensional data where metric choice is critical.  
- Should analyze the impact of different matching algorithms and provide trade-offs between speed and accuracy.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a practical problem, theoretically sound, convincing empirical evidence, and well-written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: hyperparameter claim needs nuance, MMD comparisons should be more rigorous, and high-dimensional limitations under-discussed.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address hyperparameter nuance, MMD tuning comparisons, and high-dimensional/algorithmic limitations.
