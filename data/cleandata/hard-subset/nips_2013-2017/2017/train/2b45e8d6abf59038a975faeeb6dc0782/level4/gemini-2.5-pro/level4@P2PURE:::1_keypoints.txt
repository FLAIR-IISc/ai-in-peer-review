- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD) as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches, computed via exact O(N^3) or approximate O(N^2) algorithms.  
- Experiments exploring this Wasserstein computation method are interesting.  
- Generated image quality appears inferior to that of a Wasserstein GAN.  
- PMD likely requires large minibatch sizes N to accurately estimate Wasserstein distance for complex, multimodal distributions.  
- Even in simpler tasks (e.g., mixtures of Gaussians with learnable variances), PMD limitations may become apparent.  
- With insufficient N, the optimization can have global minima that do not match the true data distribution, leading to lower entropy and mode collapse (e.g., SVHN samples overrepresent digit '8').  
- Minor typo: "usally" (Line 141) should be "usually".  
- Authors provided additional experiments in rebuttal, but reviewers request clearer articulation of PMD limitations versus MMD.  
- MMD can be trained with very small minibatches using an unbiased estimator, enabling SGD convergence to the target distribution; it is unclear whether an unbiased estimator for PMD gradients exists, which is a significant disadvantage for stochastic optimization.
