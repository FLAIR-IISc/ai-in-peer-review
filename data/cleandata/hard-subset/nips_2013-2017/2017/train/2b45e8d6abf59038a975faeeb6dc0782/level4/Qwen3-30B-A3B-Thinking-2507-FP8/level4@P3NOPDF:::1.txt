

The paper introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), defined as the Wasserstein distance between two minibatches drawn from target and generated distributions. While the authors compute this distance via exact O(N³) or approximate O(N²) algorithms, the core contribution and its practical implications warrant careful evaluation.

**Strengths:**  
The experimental exploration of PMD as a training objective is novel. The ablation studies on image generation (e.g., Figure 4) provide useful insights into the method’s behavior, particularly regarding the trade-offs between minibatch size and sample quality.

**Major Limitations:**  
1.  **Fundamental Scalability Issue:** PMD requires large minibatch sizes *N* to accurately estimate the Wasserstein distance between diverse, multimodal distributions. This is critical for realistic applications. For instance, matching a mixture of Gaussians (especially when learning variances) would expose severe limitations at small *N*, as the minibatch Wasserstein estimator fails to capture the true distributional structure. The authors’ experiments (e.g., SVHN in Figure 4) suggest this problem manifests empirically: the generated samples show reduced diversity (e.g., overrepresentation of digit '8'), indicative of a low-entropy solution arising from insufficient *N*.  
2.  **Lack of Practical Gradient Estimation:** Crucially, PMD lacks a known unbiased gradient estimator for optimization. This contrasts sharply with Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), which benefits from an unbiased estimator (from Gretton et al., *A Kernel Two-Sample Test*) enabling SGD training even with small minibatches (e.g., *N*=2). MMD’s convergence to the true distribution is theoretically supported under standard conditions, while PMD’s optimization behavior with small *N* remains undefined—potentially converging to a suboptimal solution (e.g., a collapsed distribution with lower entropy, as observed in Figure 4). The authors’ rebuttal acknowledges this limitation but fails to clarify it as a *fundamental* constraint of PMD, not merely a technical detail.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- Line 141: "s/usally/usually" → "usually" (typo).  
- The claim that "MMD can be trained with SGD" should explicitly reference the unbiased MMD estimator from the original kernel two-sample test paper to avoid ambiguity.  

**Conclusion and Recommendation:**  
PMD is an interesting theoretical construct, but its practical utility is severely constrained by the *N* requirement and the absence of a scalable gradient estimator. The experiments, while suggestive, do not overcome these core limitations. The comparison to MMD is particularly damning: MMD achieves comparable or better performance with small minibatches and well-established optimization guarantees, while PMD’s reliance on large *N* makes it impractical for most real-world scenarios where minibatch sizes are limited by memory or computational constraints.  

The authors should explicitly state that PMD is *only* viable for very large *N* (e.g., *N* > 1000), and that it cannot replace MMD or WGAN in standard training regimes. The rebuttal’s additional experiments do not address the fundamental scalability issue. Without this clear limitation, the paper risks misleading readers about PMD’s applicability.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Reject**. The method’s impracticality for standard deep learning pipelines (due to the *N* bottleneck and lack of gradient estimators) outweighs its theoretical novelty. The MMD comparison demonstrates that a superior alternative exists with established theoretical support. The paper would benefit from a stronger discussion of *why* PMD cannot leverage small-batch optimization (e.g., no unbiased gradient), rather than merely noting its limitations. Without this, the contribution lacks sufficient practical justification for NIPS.
