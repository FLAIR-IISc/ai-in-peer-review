- Introduces Population Matching Discrepancy (PMD), a differentiable distribution-distance estimator based on minimum-weight matching, consistent with the first Wasserstein metric.  
- Extends WGAN/GMMN ideas and targets MMD’s limitations by removing kernel bandwidths, yielding stronger gradients and enabling smaller mini-batches (100 vs. 1000).  
- Empirically compared to MMD on domain adaptation (Office) and generative modeling (MNIST/SVHN/LFW), showing faster convergence and improved performance.  
- Theorem 3.3 provides rigorous proof of PMD’s consistency with Wasserstein distance.  
- Empirical results (Fig. 2b, 5a) support claims of stronger gradients and mini-batch efficiency.  
- Implementation discussion of exact vs. approximate matching and trade-offs is technically sound.  
- Paper is very clear and well-structured, with precise definitions, good figures, and careful experimental design.  
- PMD is an original combinatorial approach distinct from kernel-based (MMD) and dual-approximation (WGAN) methods.  
- Method is significant for addressing a practical bottleneck in distribution-distance estimation, improving training speed and stability.  
- Lacks finite-sample error bounds for PMD (not addressed in theoretical analysis).  
- Generative samples on SVHN/LFW are blurry, suggesting PMD’s L1-based objective may be ill-suited for natural images.  
- Cubic complexity of exact matching is noted; more thorough validation of approximate matching’s statistical impact for large N is needed.  
- Acceptance pros: novel, theoretically grounded, empirically strong and practically useful; cons: limited analysis when N < number of modes and insufficient validation on natural-image generation.  
- Overall, aligns with NIPS scope and makes a clear, impactful contribution; minor weaknesses do not undermine scientific quality.
